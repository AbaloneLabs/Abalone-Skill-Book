# Reading Selection And Workload

Reading lists are where good course design most often collapses. Instructors, loving their field, assign everything that matters; students, facing finite hours, read nothing well. The result is a course where the readings are excellent on paper and unread or skimmed in practice, defeating the purpose. Selecting readings is not a matter of choosing the best texts; it is a matter of choosing the texts students will actually engage with deeply enough to learn, within the time they realistically have.

Use this skill before selecting readings, estimating reading workload, curating a reading list, or deciding required versus optional status. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a reading list that is intellectually comprehensive but practically unreadable, or that loads so much that students resort to skimming and shortcuts.

## Core Rules

### Estimate Reading Time Realistically

Students read more slowly than instructors assume, especially dense academic prose, primary sources, and texts in a non-native language. Estimate time per page by text type and adjust for the student population.

Rough baselines for undergraduates reading academic text:

- dense theory or philosophy: roughly five to eight minutes per page;
- standard academic article: roughly three to five minutes per page;
- textbook prose: roughly two to three minutes per page;
- primary sources in translation or older English: six to ten minutes per page;
- non-native language reading: add fifty to one hundred percent.

Multiply by pages and sum across the week. Compare to the credit-hour workload expectation. If the estimate exceeds available time, cut.

### Match Reading Density To The Course's Role

A graduate seminar can sustain two hundred pages of dense theory weekly because students are specialists with time. An introductory survey cannot. Calibrate density to level, prerequisites, and student preparation.

Density factors:

- course level and student preparation;
- number of other demanding courses students typically take;
- whether reading is the primary learning mode or supplements lecture;
- whether students are majors or non-majors;
- term length and weekly contact hours.

### Choose Required Versus Optional Deliberately

Not everything valuable must be required. Distinguish:

- required reading: essential for class activities and assessment, must be done;
- recommended reading: deepens understanding, useful but not essential;
- reference reading: consulted as needed for specific tasks.

Students optimize for grades, so they will do required reading and skip the rest. Make required reading the set students must do to participate and succeed, and be honest that optional reading will mostly go unread. Do not label optional reading as required hoping students will do more; they will just do less of each.

### Prioritize Depth Over Coverage

A few readings engaged deeply teach more than many readings skimmed. Depth lets students practice close reading, develop arguments, and retain concepts. Coverage without depth produces recognition without understanding.

Depth practices:

- assign fewer pages with reading guides or questions;
- build class activities that require close engagement;
- assess reading through discussion, quizzes, or response papers;
- revisit core texts across the term to deepen engagement.

### Provide Reading Guidance For Dense Texts

Difficult texts defeat students who lack disciplinary reading strategies. Provide scaffolding so students can engage rather than give up.

Guidance options:

- reading questions that focus attention on key arguments;
- a brief framing of the text's context and stakes;
- a glossary for discipline-specific or archaic terms;
- annotation guides modeling how experts read the text;
- a "what to do if you get stuck" note.

Scaffolding is especially important for primary sources, theory, and texts from unfamiliar traditions.

### Diversify Text Types And Voices

A reading list drawn from one tradition, era, or demographic narrows students' understanding and signals whose work counts. Diversify:

- canonical and non-canonical voices;
- multiple methodological or theoretical traditions;
- contemporary and historical perspectives;
- global and local perspectives;
- voices of scholars from underrepresented groups.

Diversification is not tokenism; it is intellectual completeness. A syllabus that presents one tradition as the whole field misrepresents the field.

### Align Readings With Activities And Assessment

Readings should serve class activities and assessment, not stand alone. If a reading is assigned but never referenced in discussion, lecture, or assessment, students learn it does not matter and stop reading.

Alignment checks:

- does each reading connect to a class activity or discussion;
- is each reading's key idea assessed somewhere;
- are readings sequenced to build on each other;
- do later readings assume earlier ones.

### Manage Cost And Access

Expensive or inaccessible materials create equity problems. Students who cannot afford the textbook or cannot access a paywalled article fall behind silently. Manage access:

- use library reserves and electronic resources;
- prefer open educational resources where quality allows;
- provide alternatives to expensive textbooks;
- ensure required materials are available before the term starts;
- scan or link rather than assume purchase.

### Update Readings Periodically

Fields change, and readings age. A reading list frozen for a decade teaches a dated field. Review readings every few years, retiring obsolete pieces and adding current work, while preserving foundational texts that endure.

### Sequence Readings To Build Complexity

Order readings so that earlier ones provide the foundation later ones require. Begin with more accessible texts that build confidence and concepts, then introduce denser or more contested material once students have the tools to engage.

## Common Traps

### Assigning Everything That Matters

Comprehensive reading lists defeat themselves. Students skim or skip. Choose fewer, deeper.

### Underestimating Reading Time

Instructors read their field fast; students do not. Estimate realistically by text type.

### Optional Labeled As Required

Calling optional reading required hoping for more engagement produces less reading of each. Be honest about status.

### Coverage Over Depth

Many readings skimmed teach recognition, not understanding. Prioritize depth.

### Dense Texts Without Scaffolding

Difficult texts without guidance produce giving up, not struggle that teaches. Scaffold.

### Monocultural Reading Lists

One tradition presented as the field misrepresents the discipline. Diversify.

### Readings Disconnected From Class

Reading never referenced in activities teaches students not to read. Align.

### Cost And Access Barriers

Expensive or inaccessible materials silently disadvantage students. Manage access.

### Frozen Reading Lists

Decade-old lists teach a dated field. Update periodically.

### Complexity Too Early

Dense material before foundations produces frustration. Sequence to build complexity.

## Self-Check

- Has reading time been estimated realistically by text type and student population?
- Does reading density match the course's level, role, and student preparation?
- Are required readings distinguished from recommended and reference, with honest expectations?
- Is depth prioritized over coverage so students engage rather than skim?
- Is scaffolding provided for dense, primary, or unfamiliar texts?
- Does the reading list diversify text types, voices, traditions, and perspectives?
- Is each reading connected to a class activity, discussion, or assessment?
- Are materials affordable and accessible to all students before the term starts?
- Is the reading list reviewed and updated periodically rather than frozen?
- Are readings sequenced to build complexity rather than front-loading difficulty?
