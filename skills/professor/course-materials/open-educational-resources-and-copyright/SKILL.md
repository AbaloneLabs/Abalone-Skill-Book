# Open Educational Resources And Copyright

Course materials carry legal and access consequences that instructors often overlook. A textbook that costs two hundred dollars silently filters out low-income students. A photocopied chapter may violate copyright. A linked article behind a paywall is invisible to students without library access. Open educational resources and sound copyright practice are not merely budget issues; they are equity and legality issues that shape who can actually participate in the course.

Use this skill before selecting course materials, evaluating open educational resources, determining fair use, using licensed library content, or choosing between commercial and open options. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a materials plan that is legally risky, financially exclusionary, or built on resources of unknown quality.

## Core Rules

### Treat Material Cost As An Equity Issue

Textbook and materials cost is a major driver of inequity in higher education. Students who cannot afford required materials share copies, skip purchase, or drop courses. Treat affordability as a design constraint, not an afterthought.

Affordability practices:

- prefer open or library-licensed materials where quality allows;
- use course reserves so students can access expensive texts;
- choose editions strategically to support used and rental markets;
- avoid requiring new editions annually unless substantively justified;
- disclose material costs on the syllabus so students can plan.

### Evaluate OER Quality Before Adopting

Open does not mean good. Some OER are excellent; others are outdated, inaccurate, or poorly suited to the course. Evaluate quality with the same rigor as commercial texts.

Quality criteria:

- accuracy and currency of content;
- disciplinary credibility of author or institution;
- alignment with course outcomes and level;
- pedagogical features (exercises, glossaries, media);
- accessibility (screen reader support, alt text, structure);
- adaptability for modification;
- reviews or adoption history at peer institutions.

A poor OER adopted only because it is free can harm learning. Vet it.

### Understand Fair Use For Course Materials

Fair use allows limited educational use of copyrighted material without permission, but it is a case-by-case judgment, not a blanket exemption. The four factors are:

- purpose and character of the use, including nonprofit educational use;
- nature of the copyrighted work;
- amount and substantiality used;
- effect on the market for the original.

Educational use weighs toward fair use but does not guarantee it. Copying entire chapters repeatedly, using the same excerpts term after term, or substituting for a textbook purchase weakens fair use claims. When in doubt, use library-licensed or link to lawful sources rather than copy.

### Use Library-Licensed Content Properly

Academic libraries license vast collections of journals, e-books, databases, and streaming media. Using licensed content through stable links or course reserves is usually lawful and free to students. Understand the terms:

- link rather than download and repost when licenses require it;
- use electronic reserves within the library's system;
- respect simultaneous user limits;
- check that the license covers course use;
- ask librarians for help locating or licensing materials.

Librarians are partners in materials selection; consult them early.

### Apply Creative Commons Licenses Correctly

When using or creating OER, Creative Commons licenses specify permitted uses. Understand the license elements:

- BY: attribution required;
- SA: share-alike, derivatives must use the same license;
- NC: non-commercial use only;
- ND: no derivatives allowed.

Comply with license terms: attribute properly, respect non-commercial and no-derivatives restrictions, and share derivatives under share-alike when required. Misusing CC-licensed material undermines the OER ecosystem and can create legal exposure.

### Ensure Accessibility Of All Materials

Legal obligations and pedagogical good practice require accessible materials. Inaccessible PDFs, uncaptioned videos, and poorly structured documents create barriers and delay accommodations.

Accessibility checks:

- screen-reader-readable text, not scanned images;
- heading structure and document tags;
- alt text for meaningful images;
- captioned or transcribed video and audio;
- sufficient color contrast;
- accessible equations, tables, and interactive elements.

Accessibility is easier when designed in from the start than when retrofitted after an accommodation request.

### Plan For Permanence And Link Stability

Linked materials disappear when URLs change or sites go down. Plan for stability:

- use persistent identifiers like DOIs or library permalinks;
- archive critical readings in the LMS or reserves;
- verify links before the term and again mid-term;
- have backup copies or alternatives for fragile sources.

A reading that vanishes mid-term disrupts the course and signals poor preparation.

### Decide Between Commercial And Open Deliberately

The choice between a commercial textbook and OER involves tradeoffs of quality, cost, currency, pedagogical fit, and instructor time. Decide deliberately:

- is a high-quality OER available for this subject and level;
- does the commercial text offer pedagogical value the OER lacks;
- is the cost justified by that value;
- can the instructor adapt OER to fit better than a fixed textbook;
- what is the maintenance burden of curating versus adopting a text.

Neither option is inherently superior; the decision should serve students and outcomes.

### Respect Student Privacy In Digital Materials

Some digital textbooks and platforms track student reading time, highlighting, and quiz performance. Be aware of what data commercial platforms collect and how it is used, and prefer options that minimize surveillance and protect student privacy.

## Common Traps

### Ignoring Cost As Equity

Expensive required materials silently filter out low-income students. Treat affordability as design.

### Adopting OER Without Vetting

Free but inaccurate or poorly suited OER harms learning. Evaluate quality.

### Assuming Education Equals Fair Use

Educational use weighs toward fair use but does not guarantee it, especially for repeated or substitutive copying. Use licensed or linked sources.

### Reposting Licensed Content

Downloading and reposting licensed articles can violate terms. Link through the library instead.

### Misusing Creative Commons Licenses

Ignoring BY, NC, SA, or ND terms undermines OER and creates exposure. Comply with licenses.

### Inaccessible Materials

Scanned PDFs and uncaptioned video create barriers. Design for accessibility.

### Fragile Links

Sources that disappear mid-term disrupt learning. Use persistent links and backups.

### Defaulting To Commercial Without Considering OER

Habitual textbook adoption without evaluating OER misses equity and adaptation opportunities. Decide deliberately.

### Surveillance Platforms

Digital platforms that track student behavior raise privacy concerns. Prefer privacy-protective options.

### Last-Minute Materials Assembly

Throwing materials together just before term leads to cost, access, copyright, and quality problems. Plan early with librarians.

## Self-Check

- Has material cost been treated as an equity constraint, with affordable options prioritized?
- Have any OER under consideration been vetted for accuracy, currency, alignment, and accessibility?
- Is fair use assessed case by case rather than assumed, with licensed or linked sources used where fair use is weak?
- Is library-licensed content used through proper linking and reserves rather than unauthorized copying?
- Are Creative Commons license terms understood and complied with when using or creating OER?
- Are all materials accessible, with readable text, structure, alt text, captions, and contrast?
- Are links persistent and backed up so materials do not vanish mid-term?
- Was the commercial-versus-open decision made deliberately based on quality, cost, fit, and maintenance?
- Are student privacy implications of digital platforms considered and minimized?
- Was materials planning started early enough to consult librarians and resolve access issues before the term?
