# Discussion Design

A seminar discussion is not a conversation that happens spontaneously when students are seated in a circle. It is a designed learning activity with goals, preparation, questions, structures, and a trajectory. Discussions that are merely opened with "what did you think?" tend to produce superficial reactions from the most confident students while the rest stay silent. Well-designed discussions produce analysis, evidence-based argument, engagement with disagreement, and visible intellectual growth across a term.

Use this skill before designing a seminar discussion, writing discussion questions, planning preparation, structuring participation, or diagnosing why discussions stall. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing discussions that are lively but shallow, or that depend on a few voices while others disengage.

## Core Rules

### Define The Cognitive Goal Of The Discussion

A discussion should produce specific thinking, not just talk. Before designing prompts, identify what students should be able to do by the end.

Goals worth designing for include:

- interpreting a difficult text;
- evaluating competing arguments;
- applying a theory to a case;
- synthesizing across readings;
- generating a research question;
- diagnosing a methodological flaw;
- articulating a position with evidence;
- revising a prior belief in light of evidence.

A discussion without a cognitive goal drifts into opinion-sharing and rewards fluency over understanding.

### Design Preparation That Makes Discussion Possible

Discussion quality depends on preparation. Students who have not done the reading cannot discuss it; they can only perform familiarity.

Structure preparation through:

- reading questions that guide attention to key passages;
- a short written response due before class;
- annotation requirements that surface specific claims;
- a required passage selection with rationale;
- a connection to a prior concept or text.

Preparation should be specific enough that students arrive having thought about particular claims, not just having "done the reading."

### Sequence Questions From Concrete To Abstract

Open with questions grounded in the text or case before moving to abstraction. Students need a shared object to anchor discussion before they can generalize.

A useful sequence:

- observation, what does the text actually say here;
- interpretation, what does this passage mean;
- evaluation, is this argument convincing and why;
- application, how does this apply to a new case;
- synthesis, how does this connect to prior work;
- implication, what follows from accepting this view.

Jumping to evaluation before observation produces arguments unmoored from evidence. Jumping to abstraction before interpretation produces vague generalities.

### Write Questions That Require Evidence And Reasoning

Questions that can be answered with opinion or personal reaction produce shallow discussion. Design questions that require students to cite the text, offer reasoning, and consider alternatives.

Strong question forms include:

- "What evidence does the author offer for claim X, and is it sufficient?";
- "Where do these two readings disagree, and which is better supported?";
- "What would this theory predict in case Y, and does the evidence bear it out?";
- "What is the strongest objection to this position, and can it be answered?";
- "What assumption does this argument depend on, and is it warranted?".

Avoid questions answerable with "I feel" or "in my experience" unless the goal is explicitly to surface prior belief for examination.

### Plan For Disagreement Productively

Disagreement is the engine of intellectual discussion, but it requires structure to remain productive. Design for it:

- ask students to steelman the opposing view before responding;
- require evidence for challenges, not just preference;
- separate critique of the argument from critique of the person;
- use structured debate formats for high-stakes disagreements;
- model intellectual humility by acknowledging strong points on both sides.

Unstructured disagreement can become personal or shut down minority views. Over-managed disagreement becomes artificial. Aim for genuine stakes with respectful norms.

### Use Structures That Distribute Voice

Open discussion systematically advantages confident, fast-speaking, and culturally normed students. Use structures to distribute participation:

- round-robins where each student speaks once before anyone speaks twice;
- think-pair-share before whole-group discussion;
- small groups that report a synthesized position;
- written contributions collected and read anonymously;
- assigned roles such as summarizer, questioner, and connector.

The goal is not equal airtime enforced mechanically but ensuring that valuable contributions from quieter students reach the group.

### Build A Trajectory Across The Term

A single discussion is more powerful when it builds on prior ones. Design a trajectory:

- early discussions establish shared analytical norms;
- middle discussions increase cognitive demand and disagreement;
- later discussions require synthesis across the term's texts;
- final discussions ask students to articulate their own positions.

A seminar where every discussion is standalone produces no cumulative intellectual growth.

### Close With Synthesis And Open Questions

End discussions by synthesizing what was said and naming what remains open. Synthesis honors the contribution and models how intellectual work consolidates. Open questions signal that the discussion did not resolve everything and set up future work.

A useful closing move is to ask each student to state one idea that changed their thinking and one question that remains. This forces reflection and gives the instructor feedback.

### Connect Discussion To Assessment

If discussion matters, it should connect to assessment. This can be through:

- participation graded against clear criteria;
- discussion-informed reflection papers;
- essays that require engaging positions raised in discussion;
- a final synthesis that draws on the term's discussions.

Without assessment connection, students learn that discussion is optional and optimize elsewhere.

## Common Traps

### Opening With "What Did You Think?"

This invitation rewards reaction over analysis and lets prepared and unprepared students sound similar. Ask specific evidence-based questions.

### No Preparation Structure

Assuming students will arrive prepared without accountability guarantees many will not. Build in pre-class artifacts.

### Jumping To Abstraction

Moving to general principles before grounding in the text produces vague discussion. Start with observation.

### Opinion Questions

Questions answerable by personal preference produce talk without learning. Require evidence and reasoning.

### A Few Dominant Voices

Open discussion without structures lets confident students carry the session. Use distribution structures.

### Avoiding Disagreement

Facilitators who smooth over disagreement to keep peace produce shallow consensus. Structure productive disagreement.

### Standalone Discussions

Discussions that do not build across the term produce no cumulative learning. Design a trajectory.

### No Synthesis

Ending without summarizing what was thought leaves the discussion feeling inconclusive. Close with synthesis and open questions.

## Self-Check

- Does the discussion have a stated cognitive goal beyond generating talk?
- Is preparation structured so students arrive having engaged specific claims?
- Are questions sequenced from concrete observation to abstract evaluation?
- Do questions require evidence and reasoning rather than opinion or reaction?
- Is disagreement planned for and structured to remain productive rather than personal?
- Are participation structures used to distribute voice beyond confident students?
- Does the discussion build on prior discussions across the term rather than standing alone?
- Does the closing synthesize contributions and name open questions?
- Is discussion connected to assessment so students know it matters?
- Would a prepared but quiet student be able to contribute meaningfully through the structures used?
