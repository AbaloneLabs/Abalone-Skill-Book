# Collaboration Formation And Partner Selection

Most research collaborations fail not at the science but at the formation. Two researchers meet at a conference, share enthusiasm, and begin work without ever clarifying who does what, who owns what, how decisions get made, and what happens when interests diverge. The excitement of a shared idea papers over differences in pace, standards, communication style, power, and career stage. By the time the misalignment surfaces — usually around authorship, data, or a decision to publish — positions have hardened and the relationship is damaged. The failure mode is treating collaboration formation as a social event rather than a professional agreement. A few hard conversations at the start prevent the slow, expensive failures that consume months or years later.

Use this skill before committing to a collaboration, when scoping a joint project, when onboarding new partners, and when diagnosing early-stage misalignment. The goal is to prevent the agent from entering collaborations that are doomed by unspoken assumptions.

## Core Rules

### Vet Collaborators On Behavior, Not Just Brilliance

A brilliant collaborator who is unreliable, conflict-prone, or unethical will cost you more than a merely competent one who is dependable. Before committing, investigate how the person works with others. Talk to former collaborators and students. Look for patterns: missed deadlines, authorship disputes, credit hoarding, ghosting, public conflict. Past behavior in collaboration is the best predictor of future behavior. Intellectual fit matters, but behavioral fit determines whether the work actually gets done.

### Define Scope, Roles, And Deliverables Explicitly

Vague collaborations drift. At the outset, write down — even informally — what the project is, what each party will contribute, what the deliverables are, and on what timeline. This is not bureaucratic overhead; it is the artifact you will return to when memories diverge. A simple shared document capturing scope and roles prevents most early-stage conflicts.

Questions to settle at formation:

- What is the scientific question and the intended output (paper, dataset, grant, tool)?
- Who is responsible for which tasks, data, and analyses?
- What is the expected timeline and the decision points along the way?
- Who leads, and how are disagreements resolved?
- What resources (funding, equipment, personnel) does each party bring?

### Set Authorship And Credit Expectations Early

Authorship is the most common source of collaboration conflict, and it is the easiest to prevent. Discuss authorship at the start, not at submission. Agree on:

- the expected author list and order, or the criteria that will determine it;
- how contribution will be tracked and assessed;
- how new contributors (students, postdocs) will be added and credited;
- who holds corresponding and senior authorship and why.

Authorship plans can evolve as contributions evolve, but the *criteria* should be agreed early. Surprising a collaborator with an authorship decision at submission is a failure of formation.

### Align On Standards, Pace, And Publication Norms

Collaborators often have different standards for what is publishable, how fast to move, and how much revision is enough. A senior theorist and a junior experimentalist may have incompatible instincts about when a result is ready. Surface these differences early:

- What standard of evidence or rigor is required to publish?
- How fast do we expect to move, and what are the external deadlines?
- How do we handle preprints, embargoes, and simultaneous submission norms?
- What is our policy on negative results and replication?

### Clarify Data Ownership, Sharing, And Reuse

Data is the second most common source of conflict. Before work begins, agree on who owns the data, where it lives, who can access it, and what happens to it when the collaboration ends. This is especially fraught when institutions, funders, or industry partners have claims. Ambiguity here produces disputes that can halt publication or trigger institutional intervention.

### Plan For The Collaboration's End

Collaborations end — through completion, drift, conflict, or career moves. Discuss at the start what happens if someone leaves the project, if interests diverge, or if the work stalls. Who keeps the data? Can each party publish their own piece? How are remaining students and postdocs supported? A brief end-of-collaboration plan prevents the messy, litigious unwinding that destroys relationships.

### Match Collaboration Structure To Power Asymmetries

Collaborations between senior and junior scholars, or between well-resourced and under-resourced groups, carry power asymmetries that shape every decision. The senior or better-resourced party must explicitly protect the junior party's credit, voice, and career interests. Defaulting to "we'll figure it out" lets structural power quietly determine outcomes.

## Common Traps

- **Collaborating on enthusiasm without structure.** The conference-hall collaboration that never gets scoped is the most common failure. Excitement is not a plan.
- **Ignoring behavioral red flags because the science is exciting.** A collaborator known for disputes will bring those disputes to your project. Investigate before committing.
- **Deferring authorship discussions.** "We'll see who contributes what" sounds reasonable but reliably produces conflict at submission, when stakes are highest and goodwill is lowest.
- **Assuming shared standards.** "Publishable" means different things to different people. A collaborator's lower bar can put your name on work you wouldn't stand behind.
- **Leaving data ownership implicit.** When the collaboration frays, ambiguous data ownership becomes the battlefield. Clarify before data exists.
- **Letting power asymmetries silently determine outcomes.** Junior collaborators end up doing the work and missing the credit because no one named the asymmetry.
- **No exit plan.** Collaborations that end badly without a plan produce data disputes, stranded students, and severed relationships.
- **Over-collaborating.** Saying yes to too many collaborations fragments attention and ensures none get the sustained effort they need.

## Self-Check

- Have I investigated potential collaborators' track record with others, not just their intellectual fit?
- Have I documented the project scope, roles, deliverables, and timeline in a shared artifact we both (all) agree on?
- Did we agree on authorship criteria and expectations at the start, rather than deferring to submission time?
- Have we aligned on standards of rigor, pace, and publication norms, including preprints and negative results?
- Is data ownership, storage, access, and post-collaboration reuse explicitly agreed?
- Have we discussed what happens if the collaboration ends — through completion, conflict, or departure?
- Where power or resource asymmetries exist, have I explicitly protected the junior or less-resourced party's credit and voice?
- Am I entering this collaboration at a sustainable load, or am I overcommitting and setting up a shallow or failed partnership?
