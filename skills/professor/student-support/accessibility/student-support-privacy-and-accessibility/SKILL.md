# Student Support Privacy And Accessibility

Professors often become the first person to notice academic struggle, disability barriers, privacy risks, distress, harassment, food or housing insecurity, or unsafe behavior. Supporting students does not mean solving every problem personally. It means responding within the professor's role, protecting student dignity and records, using institutional channels, and making the course accessible enough that students can participate.

Use this skill before drafting student support guidance, responding to accommodation requests, discussing grades, designing office hour policies, using student data, handling concerning behavior, communicating with parents or third parties, or deciding when to refer a student to campus services. The goal is to prevent the agent from giving helpful-sounding advice that violates privacy, ignores access obligations, or exceeds the professor's role.

## Core Rules

### Know The Professor's Role And Limits

Professors provide academic guidance and connect students to support. They are not usually disability officers, therapists, legal advisers, immigration advisers, financial aid officers, or investigators.

Clarify whether the issue is:

- academic question;
- course policy question;
- grade concern;
- accommodation request;
- disability access issue;
- mental health concern;
- harassment or discrimination report;
- threat or safety concern;
- financial or housing need;
- immigration or visa issue;
- research supervision issue;
- conduct issue.

Respond within role and refer to the correct campus office when needed. Do not promise confidentiality if institutional reporting duties may apply.

### Protect Student Education Records

Student grades, academic performance, accommodations, advising notes, disciplinary records, and other maintained records may be protected by FERPA or local equivalents. In U.S. postsecondary education, students are generally "eligible students" and privacy rights transfer to them.

Check before sharing:

- whether the information is from an education record;
- whether the recipient has legitimate educational interest;
- whether written consent exists;
- whether an exception applies;
- whether disclosure must be recorded;
- whether the communication channel is approved;
- whether the information can be minimized.

Do not discuss grades or academic standing with parents, employers, classmates, or outside parties without proper authorization or institutional process.

### Use Secure Grade And Feedback Communication

Grade communication should happen through approved systems. Avoid channels that expose records.

Avoid:

- posting grades publicly by name or identifiable ID;
- returning work where students see others' grades;
- discussing grades in public spaces;
- sending detailed grades through unsecured channels against policy;
- using personal email for sensitive records;
- sharing student work without permission when identifiable.

When using examples, anonymize and remove identifying details unless permission and policy allow.

### Handle Accommodations Through Institutional Process

Postsecondary accommodations generally involve student notice and institutional approval. Professors should implement approved accommodations while coordinating with the disability office.

Check:

- accommodation letter or official notice;
- effective date;
- exam accommodations;
- note-taking or recording;
- accessible materials;
- deadlines or attendance adjustments;
- lab or field access;
- technology needs;
- confidentiality of disability information;
- fundamental alteration concerns;
- who to contact for questions.

Do not ask students to disclose diagnosis beyond what the official process requires. Do not deny approved accommodations informally. If an accommodation appears to fundamentally alter the course, consult the disability office rather than deciding alone.

### Design Access Into The Course

Accessibility should not depend only on individual accommodations. Professors can reduce barriers through course design.

Use:

- accessible documents;
- captions or transcripts;
- structured LMS organization;
- readable slides;
- clear deadlines;
- multiple participation paths;
- predictable assignment formats;
- early posting of materials;
- flexible office hours;
- inclusive examples;
- accessible lab or field instructions;
- technology alternatives.

Good design reduces last-minute crisis and helps many students, including those without formal accommodations.

### Support Students Without Overexposure

When a student is struggling, respond privately, concretely, and with boundaries.

Use:

- specific observation;
- academic impact;
- invitation to meet;
- referral to resources;
- clear next academic step;
- documentation of agreed plan;
- follow-up date.

Avoid asking for unnecessary personal details. A student can receive flexibility or referral without explaining trauma in detail if policy permits. Use institutional guidance for extensions, incompletes, withdrawals, and emergency support.

### Respond To Concerning Behavior Through Policy

Concerning behavior may involve self-harm, threats, stalking, harassment, discrimination, substance crisis, medical emergency, or disruptive conduct. Professors should not improvise.

Know:

- emergency contact process;
- campus police or security process;
- behavioral intervention team;
- counseling referral;
- Title IX or civil rights reporting;
- academic conduct process;
- student conduct process;
- mandatory reporting duties;
- classroom disruption policy;
- documentation expectations.

When there is immediate danger, follow emergency procedures. When the concern is serious but not immediate, consult the appropriate campus office.

### Maintain Fairness And Consistency

Student support should be compassionate and fair. Inconsistent hidden exceptions can create inequity and grade disputes.

Document:

- extension requests;
- approved exceptions;
- makeup work;
- incomplete agreements;
- attendance flexibility;
- grade changes;
- accommodation implementation;
- referral communications.

Use the syllabus and institutional policy, but allow documented flexibility when policy permits. Apply similar cases similarly while considering approved accommodations and individual circumstances.

### Respect Boundaries In Office Hours And Communication

Clear boundaries help students know how to get help and protect the professor from unsustainable support patterns.

Define:

- office hour schedule;
- appointment process;
- email response time;
- emergency limits;
- feedback turnaround;
- discussion topics appropriate for professor;
- referral topics;
- online meeting privacy expectations;
- recording rules.

Professors can be supportive without being constantly available.

## Common Traps

### Trying To Diagnose Or Counsel

Professors can listen and refer, but should not diagnose disability, mental health, trauma, or medical conditions unless specifically qualified and acting in that role.

### Sharing Grades With Parents

Postsecondary students generally control their education records under FERPA unless a valid exception or consent applies. Check policy before disclosure.

### Asking For Medical Details Publicly

Accommodation and health information should be handled privately through official channels.

### Treating Accessibility As Only The Disability Office's Job

The disability office coordinates accommodations, but professors design materials, assessments, communication, and participation conditions.

### Making Private Exceptions Without Records

Unrecorded flexibility can create unfairness, memory disputes, and grade appeal risk.

### Ignoring Safety Signals

Threats, self-harm statements, harassment, or abuse disclosures require institutional procedures. Do not handle them alone.

### Overpromising Confidentiality

Professors may have reporting obligations. Be honest about limits before students disclose sensitive information when possible.

## Self-Check

- Is the issue academic, accommodation-related, privacy-related, safety-related, conduct-related, or outside the professor's role?
- Are FERPA or local student-record privacy rules considered before sharing information?
- Are grades and feedback communicated through approved private channels?
- Are approved accommodations implemented through institutional process?
- Are disability details kept confidential and limited to need-to-know?
- Are course materials and participation routes designed for access before individual requests arise?
- Are struggling students contacted privately with concrete academic options and appropriate referrals?
- Are safety, harassment, discrimination, self-harm, threats, and conduct concerns escalated through official channels?
- Are exceptions, extensions, makeup work, and grade changes documented consistently?
- Are office hour, email, emergency, and referral boundaries clear?
