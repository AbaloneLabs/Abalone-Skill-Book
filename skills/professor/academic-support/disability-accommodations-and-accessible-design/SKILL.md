# Disability Accommodations And Accessible Design

Disabled students are entitled to accommodations, and providing them is both a legal obligation and a matter of educational justice. But there is a gap between technical compliance with accommodations and genuine access. A professor who accepts an accommodation letter but designs a course no disabled student can fully participate in, or who provides the lettered accommodation while maintaining a hostile climate around disability, has met the letter of the law while failing the spirit. The failure mode is treating accommodations as a bureaucratic transaction — receive the letter, apply the modification, move on — rather than as part of designing for genuine access. Accessible design upstream reduces the need for individual accommodations downstream and serves disabled students whether or not they have requested accommodations, which matters because many disabled students do not disclose. The professor's job is both to implement accommodations faithfully and to design accessibly from the start.

Use this skill when implementing accommodations, when designing courses and materials, when working with disability services, and when diagnosing why a course is compliant yet inaccessible. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating accommodations as a checkbox while leaving the actual learning environment inaccessible.

## Core Rules

### Implement Accommodations Promptly And Confidentially

When you receive an accommodation letter from the disability services office, implement it promptly, consistently, and confidentially. The letter tells you what accommodations the student is entitled to, not why (the diagnosis is confidential and not your business). Do not require the student to re-disclose or justify, do not negotiate the accommodations, and do not single the student out in class. If you have questions about implementation, consult the disability services office, not the student's diagnosis.

### Design Accessibly From The Start

Many accommodations exist because courses are designed inaccessibly by default. Designing accessibly upstream — readable documents, captioned videos, multiple means of engagement, flexible assessment where appropriate, clear structure and instructions — reduces the need for individual accommodations and serves students who haven't disclosed. Universal Design for Learning offers a useful framework: provide multiple means of representation, engagement, and expression. Accessible design is not lowering standards; it is removing unnecessary barriers so that capable disabled students can demonstrate their learning.

### Provide Materials In Accessible Formats

Inaccessible materials are a common, preventable barrier. Use accessible document practices: properly structured headings, alt text for images, sufficient color contrast, readable fonts and sizes, and captions or transcripts for audio and video. Avoid scanned PDFs that are images rather than text, which screen readers cannot parse. Provide materials early so that students who need to convert formats (e.g., to braille or audio) can do so in time to use them. If you are unsure whether your materials are accessible, consult your institution's accessibility services.

### Make Accommodation Implementation Seamless, Not Stigmatizing

How you implement accommodations matters as much as whether. Extended test time should be arranged without the student having to negotiate or feel conspicuous; a note-taker should be arranged without announcing the recipient; a quiet testing space should be genuinely quiet. Avoid arrangements that single out or inconvenience the disabled student. The goal is that using an accommodation is unremarkable, not that it is invisible or burdensome.

### Treat Flexibility Accommodations Thoughtfully

Some accommodations involve flexibility — extended deadlines, modified attendance policies — that interact with course structure. Work with the disability services office to understand the intent and to implement it in a way that serves the student without undermining the course's essential requirements. Distinguish essential requirements (which cannot be modified without changing the course) from conventional ones (which can). When a flexibility accommodation is frequent, it may signal that the course design itself is creating barriers worth examining.

### Do Not Require Disclosure To Get Basic Access

Many disabled students do not request accommodations — because of stigma, because they don't know they qualify, or because the process is burdensome. Designing accessibly by default serves these students too. Do not assume that the absence of accommodation letters means no disabled students are present; assume they are present and design accordingly.

### Address Climate Around Disability

Climate matters as much as structure. Avoid language that frames disability as deficit or accommodation as unfair advantage ("if I give you extra time, I have to give everyone extra time"). Do not disclose a student's disability status to peers. Address disability respectfully in course content, including disabled scholars and perspectives where relevant. A course that is structurally accessible but climatically hostile still fails disabled students.

### Consult Disability Services As A Partner

The disability services office is your partner, not just a paperwork router. Consult them when you are unsure how to implement an accommodation, when a student's needs seem to exceed the letter, when you are designing a new course, or when you want to assess your materials' accessibility. They have expertise you likely don't, and collaboration produces better access than either party working alone.

## Common Traps

- **Treating the accommodation letter as a transaction.** Implementing the modification without considering whether the course design itself is creating barriers.
- **Requiring re-disclosure or justification.** Asking the student to explain their diagnosis or repeatedly request their accommodations is stigmatizing and often illegal.
- **Inaccessible default design.** Scanned image PDFs, uncaptioned video, low-contrast slides, and rigid structures create barriers that accommodations then have to work around.
- **Stigmatizing implementation.** Arranging accommodations in ways that single out or inconvenience the disabled student.
- **Confusing flexibility with lowered standards.** Flexibility accommodations remove barriers; they don't change what students must learn. Distinguish essential from conventional requirements.
- **Assuming no disabled students if no letters arrive.** Many disabled students don't disclose. Design accessibly by default to serve them.
- **Hostile climate language.** Framing accommodations as unfair advantage erodes belonging and can violate policy.
- **Going it alone.** Failing to consult disability services when unsure leads to well-intentioned but noncompliant or ineffective implementation.

## Self-Check

- When I receive an accommodation letter, do I implement it promptly, consistently, and confidentially, without requiring re-disclosure or justification?
- Have I designed the course accessibly from the start — readable documents, captions, multiple means of engagement, clear structure — rather than relying on accommodations to fix inaccessible design?
- Are my materials in accessible formats — structured headings, alt text, sufficient contrast, text-based rather than image PDFs — and provided early enough for format conversion?
- Do I implement accommodations in ways that are seamless and unremarkable rather than stigmatizing or conspicuous?
- For flexibility accommodations, have I distinguished essential from conventional requirements and worked with disability services on implementation?
- Am I designing accessibly by default, on the assumption that disabled students are present whether or not I've received letters?
- Does my climate around disability — in language, content, and interactions — treat disabled students with respect rather than framing them as deficits or accommodations as unfair advantage?
- Do I consult disability services as a partner when unsure, rather than improvising implementation?
