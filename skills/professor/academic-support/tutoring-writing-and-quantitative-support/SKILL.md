# Tutoring, Writing, And Quantitative Support

Academic support centers — writing centers, math and quantitative support labs, subject tutoring, supplemental instruction — exist to help students succeed, yet they are chronically underused by the students who need them most. The failure mode is twofold: professors either ignore these services and try to provide all support themselves (and burn out while reaching few students), or they make perfunctory referrals ("visit the writing center") that students never act on. Effective use of academic support requires knowing what each service actually offers, designing courses that integrate with them, making referrals that convert, and collaborating with support staff rather than treating them as a separate, optional layer. When professors and support services work together, students get coherent help; when they don't, students fall through the gaps between them.

Use this skill when designing assignments and referral practices, when integrating support services into a course, and when diagnosing why students aren't using available support. The goal is to prevent the agent from either shouldering support alone or making referrals that don't convert.

## Core Rules

### Know What Each Support Service Actually Does

You cannot refer well to services you don't understand. Learn what your institution's writing center, quantitative support, subject tutoring, and supplemental instruction actually offer: their philosophy, what they will and won't do, their hours and modes (drop-in, appointment, online), and their capacity constraints. A writing center that coaches revision is different from one that proofreads; a math lab that works problems alongside students is different from one that re-teaches lectures. Match referrals to what the service actually provides.

### Make Referrals Specific And Warm

"Visit the writing center" rarely converts. A referral that converts names what the student should bring, what to ask for, when to go, and why it will help: "Bring your draft to the writing center and ask them to work with you on your thesis and organization; they're most useful before you've polished the sentences. They take appointments Tuesday and drop-ins Thursday afternoon. Would you like me to show you how to book?" Specificity and warmth turn a perfunctory referral into action.

### Design Assignments That Integrate With Support

The best use of support services is built into the course design, not bolted on. Structure assignments so that support is a natural part of the workflow: drafts that go to the writing center before revision, problem sets that reference the math lab, milestones that align with supplemental instruction sessions. When support is integrated, using it is normal rather than remedial, and uptake rises dramatically.

### Normalize And De-Stigmatize Support Use

Students avoid support because seeking it feels like an admission of inadequacy. Counter this actively: mention that you use editors and collaborators, frame support as what successful students do, and avoid reserving referrals for struggling students. When all students are encouraged to use the writing center or attend supplemental instruction, the students who most need it are no longer singled out and are more likely to go.

### Collaborate With Support Staff, Don't Just Refer To Them

Support center staff are professionals with expertise about how students learn. Collaborate with them: share your assignment prompts so they can prepare, invite them to visit your class, consult them about students who are struggling, and seek their input on assignment design. A professor who treats support staff as partners gets better support for their students than one who treats them as a drop-off service.

### Use Supplemental Instruction And Peer Mentoring Strategically

Supplemental instruction — peer-led sessions attached to historically difficult courses — and peer mentoring are among the most evidence-supported interventions for student success, especially in gateway STEM courses. If your institution offers SI, integrate it: build it into the syllabus, attend occasionally to signal its value, and align session timing with course milestones. Peer support often reaches students whom faculty office hours do not.

### Provide Targeted Help For Quantitative And Writing-Intensive Courses

Writing-intensive and quantitative courses have distinctive support needs. For writing-intensive courses, build in drafting, peer review, and writing-center integration; teach disciplinary writing conventions explicitly rather than assuming them. For quantitative courses, provide worked examples, practice problems with solutions, and integration with the math or quantitative support lab; normalize getting stuck and seeking help as part of doing quantitative work. Generic "study hard" advice fails in these contexts.

### Track Whether Referrals Convert

A referral that doesn't convert is a referral that didn't happen. Where possible, follow up: ask the student whether they went, what was helpful, and what's still unclear. Some institutions allow support centers to confirm attendance (with student consent). Tracking conversion lets you adjust your referral approach and identify students who need more direct intervention.

## Common Traps

- **Perfunctory referrals.** "Visit the writing center" without specifics rarely converts. Make referrals specific and warm.
- **Shouldering all support yourself.** Professors who try to tutor every struggling student burn out and reach few. Use the services that exist.
- **Misunderstanding what services do.** Referring a student for proofreading to a center that coaches revision frustrates everyone. Learn what each service offers.
- **Bolting support on rather than integrating it.** Support offered as a remedial afterthought is stigmatized and underused; support built into the workflow is normal and effective.
- **Reserving referrals for struggling students.** This marks support as remedial and discourages the students who need it. Normalize use for all.
- **Treating support staff as a drop-off service.** Failing to collaborate with support professionals misses their expertise and produces incoherent help.
- **Ignoring SI and peer mentoring.** These are among the most effective interventions and are especially good at reaching students who avoid faculty office hours.
- **Not following up.** A referral without follow-up leaves you not knowing whether help was received, and leaves the student without the nudge that often makes the difference.

## Self-Check

- Do I know what each support service — writing center, quantitative lab, tutoring, SI — actually offers, including its philosophy, hours, and capacity?
- Are my referrals specific and warm — naming what to bring, what to ask, when to go, and why — rather than perfunctory?
- Have I designed assignments so that support is integrated into the workflow rather than bolted on as remedial?
- Have I normalized support use for all students, so that seeking help signals success rather than inadequacy?
- Am I collaborating with support staff — sharing prompts, consulting on struggling students, seeking input on design — rather than treating them as a drop-off?
- Have I integrated supplemental instruction and peer mentoring where available, and aligned them with course milestones?
- For writing-intensive and quantitative courses, have I provided the targeted structures — drafting, worked examples, conventions teaching — that these contexts require?
- Do I follow up on referrals to confirm they converted and to identify students who need more direct help?
