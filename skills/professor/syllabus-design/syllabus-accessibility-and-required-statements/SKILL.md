# Syllabus Accessibility And Required Statements

A syllabus is simultaneously a pedagogical document, a communication to students, and a legal and institutional instrument. Most attention goes to the pedagogy, but the syllabus also carries obligations that, if neglected, create real harm and real risk: students with disabilities cannot access materials that are not designed accessibly, and institutions face legal and accreditation exposure when required statements are missing, outdated, or in conflict with policy. Professors often treat accessibility as something to handle when an accommodation letter arrives and required statements as boilerplate to copy without reading, then discover that scanned PDFs delayed accommodations, that a missing Title IX statement created liability, or that a self-written integrity policy conflicted with institutional rules. The discipline of syllabus accessibility and compliance is the discipline of building the document so that every student can use it and every required obligation is met, before problems arise.

Use this skill before finalizing a syllabus, ensuring required statements are present and current, making the syllabus document accessible, handling institutional policy language, or auditing a syllabus for compliance and usability. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a syllabus that is pedagogically strong but inaccessible to some students or non-compliant with institutional and legal requirements. Note that this skill addresses document-level accessibility and the institutional compliance inventory, whereas course-policy design is addressed separately.

## Core Rules

### Treat Accessibility As A Default, Not An Accommodation

Accessibility is not a favor granted when a student requests it; it is a baseline obligation that benefits many students beyond those with documented accommodations. Build it in from the start:

- provide the syllabus in an accessible, screen-reader-friendly format, not a scanned image;
- use real text with proper heading structure rather than images of text;
- ensure readable fonts, sufficient size, and strong contrast;
- use descriptive link text rather than bare URLs or "click here";
- provide text alternatives for any non-text content in the syllabus.

Accessible-by-default design serves students with disabilities, students using mobile devices, students reading in a second language, and students reviewing materials in poor conditions. Retrofitting after a request is slower, more stressful, and legally riskier.

### Use Proper Document Structure For Navigability

A well-structured document is navigable by assistive technology and by all readers. Practices:

- use built-in heading styles, Heading 1, Heading 2, rather than bold text;
- use true lists rather than typed characters that look like lists;
- use real tables with header rows for tabular information, not tabs or spaces;
- maintain a logical reading order;
- add a document title and language.

Structural accessibility lets screen-reader users navigate by heading and lets all students find what they need quickly. Unstructured documents are inaccessible regardless of their content.

### Maintain The Complete Inventory Of Required Statements

Institutions require specific statements on every syllabus, and the inventory is larger and more specific than many professors assume. Identify and include the current versions of:

- disability and accommodation statements, with the correct office and process;
- academic integrity statements, aligned with institutional policy;
- Title IX or nondiscrimination statements;
- harassment and discrimination reporting information;
- mandatory reporting disclosures, where the instructor is a mandatory reporter;
- religious observance accommodation;
- student privacy and FERPA notices;
- emergency procedures and campus safety information;
- mental health and student support resources;
- any program- or institution-specific required statements.

Do not rely on memory or last year's version. Obtain the current required-statement inventory from the institution, because these statements change and using outdated language creates compliance gaps.

### Use Institutional Language For Required Statements Rather Than Rewriting

Required statements exist for legal and policy reasons, and they are often crafted to meet specific obligations. Practices:

- use the institution's approved language verbatim where required;
- avoid paraphrasing statements in ways that weaken or contradict policy;
- supplement, do not replace, required language with course-specific context where helpful;
- link to the full policy rather than summarizing it incompletely.

A professor who rewrites the accommodation statement to sound friendlier may inadvertently create language that does not meet the institution's legal obligations. Use approved language and add context around it.

### Check That Course-Specific Policies Do Not Conflict With Institutional Policy

Professors may set course policies, but those policies cannot override institutional rules. Audit your policies against:

- attendance policies that conflict with accommodation or religious observance rules;
- late-work or makeup policies that conflict with excused-absence policy;
- technology policies that conflict with accommodation requirements;
- integrity policies that conflict with the institutional process;
- communication policies that conflict with student privacy rules.

A course policy that conflicts with institutional policy is unenforceable and creates disputes. Align course policies with, or stay silent and defer to, institutional rules.

### Ensure Required Statements Are Accurate And Current

Required statements reference specific offices, processes, and contact information that change over time. Verify:

- the correct name and contact information for disability services;
- the current reporting process for discrimination or harassment;
- the current integrity process and consequences;
- the current emergency notification system and procedures;
- links that still work and point to current pages.

Outdated contact information or broken links in required statements defeat their purpose. Verify currency each term.

### Make Required Statements Findable, Not Buried

Required statements exist so students can find and use them. Practices:

- place required statements in consistent, findable locations;
- do not bury them in dense paragraphs where they are missed;
- use clear headings for each category of required information;
- consider a dedicated section or appendix for institutional statements.

A statement a student cannot find does not fulfill its obligation, even if it is technically present.

### Address Digital And Format Accessibility

The syllabus may be distributed in multiple formats, and each must be accessible. Consider:

- the LMS page or module where the syllabus lives;
- any PDF version, which must be tagged and text-based, not scanned;
- any Word or document version, which must use styles and structure;
- any printed version, which must have sufficient contrast and font size;
- any linked materials, which must also be accessible.

An accessible source document exported to an inaccessible format loses the accessibility. Check every format students will actually use.

### Coordinate With Disability Services On Implementation

Accessibility design and accommodation implementation involve the disability services office. Coordinate:

- use the institution's approved accommodation statement and process;
- respect the role of disability services in determining accommodations;
- implement approved accommodations promptly and confidentially;
- do not ask students to disclose disability details beyond what they choose;
- consult disability services when design questions arise.

The professor controls many design barriers but is not the sole actor. Coordinate with the office that holds the institutional role.

### Document Compliance For Your Own Protection

Maintain evidence that required statements were included and current. Practices:

- keep the version of the syllabus distributed to students;
- note the source and date of each required statement;
- update from the institutional source each term;
- retain records per institutional policy.

Documentation protects the professor and the institution if a compliance question arises, and it makes term-to-term updating efficient.

### Audit The Syllabus Before Each Offering

Before distributing the syllabus each term, run a compliance and accessibility audit:

- are all required statements present, current, and accurate;
- do course policies conflict with institutional rules;
- is the document structurally accessible, with headings, lists, and real text;
- are all formats students will use accessible;
- are links and contact information current;
- has the institutional required-statement inventory been checked for changes.

A short pre-term audit catches problems that mid-term crises expose. Make it a habit.

## Common Traps

### Accessibility As Afterthought

Treating accessibility as something to handle when a request arrives delays accommodations and creates risk. Build it in by default.

### Unstructured Documents

Bold text instead of headings and typed characters instead of lists make documents unnavigable for assistive technology. Use real structure.

### Incomplete Required-Statement Inventory

Relying on memory or old versions misses required statements that have been added or changed. Obtain the current inventory.

### Rewriting Required Language

Paraphrasing approved statements to sound friendlier can weaken legal compliance. Use institutional language.

### Policies Conflicting With Institutional Rules

Course policies that override accommodation, religious observance, or integrity process create unenforceable disputes. Align with institutional rules.

### Outdated Contact Information

Broken links and wrong office names in required statements defeat their purpose. Verify currency each term.

### Buried Statements

Required statements hidden in dense paragraphs are effectively absent for the students who need them. Make them findable.

### Format Accessibility Ignored

An accessible source exported to an inaccessible PDF loses the benefit. Check every format.

### Acting Outside The Accommodation Process

Setting accommodations unilaterally rather than coordinating with disability services creates inconsistency. Coordinate.

### No Compliance Audit

Distributing without auditing each term allows drift and gaps. Audit before each offering.

## Self-Check

- Is the syllabus accessible by default, with real text, proper structure, and strong contrast, rather than retrofitted on request?
- Does the document use built-in headings, true lists, and real tables so it is navigable by assistive technology and all readers?
- Is the complete current inventory of required institutional statements present, obtained from the official source?
- Are required statements in the institution's approved language rather than paraphrased in ways that weaken compliance?
- Do course-specific policies avoid conflicting with institutional rules on accommodation, religious observance, integrity, and privacy?
- Are all required statements accurate and current, with working links and correct contact information?
- Are required statements placed in findable locations with clear headings rather than buried?
- Is every format students will use, LMS, PDF, Word, print, accessible?
- Is implementation coordinated with disability services rather than handled unilaterally?
- Is compliance documented and audited before each offering to catch drift and gaps?
