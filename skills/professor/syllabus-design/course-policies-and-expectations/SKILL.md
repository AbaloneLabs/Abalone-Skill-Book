# Course Policies And Expectations

Course policies set the rules of engagement for a class. They govern attendance, late work, integrity, technology, communication, accommodation, and collaboration. Well-written policies protect both students and instructors, reduce ambiguity, and create a predictable environment where students can focus on learning. Poorly written policies create disputes, appear arbitrary, conflict with institutional rules, or disadvantage students who cannot decode unstated norms.

Use this skill before drafting or revising course policies, writing required syllabus statements, setting participation and collaboration rules, or reviewing a syllabus for fairness and compliance. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing policies that are punitive, vague, inconsistent with institutional rules, or inaccessible to students unfamiliar with academic conventions.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Instructor Discretion From Institutional Mandate

Some policies are set by the institution and cannot be overridden by an instructor. Others are legitimately within instructor discretion. Confusing the two creates legal and procedural risk.

Typically institutionally mandated:

- disability accommodation process and approved accommodations;
- academic integrity definitions and adjudication procedures;
- Title IX and nondiscrimination reporting obligations;
- religious observance accommodations;
- grade appeal and grievance procedures;
- student privacy under FERPA or local equivalent;
- mandatory reporting duties.

Typically within instructor discretion:

- attendance expectations for non-mandated absences;
- late work penalties and deadlines;
- participation grading structure;
- technology use in class;
- collaboration rules on specific assignments;
- communication norms and response times.

When a topic is institutionally governed, use the institution's required language or link to the official policy rather than paraphrasing it into something weaker or contradictory.

### Write Policies That Are Specific And Enforceable

A policy that cannot be applied consistently will be applied inconsistently, which students perceive as unfair. Each policy should specify the condition, the consequence, and any exception process.

For late work, specify:

- what counts as late;
- the penalty structure per day or per incident;
- whether any grace period exists;
- how to request an extension and the criteria;
- whether revision is possible;
- how the policy applies to group work.

For attendance, specify:

- what counts as attendance for your class;
- how many absences are permitted before consequence;
- whether absences are excused and how;
- how students recover material after absence;
- how attendance connects to participation or grading.

Avoid policies that depend on instructor judgment at every instance, because that invites bias and inconsistency.

### Make Academic Integrity Expectations Concrete

Generic integrity statements ("do not plagiarize") do not help students who do not know what plagiarism looks like in your discipline. Specify the integrity expectations that actually arise in your course.

Address:

- citation format and what must be cited;
- collaboration boundaries on problem sets, labs, code, and writing;
- whether and how AI tools may be used, with disclosure requirements;
- reuse of prior work and self-plagiarism;
- data fabrication and falsification in lab or research work;
- exam conduct and permitted materials;
- group work individual contribution expectations.

Connect violations to the institutional adjudication process rather than inventing private penalties. Instructors usually cannot unilaterally fail a student for integrity violations without process.

### Address AI Tools Explicitly

Vague AI policies create the most disputes in current courses. State clearly whether AI tools are prohibited, permitted with disclosure, or required, and for which assignments.

Specify:

- which tools count as AI for your policy;
- permitted uses such as brainstorming, grammar checking, code debugging, or drafting;
- prohibited uses such as generating submitted text or solving graded problems;
- disclosure format when AI is used;
- how AI use interacts with citation and integrity policy;
- consequences that route through institutional process.

A blanket ban that is unenforceable teaches students to hide use rather than learn boundaries. A permission without disclosure invites overreliance. Choose deliberately and explain the rationale.

### Design Accommodation-Friendly Defaults

Accommodations are processed through disability services, but instructors control many design choices that reduce the need for last-minute adjustments.

Build in:

- captioned media and accessible documents from the start;
- flexible timing where pedagogically appropriate;
- multiple participation channels;
- clear advance notice of due dates;
- materials available early enough for accommodation technology to process;
- a stated process for students to share accommodation letters.

Do not promise accommodations you cannot deliver or that bypass the official process. Route accommodation requests to the disability services office.

### Set Communication Norms Realistically

Students benefit from knowing how and when to reach you, but policies should not create 24/7 availability expectations.

Specify:

- preferred channels (email, LMS, office hours);
- expected response time;
- office hour format and whether appointments are needed;
- what kinds of questions belong on a public forum versus private message;
- emergency and absence notification expectations.

Avoid policies that require students to monitor multiple channels or that penalize them for not knowing an unstated preference.

### Make Policies Visible And Human

Policies buried in a dense syllabus are effectively invisible. Surface the most important ones in a policies summary, restate them at key moments, and reference them when they apply.

Frame policies supportively where possible. "Late work loses 10 percent per day" can also note that extensions are available for documented circumstances. This preserves standards while signaling that the instructor wants students to succeed.

### Review For Equity And Access

Policies can inadvertently disadvantage particular students. Review for:

- students with disabilities who need flexibility;
- working students with inflexible job schedules;
- commuter students facing transit disruption;
- student-athletes with travel obligations;
- students with caregiving responsibilities;
- international students navigating visa or time-zone issues;
- first-generation students unfamiliar with academic norms.

Equity does not mean eliminating standards. It means designing policies whose consequences fall on learning, not on circumstance.

## Common Traps

### Writing A Punitive Syllabus

A syllabus that reads as a list of prohibitions signals distrust and undermines engagement. Policies should be clear but not hostile.

### Conflicting With Institutional Policy

Inventing an integrity penalty or attendance rule that contradicts institutional procedure creates legal exposure and undermines the process.

### Vague AI Rules

"Use AI responsibly" without definition guarantees disputes. Specify permitted and prohibited uses per assignment.

### Penalizing Circumstance Over Learning

Strict absence policies that punish illness, caregiving, or transit failure without an exception path penalize students for things outside their control.

### Inconsistent Enforcement

Policies applied differently to different students appear biased and erode trust. Write policies you can apply uniformly.

### Assuming Students Know Norms

First-generation, transfer, and international students may not know office hour etiquette, email norms, or citation expectations. Make implicit rules explicit.

### Ignoring Privacy In Communication

Policies that require students to disclose personal circumstances to the whole class, or that share student information improperly, violate privacy and trust.

## Self-Check

- Are institutionally mandated policies reproduced or linked accurately rather than paraphrased into weaker versions?
- Does each discretionary policy specify condition, consequence, and exception process clearly enough to apply consistently?
- Are academic integrity expectations concrete for the discipline, including citation, collaboration, AI use, and data integrity?
- Is the AI tool policy explicit about permitted uses, prohibited uses, disclosure, and connection to integrity process?
- Are accommodation-friendly defaults built into course design rather than treated as last-minute exceptions?
- Do communication norms set realistic response times without implying 24/7 availability?
- Are the most important policies surfaced visibly rather than buried in syllabus text?
- Have policies been reviewed for disparate impact on students with disabilities, work, caregiving, commute, or first-generation status?
- Could a student reading the syllabus predict how a late submission, absence, or integrity concern would be handled?
- Do policies preserve standards while leaving a fair path for students facing genuine circumstances?
