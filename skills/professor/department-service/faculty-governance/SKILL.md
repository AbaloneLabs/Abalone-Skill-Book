# Faculty Governance

Faculty governance is the system through which faculty share authority over academic matters with administration. It is governed by bylaws, policies, traditions, and law, and it determines who decides what about curriculum, personnel, standards, and the academic enterprise. Faculty often experience governance as opaque, slow, or symbolic, and disengage, which cedes authority to administration and erodes the shared governance that protects academic quality and faculty rights. Effective participation requires understanding the governance structure, engaging substantively, respecting process and confidentiality, and recognizing that governance decisions shape the institution and the profession for years.

Use this skill before participating in governance, interpreting bylaws or policy, engaging in a department or university decision, or handling a confidential personnel or governance matter. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing governance participation that is uninformed, procedurally careless, or that violates confidentiality or process in ways that damage the institution and the individual.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Governance Structure And Your Role In It

Effective participation requires understanding the system. Know:

- the faculty governance bodies and their authority, department, college, university senate;
- which decisions are faculty authority, which are administrative, which are shared;
- the bylaws and policies that govern processes;
- your role, voter, committee member, chair, department representative;
- the relationship between governance and collective bargaining where applicable;
- the appeal and grievance pathways.

Governance misunderstood produces participation that is ineffective or that violates process. Learn the structure before engaging.

### Distinguish Faculty Authority From Administrative Authority

Authority over different matters is distributed. Distinguish:

- curriculum, degree requirements, and academic standards are typically faculty authority;
- personnel decisions involve faculty peer review with administrative final authority;
- budget and resource allocation are largely administrative with faculty consultation;
- strategic direction is shared in varying degrees;
- individual student academic decisions follow established faculty and registrar processes.

Confusing these leads to acting where you lack authority or failing to act where you hold it. Know which decisions are yours to influence.

### Engage Substantively, Not Symbolically

Governance is where real decisions are made, and disengagement cedes them. Engage substantively:

- attend and participate in governance bodies;
- prepare by reading materials and understanding issues;
- contribute expertise and perspectives;
- vote based on the merits, not on faction or friendship;
- follow issues across meetings rather than treating each in isolation.

Faculty who treat governance as symbolic discover that decisions are made without their input. Substantive engagement shapes outcomes.

### Respect Process And Bylaws

Governance processes exist for reasons, often rooted in protecting rights and ensuring fairness. Respect them:

- follow bylaws and established procedures;
- do not bypass process for speed or preferred outcomes;
- raise procedural concerns through proper channels;
- accept outcomes of legitimate process even when unfavorable;
- advocate for process changes through governance rather than violation.

Shortcuts through process produce decisions that are challenged, reversed, or that damage institutional culture and trust. Process discipline protects decisions and people.

### Handle Confidentiality Appropriately

Much governance involves confidential matters, especially personnel. Handle confidentiality:

- understand what is confidential and why;
- do not disclose confidential personnel or deliberative content;
- distinguish confidentiality of deliberations from transparency of outcomes;
- respect the privacy of colleagues and candidates under review;
- recognize that confidentiality protects fairness, not secrecy for its own sake.

Confidentiality breaches damage individuals under review, violate policy, and can create legal liability. They also destroy the trust governance requires to function.

### Participate Effectively In Personnel Processes

Personnel decisions, hiring, tenure, promotion, post-tenure review, are central faculty governance. Participate effectively:

- understand the criteria and standards;
- evaluate candidates against evidence and criteria, not preference;
- write evaluations that are specific, evidence-based, and fair;
- disclose conflicts of interest and recuse where required;
- respect confidentiality of deliberations and outcomes;
- recognize the weight of these decisions on colleagues' careers.

Personnel decisions made carelessly, factionally, or outside criteria damage colleagues and the institution's integrity. They are among the most consequential governance acts.

### Build Influence Through Credibility, Not Faction

Influence in governance comes from credibility built over time. Build it:

- develop expertise on the issues you engage;
- be reliable in preparation and follow-through;
- argue on merits with evidence;
- build relationships across constituencies and viewpoints;
- maintain integrity even when it costs a preferred outcome.

Influence built through faction, logrolling, or intimidation is fragile and corrosive. Influence built through credibility compounds and sustains.

### Represent Constituents Without Becoming A Delegate

If you represent a department or constituency, balance representation with judgment:

- understand your constituents' views and concerns;
- communicate governance matters back to constituents;
- exercise judgment on issues where you have expertise they lack;
- avoid becoming a pure delegate who votes only as instructed;
- avoid ignoring constituents entirely.

Pure delegation reduces governance to a headcount; pure autonomy betrays representation. Balance constituent voice with informed judgment.

### Use Shared Governance To Protect Academic Quality And Rights

Shared governance exists partly to protect academic matters from purely administrative or political logic. Use it:

- advocate for academic standards and quality in decisions;
- protect faculty rights in policy and personnel matters;
- ensure academic freedom is preserved in governance decisions;
- bring disciplinary expertise to bear on institutional questions;
- resist pressures that compromise educational integrity.

Faculty who do not exercise shared governance lose it. Engagement is how the faculty role in the institution is sustained.

### Navigate Conflict Constructively

Governance involves conflict, often genuine and substantive. Navigate it:

- engage disagreement on the merits;
- avoid personalizing disputes;
- use proper channels for procedural or ethical concerns;
- accept legitimate outcomes while continuing to advocate through process;
- maintain professional relationships across disagreement.

Conflict handled constructively strengthens governance; conflict handled factionally or personally erodes it. Distinguish principled dissent from factional warfare.

### Document And Follow Through

Governance decisions require documentation and follow-through:

- ensure decisions are recorded accurately;
- track implementation of governance decisions;
- hold administration accountable for acting on faculty authority;
- revisit decisions that are not implemented;
- maintain institutional memory across turnover.

Decisions undocumented or unimplemented are effectively reversed. Documentation and follow-through make governance consequential.

## Common Traps

### Governance Misunderstanding

Participating without understanding structure and authority is ineffective. Learn the system.

### Disengagement

Treating governance as symbolic cedes decisions to others. Engage substantively.

### Process Violation

Bypassing bylaws for speed or preference produces reversible decisions. Respect process.

### Confidentiality Breach

Disclosing confidential personnel or deliberative content damages people and trust. Handle confidentiality.

### Careless Personnel Decisions

Evaluating candidates on preference rather than criteria harms colleagues and integrity. Evaluate against evidence.

### Faction-Based Influence

Building influence through coalitions and intimidation is corrosive. Build credibility.

### Pure Delegation Or Pure Autonomy

Voting only as instructed or ignoring constituents both misrepresent. Balance both.

### Abdicating Shared Governance

Failing to exercise faculty authority loses it. Use governance to protect academic quality.

### Personalized Conflict

Factional and personal disputes erode governance. Navigate conflict constructively.

### Undocumented Decisions

Decisions not recorded or implemented are effectively reversed. Document and follow through.

### Procedural Cynicism

Treating process as obstacle rather than protection undermines governance. Respect process purposes.

## Self-Check

- Do you understand the governance structure, bodies, authority distribution, bylaws, and your role within them?
- Can you distinguish faculty authority from administrative authority across curriculum, personnel, budget, and strategy?
- Do you engage substantively in governance through preparation, participation, and merit-based voting?
- Do you respect bylaws and process even when outcomes are unfavorable or speed is tempting?
- Do you handle confidentiality appropriately, protecting personnel and deliberative content while ensuring outcome transparency?
- In personnel processes, do you evaluate against criteria and evidence, disclose conflicts, and respect confidentiality?
- Is your influence built through credibility and expertise rather than faction or intimidation?
- Do you balance representing constituents with exercising informed judgment?
- Do you use shared governance to protect academic quality, standards, and faculty rights?
- Do you navigate conflict on the merits without personalizing disputes?
- Are governance decisions documented, implemented, and held accountable over time?
- Would colleagues describe your governance participation as informed, principled, and respectful of process and people?
