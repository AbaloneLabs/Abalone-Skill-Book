# Program Development

An academic program, a major, minor, certificate, or degree, is a coherent educational structure, not a collection of courses. Programs are developed and revised through governance processes that require alignment among outcomes, curriculum, resources, and assessment, and that must satisfy institutional and often accreditation requirements. Faculty often approach program development as assembling courses they want to teach, which produces programs that lack coherence, cannot demonstrate student learning, and struggle in review. Effective program development designs backward from what graduates should be able to do, builds a curriculum that develops those capabilities, and builds in the assessment that demonstrates and improves the program over time.

Use this skill before developing or revising a program, designing curriculum, conducting program review, or building program-level outcomes and assessment. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing programs that are course collections rather than coherent educational designs, or that cannot survive governance and accreditation review.

## Core Rules

### Start From Program-Level Learning Outcomes

A program should be designed from what its graduates should be able to do. Define program outcomes that:

- describe capabilities graduates will have, not topics covered;
- span knowledge, skills, and dispositions appropriate to the field;
- are measurable through student work and performance;
- connect to institutional and accreditation outcomes where required;
- reflect both the discipline's requirements and the program's distinctive goals.

Program outcomes are the spine. Curriculum, assessment, and resources should all be derived from them, not added afterward.

### Build A Coherent Curriculum That Develops The Outcomes

The curriculum is the sequence through which students develop the outcomes. Design it so:

- each outcome is developed across multiple courses, not addressed once;
- courses build on each other in a deliberate sequence;
- foundational courses precede advanced application;
- capstone or integrative experiences synthesize the outcomes;
- electives extend rather than substitute for core development.

Map each outcome to the courses that develop and assess it. Outcomes addressed in no course are aspirations; outcomes addressed in only one are fragile.

### Design For Sequencing And Prerequisite Coherence

Programs have prerequisite structures that must be navigable. Design for:

- prerequisite chains that are necessary, not arbitrary;
- sequences that can be completed in the expected timeline;
- courses offered frequently enough to prevent bottlenecks;
- pathways for transfer and changing majors that do not extend time excessively;
- awareness of which courses are offered rarely and planning around them.

A program whose prerequisite structure cannot be completed in four years, or that bottlenecks at a rarely offered course, fails students regardless of its intellectual coherence.

### Align Resources With The Program's Demands

A program requires resources to deliver. Align them:

- faculty expertise matched to the courses offered;
- sufficient course offerings to meet student demand;
- labs, studios, clinical, or field resources where required;
- library and information resources for the curriculum;
- advising capacity to guide students through the program;
- budget that sustains the program over time.

A program designed without resource alignment produces courses that cannot be staffed, bottlenecks that delay students, and quality that erodes under demand.

### Connect To Institutional And Accreditation Requirements

Programs exist within institutional and accreditation frameworks. Connect:

- institutional learning outcomes and mission;
- general education and program integration;
- accreditation standards and reporting requirements;
- transfer articulation agreements;
- professional licensure or certification requirements where relevant.

Programs that ignore these frameworks face approval difficulty and ongoing compliance burdens. Design for them from the start.

### Build In Program-Level Assessment

Programs must demonstrate and improve student learning. Build in assessment:

- identify where each outcome is assessed, not just taught;
- use direct measures of student learning, not just satisfaction or completion;
- collect evidence systematically across offerings;
- review assessment results on a regular cycle;
- use results to revise the curriculum and improve outcomes.

Assessment is not surveillance of faculty; it is the evidence base for program improvement and the documentation accreditation requires. Build it in rather than retrofitting it for review.

### Conduct Genuine Program Review

Periodic program review is both a requirement and an opportunity. Conduct it genuinely:

- gather evidence on enrollment, completion, student learning, and outcomes;
- involve faculty, students, alumni, and external reviewers;
- examine strengths and weaknesses honestly;
- develop an action plan with priorities and timelines;
- follow through on the plan and document progress.

Program review treated as paperwork produces no improvement and is visible to accreditors. Review treated as genuine improvement drives program quality.

### Engage Faculty In Development And Ownership

Programs are delivered by faculty, who must own them. Engage faculty:

- involve the full department in outcome and curriculum design;
- address concerns about workload and expertise honestly;
- build shared understanding of the program's goals;
- distribute ownership so the program does not depend on one person;
- manage the transition from old to new curriculum fairly.

Programs imposed by a few faculty or an administrator generate resistance and erode. Programs developed with faculty ownership are sustained.

### Consider Students' Trajectories And Diversity

Programs serve diverse students with varied trajectories. Consider:

- pathways for full-time and part-time students;
- transfer students and credit applicability;
- students changing majors into the program;
- students with varying preparation and backgrounds;
- career and graduate school destinations;
- the program's role for minors and non-majors.

A program designed only for the traditional full-time four-year major underserves many of its actual students.

### Plan For Sustainability And Evolution

Programs must be sustainable and able to evolve. Plan for:

- faculty turnover and the preservation of institutional knowledge;
- curriculum revision cycles to keep content current;
- resource needs as enrollment changes;
- assessment and review cycles that drive ongoing improvement;
- adaptation to disciplinary, technological, and workforce changes.

A program treated as fixed decays; one designed to evolve stays vital. Build in the capacity for revision.

### Navigate Governance Effectively

Program development goes through governance. Navigate it:

- understand the approval pathway and required bodies;
- prepare proposals with required components and evidence;
- consult affected departments and programs early;
- address concerns before formal submission;
- maintain documentation through the process.

Programs that fail governance do so often for procedural reasons that preparation could have prevented. Navigate governance deliberately.

## Common Traps

### Course Collection Programs

Assembling courses faculty want to teach produces incoherent programs. Design from outcomes.

### Outcomes Without Curriculum Mapping

Outcomes addressed in no course are aspirations. Map outcomes to courses.

### Unworkable Prerequisite Structures

Structures that cannot be completed on time fail students. Design navigable sequences.

### Resource Ignored

Programs without faculty, offerings, or facilities cannot be delivered. Align resources.

### Framework Ignored

Ignoring institutional and accreditation requirements creates approval and compliance problems. Connect from the start.

### Assessment As Afterthought

Retrofitting assessment for review produces paperwork, not improvement. Build it in.

### Paperwork Program Review

Review treated as compliance produces no change. Conduct genuine review.

### Faculty Imposition

Programs imposed without faculty ownership generate resistance. Engage faculty.

### Traditional-Student Default

Designing only for full-time four-year majors underserves actual students. Consider diversity.

### Fixed Programs

Programs that cannot evolve decay. Plan for sustainability and revision.

### Governance Mishandling

Failing to navigate approval pathways defeats good proposals. Navigate governance deliberately.

## Self-Check

- Are program-level learning outcomes defined as graduate capabilities, measurable, and connected to institutional and accreditation outcomes?
- Does the curriculum coherently develop each outcome across multiple courses with deliberate sequencing and a capstone?
- Is the prerequisite structure navigable within the expected timeline without bottlenecks?
- Are resources, faculty, offerings, facilities, advising, budget, aligned with the program's demands?
- Does the program connect to institutional mission, general education, accreditation, transfer, and licensure requirements?
- Is program-level assessment built in, with direct measures, systematic collection, and a review-and-improve cycle?
- Is program review conducted genuinely with evidence, multiple perspectives, and follow-through?
- Are faculty engaged in development and ownership so the program is sustained?
- Does the program serve diverse student trajectories, transfer, part-time, changing majors, varying preparation?
- Is there a plan for sustainability and evolution across faculty turnover and disciplinary change?
- Is the governance approval pathway understood and navigated with consultation and documentation?
- Could you defend this program to an accreditor as coherent, resourced, assessed, and improving?
