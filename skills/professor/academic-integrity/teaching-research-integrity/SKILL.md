# Teaching Research Integrity

Research integrity is not a compliance checkbox completed once in a training module; it is a culture that must be taught, modeled, and reinforced throughout a researcher's formation. Students and junior researchers who learn integrity as a list of rules learn to navigate rules, not to reason ethically. When integrity is taught as reasoning about real dilemmas, with attention to the pressures and ambiguities of actual research practice, it builds the judgment that sustains honest science and scholarship. The failure mode is integrity training that is abstract, generic, and disconnected from the discipline's real temptations.

Use this skill before teaching research integrity, designing responsible conduct training, addressing data fabrication or authorship issues, or building integrity culture. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing integrity education that is compliance-theater, abstract to the point of uselessness, or focused on obvious misconduct while ignoring the everyday pressures that erode integrity.

## Core Rules

### Teach Integrity As Reasoning, Not Just Rules

Rules cannot anticipate every situation, and researchers face genuine ambiguities: how to handle outliers, when a contribution merits authorship, how to credit ideas from review, how to report null results. Teach the reasoning behind rules so researchers can navigate novel situations, not just memorize prohibitions.

Reasoning-based teaching:

- present real dilemmas with no easy answer;
- examine the principles at stake, honesty, rigor, fairness, openness;
- discuss how different choices affect the scientific record and others;
- connect decisions to consequences for the field and public trust;
- develop the capacity to recognize and reason about integrity questions.

Researchers who can reason can act with integrity in situations rules did not anticipate.

### Address The Full Spectrum Of Misconduct

Research misconduct is often reduced to fabrication, falsification, and plagiarism, the "FFP" definition. But integrity extends to a wider range of everyday practices that erode the record:

- selective reporting of supportive results;
- p-hacking and questionable analytical practices;
- inadequate record-keeping that prevents verification;
- unfair authorship practices and ghost or gift authorship;
- insufficient peer-review contribution;
- inadequate mentoring or supervision;
- conflicts of interest that shape interpretation;
- poor data sharing that prevents scrutiny.

Teaching only FFP misses the practices that more commonly compromise integrity.

### Use Discipline-Relevant Cases

Generic integrity cases feel irrelevant. Use cases from the students' discipline, including landmark misconduct cases, ambiguous everyday situations, and the instructor's own experiences where appropriate. Discipline-relevant cases make the stakes and reasoning concrete.

Case-based teaching:

- present the situation and the pressures involved;
- have students identify the integrity questions;
- discuss the options and their consequences;
- connect to the principles and the field's norms;
- examine what would prevent or detect the problem.

Cases that students recognize as their future build judgment that abstract rules do not.

### Address The Pressures That Erode Integrity

Most misconduct is not pure malice; it is the product of pressure: publish-or-perish, funding competition, career stakes, supervisor expectations, and the desire to tell a clean story. Teach researchers to recognize these pressures and develop strategies to resist them, rather than pretending integrity is simply a matter of individual virtue.

Pressure-aware teaching:

- name the structural pressures honestly;
- discuss how pressure distorts judgment;
- develop strategies for resisting pressure, documentation, transparency, consultation;
- address the role of mentors and lab culture in supporting integrity;
- connect to systemic issues without excusing individual responsibility.

Ignoring pressure produces moralism; addressing it produces practical integrity.

### Teach Data Management As An Integrity Practice

Good data management is integrity infrastructure. Records that allow verification, transparent analysis pipelines, and preserved raw data are what make research trustworthy and what protect researchers from accusations. Teach data management as part of integrity, not as a separate administrative topic.

Data integrity practices:

- maintain records that another could follow;
- preserve raw data and analysis code;
- document decisions about exclusion and transformation;
- distinguish exploratory from confirmatory analysis;
- preregister where appropriate;
- share data and materials when possible.

Researchers with strong data practices can defend their work and contribute to a verifiable record.

### Teach Authorship Ethics Concretely

Authorship disputes are among the most common integrity conflicts. Teach authorship ethics concretely, including the discipline's standards, how contributions are assessed, the ethics of order and inclusion, and how to negotiate authorship before conflicts arise.

Authorship teaching:

- the criteria for authorship in the discipline;
- the difference between authorship and acknowledgment;
- how to discuss and agree on authorship early;
- the ethics of ghost, gift, and honorary authorship;
- how to handle disputes and power imbalances.

Vague authorship norms produce avoidable conflict and unfair outcomes, especially for junior researchers.

### Teach Responsible Peer Review

Peer review is where researchers exercise power over others' work, and it is a frequent site of integrity failures: delayed reviews, breaches of confidentiality, theft of ideas, and reviews that advance the reviewer's agenda. Teach responsible review as an integrity obligation.

Responsible review practices:

- confidentiality of submitted work;
- timely and constructive review;
- declaring conflicts of interest;
- not using ideas from reviewed work;
- fair assessment regardless of agreement or competition.

Researchers trained in review integrity both review better and recognize when they are reviewed unfairly.

### Model Integrity In Your Own Practice

Students learn integrity from what mentors do, not just what they say. Model integrity in your own research: transparent methods, fair authorship, honest reporting, responsible review, and admission of error. Hypocrisy between taught and practiced integrity is corrosive; consistency builds a culture that sustains itself.

### Build A Lab And Classroom Culture Of Integrity

Integrity is cultural, not just individual. Build environments where integrity is expected, questions can be raised safely, mistakes can be acknowledged, and pressure does not push people toward cutting corners. Culture is built through norms, mentorship, and the response when issues arise.

### Connect Integrity To Consequences For The Field And Public

Research integrity is not an internal matter. Misconduct and questionable practices erode public trust, waste resources, harm patients and policy, and damage colleagues. Connect integrity to its real-world consequences so researchers understand the stakes beyond their own careers.

## Common Traps

### Compliance-Only Training

Rule memorization does not build reasoning. Teach integrity as judgment.

### Reducing Misconduct To FFP

Everyday questionable practices erode integrity more than rare fraud. Address the full spectrum.

### Generic Cases

Abstract cases feel irrelevant. Use discipline-relevant situations.

### Ignoring Structural Pressure

Pretending integrity is only individual virtue misses the drivers. Address pressure.

### Data Management As Separate

Treating records as administrative misses their integrity role. Teach data practices as integrity.

### Vague Authorship Norms

Unclear authorship standards produce conflict and unfairness. Teach concretely.

### Peer Review As Afterthought

Untrained reviewers harm the system. Teach responsible review.

### Taught But Not Modeled

Hypocrisy between words and practice is corrosive. Model integrity.

### Individual-Only Culture

Integrity is cultural. Build environments that sustain it.

### Integrity As Internal Matter

Misconduct harms the public and field. Connect to real consequences.

## Self-Check

- Is integrity taught as reasoning about principles and dilemmas, not just memorization of rules?
- Does teaching address the full spectrum of misconduct including everyday questionable practices beyond FFP?
- Are cases discipline-relevant so students recognize them as their own future situations?
- Are the structural pressures that erode integrity named and strategies to resist them developed?
- Is data management taught as an integrity practice that enables verification and defense of work?
- Is authorship ethics taught concretely including criteria, negotiation, and power dynamics?
- Is responsible peer review taught as an integrity obligation with specific practices?
- Does the instructor model integrity in their own research, consistent with what is taught?
- Is a lab and classroom culture built where integrity is expected and questions can be raised safely?
- Are the real-world consequences of integrity failures for the field and public made clear?
