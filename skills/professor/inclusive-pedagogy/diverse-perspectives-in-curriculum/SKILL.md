# Diverse Perspectives In Curriculum

What a curriculum includes and excludes shapes whose knowledge counts, whose experiences are represented, and who feels the field belongs to them. Curricula that present a narrow canon as universal reproduce exclusions, signal to some students that they do not belong, and impoverish all students' understanding of the field. Diversifying the curriculum is not tokenism or adding a week of diverse voices to an unchanged course; it is rethinking what knowledge is valued, whose contributions are centered, how examples and cases are chosen, and how the field's history of exclusion is acknowledged. Done well, it deepens disciplinary understanding, broadens relevance, and supports equity. Done poorly, it is performative, superficial, and can provoke backlash that sets the work back.

Use this skill before diversifying a curriculum, revising a reading list, selecting texts and examples, decolonizing a syllabus, or addressing canon and representation. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing performative or tokenistic diversity, from provoking avoidable backlash, or from sacrificing disciplinary integrity in the name of representation.

## Core Rules

### Treat Diversification As Rethinking, Not Addition

Genuine curricular diversification rethinks the course, not just appends to it. Practices:

- examine what the course centers and what it marginalizes;
- reconsider the organizing questions and frameworks;
- integrate diverse perspectives throughout, not in a segregated unit;
- revise examples, cases, and applications across the course;
- avoid the "add diversity and stir" approach that leaves the canon untouched.

Addition preserves the existing structure and marginalizes the new material. Rethinking integrates diverse perspectives into the course's substance.

### Examine The Canon Critically

The canon is not neutral; it reflects histories of power and exclusion. Examine it:

- ask whose work is included and whose is absent;
- understand the historical and institutional reasons for the canon's shape;
- distinguish foundational contributions from reproduced exclusion;
- present the canon as constructed, not natural or complete;
- contextualize canonical work in its history, including exclusion.

Critical examination does not mean discarding the canon; it means understanding it honestly and deciding deliberately what to keep, add, and contextualize.

### Select Diverse Texts And Authors Deliberately

Text and author selection signals whose knowledge counts. Select deliberately:

- include authors and creators diverse in identity, geography, and perspective;
- ensure representation is substantive, not tokenistic;
- choose works for their contribution, not only identity;
- address gaps in the existing curriculum meaningfully;
- avoid isolating diverse voices in a single week or unit.

Tokenism, one representative author treated as standing for a group, is visible and damaging. Select for substance and integrate throughout.

### Choose Examples And Cases That Reflect Diverse Contexts

Examples and cases shape relevance and belonging. Choose them:

- vary contexts so diverse students see themselves and their worlds;
- avoid examples that assume a narrow background or experience;
- include cases from diverse geographies, cultures, and settings;
- examine how examples might stereotype or exoticize;
- ensure examples serve the learning, not decoration.

Examples that always assume a dominant context signal to others that the field is not about them. Diversify contexts meaningfully.

### Acknowledge The Field's History Of Exclusion

Fields have histories of exclusion that shape their current shape. Acknowledge them:

- address how the field has excluded people and perspectives;
- examine how methods and questions reflect biases;
- present critique as part of disciplinary rigor, not attack;
- connect historical exclusion to current inequities;
- avoid both whitewashing and caricature.

Honest acknowledgment builds credibility and deepens understanding. Avoiding it leaves the curriculum intellectually incomplete.

### Integrate Rather Than Segregate

Diverse perspectives integrated throughout differ from those segregated into a unit. Integrate:

- weave diverse voices into every unit, not a diversity week;
- connect diverse perspectives to core concepts, not periphery;
- avoid signaling that mainstream is default and diversity is supplement;
- structure the course so diverse perspectives are central, not additive;
- assess diverse perspectives as part of the core, not optional.

Segregation reinforces the idea that diverse perspectives are supplementary. Integration treats them as constitutive of the field.

### Preserve Disciplinary Rigor

Diversification must not sacrifice rigor. Preserve it:

- select works for scholarly or creative quality and contribution;
- maintain analytical and methodological standards;
- engage diverse perspectives with the same rigor as canonical work;
- avoid lowering expectations or exempting diverse work from critique;
- present diversification as deepening, not diluting, the field.

Rigor and diversity are not opposed. Treating diverse work with the same serious engagement strengthens both.

### Prepare For And Navigate Backlash

Curricular diversification can provoke backlash. Prepare:

- be able to articulate the scholarly rationale for choices;
- connect diversification to learning goals and disciplinary integrity;
- anticipate common objections and prepare responses;
- distinguish good-faith critique from bad-faith attack;
- document decisions and consult allies and mentors.

Backlash is often predictable and addressable with preparation. Avoid both defensiveness and capitulation.

### Avoid Performative Or Tokenistic Diversity

Performative diversity is visible and counterproductive. Avoid it:

- do not add diverse voices only for appearance;
- ensure representation is substantive and integrated;
- avoid treating individuals as representatives of groups;
- do not use diversity to signal virtue without doing the work;
- examine your own motivations honestly.

Performative diversity provokes cynicism and backlash and fails to advance learning or equity. Do substantive work.

### Engage Students In The Process

Students can be partners in diversification. Engage them:

- invite students to contribute perspectives and examples;
- discuss curricular choices and their rationale openly;
- create assignments that explore diverse perspectives;
- treat student knowledge and experience as resources;
- navigate disagreement as learning.

Engagement builds ownership and deepens the work, while avoiding the instructor-as-sole-author model.

### Connect To Learning Goals And Assessment

Diversification must connect to learning, not stand alone. Connect it:

- align diverse perspectives with course learning outcomes;
- assess diverse perspectives as part of the core;
- avoid treating diversity as enrichment separate from assessment;
- ensure the curriculum's substance reflects its diversity;
- evaluate whether diversification advanced learning.

Diversification unconnected to goals and assessment is decorative. Connect it to the course's intellectual core.

### Iterate And Continue Learning

Curricular diversification is ongoing. Iterate:

- revise the curriculum over multiple offerings;
- continue learning about the field's exclusions and diverse contributions;
- seek feedback from students and colleagues;
- stay current with scholarship on diversification and equity;
- treat the work as career-long, not one-time.

A diversified curriculum is not a finished product but an ongoing practice of rethinking and revision.

### Consider Positionality And Humility

Your own positionality shapes the work. Consider it:

- recognize the limits of your own perspective;
- avoid claiming authority over experiences that are not yours;
- consult and center voices from the communities represented;
- be open to critique and correction;
- approach the work with humility, not performance.

Positionality ignored produces missteps; acknowledged, it supports more honest and effective work.

## Common Traps

### Add-And-Stir

Appending diverse voices to an unchanged course is tokenistic. Rethink the structure.

### Tokenism

One representative author standing for a group is visible and damaging. Select for substance.

### Segregated Diversity Unit

A single diversity week marginalizes diverse perspectives. Integrate throughout.

### Uncritical Canon Acceptance

Presenting the canon as natural hides its exclusions. Examine it critically.

### Narrow Examples

Examples assuming a dominant context signal exclusion. Diversify contexts meaningfully.

### Rigor Sacrificed

Lowering expectations for diverse work betrays both rigor and diversity. Preserve standards.

### Performative Diversity

Diversity for appearance provokes cynicism. Do substantive work.

### Unprepared For Backlash

Without scholarly rationale, diversification is hard to defend. Prepare responses.

### Disconnection From Assessment

Diversity unconnected to goals and assessment is decorative. Connect to the core.

### One-Time Revision

A curriculum diversified once and never revised stagnates. Iterate ongoingly.

### Positionality Ignored

Ignoring your own limits produces missteps. Acknowledge positionality with humility.

### Exoticization

Presenting diverse perspectives as exotic rather than substantive reinforces othering. Engage as constitutive.

## Self-Check

- Does the course rethink its structure and centers, not just append diverse voices?
- Is the canon examined critically for its history of inclusion and exclusion?
- Are texts and authors selected for substance and integrated throughout, not tokenized?
- Do examples and cases reflect diverse contexts without stereotyping or exoticizing?
- Is the field's history of exclusion acknowledged honestly, without whitewashing or caricature?
- Are diverse perspectives integrated into every unit rather than segregated?
- Is disciplinary rigor preserved and applied equally to diverse and canonical work?
- Are you prepared to articulate the scholarly rationale and navigate backlash?
- Is the diversity substantive rather than performative or tokenistic?
- Are students engaged as partners in exploring diverse perspectives?
- Is diversification connected to learning goals and assessed as part of the core?
- Is the work iterative, with ongoing revision and learning?
- Have you considered your own positionality and approached the work with humility?
