# Seminar Question Design

A seminar lives or dies by its questions. Poor questions produce silence, because there is nothing to say, or shallow responses, because the question asks for recall or opinion rather than reasoning. Good questions make thinking visible, surface disagreement, and push students to use evidence and engage the text. The craft of seminar questioning is not improvisation; it is deliberate design that anticipates where students will struggle and structures the path through it.

Use this skill before designing seminar questions, sequencing a discussion, crafting prompts for readings, or diagnosing why a discussion fell flat. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing questions that are too vague to answer, too obvious to discuss, or so leading that they elicit performance rather than thought.

## Core Rules

### Design Questions That Require Reasoning, Not Recall Or Opinion

The most common seminar failure is the question that asks what the text said (recall) or what students think about it (opinion). Neither produces discussion worth having. Reasoning questions ask students to use the text to build, compare, or defend a position.

Question types ranked by discussion value:

- interpretive: what does the author mean by X and what evidence supports that reading;
- comparative: how does this argument compare to last week's, and where do they diverge;
- evaluative: which position is better supported and why;
- applicative: does this concept explain the case we examined;
- generative: what follows from this claim, or what would the author have to say about Y;
- diagnostic: why does this passage matter to the overall argument.

Recall questions close discussion; reasoning questions open it.

### Sequence Questions To Build From Accessible To Demanding

A seminar that opens with the hardest question produces silence. Sequence questions so early ones build the shared understanding later ones require.

Sequencing pattern:

- opening: a question grounded in the text that everyone can enter;
- development: questions that complicate, compare, or extend;
- synthesis: a question that requires integrating the thread;
- transfer or critique: a question that applies or challenges the ideas.

The opening question should not be easy in the sense of trivial; it should be accessible in the sense that students can begin reasoning from a concrete textual anchor.

### Anchor Questions In Specific Textual Evidence

Abstract questions produce abstract, floaty discussion. Anchor questions in specific passages, examples, or data so reasoning has something to grip.

Anchoring practices:

- cite a passage and ask what it means or why it matters;
- present a case and ask which concept applies;
- juxtapose two passages and ask how they relate;
- point to a tension in the text and ask how to resolve it.

Anchored questions produce evidence-based reasoning; unanchored questions produce opinion swapping.

### Prepare More Questions Than You Will Use, In A Priority Order

Discussions are unpredictable. Prepare a bank of questions ranked by importance so you can adapt to where students go without losing the thread. Know which question is essential and which are expendable.

Prepare:

- one or two essential questions the discussion must reach;
- several supporting questions that build toward them;
- backup questions for if students get stuck or finish early;
- questions that reframe if the discussion stalls.

A rigid script fails; a prioritized bank adapts.

### Use Questions That Surface Disagreement Productively

Discussion thrives on productive disagreement. Design questions that reveal legitimate tensions in the text or among reasonable readings, so students must take positions and defend them.

Disagreement-generating questions:

- present two defensible readings and ask which is better supported;
- ask whether the author's claim holds in a counterexample;
- ask what the strongest objection to the position would be;
- ask which assumption, if false, would undermine the argument.

Avoid questions engineered to produce a single correct answer; they end discussion.

### Give Thinking Time Before Expecting Responses

Silence after a question is not failure; it is thinking. Instructors often fill silence by rephrasing or answering, which teaches students to wait. After posing a question, wait at least five to ten seconds before speaking.

For demanding questions, offer writing time: have students write for a minute before discussion. Writing produces more equitable participation because it gives processing time to students who need it.

### Use Follow-Up Questions That Deepen Rather Than Move On

The first response is rarely the deepest. Use follow-up questions that push reasoning further rather than accepting the first answer and moving on.

Deepening follow-ups:

- what evidence supports that reading;
- what would someone who disagrees say;
- how does that connect to what was said earlier;
- what assumption are you making;
- can you say more about how you got there.

Follow-ups turn shallow responses into substantive discussion.

### Distribute Participation Deliberately

A few voices can dominate discussion. Structure participation so more students contribute:

- use pair or small-group discussion before whole-class sharing;
- use warm cold-call with preparation;
- invite quiet students by name with low-risk invitations;
- track who has spoken and intentionally draw in others;
- value questions as much as answers.

Equitable participation improves discussion quality because more perspectives enter.

### Connect Questions To Learning Outcomes

Every question should serve a learning outcome. If you cannot say what thinking a question produces or what understanding it builds, it is filler. Map questions to outcomes so the discussion accumulates toward the goals of the course.

### Prepare For The Discussion To Exceed Your Plan

The best discussions sometimes go somewhere unexpected and valuable. Hold your plan loosely enough to follow productive threads, while knowing enough about the essential questions to steer back when the thread runs out. Rigidity kills the best moments; total openness loses the thread.

## Common Traps

### Recall Or Opinion Questions

Questions about what the text said or what students think produce no reasoning. Ask interpretive, comparative, evaluative questions.

### Opening With The Hardest Question

Starting demanding produces silence. Build from accessible to demanding.

### Unanchored Abstract Questions

Floaty questions produce floaty discussion. Anchor in textual evidence.

### Rigid Script

A fixed list of questions cannot adapt. Use a prioritized bank.

### Single-Answer Questions

Questions engineered for one right answer end discussion. Surface productive disagreement.

### Filling Silence

Answering your own question teaches students to wait. Give thinking time.

### Accepting The First Response

Moving on after one answer forgoes depth. Use deepening follow-ups.

### Dominant Voices

A few voices monopolize. Structure equitable participation.

### Questions Without Outcome Connection

Questions that produce no target thinking are filler. Map to outcomes.

### Over-Control Or Total Drift

Rigidity kills good moments; total drift loses the thread. Hold the plan loosely.

## Self-Check

- Do questions require reasoning rather than recall or opinion?
- Are questions sequenced from accessible textual anchors to demanding synthesis?
- Are questions anchored in specific passages, cases, or evidence rather than floating abstractly?
- Is there a prioritized bank of questions with essential and supporting items, not a rigid script?
- Do some questions surface legitimate disagreement that requires students to take and defend positions?
- Is thinking or writing time given before expecting responses?
- Do follow-up questions deepen reasoning rather than moving on after the first answer?
- Is participation distributed so more than a few voices contribute?
- Does each question connect to a learning outcome so discussion accumulates toward course goals?
- Is the plan held loosely enough to follow productive threads while preserving the essential questions?
