# Active Learning Technique Selection

Active learning is not a single technique but a family of structured activities that make students think during class. The common failure is not in whether to use active learning but in choosing techniques that do not fit the objective, the class size, or the students' readiness. A jigsaw exercise in a fifty-minute survey overwhelms; a single think-pair-share in a graduate seminar underuses the room. Technique selection is a design decision that should follow from the cognitive goal and the constraints, not from habit or fashion.

Use this skill before choosing among active learning techniques, matching techniques to objectives and class size, or planning a sequence of activities across a course. The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to one technique for every situation or choosing elaborate methods whose setup cost exceeds their learning benefit.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Cognitive Goal, Not The Technique

Every active learning technique serves certain kinds of thinking better than others. Choose the technique that produces the thinking the objective requires, rather than starting from a favorite method.

Goal-technique alignment:

- check understanding of a concept: poll, minute paper, think-pair-share;
- practice application: problem solving, worked-example pairs;
- compare approaches: structured comparison, debate;
- build a complex understanding from parts: jigsaw;
- apply concepts to an authentic case: case-based learning;
- develop problem-solving under uncertainty: problem-based learning;
- surface and resolve misconceptions: peer instruction with polling;
- generate and critique arguments: structured peer critique.

If the technique does not produce the target thinking, it is the wrong technique regardless of how engaging it feels.

### Match Technique To Class Size And Room Constraints

Techniques that thrive in a seminar collapse in a large lecture, and vice versa. Match to the actual setting.

Size fit:

- small seminar (under twenty-five): sustained discussion, debate, fishbowl, peer critique, jigsaw;
- medium class (twenty-five to sixty): think-pair-share, structured group work, case discussion, peer instruction;
- large lecture (over sixty): individual response, pair work with neighbors, polls, minute papers, staged problem solving;
- online synchronous: chat, breakout rooms, polls, shared docs;
- asynchronous: discussion boards, reflection prompts, embedded checks.

Also consider the room: fixed-seat amphitheaters prevent easy group movement; labs and studios enable different techniques than lecture halls.

### Match Technique To Time Available

Techniques have different time costs. A jigsaw needs a full class period or more; a minute paper needs three minutes. Do not schedule a technique whose setup and debrief exceed the time slot, and do not use a heavyweight technique for a goal a lightweight one achieves.

Time-aware selection:

- minute paper, poll: three to five minutes;
- think-pair-share: five to eight minutes;
- structured group problem: ten to twenty minutes;
- case discussion: twenty to forty minutes;
- jigsaw, problem-based learning unit: one or more full sessions.

### Use Think-Pair-Share As The Default Workhorse

For most objectives and class sizes, think-pair-share is the most reliable, lowest-setup technique. It gives every student individual thinking time before social pressure, surfaces reasoning rather than just answers, and scales from seminars to large lectures.

Structure it deliberately:

- pose a question with defensible reasoning, not a single obvious answer;
- give individual thinking time, thirty seconds to two minutes;
- have pairs compare reasoning, not just answers;
- cold-call or invite pairs to report reasoning;
- surface the range of reasoning and address misconceptions.

Reserve more elaborate techniques for objectives that think-pair-share cannot serve.

### Use Jigsaw When Integration Across Perspectives Is The Goal

Jigsaw divides a complex topic into parts, has groups master one part each, then re-forms groups with one expert per part to teach each other and integrate. It is powerful when the objective is to build understanding of a whole from diverse components.

Jigsaw suits:

- a topic with distinct sub-areas that must integrate;
- objectives about synthesis and teaching;
- classes large enough to form multiple groups;
- time sufficient for mastery and re-forming.

It is too elaborate for a quick check or a single concept.

### Use Case-Based And Problem-Based Learning For Authentic Application

Case-based learning uses realistic scenarios; problem-based learning uses open-ended problems as the driver of learning. Both suit objectives about applying concepts to authentic, messy situations and developing professional judgment.

Use them when:

- the objective is application, diagnosis, or decision-making under uncertainty;
- authentic cases or problems are available or constructible;
- time allows for sustained engagement across sessions;
- the instructor can facilitate rather than lecture.

They are inefficient for introducing foundational concepts that students do not yet have the tools to apply.

### Use Peer Instruction For Conceptual Misconceptions

Peer instruction, popularized by Mazur, poses a conceptual question, has students answer individually, reveals the distribution, has them discuss in pairs, and re-answers. It is exceptionally effective for surfacing and resolving common misconceptions in quantitative and conceptual domains.

Use peer instruction when:

- the concept has known common misconceptions;
- a well-designed conceptual question can expose them;
- the class is large enough that individual polling is feasible;
- the instructor will act on the distribution rather than move on.

### Build Individual Accountability Into Every Technique

Active learning without individual accountability produces social loafing and hides which students understand. Build in:

- individual response before group discussion;
- random or warm cold-call for reporting;
- individual written reflection or submission;
- group product with named contributions;
- follow-up individual check.

Accountability does not require grading every moment; it requires structuring tasks so each student must think.

### Sequence Techniques Across The Term For Variety And Progression

Using the same technique every class loses its attention-resetting power. Rotate techniques and progress from lower- to higher-stakes activities as students build skill and trust. Early in the term, use low-risk structures (pair work, minute papers); later, use higher-stakes structures (debate, peer critique, jigsaw) once students know each other and the norms.

### Plan The Debrief, Not Just The Activity

The debrief is where learning consolidates. An activity without a debrief leaves thinking scattered. Plan how to:

- surface the range of student reasoning;
- connect activity insights to the concept;
- address misconceptions revealed;
- synthesize toward the learning outcome;
- close the loop by adjusting instruction if needed.

## Common Traps

### Technique Before Goal

Choosing a favorite technique and forcing the objective to fit inverts the design logic. Start from the cognitive goal.

### Size Mismatch

A seminar technique in a large lecture, or vice versa, fails. Match to class size and room.

### Time Underestimation

Techniques take longer than expected, especially debrief. Budget realistically.

### Defaulting To One Technique

Using think-pair-share every class loses novelty and reach. Rotate and progress.

### Jigsaw For Quick Checks

Elaborate techniques for simple goals waste time. Match technique complexity to objective.

### No Individual Accountability

Group work without individual accountability hides confusion and enables loafing. Build it in.

### Skipping The Debrief

Activity without debrief leaves learning unconsolidated. Plan the synthesis.

### Ignoring The Distribution

Peer instruction whose poll results are ignored teaches students the activity is theater. Act on results.

### Activity Without Cognitive Goal

Engaging activity that does not produce target thinking is filler. Tie every activity to an objective.

### Over-Engineering

Elaborate games and simulations rarely pay off relative to setup. Prefer simple repeatable structures.

## Self-Check

- Is the technique chosen to produce the cognitive thinking the objective requires?
- Does the technique fit the actual class size, room, and modality?
- Is the time cost of the technique, including setup and debrief, within the available class time?
- Is think-pair-share used as the default, with elaborate techniques reserved for objectives it cannot serve?
- Are jigsaw, case-based, and problem-based learning used only when their specific strengths are needed?
- Is peer instruction used to surface and resolve known misconceptions, with action on the poll distribution?
- Does every technique include individual accountability so each student must think?
- Are techniques sequenced across the term for variety and progression from low- to higher-stakes?
- Is the debrief planned to surface reasoning, address misconceptions, and synthesize toward the outcome?
- Would a colleague observing the class be able to identify the cognitive goal of each activity?
