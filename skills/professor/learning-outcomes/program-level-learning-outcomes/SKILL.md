# Program Level Learning Outcomes

Program-level learning outcomes describe what a graduate of a major, minor, or degree can do by completion. They differ from course outcomes in scope and ownership: a single instructor controls course outcomes, but program outcomes require faculty consensus, span multiple courses, and must be demonstrable across a curriculum. The common failure is to write program outcomes by aggregating course outcomes after the fact, producing a list that describes what is taught rather than what every graduate can be expected to demonstrate.

Use this skill before drafting program outcomes, building a curriculum map, preparing accreditation self-study evidence, or coordinating outcome assessment across a department. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing program outcomes that are unfalsifiable, unowned, or disconnected from the actual curriculum students experience.

## Core Rules

### Define What Every Graduate Should Demonstrate, Not What The Program Offers

Program outcomes describe the graduate, not the curriculum. The subject of each outcome is the student at exit. Distinguish:

- program outcome: what a graduate can do upon completion;
- course outcome: what a student can do at the end of a course;
- curriculum element: what the program provides to develop the outcome.

Weak program outcomes describe offerings: "The program offers coursework in research methods." Strong outcomes describe capabilities: "Graduates can design and execute a research study appropriate to the discipline."

### Secure Faculty Ownership Before Finalizing

Program outcomes that one person drafts and the rest of the faculty ignore will never be assessed or used. Outcomes require collective commitment because assessment depends on multiple instructors contributing evidence.

Ownership practices:

- convene the faculty to draft outcomes collaboratively;
- map each outcome to the courses and instructors responsible;
- assign assessment stewardship so no outcome is orphaned;
- revisit outcomes on a regular cycle, not once and forever;
- document the process for accreditation evidence.

A program outcome with no assigned steward will silently drop from assessment.

### Write Outcomes That Span The Curriculum

Program outcomes are broader than course outcomes because they develop across multiple courses and years. They should capture integrative capabilities that no single course fully delivers.

Typical program outcome domains:

- disciplinary knowledge and its structure;
- disciplinary methods and inquiry;
- critical analysis and evidence-based reasoning;
- communication in disciplinary and public modes;
- ethical and professional judgment;
- collaboration and teamwork;
- research or creative practice;
- integration across subfields and contexts.

Each domain may yield one or two outcomes; avoid producing twenty outcomes no one can assess.

### Build A Curriculum Map That Shows Where Outcomes Are Developed

A curriculum map displays which courses introduce, reinforce, and master each program outcome. It is the primary tool for verifying that the curriculum actually develops every outcome.

Map levels:

- introduce: outcome is first encountered, foundational exposure;
- reinforce: outcome is practiced and developed further;
- master: outcome is demonstrated at exit level, often in a capstone;
- assess: explicit evidence of student achievement is collected.

The map reveals gaps where an outcome is introduced but never reinforced or mastered, and redundancies where many courses introduce an outcome none ever advance.

### Ensure Every Outcome Has A Mastery And Assessment Point

An outcome that is introduced and reinforced across courses but never assessed at mastery is unverifiable. Designate a capstone, senior seminar, portfolio, comprehensive exam, or performance assessment where each outcome is demonstrated at exit level.

For each outcome, identify:

- the course or experience where mastery is expected;
- the specific assessment artifact that evidences mastery;
- the rubric or standard used to judge it;
- the faculty member responsible for collecting and analyzing results.

### Distinguish Direct From Indirect Assessment Evidence

Accreditation values direct evidence of student learning over indirect signals.

- direct evidence: capstone projects, performances, portfolios, scored exams, authenticated work;
- indirect evidence: graduation rates, student satisfaction, self-reported confidence, enrollment.

Both matter, but a program that reports only indirect evidence cannot demonstrate that learning occurred. Build a direct evidence collection around the mastery points.

### Close The Assessment Loop At The Program Level

Program assessment is pointless if results do not inform change. Establish a cycle:

- collect evidence on a subset of outcomes each year on a rolling basis;
- analyze results against a performance standard;
- identify where students fall short and hypothesize causes;
- implement curricular or pedagogical changes;
- reassess to confirm improvement.

Documenting the closed loop is what accreditors and program reviewers look for. Assessment without change is compliance theater.

### Align Program Outcomes With Institutional And Accreditor Frameworks

Program outcomes usually must map to institutional learning outcomes and to disciplinary accreditation standards. Build this mapping explicitly so evidence collected for program outcomes can serve multiple reporting needs.

Check:

- institutional outcomes (communication, quantitative reasoning, ethics, etc.);
- disciplinary professional society standards;
- regional accreditor expectations;
- state or national regulatory requirements where applicable.

One well-designed outcome set should satisfy several frameworks rather than requiring separate evidence for each.

### Account For Diverse Student Pathways

Not every graduate takes the same courses, especially in programs with electives, concentrations, or transfer pathways. Program outcomes must be demonstrable regardless of path. Verify in the curriculum map that every reasonable pathway provides introduction, reinforcement, mastery, and assessment for each outcome.

Transfer students and part-time students are particularly at risk of missing mastery points designed into a specific course sequence.

## Common Traps

### Aggregating Course Outcomes Upward

Listing what courses teach produces a curriculum description, not graduate capabilities. Write from the graduate backward.

### Outcomes Without Faculty Ownership

Outcomes drafted in isolation are never assessed. Secure collective commitment.

### Outcomes With No Mastery Point

Introduced and reinforced but never assessed at exit level means no verifiable evidence. Designate a capstone or equivalent.

### Indirect Evidence Only

Satisfaction and graduation rates do not prove learning. Collect direct evidence.

### Assessment Without Change

Collecting data and filing it teaches no one. Close the loop and document improvements.

### Ignoring Transfer And Elective Pathways

Outcomes demonstrable only on one course sequence fail for transfer and elective students. Verify across pathways.

### Too Many Outcomes

A program with fifteen outcomes cannot assess them all meaningfully. Consolidate to a set that can be assessed on a rolling cycle.

### Mapping To Accreditor Without Internal Use

Outcomes written only to satisfy accreditation, never used internally, produce compliance without improvement. Make outcomes work for the program first.

## Self-Check

- Do program outcomes describe graduate capabilities rather than curriculum offerings?
- Has faculty ownership been secured, with stewardship assigned for each outcome's assessment?
- Do outcomes span integrative domains that develop across the curriculum rather than single courses?
- Is there a curriculum map showing where each outcome is introduced, reinforced, mastered, and assessed?
- Does every outcome have a designated mastery point with a specific assessment artifact and rubric?
- Is direct evidence of student learning collected, not only indirect signals?
- Is the assessment loop closed so results lead to documented curricular change and reassessment?
- Are program outcomes mapped to institutional and accreditor frameworks to serve multiple reporting needs?
- Are outcomes demonstrable across diverse student pathways including transfer and elective routes?
- Is the number of outcomes manageable for assessment on a rolling cycle rather than so numerous that none are assessed well?
