# Outcome Writing And Blooms Taxonomy

Learning outcomes are promises a course makes to students about what they will be able to do by the end. When outcomes are vague, students cannot tell what mastery looks like, instructors cannot design aligned assessments, and accreditation reviewers cannot verify that learning occurred. The most common failure is writing outcomes that describe what the instructor will cover rather than what the student will demonstrate. The second most common failure is choosing verbs so abstract that no evidence could confirm them.

Use this skill before drafting or revising course-level learning outcomes, selecting action verbs, mapping cognitive demand, or auditing an existing set of outcomes for measurability. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing outcomes that sound scholarly but cannot be observed, assessed, or used by students to guide their study.

## Core Rules

### Write Outcomes About Student Performance, Not Instructor Coverage

An outcome must describe what the student will be able to do, not what the course will present. The grammatical subject of every outcome should be the student, implied by the verb.

Weak (coverage-oriented):

- "Introduce the major theories of motivation."
- "Cover regression analysis and its assumptions."
- "Expose students to contemporary Latin American poetry."

Strong (performance-oriented):

- "Compare and contrast the major theories of motivation and predict which best explains a given behavior."
- "Run a regression analysis, test its assumptions, and interpret the results in plain language."
- "Analyze a contemporary Latin American poem using formal, historical, and thematic lenses."

If you can rewrite an outcome as "the instructor will talk about X," it is a topic, not an outcome.

### Choose Verbs Whose Achievement Can Be Evidenced

An outcome is only useful if you can imagine a piece of student work that would demonstrate it. Test each verb against the evidence question: what would the student produce, say, or do to prove this?

Reliable evidence-producing verbs:

- analyze, evaluate, critique, defend, justify;
- design, build, compose, construct, prototype;
- apply, calculate, solve, diagnose, troubleshoot;
- compare, contrast, classify, rank, prioritize;
- model, simulate, predict, estimate;
- write, present, explain-to-a-novice, document.

Weak evidence verbs to avoid or qualify:

- understand, know, learn, appreciate, grasp;
- be aware of, be familiar with, gain exposure to;
- think critically about (without specifying the object or standard).

"Understand" is not forbidden, but it must be operationalized. "Understand photosynthesis" is unverifiable; "diagram the photosynthetic pathway and explain how light intensity affects each stage" is verifiable.

### Map Verbs To Cognitive Level Deliberately

Bloom's revised taxonomy orders cognitive demand from lower to higher: remember, understand, apply, analyze, evaluate, create. Outcomes should reflect the level of thinking the course actually demands, not default to the highest level for prestige.

Match level to purpose:

- introductory survey courses need remember, understand, and apply outcomes;
- intermediate courses should add analyze and evaluate;
- capstone and graduate courses should include evaluate and create;
- professional and clinical courses need apply, analyze, and evaluate tied to authentic tasks.

Do not write only remember-level outcomes for a senior seminar, and do not write only create-level outcomes for an introductory survey. The distribution should match the course's role in the curriculum.

### Specify The Object, Condition, And Standard

A complete outcome names what students act on, under what conditions, and to what standard. The condition and standard make the outcome assessable and prevent grade inflation or deflation.

Components:

- action verb (cognitive level);
- object (the content or skill);
- condition (tools, resources, constraints, context);
- standard (accuracy, depth, speed, audience, format).

Example with all components: "Given a raw dataset [condition], students will clean, visualize, and analyze [verbs] the data [object] to produce a written report that a non-technical manager could act on [standard]."

The standard does not always need a numeric threshold, but it should make quality distinguishable from non-quality.

### Limit The Number Of Outcomes

A course with twenty outcomes cannot teach or assess any of them well. Aim for five to eight major outcomes for a standard course, each significant enough to warrant dedicated instruction and assessment.

Consolidation principles:

- merge narrow outcomes that share a verb and object;
- keep outcomes that require distinct instruction and evidence;
- distinguish course outcomes from weekly objectives;
- treat outcomes as durable across offerings, objectives as term-specific.

If a syllabus lists fifteen outcomes, the course is probably confusing topics with outcomes.

### Distinguish Outcomes From Goals And Objectives

These terms are often conflated, which confuses students and reviewers. Keep them distinct:

- broad goal: the aspirational purpose of the course or program;
- learning outcome: what students can demonstrably do by the end;
- weekly objective: the sub-skill or knowledge target of a specific session;
- competency: a durable, transferable capability assessed across contexts.

Write outcomes at the course level. Push finer-grained targets down to objectives, and push aspirations up to goals.

### Make Outcomes Visible To Students

Outcomes that exist only in a syllabus appendix do not guide learning. Reference outcomes in assignment prompts, rubric rows, module headers, and feedback. Students should be able to answer "which outcome is this task developing?" without guessing.

Visibility practices:

- tag each assignment with the outcome(s) it assesses;
- restate the relevant outcome at the start of a module;
- use outcome language in rubric criteria;
- ask students to self-assess against outcomes at midterm and end.

### Write For The Discipline's Authentic Practices

Outcomes should reflect what practitioners in the field actually do, not a generic academic skill set. A history outcome should involve source interpretation and argumentation; an engineering outcome should involve design under constraints; a nursing outcome should involve clinical judgment under uncertainty.

Consult disciplinary professional societies, accreditation criteria, and authentic task analyses to ground outcomes in real practice rather than textbook exercises.

## Common Traps

### Coverage Disguised As Outcomes

"Introduce," "cover," and "expose" describe teaching, not learning. Rewrite them as student performance.

### Unverifiable Verbs

"Understand," "appreciate," and "know" cannot be evidenced directly. Operationalize them into observable verbs.

### Prestige Inflation Of Cognitive Level

Writing only evaluate and create outcomes for an intro course sets students up to fail and instructors up to inflate. Match level to course role.

### Outcome Sprawl

Too many outcomes dilute focus and cannot all be assessed. Consolidate to a teachable, assessable set.

### Missing Condition Or Standard

"Analyze data" without context or quality bar is ungradeable. Add condition and standard.

### Verbs Without Objects

"Think critically" is incomplete. Think critically about what, using which frameworks, judged by which standard?

### Copying Outcomes From A Template

Generic outcomes ("communicate effectively") disconnected from the discipline produce courses that look aligned on paper but teach nothing distinctive.

### Ignoring Prerequisite Cognitive Levels

Jumping to create-level outcomes without remember, understand, and apply foundations produces shallow work. Scaffold the cognitive ladder.

## Self-Check

- Does each outcome describe student performance rather than instructor coverage?
- Can you name a specific piece of student work that would evidence each outcome?
- Are verbs chosen for their cognitive level and matched to the course's role in the curriculum?
- Does each outcome include an object, condition, and standard where assessability requires them?
- Is the total number of outcomes teachable and assessable within the term, roughly five to eight?
- Are outcomes distinguished from broad goals, weekly objectives, and program competencies?
- Are outcomes visible to students in assignments, rubrics, and module framing?
- Do outcomes reflect authentic disciplinary practice rather than generic academic skills?
- Have you avoided unverifiable verbs like understand, know, and appreciate, or operationalized them?
- Could a colleague read these outcomes and design a valid assessment for each?
