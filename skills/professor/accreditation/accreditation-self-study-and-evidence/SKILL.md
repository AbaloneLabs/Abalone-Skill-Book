# Accreditation Self-Study And Evidence

Accreditation is the process by which institutions and programs demonstrate that they meet the standards of quality and integrity required to operate, award degrees, and receive federal funding. For faculty, accreditation work centers on the self-study: the evidence-based argument that the institution or program meets each standard. The failure mode is treating accreditation as a paperwork exercise — gathering whatever evidence is handy, writing narrative that asserts compliance, and hoping the reviewers accept it — when in fact reviewers are experienced, skeptical, and trained to find the gaps between what is claimed and what is actually practiced. A self-study that asserts quality without evidence, or whose evidence contradicts the narrative, triggers scrutiny, recommendations, and sometimes sanctions. Good self-study work is honest, evidence-grounded, and internally coherent: it demonstrates compliance where it exists, acknowledges and addresses gaps where it doesn't, and uses the process to drive genuine improvement rather than to paper over problems.

Use this skill when preparing for accreditation, when writing or contributing to a self-study, and when diagnosing why a review is at risk. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a self-study that asserts rather than demonstrates, or that hides problems that reviewers will find.

## Core Rules

### Build The Self-Study On Evidence, Not Assertion

Every claim in a self-study must be backed by evidence that a reviewer can examine: assessment data, syllabi, policies, minutes, surveys, budgets, samples of student work. "We assess student learning" is an assertion; "here is our assessment plan, three cycles of results, and the curricular changes we made in response" is evidence. Reviewers are trained to distinguish the two, and self-studies that assert without evidence are flagged. Build the evidence base first, then write the narrative around it.

### Map Claims To Standards Explicitly

Accreditation standards are specific, and reviewers evaluate each standard against the evidence. Map your self-study explicitly to the standards: for each standard, state how you meet it and point to the evidence. Don't make reviewers hunt for the connection. A self-study that is organized around the institution's own categories rather than the standards forces reviewers to reconstruct the mapping, which frustrates them and increases the chance of a finding.

### Be Honest About Gaps And Address Them

No institution meets every standard perfectly, and reviewers know this. A self-study that claims perfection where gaps exist is less credible than one that acknowledges gaps and presents a credible plan to address them. Honesty about gaps — paired with evidence of action — demonstrates the culture of continuous improvement that accreditors actually want to see. Concealing gaps that reviewers will discover (and they will) damages credibility far more than acknowledging them.

### Align Narrative With Actual Practice

A common failure is a self-study narrative that describes the intended or official practice while the actual practice differs — policies that exist on paper but aren't followed, assessment that is reported but not actually used, shared governance that is described but bypassed. Reviewers check the narrative against reality through interviews, document review, and site visits. Misalignment between narrative and practice is a major source of adverse findings. Ensure the narrative describes what actually happens, and where actual practice falls short of policy, address the gap rather than describing the policy as if it were the practice.

### Engage Faculty Broadly In The Process

Accreditation is not the work of a single administrator or committee; it reflects the actual practices of the faculty, who implement curriculum, assessment, and governance. Engage faculty broadly in gathering evidence, validating the narrative, and understanding the standards. A self-study written by a few people that the rest of the faculty don't recognize will be contradicted in reviewer interviews, where faculty are asked whether the narrative matches their experience. Broad engagement ensures the self-study is accurate and that faculty can speak to it credibly.

### Use Assessment Data Credibly

Assessment of student learning is a central accreditation concern, and the most common weakness is assessment that is performed for compliance but not used for improvement. Reviewers look for the assessment loop: data collected, analyzed, acted upon, and re-assessed. Present assessment honestly — including results that show weakness — and demonstrate the changes made in response. Assessment that shows only success, or that is never acted upon, is read as performative and undermines credibility.

### Connect Accreditation To Genuine Improvement

The strongest self-studies use accreditation as a catalyst for genuine improvement: identifying weaknesses, making changes, and demonstrating progress. Treat the self-study as an opportunity to take stock and improve, not just to defend the status quo. Accreditors can tell the difference, and a self-study that shows a culture of honest self-assessment and improvement is far more compelling than one that defends against every implication of weakness.

### Prepare For The Site Visit Credibly

The site visit is where reviewers test the self-study against reality through interviews and observation. Prepare the institution honestly: make sure faculty, staff, and students can speak credibly to the practices described, ensure documents are available, and don't coach people to give scripted answers (reviewers detect this and it destroys credibility). The site visit should confirm a self-study that already reflects reality; it should not be a performance layered over a self-study that doesn't.

## Common Traps

- **Assertion without evidence.** "We meet this standard" without the data, documents, and examples that demonstrate it. Build the evidence base first.
- **Narrative organized for the institution, not the standards.** Forcing reviewers to reconstruct the mapping. Organize explicitly to the standards.
- **Concealing gaps.** Hiding weaknesses reviewers will find destroys credibility. Acknowledge gaps and present action plans.
- **Narrative-practice misalignment.** Describing official policy while actual practice differs. Reviewers check against reality; describe what actually happens.
- **Self-study written by a few, unrecognized by the many.** Faculty contradict the narrative in interviews. Engage broadly.
- **Performative assessment.** Assessment that shows only success or is never used for improvement. Show the loop: data, action, re-assessment.
- **Defending rather than improving.** Treating accreditation as a defense of the status quo rather than an opportunity to improve. Use it to drive improvement.
- **Scripted site-visit preparation.** Coaching people to give rehearsed answers. Reviewers detect performance; prepare for honest confirmation instead.

## Self-Check

- Is every claim in the self-study backed by specific, examinable evidence rather than mere assertion?
- Is the self-study organized and mapped explicitly to the accreditation standards, so reviewers can find the connection?
- Are gaps acknowledged honestly and paired with credible action plans, rather than concealed?
- Does the narrative describe actual practice — confirmed by faculty and documents — rather than official policy that isn't followed?
- Was the self-study developed with broad faculty engagement, so that faculty can speak to it credibly in interviews?
- Does the assessment presentation show the full loop — data, analysis, action, re-assessment — including results that revealed weakness?
- Is accreditation being used to drive genuine improvement, not just to defend the status quo?
- Is site-visit preparation honest — ensuring the visit confirms a self-study that reflects reality — rather than a performance layered over gaps?
