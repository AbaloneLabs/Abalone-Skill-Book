# Course Selection Advising

Course selection is where a student's stated goals meet the realities of requirements, prerequisites, workload, sequencing, and the limits of a semester. Students often select courses by assembling what fits their schedule, what friends are taking, or what seems interesting, without considering whether the combination is coherent, feasible, or serves their trajectory. Advisors who simply approve lists miss the chance to prevent avoidable problems; advisors who impose their own preferences override the student's ownership. Good course selection advising helps students build a schedule that meets requirements, serves their goals, is realistic in workload, and leaves room for the learning and life that happen outside the classroom.

Use this skill before advising a student on course selection, reviewing a proposed schedule, planning across semesters, or diagnosing why a student is struggling with their load. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing course advice that is requirement-focused but incoherent, or that ignores workload, sequencing, and the student's actual goals.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Student's Goals And Trajectory

Course selection should serve the student's goals, not just fill requirements. Understand:

- the student's major, minor, or intended program;
- their post-degree direction, graduate, professional, workforce;
- their interests and what they want to explore;
- their strengths and areas for growth;
- their timeline, including any acceleration or extension;
- prerequisite chains that constrain options.

A schedule that meets requirements but ignores goals produces a compliant transcript and an unprepared student. Start from goals and work back to courses.

### Map Requirements Before Selecting Courses

Requirements, major, minor, general education, program-specific, constrain selection and must be tracked. Map:

- which requirements remain;
- which courses satisfy each;
- prerequisites and when they are offered;
- sequencing constraints, what must precede what;
- the credit count needed and the timeline to completion;
- any minimum grade or GPA requirements.

A student who discovers a missing requirement late faces delayed graduation or summer overload. Mapping requirements early prevents this.

### Balance Requirements, Interests, And Exploration

A good schedule balances three demands:

- requirements that must be met for progression;
- courses that serve the student's specific goals;
- exploration that broadens the student's education.

A schedule of only requirements produces a transactional education. A schedule of only interests may not graduate the student. Balance deliberately, and protect some space for exploration, especially early in a program.

### Assess Workload Realistically

The most common advising failure is approving schedules whose combined workload is unrealistic. Assess:

- the number of credits and whether it matches the student's capacity;
- the combination of demanding courses in the same term;
- lab, studio, clinical, and practicum components that consume time;
- writing-intensive and project-based courses that peak together;
- the student's other commitments, work, caregiving, commute;
- the student's prior performance under similar loads.

A student who has historically struggled with four STEM courses should not be approved for five. Use the student's track record as evidence, not aspiration.

### Respect Prerequisite And Sequencing Chains

Many courses depend on prerequisites offered in specific terms. Respect the chains:

- identify courses whose prerequisites are offered rarely;
- sequence so prerequisites are completed before advanced courses;
- avoid creating bottlenecks by deferring rare-offered prerequisites;
- plan multi-semester sequences, especially in lab sciences and languages;
- account for courses offered only in fall or only in spring.

A student who defers a fall-only prerequisite may wait a full year. Sequencing awareness prevents this.

### Consider The Student's Whole Life

Course selection exists alongside the student's other commitments. Consider:

- work hours and whether the student can sustain the load;
- caregiving and family responsibilities;
- commute time and class spacing;
- extracurricular and leadership commitments;
- health and disability considerations;
- financial constraints that may require a heavier or lighter load.

A schedule that ignores the student's life produces overload and withdrawal. A schedule that accounts for life is sustainable.

### Address Overload And Underload

Students err in both directions. Address both:

- overload, too many credits or too many demanding courses, risks burnout, poor performance, and withdrawal;
- underload, too few credits or too many easy courses, risks delayed progression and wasted tuition;
- the right load matches the student's capacity and goals for the term.

Use the student's history as a guide. A student who thrived at 15 credits can consider 16 or 17; a student who struggled at 12 should not take 18.

### Plan Across Semesters, Not Just One

Single-semester advising produces schedules that do not chain coherently. Plan across semesters:

- sketch a multi-semester plan for the major;
- identify when rare-offered courses appear;
- ensure prerequisite chains complete on time;
- leave flexibility for study abroad, internships, or changes of direction;
- revisit the plan each term as circumstances change.

Multi-semester planning catches problems single-term advising misses, like a required course that conflicts with study abroad in junior year.

### Help The Student Own The Decision

The student, not the advisor, lives with the schedule. Help them own it:

- explain the reasoning behind recommendations;
- present options with trade-offs rather than directives;
- let the student make the final choice, including choices you would not make;
- document the plan so the student can track progress;
- make clear the student can revise.

Ownership produces commitment. Directives produce compliance that dissolves when the advisor is absent.

### Refer To Resources For Specific Needs

Some course selection needs exceed general advising. Refer to:

- the major department for sequence and specialization advising;
- disability services for accommodation-related scheduling;
- financial aid for load requirements tied to aid;
- international student services for visa-related load constraints;
- pre-professional advisors for graduate, medical, law preparation.

Referral connects the student to specialized knowledge. It is part of good advising, not a substitute for it.

### Document And Follow Up

Keep a record of the advising plan and follow up:

- note the agreed plan and its rationale;
- check in after the term on how the load worked;
- revise future plans based on actual experience;
- flag students who are falling behind for early intervention.

Documentation and follow-up turn one-time advising into a trajectory of informed decisions.

## Common Traps

### Approving Without Understanding Goals

Approving course lists without understanding the student's goals produces a compliant but unprepared transcript. Start from goals.

### Ignoring Workload

Approving schedules whose combined demand is unrealistic causes burnout and withdrawal. Assess workload.

### Single-Term Planning

Advising one term at a time misses sequencing and bottleneck problems. Plan across semesters.

### Ignoring Rare-Offered Courses

Deferring fall-only or spring-only prerequisites creates year-long delays. Track offerings.

### Ignoring The Student's Life

Schedules that ignore work, caregiving, and commute are unsustainable. Consider the whole life.

### Allowing Overload Or Underload By Default

Failing to address loads that are too heavy or too light harms progression. Address both.

### Directive Advising

Imposing the advisor's preferences overrides student ownership. Present options and let the student decide.

### No Follow-Up

One-time advising without follow-up misses problems and revisions. Document and check in.

### Failure To Refer

Trying to advise on specialized needs misses better resources. Refer to specialists.

### Schedule-Only Focus

Focusing on what fits the calendar rather than what serves goals produces incoherent schedules. Prioritize goals and sequencing.

## Self-Check

- Have you understood the student's goals, trajectory, strengths, and constraints before recommending courses?
- Have requirements, prerequisites, sequencing, and offering patterns been mapped across remaining terms?
- Does the schedule balance requirements, goal-serving courses, and exploration?
- Has the combined workload been assessed against the student's capacity and track record?
- Are prerequisite and sequencing chains respected, including rarely offered courses?
- Does the schedule account for the student's work, caregiving, commute, health, and finances?
- Have overload and underload been addressed relative to the student's history?
- Is there a multi-semester plan, not just a single-term schedule?
- Does the student own the decision, with options and trade-offs rather than directives?
- Are specialized needs referred to major advisors, disability services, financial aid, or international services?
- Is the plan documented and followed up so advising becomes a trajectory of informed decisions?
- Would this schedule, if completed, leave the student on track and prepared for their next step?
