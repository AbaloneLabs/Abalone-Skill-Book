# Degree Requirements Navigation

Degree requirements are a complex, often opaque system of rules that determine whether a student graduates. They include major and minor requirements, general education, credit totals, residency rules, GPA thresholds, timing rules, and program-specific conditions, and they change across catalog years. Students routinely discover missing requirements late, sometimes after they expected to graduate, because no one helped them navigate the system accurately. Advisors who treat requirements as the student's responsibility, or who interpret policy loosely, set students up for delayed graduation, extra cost, and sometimes the loss of a job or graduate admission that depended on timely completion.

Use this skill before helping a student plan degree completion, interpreting requirements, handling transfer or exception cases, or auditing a student's progress toward graduation. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing advice that is vague, outdated, or non-compliant, any of which can cause a student to miss requirements they believed were met.

## Core Rules

### Use The Correct Catalog And Program Version

Requirements vary by catalog year and program version, and using the wrong one produces incorrect advice. Confirm:

- the student's catalog year, often their entry year or a chosen subsequent year;
- the specific program version, major, concentration, minor;
- any catalog changes that affect the student;
- program-specific requirements that differ from the general catalog;
- whether the student has approved substitutions or waivers.

Advising from memory or a general impression, rather than the specific catalog, is a leading cause of incorrect advice. Always confirm the governing requirements.

### Audit Progress Against All Requirement Categories

Degree completion requires meeting multiple categories of requirements. Audit against all:

- major requirements, including core, electives, and capstone;
- minor requirements if applicable;
- general education or distribution requirements;
- total credit count, including upper-division minimums;
- residency requirements, credits taken at the institution;
- GPA thresholds, overall, major, and sometimes general education;
- timing rules, such as senior residency or final-year credits;
- program-specific conditions, theses, recitals, internships, certifications.

Missing any category delays graduation. Audit comprehensively, not just the major.

### Use The Official Degree Audit Tool

Most institutions provide a degree audit system. Use it:

- run the audit and read it carefully;
- understand what it flags as met, in progress, and unmet;
- recognize its limitations, it may not catch program-specific conditions;
- reconcile the audit with your own check;
- do not rely on the audit alone for complex cases.

The audit is a tool, not an oracle. It can be wrong, especially with transfer credit, substitutions, or recent policy changes. Verify its output.

### Handle Transfer And Alternative Credit Accurately

Transfer and alternative credit, AP, IB, dual enrollment, military, create frequent errors. Handle accurately:

- confirm what transferred and how it applies, not just that it transferred;
- identify equivalencies and whether they satisfy specific requirements;
- check for residency implications of transfer credit;
- watch for credit that transferred as elective but not as requirement;
- know the institution's policies on transfer credit limits.

A student with transferred credits that satisfy electives but not requirements may appear on track while actually missing core requirements. Verify how transfer credit applies, not just that it was accepted.

### Document Substitutions, Waivers, And Exceptions

Where requirements are modified by substitution, waiver, or exception, document them:

- ensure approvals are in writing and on file;
- confirm the approval is recorded in the system the audit uses;
- keep copies the student can access;
- verify the exception is still valid under current policy.

Verbal approvals that are not recorded disappear, leaving the student apparently unmet. Documentation protects the student across staff turnover and system changes.

### Plan For Graduation Multiple Terms Ahead

Graduation planning should begin multiple terms before completion. Plan:

- map remaining requirements across the remaining terms;
- identify any course offered only in specific terms;
- check for prerequisites of remaining requirements;
- confirm the application-for-degree deadline and process;
- identify any non-course requirements, exams, theses, certifications, with their timelines.

Late planning discovers problems too late to solve without delay. Early planning creates time to address gaps.

### Know The Limits Of Your Authority

Advisors can interpret and explain requirements but usually cannot change them. Know:

- which decisions are within your discretion;
- which require department or program approval;
- which require a petition or committee;
- which require the registrar or dean;
- when to refer rather than advise.

Advising beyond your authority, by promising an exception you cannot grant, sets up the student for a later reversal. Refer decisions to those who hold them.

### Communicate Requirements Clearly And In Writing

Students forget verbal advice, especially complex requirement explanations. Communicate in writing:

- summarize the audit results and remaining requirements;
- note the plan for the remaining terms;
- flag any risks or contingencies;
- provide the student a copy;
- reference the official audit and catalog.

Written communication creates a record the student can revisit and protects both student and advisor if questions arise later.

### Anticipate Common Pitfalls

Certain pitfalls recur and should be checked proactively:

- courses that look like they satisfy a requirement but do not, similar numbers, cross-listed confusion;
- requirements with hidden conditions, minimum grade, must be taken together, residency;
- GPA thresholds in the major that differ from overall GPA;
- timing rules that require certain credits in the final terms;
- caps on certain course types, pass-fail, online, transfer, that can be exceeded unknowingly.

Knowing these pitfalls lets you check for them specifically rather than discovering them at graduation.

### Update Your Knowledge As Policy Changes

Degree requirements and policies change. Keep current:

- attend advisor training and updates;
- read policy change announcements;
- consult the registrar and catalog for current rules;
- do not rely on how things were when you started advising;
- verify anything uncertain before stating it as fact.

Outdated advice is a major source of graduation problems. Currency is a professional obligation.

### Refer Students To The Right Offices For Specific Processes

Many degree processes involve other offices. Refer appropriately:

- registrar for official audits, graduation application, transcript issues;
- financial aid for aid-related load and progression questions;
- international student services for visa-related requirements;
- disability services for accommodation-related requirements;
- department for major-specific exceptions and approvals.

Referral ensures the student gets authoritative answers and that decisions are properly recorded.

## Common Traps

### Wrong Catalog Or Program Version

Advising from memory or the wrong catalog produces incorrect advice. Confirm the governing requirements.

### Partial Audit

Checking only the major misses general education, residency, GPA, and timing requirements. Audit all categories.

### Blind Trust In The Audit Tool

Degree audits can be wrong, especially with transfer credit and exceptions. Verify their output.

### Transfer Credit Misapplied

Credits that transferred as electives but not requirements leave students apparently on track but actually short. Verify application.

### Undocumented Exceptions

Verbal approvals not recorded disappear. Document substitutions and waivers.

### Late Graduation Planning

Discovering missing requirements the term of graduation causes delay. Plan multiple terms ahead.

### Advising Beyond Authority

Promising exceptions you cannot grant sets up later reversal. Refer decisions to those who hold them.

### Verbal-Only Communication

Students forget complex verbal advice. Communicate in writing.

### Ignoring Common Pitfalls

Failing to check for hidden conditions, GPA thresholds, and caps discovers problems at graduation. Check proactively.

### Outdated Knowledge

Relying on how things were produces incorrect advice. Stay current.

### Single-Office Advising

Failing to refer to registrar, financial aid, and specialized offices gives incomplete answers. Refer appropriately.

## Self-Check

- Have you confirmed the correct catalog year and program version governing the student's requirements?
- Has progress been audited against all requirement categories, major, minor, general education, credits, residency, GPA, timing, program-specific?
- Has the official degree audit been run and its output verified against your own check?
- Has transfer and alternative credit been confirmed for how it applies, not just that it was accepted?
- Are substitutions, waivers, and exceptions documented in writing and recorded in the system?
- Has graduation been planned multiple terms ahead with term-by-term mapping of remaining requirements?
- Do you know which decisions are within your authority and which require referral or petition?
- Are requirement explanations communicated to the student in writing with a copy retained?
- Have common pitfalls, hidden conditions, GPA thresholds, caps, residency, been checked proactively?
- Is your knowledge of requirements and policies current rather than based on prior practice?
- Are students referred to registrar, financial aid, international services, and departments for processes beyond general advising?
- Could you defend your advice to the student, the registrar, and the department as accurate and current?
