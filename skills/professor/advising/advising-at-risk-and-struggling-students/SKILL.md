# Advising At-Risk And Struggling Students

Academic advising is often imagined as helping well-prepared students choose courses and plan degrees, but some of the most consequential advising happens when a student is in trouble. A student failing multiple courses, placed on probation, losing financial aid, considering withdrawal, or quietly disengaging is making decisions under stress that will shape their academic and financial future for years. Advisors in these moments hold significant influence, and the quality of their response determines whether the student recovers, leaves on their own terms, or disappears in a way that benefits no one. The failure modes are symmetric: an advisor who minimizes the problem leaves the student to spiral, while an advisor who panics or over-manages strips the student of agency in an already disempowering moment. Good advising of at-risk students is calm, honest, option-focused, and grounded in institutional reality.

Use this skill before advising a student who is academically at risk, on probation, failing, losing aid, considering withdrawal or leave, or showing warning signs of disengagement. The goal is to prevent the agent from minimizing serious academic risk, overpromising outcomes the institution will not support, or pushing the student toward a decision that serves the advisor or the institution rather than the student.

## Core Rules

### Take Warning Signs Seriously And Intervene Early

Academic risk rarely appears suddenly; it accumulates through visible signals. Watch for:

- missed classes, assignments, or advising appointments;
- deteriorating grades across multiple courses;
- sudden drops in engagement or communication;
- expressions of hopelessness, overwhelm, or self-doubt;
- financial or personal crises disclosed in passing;
- patterns of withdrawal from activities or relationships.

Early intervention, a check-in, a conversation, a revised plan, is far more effective than waiting until failure is official. Advisors who wait for the student to ask often wait until recovery is much harder.

### Get An Accurate Picture Of The Situation Before Acting

Before advising, understand the full picture. Gather:

- current grades and academic standing;
- the specific requirements or standards at risk, probation, SAP, major GPA;
- financial aid implications of the current trajectory;
- the student's other commitments, work, family, health, finances;
- what the student has already tried;
- relevant institutional policies and deadlines.

Advising on partial information produces plans that fail. Build the full picture, with the student's input, before recommending action.

### Be Honest About The Reality Without Crushing Hope

At-risk students need honesty and hope, and balancing them is the core advising skill. Practices:

- name the situation clearly without minimizing, "you are currently failing three courses";
- avoid false reassurance that the situation is fine when it is not;
- avoid catastrophizing that removes the student's sense of agency;
- distinguish what is recoverable from what is not, at this point;
- frame the conversation around options and next steps, not verdicts.

Honesty without hope paralyzes; hope without honesty deceives. Offer both.

### Understand The Institutional Stakes And Deadlines

Academic risk has institutional consequences that operate on deadlines. Know:

- probation and dismissal thresholds and timelines;
- satisfactory academic progress, SAP, rules for financial aid;
- withdrawal, drop, and leave deadlines and their transcript consequences;
- grade forgiveness, repeat, or replacement policies;
- appeal and exception processes and their criteria;
- residency and progression rules that affect aid or status.

Advising that ignores these mechanics produces plans that the institution will not honor. Know the rules and their deadlines precisely.

### Lay Out Real Options Rather Than A Single Directive

Students in trouble benefit from understanding their actual options, with trade-offs. Lay out:

- recovering in place: what it would take to pass current courses and regain standing;
- strategic withdrawal: dropping a course to protect GPA or aid, with consequences;
- taking a leave or withdrawing from the term: with implications for aid, housing, visa, return;
- changing course load or major: to a more sustainable path;
- accessing support: tutoring, counseling, accommodation, financial aid review;
- the realistic probability of each option succeeding.

Present options with honest trade-offs and let the student decide. Directives override agency; options restore it.

### Protect Financial Aid And Visa Status Deliberately

For many students, financial aid or visa status is the binding constraint. Attend to:

- how current performance affects SAP and aid eligibility;
- how withdrawal or reduced load affects aid, housing, and loan status;
- for international students, how changes affect visa and enrollment requirements;
- deadlines for actions that protect or restore aid;
- the financial consequences of each option over time.

A student who protects their GPA by withdrawing but loses their aid may have traded a short-term problem for a long-term one. Weigh financial and immigration consequences explicitly.

### Connect To Support Rather Than Solving Solo

Advisors cannot meet every need, and at-risk students usually need more than academic planning. Refer and connect:

- tutoring, writing, and quantitative support for academic skill gaps;
- counseling and mental health services for distress;
- disability services for undiagnosed or unaccommodated barriers;
- financial aid and basic needs support for material insecurity;
- student affairs or the dean of students for broader intervention;
- specialized advisors for international, veteran, or first-generation needs.

Referral is part of good advising. Stay involved while connecting the student to the help they need.

### Respect The Student's Agency And Decision

The student, not the advisor, lives with the consequences. Respect that:

- the student may choose an option the advisor would not;
- the student may need to pause, leave, or change direction;
- pushing the student toward the advisor's preferred outcome overrides their agency;
- a decision to leave may be the right one, even if disappointing;
- the advisor's role is to inform and support, not to decide.

Advising supports agency; it does not replace it. The student owns the decision.

### Document The Conversation And Plan

At-risk advising has institutional and sometimes legal weight. Document:

- the situation as understood;
- the options presented and their consequences;
- the student's decision and rationale;
- referrals made and follow-up planned;
- deadlines and next steps.

Documentation protects the student, the advisor, and the institution, and it ensures continuity if the student returns or if the situation escalates.

### Follow Up And Sustain Support

A single advising conversation rarely resolves academic risk. Follow up:

- check on whether the plan is being executed;
- monitor grades and standing through the term;
- adjust the plan as circumstances change;
- remain available if the student needs to revisit the decision;
- connect early-alert systems where they exist.

Students who feel abandoned after the conversation learn that support was performative. Sustained follow-up makes it real.

### Know When To Escalate

Some situations exceed the advising role. Escalate when there is:

- risk of harm to self or others;
- disclosure of harassment, assault, or discrimination triggering reporting duties;
- severe mental health crisis;
- financial or basic-needs emergency requiring institutional response;
- academic integrity issues;
- situations requiring exception or appeal beyond the advisor's authority.

Know your duties and the relevant offices. Escalation is not abandonment; it is how serious situations get the response they require.

## Common Traps

### Waiting For The Student To Ask

Waiting until the student asks for help often means waiting until recovery is hard. Intervene on warning signs.

### Advising On Partial Information

Recommending action without the full academic, financial, and personal picture produces plans that fail. Gather the picture first.

### Minimizing Or Catastrophizing

Either minimizing serious risk or catastrophizing beyond recovery distorts the student's decisions. Be honest with hope.

### Ignoring Institutional Mechanics

Advising that ignores probation, SAP, deadlines, and aid rules produces plans the institution will not honor. Know the rules.

### Single Directive Instead Of Options

Pushing one course of action overrides the student's agency. Lay out options with trade-offs.

### Overlooking Aid And Visa Consequences

Focusing only on academics while ignoring financial aid or visa status trades one problem for another. Weigh all consequences.

### Trying To Solve Everything Solo

Failing to refer to tutoring, counseling, financial aid, or disability services leaves needs unmet. Connect to support.

### Overriding The Student's Decision

Pushing the student toward the advisor's preferred outcome disrespects their agency. Inform and support their choice.

### No Documentation

Failing to document the conversation, options, and plan risks continuity and protection. Document.

### One-Shot Advising

Treating a single conversation as resolution leaves the student unsupported. Follow up.

### Failing To Escalate

Handling serious situations informally to avoid discomfort can violate duties and endanger the student. Escalate when required.

## Self-Check

- Have warning signs been taken seriously and addressed early rather than awaited?
- Is the full academic, financial, personal, and institutional picture understood before advising?
- Is the reality named honestly while preserving the student's agency and hope?
- Are the institutional stakes, probation, SAP, deadlines, aid, and visa, understood and accounted for?
- Are real options with trade-offs presented rather than a single directive?
- Have financial aid and visa consequences been weighed alongside academic ones?
- Has the student been connected to appropriate support rather than the advisor solving everything solo?
- Is the student's agency respected, including the possibility that leaving or changing direction is the right choice?
- Is the conversation, options, decision, referrals, and plan documented?
- Is support sustained through follow-up rather than ending after one conversation?
- Are situations requiring escalation, risk, misconduct, crisis, recognized and referred to the right offices?
