# Grading Consistency

Grading is supposed to measure student learning, but in practice it also measures who graded the work, when it was graded, and in what order. Without deliberate process, grading varies by grader, drifts across a grading session, is influenced by fatigue and mood, and is shaped by biases the grader does not recognize. Inconsistent grading is not just unfair to students; it undermines the validity of every inference drawn from the grade, from course standing to program readiness. Consistency is a property of the grading process, not of the grader's good intentions.

Use this skill before grading student work, calibrating multiple graders, designing a grading workflow, or diagnosing why grading varies. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing grades that reflect grader variability as much as student learning.

## Core Rules

### Grade Against A Rubric, Not Impression

Impression-based grading is the largest source of inconsistency. Two graders with different impressions, or one grader whose impression shifts across a session, will produce different scores for equivalent work.

Practices:

- use a rubric with observable descriptors;
- score each criterion before assigning an overall judgment;
- resist the urge to adjust based on overall feel, which reintroduces impression;
- document the basis for borderline decisions;
- keep the rubric visible while grading.

A rubric does not eliminate judgment, but it constrains judgment to defined dimensions and makes disagreement discussable.

### Calibrate Graders Before Independent Grading

When multiple graders, instructor, TAs, peer reviewers, work on the same assignment, calibration is essential. Without it, students are graded against different standards depending on who reads their work.

Calibration process:

- select sample papers spanning the performance range;
- have all graders grade the same papers independently;
- compare scores and discuss disagreements;
- refine rubric language where graders diverge;
- re-grade until graders are within an agreed tolerance;
- document calibration decisions for reference.

Calibration is not a one-time event. Re-calibrate at the start of each major assignment and when new graders join.

### Use Anonymous Grading To Reduce Bias

Knowing whose paper you are grading influences the grade, even unconsciously. Prior performance, identity, likeability, and prior conflicts all shape impression.

Practices:

- have students submit without names, using ID numbers;
- use the LMS anonymity feature where available;
- avoid grading in an order that reveals identity, such as alphabetical;
- keep grading separate from any contextual information that is not relevant to the work.

Anonymous grading is one of the highest-leverage, lowest-cost fairness practices available.

### Manage Order Effects And Fatigue

Grading quality degrades across a session. Papers graded early in a long session may be scored differently than those graded when the grader is tired, and the first papers often set an anchor that drifts.

Practices:

- grade in focused sessions of limited length;
- take breaks and re-read anchor papers after breaks;
- randomize the order of papers rather than grading alphabetically;
- mix strong and weak papers rather than clustering;
- re-grade a sample from early in the session at the end to check drift.

Order effects are subtle but measurable. Structuring the session reduces them.

### Watch For Central Tendency And Severity Bias

Two common grader biases distort distributions. Central tendency bias clusters all grades in the middle, failing to distinguish excellent from poor work. Severity bias grades everything harshly, producing depressed scores unrelated to actual quality.

Detection:

- review the distribution after grading a batch;
- compare your distributions across assignments and to prior offerings;
- compare to co-graders if calibrated;
- look for criteria where everyone scores the same level.

If detected, recalibrate and re-grade affected work rather than adjusting scores arbitrarily.

### Apply Partial Credit Consistently

Partial credit on problem sets, code, and multi-step questions is a major source of inconsistency. Without a scheme, similar errors receive different deductions.

Practices:

- decide the partial credit scheme before grading;
- apply it uniformly, for example, method error minus X, arithmetic error minus Y;
- note common errors and their deductions as you grade;
- review borderline cases against the scheme;
- have a second grader check a sample for consistency.

Ad hoc partial credit rewards students whose errors happened to match the grader's mood.

### Separate Grading From Feedback Tone

The grade and the feedback serve different purposes. The grade measures the work; the feedback helps the student improve. Tone that is harsh in feedback can make a fair grade feel punitive, and tone that is overly kind can make an unfair grade feel acceptable.

Practices:

- keep feedback specific and tied to rubric descriptors;
- address the work, not the student;
- balance identification of strengths and areas to improve;
- avoid sarcasm, shaming, or excessive praise;
- let the rubric carry the judgment so feedback can focus on growth.

Consistent tone supports consistency of perception across students.

### Handle Borderline Cases With A Stated Rule

Borderline cases, just below a cutoff, are where inconsistency and bias most often enter. Decide in advance how to handle them.

Options:

- a stated rounding rule applied uniformly;
- re-grading borderline work blindly;
- using a second criterion to break ties;
- defaulting in the student's favor with a documented rule.

Whichever rule is chosen, apply it uniformly. Ad hoc decisions at borders invite bias and dispute.

### Document The Grading Process

For assignments with significant weight, document the process: rubric, calibration notes, partial credit scheme, borderline rule, and any adjustments. This documentation supports regrade requests, identifies process improvements, and provides evidence if grading is challenged.

### Review Distributions For Disparate Impact

After grading, examine whether scores correlate with identity, section, prior performance, or submission order. Patterns of disparity may indicate bias in the rubric, the prompt, or the grading process. Investigate before assuming the scores reflect learning.

## Common Traps

### Impression Grading

Grading by feel varies by grader and session. Use a rubric.

### No Calibration

Multiple graders without calibration apply different standards. Calibrate before independent grading.

### Identity Influence

Knowing the student shifts the grade. Use anonymous grading.

### Fatigue And Order Effects

Long sessions and fixed order produce drift. Structure sessions and randomize order.

### Central Tendency Or Severity

Clustering or harshness distorts distributions. Detect and recalibrate.

### Ad Hoc Partial Credit

Inconsistent deductions reward chance. Use a stated scheme.

### Harsh Feedback On Fair Grades

Punitive tone makes fair grades feel unjust. Keep feedback work-focused.

### Arbitrary Borderline Decisions

Ad hoc cutoff decisions invite bias. Apply a stated rule uniformly.

### Undocumented Process

Without records, regrade requests and improvements are harder. Document the process.

### Unexamined Disparities

Score patterns by identity may indicate bias. Review distributions.

## Self-Check

- Is all grading done against a rubric with observable descriptors rather than overall impression?
- When multiple graders are involved, has calibration produced agreement within tolerance on sample papers?
- Is grading anonymous where feasible to reduce bias from identity and prior performance?
- Are grading sessions structured to manage fatigue, with randomized order and drift checks?
- Have distributions been reviewed for central tendency or severity bias and recalibrated if found?
- Is partial credit applied through a stated scheme rather than ad hoc deductions?
- Is feedback tone work-focused and balanced, so fair grades do not feel punitive?
- Are borderline cases handled by a stated rule applied uniformly rather than ad hoc?
- Is the grading process documented for regrade response and process improvement?
- Have score distributions been reviewed for disparate impact by identity, section, or submission order?
- Would two graders following this process reach similar scores for the same work?
