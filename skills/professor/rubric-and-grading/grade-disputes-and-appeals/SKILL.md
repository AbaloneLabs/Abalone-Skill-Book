# Grade Disputes And Appeals

Grade disputes are inevitable in any course where grades carry consequence. Students will sometimes believe a grade is wrong, unfair, or based on criteria they did not understand. How an instructor handles these disputes affects student trust, the defensibility of the grade, the instructor's workload, and the climate of the course. A fair, consistent, well-communicated process resolves most disputes at the lowest possible level, protects students who have legitimate concerns, protects instructors from capricious challenges, and stays within institutional policy. An ad hoc, defensive, or inconsistent process escalates disputes, erodes trust, and creates exposure.

Use this skill before responding to a regrade request, designing a regrade process, communicating about grading, or handling an appeal. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing responses that are dismissive, inconsistent, or outside institutional procedure, any of which can turn a manageable request into a formal conflict.

## Core Rules

### Establish A Regrade Process In The Syllabus

A regrade process should exist before any dispute arises, published in the syllabus so students know the path. Surprising students with process after they are upset increases friction.

A clear process includes:

- the window for requesting regrades, typically one to two weeks after return;
- the required format, written request identifying the specific concern;
- the scope, re-examination of the specific question, not the whole exam;
- the possibility that the grade may go down as well as up;
- the path to formal appeal if the instructor's decision is disputed;
- the institutional policy that governs final appeals.

Publishing the process signals that grading is open to review and channels disputes into a consistent path.

### Require Specific, Written Requests

Vague regrade requests, "I think I deserve more points," are hard to address and often driven by emotion rather than identified error. Require students to submit a written request that specifies:

- the question or criterion in dispute;
- why they believe the grade is incorrect;
- what evidence or reasoning supports their position;
- what they are requesting.

A written, specific request forces the student to identify the actual concern, filters out impulsive requests, and gives the instructor a clear basis for review. It also creates a record.

### Define The Scope Of Re-Examination

A regrade is a re-examination of the disputed question against the rubric, not a renegotiation of the whole grade. State the scope clearly:

- only the identified question or criterion is re-examined;
- the rest of the grade stands;
- the re-examination may result in a higher, lower, or unchanged score;
- the rubric is the standard, not the student's expectation.

Re-examining only the disputed item protects against the practice of regrades that only go up, which is inconsistent and invites strategic requests. Re-examination can lower a score if the original grade was generous, though this should be used judiciously and explained.

### Re-Grade Against The Rubric, Not The Student

When re-examining, grade against the rubric and the original grading standard, without regard to who the student is or how they have performed otherwise. Practices:

- re-grade anonymously if possible, masking the identity;
- apply the same rubric and partial credit scheme used originally;
- compare to other papers graded at the same level;
- document the basis for the revised decision;
- avoid being influenced by the student's tone, persistence, or prior standing.

Identity-blind re-grading protects both the student and the instructor from bias claims.

### Respond Promptly And In Writing

Delay breeds suspicion and escalation. Respond within the stated window, in writing, with the basis for the decision. A written response:

- acknowledges the specific concern;
- states the decision, upheld, raised, or lowered;
- explains the reasoning against the rubric;
- references the standard applied;
- notes the path to formal appeal if the student remains dissatisfied.

A written response creates a record, reduces miscommunication, and demonstrates that the decision was considered rather than dismissive.

### Distinguish Errors From Disagreement About Standards

Some regrade requests identify genuine errors: an addition mistake, a misread answer, a question graded against the wrong rubric row. These should be corrected promptly and gratefully. Other requests are disagreements about the standard, the student believes their work met a higher level than the rubric supports. These require explanation, not concession.

Conflating the two, either by refusing to fix clear errors or by raising grades to avoid conflict, undermines the integrity of grading. Identify which kind of request it is and respond accordingly.

### Keep The Door Open Without Inviting Renegotiation

A fair process invites legitimate review without inviting endless negotiation. Balance:

- welcome identification of genuine errors;
- explain standards clearly for disagreements;
- apply the regrade window consistently;
- decline requests outside the window or outside the scope;
- do not reward persistence with grade inflation.

Students learn quickly whether persistence changes grades. If it does, requests proliferate and grading loses meaning.

### Know The Institutional Appeal Path

The instructor's decision is usually not the final word. Institutional policy typically provides a path to appeal to a chair, a committee, or a dean, often with strict timelines and required documentation. Know this path so you can:

- inform students of their appeal rights;
- provide documentation that supports the institutional review;
- avoid actions that violate policy, such as retaliating or changing a grade outside process;
- cooperate with formal review when it occurs.

Ignoring or circumventing institutional process creates risk for the instructor and the institution.

### Maintain Professional Tone Under Pressure

Grade disputes can be emotional. Students may be anxious, angry, or confrontational. The instructor's tone sets the climate. Practices:

- respond to the concern, not the tone;
- avoid defensiveness or sarcasm;
- acknowledge the student's stake without conceding the standard;
- keep communication in the established channel;
- involve a third party, chair or ombud, if a discussion becomes unproductive.

Professional tone de-escalates most disputes. Defensive or dismissive tone escalates them.

### Use Disputes To Improve The Process

Patterns in regrade requests reveal problems with the exam, the rubric, or the teaching. Track:

- questions that generate frequent disputes, suggesting ambiguity;
- criteria students systematically misunderstand, suggesting unclear language;
- grading errors that recurred, suggesting process gaps;
- outcomes of disputes to detect bias.

Use these patterns to revise the exam, rubric, and grading process for the next offering. A dispute is feedback about the assessment system, not just about one student.

## Common Traps

### No Published Process

Surprising students with regrade rules after a dispute increases friction. Publish the process in the syllabus.

### Accepting Vague Requests

"I deserve more" is unactionable. Require specific written requests.

### Regrades That Only Go Up

A policy where grades only rise invites strategic requests and is inconsistent. Re-examination can lower a score.

### Identity-Influenced Re-Grading

Knowing the student biases the re-grade. Re-grade anonymously.

### Delayed Or Verbal-Only Responses

Delay breeds suspicion; verbal responses create no record. Respond promptly in writing.

### Conflating Errors With Standard Disputes

Refusing to fix clear errors or conceding standard disputes both undermine integrity. Distinguish the two.

### Rewarding Persistence

If persistence raises grades, requests proliferate. Apply the process consistently.

### Ignoring Institutional Appeal Path

Circumventing policy creates risk. Know and follow the appeal process.

### Defensive Tone

Sarcasm and defensiveness escalate disputes. Maintain professional tone.

### Ignoring Patterns

Recurring disputes signal assessment problems. Use patterns to improve.

## Self-Check

- Is a regrade process published in the syllabus with window, format, scope, and appeal path?
- Are regrade requests required to be specific and written, identifying the disputed item and reasoning?
- Is the scope of re-examination limited to the disputed question against the rubric, with the possibility of a lower score?
- Is re-grading done against the rubric, anonymously where possible, without identity influence?
- Are responses prompt, written, and explanatory, with the basis for the decision and the appeal path noted?
- Are genuine errors distinguished from standard disagreements and handled accordingly?
- Is the process applied consistently so persistence does not produce grade inflation?
- Is the institutional appeal path known and followed, with documentation provided for formal review?
- Is professional tone maintained under pressure, with escalation to a third party when needed?
- Are dispute patterns tracked and used to improve the exam, rubric, and grading process?
- Could a reviewer examining this process conclude it was fair, consistent, and within policy?
