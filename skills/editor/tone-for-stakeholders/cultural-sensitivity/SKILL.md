# Cultural Sensitivity

Documents for multicultural or international audiences cross cultural boundaries, and what works in one culture may confuse, offend, or exclude in another. Idioms that do not translate, examples that assume one context, humor that misfires, or framing that reflects ethnocentric assumptions can alienate readers and undermine the document's effectiveness. Cultural sensitivity is the discipline of ensuring documents respect cultural diversity and reach audiences across boundaries. Editors who edit for their own cultural context without examining how the content lands elsewhere miss the barriers that prevent documents from serving global or diverse readers.

Use this skill when editing business or professional documents for multicultural or international audiences to verify cultural sensitivity. It covers language, examples, references, and framing. The goal is a document that respects and reaches readers across cultural differences.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Cultural Scope Of The Audience

Cultural sensitivity begins with knowing the audience's cultural scope. A document for a single culture can use that culture's references; one for multiple cultures or an unknown international audience needs broader sensitivity. Identify scope.

Determine the cultural scope of the intended audience. Is the document for a specific national or cultural group, for a multicultural domestic audience, or for an international audience spanning many cultures? The scope determines how much cultural translation and sensitivity are needed. A document for a known single culture can use that culture's idioms and references; a document for diverse or unknown audiences needs broader care. Flag documents where the cultural scope is unclear, which makes sensitivity calibration difficult. Identify the scope to calibrate the level of cultural attention required.

### Check Idioms, Metaphors, And Figurative Language

Idioms and figurative language often do not cross cultures. An idiom vivid in one language may be meaningless, confusing, or offensive in another. Check figurative language.

Review the document for idioms, metaphors, and figurative expressions. For each, assess whether it will translate across the audience's cultures. Sports idioms, such as "hit it out of the park," are culture-specific. Metaphors rooted in one culture's practices or geography may not resonate elsewhere. Religious or mythological references may exclude or confuse. Flag figurative language that is culture-bound and recommend clearer, more universal phrasing, especially in documents for international audiences. Not all figurative language must be removed, but culture-bound expressions should be used deliberately, with awareness of how they land across cultures.

### Verify Examples And Scenarios Are Culturally Inclusive

Examples and scenarios should be culturally inclusive, using contexts and situations that resonate across the audience, or varied enough to represent different cultures. Verify inclusivity.

Assess the document's examples and scenarios. Do they assume a single cultural context, such as Western business practices, American holidays, or specific family structures? Or do they use universal situations or vary across cultures? Flag examples that assume one cultural context as normative, which may exclude or alienate readers from other contexts. Recommend examples that translate across cultures or that vary to represent diversity. Inclusive examples help all readers see themselves in the document and signal respect for the audience's diversity. Exclusive examples, even unintentionally, communicate that the document is really for one cultural group.

### Detect And Remove Stereotypes And Ethnocentrism

Stereotypes reduce cultures to caricature, and ethnocentrism treats one culture as the norm against which others are measured. Both offend and mislead. Detect and address them.

Review the document for stereotypes and ethnocentric assumptions. Are cultures or cultural groups portrayed through stereotype, such as associating a nationality with a single trait? Is one culture, often the writer's own, presented as normative, with others as deviation? Flag stereotyping and ethnocentrism, which may be unconscious. Recommend portrayals that treat cultures and individuals within them as complex and valid, not reductive. Ethnocentrism often appears in assumptions about what is normal, professional, or effective; examine these assumptions for cultural bias. Removing stereotypes and ethnocentrism respects the audience and improves accuracy.

### Check Humor And Tone For Cross-Cultural Appropriateness

Humor is highly culture-bound. What is funny in one culture may be confusing, unfunny, or offensive in another. Tone, too, varies in what is considered appropriate. Check humor and tone.

Assess the document's humor and tone for cross-cultural appropriateness. Humor that relies on cultural references, wordplay, or shared assumptions may not translate. Humor that touches on sensitive topics may offend across cultures. Tone that is warm and informal in one culture may seem unprofessional in another. Flag humor and tone choices that risk misfiring across the audience's cultures. Recommend caution with humor in international contexts, favoring clarity over wit, and calibrating tone to what is broadly appropriate. When humor is used, ensure it translates and does not depend on excluding or mocking any group.

### Verify Names, Titles, And Forms Of Address

Names, titles, and forms of address vary across cultures and carry significance. Using the wrong form, mispronouncing or misspelling names, or assuming naming conventions can disrespect readers. Verify forms.

Check how the document handles names, titles, and forms of address. Are names from various cultures rendered correctly, with appropriate attention to naming conventions such as family name order? Are titles and forms of address appropriate to the cultures represented? Flag assumptions of a single naming convention, such as assuming everyone has a first name and last name in the Western order, or misuse of titles. Recommend respecting the naming and address conventions of the cultures represented. Names and titles are tied to identity and respect; getting them wrong signals disregard for the people represented.

### Consider Cultural Variations In Communication Norms

Communication norms vary across cultures: directness versus indirectness, formality, use of context, and attitudes toward hierarchy. A document optimized for one culture's norms may mismatch another's. Consider variations.

Reflect on how the document's communication style aligns with the audience's cultural norms. Is the directness appropriate, or might it seem blunt or evasive depending on the culture? Is the formality level right? Does the document rely on context or spell things out, and how does that fit the audience's expectations? Flag styles that may mismatch the audience's norms. There is no universally correct style, but awareness of variation helps calibrate. For diverse audiences, a style that is clear and moderately formal often serves broadly, while extreme directness or indirectness may suit some cultures but not others.

## Common Traps

### Assuming A Single Cultural Context

Identify the audience's cultural scope and calibrate sensitivity to it.

### Culture-Bound Idioms And Metaphors

Check figurative language for cross-cultural translation. Use universal phrasing.

### Examples That Assume One Cultural Norm

Use inclusive or varied examples that resonate across cultures.

### Stereotypes And Ethnocentric Assumptions

Portray cultures and individuals as complex. Examine normative assumptions.

### Humor That Misfires Across Cultures

Be cautious with humor in international contexts. Favor clarity over wit.

### Wrong Names, Titles, Or Forms Of Address

Respect naming conventions and titles across the cultures represented.

### Communication Style Mismatched To Cultural Norms

Consider variations in directness, formality, and context reliance.

## Self-Check

Before treating cultural sensitivity as adequately addressed, verify:

- The cultural scope of the audience has been identified.
- Idioms, metaphors, and figurative language are checked for cross-cultural translation.
- Examples and scenarios are culturally inclusive, not assuming a single normative context.
- No stereotypes or ethnocentric assumptions are present.
- Humor and tone are appropriate across the audience's cultures.
- Names, titles, and forms of address respect the conventions of cultures represented.
- The communication style considers cultural variations in norms.
- Language does not inadvertently offend or exclude readers from different backgrounds.
- References to cultures or cultural practices are accurate and respectful.
- A reader from any of the audience's cultures would feel respected and included by the document.
