# Persuasion Vs Information

Documents serve different purposes: some inform, presenting facts and analysis for the reader's understanding; others persuade, advocating for a position, decision, or action. The rhetorical approach should match the purpose. A document that aims to inform but covertly persuades betrays the reader's trust; one that aims to persuade but presents itself as neutral fails to make its case honestly. Persuasion versus information is the discipline of aligning rhetorical approach with purpose, and making that alignment visible to the reader. Editors who edit for content without examining rhetorical intent miss documents that mislead about their own purpose.

Use this skill when editing business or professional documents to distinguish persuasion from information. It covers purpose identification, approach-purpose alignment, and honesty about intent. The goal is a document whose rhetorical approach serves its purpose transparently.

## Core Rules

### Identify Whether The Document Aims To Inform Or Persuade

The first step is identifying the document's primary purpose. Is it informational, presenting facts, analysis, or explanation for understanding? Or persuasive, advocating for a position, recommendation, or action? Identify purpose.

Determine the document's primary intent. Informational documents include reports, analyses, briefings, and explanations that aim to give readers what they need to understand or decide. Persuasive documents include proposals, recommendations, pitches, and advocacy that aim to move readers toward a position or action. Some documents blend both, informing to support a persuasive end. Identifying the primary purpose provides the basis for evaluating whether the approach matches. Flag documents whose purpose is unclear, which may drift between informing and persuading without commitment. A document should know what it is trying to do.

### Match Rhetorical Approach To Purpose

The rhetorical approach should match the purpose. Informational documents should present information neutrally, with balanced treatment of relevant perspectives. Persuasive documents should argue openly, with clear position and supporting reasoning. Match approach.

Assess whether the document's approach matches its purpose. For informational documents, verify the treatment is balanced, presenting facts and perspectives without stacking toward a conclusion the document does not openly advocate. For persuasive documents, verify the position is clear and the argument is open, not disguised as neutral presentation. Flag mismatches: informational documents that subtly advocate, through selective emphasis or loaded language, or persuasive documents that hide their position behind a veneer of neutrality. The approach should serve the purpose honestly, whether that purpose is to inform or to persuade.

### Ensure Informational Documents Do Not Covertly Persuade

Informational documents carry an implicit promise of neutrality. When they covertly persuade, through selective evidence, loaded framing, or buried advocacy, they betray that promise. Ensure informational honesty.

For documents presented as informational, check for covert persuasion. Is the evidence selection balanced, or stacked toward a conclusion? Is the framing neutral, or loaded? Are conclusions presented as findings when they are advocacy? Flag subtle persuasion in informational guise, which misleads readers who trust the document's neutrality. Recommend either making the persuasive intent explicit, if the document aims to advocate, or restoring neutrality, if it aims to inform. Covert persuasion is ethically problematic because it exploits the reader's trust in the document's informational nature; honesty about intent respects the reader.

### Ensure Persuasive Documents Argue Openly

Persuasive documents should argue openly, making their position clear and supporting it with visible reasoning and evidence. Hiding the position or disguising advocacy as neutral presentation weakens the argument and seems evasive. Ensure open argument.

For persuasive documents, check that the argument is open. Is the position stated clearly, or hidden behind neutral-sounding presentation? Is the reasoning visible, or implied? Are the supporting evidence and the value judgments explicit? Flag persuasive documents that obscure their position, perhaps fearing that open advocacy will meet resistance. Open argument is more persuasive and more honest than covert; readers can engage with a clear position, evaluating its merits, whereas hidden advocacy breeds suspicion. Recommend making the position, reasoning, and evidence explicit, so the document argues honestly for what it advocates.

### Check That Evidence Use Is Appropriate To Purpose

How evidence is used differs by purpose. Informational documents present evidence comprehensively and neutrally; persuasive documents select and emphasize evidence that supports the position, while acknowledging counter-evidence. Check evidence use.

Assess evidence use against purpose. For informational documents, verify evidence is presented comprehensively, with relevant perspectives represented, not selectively. For persuasive documents, verify evidence supports the position while counter-evidence is acknowledged and addressed, not hidden. Flag informational documents that cherry-pick evidence toward a conclusion, or persuasive documents that ignore counter-evidence. Appropriate evidence use respects the purpose: informing requires completeness and neutrality; persuading requires selection and emphasis balanced by honesty about counter-evidence. Misusing evidence, by selecting incompletely in an informational document or hiding counter-evidence in a persuasive one, undermines the document's integrity.

### Verify The Reader Understands The Document's Intent

The reader should understand whether they are being informed or persuaded. Documents that obscure their intent leave readers uncertain how to receive the content. Verify intent clarity.

Assess whether the document's intent is clear to the reader. Does the framing, language, or structure signal whether the document informs or persuades? Flag documents whose intent is ambiguous, leaving readers unsure whether to receive content as neutral information or positioned advocacy. Recommend clear signaling: informational documents that present as analyses or briefings; persuasive documents that present as proposals or recommendations. Intent clarity lets readers calibrate their reception, applying appropriate scrutiny to persuasion or receiving information with appropriate openness. Obscuring intent prevents readers from engaging appropriately with the content.

### Ensure Persuasion Does Not Distort Or Mislead

Even openly persuasive documents must not distort or mislead. Persuasion should rely on sound reasoning and accurate evidence, not on misrepresentation, exaggeration, or manipulation. Ensure honest persuasion.

For persuasive documents, check that the persuasion is honest. Does the argument rely on accurate evidence and sound reasoning, or on distortion, exaggeration, or manipulation? Flag misleading tactics: evidence taken out of context, exaggeration of benefits or minimization of risks, emotional manipulation, or logical fallacies. Honest persuasion makes its case through merit; dishonest persuasion seeks to win through deception. Even when advocating strongly, the document should represent evidence accurately and reason fairly. Dishonest persuasion may achieve short-term agreement but destroys long-term trust when the distortion emerges.

## Common Traps

### Unclear Purpose Drifting Between Inform And Persuade

Identify the primary intent. A document should know what it is trying to do.

### Covert Persuasion In Informational Documents

Do not advocate subtly under a neutral guise. Make intent explicit.

### Hidden Position In Persuasive Documents

Argue openly. State the position and reasoning clearly.

### Cherry-Picked Evidence Or Ignored Counter-Evidence

Use evidence appropriately: comprehensively for information, with counter-acknowledgment for persuasion.

### Ambiguous Intent The Reader Cannot Discern

Signal clearly whether the document informs or persuades.

### Distortion Or Manipulation In Persuasion

Persuade through accurate evidence and sound reasoning, not deception.

### Assuming Neutrality Without Examining Framing

Check framing and emphasis, not just explicit claims, for covert advocacy.

## Self-Check

Before treating the persuasion-information distinction as appropriately handled, verify:

- The document's primary purpose, informational or persuasive, has been identified.
- The rhetorical approach matches the purpose, whether neutral presentation or open argument.
- Informational documents do not covertly persuade through selective evidence or loaded framing.
- Persuasive documents argue openly, with clear position and visible reasoning.
- Evidence use is appropriate: comprehensive for information, supportive with counter-acknowledgment for persuasion.
- The reader can discern whether they are being informed or persuaded.
- Persuasion relies on accurate evidence and sound reasoning, not distortion or manipulation.
- No evidence is taken out of context, exaggerated, or selectively minimized.
- The framing and emphasis, not just explicit claims, align with the stated purpose.
- A reader receiving the document understands its intent and can engage appropriately with its content.
