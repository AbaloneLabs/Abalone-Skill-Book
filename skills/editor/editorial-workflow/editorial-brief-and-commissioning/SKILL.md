# Editorial Brief And Commissioning

A commissioned piece is shaped as much by its brief as by the writing that follows. The brief is where the editor translates a publication's needs into instructions a writer can act on, and its quality determines whether the resulting work fits, succeeds, or requires expensive rescue. A vague brief produces a draft that misses the mark and requires rounds of revision to find it; a precise brief produces a draft that lands close to the intended target. Commissioning and briefing are therefore upstream of most editorial pain, and investing in them is the highest-leverage work an editor can do before a word is drafted.

Use this skill when writing or responding to an editorial brief, commissioning work from writers or contributors, evaluating pitches, or defining an assignment's scope and direction. It covers defining angle and scope, specifying length and approach, identifying sources and constraints, negotiating deliverables and deadlines, and setting up the writer and the work to succeed. The editor's task is to make the assignment's intent, boundaries, and expectations explicit enough that a competent writer can deliver a usable draft on the first attempt.

## Core Rules

### Define The Assignment's Core Intent Before Anything Else

Before any detail of a brief is written, the editor must know what the piece is fundamentally for. What question does it answer, what does the reader take away, and what role does it play in the publication? A brief written without a clear core intent produces work that is competent in parts but purposeless as a whole, because the writer has no target to aim at.

Articulate the core intent in a sentence or two: the piece's central question or claim, its intended reader, and the job it does for the publication. Test the intent against the publication's needs, is this piece actually worth commissioning, and does this framing serve those needs? Where the intent is unclear, clarify it before briefing, because every later detail, angle, length, sources, depends on it. A brief whose core intent the editor cannot state plainly is not ready to commission. The intent is the standard against which the resulting draft will be judged, and it must exist before the writing begins.

### Specify Angle And Scope To Make The Piece Actionable

A topic is not an assignment. "Write about climate policy" could produce a thousand different pieces, most of them wrong for the publication. The brief's job is to narrow the topic to an angle, a specific take, question, or framing, and to bound it with scope, what the piece covers and what it does not. Without these, the writer must guess, and guesses produce misalignment.

Define the angle specifically enough that the writer knows what kind of piece to write: an analysis, a reported feature, an opinion piece, an explainer. State the specific question or claim the piece pursues, not merely the subject area. Bound the scope explicitly: the time period, the geography, the actors, the aspects in focus, and, just as importantly, what is out of scope. A brief that says what to exclude prevents the common failure of a piece that sprawls into adjacent territory the publication did not ask for. Angle and scope together convert a topic into an assignment a writer can actually execute.

### State Length Format And Structural Expectations

Writers need to know the shape of the piece they are writing, not only its content. Length and format determine how the writer allocates effort, how much reporting is feasible, how many sources are needed, and what structure the piece can sustain. Leaving these unstated forces the writer to infer them, often incorrectly, and produces drafts that must be cut or expanded substantially to fit.

Specify the target length, both a word count and a sense of the piece's scale, and clarify whether it is a firm limit or a guide. State the format and any structural expectations: does the publication expect a news peg, a narrative opening, a nut graph, sections with subheads, a particular argument structure? Where the publication has conventions, name them, since a writer unfamiliar with the house style will not infer them. For digital pieces, note any multimedia expectations, sidebars, pull quotes, embedded elements. Length and format are not constraints imposed on the writer; they are the frame within which the piece can succeed, and stating them clearly lets the writer design for them from the start.

### Identify Sources Approach And Reporting Expectations

For reported work, the brief must convey what kind of reporting the piece requires. The depth and type of sourcing determine whether the resulting piece is adequately supported, and mismatched expectations here are a common cause of weak drafts that require extensive additional reporting after submission.

Specify the sourcing expectations: how many sources, what kinds, experts, affected people, official documents, data, and whether original reporting is required or synthesis of existing material suffices. Identify any specific sources the writer should contact, especially where the publication has access or relationships, and any the writer should treat with caution. State the approach to evidence and balance: must the piece seek comment from all sides, adhere to a particular standard of attribution, or handle anonymous sources per policy? Where the piece involves specialized verification, fact-checking, legal review, or data analysis, flag this early so the writer gathers what will be needed. Clear reporting expectations prevent the disappointment of a draft that is well-written but insufficiently supported.

### Negotiate Deliverables Deadlines And Compensation

The practical terms of the commission, what is delivered, when, and for what compensation, are part of the brief and must be agreed before work begins. Ambiguity here causes disputes that damage relationships and delay publication, and it often arises from assumptions left unstated rather than from disagreement.

Specify the deliverables: a single draft, multiple revisions, a revised version after edits, any sidebars or additional elements. State the deadline, grounded in realism about the reporting and writing required, and confirm the writer can meet it. Agree on compensation, the fee, payment terms, kill fee policy if the piece is not accepted, and any expense coverage, so that financial expectations are explicit. For work requiring significant reporting, consider interim deliverables, a source list, an outline, a partial draft, to surface problems before the full draft. Putting these terms in writing, even informally, protects both parties and gives the collaboration a clear basis. Terms assumed rather than stated are terms that will eventually be contested.

### Evaluate Pitches Against Publication Fit And Feasibility

When commissioning from a pitch rather than assigning directly, the editor must evaluate whether the proposed piece fits the publication and whether the writer can deliver it. A compelling pitch is not always a commissionable piece, and enthusiasm for a topic is not the same as judgment that it belongs and can be executed.

Evaluate the pitch against the publication's scope, audience, and standards: does this piece serve the readers, fit the editorial direction, and meet the quality bar? Assess feasibility: can this angle be executed at the proposed length and deadline, with the sourcing available, and does the writer have the access and capability to deliver it? Consider the writer's track record and fit for the piece, since a strong writer on the wrong topic may produce a weaker piece than a suitable writer with the right expertise. Where a pitch is close but not right, negotiate, refining the angle, adjusting the scope, or clarifying the approach, rather than accepting or rejecting it wholesale. Commissioning is a judgment about fit and feasibility together, not about the pitch's appeal in isolation.

### Set The Writer Up To Succeed

A brief is not merely a set of instructions; it is the editor's contribution to making the piece possible. The best editors use the brief to give the writer what they need to succeed, context, access, clarity, and a relationship they can rely on during the work. A brief that treats the writer as a vendor delivering a transactional product produces transactional work.

Provide context that helps the writer understand why the piece matters and how it fits the publication's direction. Offer access the publication can provide, introductions, documents, prior coverage, that improves the piece. Make yourself available during the work for questions and course-correction, since a writer who can check direction mid-stream avoids large missteps. Frame the brief as a collaboration toward a shared goal, with the editor supporting the writer's work rather than only judging the result. A writer set up to succeed delivers better work with less revision, and the relationship built through good commissioning is an asset for future work.

## Common Traps

### Topic Without Angle Or Scope

A subject area is not an assignment. Specify the angle and bound the scope so the writer has a target.

### Length And Format Left To Inference

Writers who must guess the shape design for the wrong frame. State length, format, and structural expectations.

### Reporting Expectations Unstated

A well-written but under-sourced draft requires expensive rescue. Specify sourcing depth and approach.

### Practical Terms Assumed Rather Than Stated

Deliverables, deadlines, and compensation left implicit cause disputes. Put the terms in writing.

### Pitch Appeal Mistaken For Commissionability

A compelling pitch may not fit or be feasible. Evaluate fit and feasibility, not just appeal.

### Brief Treated As Transactional Instruction

A brief that offers no context, access, or relationship produces transactional work. Set the writer up to succeed.

### No Interim Check-In On Reported Work

Waiting for the full draft to discover misalignment wastes effort. Use interim deliverables for complex pieces.

### Exclusions Left Unstated

Without stating what is out of scope, pieces sprawl into territory the publication did not commission.

## Self-Check

Before treating an editorial brief or commission as complete, verify:

- The piece's core intent, its central question, intended reader, and role for the publication, has been articulated clearly.
- The angle is specific enough to be actionable and the scope bounds what is in and out of focus.
- Target length, format, and structural expectations are stated, including any house conventions the writer must meet.
- Sourcing and reporting expectations are specified, including depth, types of sources, and any specialized verification needs.
- Deliverables, deadline, and compensation are agreed and documented, with interim deliverables for complex reported work.
- Pitches have been evaluated for both publication fit and execution feasibility, not only for appeal.
- The writer has been given the context, access, and availability needed to succeed, framing the work as collaboration.
- The brief is explicit enough that a competent writer could deliver a usable first draft that lands near the intended target.
- The commission sets up both the work and the relationship to succeed rather than merely transacting an assignment.
