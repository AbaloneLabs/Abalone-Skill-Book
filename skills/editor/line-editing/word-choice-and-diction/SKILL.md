# Word Choice And Diction

Words are the smallest unit of meaning a reader encounters, and the wrong word at any scale weakens the whole. A precise claim blurred by a vague term, a formal register broken by a casualism, a vivid idea flattened by a cliché, a key concept named three different ways, a neutral subject loaded by a connotative verb, a technical term used outside its field, all are word-choice failures that no amount of grammar correction can repair. Line editing for diction is the craft of choosing, at each point, the word that carries exactly the meaning, tone, and force the moment requires, while preserving the writer's voice and resisting the pull toward the editor's own preferred vocabulary. It is patient, granular work, and it is where much of the felt quality of prose is made or lost.

Use this skill at the line-editing stage, after structure and meaning are stable, when the task is to refine word choice. It covers choosing precise over vague diction, matching register to context and audience, handling connotation and loaded language, removing cliché and filler, managing repetition and variation, and preserving voice through diction. The editor's task is to make every word earn its place without imposing a generic smoothness.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Precise Word Over The Vague One

Vague diction forces the reader to guess at meaning and weakens credibility. Precise diction delivers meaning without translation. The difference between "a big problem" and "a systemic failure," between "things got worse" and "unemployment doubled," between "good results" and "a 23 percent reduction," is the difference between prose that informs and prose that washes over.

For each significant claim, ask whether the word is as precise as the meaning requires. Where a general term stands in for a specific one, replace it with the specific term or query the writer for it. Precision is not about long words or jargon; it is about the word that exactly fits the meaning, which may be simple. Reserve vagueness for moments where genuine uncertainty is honest; do not let it stand where precision is available.

### Match Register To Context And Audience

Register is the level of formality and familiarity the prose adopts, and it should match the context, the audience, and the writer's stance. A draft that shifts register without reason, formal in one sentence and casual in the next, reads as unstable, and a register mismatched to its audience reads as either stiff or patronizing.

Identify the operative register from the piece's purpose and audience, and check that the diction holds it consistently. Technical work for experts can use specialized terms; the same topic for a general audience needs accessible diction. A formal argument should not suddenly deploy slang; a personal essay should not suddenly stiffen into academese. Where register shifts, make the shift deliberate and motivated, not accidental.

### Handle Connotation And Loaded Language Deliberately

Words carry meaning beyond their denotation: connotation, emotional coloring, and evaluative weight. Two words with the same denotation can frame a subject oppositely. "Thrifty" versus "cheap," "confident" versus "arrogant," "reform" versus "overhaul," each pair names similar conduct with different judgment embedded.

Read for connotative weight, especially around contested subjects, people, and groups. Where a word's connotation loads the prose beyond what the evidence supports, choose a more neutral term or balance it deliberately. Connotation is not always wrong; deliberate connotative language is a tool. The failure is unconscious connotation that smuggles in judgment the writer did not earn. Make connotative choices deliberate and defensible.

### Remove Cliché, Filler, And Empty Intensifiers

Clichés are phrases worn smooth by overuse; they communicate that the writer reached for a ready-made expression rather than the right one. Filler words add length without meaning. Empty intensifiers, very, really, truly, literally used loosely, attempt to strengthen claims without adding force. Each makes prose feel generic and weak.

Flag clichés and replace them with fresh, specific language where possible. Cut filler that carries no meaning. Reduce intensifiers, letting strong nouns and verbs carry the force instead. Some clichés are acceptable in casual registers or where they communicate efficiently, but their accumulation flattens prose. The goal is not to eliminate every familiar phrase but to prevent the draft from relying on them where original diction would serve better.

### Manage Repetition And Variation

Repetition is a tool when deliberate and a flaw when accidental. A key term repeated for emphasis reinforces meaning; the same word repeated inadvertently in adjacent sentences reads as carelessness. The same sentence opening repeated across a paragraph drones; varied openings create movement.

Distinguish deliberate repetition from accidental. Keep deliberate repetition where it serves emphasis, cohesion, or rhythm. Vary accidental repetition by substituting synonyms where meaning is preserved, restructuring sentences, or rearranging. Be careful with synonym substitution for technical terms, where consistency matters more than variation; vary ordinary language, but keep specialized terminology stable. The line between effective repetition and monotony is intent.

### Preserve Voice Through Diction Choices

Every writer has a characteristic vocabulary, a set of words and phrasings that constitute their voice. Line editing that substitutes the editor's preferred words for the writer's erases that voice, producing prose that is correct but impersonal. Diction editing must improve the word choice without replacing the writer's lexical signature.

Before substituting a word, ask whether the original is genuinely wrong or merely not the word you would have chosen. Preserve the writer's characteristic diction where it works, even if it is not your preference. Edit diction that is genuinely imprecise, mismatched, or distracting, but do not standardize toward your own vocabulary. Voice lives in diction, and diction editing that cannot tell the difference between improvement and imposition will flatten the work.

### Use Strong Verbs And Concrete Nouns

The skeleton of a sentence is its verbs and nouns, and strong verbs and concrete nouns carry more meaning with fewer words than weak ones propped up by modifiers. "She decided quickly" is weaker than "she decided" if the speed is implied; "he made his way across the room" is weaker than "he crossed the room."

Look for weak verb constructions, nominalizations that turn verbs into nouns, and abstract nouns that could be concrete. Where a strong verb can replace a weak verb plus modifier, make the swap. Where a concrete noun can replace an abstract one, do so. This is not a rule to strip every modifier; it is a principle that the load-bearing words should carry real weight, with modifiers reserved for genuine addition.

### Check Idiom And Collocation

Idioms and collocations, words that conventionally go together, are easy to get subtly wrong, and errors here signal carelessness to attentive readers. "Comprised of" versus "composed of," "different than" versus "different from," preposition choices, and phrasal verb constructions all have conventional forms that vary by region and register.

Check idiomatic constructions against the work's chosen standard. Where a construction is nonstandard, decide whether it is a deliberate regional or voice choice or an error, and treat accordingly. Be especially careful with idioms in translated or non-native writing, where collocation errors are common and where the right fix may be a query rather than a silent change.

## Common Traps

### Vague Diction Where Precision Is Available

General terms blur meaning. Replace with the specific word the meaning requires.

### Unintentional Register Shifts

Shifting formality without reason reads as unstable. Hold register consistently unless a shift is deliberate.

### Unconscious Connotative Loading

Words that smuggle in judgment the evidence did not earn. Make connotative choices deliberate.

### Cliché And Empty Intensifiers

Ready-made phrases and loose intensifiers flatten prose. Use fresh, specific language and strong words.

### Accidental Repetition

Inadvertent repetition reads as carelessness. Vary where meaning is preserved; keep deliberate repetition.

### Replacing The Writer's Words With The Editor's

Substituting preferences for voice erases the writer. Edit genuine problems; preserve characteristic diction.

### Weak Verbs Propped By Modifiers

Strong verbs and concrete nouns carry more with less. Let load-bearing words do the work.

### Idiom Errors Treated As Style

Nonstandard collocations signal carelessness. Check against the chosen standard and distinguish error from voice.

## Self-Check

Before treating word choice and diction editing as complete, verify:

- Significant claims use precise diction rather than vague general terms.
- Register is matched to context and audience and held consistently unless shifts are deliberate.
- Connotative and loaded language has been reviewed and made deliberate rather than unconscious.
- Clichés, filler, and empty intensifiers have been reduced, with fresh specific language where it serves better.
- Repetition has been distinguished as deliberate or accidental, with accidental repetition varied.
- The writer's characteristic vocabulary and voice have been preserved, with only genuine diction problems edited.
- Strong verbs and concrete nouns carry the load, with modifiers reserved for genuine addition.
- Idioms and collocations have been checked against the work's standard, with errors distinguished from voice.
- Every word earns its place, and the prose carries exactly the meaning, tone, and force each moment requires.
