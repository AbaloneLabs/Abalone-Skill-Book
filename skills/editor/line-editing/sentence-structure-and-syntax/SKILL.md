# Sentence Structure And Syntax

A sentence is a structure the reader must parse in real time, and syntax determines whether that parsing is effortless or labored. A main clause buried under introductory matter, a modifier dangling away from its head, a subject separated from its verb by a long interruption, subordination stacked until the reader loses the main point, parallelism broken mid-series, a sentence so long the reader runs out of breath and thread before the period, all are structural failures that make prose hard to read no matter how good the ideas. Line editing for syntax is the craft of arranging words, clauses, and phrases so each sentence delivers its meaning with minimum friction, while preserving the writer's characteristic sentence architecture. It is engineering at the sentence level, and it is where clarity is built or broken.

Use this skill at the line-editing stage, after meaning and structure are stable, when the task is to make sentences structurally sound. It covers arranging clauses for clarity, managing subordination and emphasis, fixing modifier placement, maintaining parallelism, controlling sentence length and complexity, and preserving the writer's syntactic voice. The editor's task is to make each sentence parse cleanly while leaving the writer's sentence shapes intact.

## Core Rules

### Make The Main Clause Visible And Reachable

The main clause carries the sentence's primary meaning, and the reader should be able to find it without excavating. When the main clause is buried under long introductions, interrupted by parentheticals, or delayed until the end of a sprawling construction, the reader loses the thread before reaching the point.

For each sentence, identify the main clause and check that it is reachable. Where introductory matter is long, consider moving it after the main clause or splitting the sentence. Where interruptions separate subject from verb or verb from object, move them or remove them if they are not essential. The reader should encounter the sentence's spine without having to hold too much in suspension. Reserve complex front-loading for moments where the delay serves emphasis.

### Manage Subordination To Serve Emphasis

Subordination, placing less important material in dependent clauses and the most important in the main clause, is the primary tool for showing what matters in a sentence. Mismanaged subordination buries the key point in a subordinate clause while a secondary detail takes the main clause, distorting emphasis.

Check that each sentence's emphasis matches its structure. The most important idea should be in the main clause; supporting and qualifying material in subordinate clauses or phrases. Where the emphasis is inverted, restructure so the key point occupies the main clause. Do not over-subordinate, stacking clause within clause until the reader cannot track the hierarchy; where a sentence carries multiple important ideas, consider splitting it so each gets its own main clause.

### Fix Modifier Placement And Dangling Constructions

Modifiers must sit close to the words they modify, or the reader misparses the sentence. A misplaced modifier attaches to the wrong head, often absurdly; a dangling modifier has no logical subject to attach to, leaving the sentence implying something it does not mean. Both are common and both undermine clarity and credibility.

Read for modifier placement. Check that adverbs, adjectives, and phrases sit adjacent to their heads. Check especially introductory participial and infinitive phrases, which dangle when the sentence's subject is not the actor they imply. Where a modifier is misplaced or dangling, move it adjacent to its head, supply the missing subject, or restructure. These errors are easy to miss in one's own reading and obvious to readers, so check deliberately.

### Maintain Parallelism In Series And Correlatives

Parallel structure makes relationships visible and series feel complete. When items in a series, or elements joined by correlatives like either/or, not only/but also, both/and, shift syntactic form mid-stream, the reader stumbles and the logic blurs. Broken parallelism reads as clumsy even when the meaning is recoverable.

Check that all items in a series share the same grammatical form, and that correlative constructions join parallel elements. Where parallelism breaks, restore it by aligning the forms. Break parallelism only deliberately, for emphasis or surprise, and ensure the break reads as intentional rather than accidental. Parallelism is both a clarity tool and a rhetorical one; maintain it by default and break it with purpose.

### Control Sentence Length And Complexity For Reader Load

Sentence length and complexity determine the cognitive load the reader bears at each moment. A run of long, complex sentences fatigues the reader and obscures meaning; a run of short, simple ones can feel choppy and juvenile. The right distribution depends on content, audience, and rhythm, but extremes in either direction fail.

Read for sentence length distribution within paragraphs. Where every sentence is long and multi-clausal, look for places to split or simplify. Where every sentence is short and declarative, look for places to combine or complexify for flow. Match complexity to content: difficult ideas may need simpler syntax so the reader can follow; simple ideas can carry more complex syntax without strain. Length variation, handled in the rhythm skill, also matters, but here the concern is structural load, ensuring no sentence is harder to parse than its content requires.

### Eliminate Unnecessary Nominalization And Weak Constructions

Nominalization, turning verbs into nouns, and weak constructions like there is/there are and it is often pad sentences and obscure who is doing what. "The committee made a decision to delay the project" is weaker than "the committee delayed the project." These constructions add words and distance the reader from the action.

Look for nominalizations and weak expletive constructions that could be stronger. Where a noun-plus-weak-verb can become a strong verb, revise. Where a there is/there are construction introduces a real subject buried later in the sentence, restructure so the subject acts. This is not a rule to eliminate every nominalization; some are necessary and natural. The goal is to remove those that pad and weaken without adding meaning.

### Preserve The Writer's Syntactic Signature

Writers have characteristic sentence architectures: some favor long, accumulative sentences with layered subordination; others favor short, direct ones; others mix in distinctive patterns. Line editing that regularizes all sentences toward a single ideal shape erases the writer's syntactic voice and produces competent but impersonal prose.

Identify the writer's syntactic signature and protect the shapes that constitute it. Edit the sentences that genuinely fail, those that lose the reader, bury the point, or break structurally, but do not rewrite every sentence into your preferred form. A writer of long, rolling sentences should remain a writer of long, rolling sentences, with only the failures repaired. Voice lives in syntax as much as in diction.

### Read For Parse-Ability, Not Just For Grammar

A sentence can be grammatically correct and still hard to parse, because grammar permits constructions that overload the reader's working memory. Syntax editing must judge not whether a sentence is grammatical but whether a reader can process it smoothly at normal reading speed.

Read each sentence as a reader would, at speed, and notice where you hesitate, backtrack, or lose the thread. Those are parse failures, and they mark sentences that need restructuring even if they are grammatically defensible. The test is the reader's experience, not the grammar checker's approval. Where a sentence is correct but hard to read, revise until it reads cleanly.

## Common Traps

### Burying The Main Clause

Long introductions and interruptions hide the sentence's spine. Keep the main clause visible and reachable.

### Inverted Subordination

Burying the key point in a subordinate clause distorts emphasis. Put important ideas in the main clause.

### Misplaced And Dangling Modifiers

Modifiers away from their heads misparse or imply absurdity. Place modifiers adjacent to their heads.

### Broken Parallelism

Series and correlatives that shift form mid-stream read as clumsy. Maintain parallel structure.

### Sentences Harder To Parse Than Their Content Requires

Length and complexity should match the idea's difficulty. Simplify syntax for hard content.

### Nominalization And Weak Constructions That Pad

Noun-plus-weak-verb constructions add words and obscure agency. Use strong verbs where possible.

### Regularizing Toward The Editor's Preferred Shape

Rewriting every sentence into one form erases voice. Edit failures; preserve the syntactic signature.

### Equating Grammatical With Readable

Grammar permits overload. Judge by whether a reader can parse at speed, not by correctness alone.

## Self-Check

Before treating sentence structure and syntax editing as complete, verify:

- The main clause of each sentence is visible and reachable, not buried under introductions or interruptions.
- Subordination serves emphasis, with important ideas in main clauses and supporting material subordinate.
- Modifiers are placed adjacent to their heads, with no misplaced or dangling constructions.
- Parallelism is maintained in series and correlatives, broken only deliberately for effect.
- Sentence length and complexity are matched to content difficulty and reader load, without extreme runs.
- Unnecessary nominalizations and weak constructions have been revised toward strong, direct forms.
- The writer's syntactic signature has been preserved, with only genuine structural failures edited.
- Each sentence has been read at speed for parse-ability, and hesitation points have been addressed.
- The sentences are structurally sound and easy to parse while still sounding like their author.
