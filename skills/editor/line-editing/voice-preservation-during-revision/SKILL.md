# Voice Preservation During Revision

Voice is what makes a piece recognizably its author's. It lives in word choice, sentence rhythm, stance toward the reader, willingness to digress or to stay tight, use of idiom and dialect, level of formality, and the particular way the writer sees. Revision that ignores voice produces clean, correct, interchangeable prose, and in doing so it destroys the thing the reader came for. The central tension of editing is that almost every improvement risks erasing voice, and almost every voice-preserving instinct risks leaving real problems unfixed.

Use this skill whenever you are revising, line editing, or substantially changing a writer's prose, whether your own draft or another author's. It covers distinguishing voice from error, calibrating how aggressively to intervene, preserving dialect and idiom, protecting rhythm and stance, avoiding the drift toward house voice, and deciding when a voice feature must yield to clarity, accuracy, or reader need. The editor's task is to make the writing work better while leaving it recognizably the writer's.

## Core Rules

### Separate Voice From Error Before Editing

Many things that look like problems are actually voice, and many things that look like voice are actually error. The first task is to tell them apart, because the two require opposite treatment.

Voice features include deliberate fragments, unusual word order, regional idiom, sustained informality or formality, characteristic sentence rhythms, repeated words used for effect, digressive structure, and a distinctive stance toward the reader. Errors include genuine ambiguity, broken syntax that obscures meaning, unintentional shifts in register, factual mistakes, continuity breaks, and unintentional repetition. Before changing anything, ask whether the feature is a choice that constitutes the writer's voice or a flaw that obscures it. Edit the second; protect the first.

### Diagnose Whether A Voice Feature Is Working

Not every voice feature succeeds. A dialect flourish can be vivid or can distract. A fragment can land hard or can confuse. A long sentence can accumulate power or can lose the reader. Voice preservation does not mean defending every voice feature; it means defending the ones that work.

For each voice feature, ask what it contributes. Does it add precision, texture, rhythm, characterization, or emotional truth? Does it cost the reader clarity, credibility, or trust? Preserve voice features whose contribution exceeds their cost. Revise or cut voice features whose cost exceeds their contribution, and do so in a way that, where possible, keeps the writer's intent even if the specific device changes.

### Calibrate Intervention To The Task And The Writer

How aggressively you revise voice depends on the stage, the purpose, and the writer. A developmental pass tolerates more structural change; a line edit should be more conservative; a copyedit should leave voice almost untouched. A writer who has asked for heavy revision welcomes intervention; a writer who has not may experience even careful edits as violation.

Match the depth of intervention to the stage and to the explicit or implicit mandate. When in doubt, revise less and query more. It is easier to deepen an edit in a later pass than to restore voice the writer feels was stripped. State the level of intervention you are applying so the writer can calibrate their expectations and push back.

### Protect Dialect, Idiom, And Culturally Specific Language

Dialect, idiom, and culturally specific phrasing are often the most voice-laden elements and the most often "corrected" by editors working from a different register. These elements carry identity, place, community, and worldview, and standardizing them erases the writer and can flatten the work into a generic voice that does not belong to anyone.

Distinguish authentic dialect and idiom from error. Nonstandard grammar that reflects a real spoken or written tradition is voice; grammar that simply confuses is error. When editing speakers of non-dominant varieties, or writers whose voice draws on a tradition not your own, slow down and question your own defaults. Do not "correct" toward a standard that erases the writer's origin. Where a feature genuinely interferes with reader comprehension, find the smallest edit that preserves the voice while solving the problem, and consider querying the writer rather than changing unilaterally.

### Preserve Rhythm And Sentence Shape

A writer's rhythm is part of voice. Some writers live in long, rolling sentences; others in short, sharp ones; others mix in characteristic patterns. Revision that regularizes sentence shape, splits every long sentence, or combines every fragment erases the rhythmic signature.

Before splitting a long sentence, ask whether its length is doing work, accumulating force, or mirroring a thought's movement. Before combining a fragment, ask whether the fragment is a deliberate beat. Edit rhythm only when it fails, when a long sentence actually loses the reader, or when a fragment actually confuses. Preserve the rhythmic choices that constitute the writer's feel for prose.

### Guard The Writer's Stance Toward The Reader

Voice includes the implied relationship between writer and reader: how close, how formal, how much the writer confides, how much distance they keep, whether they joke, whether they lecture, whether they invite. Revisions that shift this stance, often by making the prose more formal, more neutral, or more explanatory, change who the writer appears to be.

Notice stance in the original and preserve it. If the writer addresses the reader directly, keep the direct address unless it fails. If the writer confides casually, do not stiffen the prose into academic register. If the writer maintains ironic distance, do not flatten it into earnest explanation. Stance shifts are sometimes necessary for clarity or appropriateness, but they should be deliberate, not accidental byproducts of editing for correctness.

### Avoid Drift Toward House Voice Or Editor Voice

Every editor has a house voice in their head, a default way prose "should" sound, and unguarded editing drifts toward it. Over a long manuscript, small voice-erasing edits accumulate until the work no longer sounds like its author.

Watch for the drift. If your edits tend to make sentences more uniform, more formally correct, more explanatory, or more like prose you would write, you may be moving toward house voice rather than serving the writer. Periodically re-read an untouched passage and compare it to your edited passages to check that the voice still matches. When you notice drift, pull back and revise more conservatively.

### Decide When Voice Must Yield

Voice is not absolute. Sometimes a voice feature must yield to clarity, accuracy, reader comprehension, genre expectation, or ethical obligation. The editor's job is to recognize these cases and make the tradeoff deliberately.

Voice yields when a dialect feature genuinely prevents comprehension, when informal phrasing undermines credibility in a high-stakes context, when a stylistic flourish distorts a fact, when a rhythm choice obscures a sequence the reader must follow exactly, or when a voice feature causes harm. In these cases, revise, but revise toward the smallest change that solves the problem while preserving as much voice as possible. Name the tradeoff to the writer so they understand why voice yielded.

### Use Queries To Preserve Authorial Control and revise Your Own Drafts With The Same Care

When a voice feature sits in the gray zone between working and not working, a query often serves better than a silent edit. The query preserves the writer's authority over their own voice while surfacing the concern.

Frame queries as observations and options, not commands. "This dialect phrase is vivid but may slow some readers; want to keep it, gloss it, or adjust?" gives the writer the decision. Queries are especially important for voice features tied to identity, region, or experience the editor does not share. Default to asking rather than changing.

When revising your own work, voice preservation matters too. Your own voice can be eroded by self-editing that chases a perceived ideal of smooth, correct prose, leaving the work competent but impersonal.

Recognize your own voice features and protect the ones that work. Resist the urge to sand every edge in pursuit of polish. The same diagnostic questions apply: is this a voice feature or an error, is it working, what does it contribute, what does it cost. Your voice is no less worth preserving than another writer's.

## Common Traps

### Correcting Voice As If It Were Error

Fragments, idiom, dialect, and unusual syntax are often deliberate. Distinguish voice from error before changing anything.

### Standardizing Toward A Dominant Register

Editing dialect or idiom toward a standard variety erases identity and flattens voice. Slow down when editing traditions not your own.

### Splitting Every Long Sentence

Long sentences often do work. Edit length only when it actually loses the reader.

### Stiffening Casual Into Formal

Shifting stance toward formality changes who the writer appears to be. Preserve stance unless it fails the piece.

### Drifting Into House Voice

Small voice-erasing edits accumulate. Re-read untouched passages periodically to check that voice still matches.

### Over-Intervening Past The Mandate

Editing more aggressively than the stage or writer welcomes reads as violation. Match intervention to task and query when unsure.

### Defending Voice Features That Do Not Work

Preservation is not blanket defense. Revise voice features whose cost exceeds their contribution.

### Silent Edits In The Gray Zone and erasing Your Own Voice In Self-Editing

When a feature might be voice or might be error, a query preserves authorial control better than a unilateral change.

Self-revision can sand away the edges that make your work yours. Protect your own voice features as carefully as another writer's.

## Self-Check

Before treating voice-preserving revision as complete, verify:

- Each potential edit has been classified as voice feature or error, and only errors have been changed automatically.
- Voice features have been evaluated for contribution versus cost, and only those whose cost exceeds contribution have been revised.
- The depth of intervention matches the editing stage and the writer's mandate, and conservative editing has been preferred where unsure.
- Dialect, idiom, and culturally specific language have been preserved unless they genuinely prevent comprehension, and the smallest possible edit has been used where intervention was needed.
- Sentence rhythm and shape have been preserved where they do work, with length edits made only where comprehension actually fails.
- The writer's stance toward the reader has been preserved rather than shifted toward formality, neutrality, or explanation.
- Edits have been checked for drift toward house voice or editor voice by comparing edited and untouched passages.
- Cases where voice yielded to clarity, accuracy, or ethics have been made deliberately, with the smallest change and the tradeoff named to the writer.
- Gray-zone features have been queried rather than silently changed, especially where identity, region, or experience are involved.
- The revised work still sounds recognizably like its author, not like generic correct prose.
