# Statistical Claim Interpretation

Statistics are powerful but easily misinterpreted, and the gap between statistical meaning and common understanding is a frequent source of error. Statistical significance is not practical significance. Correlation is not causation. A confidence interval is not a probability about the parameter. An average may mislead when the distribution is skewed. When a work makes statistical claims, the interpretation must be statistically correct, not merely plausible-sounding. Misinterpreting statistics overstates findings, misleads readers, and undermines the work's credibility when the error is caught. Statistical claim interpretation is the discipline of checking that statistical concepts are used and explained correctly, so the work claims no more, and no less, than the statistics actually show. Editors who are not statistically careful publish works whose quantitative claims do not withstand scrutiny.

Use this skill when editing works that make statistical claims, including research, data journalism, policy reports, and quantitative nonfiction. It covers checking significance, causation, confidence intervals, effect sizes, and common interpretive errors. The goal is statistical claims that are correct, appropriately hedged, and honestly presented.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Statistical Significance From Practical Significance

Statistical significance means an observed result is unlikely to be due to chance, given the sample size. It does not mean the result is large or important. With a large enough sample, trivial differences become statistically significant. Confusing statistical with practical significance overstates findings. Check that the work does not equate significance with importance.

For each claim of statistical significance, verify the work interprets it correctly. Statistical significance indicates detectability, not magnitude. Check whether effect sizes are reported alongside significance, because significance without effect size is uninformative about practical importance. Flag claims that present statistical significance as proof of a meaningful or large effect. The correct interpretation is that the result is unlikely due to chance; whether it matters requires considering the effect size and context. Conflating the two is a common and consequential statistical error.

### Verify Causal Claims Against The Study Design

Causation is a strong claim that requires specific evidence, typically from experimental or quasi-experimental designs. Observational studies can establish correlation but not causation, because confounding variables may explain the association. Verify that causal claims are supported by the study design and do not overstate what observational data can show.

For each causal claim, identify the evidence. If the claim is based on an experiment or strong quasi-experiment, causation may be warranted. If based on observational data, the claim should be hedged as association or correlation, not causation, unless the author provides specific causal reasoning. Flag causal language, "causes," "leads to," "results in," applied to correlational evidence. Check that the work acknowledges confounders where relevant. Causal overstatement from observational data is one of the most common and damaging statistical errors in public-facing writing.

### Check Confidence Interval And Margin Of Error Interpretation

Confidence intervals and margins of error express the uncertainty in estimates, but they are frequently misinterpreted. A 95 percent confidence interval is not a 95 percent probability that the true value lies within it. A margin of error describes sampling variability, not all sources of uncertainty. Check that these concepts are interpreted correctly.

For each use of confidence intervals or margins of error, verify the interpretation. Check that confidence intervals are not described as probabilities about the parameter. Check that margins of error are not presented as capturing all uncertainty, since they typically reflect only sampling error and not bias or other systematic errors. Flag interpretations that overstate the precision or certainty these measures provide. Correct interpretation acknowledges the specific uncertainty the measure captures and does not imply more. Misinterpretation inflates confidence in the findings.

### Verify Effect Sizes Are Reported And Interpreted

Effect sizes describe the magnitude of a finding, and they are essential for interpreting practical importance. A statistically significant result with a tiny effect size may be unimportant; a non-significant result with a moderate effect size may still be meaningful. Verify that effect sizes are reported where relevant and interpreted appropriately.

For statistical claims, check whether effect sizes are reported. Flag claims of significance that omit effect sizes, leaving the reader unable to assess importance. Where effect sizes are reported, verify they are interpreted in context, not merely labeled large or small without reference. Check that the work does not rely solely on significance tests to establish importance. Effect size reporting and interpretation allow readers to understand not just whether an effect exists but how large it is, which is usually what matters for practical conclusions.

### Check Base Rates And Percentage Interpretations

Percentages and proportional claims require correct base rates and proper interpretation. A percentage increase from a small base may be modest in absolute terms. A percentage without a base is uninterpretable. Confusing percentage points with percent change distorts magnitude. Check base rates and percentage interpretations.

For each percentage claim, verify the base rate is clear. Check that percentage increases and decreases are interpreted relative to their base, not in absolute terms. Distinguish percentage point changes from percent changes, which are very different. Check that percentages are not presented without context that makes them interpretable. Watch for misleading percentage framing, such as relative risk presented without absolute risk. Base rate and percentage errors are common in data presentation and can dramatically distort the reader's understanding of magnitude.

### Watch For Selective Or Misleading Comparisons

Statistics can be framed to support a desired impression through selective comparison: choosing a favorable baseline, comparing to an atypical reference, or cherry-picking time periods. These framings are technically accurate but misleading. Watch for selective or misleading comparisons in statistical claims.

For each statistical comparison, check the reference point. Is the baseline reasonable or chosen to flatter the result? Is the comparison group appropriate? Is the time period representative or cherry-picked? Flag comparisons that appear selected to support a particular impression rather than to inform. Check that trend claims use appropriate start and end points. Misleading comparisons are a form of statistical dishonesty that is hard to catch without examining the choices behind the framing. Examine those choices.

### Acknowledge Limitations And Uncertainty

Honest statistical reporting acknowledges limitations and uncertainty. Studies have methodological limitations, samples have constraints, and findings have uncertainty ranges. A work that presents statistics without acknowledging limitations overstates their reliability. Check that limitations and uncertainty are acknowledged appropriately.

For statistical claims, check whether the work acknowledges relevant limitations: sample constraints, design limitations, generalizability concerns, and uncertainty ranges. Flag confident presentation of findings that have significant acknowledged limitations in the source. Check that uncertainty is conveyed to the reader, not buried or omitted. Honest statistical communication includes the caveats, allowing the reader to calibrate confidence. Omitting limitations to strengthen the impression is a form of statistical misrepresentation.

## Common Traps

### Equating Statistical With Practical Significance

Significance indicates detectability, not importance. Check effect sizes.

### Causal Claims From Correlational Evidence

Observational data supports association, not causation. Hedge causal language.

### Misinterpreting Confidence Intervals

They are not probabilities about the parameter. Interpret correctly.

### Omitting Effect Sizes

Significance without effect size is uninformative. Report and interpret magnitudes.

### Misleading Percentage And Base Rate Framing

Percentages need context. Distinguish percent from percentage point changes.

### Selective Comparisons

Cherry-picked baselines and time periods mislead. Examine framing choices.

### Omitting Limitations And Uncertainty

Honest reporting includes caveats. Do not overstate reliability.

## Self-Check

Before treating statistical claim interpretation as complete, verify:

- Statistical significance is not equated with practical importance in any claim.
- Effect sizes are reported and interpreted where relevant to practical conclusions.
- Causal claims are supported by the study design or appropriately hedged.
- Observational findings are presented as association, not causation, with confounders noted.
- Confidence intervals and margins of error are interpreted correctly, not as probabilities.
- Margins of error are not presented as capturing all uncertainty.
- Percentages include clear base rates and distinguish percent from percentage point changes.
- Statistical comparisons use reasonable baselines, reference groups, and time periods.
- Limitations and uncertainty are acknowledged rather than buried or omitted.
- The work's statistical claims claim no more than the statistics actually show.
