# Numerical Accuracy Check

Numbers carry an aura of authority, and readers tend to trust them. This makes numerical errors especially damaging: a transposed digit, a misplaced decimal, a wrong unit, or a calculation error can mislead readers who assume the number is correct because it is precise. Numerical accuracy checking verifies that every number in a work is correct against its source, that calculations are sound, that units and scales are consistent, and that figures agree across the text, tables, and charts. Editors who treat numbers as the author's or data team's responsibility miss transcription errors, calculation mistakes, and inconsistencies that undermine the work's credibility. In data-heavy works, numerical accuracy is not a nicety; it is the foundation of the work's claim to reliability.

Use this skill when editing works that rely heavily on numbers, including research reports, data journalism, business and policy documents, and quantitative academic writing. It covers source verification, calculation checking, unit and scale consistency, and cross-element agreement. The goal is a work where every number is correct, traceable, and consistent.

## Core Rules

### Verify Numbers Against Authoritative Sources

Every number in a data-heavy work should be traceable to a source, and the accuracy check verifies the number matches its source exactly. Transcription errors, where a digit is mistyped or a number is carried over wrong, are common and catchable only by checking against the source.

For each number, identify its source: a dataset, a study, a report, or author-provided data. Verify the number matches the source exactly, including digits, decimal places, and units. Check that the source is the correct and current one. Where a number is attributed, verify the attribution. Flag any number that cannot be traced or that mismatches its source. Numbers that cannot be verified are liabilities; they may be correct, but the work cannot defend them if questioned. Source verification is the primary defense against transcription error.

### Recalculate To Verify Arithmetic

Many numbers in a work are derived through calculation: totals, percentages, averages, growth rates, and conversions. Calculation errors are common, especially in complex derivations, and they survive because the result looks plausible. Recalculate derived figures to verify the arithmetic is sound.

For each calculated figure, identify the inputs and the operation. Recalculate the result independently. Compare to the figure in the work. Flag any discrepancy. Common calculation errors include percentage base confusion, averaging of averages, rounding accumulation, and unit conversion mistakes. For complex derivations, verify each step. Recalculation catches errors that source-checking cannot, because the error is in the math, not the source. In data-heavy works, a single calculation error can propagate through multiple derived figures, so checking calculations is high-value.

### Check Units, Scales, And Decimal Places

Numerical errors often involve units and scales rather than the digits themselves. A figure in millions that should be in billions, a decimal point in the wrong place, a unit confusion between metric and imperial, or inconsistent decimal places across related figures, these change meaning drastically while looking superficially correct. Check units, scales, and decimal places explicitly.

For each number, verify the unit is correct and consistently stated. Check that the scale is appropriate and consistent across related figures. Verify decimal places are correct against the source and consistent across comparable figures. Check unit conversions and verify they are correct. Check that units are stated explicitly where needed for clarity. Unit and scale errors can change meaning by orders of magnitude and are easy to miss because the digits look right. Explicit checking of units and scales catches this category of error.

### Verify Internal Consistency Across The Work

Even when individual numbers are correct against their sources, they may be inconsistent with each other within the work. A total stated in the summary may not match the sum of components in a table. A percentage may not match its underlying count. A growth rate may not match the beginning and ending figures. Internal consistency checking catches these contradictions.

Scan for numbers that should relate to each other and verify they do. Check that totals equal the sum of their components. Check that percentages match their base figures. Check that growth rates and changes match the underlying values. Check that figures appearing in multiple places, in text, tables, charts, and summaries, agree exactly. Internal contradictions are common because different sections may draw on data at different times or with different rounding. They are highly visible to attentive readers and undermine confidence in the work's data handling.

### Cross-Reference Text With Tables And Charts

Numbers often appear in the running text and in tables or charts, and the two must agree. A text that cites one figure while the table shows another is a glaring inconsistency. Cross-referencing catches mismatches between prose and structured data presentations.

For each table and chart, verify that figures cited in the text match the table or chart exactly. Check that the text characterizes the data accurately, not just that numbers match but that descriptions are correct. Check that table and chart titles, labels, and notes are consistent with the text. Check that the same data presented in multiple formats is consistent across formats. Text-table-chart consistency is a high-yield area because the same data appears in multiple places where drift can occur during revision.

### Check Rounding And Significant Figures

Rounding is necessary but introduces its own issues. Rounded figures may not sum exactly to a rounded total. Significant figures imply a precision that may not be warranted. Inconsistent rounding across related figures looks careless. Check rounding and significant figures for appropriateness and consistency.

Verify that rounding is applied consistently across comparable figures. Check that rounded components reconcile with rounded totals, noting where they do not and whether a note is needed. Check that significant figures reflect the actual precision of the data, not implying false precision. For percentages, verify they sum appropriately and note rounding effects. Rounding handling is a detail that affects the work's apparent precision and care. Consistent, appropriate rounding supports credibility; inconsistent or misleading rounding undermines it.

### Flag Implausible Figures For Verification

Some numerical errors are not mismatches against a source but figures that are implausible on their face. A value outside the expected range, a change too extreme to be credible, a figure that contradicts general knowledge, these may be errors even without a specific source check. Flag implausible figures for author verification.

Read with a skeptical eye for figures that seem off. A value orders of magnitude different from expectations, a trend that reverses without explanation, a figure that contradicts well-established benchmarks, these warrant a flag. Note the concern and ask the author to verify. Do not change the figure unilaterally, because it may be correct and surprising, but do not let it pass without verification. Implausibility flags catch errors that source-checking and recalculation miss, because the error is in the figure's relationship to reality, not its transcription or derivation.

## Common Traps

### Treating Numbers As The Author's Responsibility

Numerical accuracy is an editorial responsibility in data-heavy works. Verify or flag.

### Transcription Errors From Sources

Digits mistyped or carried over wrong are common. Check against sources.

### Uncalculated Derived Figures

Calculations contain errors that look plausible. Recalculate independently.

### Unit And Scale Confusion

Right digits with wrong units change meaning drastically. Check explicitly.

### Internal Inconsistencies

Numbers correct individually may contradict each other. Check relationships.

### Text-Table-Chart Mismatches

The same data drifts across formats. Cross-reference for agreement.

### Misleading Rounding And Precision

Inconsistent or false precision undermines credibility. Check appropriateness.

## Self-Check

Before treating numerical accuracy checking as complete, verify:

- Every number has been traced to a source and verified to match, including units and decimals.
- Derived figures have been recalculated independently to verify the arithmetic.
- Units, scales, and decimal places are correct and consistent across related figures.
- Internal consistency has been checked for totals, percentages, growth rates, and repeated figures.
- Text figures have been cross-referenced with tables and charts for exact agreement.
- Rounding is applied consistently and significant figures reflect actual precision.
- Rounded components reconcile with rounded totals, with notes where needed.
- Implausible figures have been flagged for author verification.
- Numbers appearing in multiple places agree exactly across all locations.
- The work's numerical content is accurate, traceable, and internally consistent.
