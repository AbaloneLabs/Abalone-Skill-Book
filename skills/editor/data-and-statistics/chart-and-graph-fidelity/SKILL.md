# Chart And Graph Fidelity

Charts and graphs are powerful because they communicate data visually, but visual power cuts both ways. A well-designed chart clarifies; a poorly designed or misleading chart distorts. Truncated y-axes exaggerate differences. Misleading area proportions misrepresent relative size. Inappropriate chart types obscure patterns. Incorrect labels mislead about what is shown. When a chart does not faithfully represent its data, readers draw false conclusions from an image they trust because it looks authoritative. Chart and graph fidelity is the discipline of verifying that visual data presentations represent their underlying data honestly and clearly. Editors who treat charts as design elements rather than data presentations miss the distortions that mislead readers.

Use this skill when editing works that contain charts, graphs, and other data visualizations. It covers checking chart type appropriateness, axes and scales, visual proportions, labels and legends, and consistency with text and source data. The goal is visualizations that inform honestly rather than mislead.

## Core Rules

### Verify The Chart Type Fits The Data And Message

Different chart types suit different data and messages. Bar charts compare categories. Line charts show trends over time. Pie charts show parts of a whole, though often poorly. Scatter plots show relationships between variables. Choosing the wrong chart type obscures the data or misleads. Verify the chart type is appropriate for what it represents.

For each chart, assess whether the type fits the data and the message. Flag pie charts with many slices or for comparison, which are usually poor choices. Flag bar charts for continuous data where a line would be clearer. Flag 3D charts, which distort perception. Flag dual-axis charts, which can imply misleading relationships. The chart type should make the data's story clear, not obscure or distort it. Appropriate chart selection is the foundation of honest visualization.

### Check Axes, Scales, And Baselines

Axes and scales determine how data appears, and they are frequent sites of distortion. A truncated y-axis exaggerates differences. A nonlinear scale can mislead if not clearly labeled. An inconsistent baseline distorts comparisons. Check axes, scales, and baselines for honesty and clarity.

For each chart, examine the axes. Check that the y-axis baseline is appropriate; bar charts should start at zero to avoid exaggerating differences, and any truncation should be clearly indicated and justified. Check that scale types, linear or logarithmic, are appropriate and clearly labeled. Check that axis intervals are regular and sensible. Check that dual axes, if used, are clearly distinguished. Flag axis choices that exaggerate, minimize, or obscure the data. Honest axes present the data without visual distortion.

### Verify Visual Proportions Represent The Data

Visual elements like bar lengths, bubble sizes, and areas must be proportional to the data they represent. A bar twice as tall should represent twice the value. A bubble with twice the area should represent twice the quantity. Disproportionate visuals mislead about magnitude. Verify visual proportions match the data.

For each chart, check that visual elements are proportional to their values. For bar charts, verify bar lengths correspond to values. For bubble or area charts, verify areas, not radii, are proportional, since radius scaling exaggerates differences. For pictograms, verify the icons scale appropriately. Flag visuals where proportions do not match values, whether through error or design. Misleading proportions are a common form of chart dishonesty, often unintentional but always distorting.

### Check Labels, Legends, And Annotations

Labels, legends, and annotations tell the reader what the chart shows. Missing or unclear labels leave the chart uninterpretable. Incorrect labels mislead. Overlapping or cluttered labels obscure. Check labels, legends, and annotations for completeness, accuracy, and clarity.

For each chart, verify all axes are labeled with what they represent and the units. Verify the legend identifies all data series clearly. Verify data points, bars, or segments are labeled or identifiable. Check that annotations explain rather than clutter. Verify that labels match the data and the text discussion. Flag charts with missing labels, tiny or overlapping text, or legends that do not clearly distinguish series. Clear labeling makes the chart self-explanatory; poor labeling makes it useless or misleading.

### Verify Consistency With Source Data And Text

A chart must agree with its source data and with any text discussion of the same data. A chart that shows different values than the underlying data, or that contradicts the text's characterization, is an error. Verify charts against source data and text.

For each chart, verify the visualized values match the source data exactly. Check that the text discussion of the chart accurately describes what the chart shows. Check that figures cited in the text match the chart. Verify that the chart's title and the text's framing are consistent. Inconsistencies between chart, data, and text are common because charts may be updated while text is not, or vice versa. Cross-verification catches drift and ensures the chart, data, and text tell the same story.

### Assess Honesty Of Visual Framing

Beyond technical correctness, charts can be framed to support a particular impression through choices about what to show and how. Selective time periods, omitted categories, and emphasized trends can all frame data to mislead. Assess the honesty of the visual framing.

For each chart, consider the framing choices. Is the time period representative or cherry-picked to show a favorable trend? Are categories omitted that would change the picture? Is the comparison group appropriate? Is the chart titled neutrally or in a way that imposes an interpretation? Flag framing that appears designed to support a conclusion rather than to inform. Honest charts present data in a way that allows the reader to draw conclusions; misleading charts present data to impose a conclusion. Framing choices are subtle but consequential.

### Check Accessibility Of Visualizations

Charts must be accessible to readers who cannot see them or who have visual impairments. Color should not be the sole means of distinguishing data. Text within charts should be legible. Alternative text should convey the chart's message. Check accessibility provisions.

For each chart, verify color is not the sole means of conveying information, since colorblind readers may not distinguish series. Check that patterns, labels, or other cues distinguish data. Verify text within the chart is legible at the size readers will encounter. Check that alternative text or long descriptions convey the chart's findings for readers who cannot see it. Accessibility ensures the visualization informs all readers, not just those with typical vision. It is both an ethical and often a legal requirement.

## Common Traps

### Treating Charts As Design Rather Than Data

Charts are data presentations. Verify they represent data honestly.

### Inappropriate Chart Types

Wrong types obscure or mislead. Match the chart to the data and message.

### Distorting Axes And Scales

Truncated axes and nonlinear scales can exaggerate or minimize. Check baselines.

### Disproportionate Visual Elements

Bars, bubbles, and areas must be proportional to values. Verify scaling.

### Missing Or Misleading Labels

Unclear labels make charts uninterpretable. Verify completeness and accuracy.

### Inconsistency With Data And Text

Charts drift from source data and text during revision. Cross-verify.

### Misleading Visual Framing

Cherry-picked periods and categories impose conclusions. Assess framing honesty.

## Self-Check

Before treating chart and graph fidelity checking as complete, verify:

- The chart type is appropriate for the data and the message it conveys.
- Axes use appropriate baselines, with bar charts starting at zero or truncation clearly indicated.
- Scale types are appropriate and clearly labeled.
- Visual elements like bars and bubbles are proportional to their values.
- All axes, legends, and data points are clearly and accurately labeled.
- Chart values match the source data exactly.
- Text discussion of the chart accurately describes what the chart shows.
- The visual framing is honest, without cherry-picked periods or misleading comparisons.
- Color is not the sole means of distinguishing data, and text is legible.
- Alternative text or descriptions convey the chart's findings for accessibility.
