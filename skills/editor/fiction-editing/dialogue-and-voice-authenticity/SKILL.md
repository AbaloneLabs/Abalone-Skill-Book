# Dialogue And Voice Authenticity

Dialogue is where characters become present to the reader. When dialogue rings true, readers hear the characters and believe in them as people; when it rings false, readers hear the author maneuvering figures through a scene. Authentic dialogue is not transcription of real speech, which is messy, repetitive, and circular, but it must carry the rhythm, indirection, and specificity that make speech feel alive. The fiction editor's dialogue work is to judge whether each character sounds like themselves, whether the dialogue advances scene or character, whether subtext operates beneath the surface, and whether the speech patterns, dialect, and voice are rendered with craft and respect. This is delicate work, because dialogue that fails collapses the illusion of character faster than any other element.

## Core Rules

### Make Each Character's Voice Distinct And Consistent

Characters must sound different from one another, and each must sound like themselves consistently. Voice is built from vocabulary, syntax, sentence length, rhythm, verbal habits, and the content a character chooses to express or withhold. The editor reads dialogue passages with attributions removed and asks whether each speaker is identifiable. If voices blend, differentiation is needed. Consistency matters too: a character's voice should hold across scenes, evolving only through deliberate arc, not drifting chapter to chapter. Track each character's voice signature and flag inconsistencies.

### Ensure Dialogue Advances Scene, Character, Or Conflict

Dialogue that fills space, restates what the reader already knows, or exists only to convey information to the reader feels inert. Every exchange should advance the scene, reveal character, deepen conflict, or create tension. The editor asks, for each passage of dialogue, what it accomplishes. Exposition delivered through characters saying things they both already know, sometimes called "as you know, Bob" dialogue, is a common failure. Flag dialogue that functions only as information delivery and ask whether the information can emerge through conflict, discovery, or action.

### Read For Subtext And Indirection

Real speech rarely states its meaning directly. People evade, imply, change the subject, and speak around what they mean, especially in emotional or conflict-laden moments. The editor reads for subtext: what is the character saying beneath the surface? Dialogue where characters state their feelings and intentions plainly feels on-the-nose and false. The strongest dialogue has the characters saying one thing while meaning another, or avoiding the real subject entirely. Flag exchanges that feel too direct, especially in emotional scenes, and ask what indirection or evasion would deepen them.

### Vary Dialogue With Action And Interior Beat

Long stretches of unbroken speech feel disembodied. Real conversation is embedded in physical context: characters gesture, move, hesitate, notice their surroundings, and think between words. The editor checks whether dialogue is grounded with action beats and interior thought that reveal character and anchor the scene. Action beats also control pacing, breaking speech to build tension or slow revelation. But avoid over-tagging; constant "he said, she said" with attached gestures becomes noisy. Balance speech with meaningful physical and interior context.

### Render Dialect And Speech Variation With Craft And Respect

Dialect, accent, and speech variation can vitalize dialogue, but they carry risks. Phonetic spelling that is hard to parse slows readers and can feel mocking. Stereotyped speech patterns attached to demographic groups reduce individuals to types. The editor evaluates dialect rendering for legibility, consistency, and respect. Prefer selective signal, a few consistent markers that suggest the speech pattern, over heavy phonetic spelling. Ensure dialect is consistent within a character and does not stereotype a group. When dialect marks a marginalized group, scrutinize especially for caricature and harm.

### Judge Dialogue Tags And Attribution

Dialogue tags, "said," "asked," and alternatives, structure who speaks. The editor evaluates tag use. "Said" is invisible and generally preferred; constant use of expressive tags like "exclaimed," "opined," or "intoned" draws attention to themselves and signals weak dialogue that needs adverbs to convey tone. Adverbial tags, "said angrily," often indicate the dialogue itself does not convey the emotion. Flag over-reliance on expressive tags and adverbs, and ask whether the dialogue can carry its own tone. Action beats can replace tags and ground the scene simultaneously.

### Calibrate Formality And Register To Character And Context

Characters speak differently in different contexts: a character formal at work may be casual at home, guarded with strangers and open with intimates. The editor checks that register, the level of formality, matches the character and the situation. A streetwise character who suddenly speaks in polished paragraphs feels false; an academic who never uses their vocabulary feels underdrawn. Register shifts with context, and consistency within context matters. Flag dialogue where the register does not match the established character or scene.

### Evaluate The Rhythm And Length Of Speech

Speech rhythm affects pace and character. Short, clipped lines create tension and speed; long, flowing lines slow pace and suggest reflection or rambling. Characters should have characteristic rhythms: an impatient character speaks in fragments; a verbose character over-explains. The editor reads dialogue aloud when possible, because the ear catches what the eye misses. Flag passages where the rhythm is monotonous, where all characters speak in similar-length sentences, or where the rhythm fights the scene's emotional intent.

## Common Traps

### On-The-Nose Dialogue That States Everything

Characters who say exactly what they feel and want feel mechanical. Real people, and convincing characters, evade, imply, and contradict themselves. The editor flags dialogue that states emotions or intentions too directly, especially in high-stakes scenes, and asks what subtext or indirection would make the exchange feel lived-in. The gap between what a character says and what they mean is where character lives.

### Exposition Disguised As Dialogue

When characters tell each other things they already know, solely so the reader learns them, the dialogue feels artificial. The editor catches "as you know" constructions and exchanges where one character explains shared history or world details to another for the reader's benefit. Prefer information emerging through conflict, a character's misunderstanding, a discovery, or a scene where the information is naturally new to at least one participant.

### Indistinguishable Voices

When characters sound alike, the reader loses the sense of distinct people. The editor reads dialogue without attributions and checks whether speakers are identifiable. If not, voices need differentiation through vocabulary, syntax, rhythm, and content. Distinctiveness is not about tics; it is about how each character thinks, which shapes how they speak. A character's preoccupations, education, and worldview should mark their speech.

### Over-Tagging And Adverbial Tags

Excessive dialogue tags, especially expressive ones with adverbs, clutter the page and signal that the dialogue itself is not carrying its weight. The editor flags passages dense with "he said angrily," "she replied softly," and similar constructions, and asks whether the dialogue can convey tone on its own. Prefer "said" or action beats, and trust the dialogue to do the emotional work.

### Phonetic Dialect That Obscures Or Stereotypes

Heavy phonetic spelling slows reading and can feel caricaturing, especially when applied to marginalized groups. The editor evaluates dialect for legibility and respect, preferring selective markers over dense phonetics. Stereotyped speech attached to demographics reduces individuals to types and can cause harm. Flag dialect that is hard to read, inconsistent, or reductive, and ask whether fewer, consistent signals would suggest the pattern without burden.

### Monologue Syndrome

Characters who deliver long speeches, especially in conversation, feel like authorial mouthpieces. Real conversation is interactive: characters interrupt, deflect, and respond. The editor flags extended monologues and asks whether the exchange can be broken into genuine interaction. Monologues have their place, in confessions, revelations, or character showcases, but they should be rare and earned, not the default mode.

### Dialogue That Fights The Scene's Emotion

Dialogue whose rhythm or content contradicts the scene's emotional intent weakens both. Rapid, witty banter in a scene of grief can work as deflection but feels wrong if unacknowledged. Slow, meandering speech in an action scene kills momentum. The editor reads dialogue against the scene's intent and flags mismatches. Dialogue should serve the scene's emotional arc, not fight it.

### Inconsistent Voice Within A Character

A character who speaks formally in one scene and casually in the next, without contextual reason, feels unstable. The editor tracks each character's voice and flags drift. Voice can evolve through arc, but random inconsistency reads as error. Maintain a voice signature for each character and check dialogue against it throughout the work.

## Self-Check

- Is each major character's voice distinct, identifiable without attribution, and consistent across the work?
- Does every passage of dialogue advance the scene, reveal character, deepen conflict, or build tension, rather than merely filling space?
- Does dialogue operate with subtext, saying one thing while meaning another, rather than stating emotions and intentions on the nose?
- Is dialogue grounded with meaningful action beats and interior thought, without over-tagging?
- Is any dialect or speech variation rendered legibly, consistently, and respectfully, without stereotyping or caricature?
- Are dialogue tags restrained, with reliance on "said" and action beats rather than expressive tags and adverbs?
- Does the register and formality of each character's speech match their established identity and the scene's context?
- Does the rhythm and length of speech vary meaningfully, fitting each character and the scene's emotional pace?
- Have exposition-heavy exchanges where characters tell each other known information been flagged for revision?; have extended monologues been evaluated for whether they earn their length or should become interaction?
- Does the dialogue's rhythm support rather than fight the scene's emotional intent?; has reading dialogue aloud, or mentally hearing it, confirmed that it sounds like distinct people rather than written text?
