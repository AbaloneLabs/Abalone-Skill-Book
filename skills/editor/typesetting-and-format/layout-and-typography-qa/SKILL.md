# Layout And Typography QA

Layout and typography are invisible when they work and conspicuous when they fail. After a manuscript is designed and typeset, new categories of error appear that did not exist in the manuscript stage: awkward page breaks, inconsistent spacing, misaligned elements, font inconsistencies, rivers of white space, and visual imbalances that distract the reader. These problems are not visible until the content is laid out, so they require a dedicated QA pass on the designed pages. Editors who treat layout as purely a designer's responsibility miss the opportunity to catch problems that affect readability and professional appearance. Layout and typography QA is the discipline of reading the designed pages critically, catching what manuscript editing cannot see.

Use this skill when reviewing proofs or designed pages before publication, for print or digital formats. It covers checking page-level layout, typography consistency, spacing and alignment, and visual coherence. The goal is designed pages that present the content readably and professionally.

## Core Rules

### Review The Designed Pages, Not The Manuscript

Layout QA happens on the typeset pages, not the manuscript. The errors you are catching, page breaks, spacing, alignment, visual balance, only exist in the laid-out form. Approach the review with the designed pages in front of you, whether print proofs or a PDF, and read them as a reader would encounter them.

Obtain the final or near-final designed pages. Review them in the format readers will experience, print proofs for print, rendered pages for digital. Page through the entire work systematically. The perspective shift from manuscript to designed pages reveals a different category of error, and both must be checked. Do not assume that a clean manuscript produces clean pages; layout introduces its own issues that require their own review.

### Check Page Breaks And Column Breaks

Page and column breaks determine how content flows, and bad breaks disrupt reading. A heading stranded at the bottom of a page, a paragraph split awkwardly across pages, a table or figure separated from its reference, these are layout errors that manuscript editing cannot prevent. Check breaks throughout.

Verify that headings are not stranded at the bottom of a page or column with their content on the next. Check that paragraphs do not break awkwardly, leaving a single line (a widow or orphan) at the top or bottom of a page. Verify that tables, figures, and their captions stay together and appear near their first reference in the text. Check that block quotations, lists, and other special elements are not split across pages where it disrupts comprehension. Where breaks are problematic, flag them for adjustment. Good breaks are invisible; bad breaks are distracting.

### Verify Typography Consistency

Typography, the fonts, sizes, weights, and treatments applied to text, must be consistent throughout. A body font that shifts, a heading size that varies, an italic treatment that appears inconsistently, these signal production problems. Verify typography consistency across the designed pages.

Check that the body text uses a consistent font, size, and leading throughout. Check that heading levels are styled consistently, with each level using the same font, size, weight, and spacing. Check that emphasis, italics, bold, and special treatments are applied consistently for the same purposes. Check that special elements like block quotations, captions, and footnotes use consistent typography. Look for font substitutions or rendering issues, especially in digital formats. Typography inconsistency is highly visible because the eye detects shifts in text treatment.

### Check Spacing And Alignment

Spacing and alignment problems are subtle but cumulative. Inconsistent paragraph spacing, misaligned elements, irregular line spacing, and uneven margins create a visual roughness that reads as unprofessional. Check spacing and alignment systematically.

Check that paragraph spacing is consistent throughout, with the same space before and after paragraphs. Check that line spacing, leading, is consistent within and across sections. Verify that elements like block quotations, lists, and tables have consistent spacing around them. Check alignment: left, right, centered, or justified text should be consistent within element types. Check that margins are consistent and that running heads and page numbers align correctly. Look for irregular spacing that may indicate hidden formatting problems. Consistent spacing and alignment create the smooth visual surface that reads as professional.

### Look For Visual Balance And White Space

Beyond mechanical consistency, designed pages should have visual balance. Pages that are too dense, pages with large awkward gaps, facing pages that are visually unbalanced, these affect the reading experience even when no rule is broken. Assess visual balance and white space distribution.

Assess each page or spread for visual balance. Look for pages that are overly dense with no relief, or pages with large unexplained gaps. Check facing pages in print for visual balance. Look for rivers of white space that run vertically through text. Check that white space is distributed in a way that supports reading rather than distracting. Where visual balance is problematic, flag for designer attention. Balance is a judgment, but gross imbalances are clear and worth catching.

### Check Special Elements And Their Integration

Tables, figures, sidebars, pull quotes, boxes, and other special elements have their own layout considerations and often introduce layout problems. They must be sized, placed, and integrated with the text consistently. Check special elements as a category.

For each table and figure, check that it fits within the page or column, is not cut off or distorted, and is sized appropriately. Verify that captions are positioned consistently and styled consistently. Check that sidebars, boxes, and pull quotes are formatted consistently and do not disrupt the main text flow. Check that special elements do not cause awkward text reflow around them. Special elements are visually prominent, so inconsistencies or problems in their treatment are especially noticeable.

### Verify Across The Entire Work

Layout consistency must hold across the entire work, not just in representative pages. Problems can appear in specific sections due to content characteristics or production issues. Review the entire work, not a sample.

Page through every page of the designed work. Do not assume that checking representative pages catches all problems, because issues can be localized. Pay attention to transitions between sections, which may reveal inconsistencies. Check front and back matter with the same rigor as the body. For long works, structure the review in sessions to maintain attention. Comprehensive review is time-consuming but necessary to catch the layout problems that detract from the published work.

## Common Traps

### Reviewing The Manuscript Instead Of Designed Pages

Layout errors only exist in laid-out form. Review the typeset pages.

### Stranded Headings And Widows Or Orphans

Bad breaks disrupt reading. Check page and column breaks throughout.

### Typography Drift Across Sections

Fonts, sizes, and treatments must be consistent. Verify throughout.

### Inconsistent Spacing And Alignment

Subtle spacing problems accumulate into an unprofessional appearance. Check systematically.

### Ignoring Visual Balance

Gross imbalances and awkward white space affect reading. Assess each page.

### Neglecting Special Elements

Tables, figures, and sidebars have their own layout issues. Check them as a category.

### Sampling Instead Of Comprehensive Review

Problems can be localized. Review every page.

## Self-Check

Before treating layout and typography QA as complete, verify:

- The review has been performed on the designed, typeset pages, not the manuscript.
- Page and column breaks have been checked for stranded headings, widows, and orphans.
- Tables and figures stay with their captions and appear near their text references.
- Typography, including fonts, sizes, weights, and treatments, is consistent throughout.
- Spacing within and between elements is consistent, including paragraph and line spacing.
- Alignment of text, margins, running heads, and page numbers is consistent.
- Visual balance and white space distribution have been assessed for each page or spread.
- Special elements like tables, figures, sidebars, and pull quotes are consistently formatted.
- Special elements do not cause problematic text reflow.
- Every page has been reviewed, not just a sample.
