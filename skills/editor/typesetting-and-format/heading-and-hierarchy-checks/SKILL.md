# Heading And Hierarchy Checks

Headings are the architecture of a document. They signal structure, guide navigation, and set reader expectations for what each section contains. When headings are inconsistent, misleveled, or poorly phrased, readers lose their way: they cannot tell what is major and what is minor, they cannot navigate efficiently, and they form inaccurate expectations of content. Heading and hierarchy checks verify that the heading structure is consistent, logical, and useful. This is not merely a formatting matter; it is a structural matter that affects how readers comprehend and navigate the work. Editors who treat headings as decoration miss the opportunity to ensure the document's architecture is sound.

Use this skill when reviewing the structure of any headed document, from articles and reports to books and web content. It covers checking heading levels, parallelism, informativeness, and navigability. The goal is a heading structure that accurately reflects and enables access to the content.

## Core Rules

### Verify Heading Levels Reflect Content Structure

Heading levels, H1, H2, H3, and beyond, encode the hierarchy of content. An H2 signals a major section, an H3 a subsection, and so on. When levels are misused, skipping from H2 to H4 or using H3 for content that is actually major, the hierarchy misrepresents the structure and confuses readers. Verify that heading levels accurately reflect the content hierarchy.

Map the heading structure of the document, noting the level of each heading. Check that levels are used consistently: an H3 should always be a subdivision of the preceding H2, not a peer. Check that levels are not skipped; do not jump from H2 to H4. Check that the visual weight, font size and treatment, matches the hierarchical level, so readers can perceive the structure visually. Where the heading levels do not match the content structure, flag for correction. The heading hierarchy should be a faithful map of the content's organization.

### Ensure Parallel Phrasing Within Levels

Headings at the same level should follow parallel phrasing, meaning they use consistent grammatical structure. If one H2 is a noun phrase, all H2s should ideally be noun phrases; if one is a question, all should be questions. Parallel phrasing makes the structure feel intentional and makes headings predictable for the reader.

Review headings at each level for parallelism. Where headings at the same level vary in structure, some noun phrases, some questions, some imperative verbs, consider standardizing to a consistent form. The chosen form should suit the content and audience. Parallelism is not absolute; occasional variation can be effective, but systematic inconsistency reads as careless. Flag non-parallel headings and recommend standardization. Parallel headings signal that the structure was designed, not assembled ad hoc.

### Check That Headings Are Informative And Accurate

Headings should tell readers what the section contains, accurately and specifically. A vague heading like "Background" or "Overview" tells the reader little; a heading that does not match the content misleads. Check that headings are informative enough to guide readers and accurate enough to match what follows.

For each heading, assess whether it conveys the section's content clearly and specifically. Flag vague or generic headings and suggest more informative alternatives. Verify that the heading accurately reflects the content; if a section titled "Methods" actually discusses results, the heading is wrong. Check that headings are not misleadingly narrow or broad relative to their content. Informative, accurate headings let readers navigate efficiently and set correct expectations, reducing frustration and supporting comprehension.

### Verify Heading Hierarchy Is Navigable

The heading hierarchy should support navigation, allowing readers to find sections, understand the structure at a glance, and move through the work efficiently. This is especially important for digital works with navigable tables of contents, but it applies to print as well. Verify the hierarchy is navigable.

Check that the heading structure produces a logical and complete table of contents or outline. Verify that the hierarchy is not too deep, which makes navigation cumbersome, or too shallow, which buries content. Check that major sections are distinguishable from minor ones. For digital works, verify that headings are tagged with correct levels for accessibility and navigation tools. For works with cross-references, verify that section references align with the heading structure. A navigable hierarchy lets readers use the document effectively rather than getting lost in undifferentiated content.

### Check Heading Numbering And Labeling

Some documents number their headings, 1, 1.1, 1.1.1, or use labels like "Chapter," "Section," "Part." Numbering and labeling must be consistent and correct throughout. Errors in numbering are highly visible and undermine confidence in the document's production.

Verify that heading numbering, if used, is sequential and correct at every level. Check that the numbering scheme is consistent and appropriate for the document type. Verify that labels like "Chapter" or "Section" are used consistently. Check that numbering updates correctly after any structural changes, since renumbering is a common source of error. For documents with complex numbering, verify the scheme is applied without gaps or duplicates. Numbering errors signal carelessness and can cause real confusion in cross-references.

### Ensure Heading Style Consistency

Beyond hierarchy, heading visual style must be consistent within levels. All H1s should look the same, all H2s should look the same, and so on. Style drift in headings is highly visible because headings are prominent. Verify heading style consistency.

Check that all headings at the same level use the same font, size, weight, color, capitalization, and spacing. Check that heading punctuation is consistent, whether headings end with periods or not. Check that heading capitalization follows a consistent style, title case, sentence case, or all caps, as appropriate. Check that the spacing before and after headings is consistent within levels. Heading style consistency contributes to the document's professional appearance and reinforces the hierarchy visually.

### Review The Complete Heading Architecture

Individual heading checks are necessary but not sufficient; the complete heading architecture should be reviewed as a whole to ensure it is coherent and balanced. A document where some sections have deep subsection hierarchies and others have none may be unbalanced. Review the architecture holistically.

Compile the complete heading outline. Assess whether the structure is balanced: are sections proportionate, is the depth of hierarchy appropriate across the document, are there sections that need subdivision or consolidation? Check that the overall organization flows logically. A coherent heading architecture reflects thoughtful organization and supports reader comprehension and navigation. Holistic review catches imbalances and structural issues that heading-by-heading checking misses.

## Common Traps

### Treating Headings As Decoration

Headings encode structure and guide navigation. Verify their hierarchy and content.

### Misused Or Skipped Heading Levels

Levels must reflect content hierarchy without skipping. Map and verify the structure.

### Non-Parallel Headings At The Same Level

Inconsistent phrasing reads as careless. Standardize within levels.

### Vague Or Inaccurate Headings

Headings must inform and match content. Flag and improve vague or misleading headings.

### Numbering Errors After Structural Changes

Renumbering is error-prone. Verify numbering is sequential and correct throughout.

### Heading Style Drift

Visual style must be consistent within levels. Check fonts, sizes, capitalization, and spacing.

### Missing Holistic Architecture Review

Individual checks miss imbalances. Review the complete heading outline.

## Self-Check

Before treating heading and hierarchy checks as complete, verify:

- Heading levels accurately reflect the content hierarchy without skips or misuse.
- Visual weight of headings matches their hierarchical level.
- Headings at the same level follow parallel phrasing.
- Each heading is informative and accurately reflects its section's content.
- The heading hierarchy is navigable and produces a logical table of contents or outline.
- Heading numbering and labeling, if used, is sequential, correct, and consistent.
- Heading visual style is consistent within levels, including font, size, capitalization, and spacing.
- The complete heading architecture has been reviewed for balance and coherence.
- Cross-references to sections align with the actual heading structure.
- Headings are tagged with correct levels for digital navigation and accessibility.
