# Image And Caption Alignment

Images, figures, and tables carry meaning that text alone cannot, but only when they are properly integrated. A figure placed far from its discussion forces readers to flip pages. A caption that does not match its image confuses. A label that does not correspond to its text reference strands the visual element. Image and caption alignment is the discipline of ensuring every visual element is correctly paired with its caption, positioned near its reference, sized and labeled consistently, and described accurately. Editors who treat images as someone else's domain miss the integration problems that make visual elements fail their purpose.

Use this skill when reviewing documents that contain images, figures, tables, or other visual elements, in print or digital formats. It covers checking figure-text pairing, positioning, labeling, caption accuracy, and consistency of treatment. The goal is visual elements that work with the text to communicate effectively.

## Core Rules

### Verify Every Visual Element Has A Caption And Reference

Every image, figure, and table should have a caption and should be referenced in the text. A visual element without a caption leaves the reader to guess its purpose; a visual element never referenced in the text floats disconnected. Verify the basic integration of every visual element.

Compile a list of all visual elements in the document. For each, verify it has a caption that identifies and describes it. Verify that each element is referenced in the text, typically by figure or table number. Flag any element lacking a caption or text reference for resolution. Elements that exist without integration are either unnecessary or insufficiently connected; either way, they fail to serve the reader. Complete pairing is the foundation of visual element integration.

### Check Positioning Relative To Text References

Visual elements should appear near their first text reference, so readers encounter them in context without searching. A figure referenced on page 10 that appears on page 20 forces readers to navigate and breaks comprehension. Check positioning throughout.

For each visual element, verify it appears near its first text reference, ideally on the same page or the facing page. Flag elements that are separated from their reference by multiple pages. Check that elements do not appear before their first reference, which can confuse. For elements referenced multiple times, verify the element appears near the first or most important reference. Where positioning is problematic, flag for layout adjustment. Proximity between text and visual supports the integrated reading experience.

### Verify Labeling And Numbering Consistency

Figures and tables are typically labeled and numbered, and the labeling must be consistent and correct. Figure 1 should be followed by Figure 2, not Figure 3; tables and figures should be numbered in separate sequences or a consistent combined scheme. Verify labeling and numbering throughout.

Check that figures and tables are numbered sequentially without gaps or duplicates. Verify that the numbering scheme is consistent, separate sequences for figures and tables, or a consistent combined scheme. Check that text references to figures and tables match the actual labels. Verify that label format is consistent, "Figure 1," "Fig. 1," "1," throughout. For works with chapters, verify whether numbering restarts per chapter and is applied consistently. Labeling errors are highly visible because readers use labels to locate elements.

### Check Caption Accuracy And Completeness

Captions should accurately describe the visual element and provide enough context for the reader to understand it. A caption that contradicts the image, omits essential information, or merely restates the heading is unhelpful. Check caption accuracy and completeness.

For each caption, verify it accurately describes the corresponding element. Check that the caption matches what is actually shown, not what was intended or an earlier version. Verify the caption provides enough context: source attribution, explanation of symbols or abbreviations, units for data, and any necessary interpretation. Check that captions are consistent in style and level of detail across the document. Flag captions that are inaccurate, vague, or incomplete. Accurate captions let readers understand visual elements without guessing.

### Verify Image Quality And Integrity

The images themselves must be of sufficient quality and correctly rendered. Low-resolution images, distorted aspect ratios, cropped-out content, or color problems undermine the visual element's effectiveness. Verify image quality and integrity.

For each image, check that resolution is sufficient for the output format, higher for print than for screen. Check that aspect ratios are not distorted, which makes images look stretched or compressed. Verify that no important content is cropped out. Check color reproduction, especially where color carries meaning. For images with text, verify the text is legible. Flag image quality problems for designer or author attention. Poor image quality reflects on the professionalism of the entire work and can render visual elements unusable.

### Check Consistency Of Visual Element Treatment

Visual elements should be treated consistently throughout the document. Caption placement, figure borders, table formatting, sizing conventions, and other treatments should follow a consistent pattern. Inconsistency reads as unprofessional. Check treatment consistency.

Verify that captions are positioned consistently relative to their elements, above or below, and aligned consistently. Check that figure and table borders, rules, and shading are consistent. Check that sizing is consistent for similar types of elements. Verify that permissions and source lines are formatted consistently. Check that the styling of captions is consistent, font, size, weight. Consistent treatment of visual elements contributes to the document's coherence and professional appearance.

### Verify Accessibility Of Visual Elements

For digital works especially, visual elements must be accessible to readers who cannot see them, through alternative text, long descriptions, or other accommodations. Accessibility is both an ethical and often legal requirement. Verify accessibility provisions.

For each visual element, verify it has appropriate alternative text that conveys its meaning to readers who cannot see it. Check that complex figures have longer descriptions where needed. Verify that text within images is also available as actual text for screen readers. Check that color is not the sole means of conveying information, since colorblind readers may miss it. For data tables, verify proper markup for screen reader navigation. Accessibility provisions ensure the work reaches all readers, not just those who can see the visuals.

## Common Traps

### Treating Images As Someone Else's Domain

Visual element integration is an editorial responsibility. Check pairing and positioning.

### Unreferenced Or Uncaptioned Elements

Every visual element needs a caption and text reference. Verify complete integration.

### Elements Separated From Their References

Proximity supports comprehension. Check positioning relative to text references.

### Labeling And Numbering Errors

Gaps, duplicates, and mismatches are highly visible. Verify sequential, consistent labeling.

### Inaccurate Or Incomplete Captions

Captions must match and explain their elements. Check accuracy and completeness.

### Neglecting Image Quality

Low resolution, distortion, and cropping undermine visuals. Check image integrity.

### Overlooking Accessibility

Visual elements must be accessible to all readers. Verify alternative text and accommodations.

## Self-Check

Before treating image and caption alignment as complete, verify:

- Every visual element has a caption and is referenced in the text.
- Each element appears near its first text reference without large separations.
- Figure and table numbering is sequential, without gaps or duplicates.
- Text references to figures and tables match the actual labels.
- Captions accurately describe their elements and provide necessary context.
- Captions include source attribution, units, and explanations where needed.
- Images are of sufficient resolution and not distorted, cropped, or poorly reproduced.
- Treatment of visual elements is consistent throughout, including caption placement and styling.
- Visual elements are accessible through alternative text and other accommodations.
- Color is not the sole means of conveying information in any element.
