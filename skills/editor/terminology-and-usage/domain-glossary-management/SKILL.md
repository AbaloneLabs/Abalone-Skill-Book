# Domain Glossary Management

Specialized works depend on domain terminology that readers may not know. When a term is used without definition, used inconsistently, or used to mean different things in different places, readers lose their footing. The work assumes knowledge the reader may not have, or worse, it teaches the reader one meaning and then uses the term differently. Domain glossary management is the discipline of building and maintaining a controlled vocabulary for a work, ensuring each term is defined on first use, used consistently throughout, and reconciled where competing terms refer to the same concept. Editors who treat terminology as the author's problem produce works that alienate or confuse the target reader.

Use this skill when editing technical, academic, or specialized works that rely on domain terminology. It covers building a glossary, establishing first-use definitions, enforcing consistency, and reconciling competing terms. The goal is a vocabulary that is transparent to the intended reader and internally coherent.

## Core Rules

### Build A Working Glossary During The Edit

The foundation of terminology management is a glossary that captures every domain term, its definition, and its usage rules. Build this glossary as you edit rather than after. Each time you encounter a domain term, verify it is in the glossary with a clear definition and consistent usage. The glossary becomes the reference against which all subsequent uses are checked.

For each domain term, record: the term itself, the definition the work establishes, the first-use location where it is defined, any variants or abbreviations used, and any usage notes such as part of speech or register. When you encounter a term not yet in the glossary, add it and check whether it should have been defined on first use. When you encounter a term already in the glossary used differently, flag the inconsistency. The glossary is a living document that grows with the edit and serves as the consistency reference.

### Ensure First-Use Definition For Reader Comprehension

Every domain term should be defined or made clear the first time a reader encounters it. A reader who meets an undefined term must either guess its meaning or stop to look it up, and both break comprehension. Check that each domain term is introduced with enough context for the target reader to understand it.

Verify that each term is defined on first use, either through an explicit definition, a parenthetical gloss, or sufficient surrounding context. Check that the first use the reader encounters is actually the first use in the work, not a later use where the term is defined. If a term is introduced in a summary or abstract and then used in the body, ensure the definition travels with the reader. For works with multiple entry points, such as reference works or chapters read independently, consider defining terms in each major section. The reader should never be left to guess what a term means.

### Enforce Consistent Usage Of Defined Terms

Once a term is defined, it must be used consistently to mean the same thing throughout. Shifting usage, using a term to mean different things in different sections, is a subtle but serious inconsistency that undermines the reader's model. After definition, verify every subsequent use matches the established meaning.

Check that each term is used with its defined meaning wherever it appears. Flag any instance where a term is used differently, and resolve by either aligning the usage to the definition or, if the shift is intentional, clarifying the new usage explicitly. Check that abbreviations and variants are used consistently: if a term is abbreviated after first use, the abbreviation should be used consistently rather than alternating with the full term. Consistent usage means the reader builds a stable mental model of what each term means and can rely on it.

### Reconcile Competing Terms For The Same Concept

A common terminology problem is multiple terms referring to the same concept. The author may use "model," "framework," and "paradigm" interchangeably, or alternate between "user" and "customer" without distinction. Unless the distinction is meaningful, competing terms create confusion about whether they refer to different things. Reconcile competing terms by choosing one and applying it consistently.

Identify clusters of terms that appear to refer to the same concept. Determine whether the author intends a distinction; if so, clarify and define each term's specific meaning. If no distinction is intended, choose the preferred term and replace the others consistently throughout. Record the decision in the glossary. Reconciliation reduces the vocabulary burden on the reader and ensures that when a different term appears, it signals a genuinely different concept.

### Manage Abbreviations And Acronyms Systematically

Abbreviations and acronyms are a specialized terminology problem. They must be introduced, expanded, and used consistently, or they become opaque to readers who do not know them. Manage abbreviations as part of the glossary, with rules for introduction and usage.

For each abbreviation or acronym, verify it is spelled out on first use with the abbreviation in parentheses, then used consistently thereafter. Check that the expansion matches the abbreviation and that the abbreviation is used consistently rather than alternating with the full form. For very common abbreviations that the target reader will know, expansion may be unnecessary, but document this assumption. For abbreviations used in summaries or abstracts, verify they are expanded there as well, since these sections may be read independently. Unmanaged abbreviations are a major source of reader confusion in technical works.

### Match Terminology To The Target Reader's Knowledge

Terminology decisions depend on who the reader is. A term that is common knowledge to experts may be opaque to general readers, and a term that is appropriately precise for specialists may be unnecessarily technical for a lay audience. Calibrate terminology management to the target reader's expected knowledge.

Determine the target reader's likely familiarity with the domain vocabulary. For expert readers, assume knowledge of standard terms and focus on consistency and precise usage. For general or student readers, expand the glossary to include more first-use definitions and accessible explanations. For mixed audiences, layer the treatment: define on first use for the less expert reader while not over-explaining for the expert. Terminology that is well-managed for the wrong reader is still poorly managed. Calibrate to the actual audience.

### Document The Glossary For Verification And Maintenance

The glossary should be documented as part of the style sheet or as a standalone reference so it can be verified and maintained. Documentation ensures that terminology decisions persist beyond the edit and that downstream editors or authors can maintain consistency. Record the glossary in a form that is useful for verification.

Compile the glossary alphabetically with each term, its definition, usage notes, and any reconciliation decisions. Note where competing terms were unified and which form was chosen. This documentation allows a proofreader to verify terminology consistency, allows the choice to be defended, and allows future editions to maintain the same vocabulary. An undocumented glossary is lost when the edit ends; a documented one persists as the work's controlled vocabulary.

## Common Traps

### Treating Terminology As The Author's Problem

Editors who do not manage terminology produce works that confuse readers. Terminology is an editorial responsibility.

### Missing First-Use Definitions

Undefined terms force readers to guess or stop. Verify each domain term is clear on first encounter.

### Allowing Shifting Usage Of Defined Terms

A term used to mean different things undermines the reader's model. Enforce consistent meaning.

### Leaving Competing Terms Unreconciled

Multiple terms for one concept confuse readers about whether distinctions exist. Reconcile to one.

### Mismanaging Abbreviations

Abbreviations must be introduced, expanded on first use, and used consistently. Check each one.

### Calibrating To The Wrong Reader

Terminology well-managed for experts may fail for general readers. Match to the actual audience.

### Failing To Document The Glossary

Undocumented terminology decisions are lost. Record the glossary for verification and maintenance.

## Self-Check

Before treating domain glossary management as complete, verify:

- A working glossary has been built capturing every domain term, definition, and usage rule.
- Each domain term is defined or made clear on first use for the target reader.
- First-use definitions appear at the actual first encounter, including in summaries or abstracts.
- Defined terms are used consistently to mean the same thing throughout the work.
- Competing terms for the same concept have been reconciled to one preferred form.
- Distinctions between similar terms are clarified where the author intends them.
- Abbreviations and acronyms are introduced, expanded on first use, and used consistently.
- Terminology management is calibrated to the target reader's expected knowledge.
- The glossary is documented in the style sheet or as a standalone reference.
- Reconciliation decisions and preferred forms are recorded for verification and maintenance.
