# Research Integrity And Ethics

Research integrity is the foundation on which the credibility of academic work rests, and integrity failures cause harm that extends far beyond the individual work, eroding trust in the publications, the institutions, and the fields involved. Editors are often the first line of detection, because the signals of misconduct, unusual text similarity, questionable data patterns, missing ethics approvals, or undisclosed conflicts, frequently appear in the manuscript before they appear anywhere else. The editorial role in integrity is not to investigate or adjudicate misconduct, which belongs to institutions, but to recognize the signals, to act on them appropriately, and to protect the publication from becoming a vehicle for harm.

Use this skill when reviewing academic or research work for integrity and ethics at any stage before publication. It covers detecting plagiarism and inappropriate text reuse, checking author attribution and contributorship, verifying ethics approval and consent for human and animal subjects, spotting data fabrication, falsification, and image manipulation, ensuring conflicts of interest are disclosed, and flagging potential misconduct for appropriate escalation. The editor's task is to recognize integrity risks accurately, apply editorial remedies where possible, and escalate suspected misconduct through proper channels rather than resolving it unilaterally.

## Core Rules

### Screen For Plagiarism And Inappropriate Text Reuse

Plagiarism, presenting another's work or words as one's own, is among the most common and most damaging integrity failures. It ranges from verbatim copying without attribution to uncredited paraphrase to extensive reuse of one's own prior work, self-plagiarism, which can also be problematic when undisclosed. Editors should screen for text similarity as a routine part of review, because plagiarism is often detectable in the manuscript before any external report.

Use similarity-detection tools where available, and read with attention to shifts in voice, register, or quality that may indicate borrowed text. For detected similarity, distinguish problematic reuse from acceptable practice. Properly attributed quotation is not plagiarism; properly cited paraphrase is not plagiarism. The problem arises when words or ideas are presented without attribution, or when prior work is reused without disclosure in venues that require originality. For self-reuse, check whether the publication's policy requires disclosure of prior publication, since norms vary by field. Where plagiarism is detected, the response depends on severity, from correction and attribution for minor cases to rejection and institutional notification for serious or extensive copying. The editor's role is to detect and characterize the problem accurately, not to resolve it alone.

### Verify Author Attribution And Contributorship

Authorship disputes and inappropriate attribution are common integrity problems. Authorship should reflect genuine contribution to the work, and practices that distort attribution, gift authorship for those who did not contribute, ghost authorship that omits those who did, or coercive authorship that pressures inclusion, are integrity failures even when the underlying research is sound. Editors should verify that authorship is appropriate and that contributions are transparent.

Check that the author list reflects genuine contribution and that the corresponding author can speak for all authors. Many publications now require contributorship statements specifying each author's role, and these help clarify attribution and detect honorary or ghost authors. Watch for signs of disputed or coerced authorship, authors added or removed late in the process, unusually large or small author lists relative to the work, or corresponding authors who seem unable to answer for all listed authors. For work with institutional or corporate involvement, check that the author list represents the intellectual contributors rather than the sponsoring organization. Where attribution seems inappropriate, query it; authorship is an integrity matter, not merely a listing detail.

### Confirm Ethics Approval And Consent For Human And Animal Subjects

Research involving human or animal subjects is governed by ethics requirements that are not optional. Work that lacks appropriate approval, or that did not obtain informed consent, may be unpublishable regardless of its scientific merit, and publishing it can violate ethical and legal obligations. Editors must verify that the work's ethics basis is documented and adequate.

For research involving human subjects, confirm that the work has ethics committee or institutional review board approval, documented with the approving body and reference number, and that informed consent was obtained where required. For research involving animals, confirm appropriate ethics approval and that the work complies with relevant welfare standards. Watch for work that should have had ethics review but does not mention it, such as research involving identifiable human data, vulnerable populations, or interventions, since the absence of an ethics statement where one is expected is itself a red flag. For case studies, case reports, and identifiable individuals, verify that consent for publication was obtained, not merely for participation. Where ethics documentation is missing or inadequate, the work cannot proceed to publication until it is resolved, and the editor's role is to require and verify the documentation, not to assume it.

### Detect Data Fabrication Falsification And Image Manipulation

Fabrication, inventing data; falsification, altering data or results; and inappropriate image manipulation are the most serious forms of research misconduct, and they are also the hardest to detect. Editors cannot fully verify the integrity of underlying data, but they can recognize patterns and signals that warrant scrutiny and escalation, and some forms of manipulation leave visible traces in the manuscript.

Read data and results with attention to patterns that seem too clean, too consistent, or too convenient, though recognizing that such patterns can have legitimate explanations. For images, figures, and gels, watch for signs of manipulation, duplicated elements across figures presented as distinct, inconsistent backgrounds suggesting splicing, or features that appear cloned or altered. Where images are central to the claims, higher scrutiny is warranted. For statistical results, watch for inconsistencies between reported data and reported analyses, or results that seem inconsistent with the described methods. Where concerns arise, the editor's role is not to prove misconduct but to flag the concern, request underlying data or original images where appropriate, and escalate to the institution or publication's integrity process. Suspected fabrication or falsification is among the most serious matters an editor encounters, and it requires careful, documented handling.

### Ensure Conflicts Of Interest Are Disclosed

Conflicts of interest do not invalidate research, but undisclosed conflicts undermine trust and can distort the interpretation of findings. Editors must ensure that relevant financial, personal, and professional interests are disclosed, so readers and reviewers can assess the work with full knowledge of influences that may bear on it.

Confirm that the work includes a conflicts-of-interest disclosure, and that it covers the relevant categories, funding sources, financial interests, personal relationships, and professional affiliations that could be perceived as influencing the work. Watch for work with commercial funding or industry involvement where disclosure is absent or incomplete, since these are the contexts where conflicts most matter. For reviewers and editors involved in the work's handling, ensure their own conflicts are managed, since conflicts on the editorial side can compromise the process. Where a conflict is disclosed, it does not require rejection, but it must be visible so readers can weigh it. Undisclosed conflicts discovered after publication are far more damaging than disclosed ones evaluated openly, so the editor's role is to require disclosure before, not after, the fact.

### Escalate Suspected Misconduct Through Proper Channels

The editor's role in integrity is primarily detection and escalation, not investigation or adjudication. Suspected misconduct, especially serious matters like fabrication, falsification, or plagiarism, belongs to the institutions that employ the researchers and the publications' integrity processes. Attempting to resolve such matters unilaterally, by quietly rejecting or by confronting the author, can fail to address the underlying problem and can expose the editor and publication to risk.

When misconduct is suspected, document the concern specifically, with the evidence and the basis for suspicion. Follow the publication's process for integrity concerns, which may involve the editor-in-chief, the integrity officer, or a designated committee. For serious concerns, the publication may contact the author's institution, which has the authority and responsibility to investigate. Communicate with the author professionally and without accusation, requesting explanation or clarification where appropriate, while recognizing that the author's response is part of the record, not the resolution. Maintain confidentiality about the concern until it is substantiated, to protect all parties from unfair reputational harm. Escalation is not an admission of editorial limitation; it is the recognition that misconduct is an institutional matter with consequences beyond any single publication.

## Common Traps

### Similarity Treated As Automatic Plagiarism

Properly attributed quotation and citation are not plagiarism. Characterize text reuse accurately.

### Authorship Assumed Appropriate

Honorary, ghost, and coerced authorship are common. Verify contributorship, especially for unusual author lists.

### Ethics Approval Assumed Rather Than Verified

Missing ethics statements where expected are red flags. Require and document approval.

### Image Manipulation Overlooked

Duplicated or altered figures leave visible traces. Scrutinize images central to the claims.

### Conflicts Treated As Disqualifying

Disclosed conflicts do not invalidate work; they enable informed evaluation. Require disclosure, not absence.

### Misconduct Confronted Unilaterally

Serious concerns belong to institutions and publication processes. Document and escalate, do not adjudicate alone.

### Undisclosed Self-Reuse Ignored

Extensive reuse of prior work without disclosure can be problematic. Check the publication's originality norms.

### Integrity Signals Dismissed As Coincidence

Patterns that seem too clean warrant scrutiny, not assumption of innocence. Flag and verify where warranted.

## Self-Check

Before treating research integrity and ethics review as complete, verify:

- The work has been screened for plagiarism and inappropriate text reuse, with similarity characterized accurately rather than assumed.
- Authorship reflects genuine contribution, with contributorship statements checked where required and signs of disputed or inappropriate attribution investigated.
- Ethics approval and informed consent are documented for research involving human or animal subjects, with missing statements flagged where expected.
- Data, results, and images have been read for patterns or traces suggesting fabrication, falsification, or manipulation, with concerns documented for escalation.
- Conflicts of interest are disclosed, covering relevant financial, personal, and professional interests, including for commercially funded work.
- Editorial and reviewer conflicts have been managed to protect the integrity of the handling process.
- Suspected misconduct has been documented specifically and escalated through the publication's or institution's proper channels, not resolved unilaterally.
- Communication with the author about integrity concerns has been professional, non-accusatory, and confidential until substantiated.
- The editor has recognized the limits of the editorial role, detecting and escalating rather than investigating and adjudicating misconduct.
