# Literature Review And Positioning

The literature review is where a scholarly work establishes its right to exist. It demonstrates that the author knows the field, identifies what prior work has and has not established, and carves out the space the current work claims to fill. A weak literature review undermines the entire work, because without a convincing positioning, the contribution is unclear, the gap may be illusory, and reviewers and readers cannot judge what the work adds. Editing the literature review is therefore a substantive judgment task, not a copy-editing one, and it requires attention to coverage, honesty, and the logic of the work's placement in its field.

Use this skill when editing the literature review, related work, background, or positioning sections of academic or scholarly work in any discipline. It covers evaluating coverage and currency of cited literature, assessing whether the review builds a genuine gap and rationale, preventing cherry-picking and misrepresentation of sources, and ensuring the contribution is framed honestly against prior research. The editor's task is to ensure the work enters its field accurately, fairly, and with a defensible claim to novelty and significance.

## Core Rules

### Assess Coverage Against The Field's Expectations

A literature review's first test is whether it covers the work it should cover. Fields have core literatures, canonical prior work, and recent developments that any contribution in the area is expected to engage with, and omitting these signals ignorance or avoidance to reviewers. Coverage is judged against the field's norms, not against an abstract ideal of completeness.

Evaluate the review's coverage against what the field expects for the topic and contribution. Identify the canonical works, the foundational prior research that established the area, and confirm they appear. Identify the recent developments, the last several years of relevant work, and confirm the review is current, since a review that stops five years ago in a fast-moving field is already stale. Watch for systematic gaps, entire subfields, methodological traditions, or competing perspectives that are absent, since these may reflect the author's selective reading rather than the field's actual state. Where coverage is thin, flag what is missing and why it matters. The review need not cite everything, but it must engage with what a knowledgeable reviewer would expect to see engaged.

### Evaluate Whether The Review Builds A Genuine Gap

The literature review's purpose is not to summarize prior work but to establish a gap that the current work fills. A review that narrates the literature without building toward a gap leaves the contribution unmotivated; a review that asserts a gap without showing it leaves the claim unsupported. The logic from prior work to the identified gap is the review's central argument.

Trace the argument the review builds. Does it move from what is established, to what is contested or unknown, to the specific gap the current work addresses? Is the gap shown to exist by the literature cited, or merely asserted? Watch for gaps that are manufactured by ignoring relevant work, the "no prior research has examined" claim that overlooks existing studies, and for gaps so narrow they do not justify a contribution. A genuine gap is one the cited literature actually demonstrates, not one the author needs the literature to have. Where the gap is weak, the remedy may be to reframe the contribution, to acknowledge and distinguish prior work rather than ignore it, or to sharpen what is genuinely new. The review's logic must earn the work's right to exist.

### Prevent Cherry-Picking And Misrepresentation Of Sources

Literature reviews are vulnerable to selective and distorted representation of prior work, sometimes deliberate and sometimes careless. A source cited to support a claim that it does not support, or summarized in a way that flatters the current work's novelty by understating what the source actually did, misrepresents the field and can be caught, embarrassingly, by reviewers who know the literature. Honesty in representing sources is both ethical and strategic.

For each cited source, confirm the review represents it accurately. Check that summaries of prior work capture the source's actual findings, scope, and limitations, not a convenient simplification. Watch for sources cited to establish a gap that they actually fill, for findings reported without their qualifiers, and for methodological critiques that misstate the criticized work. Watch especially for systematic bias in representation, sources that support the author's view cited favorably, sources that complicate it cited critically or omitted. A literature review must engage honestly with work that challenges the contribution, not only with work that supports it. Misrepresentation discovered by reviewers damages credibility more than the weakness it concealed would have.

### Ensure The Contribution Is Framed Honestly

Positioning is not only about the gap; it is about how the work characterizes its own contribution relative to prior work. Overclaiming novelty, significance, or generality relative to what the work actually does is a common and damaging failure, because reviewers judge contributions against their framing and punish inflated claims. Honest framing claims what the work demonstrates, in the terms the evidence supports.

Examine how the work frames its contribution. Are claims of novelty, "the first to," "no prior work has," supported by the literature reviewed? Are claims of significance or impact proportionate to the evidence? Watch for framing that elevates an incremental contribution to a paradigm shift, or that generalizes a narrow finding to a broad claim. Where the framing overstates, bring it into line with what the work actually shows; understating is safer than overstating, and reviewers are more forgiving of modest honest claims than of grand unsupported ones. Honest framing also means acknowledging the work's limitations and relation to prior work, positioning the contribution as building on, rather than superseding, the field. A contribution framed honestly is one reviewers can evaluate on its merits rather than dismiss for overreach.

### Check That The Review Serves The Work's Specific Contribution

A literature review should be tailored to the work it introduces, not written as a generic survey of the field. A review that covers broad background unrelated to the specific contribution wastes space and obscures the argument; a review tightly focused on the work's actual gap and method builds the case efficiently. The review's scope should serve the contribution, not demonstrate erudition.

Evaluate whether the review's scope matches the work's needs. Background should establish what is necessary to understand the gap and the contribution, no more. Excessive breadth, reviewing the entire field when the contribution addresses a narrow question, dilutes the argument and signals uncertainty about the work's own placement. Excessive narrowness, failing to establish the context the contribution requires, leaves readers unable to judge significance. The review should foreground the prior work most relevant to the current contribution, whether methodologically, topically, or theoretically, and treat more peripheral work briefly or omit it. A focused review demonstrates command of what matters, not familiarity with everything.

### Verify Currency And Traceability Of Citations

A literature review's credibility depends on its citations being accurate and current. Misattributed, outdated, or non-existent citations undermine the review's foundation and can be caught by reviewers who check. Currency matters especially in fast-moving fields, where a review dominated by older work may miss developments that change the contribution's context.

Verify that cited works exist, are attributed to the right authors, and say what the review claims. For recent and fast-moving topics, confirm the review includes the relevant recent work and is not anchored to an earlier state of the field. Check that foundational works are cited in their correct form and that the review engages with current versions of ongoing debates rather than outdated positions. Where the review relies heavily on secondary summaries rather than primary sources, flag the weakness, since secondhand representation risks distortion. Currency and traceability are part of the review's honesty; a review built on inaccurate or stale citations cannot bear the weight the work places on it.

## Common Traps

### Coverage Gaps Ignoring Expected Works

Omitting canonical or recent work signals ignorance. Engage with what the field expects.

### Gap Asserted Rather Than Shown

A gap must be demonstrated by the literature, not merely claimed. Build the logic from prior work to the opening.

### Cherry-Picking Supportive Sources

Ignoring complicating work misrepresents the field. Engage honestly with challenges to the contribution.

### Overclaiming Novelty Or Significance

Inflated framing relative to the evidence invites reviewer rejection. Claim what the work shows.

### Generic Survey Unrelated To The Contribution

Broad background unrelated to the gap wastes space. Focus the review on the work's specific needs.

### Outdated Review In A Fast-Moving Field

A review anchored to older work misses developments that change the contribution's context. Keep current.

### Misrepresentation Of Cited Findings

Summarizing sources to flatter the current work distorts the field. Represent prior work accurately.

### Secondary Summaries Treated As Primary

Relying on secondhand accounts risks distortion. Trace to primary sources where stakes warrant.

## Self-Check

Before treating literature review and positioning editing as complete, verify:

- Coverage includes the canonical and recent works the field expects for the topic and contribution.
- The review builds a genuine gap demonstrated by the cited literature, not merely asserted.
- Cited sources are represented accurately, without cherry-picking or distortion to flatter the contribution.
- The work's contribution is framed honestly, with novelty and significance claims proportionate to the evidence.
- The review's scope is focused on the work's specific gap and method, not a generic field survey.
- Citations are accurate, traceable, and current, especially in fast-moving fields.
- Work that challenges the contribution is engaged with honestly, not omitted or misrepresented.
- A knowledgeable reviewer would find the review fair, current, and appropriately scoped.
- The work enters its field accurately and with a defensible claim to novelty and significance.
