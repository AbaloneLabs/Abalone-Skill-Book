# Recommendation Clarity

Recommendations are calls to action. They tell the reader what to do, decide, or approve based on the document's findings. A clear recommendation is specific, actionable, and justified, leaving no doubt about what is being recommended and why. An unclear recommendation, whether vague, hedged, or buried, leaves the reader uncertain what to do and undermines the document's purpose. Recommendation clarity is the discipline of ensuring recommendations serve their function as actionable directives. Editors who treat recommendations as conclusions rather than calls to action miss the specificity that makes them useful.

Use this skill when editing executive summaries, reports, or briefings that contain recommendations to verify clarity. It covers specificity, actionability, justification, and presentation. The goal is recommendations the reader can understand, evaluate, and act on without ambiguity.

## Core Rules

### Make Recommendations Specific And Concrete

A recommendation must be specific enough to act on. "Improve operations" is too vague; "Implement the lean process in the assembly line by Q3" is specific. Specificity enables action. Make recommendations concrete.

Assess each recommendation for specificity. Does it state exactly what is recommended, in terms concrete enough to guide action? Flag vague recommendations that name a direction without specifics, such as "enhance," "optimize," or "leverage" without defining what exactly to do. Recommend concrete formulations that specify the action, scope, target, or timing. Specific recommendations let the reader know what acting on them would involve, which enables evaluation and execution. Vague recommendations sound meaningful but provide no directive, leaving the reader to define the action, which defeats the recommendation's purpose.

### Ensure Recommendations Are Actionable

Recommendations should be actionable, meaning the reader can act on them with appropriate authority and resources. A recommendation the reader cannot act on, because it requires authority or resources they lack, is misdirected. Ensure actionability.

Assess whether each recommendation is actionable by the intended reader. Does the reader have the authority, resources, and position to act on it? Flag recommendations directed at the wrong audience, such as a recommendation to the executive team that requires operational action by line managers. Recommend either directing the recommendation to the right actor or framing it as a request or authorization for the reader to enable action by others. Actionability respects the reader's role and makes the recommendation practical, not aspirational. A recommendation the reader cannot act on creates frustration rather than enabling decision.

### Provide Justification For Each Recommendation

A recommendation should be justified, with the reasoning or evidence that supports it. Readers need to understand why a recommendation is made to evaluate and accept it. Provide justification.

For each recommendation, verify that justification accompanies it, either in the recommendation itself or in the supporting content. Why is this the right action? What finding, analysis, or reasoning supports it? Flag recommendations presented without justification, which read as assertions the reader must take on faith. Recommend connecting each recommendation to the findings or analysis that ground it. Justification enables the reader to evaluate the recommendation's soundness, not just receive it. Strong recommendations earn acceptance through their reasoning; unsupported recommendations rely on authority or hope.

### Distinguish Recommendations From Findings And Conclusions

Recommendations are distinct from findings, what the analysis shows, and conclusions, what the findings mean. Recommendations go further, stating what to do. Conflating these levels confuses the reader. Distinguish them.

Verify that recommendations are clearly distinguished from findings and conclusions. A finding states a fact or result; a conclusion interprets the finding; a recommendation proposes action based on the conclusion. Flag content that blurs these levels, such as a recommendation that is really a conclusion or a conclusion presented as a recommendation. Use clear structure or labeling to separate findings, conclusions, and recommendations, so the reader knows what they are reading at each point. Clear distinction helps the reader follow the logic from evidence to interpretation to action, and to evaluate each level appropriately.

### Prioritize Recommendations When There Are Multiple

When a document contains multiple recommendations, they should be prioritized, so the reader knows which matter most or should come first. Unprioritized lists leave the reader to sequence. Prioritize.

Assess whether multiple recommendations are prioritized. Is there a clear indication of which are most important, most urgent, or should be acted on first? Flag lists of recommendations presented as equal when some matter more. Recommend prioritization through ordering, labeling, or explicit ranking. Prioritization serves decision-making by guiding the reader's attention and sequencing. Executive readers often cannot act on everything at once; prioritized recommendations help them allocate attention and resources effectively. Even a simple high, medium, low categorization or a stated sequence adds significant value over a flat list.

### State The Expected Outcome Or Benefit

A recommendation should state the expected outcome or benefit, what acting on it will achieve. Readers need to know the payoff to evaluate whether the action is worth it. State outcomes.

For each recommendation, verify the expected outcome or benefit is stated. What will acting on this recommendation accomplish? What problem does it solve, what opportunity does it capture, what risk does it mitigate? Flag recommendations presented without their expected benefit, which ask the reader to act without knowing the return. Recommend connecting each recommendation to the outcome it aims to produce. Stating outcomes enables cost-benefit evaluation and motivates action. A recommendation whose benefit is clear is more likely to be accepted and acted on than one presented as an end in itself.

### Make Recommendations Easy To Find And Act On

Recommendations should be easy to find in the document, so the reader can locate and act on them. Buried recommendations, scattered through prose, are easily missed. Make them findable.

Assess whether recommendations are easy to find. Are they collected in a clear recommendations section, labeled with headings, or formatted distinctively? Flag documents where recommendations are scattered, implicit, or buried in paragraphs. Recommend consolidating recommendations in a visible section, using clear headings, and formatting them so a scanning reader can find and grasp them. Executive readers may go directly to recommendations; making them findable serves this reading pattern. A document whose recommendations are hard to locate fails its purpose, because the call to action is lost.

## Common Traps

### Vague Recommendations Without Specifics

State exactly what is recommended. Avoid "enhance" or "optimize" without definition.

### Recommendations The Reader Cannot Act On

Ensure actionability by the intended audience's authority and resources.

### Recommendations Without Justification

Connect each recommendation to the findings or reasoning that support it.

### Conflating Findings, Conclusions, And Recommendations

Distinguish what the analysis shows, what it means, and what to do.

### Unprioritized Lists Of Recommendations

Prioritize so the reader knows what matters most or comes first.

### Missing Expected Outcomes Or Benefits

State what acting on the recommendation will accomplish.

### Recommendations Buried Or Hard To Find

Make recommendations visible and easy to locate for action.

## Self-Check

Before treating recommendation clarity as achieved, verify:

- Each recommendation is specific and concrete enough to guide action.
- Recommendations are actionable by the intended reader's authority and resources.
- Each recommendation is justified by findings, analysis, or reasoning.
- Recommendations are clearly distinguished from findings and conclusions.
- Multiple recommendations are prioritized by importance, urgency, or sequence.
- Each recommendation states the expected outcome or benefit of acting.
- Recommendations are easy to find through clear structure, headings, and formatting.
- No recommendation is vague, unsupported, or misdirected to the wrong audience.
- A reader could understand, evaluate, and act on each recommendation without ambiguity.
- The recommendations collectively serve the document's purpose as calls to action.
