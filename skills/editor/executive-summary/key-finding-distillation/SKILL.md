# Key Finding Distillation

Executive summaries and briefings distill longer documents into their key findings: the most important results, conclusions, or insights that the reader needs. Distillation is selective and reductive, choosing what matters most from a larger whole. Poor distillation either overloads, including too much, or distorts, misrepresenting the source through selection or simplification. Key finding distillation is the discipline of selecting and presenting findings that serve the reader without betraying the source. Editors who summarize by shortening rather than selecting miss the judgment that distillation requires.

Use this skill when editing executive summaries, briefings, or abstracts to verify key finding distillation. It covers selection, accurate and concise statement, and service to decision-making. The goal is a summary whose key findings are the right ones, stated truly and usefully.

## Core Rules

### Select Findings By Importance To The Reader

Distillation begins with selection: which findings, from all the document contains, matter most to the reader. Selection should be driven by importance to the reader's decisions and needs, not by what is interesting or easy to summarize. Select by importance.

Review the source document's findings and results. Identify which matter most to the executive reader: the findings that affect decisions, reveal risks, confirm or challenge assumptions, or enable action. These are the key findings to distill. Flag summaries that include findings for completeness rather than importance, which overload the reader, or that omit findings the reader needs, which under-serve them. Selection is the core judgment of distillation; it requires understanding what the reader cares about and what the document actually shows. The right selection serves the reader; the wrong selection wastes their time or misleads.

### State Findings Concisely Without Losing Accuracy

Key findings should be stated concisely, but concision must not cost accuracy. A finding compressed to the point of distortion betrays the source. Balance brevity and accuracy.

For each key finding, verify the summary statement is both concise and accurate. Does it capture the finding's essence without distortion? Flag statements that oversimplify, dropping qualifications or context that matter; that overstate, making a finding sound stronger than the source supports; or that distort, changing the finding's meaning through compression. Concision serves the reader by saving time; accuracy serves by not misleading. The balance is finding the briefest statement that remains true to the source. Where a finding cannot be stated both briefly and accurately, prefer accuracy and allow slightly more length, or note that the finding is complex and requires the full document.

### Preserve Necessary Qualifications And Uncertainty

Findings often carry qualifications: confidence levels, conditions, limitations, or uncertainties. These qualifications matter for decision-making and must survive distillation, not be stripped for brevity. Preserve qualifications.

Check that key findings retain the qualifications that affect their interpretation. If a result is preliminary, conditional, or uncertain, the summary should say so. Flag distillations that present qualified findings as certain, conditional findings as universal, or limited findings as general. Stripping qualifications to create a cleaner summary distorts the source and can lead to overconfident decisions. The qualification is part of the finding; a finding without its qualification is a different, stronger claim that the source may not support. Preserve the hedges, conditions, and limitations that calibrate the finding's strength.

### Connect Findings To Implications And Actions

Executive readers need not just findings but their implications: what the findings mean for decisions and actions. Distillation should connect findings to implications where appropriate. Connect to implications.

Assess whether the summary connects key findings to their implications. Does it note what the finding means for the business, the decision, or the next step? Flag summaries that present findings in isolation, leaving the reader to infer implications. Where the source document draws implications or recommendations, the summary should convey them alongside the findings. Connecting findings to implications serves decision-making, which is the executive reader's purpose. The connection should be accurate, growing from the finding, not imposed. Findings without implications inform; findings with implications enable action.

### Avoid Overloading With Too Many Findings

Distillation requires selectivity. Including too many findings turns a summary into a list that overwhelms rather than distills. Avoid overload.

Assess the number of key findings presented. Is it a manageable set that the reader can hold and act on, or a long list that dilutes focus? There is no fixed number, but executive summaries generally work best with a small set of truly key findings, often three to seven, rather than an exhaustive catalog. Flag summaries that overload, presenting every finding rather than the key ones. Recommend prioritizing to the most important, moving secondary findings to an appendix or the full document. Distillation means choosing; a summary that includes everything is not a summary. Overload defeats the purpose by requiring the reader to do the distillation themselves.

### Ensure Findings Are Presented In A Logical Order

The order of findings affects how the reader processes them. Findings should be arranged logically, whether by importance, chronology, causality, or theme, to aid comprehension. Verify ordering.

Assess the order of key findings. Does it follow a logic the reader can track, such as most important first, cause to effect, or theme by theme? Flag summaries where findings appear in arbitrary or source-document order, which may not serve the reader. Recommend an order that aids comprehension and decision-making. Often, leading with the most important or actionable finding serves executive readers, with supporting findings following. A logical order helps the reader build understanding and see relationships among findings, rather than receiving a random sequence.

### Verify Distillation Is Faithful To The Source

The ultimate test of distillation is faithfulness: does the summary represent the source accurately, without distortion, exaggeration, or omission of what matters? Verify faithfulness.

Compare the summary's key findings against the source document. Does each finding appear in the source, with the meaning and strength the summary conveys? Flag any finding that distorts the source, whether by overstating, understating, changing meaning, or inventing. Flag any key finding of the source that the summary omits without justification. Faithfulness is the ethical core of distillation; a summary that betrays the source misleads readers who rely on it. Where the summary and source diverge, revise the summary to align. Faithful distillation respects both the source's content and the reader's trust.

## Common Traps

### Summarizing Everything Rather Than Selecting

Select by importance to the reader. Distillation means choosing.

### Concision That Distorts Accuracy

Balance brevity with accuracy. Do not compress findings into falsehood.

### Stripping Qualifications For Cleaner Statements

Preserve the conditions and uncertainties that calibrate finding strength.

### Findings Without Implications

Connect findings to what they mean for decisions and actions.

### Overloading With Too Many Findings

Prioritize to a manageable set. Move secondary findings to detail.

### Arbitrary Or Source-Order Presentation

Arrange findings logically to aid comprehension and decision-making.

### Distortion Or Invention Through Selection

Verify faithfulness. The summary must represent the source accurately.

## Self-Check

Before treating key finding distillation as complete, verify:

- Findings have been selected by importance to the reader's decisions and needs.
- Each key finding is stated concisely without losing accuracy.
- Necessary qualifications, conditions, and uncertainties are preserved.
- Findings are connected to their implications for decision-making where appropriate.
- The number of findings is manageable, not an exhaustive overload.
- Findings are presented in a logical order that aids comprehension.
- Each finding in the summary is faithful to the source, without distortion.
- No key finding of the source is omitted without justification.
- No finding is overstated, understated, or changed in meaning through compression.
- A reader relying on the summary would receive an accurate, useful representation of the source's key findings.
