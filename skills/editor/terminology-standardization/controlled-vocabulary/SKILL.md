# Controlled Vocabulary

A controlled vocabulary is an approved set of terms with designated meanings, used consistently across a document or corpus. It suppresses synonyms, variant spellings, and ambiguous terms in favor of authorized forms. Controlled vocabularies matter in technical, scientific, medical, and legal content, where precision, retrievability, and consistent comprehension are essential. Without one, the same concept appears under multiple terms, confusing readers and fragmenting retrieval. Controlled vocabulary management is the discipline of defining, applying, and enforcing an authorized term set. Editors who allow terms to drift, assuming readers will infer equivalence, undermine the precision that specialized content requires.

Use this skill when editing technical or specialized content that requires or would benefit from a controlled vocabulary. It covers term authorization, synonym suppression, and consistency enforcement. The goal is content where each concept has one authorized term used throughout, supporting precision and retrieval.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Authorized Term Set

A controlled vocabulary begins with the authorized term set: the approved terms and their designated meanings. Without an established set, consistency has no anchor. Establish or identify the set.

Determine whether an authoritative vocabulary already exists for the domain, such as a medical thesaurus, a legal term list, or an industry standard. If one exists, adopt it. If not, work with subject experts to establish the authorized terms for key concepts. For each concept, designate one term as authorized and document its meaning precisely. Record variant terms, synonyms, and abbreviations as non-preferred forms that map to the authorized term. The authorized set is the reference against which consistency is measured. Document it in a terminology database or style sheet that editors and authors can consult.

### Suppress Synonyms And Variant Forms

The core discipline of a controlled vocabulary is suppressing synonyms and variant forms in favor of the authorized term. Variety, valued in literary prose, is the enemy of technical precision. Suppress variants.

When a non-preferred term appears, replace it with the authorized term. This includes synonyms, variant spellings, abbreviations used in place of the full term, and casual or colloquial equivalents. For example, if the authorized term is "physician," replace "doctor," "clinician," and "MD" in contexts where the controlled vocabulary applies. Document the mappings so the rationale is clear and the replacements are consistent. Suppression does not mean the variant terms are wrong in general English; it means they are not authorized for this controlled vocabulary context. Consistency of term supports consistent comprehension and reliable retrieval.

### Define Each Term Precisely

An authorized term is only as good as its definition. If the term's meaning is vague or overlaps with others, consistency of usage cannot be verified. Define precisely.

For each authorized term, provide a precise definition that distinguishes it from related terms. Note the scope, what the term includes and excludes. Where terms are easily confused, clarify the distinction explicitly. For example, in a medical vocabulary, distinguish "adverse effect" from "side effect" if both are authorized but mean different things. Precise definitions allow editors to verify that a term is used correctly, not just consistently. They also help authors choose the right authorized term when multiple seem to fit. Definitions should be recorded with the term set and updated as understanding evolves.

### Document Preferred Forms For Spelling And Capitalization

A controlled vocabulary includes not just which term to use but which form: spelling, capitalization, hyphenation, and abbreviation rules. Inconsistent forms fragment the vocabulary even when the term is the same. Document forms.

For each authorized term, specify the preferred spelling, capitalization, and hyphenation. For example, is it "email" or "e-mail," "decision-making" or "decisionmaking," "Internet" or "internet"? Specify whether the term is capitalized in all contexts or only specific ones. Specify the rules for abbreviation: when an abbreviation is allowed, what it is, and whether the full term must appear on first use. Documenting forms prevents the subtle inconsistencies that arise when different authors or editors make different default choices. Form consistency is part of full vocabulary control.

### Enforce Consistency Across The Document Or Corpus

A controlled vocabulary only works if it is enforced consistently. A term used correctly in one section and as a synonym in another defeats the purpose. Enforce across scope.

Check term usage across the entire scope of the controlled vocabulary, whether a single document or a corpus. Use search to find all instances of each concept and verify the authorized term is used throughout. Flag and correct deviations. Consistency enforcement is where many controlled vocabularies fail, because it requires systematic checking rather than spot editing. For large corpora, terminology management software can assist, but editorial judgment is still needed to handle borderline cases. The standard is that a reader encountering a concept anywhere in the scope finds the same authorized term.

### Map Non-Preferred Terms To Authorized Equivalents

To support enforcement and help authors, document the mapping between non-preferred terms and their authorized equivalents. This mapping guides replacement and prevents recurrence. Map equivalents.

For each authorized term, list the non-preferred variants that should be replaced. This includes synonyms, variant spellings, abbreviations, and colloquial forms. Record the mapping in the terminology database. When editing, use the mapping to find and replace variants systematically. The mapping also helps authors, who can look up whether a term they want to use is authorized or should be replaced. Over time, the mapping reveals patterns of common confusion that may indicate terms needing clearer definitions or distinction.

### Support Retrieval And Indexing

A key purpose of controlled vocabularies is supporting retrieval and indexing. When each concept has one authorized term, search and indexing work reliably. Consider retrieval in vocabulary decisions.

When establishing or revising the vocabulary, consider how terms will be used for retrieval and indexing. Authorized terms should be the forms users are likely to search, or should be cross-referenced from those forms. Terms should be specific enough to retrieve precisely but not so obscure that users cannot find them. If the content will be indexed, ensure the authorized terms work as index entries. The retrieval function connects the vocabulary to the content's usability; a vocabulary that is internally consistent but uses terms no one searches for fails its retrieval purpose.

## Common Traps

### Allowing Synonyms For stylistic Variety

Variety fragments technical precision. Use the authorized term consistently.

### Vague Or Overlapping Definitions

Define terms precisely so correct usage can be verified.

### Inconsistent Spelling Or Capitalization Forms

Document preferred forms. Consistency includes form, not just word choice.

### Spot Editing Instead Of Systematic Enforcement

Check usage across the full scope. Consistency requires systematic verification.

### Undocumented Term Mappings

Map non-preferred to authorized terms. Guide replacement and prevent recurrence.

### Ignoring Retrieval Implications

Consider how terms support search and indexing, not just internal consistency.

### Adopting Terms No One Searches For

Authorized terms should be findable. Balance precision with retrievability.

## Self-Check

Before treating the controlled vocabulary as established and enforced, verify:

- An authorized term set exists or has been established for key concepts.
- Each authorized term has a precise definition distinguishing it from related terms.
- Preferred spelling, capitalization, hyphenation, and abbreviation forms are documented.
- Non-preferred synonyms, variants, and abbreviations are mapped to authorized equivalents.
- Term usage has been checked across the full scope for consistency.
- Deviations from the authorized vocabulary have been corrected.
- The vocabulary supports retrieval and indexing, with terms users are likely to search.
- Borderline or easily confused terms are distinguished explicitly.
- The terminology documentation is accessible to authors and editors.
- A reader encountering a concept anywhere in the scope finds the same authorized term, form, and meaning.
