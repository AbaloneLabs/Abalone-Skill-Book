# Translation Readiness

Content destined for translation carries hidden costs and risks. Inconsistent terminology forces translators to make choices that fragment meaning across languages. Ambiguous phrasing multiplies into multiple possible translations, some wrong. Cultural references and idioms that work in the source language may confuse or offend in others. Translation readiness is the discipline of preparing source content so that translation is accurate, efficient, and consistent. Editors who edit only for the source-language reader miss the second audience: the translators and the readers in other languages whose experience depends on the source's clarity.

Use this skill when editing content that will be translated or localized. It covers terminology consistency, ambiguity reduction, cultural and idiomatic handling, and structural preparation. The goal is source content that translates cleanly, with minimal cost, error, and loss of meaning.

## Core Rules

### Enforce Strict Terminology Consistency

Translation amplifies terminology inconsistency. If the source uses three terms for one concept, translators must decide whether they are the same or different, and may render them differently across languages, fragmenting meaning. Enforce consistency.

Verify that each concept has one authorized term used throughout, as in controlled vocabulary work, but with heightened rigor because translation magnifies inconsistency. Document the terminology in a glossary or termbase that translators can reference. When a term must vary for legitimate reasons, such as grammatical form, document the variation. Terminology consistency is the single highest-impact factor in translation quality and cost, because consistent terms enable translation memory leverage and termbase application. Inconsistent terms defeat both and produce inconsistent translations.

### Reduce Ambiguity In Phrasing

Ambiguous source phrasing produces ambiguous translations. A sentence with two readings may be translated correctly for one and wrongly for the other. Reduce ambiguity.

Review the content for phrasing with multiple interpretations. This includes syntactic ambiguity, where sentence structure allows different parses; lexical ambiguity, where words have multiple meanings; and referential ambiguity, where pronouns or references are unclear. Revise to resolve the ambiguity explicitly. For example, "She told her sister that she was late" is ambiguous about who was late; revise to clarify. Translators work from the source; if the source is ambiguous, they must guess, and guesses produce errors. Clear, unambiguous source enables accurate translation.

### Handle Idioms And Figurative Language

Idioms and figurative language often do not translate. An idiom that is vivid in the source language may be meaningless, confusing, or inadvertently offensive in another. Handle idioms deliberately.

Identify idioms, metaphors, and figurative expressions. For each, assess whether it will translate. Some idioms have equivalents in other languages; many do not. Where an idiom will not translate, consider replacing it with clear literal language, especially in content where accuracy matters more than style, such as technical or instructional text. Where figurative language is retained for style, flag it for translators so they can find an equivalent or translate the meaning rather than the words. Sports idioms, cultural references, and colloquialisms are common sources of translation difficulty. Handling them deliberately prevents translation failures.

### Address Cultural References And Examples

Cultural references, examples, and analogies may not work across cultures. A reference meaningful in one region may be unknown or carry different connotations elsewhere. Address cultural specificity.

Review the content for cultural references: historical events, public figures, geographic references, cultural practices, units of measure, date and time formats, and examples that assume a specific context. Assess whether each will work in the target cultures. Where a reference is culture-specific and not essential, consider replacing it with a universal equivalent. Where it is essential, ensure it is explained enough for readers in other cultures to understand. Examples and analogies should use scenarios that translate across cultures, or be adapted for each locale. Cultural reference handling is part of localization, which goes beyond translation to cultural appropriateness.

### Use Clear, Simple Sentence Structures

Complex sentence structures increase translation difficulty and error risk. Long sentences with multiple clauses, embedded structures, and complex dependencies are harder to translate accurately. Favor clarity.

Review sentence length and structure. Break long sentences into shorter ones where possible. Reduce embedded clauses and complex dependencies. Favor subject-verb-object structures, which translate cleanly across most languages. Avoid heavy nominalization, which can obscure the action and actor. Clear, simple structures are easier for translators to parse accurately and for translation tools to leverage. This does not mean dumbing down the content; it means presenting complex ideas in structures that are easy to process. Complexity of thought does not require complexity of sentence.

### Prepare Structural Elements For Localization

Translation affects layout and structure. Text expands or contracts in different languages; lists, tables, and headings may need adjustment; dates, numbers, and currencies need localization. Prepare structure.

Consider how structural elements will fare in translation. Text in some languages is longer than English; ensure layouts accommodate expansion. Avoid hard-coded formatting that breaks when text length changes. Ensure lists, tables, and headings use structures that translation tools handle well. Separate translatable text from code or formatting where possible. Standardize date, time, number, and currency formats, or flag them for localization. Use Unicode to support character sets across languages. Structural preparation prevents the layout and display problems that arise when translated content is forced into source-language structures.

### Provide Context For Translators

Translators work best with context. Source content presented as isolated strings, without knowing where they appear or how they relate, invites mistranslation. Provide context.

Where possible, provide translators with context for the content they translate. This includes where text appears in the interface or document, what surrounds it, and what it refers to. For software, provide screenshots or context comments. For documents, provide the surrounding text or section context. Context helps translators choose the right meaning of ambiguous terms, match the right tone, and understand references. Content prepared with translator context produces better translations than content delivered as decontextualized strings.

## Common Traps

### Terminology Inconsistency Magnified By Translation

Use one term per concept. Document in a termbase for translators.

### Ambiguous Phrasing Producing Wrong Translations

Reduce ambiguity. Translators must guess when the source is unclear.

### Idioms That Do Not Cross Languages

Handle idioms deliberately. Replace or flag for translator attention.

### Culture-Specific References That Fail Elsewhere

Assess and adapt cultural references, examples, and formats.

### Complex Sentences Increasing Translation Risk

Favor clear, simple structures. Complexity of thought does not require complex syntax.

### Layouts That Break On Text Expansion

Prepare structure for length changes and localization of formats.

### Decontextualized Text For Translators

Provide context so translators can choose accurate meanings and tones.

## Self-Check

Before treating the content as translation-ready, verify:

- Each concept has one authorized term used consistently, documented in a termbase.
- Phrasing has been reviewed for ambiguity and revised to resolve multiple interpretations.
- Idioms and figurative language are identified and either replaced or flagged for translators.
- Cultural references, examples, and formats are assessed for cross-cultural appropriateness.
- Sentence structures are clear and simple enough to translate accurately.
- Structural elements accommodate text expansion and localization of dates, numbers, and currencies.
- Translatable text is separated from code or formatting where possible.
- Context is provided for translators, including location, surroundings, and references.
- The content minimizes translation cost through consistency and translation memory leverage.
- A translator working from this source could produce accurate, consistent translations with minimal guesswork.
