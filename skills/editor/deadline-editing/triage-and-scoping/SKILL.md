# Triage and Scoping

Editing under a deadline is fundamentally a resource-allocation problem. When time is finite, the editor cannot fix everything and must decide which problems to address, which to flag for later, and which to leave alone. Triage is the act of ranking editorial problems by consequence and assigning each to a tier of effort. Scoping is the act of matching that ranked list to the hours actually available. An editor who skips triage tends to fix the easy, visible problems first and run out of time before reaching the structural or factual ones that matter more. An editor who skips scoping tends to promise a full edit and deliver a partial one without telling anyone.

The core judgment problem is that every editorial intervention has a cost and a benefit, and under deadline pressure both are easy to misestimate. Costs are underestimated because a "quick fix" often uncovers a deeper problem. Benefits are overestimated because a polished sentence in a structurally weak section does not save the work. This skill exists to prevent the editor from spending scarce time on low-impact changes while high-impact problems remain untouched.

## Core Rules

### Rank by Consequence, Not by Visibility

Before touching the manuscript, build a ranked list of every problem you have identified. Rank by the harm each problem does to the reader or to the work's purpose, not by how obvious or annoying it is. A misspelled heading is highly visible but low consequence; a buried thesis statement is invisible but high consequence. The triage order should reflect what will damage the work most if left unfixed.

A practical ranking order, from highest to lowest:

1. Factual errors and legal or safety risks.
2. Structural problems that prevent the reader from following the argument or story.
3. Claims unsupported by evidence.
4. Clarity failures that mislead or confuse.
5. Consistency errors across the document.
6. Sentence-level grammar and mechanics.
7. Style preferences and aesthetic polish.

This order is a default, not a law. In a marketing piece, a broken call-to-action link may outrank a minor factual imprecision because it directly blocks the work's purpose. In a safety manual, a wrong unit conversion is catastrophic and must come first regardless of its position in the text.

### Estimate Time Honestly and Add a Buffer

For each item on the ranked list, estimate the time it will take to fix. Then add a buffer of roughly 30 to 50 percent, because editorial work expands. A sentence rewrite that looks like two minutes often becomes ten when you discover the sentence depends on a paragraph that also needs adjustment. Track your estimates against actual time as you go, so you can recalibrate.

If the total estimated time exceeds the available time, you must cut scope before you begin, not discover the shortfall at the end. Decide which tiers you will fully address, which you will sample, and which you will leave. Communicate this decision to the author or publisher.

### Distinguish Must-Fix from Should-Fix from Nice-to-Fix

Sort every problem into three buckets.

**Must-fix** problems will cause tangible harm if left alone: factual errors, broken logic, missing required sections, legal exposure, safety risks. These get attention first, regardless of how long they take.

**Should-fix** problems degrade the work but do not break it: unclear transitions, weak evidence, inconsistent terminology, awkward sentences. These get attention only after must-fix items are cleared and only if time remains.

**Nice-to-fix** problems are matters of preference and polish: optional rewording, stylistic variation, minor consistency tweaks. These get attention only if all higher tiers are complete and time remains. Under tight deadlines, nice-to-fix items are usually dropped entirely.

### Match Edit Depth to Available Time

The depth of edit you can perform is constrained by time. Be explicit about which level you are delivering. A common mistake is to begin a deep developmental edit, realize time is short, and then deliver a shallow copy edit without telling anyone the scope changed. Decide the level up front.

- **Deep edit:** structural reorganization, argument reconstruction, paragraph-level rewriting. Requires substantial time per page.
- **Medium edit:** paragraph-level clarity, evidence checking, consistency enforcement. Moderate time per page.
- **Light edit:** sentence-level corrections, obvious errors, consistency of mechanics. Low time per page.
- **Proofread:** typos, formatting, final mechanical errors. Lowest time per page.

If time allows only a light edit, say so. A clearly scoped light edit is more valuable than an ambiguous partial deep edit.

### Sequence Work to Protect High-Value Tasks

Do the highest-consequence work first, when your attention is freshest. Resist the temptation to clear small easy items first for the psychological satisfaction of progress. Those small items will still be there at the end; the structural analysis will not get done well if you are tired and rushed.

A sound sequence: read the whole work once to build the ranked list, address must-fix structural and factual items, then move down the tiers. Leave proofreading for last, because substantive changes will introduce new mechanical errors that need catching.

### Communicate Scope Decisions Before Starting Work

If the ranked list exceeds available time, tell the author or publisher what will and will not be addressed. Do this before editing, not after. A note such as "I can address factual errors, structural logic, and clarity, but I will not have time for stylistic polish or consistency of hyphenation" sets honest expectations and protects you from complaints about unfinished work.

If the deadline is imposed externally and cannot be moved, scope is the variable you control. If the scope is fixed and non-negotiable, then the deadline or the quality must flex. Make the tradeoff explicit rather than silently letting quality slip.

## Common Traps

### Polishing the Deck Chairs While the Hull Leaks

Spending the first hour perfecting sentence rhythm in a chapter whose central argument is unsupported. The polished sentences will still be wrong because the argument beneath them is weak. Always confirm structural and factual soundness before investing in sentence-level polish.

### Underestimating Cascade Costs

A single "small fix" such as changing a key term can require dozens of follow-up changes throughout a long document. When estimating time, consider whether a fix triggers cascading edits. A term change in a glossary, for instance, may require updates in every example, every cross-reference, and the index.

### The Sunk-Cost Spiral on a Single Paragraph

Getting stuck rewriting one stubborn paragraph for forty minutes because it will not come out right. Under deadline pressure, this is catastrophic. Set a time limit per item. If a paragraph will not yield in the allotted time, flag it for the author with a note and move on.

### Promising Full Coverage and Delivering Partial

Telling the author you will "edit the whole thing" when time only permits a sample. The author then trusts that uncovered sections are clean, when in fact they were never read. Always state exactly what was and was not reviewed.

### Confusing Speed with Triage

Working fast is not the same as working in priority order. An editor can move quickly through low-value items and still miss the one high-value fix that would have mattered. Speed without triage produces a lot of motion and little impact.

### Ignoring the Cost of Context Switching

Jumping between structural analysis and typo-fixing in the same pass wastes mental energy, because each switch requires reloading context. Batch similar work together: do all structural review, then all fact-checking, then all sentence-level work. This reduces switching cost and improves throughput under pressure.

### Failing to Recalibrate Mid-Edit

Discovering at the halfway point that estimates were wrong but pressing on with the original plan anyway. When actual time diverges from estimates, stop and rescope immediately. It is better to deliver a complete light edit than an incomplete deep edit.

## Self-Check

- Did I build a ranked problem list based on consequence before I started fixing anything?
- Did I separate items into must-fix, should-fix, and nice-to-fix, and address them in that order?
- Did I estimate time per item with a buffer, and did the total fit within the available hours?
- Did I explicitly choose and communicate the depth of edit I am delivering, rather than letting it drift?
- Did I do the highest-consequence work while my attention was freshest, instead of clearing easy items first?
- Did I flag items I could not complete rather than silently leaving them, so the author knows what was not addressed?
- Did I recalibrate when actual time exceeded estimates, and rescope rather than press on with an impossible plan?
- Did I batch similar work to reduce context-switching cost?
- If I ran out of time, did the must-fix items get done, or did I spend the time on lower-tier work?
- Did I tell the author or publisher, before starting, exactly what was in and out of scope?
