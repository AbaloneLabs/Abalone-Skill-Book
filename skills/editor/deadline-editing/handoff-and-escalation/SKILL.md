# Handoff and Escalation

Not every editing problem can or should be solved by the editor in front of the text. Some problems exceed the available time, the editor's expertise, or the editorial role. Handoff is the act of transferring unfinished or out-of-scope work to the right person with enough documentation that they can continue effectively. Escalation is the act of raising a problem to someone with authority or expertise to resolve it, such as a fact-checker, a lawyer, a subject-matter expert, or the author. Both are acts of professional responsibility, not admissions of failure. An editor who silently leaves a legal-risk passage unaddressed because it was "out of scope" has failed the work. An editor who flags it clearly and routes it to the right person has protected it.

The judgment problem is deciding what to handle, what to hand off, what to escalate, and how to document each so that nothing falls through the cracks. Under deadline pressure, the temptation is to either fix everything badly or ignore everything that is hard. The disciplined path is to resolve what you can, document what you cannot, and route the rest to the right destination with a clear note.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish In-Scope from Out-of-Scope Problems

Before deciding to hand off or escalate, confirm whether the problem is actually outside your scope. Many problems that feel out of scope are simply hard versions of in-scope work. A confusing sentence is in scope even if it takes effort; a potentially defamatory claim is out of scope for a copy editor and must be escalated to legal review.

Use these categories:

- **In scope and fixable now:** resolve it yourself.
- **In scope but blocked by missing information:** query the author, then resolve.
- **Out of scope for expertise:** escalate to a specialist, such as legal, medical, or technical review.
- **Out of scope for role:** hand off to the appropriate person, such as the designer for layout or the fact-checker for verification.
- **Beyond the work's tolerance:** escalate to the publisher or author as a go-or-no-go decision.

Misclassification in either direction causes harm. Treating a legal risk as a style issue leaves the work exposed. Treating a style preference as a legal issue wastes specialist time and delays the work.

### Document What Was Done and What Was Not

A handoff is useless without a record of the current state. When transferring work, produce a brief handoff note that covers: what was reviewed, what was changed, what was flagged but not changed and why, what was not reviewed at all, and any open questions. The note should let the next person continue without re-reading everything you already read.

Be specific about what was not done. "I did not have time to check the references" is more useful than a vague claim of completion. The receiving editor needs to know which sections are trusted and which are unverified, so they do not waste time re-checking clean work or trusting unchecked work.

### Write Actionable Queries, Not Vague Notes

When handing off open questions to the author or another editor, write queries that can be acted on without a follow-up round. A good query states the problem, the location, the options, and what is needed. "Page 42: the statistic cites a 2021 source but the surrounding text says 2023. Which is correct? Please confirm the source and date." A bad query is just a question mark in the margin.

Actionable queries reduce the number of revision rounds, which is critical under deadline. Each round of back-and-forth consumes time, so front-load the clarity of every query.

### Escalate Risk Problems Immediately, Not at the End

Problems involving legal risk, safety, plagiarism, serious factual error, or harm to individuals or groups must be escalated as soon as they are identified, not batched into a final report. These problems can change whether the work is publishable at all, and early escalation gives the publisher time to act. A late escalation may leave no time for resolution before the deadline.

State the risk plainly and recommend the type of review needed. "This passage makes a specific medical claim about a treatment; I recommend medical expert review before publication." Do not soften the recommendation to avoid causing alarm; the alarm is the point.

### Route to the Right Specialist, Not the Convenient One

Escalation is only useful if it reaches someone with the authority and expertise to resolve the problem. Routing a legal question to a junior editor wastes time. Routing a fact-check question to the author when the author is the source of the questionable claim creates a conflict. Think about who can actually resolve the issue and route there directly, or recommend the correct destination if you cannot route it yourself.

When you are unsure who the right person is, escalate to the person who can decide, such as the commissioning editor or project lead, with a clear statement of the problem and the kind of expertise needed.

### Preserve Context So the Receiver Does Not Start from Zero

A handoff or escalation should include enough context that the receiver understands why the problem matters. Include the relevant passage, the nature of the concern, any relevant constraints such as deadline or house style, and what you have already ruled out. A bare flag with no context forces the receiver to reconstruct your reasoning, which wastes time and can lead to a different, wrong conclusion.

### Confirm Receipt and Resolution for Critical Items

For high-stakes escalations, do not assume the problem was handled just because you flagged it. Confirm that the right person received it and that it was resolved. A flag that sits in an inbox unread is the same as no flag at all. For legal, safety, or factual-risk items, follow up and get explicit confirmation before the work moves forward.

## Common Traps

### Silently Leaving Out-of-Scope Problems Unaddressed

Noting a potential legal issue in your own head but not flagging it because it is "not my job." If you saw it, it is now partly your responsibility to route it. Silent omission is how serious problems reach publication.

### Over-Escalating Routine Problems

Routing every style question to the author or every minor ambiguity to a specialist. Over-escalation wastes expert time, delays the work, and trains receivers to ignore your flags. Reserve escalation for problems that genuinely require other expertise or authority.

### Vague Handoff Notes

Handing off with a note like "needs more work" or "check this." The receiver does not know what work or what to check, and either re-does your analysis or misses the actual problem. Specificity is the entire value of a handoff note.

### Treating the Author as the Resolver of Editorial Problems

Sending the author a long list of questions that you could resolve yourself with house style or general judgment. The author should be queried only for things only they can answer, such as intent, source, or factual confirmation. Over-querying the author burns their time and yours.

### Escalating Too Late for Action

Flagging a legal or safety risk in the final report when publication is hours away. Late escalation may leave no time for review and forces a choice between delay and publishing a known risk. Escalate risk problems when you find them.

### Assuming a Flag Equals Resolution

Flagging a problem and moving on without confirming it was received and resolved. Flags get lost, especially under deadline pressure when inboxes are full. For critical items, confirm the loop is closed.

### Losing Track of Open Items Across Handoffs

When work passes through multiple hands, open items can fall into the gaps between people. Maintain a single shared list of open items, queries, and escalations so that nothing depends on one person's memory. A scattered set of margin notes and emails is how items get lost.

## Self-Check

- Did I classify each problem correctly as in-scope, out-of-scope, or requiring escalation?
- Did I produce a handoff note documenting what was reviewed, changed, flagged, and not reviewed?
- Are my queries actionable, stating the problem, location, options, and what is needed?
- Did I escalate legal, safety, plagiarism, and serious factual risks immediately, not at the end?
- Did I route each escalation to someone with the actual expertise and authority to resolve it?
- Did I include enough context in each handoff and escalation that the receiver does not start from zero?
- For critical items, did I confirm receipt and resolution rather than assuming the flag was handled?
- Did I avoid over-escalating routine problems that I could resolve with my own judgment?
- Did I query the author only for things only the author can answer?
- Is there a single shared list of open items so nothing falls through the gaps between handoffs?
