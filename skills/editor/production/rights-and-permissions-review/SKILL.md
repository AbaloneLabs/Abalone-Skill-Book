# Rights And Permissions Review

Rights and permissions work determines what a publication may legally include, what it must license, what it must cut, and what exposures it carries. A manuscript can be editorially excellent and legally unpublishable because it reproduces copyrighted text, images, lyrics, or data without clearance, relies on a fair use claim that will not hold, or includes material whose rights were never secured from a contributor. This is production-stage work with real liability, and treating it casually creates risk for the publisher, the author, and the editor.

Use this skill when reviewing a work nearing publication for rights compliance. It covers identifying third-party material, assessing fair use, clearing permissions, managing contributor and licensing contracts, handling public domain and orphan works, addressing trademarks and publicity rights, and knowing when to escalate to legal counsel. The editor's task is to ensure the work contains only what it has the right to contain, and to surface rights problems early enough to solve them without derailing the schedule.

## Core Rules

### Inventory All Third-Party Material Early

Rights problems are cheapest to solve early and most expensive to solve at the printer or upload. The first task is to find every piece of material the work draws from outside its own original content.

Build an inventory of all third-party material: quoted text beyond de minimis use, song lyrics, poems, images, photographs, illustrations, charts, tables, maps, extended data, software, and any content sourced from elsewhere. For each item, record the source, the amount used, the purpose of the use, and the rights status as far as known. An accurate inventory is the foundation of every later decision; a work with hidden third-party material is a work with hidden risk.

### Assess Fair Use Honestly, Not Optimistically

Fair use is a real doctrine, but it is fact-specific, uncertain, and often invoked to justify uses that would not survive a challenge. Editors must assess fair use claims critically rather than accepting the writer's confidence.

Fair use turns on factors including the purpose and character of the use, the nature of the copyrighted work, the amount and substantiality used, and the effect on the market for the original. Commentary, criticism, scholarship, and news reporting favor fair use; decorative or substitutive use does not. Using the heart of a work, even a small amount, weighs against fair use. When a use is commercial and could plausibly be licensed, the fair use argument weakens. When in doubt, treat the use as requiring permission rather than relying on fair use, and escalate genuinely uncertain cases to counsel.

### Clear Permissions With Lead Time

Permissions clearance is slow. Rights holders can take weeks or months to respond, may charge fees, may refuse, or may be impossible to locate. Late clearance becomes a schedule crisis or a forced cut.

Start clearance as soon as the inventory is stable. Identify the rights holder, which is not always the author, request the specific rights needed for the specific territory, format, term, and quantity, and get the grant in writing. Track each request, its status, its cost, and its conditions. Build schedule slack for the items most likely to be slow or refused. Never assume permission; assume refusal until a written grant exists.

### Match The Permission To The Actual Use

Permissions are specific. A grant to quote in a print edition does not cover an e-book, an audiobook, a translation, a course pack, or a website unless those rights were included. Mismatched permissions are a common and quiet violation.

For each cleared item, confirm that the permission covers every format, territory, language, and edition the work will actually use, plus any likely future uses. If the work will be translated, distributed globally, or adapted, secure those rights now or flag them as out of scope. Re-clear when uses expand beyond the original grant. Document the scope of each permission so future format decisions can be checked against it.

### Secure Contributor Rights In Writing

Contributors, including writers, photographers, illustrators, translators, and interviewees, hold rights in their contributions unless those rights are assigned or licensed in writing. Verbal agreements and assumptions create disputes later.

Ensure every contributor has a written agreement specifying what rights are transferred or licensed to the publisher, in what formats and territories, for what term, and with what attribution. Distinguish work made for hire, which vests rights in the publisher, from licensed contributions, where the contributor retains underlying rights. Confirm that contributors had the rights they conveyed, especially for material they sourced or adapted from elsewhere. Unsecured contributor rights are a recurring source of post-publication claims.

### Handle Public Domain And Orphan Works Carefully

Public domain material is free to use, but determining that material is in the public domain is harder than it looks, especially across jurisdictions and for recent works. Orphan works, whose rights holders cannot be located, carry residual risk even after diligent search.

Verify public domain status by the rules of the relevant jurisdiction, noting that terms differ by country and that restoration of rights has expanded some domains unexpectedly. For adaptations, translations, or new editions of public domain works, remember that the new layer may carry its own copyright. For orphan works, document a diligent, good-faith search for the rights holder, and weigh the risk of use against the option to cut or replace. Treat "I could not find the owner" as risk reduction, not elimination.

### Address Trademarks, Publicity, And Defamation Adjacencies

Copyright is not the only rights surface. Trademarks, rights of publicity, and defamation concerns affect what a work can safely include, especially in commercial, biographical, and critical contexts.

For trademarks, use them as nouns with appropriate attribution when referring to branded goods, and avoid uses that imply endorsement or dilution. For publicity rights, especially for commercial works, be cautious with names, images, and likenesses of identifiable people used for promotional rather than informational purposes. For material that could defame or invade privacy, coordinate with the substantive and legal review, because a rights-clear work can still carry liability on other grounds. Keep these surfaces on the same checklist as copyright.

### Credit And Attribute Per The Grant

Permissions and contributor agreements usually specify how material must be credited. Failing to provide the required credit is a breach of the grant, even when the use itself was permitted.

Carry credit and attribution requirements from each permission into the production files and verify them at proof. Check placement, wording, copyright notices, and any disclaimers the grant required. A common failure is securing permission correctly and then dropping or altering the credit in layout. Treat credit lines as contractual obligations, not editorial decoration.

### Escalate Uncertain And High-Stakes Cases To Counsel and document Everything For The Record

Editors can resolve routine permissions, but some cases require legal judgment: substantial fair use claims on commercial works, disputes over rights ownership, potential defamation, takedown history, or material from litigious rights holders. Recognize the line and escalate.

Escalate when the financial or reputational stakes are high, when the rights status is genuinely unclear, when a rights holder has objected, or when the use pushes the boundaries of fair use in a commercial context. Document what you escalated and the guidance received. Escalation is not failure; it is the correct handling of risk that exceeds editorial authority.

Rights decisions must be reconstructable. If a work is challenged, the publisher will need to show what was cleared, on what terms, and on what basis any un-cleared use was deemed permissible.

Maintain a rights file for each work: the inventory, permissions granted, fees paid, fair use rationales, contributor agreements, public domain determinations, orphan work searches, and counsel guidance. Keep credits and conditions tied to the specific items they govern. A complete record converts a future challenge from a crisis into a routine response.

## Common Traps

### Hidden Third-Party Material

Lyrics, images, charts, and extended quotes buried in the manuscript create hidden risk. Inventory everything early.

### Optimistic Fair Use

Fair use invoked to avoid clearance often fails under scrutiny. Assess honestly and license when in doubt.

### Late Permissions Clearance

Clearance is slow and rights holders may refuse. Start early and build schedule slack.

### Permission Scope Mismatch

A grant for one format or territory does not cover others. Match permission to every actual and likely use.

### Verbal Contributor Agreements

Assumptions about contributor rights become disputes. Secure rights in writing for every contributor.

### Misjudged Public Domain

Public domain status varies by jurisdiction and is complicated by restoration and new layers. Verify carefully.

### Treating Orphan Works As Free and ignoring Trademark, Publicity, And Defamation

A diligent search reduces risk but does not eliminate it. Weigh use against the option to cut.

Copyright is one surface among several. Keep adjacent rights on the same checklist.

### Dropped Or Altered Credits and handling High-Stakes Cases Without Counsel

Failing to provide required credit breaches the permission grant. Verify credits at proof.

Some decisions exceed editorial authority. Escalate uncertain, contested, or high-stakes cases.

## Self-Check

Before treating rights and permissions review as complete, verify:

- A full inventory of third-party material has been built, with source, amount, purpose, and rights status for each item.
- Each fair use claim has been assessed critically against the four factors, with uncertain cases treated as requiring permission or escalated.
- Permissions clearance began early enough, with each request tracked for status, cost, and conditions.
- Every cleared permission covers all actual and likely formats, territories, languages, and editions the work will use.
- Every contributor has a written agreement specifying the rights transferred or licensed, with scope and attribution.
- Public domain determinations account for jurisdiction, restoration, and new layers, and are not assumed.
- Orphan works have a documented diligent search, with use weighed against the option to cut or replace.
- Trademark, publicity, and defamation surfaces have been checked alongside copyright.
- Required credits and attributions are carried into production and verified at proof; high-stakes, uncertain, or contested cases have been escalated to legal counsel with the guidance documented
- A complete rights file exists so any future challenge can be answered from the record; the work contains only material it has the right to contain, in the formats and territories it will actually use
