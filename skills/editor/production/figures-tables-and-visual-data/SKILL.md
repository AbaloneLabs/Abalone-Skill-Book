# Figures Tables And Visual Data

Figures and tables condense complex information into a form readers can scan, compare, and remember. Because they appear authoritative and compact, errors and distortions in them are both more likely to be trusted and harder to detect than the same problems in prose. A chart with a truncated axis, a table with misaligned columns, or a legend that does not match the data can mislead readers more effectively than any sentence, because readers treat visual displays as evidence. Editing visual data is therefore a distinct discipline from editing prose, requiring attention to accuracy, integrity, structure, and the relationship between the visual and the claims it supports.

Use this skill when editing any work containing charts, graphs, diagrams, tables, maps, or other visual data displays. It covers reviewing chart accuracy and axis integrity, checking table structure and alignment, writing and verifying captions and legends, ensuring readability of color and labeling, preventing visual distortion of the underlying data, and preparing visual material for publication across formats. The editor's task is to ensure that every visual display is accurate, honest, legible, and genuinely supportive of the work's claims.

## Core Rules

### Verify The Visual Against The Underlying Data

The first task is to confirm that the visual represents the data it claims to represent. A figure is a claim about numbers, relationships, or categories, and like any claim it must be checked against its source. Visuals are often rebuilt or restyled during production, and each rebuild can introduce errors that prose review will not catch.

For each figure or table, obtain the underlying data and confirm that the visual matches it. Check that values, categories, and labels correspond to the source dataset and that no points have been dropped, merged, or transposed during production. For derived values, percentages, sums, averages, confirm the calculation is correct and the method is stated. For tables, verify that row and column totals are consistent with their cells and that any computed columns are correct. A visual that cannot be reconciled to its data cannot be trusted, regardless of how polished it appears. Reconciliation is the foundational check; everything else assumes it has been done.

### Preserve Axis And Scale Integrity

Axes and scales are where visual integrity is most often compromised, sometimes deliberately and sometimes through careless defaults. A chart's axes determine how readers perceive magnitude, trend, and comparison, and a manipulated or distorted axis can make a small change look dramatic or a large change look negligible. Editing must check that the scale serves honesty, not effect.

For each quantitative chart, examine the axes. Confirm that bar charts start at zero unless a non-zero baseline is clearly justified and labeled, since truncated baselines exaggerate differences. Confirm that line charts use scales appropriate to the variation shown and that the aspect ratio does not distort the apparent trend. Check that time axes are evenly spaced or clearly note irregular intervals, and that dual-axis charts, which invite spurious correlation, are used only with clear justification. Where a scale choice emphasizes or minimizes a pattern, ask whether it serves the reader or the argument, and adjust or annotate where it misleads. Integrity of scale is not a matter of convention; it is a matter of honesty.

### Ensure Tables Are Structured For Comparison

Tables exist to enable comparison, and their structure should serve that purpose. A well-structured table lets a reader find a value, compare across rows or columns, and understand the relationship between entries at a glance. A poorly structured table, inconsistent units, misaligned columns, or unclear headers, defeats the purpose and invites misreading.

For each table, confirm that the structure serves comparison. Check that column and row headers are clear and complete, including units of measurement and the time period or category each axis represents. Ensure that values within a column use consistent units and precision, so that comparison is not broken by mixed formats. Align cells to aid scanning, numbers right-aligned or decimal-aligned, text left-aligned, and use white space and rules to guide the eye rather than heavy gridlines. Where a table is large, consider whether summary rows, grouping, or splitting would aid comprehension. The test is whether a reader can answer the table's implicit questions, what is largest, what changed, how do these compare, without re-reading or recalculating.

### Write Captions And Legends That Match The Visual

Captions and legends are the bridge between a visual and the reader's interpretation of it. They should identify the visual, define its symbols, and state what it shows, and they must be consistent with the visual itself. Mismatch between caption and figure is a common and damaging error, because readers trust whichever they notice first.

For each visual, confirm the caption identifies the figure, defines abbreviations and symbols, and states the main point or the data source. Check that the legend matches the visual: colors and labels in the legend must correspond to those in the figure, with no orphaned or swapped entries. Ensure that units, time periods, and categories named in the caption match those in the visual. Where the caption makes a claim about what the figure shows, confirm the figure actually supports that claim, since a caption can assert a trend the visual does not clearly display. Captions and visuals must tell the same story; where they diverge, the reader is misled regardless of which is right.

### Check Color Labeling And Legibility Across Formats

Visuals are often designed on a large, high-resolution screen and then published across formats that degrade them. Color choices, label sizes, and line weights that work in a desktop proof can fail in print, on a phone, or in grayscale. Editing for publication must check that the visual survives the formats it will encounter.

For each visual, confirm that colors are distinguishable not only in full color but also for color-blind readers and in grayscale, using patterns or direct labels where color alone is risky. Check that labels and axis text are large enough to read at the published size, accounting for reduction from proof to final. Confirm that line weights and point markers are visible and that overlapping elements do not obscure data. For digital formats, check that the visual is legible on small screens and, where relevant, that it reflows or scales rather than breaking. A visual legible only at full size on a designer's monitor is not publication-ready.

### Prevent Visual Distortion Of The Surrounding Claims

A visual does not stand alone; it participates in the argument of the surrounding text. The same data, displayed differently, can support, undercut, or exaggerate the prose claims near it. Editing must check the relationship between the visual and the claims it is asked to support, not only the visual in isolation.

For each visual, read the surrounding text and confirm the visual supports the claims made near it. Watch for visuals that show a weak or ambiguous pattern while the text asserts a strong one, or visuals that emphasize a dramatic comparison the text then overstates. Watch for cherry-picked time windows or categories that make the visual support a claim the full data would not. Where the visual and the claim diverge, the remedy is to adjust the visual, narrow the claim, or add the qualifier the data requires. The obligation is that the visual and the prose tell a consistent and defensible story.

### Prepare Visuals For The Target Format And Medium

Different formats impose different constraints on visuals. A figure built for a full-color web page may fail in a printed book; a table designed for a wide spreadsheet may break in a narrow column or a reflowing e-book. Production editing must prepare each visual for the formats it will actually encounter, not only the one in which it was designed.

For each visual, confirm it is prepared for every target format. Check resolution and color mode for print, responsive behavior for web and e-book, and whether complex tables need to be simplified, split, or offered as downloadable data for narrow formats. Confirm that file formats are appropriate, vector where scalability matters, raster where photographic detail is needed, and that nothing depends on a format feature that will not render. Plan for the worst-case format the visual will meet, since a reader encountering the broken version has no way to know it was clear in the original.

## Common Traps

### Visual Unreconciled With Underlying Data

A polished figure can still misrepresent its source. Reconcile every visual to its data before anything else.

### Truncated Or Distorted Axes

Non-zero baselines and misleading aspect ratios exaggerate patterns. Check that scales serve honesty.

### Inconsistent Units Within A Table

Mixed units and precision break comparison. Standardize within columns and state units in headers.

### Caption And Legend Mismatch

Legend entries and caption claims must match the visual exactly. A swap misleads regardless of intent.

### Color Only Distinguishable In Full Color

Color-only encoding fails for color-blind and grayscale readers. Add patterns or direct labels.

### Labels Unreadable At Published Size

Proof legibility is not publication legibility. Check text size at the final rendered scale.

### Visual Overstating The Surrounding Claim

A weak pattern supporting a strong prose claim misleads. Align the visual and the text.

### Format-Specific Failure Overlooked

A visual clear in one medium may break in another. Prepare for every target format.

## Self-Check

Before treating figure and table editing as complete, verify:

- Every figure and table has been reconciled against its underlying data, with values, categories, and labels confirmed correct.
- Axes and scales present data honestly, with truncated baselines justified and labeled and aspect ratios not distorting trends.
- Tables are structured for comparison, with clear headers, consistent units and precision, and alignment that aids scanning.
- Captions and legends match their visuals exactly, with symbols, units, and claims consistent between text and figure.
- Colors, labels, and line weights are legible across formats, including for color-blind and grayscale readers and at published size.
- Each visual supports, rather than distorts or exaggerates, the claims made in the surrounding text.
- Visuals are prepared for every target format, with resolution, color mode, and responsive behavior appropriate to each medium.
- No visual depends on a format feature that will fail in a format the work will actually encounter.
- A reader encountering each visual in its weakest target format would still understand the information it exists to convey.
