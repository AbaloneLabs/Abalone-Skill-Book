# Pre-Publication QA

Pre-publication quality assurance is the final gate before release, and it is fundamentally different from editing. By this stage, the work has been developed, line-edited, copy-edited, proofread, designed, and built. QA is not improving the work; it is confirming that every required component is present, correct, and functional, and that nothing broken will reach the reader. The failure mode at this stage is not bad writing but missing pieces, broken functionality, mismatched metadata, unresolved legal items, and platform incompatibilities that no earlier stage was positioned to catch. QA is the systems check that prevents the embarrassing and the catastrophic.

Use this skill in the final stage before publication, across print, digital, web, and multi-format releases. It covers running a comprehensive release checklist, verifying text and format integrity, checking metadata and identifiers, confirming legal and rights clearance, validating accessibility, testing platform and distribution requirements, and managing the sign-off process. The editor's task is to ensure the work is complete and correct as a whole system, not just as a text.

## Core Rules

### Run A Release Checklist Covering Every Component

Pre-publication QA fails when it relies on memory. The number of components, formats, and requirements is too large to hold in mind, and the cost of a missed item is publication of an incomplete or broken work. A written release checklist is the foundation.

Build a checklist that covers every component of the release: final text in every format, cover and design files, metadata and identifiers, legal and rights items, accessibility, platform and distribution requirements, marketing and catalog materials, and the sign-offs required from each role. Run the checklist item by item, marking each verified. Treat anything unchecked as unresolved. The checklist converts the release from a hope-that's-everything moment into a verified process, and it creates a record that QA was performed.

### Verify Text Integrity Across Every Format

The text must be correct not just in the master file but in every format that will be published. Conversion to print, e-book, web, and other formats routinely introduces errors, drops content, or breaks formatting that was correct in the source.

Verify each published format against the approved final text. Check that no content was dropped during conversion, that special characters and equations survived, that headings and hierarchy are intact, that cross-references and links resolve, and that formatting, italics, lists, tables, renders correctly in each format. Do not assume that because the master is correct, the derivatives are. Each format is a separate artifact that must be checked, because conversion is where integrity breaks.

### Confirm Metadata, Identifiers, And Catalog Information

Metadata and identifiers travel with the work into catalogs, stores, libraries, and search systems, and errors here affect discoverability, sales, and citation for the life of the work. Pre-publication is the last chance to catch them.

Verify that titles, subtitles, author names, contributor roles, descriptions, categories, keywords, and series information are correct and consistent across all systems. Confirm identifiers such as ISBNs, DOIs, and ISSNs are assigned correctly, one per distinct edition or format, and recorded accurately. Check publication dates, prices, territories, and format specifications. Verify that the metadata in the work's files matches the metadata in catalog and distribution systems. Metadata errors are hard to fix after release and damage discoverability, so they carry disproportionate weight at QA.

### Confirm Legal, Rights, And Permissions Are Closed

Legal items must be fully resolved before release. A work can be editorially perfect and legally unpublishable because a permission was never finalized, a credit line is missing, or a contributor agreement was never signed.

Confirm that every permission is granted in writing and covers the actual formats and territories of release. Verify that all required credits and copyright notices are present and correctly placed. Confirm contributor agreements are signed and that rights are secured for every format. Check that any fair use decisions are documented and that high-stakes cases were reviewed by counsel. Confirm trademarks, disclaimers, and any required legal language are present. Treat any open legal item as a release blocker, because publishing with unresolved rights is a liability, not a risk to manage later.

### Validate Accessibility Requirements

Accessibility is both an ethical obligation and, in many contexts, a legal requirement. Pre-publication QA must verify that the work meets the accessibility standards appropriate to its format and audience.

Check that alt text is present and meaningful for all informative images and that decorative images are marked. Verify document structure, headings, lists, and reading order, is logical for assistive technology. Confirm color is not the sole means of conveying information and that contrast meets standards. For digital formats, check that the work is navigable by keyboard and screen reader, that metadata indicates accessibility features, and that any video or audio has captions or transcripts. Accessibility validation is a distinct QA pass because it requires different tools and a different reading than text or format checks.

### Test Platform And Distribution Requirements

Each distribution platform has technical and content requirements, and failing them causes rejection, delayed release, or broken presentation. QA must verify the work meets the requirements of every platform it will reach.

Check file formats, sizes, and specifications against each platform's requirements. Verify that metadata fields required by each platform are populated correctly. Test that links, embedded media, and interactive elements function on the target platforms. For app stores, aggregators, and institutional repositories, confirm compliance with their specific content and technical rules. Run any platform-provided validation tools. A work that is correct in the abstract but non-compliant with a platform's rules will not reach its readers through that platform, so platform testing is part of release readiness.

### Verify Cross-References, Links, And Interactive Elements

Internal and external references must resolve correctly in the final published form. Broken cross-references, dead links, and non-functional interactive elements are common and highly visible defects that erode reader trust.

Check every internal cross-reference, to chapters, sections, figures, tables, and notes, for accuracy after any structural changes. Test every external link to confirm it resolves to the intended destination, and consider link rot for works with long lifespans. Verify that interactive elements, forms, embedded media, and navigation function as intended. For digital works, test links and navigation on the actual platforms and devices readers will use. Reference and link integrity is a distinct QA category because it requires functional testing, not just reading.

### Coordinate And Document Sign-Offs

Publication requires sign-off from multiple roles: editorial, design, production, legal, marketing, and author. Uncoordinated sign-off leads to release of a work that one party had not approved, or to disputes after release about who approved what.

Identify every role whose sign-off is required and obtain it explicitly, in writing, before release. Document what each sign-off covered, the final text, the design, the metadata, the legal clearance, so that approvals are specific. Confirm the author's final approval of the work as it will be published. Track sign-offs to closure and do not release until all are received. A documented sign-off process protects against post-release disputes and ensures no party is surprised by what is published.

### Manage The Final Correction Loop Safely and prepare For Post-Publication Correction Processes

Last-minute corrections are sometimes necessary, but each one risks introducing new errors. QA must manage the final correction loop so that necessary fixes happen without destabilizing the verified work.

Triage last-minute changes by severity: only blocking errors should be fixed at this stage, and everything else should defer to a post-publication correction or next edition. For each accepted change, re-verify the affected areas and their surroundings after the change is entered, because corrections introduce errors at a high rate. Limit the number of correction rounds, because each round erodes the stability of the verified state. When the work reaches a clean, verified state, freeze it and resist further changes unless a blocking error appears.

Even with thorough QA, errors sometimes appear after publication. Pre-publication QA should establish how post-publication corrections will be handled, because the absence of a process turns a small error into a crisis.

Confirm how errata, corrections, and updates will be issued for each format, and who has authority to approve them. For digital works, understand the update mechanism and its implications for versioning and citation. For print, understand the reprint or correction process. Establishing this before publication means that when an error is found, the response is routine rather than panicked. Pre-publication QA includes preparing for the reality that publication is not always the final state.

## Common Traps

### Relying On Memory Instead Of A Checklist

Too many components to hold in mind. Run a written release checklist and mark each item verified.

### Assuming Derivative Formats Match The Master

Conversion introduces errors. Verify text and format integrity in every published format.

### Metadata Errors At Release

Metadata travels with the work for its life. Verify titles, identifiers, and catalog information across all systems.

### Open Legal Items Treated As Manageable Risk

Unresolved rights are a release blocker. Confirm all permissions, credits, and agreements are closed.

### Skipping Accessibility Validation

Accessibility requires distinct tools and reading. Run it as a separate QA pass.

### Non-Compliance With Platform Requirements

Platform rules govern reach. Test against each platform's technical and content requirements.

### Unverified Links And References and uncoordinated Or Assumed Sign-Off

Broken references erode trust. Functionally test internal and external links and interactive elements.

Release without explicit, documented approval from each role causes disputes. Track sign-offs to closure.

### Destabilizing Late Corrections and no Post-Publication Correction Process

Each correction risks new errors. Triage strictly and re-verify after every change.

Absence of a process turns small errors into crises. Establish correction handling before release.

## Self-Check

Before treating pre-publication QA as complete, verify:

- A release checklist has been run covering every component, with each item marked verified.
- Text integrity has been confirmed in every published format, not just the master file.
- Metadata, identifiers, and catalog information are correct and consistent across all systems.
- All permissions are granted in writing with required credits present, and high-stakes legal items are closed.
- Accessibility requirements have been validated with appropriate tools for the format and audience.
- The work meets the technical and content requirements of every distribution platform.
- Cross-references, links, and interactive elements have been functionally tested on target platforms.
- Sign-offs have been obtained explicitly and documented from every required role, including the author.
- Last-minute corrections have been triaged to blocking items only, with affected areas re-verified.
- A post-publication correction process has been established for each format; the work is complete and correct as a whole system, ready to reach the reader with nothing broken or missing
