# Final Error Detection

Proofreading is the last reading before publication, and its purpose is narrow but absolute: to catch the errors that survived every earlier stage. By the time a work reaches proof, structure, argument, voice, and even most copy-editing decisions are settled. The proofreader is not revising; the proofreader is hunting. This narrowness is what makes the stage effective, and abandoning it, by reopening editorial questions or treating the proof as another revision pass, is what makes it fail. Errors that reach publication are often not subtle; they are the ones nobody looked for because everyone assumed an earlier stage caught them.

Use this skill at the proofreading stage, on proofs, galleys, or final files, when the task is to detect residual errors before release. It covers reading strategies that surface what normal reading misses, the categories of error most likely to survive, the discipline of not editing at this stage, and the systems that prevent errors from re-entering after correction. The editor's task is to find what is wrong without changing what is right.

## Core Rules

### Read For Error, Not For Improvement

The defining discipline of proofreading is that you are not improving the work; you are catching defects. Once a work is in proof, every change risks introducing new errors, and every editorial temptation dilutes the attention needed to catch the errors that matter.

Resist the urge to revise, rephrase, or "improve" at this stage. If you notice a substantive issue, flag it separately for the appropriate authority rather than fixing it in the proof. Focus your reading on detecting what is wrong against the established standard: typos, wrong words, punctuation errors, formatting defects, broken references, and inconsistencies with the style sheet. The proofreader who starts rewriting has stopped proofreading, and the errors that reach publication are the ones missed during that drift.

### Change Your Reading Strategy To Surface What Normal Reading Misses

Normal reading is optimized for meaning, which means the brain corrects errors automatically and skips over them. Proofreading requires defeating this optimization. The same text read the same way will hide the same errors.

Use strategies that disrupt automatic reading. Read slowly and deliberately, ideally aloud or with a pointer. Read backward by word or by sentence to break the flow of meaning and force attention onto each token. Change the visual format: zoom in, change font, print on paper, or use a different screen. Read in short sessions because proofreading attention decays fast. For critical work, have a second proofreader read independently, because different readers miss different errors. The goal is to make the familiar text unfamiliar enough that errors become visible.

### Target The Error Categories Most Likely To Survive

Not all errors are equally likely to reach proof. Earlier stages catch most grammar and structure problems. What survives tends to fall into predictable categories, and proofreading should target them deliberately.

Hunt especially for: typographical errors that form valid words, such as form versus from or public versus pubic, which spell-check will not flag; repeated or dropped words; wrong homophones and confusable pairs; inconsistent names, terms, numbers, and dates; broken or stale cross-references after structural changes; figure, table, and note numbering errors; caption and label mismatches; heading capitalization inconsistencies; punctuation errors around quotes, series, and parentheticals; and formatting glitches like missing italics, broken spacing, or widow and orphan lines. Build a mental or written checklist of these categories and actively search for each, rather than passively reading and hoping to notice.

### Proof Display Text And Metadata Separately And First

Headlines, subheads, captions, pull quotes, callouts, tables, and metadata carry disproportionate weight because readers and search engines see them first, and they often survive from early drafts while the body was revised. Errors here damage credibility more than errors in body text.

Proof display text as a separate pass, reading all headlines, then all captions, then all pull quotes, rather than interleaving them with body reading. This surfaces patterns, like inconsistent capitalization or tense across headings, that disappear when read in context. Check that pull quotes are verbatim from the body, that captions match their figures, and that metadata like titles, descriptions, and author names are correct. Display text errors are the most embarrassing to publish because they are the most visible.

### Verify Numbers, Names, And Cross-References As A System

Numbers, proper names, and cross-references are factual elements where a single wrong character creates a real error, and they are exactly the elements that automatic tools handle poorly. They require systematic verification.

Check every number against its source where possible: dates, quantities, statistics, prices, and measurements. Verify proper names against authoritative sources, watching for subtle errors like transposed letters or wrong initials. Check cross-references by mapping them: every "see Chapter 4" points to the current Chapter 4, every figure number is sequential and referenced correctly, every note matches its anchor. Treat these as a system to verify, not as text to read, because reading alone will not catch a number that is internally consistent but wrong.

### Compare Against The Copy-Edited Version, Not Just Read Cold

Cold proofreading catches many errors but misses changes that were correct in the copy-edited version and broke during typesetting or file conversion. Comparison against the prior version catches the errors introduced between stages.

Where possible, compare the proof against the final edited version, ideally using a diff tool for digital text. Look for dropped words, changed numbers, altered punctuation, missing formatting, and reflowed text that introduced errors. Typesetting and conversion routinely introduce errors at this boundary, and only comparison catches them. Cold reading alone assumes the proof matches the edited version, which is exactly the assumption that lets conversion errors reach publication.

### Do Not Reopen Settled Editorial Decisions

A proof that reopens style, voice, or substance decisions becomes a revision pass and stops being a proofread. Each reopened decision costs attention and risks introducing errors, and it delays publication.

If a proofreader disagrees with a settled decision, flag it once for the appropriate editor rather than changing it. Do not silently "correct" a usage that was deliberately chosen, even if you would have chosen differently. Do not standardize something the style sheet left variable. Do not rewrite a sentence that is correct but not to your taste. The proof stage's authority is to catch error against the established standard, not to revise the standard. Preserving this boundary is what keeps the stage fast and effective.

### Track Queries And Corrections So Nothing Is Lost

Proofreading generates queries and corrections that must be tracked to closure. A query that goes unanswered or a correction that is not entered is an error that reaches publication. The system matters as much as the reading.

Record every query and correction with its location and the action required. Track each to resolution: entered, rejected, deferred, or escalated. After corrections are entered, re-proof the changed areas, because corrections themselves introduce errors at a high rate. Close the loop only when every query is resolved and every changed area has been re-checked. An untracked query is worse than no query because it creates false confidence that the issue was handled.

### Re-Proof After Every Round Of Corrections

Corrections introduce new errors. A typesetter fixing a typo may reflow a paragraph, drop a line, or break a cross-reference. The work is not done when corrections are entered; it is done when the corrected areas have been re-proofed.

After each correction round, re-read the changed areas and their immediate surroundings, since reflow affects nearby text. For high-stakes work, do a final full pass after the last correction. Treat the pre-publication state as suspect until it has been read after the final change. The most common path by which an error reaches publication is a correction entered correctly that broke something adjacent, in a section nobody re-read.

## Common Traps

### Editing Instead Of Proofreading

Reopening revision at proof stage dilutes attention and introduces errors. Read for error, not improvement.

### Reading In The Normal Way

Normal reading auto-corrects errors. Change format, direction, and pace to make errors visible.

### Passive Reading Without A Target List

Hoping to notice errors catches only the obvious. Actively hunt the categories most likely to survive.

### Ignoring Display Text And Metadata

Headlines and captions carry disproportionate weight and often survive from early drafts. Proof them separately and first.

### Treating Numbers And Names As Text

Numbers and names need systematic verification against sources, not reading. Internal consistency does not mean correctness.

### Cold Reading Without Comparison

Conversion and typesetting introduce errors between stages. Compare against the prior version.

### Reopening Settled Decisions

Silently "correcting" deliberate choices turns proof into revision. Flag disagreements, do not change.

### Untracked Queries And Corrections and skipping Re-Proof After Corrections

A query not closed is an error published. Track every item to resolution.

Corrections introduce new errors. Re-proof changed areas and surroundings after every round.

## Self-Check

Before treating final error detection as complete, verify:

- Reading has been confined to detecting error against the established standard, with no revision or improvement at this stage.
- Reading strategies have been varied, format, direction, pace, and reader, to disrupt automatic comprehension.
- A targeted checklist of likely-surviving error categories has been actively hunted, not passively watched for.
- Display text and metadata have been proofed separately and first, with pull quotes verified against body text.
- Numbers, names, and cross-references have been verified as a system against sources, not merely read.
- The proof has been compared against the copy-edited version to catch conversion and typesetting errors.
- Settled editorial decisions have not been reopened; disagreements have been flagged, not silently changed.
- Every query and correction has been tracked to resolution.
- Changed areas and their surroundings have been re-proofed after every correction round.
- A final pass has confirmed the pre-publication state after the last change; the work is free of the residual errors this stage exists to catch, without changes that introduced new ones
