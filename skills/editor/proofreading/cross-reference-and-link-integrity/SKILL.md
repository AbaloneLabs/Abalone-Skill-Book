# Cross Reference And Link Integrity

Cross-references and links are the connective tissue of a finished work, and they are uniquely fragile because they point to targets that move. A "see Chapter 4" becomes wrong when chapters are renumbered; a figure reference breaks when a figure is moved or deleted; a footnote anchor loses its note during reflow; a hyperlink goes to the wrong page or rots after publication; a table of contents page number drifts after pagination changes; an index entry points to the wrong location. Each of these is invisible in a normal read of the body text and obvious the moment a reader follows the pointer and lands nowhere, or worse, somewhere misleading. Proofreading for cross-reference and link integrity is the discipline of treating every pointer as a claim that must be verified against its target, systematically, because reading alone will never catch a reference that is internally consistent but wrong. Errors here are common, consequential, and exactly what final proofreading exists to catch.

Use this skill at the proofreading stage, when the task is to verify that all internal references and links resolve correctly. It covers checking textual cross-references, hyperlinks, figure and table references, footnote and endnote anchors, table of contents and index entries, and pagination-dependent references. The editor's task is to confirm every pointer reaches its intended target and that no broken or misdirected reference reaches the reader.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Reference As A Claim To Verify Against Its Target

A cross-reference is a promise that something exists at a location, and like any factual claim it must be verified, not assumed. The most common proofreading failure is reading references as text rather than as pointers, assuming they are correct because they look right. Internal consistency, a reference that matches a nearby label, does not guarantee correctness if both moved together to the wrong place.

For each reference, follow it to its target and confirm the target is what the reference claims. "See Figure 3.2" must lead to Figure 3.2, not 3.1 or a different figure now numbered 3.2. "See page 47" must lead to the content intended, not whatever now sits on page 47 after reflow. Treat verification as mechanical and exhaustive, because no sampling strategy catches the one broken reference that matters.

### Verify Textual Cross-References After Any Structural Change

Textual cross-references, references to chapters, sections, pages, or numbered elements, are especially vulnerable to structural changes. Any reorganization, renumbering, insertion, or deletion can invalidate them, and they are rarely re-checked automatically.

After any structural change, re-verify all textual cross-references. Check chapter and section numbers against the current structure. Check page references against current pagination, which changes with any reflow. Check numbered element references, figures, tables, equations, footnotes, against current numbering. Where a reference uses a label rather than a number, "see the section on X," verify the labeled content still matches. Make this re-verification a mandatory step after restructuring, because stale references are the signature defect of revised works.

### Check Hyperlinks Resolve To The Correct Destination

Hyperlinks fail in two ways: they break, pointing to a page that no longer exists, and they misdirect, pointing to a page that exists but is not the intended one. Both reach the reader. Link rot, where targets disappear over time, and link drift, where targets change content, mean links must be checked close to publication.

Check every hyperlink by following it to its destination, not just confirming it loads. Verify the destination is the intended target and that it supports the context where the link appears. For links to specific sections or anchors, verify the anchor resolves correctly, since section links break when pages are restructured. Where possible, use archival or permanent links (DOIs, archived versions) for sources likely to change. Record link-checking as a distinct pass, because links are the references most likely to fail after the editor's last read.

### Verify Figure, Table, And Exhibit References

Figures, tables, exhibits, and other numbered visual elements are referenced from the text and captioned at their location, and mismatches between the two are common, especially after elements are added, removed, or reordered. A reference to "Table 4" that points to a table now numbered "Table 5," or a caption that does not match its in-text reference, confuses readers and undermines credibility.

Build a map of all visual elements and their references. For each, verify that the in-text reference, the element's label, and the caption all agree, and that the element appears in the right sequence. Check that every referenced element exists and that every element is referenced where it should be. Orphaned elements, figures with no in-text reference, and dangling references, text pointing to non-existent elements, are both failures to catch.

### Reconcile Footnote And Endnote Anchors With Their Notes

Footnotes and endnotes have anchors in the text and notes at the document or page end, and the connection between them breaks during reflow, editing, and conversion. An anchor that loses its note, a note that loses its anchor, or numbering that falls out of sequence are common and disorienting to readers.

Verify each footnote and endnote anchor against its corresponding note. Check that numbering is sequential and that each anchor maps to the correct note, not just the next one in sequence. After any reflow or renumbering, re-verify the anchor-note mapping. Check the transition between note numbering systems if the work uses both footnotes and endnotes. Note integrity is fragile and must be checked mechanically, not by reading.

### Verify Table Of Contents, Lists, And Index Entries

Front matter lists (table of contents, list of figures, list of tables) and back matter indexes are dense with references that must match the body exactly. Titles in the contents must match section titles verbatim; page numbers must match current pagination; index entries must point to the right locations. These are high-visibility elements where errors are immediately apparent to readers using them for navigation.

Verify every table of contents entry against the corresponding section title, checking for verbatim match including wording, capitalization, and punctuation. Verify all page numbers against current pagination. For lists of figures and tables, verify titles and page numbers. For the index, spot-check entries against their referenced pages, with extra attention to entries that may have shifted after reflow. Front and back matter references are where readers navigate from, so their accuracy is disproportionately important.

### Check Pagination-Dependent References After Reflow

Any reference that includes a page number is pagination-dependent, and pagination changes with every reflow: a corrected typo, an inserted paragraph, a changed figure size shifts everything after it. References verified before a reflow become stale after it, and re-verification is mandatory.

After the final reflow, re-verify all page-number references: cross-references, table of contents, index, lists of figures and tables. Treat the pre-publication state as suspect until page references have been checked against the final pagination. The most common path by which a page reference reaches publication wrong is that it was correct before the last correction and never re-checked after.

### Document And Track Reference Corrections To Closure

Reference corrections, like all proofreading corrections, must be tracked to closure, and because reference fixes often trigger reflow that invalidates other references, the tracking must account for ripple effects. A corrected cross-reference may shift pagination, breaking page references elsewhere.

Record every reference correction and re-verify affected references after the correction is entered, since fixes introduce new errors. For works with many cross-references, plan a final reference-verification pass after all other corrections are stable, so that the last change does not leave stale references. Close the loop only when every reference has been verified against the final, stable state of the work.

## Common Traps

### Reading References As Text Instead Of Pointers

A reference that looks right may point wrong. Follow every reference to its target and verify.

### Stale References After Structural Change

Renumbering and reflow invalidate textual references. Re-verify after any structural change.

### Links That Load But Misdirect

A working link to the wrong page is still an error. Verify the destination matches the intent.

### Figure And Table Reference Mismatches

In-text references, labels, and captions must agree. Map and verify all three.

### Broken Footnote And Endnote Anchor Mapping

Reflow separates anchors from notes. Verify the mapping mechanically after any change.

### Front And Back Matter Errors

Contents, lists, and indexes are navigation-critical. Verify titles and page numbers verbatim.

### Page References Not Re-Checked After Final Reflow

The last correction shifts pagination. Re-verify page references against final pagination.

### Untracked Reference Corrections And Ripple Effects

Reference fixes trigger reflow that breaks other references. Track and re-verify after corrections.

## Self-Check

Before treating cross-reference and link integrity proofreading as complete, verify:

- Every cross-reference has been followed to its target and confirmed correct, not merely read as text.
- Textual cross-references have been re-verified after any structural change, renumbering, or reflow.
- Every hyperlink has been followed to its destination and confirmed to resolve to the intended target.
- Figure, table, and exhibit references agree with their labels and captions, with no orphans or dangling references.
- Footnote and endnote anchors map correctly to their notes, with sequential numbering verified.
- Table of contents, lists of figures and tables, and index entries match the body verbatim with correct page numbers.
- All pagination-dependent references have been checked against the final pagination after the last reflow.
- Reference corrections have been tracked to closure, with affected references re-verified after fixes.
- A final reference-verification pass has confirmed every pointer resolves correctly in the stable final state.
