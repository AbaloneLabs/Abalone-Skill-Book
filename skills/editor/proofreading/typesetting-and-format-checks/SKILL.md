# Typesetting And Format Checks

Typesetting and formatting are the visual structure of a published work, and errors here are as real as errors in the text. A heading that loses its style, a table that breaks across pages illegibly, a list that renders as plain text, a figure that drifts from its reference, a page break that strands a heading at the bottom, or inconsistent indentation all damage readability and professionalism. These defects are often invisible to the writer and to text-focused editors, and they survive to publication because nobody checked the work as a designed object. Format checking is the proof-stage discipline of verifying that the work looks and behaves as intended across every surface.

Use this skill at the proof stage, on designed pages, galleys, or rendered output, when the task is to verify typesetting and formatting against the design specification. It covers checking typography and styles, page and line layout, headings and hierarchy, lists and tables, figures and captions, white space and breaks, and consistency across the whole. The editor's task is to confirm the work is visually correct and consistent before it is fixed in publication.

## Core Rules

### Verify Against The Design Specification, Not Personal Aesthetic

Format checking is not design opinion; it is verification that the typeset work matches the intended design. The specification, whether a formal design template or an established house style, defines what correct looks like. Drifting from it produces inconsistency; imposing personal taste produces conflict.

Obtain or reconstruct the design specification: the styles for each heading level, body text, captions, and special elements; the rules for page layout, margins, columns, and running heads; the treatment of lists, tables, figures, and white space; and the conventions for breaks, spacing, and emphasis. Check the proofs against this specification systematically. Where the spec is ambiguous, query the designer rather than improvising. The goal is faithful, consistent execution of the intended design, not a redesign at proof stage.

### Check Styles And Typography For Consistency

Typography is governed by paragraph and character styles, and inconsistency in their application is the most common formatting defect. A heading that lost its style, body text that picked up caption formatting, or emphasis applied through ad hoc formatting rather than the defined style all break the visual system.

Verify that every element uses its correct style: headings at each level, body text, block quotes, captions, callouts, and special elements. Check that emphasis, italics, bold, and small caps are applied through styles or consistently, not as one-off overrides. Look for style drift, where similar elements are formatted slightly differently across the work. Confirm fonts, sizes, leading, and tracking match the spec. Style consistency is what makes a designed work feel unified rather than assembled.

### Examine Page Breaks, Line Breaks, And White Space

Where pages and lines break affects readability and professionalism. Bad breaks strand headings, orphan lines at the top of a page or widow lines at the bottom, split tables, and awkward white space that disrupts flow.

Check every page break for problems: headings that appear at the bottom of a page with their text on the next, widows and orphans, paragraphs that begin or end with a single word or line, and excessive or missing white space. Check line breaks within paragraphs for awkward hyphenation, rivers of white space, or lines that end awkwardly. For print, check facing pages for balance. Most layout applications can flag widows and orphans automatically, but visual checking catches the subtler problems of flow and balance that rules miss.

### Verify Headings, Hierarchy, And Navigation Elements

Headings carry the work's structure and are high-visibility formatting. Inconsistent heading levels, missing or duplicate numbers, and broken hierarchy confuse readers about the structure and break navigation, especially in digital formats with generated tables of contents.

Confirm that every heading is assigned the correct level and that the hierarchy is logical, with no level skipped. Check heading numbering for sequence and consistency. Verify that the table of contents, list of figures, and list of tables match the actual headings and numbers in the body, and that page or anchor references are correct. In digital formats, check that navigation, bookmarks, and any generated structure reflect the heading hierarchy. Heading errors propagate into navigation and metadata, so they carry more weight than their length suggests.

### Check Lists, Tables, And Structured Elements

Lists, tables, and other structured elements have formatting rules that break easily during layout and conversion. A numbered list that restarts incorrectly, a table that loses its grid, or a definition list that renders as plain text are common defects.

Verify numbered lists for correct sequence, continuation across pages, and proper nesting. Check bulleted lists for consistent bullet style and indentation. For tables, check that headers repeat on continued pages, that alignment is consistent and appropriate to the data, that gridlines and shading match the spec, and that the table fits without truncation. For other structured elements like definition lists, sidebars, and code blocks, confirm they render in their intended format rather than degrading to body text. Structured elements are where conversion errors concentrate.

### Verify Figures, Captions, And In-Text References

Figures and their captions must be present, correctly placed, legibly rendered, and accurately referenced. Errors here are both visual and substantive, because a mislabeled figure misinforms.

Confirm every figure is present and renders correctly, not pixelated, clipped, or missing. Check that each figure appears near its first reference and that the reference, figure number, and caption all match. Verify captions are styled correctly and that any required credits or source lines are present. Check that figures fit within the page or column without truncation and that text does not overlap them. For charts and diagrams, confirm labels and legends are legible at the final size. A figure that is technically present but unreadable is a formatting failure.

### Check Running Heads, Folios, And Page Furniture

Running heads, footers, page numbers, and other page furniture appear on every page and are therefore high-visibility consistency surfaces. Errors here repeat across the entire work.

Verify running heads and footers against the spec: correct content for each section, correct alternation on recto and verso pages, and correct treatment of front matter, part openers, and blank pages. Check page numbers, folios, for sequence and placement, including roman numerals in front matter and arabic in the body where applicable. Confirm that blank pages are truly blank or carry the correct furniture. Because page furniture repeats, a single error multiplies across the work, making systematic checking essential.

### Verify Special Characters, Equations, And Non-Latin Scripts

Special characters, mathematical equations, and non-Latin scripts are prone to rendering errors, font substitution, and encoding problems. What looks correct in the source may render as boxes, question marks, or wrong glyphs in the final output.

Check every special character, including accented letters, currency symbols, and punctuation, for correct rendering. For equations, verify that symbols, subscripts, superscripts, and special notation render correctly and legibly, and that equations fit within the text or display area. For non-Latin scripts, confirm the correct font is used and that characters render properly rather than substituting. These elements fail silently in conversion, so they require explicit visual checking rather than assumption.

### Check Consistency Across The Whole Work and test Digital Formats For Reflow And Rendering

Individual pages may be correct while the whole is inconsistent. Format checking must verify consistency across the entire work, not just sample pages, because drift accumulates.

Read through the work looking specifically at formatting, scanning for inconsistencies in styles, spacing, alignment, and treatment of recurring elements. Compare early chapters to late ones, because late material often receives less attention. Check that any global changes, like a term replacement or a style update, applied throughout. A work that starts well-formatted and drifts is more damaging than one that is consistently imperfect, because the drift signals declining care.

For digital publication, formatting must survive reflow across devices, screen sizes, and reader settings. Fixed-layout assumptions that work in print break in reflowable formats.

Test digital output on multiple devices and reader apps, and at different font sizes and themes. Check that styles survive reflow, that tables and figures remain legible or degrade gracefully, that hyperlinks and cross-references work, and that navigation functions. Verify that fixed elements like drop caps or sidebars, which may not reflow, are handled per the format's conventions. Digital formatting defects often appear only on specific devices, so testing across a range is necessary, not optional.

## Common Traps

### Treating Format Checking As Design Opinion

Format checking verifies against a spec, not personal taste. Obtain the spec and check against it.

### Style Drift Across The Work

Similar elements formatted differently break the visual system. Verify style application throughout.

### Ignoring Page And Line Breaks

Stranded headings, widows, and orphans disrupt readability. Check every break.

### Broken Heading Hierarchy And Navigation

Heading errors propagate into tables of contents and navigation. Verify hierarchy and references.

### Degraded Structured Elements

Lists, tables, and code blocks that render as plain text are conversion failures. Check each element type.

### Unreadable Figures And Captions

Present but illegible figures fail. Verify rendering, placement, and caption matching.

### Repeated Page Furniture Errors and silent Special-Character And Equation Failures

Running heads and folios repeat on every page, multiplying single errors. Check systematically.

Special characters and equations fail in conversion without obvious signals. Check visually.

### Drift Between Early And Late Material and untested Digital Reflow

Late chapters receive less attention and drift. Compare across the whole work.

Print assumptions break in reflowable formats. Test across devices and settings.

## Self-Check

Before treating typesetting and format checks as complete, verify:

- The work has been checked against the design specification, not against personal aesthetic preference.
- Paragraph and character styles are applied consistently, with no style drift or ad hoc overrides.
- Page and line breaks are clean, with no stranded headings, widows, or orphans, and balanced white space.
- Heading hierarchy is logical, numbering is sequential, and the table of contents and navigation match the body.
- Lists, tables, and structured elements render in their intended format without degrading to plain text.
- Figures are present, legible, correctly placed, and accurately referenced, with captions and credits intact.
- Running heads, footers, folios, and page furniture are correct and consistent throughout, including front matter and blanks.
- Special characters, equations, and non-Latin scripts render correctly without substitution or encoding errors.
- Formatting is consistent across the whole work, with early and late material compared for drift.
- Digital formats have been tested for reflow, rendering, navigation, and links across devices and settings; the work looks and behaves as a unified, professionally designed object on every surface the reader will encounter
