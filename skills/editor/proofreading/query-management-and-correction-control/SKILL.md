# Query Management And Correction Control

Proofreading generates two streams of work, queries for the writer or editor and corrections to be entered, and both must be managed with the same rigor as the reading itself. A query that goes unanswered, a correction that is never entered, a correction entered wrongly, a correction that introduces a new error, a query resolved but never tracked, any of these lets an error reach publication that the proofreader actually caught. The reading finds the problems; the query and correction system ensures they are actually fixed. This system is where proofreading most often fails, not because the proofreader missed the error but because the query or correction was lost, mishandled, or entered in a way that broke something else. Managing queries and corrections is the discipline of capturing every issue, routing it correctly, tracking it to closure, entering fixes carefully, and re-verifying after every change. A proofread with excellent reading but poor correction control publishes errors it had already found.

Use this skill at the proofreading stage, when the task includes managing the query and correction process. It covers deciding what to query versus fix silently, framing queries usefully, tracking queries and corrections to resolution, controlling correction entry, and re-proofing after changes. The editor's task is to ensure every issue found is actually resolved before publication, without introducing new errors in the process.

## Core Rules

### Decide What To Query Versus What To Fix Silently

Not every issue found at proof should be queried. Clear mechanical errors, obvious typos, and style-sheet violations within the proofreader's authority can be fixed silently. Ambiguous, high-stakes, or judgment-call issues, and anything that might change meaning, must be queried. Conflating the two either clutters the writer with trivial queries or silently changes things that should be the writer's call.

Classify each issue. Fix silently only what is unambiguous, low-stakes, and within the proofreader's authority: a clear typo, a style-sheet-consistent change, an obvious formatting fix. Query everything where the right answer depends on the writer's intent, where the stakes are high, where meaning could change, or where the proofreader is unsure. When in doubt, query rather than silently change, because a silent wrong fix is worse than a query. Record the principle of what is silently fixable so the boundary is consistent.

### Frame Queries So They Can Be Resolved Without Back-And-Forth

A query that is vague or lacks context forces the writer to investigate before they can answer, and often generates back-and-forth that delays publication. A well-framed query gives the writer everything needed to decide immediately: location, the issue, the options, and a recommended resolution.

Frame each query with its exact location, a clear statement of the issue, the relevant context or rule, the available options, and a recommended resolution with reasoning. "P. 47, para 2: 'affect' vs 'effect' in context of influence on outcome; recommend 'effect' per meaning; confirm?" is resolvable in seconds. "Check this word" is not. Good query framing respects the writer's time and speeds resolution, and it creates a record that explains the decision if it is later questioned.

### Track Every Query And Correction To Resolution

Every query and every correction must be tracked from identification to resolution, with no item left open. An untracked query is worse than no query, because it creates false confidence that the issue was handled when it was not. The tracking system is what converts finding into fixing.

Maintain a query and correction log with each item's location, the issue, the action required, the owner, and the status, open, resolved, deferred, rejected. Update status as items move through resolution. Before publication, confirm the log is fully closed, with no open items. Deferred or rejected items should be conscious decisions, recorded with reasoning, not items that simply fell through the cracks. A closed log is the evidence that the proofread's findings were actually addressed.

### Control Correction Entry To Prevent Introduced Errors

Corrections are entered by someone, a typesetter, a production editor, the writer, and entry is where new errors are introduced. A correction entered in the wrong place, entered partially, or entered in a way that reflows text can break adjacent material. Correction entry must be controlled and verified, not assumed.

Specify each correction precisely, with exact location and the exact change, so the person entering it cannot misinterpret. After corrections are entered, verify they were entered correctly, not just that work was done. Check that the correction appears as intended and that it did not disrupt adjacent text, references, or formatting. Correction entry is a high-risk transition, and treating it as routine is how introduced errors reach publication.

### Re-Proof After Every Round Of Corrections

Corrections introduce new errors. A typesetter fixing a typo may reflow a paragraph, drop a line, break a cross-reference, or shift pagination. The work is not done when corrections are entered; it is done when the corrected areas have been re-proofed and confirmed clean. Skipping re-proof is the most common path by which a correction-introduced error reaches publication.

After each correction round, re-read the changed areas and their immediate surroundings, since reflow affects nearby text. For high-stakes works, do a final full pass after the last correction. Treat the pre-publication state as suspect until it has been read after the final change. Plan the schedule to allow re-proof rounds, because a deadline that skips re-proof ships errors.

### Manage Query Volume To Avoid Overwhelming The Writer

A proof that generates hundreds of queries can overwhelm the writer and delay resolution, and many queries may be resolvable without the writer's input. Query volume should be managed so the writer sees what genuinely needs their decision.

Consolidate related queries, resolve what can be resolved silently within authority, and prioritize queries by importance so the writer can address the critical ones first. Distinguish must-resolve queries from nice-to-resolve ones. Where query volume is high, consider whether the proof is being over-queried, treating judgment calls as queries that a confident proofreader could resolve. A well-managed query set is focused, resolvable, and respectful of the writer's time.

### Distinguish Proof-Stage Queries From Revision-Stage Feedback

At proof stage, the temptation arises to flag substantive issues discovered during reading. But reopening substantive questions at proof turns the proof into a revision pass, diluting attention from error detection and delaying publication. Proof-stage queries should be confined to errors and necessary corrections, not revision suggestions.

If a substantive issue is noticed at proof, flag it separately and route it to the appropriate editor rather than treating it as a proof query to be resolved before publication. Do not let substantive flags delay the proof's core purpose. The proof's authority is to catch error against the established standard; reopening the standard at this stage undermines the stage's effectiveness. Keep the boundary clear even when tempting issues appear.

### Close The Loop With A Final Verification

Before publication, confirm that the query and correction process is fully closed. Every query resolved or consciously deferred, every correction entered and verified, every changed area re-proofed, and the final state confirmed clean. This closure is the proofread's deliverable, not just the reading.

Conduct a final verification pass: confirm the query log is closed, confirm corrections were entered correctly, confirm re-proofing was done after the last change, and confirm no open items remain. Only this closure converts the proofread's findings into a publication-ready state. A proofread without closure is reading without assurance.

## Common Traps

### Silently Fixing What Should Be Queried

High-stakes or ambiguous changes made silently can introduce errors and override the writer's intent. Query when unsure.

### Vague Queries That Generate Back-And-Forth

Queries without context force investigation. Frame queries to be resolvable immediately.

### Untracked Queries And Corrections

An untracked item creates false confidence and lets errors through. Track every item to closure.

### Assuming Corrections Were Entered Correctly

Correction entry introduces errors. Verify each correction was entered as intended.

### Skipping Re-Proof After Corrections

Corrections introduce new errors. Re-proof changed areas after every correction round.

### Over-Querying And Overwhelming The Writer

Excessive queries delay resolution. Resolve what is within authority and prioritize the rest.

### Reopening Substantive Issues At Proof

Substantive flags turn proof into revision. Route them separately and keep the proof focused on error.

### Declaring Done Without Closure

Reading without closure does not assure fixes. Confirm the query and correction log is fully closed.

## Self-Check

Before treating query management and correction control as complete, verify:

- Each issue has been classified as silently fixable or requiring a query, with the boundary applied consistently.
- Queries are framed with location, issue, context, options, and recommendation so they can be resolved without back-and-forth.
- Every query and correction is tracked in a log from identification to resolution, with status current.
- Corrections were entered correctly, verified against the intended change, not assumed done.
- Changed areas and surroundings have been re-proofed after every correction round, with a final pass for high-stakes works.
- Query volume has been managed to avoid overwhelming the writer, with consolidation and prioritization.
- Substantive issues noticed at proof have been routed separately rather than reopening revision at proof stage.
- A final verification confirms the query log is closed, corrections verified, and re-proofing complete.
- Every issue the proofread found has been actually resolved before publication, without introduced errors.
