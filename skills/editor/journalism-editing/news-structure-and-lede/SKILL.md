# News Structure And Lede

News writing is structured for how people actually read news: fast, partially, and with decreasing attention. The lede must deliver the news in a sentence or two; the structure that follows must let readers grasp the essentials quickly and continue for detail if they choose. This is the inverted pyramid and its relatives, designed so that the most important information comes first and the least important last, allowing readers to stop at any point and still have the core. The journalism editor's structural work is to ensure the lede captures the real news, the nut graf states the stakes and context, quotes and evidence support the claims, and the structure serves the reader rather than the writer's process. A news story that buries its news or wanders through chronology loses readers who never find what mattered.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Real News And Lead With It

The lede must convey the news: what happened, what changed, what matters. The editor's first task is to identify the real news of the story, which is often not what the writer led with. Writers sometimes lead with atmosphere, a scene, a tangent, or the most recent development rather than the most important one. The editor asks what the reader most needs to know and confirms the lede delivers it. A lede that withholds the news to build suspense may work in features but fails in straight news, where readers want the point immediately. Flag ledes that bury the news and restructure to lead with it.

### Write A Lede That Is Accurate, Specific, And Compact

The lede compresses the story into a sentence or two. It must be accurate, containing only verified information; specific, naming who, what, when, and where concretely rather than vaguely; and compact, carrying maximum information in minimum words. The editor scrutinizes the lede for unsupported claims, vague language, and unnecessary words. "Officials announced a new policy today" is weaker than "The city council voted 7-2 Tuesday to require masks in all indoor public spaces starting Friday." Specificity and concreteness make the lede credible and useful.

### Place The Nut Graf To Establish Stakes And Context

After the lede, the nut graf explains why the story matters: the context, the stakes, the significance. Not every story needs a formal nut graf, but every story must answer the reader's question of "so what" early. The editor checks that context and significance appear high in the story, not buried in the tenth paragraph. The nut graf positions the news within ongoing events, explains who is affected, and signals what makes this story worth reading. Flag stories that delay their significance and bring the nut graf forward.

### Order Information By Importance, Not Chronology

News structure ranks information by importance to the reader, not by the order it happened. The editor evaluates whether each paragraph earns its position by delivering information the reader needs. Chronological structure, common in amateur writing, forces the reader to wait for the news. Inverted pyramid and related structures front-load essentials. Flag sections ordered by convenience or chronology and restructure by importance, moving the most reader-relevant material up.

### Integrate Quotes To Support, Not Replace, Narrative

Quotes add voice, authority, and human presence, but they must support the narrative, not carry it. The editor checks that quotes are relevant, attributed, and positioned where they add value, after the claim they support or the point they illustrate. A story that strings quotes together without narrative connective tissue reads as a transcript. Flag quote-heavy sections and ensure each quote is introduced, contextualized, and connected to the story's thread. Avoid "quote stacks" where multiple sources are quoted in succession without narrative.

### Attribute Carefully And Consistently

Attribution establishes credibility and lets readers judge sources. The editor checks that claims are attributed to named sources where possible, that attribution is consistent in style, and that anonymous sources are used sparingly with explanation. "According to police records" differs from "police said" differs from "according to sources familiar with the matter." Each carries different weight. Flag unsupported assertions, vague attribution, and over-reliance on anonymity. Ensure direct quotes are exact and paraphrases are faithful.

### Provide Background Without Burying The News

Background and context are necessary but must not overwhelm the news. The editor ensures background appears where needed, high enough to explain significance but not so heavy that it delays the news. A common failure is the long background paragraph that interrupts the news flow. Keep background tight, relevant, and positioned to serve the reader who needs it without burdening the reader who does not. Link or sidebar background for readers who want depth.

### Match Structure To Story Type

Different story types call for different structures. Breaking news uses a tight inverted pyramid; features may use narrative or anecdotal ledes with the nut graf later; analysis frames the news with interpretation; profiles center on character. The editor identifies the story type and confirms the structure serves it. A breaking news story told as a feature delays the news; a feature told as inverted pyramid kills the narrative. Flag structure-story mismatches and adjust.

## Common Traps

### The Buried Lede

The most common news editing failure is a lede that does not contain the news. Writers may lead with a scene, a quote, the latest development, or a tangential detail, leaving the actual news in paragraph four. The editor identifies the real news and moves it to the lede, repositioning the original opening as context or scene-setting later. The buried lede loses readers who never reach the news.

### The Vague Or Generic Lede

Ledes like "A local event drew attention this week" or "Officials addressed concerns at a recent meeting" convey almost nothing. The editor flags generic ledes and asks for the specific who, what, when, and where. Concrete detail makes the lede credible and useful; vagueness makes it skippable.

### Chronology Replacing Importance

Writers often narrate events in the order they happened, which is natural for the writer but wrong for the reader. The editor restructures from chronology to importance, leading with the outcome or the news and weaving in chronology where it serves understanding. The reader cares what happened, not the order the writer learned it.

### Quote Stacking

Stringing quotes from multiple sources without narrative connective tissue produces a transcript-like passage that loses readers. The editor breaks quote stacks by introducing, contextualizing, and connecting each quote to the story's thread. Quotes should punctuate the narrative, not replace it.

### Missing Or Late Nut Graf

Without an early statement of significance, readers do not know why the story matters. The editor ensures the "so what" appears high, within the first few paragraphs for most news stories. A nut graf buried in paragraph eight loses readers who quit before reaching it.

### Unsupported Or Vague Attribution

Claims presented without attribution, or with vague attribution like "sources say," reduce credibility. The editor flags unattributed assertions and asks for the source. Where anonymity is necessary, explain why and characterize the source's position. Consistency in attribution style also matters; mixing "said," "stated," "noted," and "explained" draws attention to the verb rather than the content.

### Over-Backgrounding Early

Too much background too early delays the news and taxes the reader. The editor moves heavy background lower or condenses it, keeping the opening focused on news and significance. Background serves the news; it does not precede it.

### Structure Mismatched To Story Type

A breaking news story with a narrative lede delays the news; a feature with a hard inverted-pyramid lede kills the texture. The editor matches structure to story type, adjusting the lede style, nut graf placement, and overall shape to what the story demands.

## Self-Check

- Does the lede convey the real news of the story, with the most important information first?
- Is the lede accurate, specific, and compact, naming who, what, when, and where concretely?
- Does the story establish its significance and context early, through a nut graf or equivalent, answering the reader's "so what"?
- Is information ordered by importance to the reader rather than by chronology or the writer's process?
- Do quotes support and punctuate the narrative rather than replace it, with each quote introduced and contextualized?
- Is attribution careful, consistent, and credible, with anonymity used sparingly and explained?
- Does background appear where needed without burying or overwhelming the news?
- Is the structure matched to the story type, whether breaking news, feature, analysis, or profile?
- Have vague or generic ledes been replaced with specific, concrete openings?; have quote stacks been broken with narrative connective tissue?
- Has heavy background been moved lower or condensed so the opening stays focused on news?; could a reader who reads only the first two or three paragraphs grasp the essential news and its significance?
