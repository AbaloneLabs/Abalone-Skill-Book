# Balance And Fairness

Balance and fairness are core obligations of journalism and nonfiction that claims to inform. Balance does not mean giving equal weight to every view regardless of merit; it means representing the relevant perspectives honestly, in proportion to their evidence and significance, without stacking the story to favor a predetermined conclusion. Fairness means treating subjects and sources with respect, presenting their views accurately, giving them opportunity to respond, and avoiding framing that distorts. The editor's task is to read for imbalance, loaded language, selective sourcing, false equivalence, and framing that betrays the standards of honest reporting. This work matters because biased or unfair reporting misleads readers, damages subjects, and erodes the credibility that journalism depends on.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Balance From False Equivalence

Balance is not mechanically giving equal space to every side. When evidence strongly supports one position, giving equal weight to an unsupported position creates false equivalence that misleads readers. The editor judges weight by evidence and significance: a story about climate change need not give equal space to denial to be balanced. Balance means representing the relevant, evidence-backed perspectives proportionally. Flag false equivalence where unsupported views are elevated to match well-supported ones, and flag imbalance where significant, relevant perspectives are omitted.

### Represent Sources Fairly And In Context

Quotes and paraphrases must represent what sources actually said, in context, without distortion. The editor checks that quotes are accurate, complete enough to preserve meaning, and not cherry-picked to mislead. A quote truncated to reverse the source's intent is a serious failure. Paraphrases must be faithful, not slanted. The editor also checks that sources are characterized fairly: their affiliations, expertise, and potential biases stated where relevant, so readers can weigh their words. Flag misrepresentation, selective quoting, and unfair characterization.

### Give Affected Parties Opportunity To Respond

Fairness requires that subjects of criticism or accusation have the opportunity to respond before publication. The editor confirms that the story sought and included responses from key parties, or notes when they declined or did not respond. Running serious allegations without seeking response denies the subject basic fairness and exposes the publication to correction and credibility loss. Flag stories that make allegations without documented attempts to contact the affected parties.

### Examine Framing For Built-In Bias

Framing, how the story is set up, what it takes as given, and how it poses the question, can embed bias without any false statement. The editor reads for framing that loads the story: defining one side's position as mainstream and the other as extreme without evidence, treating one outcome as normal and another as deviant, or posing questions that presuppose a conclusion. Flag loaded framing and ask whether a more neutral frame would change the story's thrust. Honest framing presents the dispute without prejudging it.

### Watch For Loaded And Slanted Language

Word choice signals stance. "Admitted" implies guilt; "said" is neutral. "Claimed" signals doubt; "stated" is neutral. "Regime" signals illegitimacy; "government" is neutral. The editor scans for loaded verbs, adjectives, and labels that favor one side, and replaces them with neutral language unless the loaded term is accurate and justified. Consistency matters: if one side's actions are described neutrally and the other's with loaded terms, the imbalance signals bias even unconsciously.

### Evaluate Source Diversity And Over-Reliance

Balance depends in part on the range of sources. The editor examines the source list: does it represent the relevant perspectives, or does it skew toward one viewpoint, institution, or demographic? Over-reliance on official sources, on one side's spokespeople, or on sources sharing the reporter's assumptions produces imbalance even without intent. Flag source lists that lack diversity of perspective and ask whose voices are missing. Anonymous sources deserve special scrutiny, as they cannot be evaluated by readers and can skew a story invisibly.

### Separate Fact From Analysis And Opinion

Readers deserve to know what is established fact, what is source interpretation, and what is the reporter's or outlet's opinion. The editor checks that these layers are distinguished clearly. Analysis and opinion are legitimate, but presented as fact they mislead. Flag passages where interpretation or opinion is stated as established fact, and ensure the story signals what is verified, what is asserted by sources, and what is the journalist's assessment.

### Apply The Same Scrutiny To All Sides

Fairness means holding all sides to the same standard. The editor checks that the reporter scrutinized claims from every source with equal rigor, sought verification for every significant assertion regardless of which side made it, and applied consistent standards for inclusion and skepticism. Scrutinizing one side's claims while accepting another's uncritically produces imbalance. Flag asymmetric scrutiny and ask for consistent treatment.

## Common Traps

### False Equivalence

Giving unsupported views equal weight with well-supported ones, in the name of balance, misleads readers into thinking the evidence is evenly divided. The editor flags false equivalence and recommends proportional treatment based on evidence and significance.

### Cherry-Picked Or Out-Of-Context Quotes

Selecting quotes to support a predetermined angle, or truncating quotes to reverse meaning, distorts the source's position. The editor checks quotes against context where possible and flags selective or misleading quotation. Quotes must represent what the source said, not what the story wishes they had said.

### Loaded Language Favoring One Side

Consistent use of neutral terms for one side and loaded terms for the other signals bias. The editor scans for asymmetry in labeling, verbs, and adjectives and standardizes toward neutrality unless loaded terms are accurate and applied consistently to all sides.

### Missing Perspectives And Sources

Stories built from a narrow source base, official voices only, one demographic, or one institutional perspective, miss relevant views. The editor identifies whose voices are absent and asks whether their inclusion would change the story. Balance requires seeking the range of relevant perspectives.

### Allegations Without Response

Publishing accusations or criticism without seeking the subject's response denies fairness and invites correction. The editor confirms that affected parties were contacted and their responses, or refusals, documented. Failure to seek response is a procedural as well as ethical failure.

### Opinion Stated As Fact

Passages where the reporter's interpretation or a source's assertion is presented as established fact blur the line between reporting and advocacy. The editor distinguishes the layers and ensures the story signals what is verified, what is claimed, and what is assessed.

### Framing That Predetermines The Conclusion

When the story's setup, definitions, or question-posing embed a conclusion, the reporting that follows cannot be fair regardless of its content. The editor examines framing for built-in bias and asks whether a neutral frame would change the story.

### Asymmetric Skepticism

Applying rigorous scrutiny to one side's claims while accepting another's at face value produces imbalance. The editor checks that verification standards are consistent across all sources and flags asymmetric treatment.

## Self-Check

- Does the story represent relevant, evidence-backed perspectives in proportion to their significance, avoiding false equivalence?
- Are quotes accurate, complete enough to preserve meaning, in context, and free of selective truncation?
- Are sources characterized fairly, with affiliations, expertise, and potential biases stated where relevant?
- Have affected parties, especially subjects of criticism or accusation, been given opportunity to respond, with their responses or refusals documented?
- Is the framing neutral, taking no side as given and not prejudging the conclusion?
- Has loaded or slanted language been identified and replaced with neutral terms, applied consistently to all sides?
- Does the source list represent the relevant range of perspectives, with no significant viewpoint missing and anonymous sources scrutinized?
- Are fact, source interpretation, and reporter opinion clearly distinguished, with no interpretation or opinion presented as established fact?
- Has the same standard of scrutiny and verification been applied to claims from every side?; have perspectives from outside official or dominant institutional sources been sought where relevant?
- Has the story been read specifically for imbalance, considering whose interests it serves and whose it may harm?; would a reader holding each major perspective recognize their view represented fairly and accurately?
