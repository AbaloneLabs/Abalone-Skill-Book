# Research Design Soundness

Research design is the architecture of a study. It determines what questions can be answered, what evidence is gathered, and what conclusions are warranted. A sound design aligns the methodology with the research question, uses an approach appropriate to the claims, and justifies each design choice. A flawed design produces results that cannot support the conclusions drawn from them, no matter how carefully the analysis is executed. Editors who focus on the writing without examining the design miss flaws that invalidate the work's contribution. Research design soundness is the discipline of evaluating whether the study's architecture can bear the weight of its claims.

Use this skill when editing research manuscripts, grant proposals, or reports that present original research, to evaluate whether the design is sound. It covers alignment of method to question, appropriateness of approach, and justification of choices. The goal is a manuscript whose design is coherent, appropriate, and defensible.

## Core Rules

### Verify Method-Question Alignment

The foundation of sound design is that the methodology can actually answer the research question. A qualitative question requires qualitative methods; a causal claim requires a design that can establish causation; a question about prevalence requires representative sampling. Mismatch between method and question is a fundamental design flaw. Verify alignment.

Identify the research question precisely. Determine what kind of evidence the question demands. Assess whether the chosen methodology can produce that evidence. A survey cannot establish causation; a case study cannot establish general prevalence; a cross-sectional design cannot establish change over time. Flag mismatches between what the question asks and what the method can deliver. The strongest manuscripts make the method-question alignment explicit, showing why this approach is suited to this question.

### Assess Approach Appropriateness For Claims

Beyond the broad question, assess whether the approach is appropriate for the specific claims the authors make. Claims vary in strength, and the approach must be adequate to the strongest claim. A design that supports a modest claim may not support a bolder one. Assess approach-claim appropriateness.

List the claims made in the manuscript, distinguishing primary from secondary and strong from modest. For each claim, assess whether the approach provides adequate support. A claim of causation requires controls for confounds; a claim of generalizability requires representative sampling; a claim of effect size requires adequate power. Flag claims that outrun the evidence the approach can provide. Authors sometimes overstate what their design supports; the editor's role is to notice the gap between claim and approach.

### Check Design Justification

Sound designs justify their choices. Why this sample size, this comparison group, this measurement instrument, this analytic technique? Unjustified choices suggest arbitrariness, while justified choices demonstrate deliberate design. Check that key design decisions are explained.

Review the methodology for justification of major choices. For each significant decision, verify the authors explain why this choice was made. Sample size should be justified, often by power analysis. Comparison groups should be justified as appropriate controls. Measurement instruments should be justified as valid for the construct. Analytic techniques should be justified as appropriate to the data and question. Flag design choices presented without rationale, which may reflect convention, convenience, or oversight. Justification demonstrates the design is reasoned, not arbitrary.

### Evaluate Internal Validity Threats

Internal validity concerns whether the design supports causal inference within the study. Threats include confounding variables, selection bias, history effects, maturation, testing effects, and instrumentation changes. Sound designs anticipate and address these threats. Evaluate internal validity.

Identify the threats to internal validity that the design faces. For experimental designs, check for adequate randomization, control groups, and control of confounds. For quasi-experimental and observational designs, check for efforts to address selection bias and confounding. For longitudinal designs, check for attrition and maturation effects. Verify the authors acknowledge threats they cannot eliminate and discuss their implications. Designs that ignore internal validity threats produce results vulnerable to alternative explanations.

### Evaluate External Validity And Generalizability

External validity concerns whether findings generalize beyond the study's specific conditions. Threats include unrepresentative samples, artificial settings, and context-specific effects. Sound designs consider generalizability and authors calibrate their claims accordingly. Evaluate external validity.

Assess the sample's representativeness relative to the population of interest. Consider whether the setting, whether a lab, clinic, field, or online, affects generalizability. Evaluate whether the conditions of the study, such as the time frame or measurement methods, limit generalization. Verify the authors do not overclaim generalizability their design cannot support. A convenience sample of undergraduates does not support claims about the general population; a single-site study does not support claims about other settings. Authors should acknowledge generalizability limits honestly.

### Verify Measurement Validity And Reliability

The measurements a study uses must be valid, measuring what they claim to measure, and reliable, producing consistent results. Flawed measurements undermine any conclusions drawn from them. Verify measurement quality.

For each key measure, assess whether validity and reliability are established. For established instruments, verify the authors cite evidence of validity and reliability and use the instrument appropriately. For new or adapted measures, verify the authors provide evidence of their psychometric properties. Check that the measurement approach captures the intended construct and not a confounded one. Self-report measures, in particular, are vulnerable to social desirability and recall bias; verify these are acknowledged. Measurement flaws are often invisible in polished writing but invalidate results.

### Check For Design Transparency

Sound designs are transparently reported. Readers should be able to understand what was done, why, and how decisions were made. Opaque reporting obscures design soundness and prevents evaluation. Check transparency.

Verify the methodology reports the design comprehensively. Check that the sample, procedures, measures, and analysis are described in enough detail to evaluate and replicate. Verify that any deviations from the planned design are reported and explained. Check that the design is pre-registered if that is the field norm, and that the report matches the pre-registration. Transparency allows readers to assess soundness; opacity prevents it and may mask problems.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Good Writing Means Good Design

Clear prose can describe a flawed design. Evaluate the design itself.

### Method-Question Mismatch

The method must fit the question. Verify alignment.

### Claims Outrunning The Design

Strong claims need strong designs. Flag overstated conclusions.

### Unjustified Design Choices

Major decisions need rationale. Check for justification.

### Ignoring Validity Threats

Internal and external validity threats must be acknowledged and addressed.

### Weak Measurements

Validity and reliability matter. Do not assume measures capture the construct.

### Opaque Reporting

Transparency enables evaluation. Report the design comprehensively.

## Self-Check

Before treating research design soundness as adequately evaluated, verify:

- The research question has been identified and the methodology's ability to answer it assessed.
- The approach is appropriate for the specific claims, especially the strongest claims.
- Major design choices, such as sample size and comparison groups, are justified.
- Threats to internal validity, such as confounds and selection bias, are identified and addressed.
- Limits to external validity and generalizability are acknowledged honestly.
- Measurement instruments are valid and reliable, with evidence cited for established measures.
- New or adapted measures have supporting evidence for their properties.
- The methodology is reported transparently enough to evaluate and replicate.
- Any deviations from the planned design are reported and explained.
- The design is coherent, appropriate, and defensible against expert scrutiny.
