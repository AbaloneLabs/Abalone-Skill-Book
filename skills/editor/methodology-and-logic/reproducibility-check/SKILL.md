# Reproducibility Check

Reproducibility is the ability of independent researchers to verify or recreate a study's findings using the reported methods and available materials. A reproducible study reports methods in enough detail, shares data and code where possible, and provides the information needed to check the work. An irreproducible study, however well-written, leaves findings that cannot be verified, which undermines their credibility and cumulative value. Reproducibility checking is the discipline of evaluating whether a manuscript provides what independent verification requires. Editors who treat methods as a narrative to be polished rather than a protocol to be complete miss gaps that prevent reproduction.

Use this skill when editing research manuscripts to evaluate reproducibility. It covers methodological detail, data and code availability, and the information needed for independent verification. The goal is a manuscript whose findings could, in principle, be checked or recreated by another competent researcher.

## Core Rules

### Verify Methods Are Reported In Replicable Detail

The foundation of reproducibility is that the methods section provides enough detail for another researcher to repeat the study. Vague or incomplete methods prevent reproduction. Verify the level of detail.

Read the methods section as if attempting to replicate the study. Can you identify the sample, how they were recruited, what they did, what was measured, and how? Are specific instruments named with citations or described in full? Are procedures described step by step? Are analysis steps specified, including software, parameters, and decision rules? Flag any point where a replicator would have to guess. Methods that say "standard procedures were followed" or "data were analyzed appropriately" are not reproducible. The standard is that a competent researcher could repeat the work from the report alone, supplemented by cited materials.

### Check Data Availability And Accessibility

Reproducible research makes data available. Data availability allows others to check analyses, reanalyze with different methods, and build on the work. Check whether data are available and accessible.

Verify the manuscript states where the data are located. Are they in a public repository with an accession number or DOI? Are they in supplementary materials? Are they available on request, and if so, are there restrictions? Flag manuscripts that do not state data availability. If data cannot be shared due to privacy, confidentiality, or proprietary concerns, verify the manuscript explains this and describes what can be shared or under what conditions. Data that exist only on the authors' hard drives, unavailable to anyone, prevent reproduction. Note that some fields have stronger data-sharing norms than others; calibrate expectations to the field.

### Check Code And Materials Availability

Beyond data, reproducible research often depends on code, analysis scripts, and specialized materials. Computational analyses, simulations, and models require code to reproduce. Experiments may require specific materials, stimuli, or apparatus. Check availability.

For computational work, verify that code or analysis scripts are available, whether in a repository, supplementary materials, or on request. Check that the software environment, including versions and dependencies, is specified enough to recreate. For experimental work, verify that specialized materials, such as stimuli, questionnaires, or apparatus designs, are described or available. Flag computational results presented without the code that generated them, which cannot be independently verified. The availability of code and materials is increasingly expected, especially in computational fields.

### Verify Analysis Transparency

Reproducibility requires that analyses are transparent enough to repeat. This includes specifying which analyses were planned, which were exploratory, how multiple comparisons were handled, and how decisions were made during analysis. Verify transparency.

Check that the manuscript distinguishes confirmatory from exploratory analyses. Verify that the analysis plan is described, whether through pre-registration, a methods section, or a protocol. Flag analyses that appear to have been selected from many run, without correction for multiple comparisons or acknowledgment of exploratory status. Check that analytic decisions, such as exclusion criteria or outlier handling, are specified and justified. Analyses that are opaque about how many were run and which are reported prevent evaluation of the findings' robustness. Transparency about the analysis process is essential to reproduction.

### Check For Pre-Registration And Protocol Alignment

Pre-registration, where the study design and analysis plan are registered before data collection, strengthens reproducibility by distinguishing planned from post-hoc analyses. Where pre-registration is the norm, verify alignment. Check pre-registration.

If the field uses pre-registration, verify the manuscript references the pre-registration and that the report matches it. Flag deviations from the pre-registered plan, and verify they are acknowledged and explained. Check that analyses not in the pre-registration are identified as exploratory. For clinical trials, verify the trial registration number and that the report matches the registered protocol. Misalignment between pre-registration and report undermines reproducibility and credibility, because it suggests the analysis plan was changed after seeing results.

### Verify Results Can Be Independently Checked

Reproducibility includes the ability to check specific results, not just repeat the whole study. This means providing the numbers, statistics, and materials needed for verification. Verify checkability.

Check that the report provides enough numerical detail to verify key statistics. Are exact values, degrees of freedom, and confidence intervals reported, not just significance flags? Are effect sizes reported? For meta-analytic or systematic review work, are the extracted data available? Flag results presented only as significant or non-significant without the underlying values, which cannot be checked. The goal is that a reader with the data and code could reproduce the specific numbers in the results section.

### Assess Field-Specific Reproducibility Norms

Reproducibility expectations vary by field. Computational sciences may expect full code and data sharing. Qualitative fields may expect detailed methodological transparency and raw materials. Clinical fields expect trial registration and protocol adherence. Assess against field norms.

Calibrate reproducibility expectations to the discipline. Verify the manuscript meets the field's specific norms for data sharing, code availability, pre-registration, and methodological detail. Do not impose norms from a different field, but do flag failures to meet the field's own standards. Note where the field is evolving toward stronger reproducibility and the manuscript could lead by exceeding current norms.

## Common Traps

### Vague Methods That Prevent Replication

Methods must be detailed enough to repeat. Flag points where a replicator would guess.

### Unstated Data Availability

State where data are and any restrictions. Do not leave data unavailable.

### Computational Results Without Code

Code generates results. Make code available for verification.

### Opaque Analysis Processes

Specify planned versus exploratory analyses and decision rules. Enable evaluation of robustness.

### Pre-Registration Misalignment

The report should match the pre-registration. Flag and explain deviations.

### Insufficient Numerical Detail

Report exact values and statistics, not just significance. Enable checking.

### Imposing Wrong Field Norms

Calibrate reproducibility expectations to the discipline's own standards.

## Self-Check

Before treating reproducibility as adequately supported, verify:

- The methods section provides enough detail for a competent researcher to replicate the study.
- Specific instruments, procedures, parameters, and analysis steps are reported, not vaguely referenced.
- The manuscript states where data are located and any access restrictions.
- Code or analysis scripts are available for computational work, with environment specified.
- Specialized materials, stimuli, or apparatus are described or made available.
- Analyses are transparent about which were planned, which exploratory, and how decisions were made.
- Where pre-registration is the norm, the report matches the pre-registration and deviations are explained.
- Results provide enough numerical detail, including exact values and confidence intervals, to be checked.
- The manuscript meets the field's specific reproducibility norms and standards.
- An independent researcher could, in principle, verify or recreate the findings from the report and available materials.
