# Sensitive Content And Harm

Editing involves judgment about harm, not only about quality. Many important works deal with violence, trauma, suicide, self-harm, abuse, hate, graphic imagery, and other content that can cause real distress to readers, endanger vulnerable subjects, or cause broader social harm. The editorial task is not to avoid all difficult material, which would suppress important truth, but to handle it responsibly: deciding what must be published, what should be contextualized, what warrants warnings, what endangers subjects, and what crosses from difficult into gratuitous or harmful. This is judgment under genuine tension, where both over-caution and under-caution cause harm.

Use this skill when reviewing any work that depicts or addresses sensitive, graphic, or potentially harmful content. It covers assessing harm to readers and subjects, applying content warnings appropriately, handling depictions of suicide and self-harm, protecting vulnerable and identified subjects, balancing public interest against harm, and deciding what to publish, contextualize, or cut. The editor's task is to minimize harm while preserving the work's honest purpose.

## Core Rules

### Assess Harm To Readers, Subjects, And The Public

Harm from published content falls on different parties, and each requires consideration. Reader harm is distress or re-traumatization from encountering graphic or triggering material. Subject harm is danger, stigma, or retaliation to people depicted or identifiable. Public harm is the work's contribution to harmful norms, misinformation, or contagion. These are distinct and may pull in different directions.

For each sensitive element, assess the harm across all three parties. Who reads this, and could it cause distress, particularly to those with relevant trauma? Who is depicted or identifiable, and could publication endanger, stigmatize, or expose them? What is the broader effect, could the work normalize harm, spread dangerous methods, or fuel misinformation? A full harm assessment considers all affected parties, not only the reader, and recognizes that minimizing one harm may increase another.

### Weigh Public Interest Against Potential Harm

The core tension of sensitive content is that important truth often carries harm, and suppressing it is itself a harm. The editorial judgment is whether the public interest justifies the potential harm, and how to publish in a way that serves the interest while reducing the harm.

For each sensitive element, identify the public interest it serves: informing about an important issue, exposing wrongdoing, preserving historical truth, serving affected communities. Weigh that interest against the assessed harm. Where interest clearly justifies the harm, publish, but mitigate where possible. Where harm exceeds interest, cut, contextualize, or withhold. Where the balance is close, seek the version that maximizes interest and minimizes harm, which may mean publishing the information without the most graphic detail, or with context that reduces misunderstanding. This is not censorship; it is responsible publication.

### Apply Content Warnings Where They Genuinely Help

Content warnings give readers information to make informed choices about material that may distress them. Used well, they protect vulnerable readers without deterring others or spoiling necessary effect. Used poorly, they become vague, over-applied, or performative.

Apply warnings for content that is genuinely likely to cause distress to relevant readers: graphic violence, sexual assault, suicide or self-harm, abuse, hate speech depicted in the work, and other recognized triggers. Be specific enough to be useful, naming the category of concern, without being so graphic that the warning itself harms. Place warnings where readers encounter them before the material. Avoid warning for everything, which dilutes usefulness and can read as performative, and avoid warning so vaguely that readers cannot judge their own risk. The goal is informed reader choice, not blanket protection.

### Handle Suicide And Self-Harm Depiction With Care

Depictions of suicide and self-harm carry a specific, evidence-based risk of contagion, where media portrayals can increase suicidal behavior among vulnerable readers. This is one of the most consequential harm areas, and established guidelines exist.

Follow recognized guidelines for responsible portrayal: avoid detailed description of means and methods, which is the strongest contagion risk; avoid romanticizing or sensationalizing; avoid presenting suicide as a rational or inevitable response to circumstances; and include help resources where appropriate. These guidelines apply to journalism, fiction, and any depiction. The evidence on contagion is strong, and ignoring it can cause real deaths. Responsible handling does not mean avoiding the topic, which also causes harm through silence, but portraying it in ways that minimize contagion risk.

### Protect Vulnerable And Identified Subjects

People depicted in or identifiable from sensitive content may face harm from publication: danger from abusers, stigma in their communities, retaliation, legal consequences, or re-traumatization. Protecting them is an editorial obligation, especially where they did not consent to the exposure.

Assess who is identifiable and what harm they face. For vulnerable subjects, including minors, survivors, people in unsafe situations, and members of stigmatized groups, apply extra protection. Consider anonymization, detail reduction, consent verification, and whether identification serves the public interest or is incidental. Distinguish public figures, where greater exposure is expected, from private individuals, where protection is stronger. Where a subject's identification is necessary but harmful, weigh whether the interest justifies the harm and provide the subject every reasonable protection short of defeating the work's purpose.

### Avoid Gratuitous Detail And Sensationalism

Difficult material that serves the work's purpose differs from graphic detail included for shock, engagement, or sensation. The latter causes harm without corresponding value and should be cut or reduced.

For each graphic element, ask what it contributes. Does the detail inform, bear witness, convey necessary reality, or serve an artistic purpose? Or does it shock, titillate, or dramatize without adding understanding? Cut or reduce detail that does not serve a clear purpose, even in work that legitimately addresses difficult topics. Sensationalism in headlines, images, and framing carries the same scrutiny: if the framing amplifies harm for attention without serving understanding, revise it. Important work can address difficult content; gratuitous detail and sensational framing cannot be justified by the importance of the topic.

### Contextualize To Prevent Misreading

Sensitive content presented without context can be misread in harmful ways: a depiction may appear to endorse what it critiques, statistics may be misinterpreted, and isolated facts may fuel prejudice. Contextualization reduces harm by guiding responsible interpretation.

Provide context that helps readers understand the content as intended: framing that clarifies the work's stance, caveats that prevent overgeneralization, background that situates graphic material, and balance that prevents a single case from implying a broader pattern. Contextualization is especially important for content touching on identity groups, where isolated depiction can reinforce stereotypes. The goal is not to tell readers what to think but to provide the frame they need to read responsibly, reducing harm from misinterpretation.

### Coordinate With Affected Communities And Experts

For content about communities or experiences the editor does not share, consultation reduces harm and improves accuracy. Lived experience catches harms that editorial judgment alone may miss.

Where content depicts a marginalized community, a trauma the editor has not experienced, or a technical harm area like suicide contagion, consult affected communities, sensitivity readers, or subject experts. Listen to concerns about depiction, language, framing, and potential consequences. Weigh consultation input alongside other editorial considerations, recognizing that no community is monolithic, but take harm signals seriously. Consultation is not about obtaining permission but about reducing harm the editor is not positioned to see.

### Document Harm-Related Decisions

Decisions to publish, cut, contextualize, or warn on sensitive content should be documented so they can be explained and reviewed. If a decision is challenged, the reasoning should be reconstructable.

Record what sensitive content was considered, what harms were assessed, what public interest justified publication, what mitigations were applied, and what consultation occurred. For significant decisions, note who was involved and what alternatives were weighed. Documentation supports consistency across the publication, enables review of decisions, and provides accountability. Opaque handling of harm decisions is itself a problem, because it prevents learning and accountability when harm does occur.

## Common Traps

### Avoiding Difficult Material Entirely

Suppressing important truth is itself a harm. Weigh interest against harm rather than defaulting to avoidance.

### Ignoring Subject And Public Harm

Harm falls on readers, subjects, and the public. Assess all affected parties, not only reader distress.

### Vague Or Over-Applied Warnings

Warnings must be specific and useful to inform reader choice, not blanket or performative.

### Reckless Suicide And Self-Harm Depiction

Detailed means and sensationalizing portrayal carry contagion risk. Follow established guidelines.

### Exposing Vulnerable Subjects

Private individuals and vulnerable groups face harm from identification. Apply extra protection.

### Gratuitous Detail And Sensational Framing

Graphic content without purpose causes harm without value. Cut detail and revise framing that serves shock over understanding.

### Decontextualized Sensitive Content and editorial Judgment Without Consultation

Isolated depiction can mislead and reinforce stereotypes. Provide context for responsible reading.

Editors miss harms outside their experience. Consult affected communities and experts.

### Undocumented Harm Decisions and confusing Harm Minimization With Censorship

Opaque handling prevents accountability and learning. Document assessments, mitigations, and reasoning.

Responsible handling serves the work's purpose while reducing harm; it is not suppression of important information.

## Self-Check

Before treating sensitive content and harm review as complete, verify:

- Harm has been assessed across readers, subjects, and the public, not only one party.
- Public interest has been weighed against potential harm for each sensitive element.
- Content warnings are specific, useful, and placed where readers encounter them before the material.
- Suicide and self-harm depictions follow contagion guidelines, avoiding detailed means and sensationalizing.
- Vulnerable and identified subjects have been assessed for harm, with protection applied where needed.
- Gratuitous detail and sensational framing have been cut or reduced.
- Sensitive content is contextualized to prevent misreading and stereotyping.
- Affected communities or experts have been consulted for content outside the editor's experience.
- Harm-related decisions have been documented with reasoning and mitigations.
- The work serves its honest purpose while minimizing harm, neither suppressing important truth nor publishing recklessly.
