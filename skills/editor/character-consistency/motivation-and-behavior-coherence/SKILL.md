# Motivation And Behavior Coherence

Characters are the heart of fiction, and their believability depends on coherence between what they want and what they do. A character whose motivations are clear, whose behaviors follow from those motivations, and whose actions align with their established desires and values feels real. A character who acts without understandable reason, or whose behavior contradicts who they have been established to be, feels like a puppet moved by plot convenience. Motivation and behavior coherence is the discipline of verifying that characters act from within rather than from without. Editors who follow plot without tracking character logic miss the inconsistencies that break belief.

Use this skill when editing fiction to verify motivation and behavior coherence. It covers clarity of motivation, behavior-motivation alignment, and detection of contradictory or inexplicable action. The goal is characters whose actions readers can understand and believe.

## Core Rules

### Identify Each Character's Core Motivations

Coherence begins with knowing what drives each character. A character's core motivations, what they want, fear, value, and avoid, should be identifiable and provide the basis for their actions. Identify motivations.

For each significant character, identify their core motivations. What do they want, both overtly and beneath the surface? What do they fear or avoid? What do they value? These motivations may be explicit, stated by the character, or implicit, revealed through action. They may evolve through the story, but at any point, the character should have comprehensible drivers. Flag characters whose motivations are unclear, who seem to act without desire or fear, or whose motivations are asserted but not evidenced. Clear motivations are the foundation of coherent behavior.

### Verify Behaviors Follow From Motivations

Once motivations are identified, verify that the character's behaviors follow from them. A character who wants safety should act to secure it; a character who fears exposure should avoid it. Check alignment.

For each significant action, trace it to a motivation. Does the action make sense given what the character wants, fears, or values? A behavior that aligns with motivation feels earned and believable; one that does not align feels arbitrary or contradictory. Note that characters can act against their own interests, out of flaw, conflict, or complexity, but even self-defeating behavior should be comprehensible, growing from an identifiable driver like fear, pride, or conflicting desire. Flag actions that cannot be traced to any motivation, that seem to serve the plot rather than the character, or that contradict established drivers without explanation.

### Check For Consistency With Established Characterization

Characters are established through their actions, words, and the narrator's presentation. Subsequent behavior should be consistent with this established characterization, or the change should be developed. Check consistency.

Track how each character has been established: their personality, values, habits, skills, and tendencies. Verify that later behaviors are consistent with this established self. A character established as cautious who suddenly acts recklessly, or one established as honest who lies without compunction, represents a discontinuity. Discontinuity is acceptable if it is developed, if the story shows the pressure or change that produces it, but jarring inconsistency without development breaks belief. Flag behaviors that contradict established characterization without narrative justification. Consistency over time is what makes characters feel like coherent people rather than plot devices.

### Ensure Motivations Are Shown, Not Just Told

Characters feel real when their motivations are shown through action and choice rather than merely stated. Told motivations, asserted by the narrator or character without evidence, feel hollow. Ensure showing.

Assess whether motivations are demonstrated or merely asserted. A character described as ambitious should be shown pursuing goals, making choices that prioritize advancement, sacrificing other goods for success. A character described as loving should be shown acting lovingly. Told motivations, where the text says a character feels or wants something without behavioral evidence, fail to convince. Flag places where motivation is asserted without support, and recommend showing through action, dialogue, or choice. Showing motivations through behavior makes characters believable in a way telling cannot.

### Verify Characters Have Agency In Their Actions

Characters should act from their own agency, making choices that drive the story, rather than being passively moved by plot. A character with agency feels active and real; one without feels like a vehicle. Verify agency.

Assess whether significant character actions are driven by the character's choices or imposed by external plot. Does the character decide, pursue, resist, or choose? Or do things happen to them, with the character reacting or being carried along? Agency does not mean characters always succeed or control events; it means their choices matter and drive the narrative. Flag sections where characters act without agency, where the plot requires them to do something out of character or where they are passive when they should be active. Characters with agency are coherent because their actions express their will.

### Check That Contradictions Are Deliberate And Developed

Real people are contradictory, and complex characters can be too. But contradictions in fiction should be deliberate and developed, not accidental. A character who is kind in one scene and cruel in another without explanation reads as inconsistent rather than complex. Check deliberate contradiction.

Distinguish between complexity and inconsistency. Complex characters have tensions, contradictions, and depths that make them feel human. But these should be developed, with the story showing the different facets and what brings each forward. Inconsistency, where a character contradicts themselves without development or reason, reads as error. Flag contradictions that seem accidental or that serve plot convenience rather than character depth. The standard is not uniformity but comprehensibility: even a character's contradictions should make sense in light of who they are.

### Evaluate Whether Motivations Evolve Believably

Characters may grow or change through the story, their motivations evolving with experience. This evolution should be believable, developed through events and reflection, not abrupt or unmotivated. Evaluate evolution.

Track how character motivations change through the story. Does the evolution follow from experiences, revelations, or pressures the character faces? A character who wants one thing at the start and another at the end should show the journey that transforms the want. Flag motivation changes that are abrupt, that occur without sufficient cause, or that feel imposed to serve the plot rather than grown from the character's experience. Believable evolution makes character arcs satisfying; imposed evolution makes them feel arbitrary.

## Common Traps

### Characters Acting Without Clear Motivation

Identify what drives each character. Behavior should follow from drivers.

### Behavior Contradicting Established Characterization

Track established traits. Flag discontinuities without development.

### Told Rather Than Shown Motivations

Demonstrate motivations through action and choice, not assertion.

### Passive Characters Moved By Plot

Characters should have agency, driving action through choice.

### Accidental Contradictions Mistaken For Complexity

Develop contradictions deliberately. Distinguish depth from inconsistency.

### Abrupt Or Unmotivated Evolution

Motivation changes should grow from the character's experience.

### Plot-Driven Behavior That Betrays Character

Characters should not act out of character merely to serve plot needs.

## Self-Check

Before treating motivation and behavior coherence as verified, confirm:

- Each significant character's core motivations are identified and clear.
- Significant actions can be traced to understandable motivations.
- Behaviors are consistent with established characterization, or changes are developed.
- Motivations are shown through action and choice, not merely told.
- Characters have agency, driving the story through their decisions.
- Contradictions are deliberate and developed, not accidental or plot-serving.
- Motivation evolution through the story is believable and earned.
- No character acts inexplicably or contradicts themselves without narrative justification.
- The diagnosis distinguishes genuine complexity from inconsistency.
- Characters behave as coherent people whose actions readers can understand and believe.
