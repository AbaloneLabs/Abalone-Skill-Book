# Arc Progression

A character arc is the trajectory of change a character undergoes across a story. The character who begins the story is not the same as the one who ends it, and the journey between is the arc. Strong arcs progress believably, driven by events and choices, with each stage of change earned through what precedes it. Weak arcs jump without development, change without cause, or fail to change when change was promised. Arc progression is the discipline of evaluating whether a character's transformation is coherent and earned. Editors who note whether a character changed without examining how miss the developmental gaps that make arcs unconvincing.

Use this skill when editing fiction to evaluate character arc progression. It covers identifying the arc's shape, verifying believable progression, and checking that change is driven rather than imposed. The goal is a character arc whose beginning, middle, and end connect coherently and satisfyingly.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Arc's Shape And Direction

The first step is identifying what arc the character undergoes and in what direction. Arcs vary: positive, the character grows or improves; negative, the character declines or falls; flat, the character does not change but tests or affirms their nature; transformation, the character becomes fundamentally different. Identify the shape.

Determine the arc's shape and direction. Where does the character begin, psychologically or morally? Where do they end? What is the nature and direction of the change, if any? Identifying the arc provides the framework for evaluating its progression. Note that the arc may be complex, with setbacks, regressions, or nested changes, but there should be a comprehensible trajectory. Flag characters whose arc is unclear, who seem to change randomly, or whose beginning and end states are not distinguishable enough to constitute an arc. A clear arc shape is the foundation of evaluation.

### Verify The Beginning State Is Established

For an arc to be visible, the character's beginning state must be established clearly enough that the reader can perceive the contrast with the end. A vague beginning makes the arc invisible. Verify establishment.

Assess whether the story establishes who the character is at the start: their beliefs, flaws, desires, limitations, or moral state. The beginning state should be shown through action, dialogue, and choice, not just described. This establishment gives the reader a baseline against which to perceive change. Flag beginnings that are vague, that do not distinguish the character enough, or that fail to set up what will change. Without a clear beginning, even a well-written transformation reads as static, because the reader has no contrast point.

### Check That Change Is Driven By Events And Choices

Believable arcs are driven by the story's events and the character's choices in response to them. The character changes because of what they experience and decide, not because the plot requires a different character at the end. Check driven change.

Trace the causes of the character's change. What events, revelations, conflicts, or choices precipitate each stage of transformation? Does the change follow from the character engaging with the story's pressures, or does it seem imposed, occurring because the author needs a changed character for the resolution? Driven change feels earned and inevitable; imposed change feels arbitrary. Flag changes that occur without sufficient precipitating cause, that happen between scenes without development, or that serve the plot rather than growing from the character's experience. Change should be the result of the character's journey, not a requirement imposed on it.

### Verify Progression Is Gradual And Earned

Believable arcs progress gradually, with each stage of change built on what precedes it. Sudden transformation, where a character changes completely in a moment or between scenes, feels unearned. Verify gradual progression.

Assess the pacing of the arc's progression. Does the character change in stages, with each stage following from prior events and choices? Or does the change happen abruptly, in a single moment or offstage? Gradual progression allows the reader to perceive the transformation developing, which makes it believable. Some arcs include crisis moments of sudden insight or reversal, but even these should be prepared by prior development. Flag arcs that leap without steps, that resolve flaws or limitations too quickly, or whose key turning points are insufficiently prepared. Earned progression makes the arc satisfying; abrupt progression makes it feel hollow.

### Ensure Setbacks And Resistance Are Present

Real change is difficult, and believable arcs include setbacks, resistance, and moments where the character reverts or struggles. An arc that progresses smoothly without obstacle feels too easy. Ensure resistance.

Evaluate whether the arc includes resistance. Does the character struggle with change, backslide, resist the transformation, or face internal conflict? Setbacks and resistance make change feel real, because they reflect the difficulty of genuine transformation. A character who abandons a lifelong flaw in a single scene, without struggle, reads as facile. Flag arcs that progress without resistance, that resolve too smoothly, or that lack moments of doubt or reversion. Resistance creates the tension that makes the eventual change meaningful; its absence makes change feel cheap.

### Verify The End State Pays Off The Beginning

A satisfying arc has an end state that pays off the beginning, showing the transformation in a way that resonates with who the character was. The contrast between beginning and end should be visible and meaningful. Verify payoff.

Assess whether the arc's end state clearly contrasts with and pays off the beginning. Can the reader see how the character has changed, in a way that connects to who they were? Does the end state reflect the journey, showing the cumulative effect of the events and choices? The payoff may be the character acting differently than they would have at the start, wanting different things, or understanding themselves anew. Flag endings where the change is invisible, where the character seems the same as they began despite an alleged arc, or where the end state does not connect meaningfully to the beginning. Payoff is what makes the arc feel complete.

### Check Internal And External Arc Alignment

Characters have internal arcs, psychological or moral change, and external arcs, what they do or achieve in the plot. These should align, with internal change supporting external outcomes and vice versa. Check alignment.

Assess whether the internal and external arcs align. Does the character's internal transformation support or enable their external outcomes? Does the external plot drive or reflect the internal change? Misalignment, where a character achieves external success without internal change, or changes internally without the external plot reflecting it, creates a disconnect. The strongest arcs weave internal and external together, so that the character's transformation and their story outcomes are expressions of each other. Flag arcs where internal and external seem disconnected or contradictory. Alignment makes the arc feel unified and meaningful.

## Common Traps

### Unclear Arc Shape Or Direction

Identify where the character begins and ends and the nature of the change.

### Vague Beginning State

Establish who the character is at the start so change is visible.

### Imposed Rather Than Driven Change

Change should grow from events and choices, not plot requirement.

### Abrupt Transformation Without Steps

Progress gradually. Each stage should build on what precedes it.

### Smooth Progression Without Resistance

Include setbacks and struggle. Change without resistance feels too easy.

### End State That Does Not Pay Off The Beginning

Show the transformation in contrast to who the character was.

### Internal And External Arcs Disconnected

Align psychological change with plot outcomes for a unified arc.

## Self-Check

Before treating arc progression as adequately evaluated, verify:

- The arc's shape and direction are identified, whether positive, negative, flat, or transformational.
- The character's beginning state is established clearly enough to contrast with the end.
- Change is driven by the story's events and the character's choices, not imposed.
- Progression is gradual, with each stage built on what precedes it.
- The arc includes setbacks, resistance, or struggle that make change feel real.
- The end state pays off the beginning, showing transformation meaningfully.
- Internal and external arcs align, with psychological change and plot outcomes connected.
- No change occurs without sufficient cause or leaps without development.
- The arc's turning points are prepared by prior development.
- The character's beginning, middle, and end connect coherently and satisfyingly.
