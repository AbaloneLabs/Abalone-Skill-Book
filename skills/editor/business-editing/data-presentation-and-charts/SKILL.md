# Data Presentation And Charts

Charts and tables carry argumentative weight. A reader who scans a chart often accepts its message more readily than prose, because visuals feel objective and immediate. This trust makes data presentation powerful and dangerous: a mislabeled axis, a truncated scale, a cherry-picked comparison, or a chart divorced from its context can mislead without any explicit falsehood. The editor's task is to ensure that every visual is accurate, honest, clearly labeled, well-chosen for the data, integrated with the surrounding narrative, and free of distortion. This is editorial judgment applied to visuals, not merely formatting, because a misleading chart undermines the credibility of the entire document and can drive wrong decisions.

## Core Rules

### Verify Chart Accuracy Against The Underlying Data

Every chart must be checked against the data it represents. The editor confirms that the values plotted match the source data, that categories are correctly assigned, and that no data has been dropped or duplicated. Common errors include transposed axes, mismatched legends, stale data behind an updated chart, and rounding that distorts. Where the editor has access to the data, reconcile the chart to it. Where access is limited, flag the chart for verification and ask the author to confirm. A chart that does not match its data is wrong regardless of how it looks.

### Check Axis Scales, Baselines, And Ranges For Honesty

The most common visual distortion is the truncated y-axis on a bar chart, which exaggerates small differences into dramatic contrasts. The editor examines every axis: does the baseline start at zero for bar charts where magnitude comparison matters? Are ranges chosen to show the data fairly or to dramatize a trend? Are dual-axis charts combining two series on different scales clearly labeled and not implying false correlation? Flag scales chosen to amplify or minimize differences and ask whether a fairer scale would change the impression. Honest scaling does not require always starting at zero, but the choice must be deliberate and defensible.

### Ensure Complete And Clear Labeling

A chart must be understandable without reference to surrounding text. The editor checks that every chart has a clear title stating what it shows, labeled axes with units, a legend where multiple series appear, and source attribution. Data points or bars should be labeled where precision matters. Acronyms and abbreviations in labels must be defined. A chart that requires the reader to hunt for meaning in the prose fails as a visual. Flag missing or ambiguous labels and require completeness. The title should convey the chart's message, not merely name its contents.

### Match Chart Type To Data And Message

Different data and messages call for different chart types. Line charts show trends over time; bar charts compare categories; pie charts show parts of a whole, though they are often misused; scatter plots show relationships between variables. The editor evaluates whether the chosen chart type fits the data and the point being made. A pie chart with twelve slices is unreadable; a bar chart for time-series data obscures trend; a line chart for unrelated categories implies false connection. Flag mismatched chart types and recommend alternatives that serve the data and the message.

### Integrate Visuals With The Narrative

Charts should not stand alone; they should be integrated with the prose. The editor checks that each chart is referenced in the text, that the text states the chart's key message rather than merely pointing to it, and that the chart and text are consistent. "As Figure 3 shows, revenue is rising" is weak if the text does not state how much or why. The text should interpret the chart for the reader, highlighting the takeaway, not assume the chart speaks for itself. Conversely, the chart should not contradict the text. Flag charts that float without narrative integration or that conflict with surrounding claims.

### Prevent Cherry-Picking And Misleading Comparison

Data presentation can mislead through selection. A chart showing a favorable time period while omitting unfavorable context, a comparison of unlike categories, or a trend line drawn through cherry-picked points all distort. The editor asks what data is not shown and whether its absence changes the impression. Comparisons must be like-for-like: same units, same time basis, same definitions. Flag comparisons that mix definitions or timeframes and ask for consistency. Where the author has chosen a favorable framing, ask whether the full picture would support the same conclusion.

### Check Tables For Alignment, Precision, And Totals

Tables present precise values and demand their own editorial care. The editor checks that columns align, that numbers use consistent precision, that units are stated, that totals and subtotals are correct and reconcile, and that the table is ordered logically. A table where percentages do not sum correctly, where totals do not match their components, or where precision is inconsistent, three decimals in one row, none in the next, signals carelessness. Flag arithmetic errors and inconsistencies. Ensure tables have clear titles and source notes, like charts.

### Provide Context For Numbers And Trends

A number without context is ambiguous. "Sales of 4.2 million" means little without comparison: to last year, to the target, to the market. The editor ensures that significant numbers and trends are contextualized so the reader can judge them. Is the trend up or down, by how much, relative to what? Context turns data into meaning. Flag bare numbers presented without the comparison that would let the reader interpret them.

## Common Traps

### Truncated Or Manipulated Axes

The truncated y-axis is the classic misleading chart. A bar chart comparing 100 to 105 with a y-axis starting at 100 makes a 5 percent difference look enormous. The editor checks baselines and ranges and flags those chosen to dramatize. The fix is often a full-scale axis or a clear note explaining the zoomed range and why.

### Pie Charts With Too Many Slices Or Close Values

Pie charts are readable with few categories of distinctly different sizes. With many slices, or with slices of similar size, they become unreadable and force the reader to estimate angles poorly. The editor flags over-sliced pies and recommends bar charts, which compare magnitude more clearly. Three-dimensional pie charts distort perception further and should be avoided.

### Dual-Axis Charts Implying False Correlation

A dual-axis chart plotting two series on different scales can imply correlation that does not exist, especially when the axes are scaled to make the lines coincide. The editor examines dual-axis charts skeptically and asks whether the implied relationship is real or an artifact of scaling. Where dual axes are necessary, label them clearly and avoid scaling that manufactures coincidence.

### 3D Charts And Decorative Distortion

Three-dimensional bar and pie charts distort perception, because perspective makes near slices and bars appear larger than far ones. Decorative elements, gradients, shadows, and excessive color add visual noise without information. The editor flags 3D and decorative distortion and recommends clean, two-dimensional visuals that let the data speak.

### Charts Without Source Or Date

A chart without a source is unverifiable, and one without a date may be stale. The editor requires source attribution and date for every chart and table. Readers must be able to assess where the data came from and how current it is. Flag missing sources and dates and require them.

### Inconsistent Units Or Definitions Across Visuals

When one chart uses dollars and another uses euros, when one uses fiscal year and another calendar year, when categories are defined differently across tables, comparisons break. The editor checks consistency of units, time periods, and definitions across all visuals in a document. Flag inconsistencies and standardize.

### Visuals That Contradict The Text

Sometimes a chart shows one trend while the text claims another, often because the text was written for an earlier version of the data. The editor reconciles visuals and prose, ensuring they tell the same story. A contradiction between chart and text destroys credibility.

### Over-Precision Implying False Accuracy

Presenting numbers with more precision than the data supports implies false accuracy. A projection of "4,237,291 units" implies precision the forecast cannot have. The editor calibrates precision to the data's real accuracy, rounding for readability and honesty. Flag over-precise numbers and ask what precision the underlying data justifies.

## Self-Check

- Has every chart been verified against its underlying data for correct values, categories, and assignments?
- Are axis baselines, scales, and ranges honest and defensible, with truncated or zoomed scales clearly noted?
- Does every chart have a clear title, labeled axes with units, a legend where needed, source attribution, and a date?
- Is the chart type appropriate for the data and the message, avoiding misuses like over-sliced pies or line charts for unrelated categories?
- Is each chart referenced and interpreted in the text, with the prose stating the key takeaway rather than merely pointing to the figure?
- Have cherry-picked timeframes, unlike comparisons, and selective data been evaluated against the full picture?
- Do tables align correctly, use consistent precision, reconcile totals and subtotals, and carry clear titles and sources?
- Are significant numbers and trends contextualized with comparison so readers can judge them?
- Have dual-axis charts been examined for false implied correlation and labeled clearly where retained?; are visuals free of 3D distortion and decorative noise that obscure the data?
- Are units, time periods, and definitions consistent across all charts and tables in the document?; do the visuals and the prose tell the same story, with no contradictions between them?
