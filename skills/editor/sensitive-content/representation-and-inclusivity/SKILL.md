# Representation And Inclusivity

Representation matters. When works portray diverse identities, the quality of that portrayal affects how readers perceive groups and how members of those groups experience the work and the world. Tokenistic inclusion, where a single representative stands in for an entire group, reinforces rather than challenges marginalization. Stereotyped portrayal trades in harmful patterns. Absence erases groups from the work's world. Authentic, substantive representation, where diverse characters or subjects are full people rather than symbols, is the goal. Representation and inclusivity review is the discipline of assessing the quality of representation and advising on improvements that make the work genuinely inclusive. Editors who do not review for representation quality publish works that may reinforce bias through careless inclusion or exclusion.

Use this skill when editing works that portray people, especially works that include or should include diverse identities. It covers assessing representation quality, identifying tokenism and stereotyping, and advising on authentic inclusion. The goal is a work whose representation is respectful, accurate, and substantive.

## Core Rules

### Assess The Quality And Substance Of Representation

Representation is not just about presence; it is about the quality and substance of that presence. A character from a marginalized group who is fully developed, with their own arc and agency, represents substantive inclusion. A character who exists only to fulfill a diversity quota or to support the protagonist's journey is tokenistic. Assess the quality and substance of representation.

For each character or subject from a marginalized group, assess their substance. Are they fully developed with their own motivations, arcs, and agency, or do they exist primarily in relation to others? Do they have interiority and complexity, or are they defined by their identity alone? Is their identity one aspect of a full person, or is it their entire characterization? Substantive representation treats diverse characters as full people; tokenistic representation reduces them to symbols or supports. Advise on deepening tokenistic characters into substantive ones, which may involve developing their arcs, agency, and complexity.

### Identify And Address Tokenism

Tokenism is the inclusion of a single representative of a group, which places an unfair burden on that character to represent an entire identity and often reduces them to their demographic. Tokenism can feel like inclusion while reinforcing marginalization. Identify and address tokenism.

Look for patterns of tokenism: a single character representing a group, characters whose primary function is diversity rather than contribution to the story, or characters whose identity is their defining trait. Tokenism often coexists with stereotypes, because a single representative is easily reduced to a type. Advise on addressing tokenism by either developing the character into a full, substantive presence or by increasing representation so that no single character bears the burden. Multiple representatives of a group allow diversity within the group, counteracting the reduction to a type. Addressing tokenism moves a work from superficial to genuine inclusion.

### Check For Stereotyping In Diverse Portrayal

Stereotyping in diverse portrayal reduces groups to familiar, often harmful patterns: the sassy sidekick, the model minority, the tragic marginalized figure, the threatening outsider. Stereotypes are recognizable and limiting, and they shape how readers perceive groups. Check for stereotyping in diverse portrayal.

For characters from marginalized groups, assess whether they are portrayed through stereotypes. Are they assigned traits that align with familiar patterns associated with their group? Are they defined by characteristics that reduce rather than complexify? Stereotyping can be subtle, operating through association and framing rather than overt description. Flag stereotyped portrayal and advise on revision that complicates the character beyond the stereotype. Complication means giving the character traits, motivations, and arcs that defy the expected pattern, portraying them as an individual rather than a type. Anti-stereotype work is essential for respectful representation.

### Evaluate Accuracy And Authenticity

Representation should be accurate and authentic to the experiences portrayed. Inaccurate or inauthentic portrayal misrepresents groups and can cause harm, even when well-intentioned. Evaluate accuracy and authenticity, recognizing that the most reliable assessment comes from within the community.

For portrayals of specific identities or experiences, assess accuracy. Does the portrayal reflect the reality of the experience, or does it rely on assumptions or misinformation? Are cultural details correct? Is the portrayal grounded in understanding or in stereotype? Recognize that accuracy assessment has limits when the editor is outside the community portrayed. Recommend authenticity review by members of the community for portrayals outside the author's experience. Authenticity is not about gatekeeping but about ensuring that portrayal does not misrepresent or harm through inaccuracy. Inauthentic portrayal, however well-intentioned, can perpetuate misinformation.

### Consider Intersectionality In Representation

People are not defined by a single identity dimension; they carry intersecting identities that shape their experience. Representation that treats identity dimensions in isolation, portraying a character as only their race or only their disability, misses the complexity of real experience. Consider intersectionality in representation.

Assess whether diverse characters are portrayed with intersecting identities or reduced to a single dimension. A character who is both a person of color and disabled has an experience shaped by both identities, not by one in isolation. Tokenism often operates by reducing characters to a single dimension; intersectional representation restores complexity. Advise on developing characters with attention to how their multiple identities interact. Intersectionality also applies to inclusion: a work that represents one dimension of diversity but not others may still be limited. Consider the full range of identities relevant to the work's context.

### Review Language And Framing For Inclusivity

Beyond character portrayal, the work's language and framing affect inclusivity. Language that assumes a default identity, framing that centers one perspective as normative, or descriptions that exoticize or other marginalized groups all undermine inclusivity. Review language and framing.

Examine the work's language for inclusivity. Does it assume a default reader or subject identity, marginalizing others? Does it use language that centers one perspective as normal and others as different? Does it exoticize or other marginalized groups through description? Advise on revising exclusionary or othering language. This includes using inclusive pronouns and terms, avoiding framings that assume a normative identity, and describing diverse groups with the same respect and specificity as the default. Inclusive language and framing make the work welcoming to diverse readers and avoid reinforcing marginalization through subtle patterns.

### Recommend Authenticity And Sensitivity Readers

For works portraying identities outside the author's and editor's experience, recommend authenticity and sensitivity readers from the communities portrayed. These readers can assess accuracy, identify harms, and advise on authentic representation that outsiders cannot provide. Recommend specialist readers for relevant content.

Where the work includes characters or subjects from marginalized communities, especially as central figures, recommend engagement with authenticity and sensitivity readers from those communities. These readers provide the most reliable assessment of accuracy and harm, and they can advise on improvements that strengthen the representation. Frame the recommendation as an investment in the work's quality and integrity, not as a deficiency in the author. Authenticity and sensitivity reading is now standard practice for works engaging with identities outside the creator's experience, and it significantly improves the quality and responsibility of representation.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Presence With Substantive Representation

Inclusion is not just about presence. Assess the quality and substance of portrayal.

### Accepting Tokenistic Inclusion

A single representative bears unfair burden and reduces to type. Address tokenism.

### Missing Subtle Stereotyping

Stereotypes operate through patterns and framing. Look beyond overt description.

### Assuming Accuracy Without Community Input

Authenticity assessment has limits from outside. Recommend community readers.

### Reducing Characters To A Single Identity Dimension

People have intersecting identities. Consider intersectionality in representation.

### Overlooking Exclusionary Language And Framing

Subtle language patterns marginalize. Review for inclusive framing.

### Skipping Specialist Review For Outside-Experience Content

Authenticity and sensitivity readers are essential for respectful representation.

## Self-Check

Before treating representation and inclusivity review as complete, verify:

- The quality and substance of representation has been assessed, not just presence.
- Tokenism has been identified and addressed through deepening or diversifying representation.
- Stereotyping in diverse portrayal has been checked and flagged for revision.
- Accuracy and authenticity of portrayal have been evaluated, with community input recommended.
- Intersectionality has been considered in character development and inclusion.
- Language and framing have been reviewed for inclusivity and othering patterns.
- Authenticity and sensitivity readers have been recommended for content outside the creator's experience.
- Diverse characters are portrayed as full people with agency, complexity, and their own arcs.
- The work's representation is respectful, accurate, and substantive.
- Inclusivity review has been conducted with awareness of the editor's own identity and limitations.
