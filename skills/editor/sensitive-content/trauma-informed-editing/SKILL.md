# Trauma Informed Editing

Works that depict or discuss trauma, violence, abuse, self-harm, or other distressing content carry responsibilities beyond ordinary editing. The subjects portrayed are often real people or represent real experiences, and careless depiction can retraumatize or exploit. Readers may have their own trauma histories, and unheralded distressing content can cause harm. Trauma-informed editing applies principles of safety, choice, and respect to the editing of distressing content, ensuring depictions are handled with care and that readers are warned and prepared. Editors who treat distressing content like any other content produce works that can harm subjects and readers in preventable ways.

Use this skill when editing works that contain depictions or discussions of trauma, violence, abuse, mental health crises, or other distressing content, in fiction and nonfiction. It covers assessing depiction handling, applying trauma-informed principles, and advising on content warnings. The goal is a work that addresses difficult content responsibly, minimizing harm to subjects and readers.

## Core Rules

### Assess How Trauma And Violence Are Depicted

The first task is assessing how distressing content is depicted. Depictions can be handled with care, focusing on impact and context, or exploitatively, focusing on graphic detail for sensation. The difference matters for both subjects and readers. Assess depiction handling throughout.

Examine depictions of trauma, violence, and distress. Consider whether the depiction serves the work's purpose or lingers on graphic detail gratuitously. Consider whether the depiction centers the subject's experience and agency or objectifies and exploits. Consider the level of graphic detail and whether it is necessary. Graphic detail is sometimes necessary for truth or impact, but it should be deliberate, not default. Flag depictions that feel exploitative, sensationalized, or gratuitously graphic, and advise on revision that serves the work's purpose with reduced harm. Careful depiction respects both subjects and readers.

### Apply Trauma-Informed Principles To Depiction And Editing

Trauma-informed practice is guided by principles: safety, trustworthiness, choice, collaboration, and empowerment. Applied to editing, these principles mean handling distressing content in ways that promote safety, give readers choice about engagement, and avoid retraumatization. Apply trauma-informed principles throughout.

Consider safety: does the work provide warnings and preparation that allow readers to engage safely? Consider choice: are readers able to make informed decisions about engaging with distressing content? Consider empowerment: does the depiction respect subjects' agency rather than reducing them to victimhood? Consider the cumulative effect of distressing content across the work. Trauma-informed editing does not mean avoiding difficult content, but handling it in ways that minimize harm. This includes avoiding gratuitous detail, providing content warnings, respecting subject agency, and being attentive to the work's cumulative impact on readers.

### Evaluate Necessity Of Graphic Detail

Graphic detail is a tool that should be used deliberately, not by default. Some works require graphic depiction to convey truth, impact, or the reality of trauma. But graphic detail that exceeds the work's purpose becomes gratuitous and harmful. Evaluate the necessity of graphic detail.

For graphic depictions, assess whether the detail level serves the work's purpose. Is the graphic detail necessary to convey the reality and impact of the experience, or does it exceed what the purpose requires? Could the same impact be achieved with less graphic depiction? Flag gratuitously graphic content and advise on revision that achieves the purpose with reduced graphic detail. This is not about sanitizing trauma but about ensuring that graphic depiction is deliberate and purposeful rather than default or sensational. Necessary graphic detail can be retained; gratuitous detail should be revised.

### Advise On Content Warnings

Content warnings allow readers to make informed choices about engaging with distressing content. They are a key trauma-informed practice that respects reader safety and autonomy. Advise on content warnings for works containing distressing material.

For works with distressing content, advise on content warnings that identify the types of content present, such as violence, sexual assault, self-harm, or abuse. Determine the appropriate placement and specificity of warnings: general warnings at the work's beginning, specific warnings before particular sections, or a list readers can consult. Content warnings should be specific enough to be useful without spoiling content unnecessarily. They are especially important for content that may trigger trauma responses, such as detailed depictions of assault or self-harm. Advise the author on implementing content warnings as part of trauma-informed practice. Warnings give readers the choice to prepare or disengage, which is essential for safety.

### Consider The Subjects Of Trauma Portrayals

In nonfiction, trauma portrayals involve real subjects whose experiences are being depicted. In fiction, portrayals may represent real experiences for many readers. Consider the subjects in trauma portrayals, ensuring their dignity and agency are respected.

For nonfiction trauma portrayals, consider whether the subjects have consented to the depiction and how they are portrayed. Are they afforded dignity and agency, or reduced to their trauma? Is identifying detail necessary, or does it expose subjects unnecessarily? For fictional portrayals, consider whether the depiction relies on harmful tropes or represents experiences respectfully. Advise on revisions that respect subject dignity and agency. Trauma portrayals that exploit or objectify subjects, even unintentionally, cause harm. Respecting subjects means depicting them as full people, not reducing them to their trauma.

### Handle Retraumatization Risks

Distressing content can retraumatize readers who have similar experiences. This risk is not a reason to avoid difficult content, but it requires management through warnings, careful pacing, and attention to cumulative impact. Handle retraumatization risks proactively.

Consider which content poses retraumatization risk, typically detailed depictions of specific traumas like assault, abuse, or self-harm. Ensure such content is warned so readers can prepare or avoid. Consider pacing: relentless distressing content without relief compounds impact. Advise on structural choices that manage cumulative impact, such as balancing distressing sections with relief. For particularly high-risk content, consider whether the level of detail is necessary. Retraumatization risk management is about giving readers control and managing the work's impact, not about avoiding difficult content altogether.

### Recommend Specialist Review For High-Risk Content

For content involving specific traumas or communities the editor is not part of, recommend specialist review. Editors can apply trauma-informed principles, but the most reliable assessment of specific content comes from those with relevant expertise or lived experience. Recommend specialist review for high-risk content.

Where the work depicts specific traumas or involves communities outside the editor's experience, recommend review by specialists or sensitivity readers with relevant expertise. This is especially important for content involving marginalized communities, specific cultural traumas, or detailed depictions of particular experiences. Specialist review can identify harms and insensitivities that general trauma-informed editing misses. Frame the recommendation as strengthening the work's responsible handling of difficult content. Specialist review is an investment in the work's integrity and in preventing harm to subjects and readers.

## Common Traps

### Treating Distressing Content Like Any Other

Trauma and violence require careful handling. Apply trauma-informed principles.

### Gratuitous Graphic Detail

Graphic depiction should be deliberate and purposeful, not default. Evaluate necessity.

### Omitting Content Warnings

Warnings allow readers to make informed choices. Advise on their use.

### Objectifying Trauma Subjects

Subjects should be afforded dignity and agency, not reduced to their trauma.

### Ignoring Retraumatization Risk

Detailed trauma depictions can retraumatize readers. Manage risk through warnings and pacing.

### Sensationalizing Trauma

Depictions should serve the work's purpose, not sensationalize. Flag exploitation.

### Relying Only On General Principles For Specific Content

Recommend specialist review for content involving specific traumas or communities.

## Self-Check

Before treating trauma-informed editing as complete, verify:

- Depictions of trauma and violence have been assessed for care versus exploitation.
- Trauma-informed principles of safety, choice, and empowerment have been applied.
- Graphic detail has been evaluated for necessity versus gratuity.
- Content warnings have been advised for works containing distressing material.
- Warnings are specific enough to be useful without unnecessary spoiling.
- Subjects of trauma portrayals are treated with dignity and agency.
- Retraumatization risks have been considered and managed through warnings and pacing.
- The cumulative impact of distressing content across the work has been assessed.
- Specialist review has been recommended for high-risk or specific content.
- The work addresses difficult content responsibly, minimizing harm to subjects and readers.
