# Harm And Stereotype Review

Words can harm as well as inform. Portrayals that rely on stereotypes, language that demeans groups, framing that biases perception, and misrepresentation of communities all cause harm, whether intended or not. This harm is real: it affects how readers perceive groups, how members of those groups experience the work, and the work's contribution to broader patterns of bias. Harm and stereotype review is the discipline of reading a work critically for harmful patterns and advising revisions that reduce harm while respecting the work's purpose. Editors who do not review for harm publish works that may perpetuate bias, alienate readers, and cause damage that could have been prevented.

Use this skill when editing works that portray people, groups, or cultures, especially where stereotypes or bias may be present. It covers identifying harmful patterns, assessing their impact, and advising revisions. The goal is a work that treats all people and groups with fairness and respect, consistent with the work's purpose.

## Core Rules

### Read Critically For Stereotypes And Bias

Stereotypes and bias often operate beneath the surface, in patterns of portrayal, attribution, and framing rather than in overt statements. Reading critically means attending to these patterns: which groups are portrayed and how, what traits are associated with which characters or groups, whose perspectives are centered and whose are marginalized. Read critically for stereotypes and bias throughout.

As you read, attend to portrayal patterns. Note when characters or groups are reduced to stereotypes, defined by a single trait rather than as full people. Note when negative traits are disproportionately associated with particular groups. Note whose voices and perspectives are centered and whose are absent or marginalized. Note framing that biases perception, such as language that associates groups with threat, dysfunction, or exoticism. Critical reading for patterns, not just overt statements, is how subtle bias is detected. Overt slurs are easy to catch; structural patterns require attentive reading.

### Identify Demeaning And Dehumanizing Language

Some language demeans or dehumanizes groups, whether through slurs, derogatory terms, or language that reduces people to subhuman status. This language causes direct harm and requires attention. Identify demeaning and dehumanizing language.

Scan for slurs, derogatory terms, and language that dehumanizes. Distinguish between language used by characters for characterization, which may be appropriate in context, and language used by the narrator or authorial voice, which carries the work's endorsement. Consider whether demeaning language is necessary for the work's purpose or whether it can be revised. Note that terms acceptable in one context or era may be harmful in another. When demeaning language is found, advise on revision or contextualization. Directly harmful language is the clearest category requiring intervention.

### Assess Framing And Perspective Bias

Beyond specific language, framing and perspective can bias portrayal. A work may frame a group through a deficit lens, emphasizing problems rather than strengths. It may present one group's perspective as normative and others as deviant. It may associate groups with particular settings or roles in limiting ways. Assess framing and perspective bias.

Consider how groups are framed. Is a group portrayed through its challenges and deficits, or through its full humanity? Is one group's experience presented as the norm against which others are measured? Are groups associated with limiting roles or settings? Framing bias shapes how readers perceive groups, often more powerfully than specific statements. Advise on reframing where bias is present, such as balancing deficit framing with strengths, presenting diverse perspectives as legitimate rather than deviant, and avoiding limiting associations. Framing revision can significantly reduce harm without changing the work's substance.

### Check Representation And Inclusion

Harm occurs not only through negative portrayal but through absence and exclusion. If a work addresses a context that includes diverse groups but portrays only one, the absence itself is a form of harm, erasing those who are present. Check representation and inclusion.

Consider whether the work's portrayal reflects the diversity of the context it addresses. If a work about a diverse setting portrays only one group, the absence of others is a representation failure. If a work addresses issues affecting particular communities but does not include those communities' perspectives, the exclusion compounds the harm. Advise on inclusion where absence is a problem, such as incorporating diverse perspectives, including affected communities in portrayal, and ensuring representation reflects the context. Inclusion is not tokenism; it means genuine, respectful presence rather than erasure.

### Evaluate Cultural And Historical Context

Portrayals must be evaluated in cultural and historical context. A portrayal that is fair in one cultural context may be harmful in another. Historical portrayals must navigate between period authenticity and contemporary harm. Evaluate cultural and historical context in harm assessment.

For portrayals involving specific cultures or historical periods, consider the context. Is the portrayal accurate and fair to the culture portrayed, or does it rely on outsider stereotypes? For historical works, how does the work balance period language and attitudes with contemporary standards of respect? Are harmful historical patterns presented critically or reproduced uncritically? Context does not excuse harm, but it affects how harm is assessed and addressed. Advise on contextualization, critical framing, or revision as appropriate. Contextual evaluation prevents both anachronistic judgment and uncritical reproduction of harmful patterns.

### Advise Revisions That Reduce Harm While Respecting Purpose

Harm review should advise revisions that reduce harm while respecting the work's purpose and the author's intent. The goal is not to sanitize or impose a perspective, but to help the work achieve its purpose without unnecessary harm. Advise revisions calibrated to the work.

For each harm identified, advise a revision that reduces the harm while preserving the work's purpose. Stereotypes can be complicated into full characters. Demeaning language can be revised or contextualized. Framing can be rebalanced. Absence can be addressed through inclusion. Harmful historical patterns can be presented critically. The advice should be specific and actionable, explaining the harm and proposing a revision that serves the work better. Respect that the author may have reasons for choices, and frame advice as collaboration toward the work's goals, not imposition of external standards. The aim is a work that fulfills its purpose with reduced harm.

### Recommend Sensitivity Reviewers For Affected Communities

For works portraying communities the editor is not part of, recommend sensitivity review by members of those communities. Editors can identify potential harm, but the most reliable assessment comes from those affected. Recommend sensitivity reviewers for affected communities.

Where the work portrays communities outside the editor's and author's experience, recommend that the author engage sensitivity reviewers from those communities. Sensitivity reviewers can identify harms that outsiders miss, assess accuracy and fairness, and advise on respectful portrayal. This is especially important for works centered on marginalized communities. Frame the recommendation as strengthening the work, not as a deficiency. Sensitivity review by affected communities is the most reliable way to catch harm and improve portrayal, and it should be part of the process for works involving communities outside the creators' experience.

## Common Traps

### Reading Only For Overt Slurs

Subtle patterns of bias cause harm too. Read for framing and portrayal patterns.

### Endorsing Rather Than Contextualizing Harmful Language

Narrator or authorial harmful language carries endorsement. Advise revision or contextualization.

### Missing Absence And Exclusion As Harm

Erasure through absence is harmful. Check representation and inclusion.

### Ignoring Framing Bias

Deficit framing and normative perspective bias perception. Assess framing.

### Sanitizing Rather Than Revising

The goal is to reduce harm while respecting purpose, not to impose external standards.

### Judging Without Cultural Or Historical Context

Context affects harm assessment. Evaluate portrayals in their context.

### Relying Only On Editor Judgment For Affected Communities

Recommend sensitivity reviewers from affected communities for reliable assessment.

## Self-Check

Before treating harm and stereotype review as complete, verify:

- The work has been read critically for patterns of stereotype and bias, not just overt statements.
- Demeaning and dehumanizing language has been identified, with attention to authorial voice versus character speech.
- Framing and perspective bias has been assessed, including deficit framing and normative perspectives.
- Representation and inclusion have been checked against the diversity of the work's context.
- Cultural and historical context has been considered in harm assessment.
- Revisions advised reduce harm while respecting the work's purpose and the author's intent.
- Advice is specific, actionable, and collaborative rather than imposed.
- Sensitivity review by affected communities has been recommended where the work portrays outside the creators' experience.
- The work treats all people and groups with fairness and respect consistent with its purpose.
- Harm review has been conducted with awareness of the editor's own perspective and limitations.
