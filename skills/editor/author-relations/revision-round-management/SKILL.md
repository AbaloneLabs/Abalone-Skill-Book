# Revision Round Management

Editing a substantial work is rarely a single pass; it is a sequence of rounds, each operating at a different level and building on the last. How those rounds are sequenced, how transitions between them are managed, and how the editor decides when to advance or to stop determines whether revision converges on a finished work or circles endlessly. Poor round management wastes effort, editing sentences that later get cut, demoralizes authors with feedback that arrives too late or too soon, and produces work that is polished in parts but unresolved as a whole. Managing revision rounds is the editorial discipline of sequencing change so that each round builds on a stable foundation and the work moves steadily toward completion.

Use this skill when managing a multi-round editorial process on any substantial work. It covers sequencing rounds at different levels, deciding when to move between developmental, substantive, line, and copy levels, handling author responses to feedback, managing the transition from big-picture to sentence-level work, preventing endless revision loops, and judging when a draft is done. The editor's task is to move the work through revision in the right order, at the right pace, so that effort is spent where it moves the work forward.

## Core Rules

### Sequence Rounds From Big Picture To Sentence

The foundational principle of round management is to work from the largest scale down to the smallest. Structural problems invalidate sentence-level work; if a chapter is cut or reordered after it has been line-edited, that effort is wasted. Sequencing from developmental and substantive concerns down to line and copy levels ensures each round builds on a stable foundation rather than on material that may be removed.

Plan rounds in descending order of scale. Begin with developmental and structural feedback, the work's argument, organization, scope, and shape, because changes here ripple through everything below. Once the structure is stable, move to substantive revision of sections and paragraphs, then to line editing of sentences, and finally to copy-editing and proofreading. Resist the temptation to fix sentences that catch your eye during a structural round, since noting them is useful but editing them now is premature. Communicate the sequence to the author so they understand why early rounds feel broad and later rounds feel granular, and why a concern raised now may not be addressed until a later round. Working in the right order is what makes each round's effort durable.

### Stabilize Each Level Before Descending

Moving to a finer level of edit before the coarser level is resolved creates rework and confusion. Line-editing a section whose structure is still in flux means editing prose that may be cut or heavily rewritten; copy-editing before substantive issues are settled means enforcing consistency on text that may change. Each level should be stabilized before the next begins.

Before descending to a finer level, confirm the coarser level is sufficiently resolved. Structure should be settled before substantive work; substantive issues resolved before line editing; line-level prose stable before copy-editing. "Sufficiently resolved" does not mean perfect; it means that further changes at the coarser level are unlikely to invalidate work at the finer level. Where a structural question remains open, address it before investing in the sentences it may sweep away. This discipline is what prevents the demoralizing experience of editing the same passage three times because the ground keeps shifting beneath it.

### Manage The Transition Between Levels Deliberately

The transition between editorial levels is a moment that authors often experience as disorienting. A round of broad developmental feedback followed suddenly by sentence-level nitpicking can feel like a change of rules, and an author may wonder why concerns never raised before now appear urgent. Managing these transitions explicitly helps the author understand where they are in the process and what each round is for.

At each transition, name what is changing and why. When moving from structural to line work, explain that the foundation is now stable and attention is shifting to expression. When moving to copy-editing, explain that the prose is settled and the focus is now consistency and correctness. Frame the shift as progress, not as a new wave of problems, so the author experiences descent through levels as convergence rather than as endless critique. Acknowledge that finer-level concerns were always present but were deliberately deferred until the coarser level was resolved. A well-managed transition maintains the author's confidence that the work is moving forward rather than being perpetually found wanting.

### Handle Author Responses And Partial Revisions

Authors rarely implement feedback perfectly or completely. They may address some concerns, misread others, resist some, or implement changes that introduce new problems. Managing revision rounds requires handling the gap between the feedback given and the revision returned, deciding what to re-raise, what to accept, and what to let go.

When reviewing a revision, compare it against the prior round's feedback rather than reading it fresh. Note which concerns were addressed, which were partially addressed, and which were ignored or resisted. For addressed concerns, confirm the fix actually resolved the issue rather than introducing a new one. For partial or ignored concerns, decide whether to re-raise them, considering their importance and the author's apparent reasons. Some resistance is worth engaging, some reflects the author's legitimate authority over their work, and some signals a communication problem worth clarifying. Distinguish these rather than treating all incomplete revision as defiance. The goal is convergence, not compliance, and a skilled editor knows which battles move the work forward and which are not worth fighting.

### Prevent Endless Revision Loops

A revision process can stall when each round introduces new concerns, the author addresses them, and the editor finds more, indefinitely. Endless revision demoralizes authors, consumes resources, and often produces diminishing returns as the work oscillates without converging. Recognizing when revision has begun to loop, and breaking the loop deliberately, is essential to finishing.

Watch for signs of a loop: the same concerns resurfacing in different forms, new issues raised that are finer than the work warrants, or the author revising eagerly without the work clearly improving. When a loop appears, diagnose its cause. It may be that the work's core problem was never properly identified, so each round treats symptoms; that the goal was never defined, so there is no clear "done"; or that the editor is raising the bar rather than holding it. Address the cause: clarify the goal, define what "done" looks like for this work, and hold the standard rather than escalating it. Some works reach a point of diminishing returns where further revision does not justify the cost, and the editor's job is to recognize that point and call the work done at a defensible level rather than perfect.

### Define And Recognize Done

A revision process without a definition of "done" has no natural endpoint and tends to end only through exhaustion or deadline. Defining what completion looks like, and recognizing when the work has reached it, is what allows revision to conclude with confidence rather than drift. "Done" is not the same as "perfect"; it is the point at which the work meets its purpose at a defensible standard.

At the outset, or early in the process, establish what completion means for this work: what level of structural soundness, clarity, consistency, and correctness is required for the work to serve its readers and meet its commitments. Use this definition to guide when to advance between levels and when to conclude. As the work nears completion, check it against the definition rather than against an ideal of perfection. Recognize that different works have different standards, a reference work demands different completeness than a personal essay, and calibrate "done" to the work's purpose and audience. Calling a work done at the right moment, neither prematurely nor after diminishing returns, is a judgment that protects both the work's quality and the people producing it.

## Common Traps

### Fine Editing Before Structure Is Stable

Line-editing material that may be cut wastes effort. Stabilize each level before descending.

### New Concerns Raised Every Round

Each round introducing fresh issues prevents convergence. Hold the standard rather than escalating it.

### Transitions Between Levels Unexplained

Sudden shifts from broad to granular feedback feel like new problems. Name the transition as progress.

### Treating Incomplete Revision As Defiance

Authors may misread, resist, or partially address feedback for legitimate reasons. Diagnose before re-raising.

### No Definition Of Done

Revision without a completion standard ends only through exhaustion. Define and recognize done.

### Perfecting Beyond Diminishing Returns

Endless polishing consumes resources without improving the work. Stop at a defensible standard.

### Same Concerns Resurfacing In Different Forms

Recurring issues signal an undiagnosed core problem. Address the cause, not the symptoms.

### Copy-Editing Before Substantive Issues Settle

Enforcing consistency on unstable text wastes effort. Wait until the prose is settled.

## Self-Check

Before treating revision round management as complete, verify:

- Rounds are sequenced from big-picture to sentence-level, so each round builds on a stable foundation.
- Each editorial level is sufficiently stabilized before descending to a finer level, preventing rework.
- Transitions between levels are explained to the author as progress, not experienced as new waves of critique.
- Author revisions are reviewed against prior feedback, distinguishing addressed, partial, and resisted concerns.
- Incomplete revisions are diagnosed for their causes rather than treated as defiance, with important concerns re-raised judiciously.
- Endless revision loops are recognized and broken by clarifying goals and holding rather than escalating the standard.
- A definition of "done," calibrated to the work's purpose and audience, guides when to advance and when to conclude.
- The work is called complete at a defensible standard, neither prematurely nor after diminishing returns.
- The author experiences the process as convergence toward a finished work rather than as endless correction.
