# Revision Coaching And Guidance

Revision coaching is a different activity from line editing or one-shot feedback. The coach's goal is not only to improve the current draft but to develop the author's capacity to revise, to see their own work critically, and to sustain the effort revision demands. A revision can be long, emotionally taxing, and full of false starts, and authors often lose perspective on their own work. The coach provides the external perspective the author has lost, frames a clear path through what may feel overwhelming, prioritizes so the author does not waste effort, and balances pushing for the work's potential with accepting the author's limits and timeline. This is work that requires diagnostic skill, because the coach must see what the draft could become, and interpersonal skill, because the author must believe the revision is possible and worth doing.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose Before Prescribing

Effective coaching begins with diagnosis, not prescription. Before suggesting changes, the editor reads the draft to identify its core problems and its core possibilities. Is the issue structural, a flaw in the argument or plot; substantive, weak evidence or underdeveloped ideas; or procedural, the author has not yet found their way into the material? Different diagnoses lead to different revision paths. Prescribing before diagnosing wastes effort and can send the author in the wrong direction. The editor articulates the diagnosis clearly, shares it with the author, and confirms the author recognizes the problems described. A diagnosis the author does not share will not drive revision.

### Frame A Revision Path With Clear Priorities

Revision can feel infinite, which paralyzes authors. The coach frames a finite path: the major tasks, in priority order, with a sense of scope and sequence. Structural issues come before line issues; foundational changes before refinements. The path should be ambitious enough to realize the work's potential but bounded enough that the author believes completion is possible. Break large tasks into manageable steps. The author should leave the coaching conversation knowing what to do first, what to do next, and what to defer. A clear path converts overwhelm into momentum.

### Help The Author See The Work From The Reader's Perspective

Authors lose the ability to read their own work as a reader would, because they know what they meant. The coach provides the reader's perspective, describing how the draft actually lands, where it confuses, where it convinces, where it loses engagement. This external perspective is the core value of coaching, because the author cannot generate it alone. The editor gives specific reader-effect feedback that helps the author internalize the reader's experience and learn to anticipate it. Over time, this builds the author's self-editing capacity.

### Build Revision Skill, Not Just This Draft

Coaching that only fixes the current draft leaves the author dependent; coaching that builds skill develops the author. The editor takes opportunities to teach: explaining why a structural choice works or fails, naming patterns the author can watch for, modeling diagnostic questions the author can ask of their own work. This does not mean lecturing; it means making the reasoning behind editorial guidance visible so the author can learn from it. Skill-building is especially valuable for authors who will revise multiple works or who work without ongoing editorial support.

### Balance Pushing For Potential With Accepting Limits

The coach holds a tension: pushing the author toward the work's potential while accepting the author's limits of time, skill, energy, and intent. Pushing too hard for an idealized version burns the author out or sets unattainable expectations; settling too easily leaves the work short of what it could be. The editor calibrates push to the author's situation and the work's purpose. A trade book under contract may warrant intense revision; a personal project with a tired author may warrant accepting a good-enough version. The judgment is honest about what the work could become and realistic about what this author can do now.

### Sustain Motivation Across A Long Revision

Long revisions test motivation. Authors stall, doubt, and consider quitting. The coach sustains momentum by marking progress, celebrating milestones, reminding the author of the work's value and their capacity, and breaking the revision into achievable chunks that produce a sense of accomplishment. Motivation is not cheerleading; it is honest acknowledgment of progress and worth. The editor also watches for the author's flagging and addresses it directly, asking what is blocking progress and helping the author find a way through. A revision that stalls indefinitely fails.

### Manage The Author's Expectations About Scope And Effort

Authors often underestimate what revision requires. The coach sets honest expectations early: this revision will involve significant restructuring, or this will be a focused polish, or this will take several rounds. Honest scoping prevents the author's shock and discouragement when the work proves harder than expected. The editor also resists under-scoping to avoid alarming the author, because a falsely optimistic scope leads to dashed expectations. Calibrate scope to the diagnosis and communicate it clearly.

### Know When To Stop Revising

Revision can continue indefinitely, and at some point the work is as good as it will be, or as good as it needs to be, or as good as the author can make it within the constraints. The coach must recognize the stopping point and say so honestly. Endless revision can degrade the work as the author overworks it, or prevent publication. The editor distinguishes productive revision from diminishing returns and guides the author toward completion. Knowing when to stop is as important as knowing what to revise.

## Common Traps

### Prescribing Before Diagnosing

Jumping to fixes before understanding the core problems leads to wasted revision and misdirected effort. The editor diagnoses first, confirms the diagnosis with the author, then frames the revision path.

### Overwhelming The Author With Everything At Once

Presenting every problem simultaneously paralyzes. The coach prioritizes, sequences, and stages the revision so the author can engage constructively and build momentum through achievable steps.

### Fixing The Draft Instead Of Building The Author

Coaching that supplies all the answers leaves the author dependent and undermines their development. The editor makes reasoning visible, teaches diagnostic patterns, and lets the author own solutions, building capacity for future work.

### Pushing For An Unrealistic Ideal

Holding the author to an idealized standard the work or author cannot reach burns out the author and sets up failure. The editor calibrates ambition to the work's potential and the author's real constraints, pushing for meaningful improvement without demanding perfection.

### Settling Too Easily

Conversely, accepting a draft that has significant unrealized potential, to avoid difficult conversations or save time, underserves the work and the author. The editor is honest about what the work could become and coaches toward it within realistic constraints.

### Failing To Address Flagging Motivation

Revisions stall, and a coach who does not notice or address the author's flagging motivation allows the revision to die. The editor watches for signs of stall and addresses them directly, helping the author find a way through.

### Under-Scoping The Revision To Avoid Alarming

Setting a falsely optimistic scope to spare the author's feelings leads to shock and discouragement when reality hits. The editor scopes honestly from the diagnosis, preparing the author for the real effort.

### Endless Revision Past The Point Of Improvement

Revision can become a loop that degrades rather than improves the work, or that prevents it from reaching readers. The editor recognizes diminishing returns and guides the author to completion, distinguishing productive revision from overwork.

## Self-Check

- Has the draft been diagnosed to identify core problems and possibilities before any revision path is prescribed?
- Is the revision path framed with clear priorities, sequence, and scope, so the author knows what to do first and what to defer?
- Does the coaching provide the reader's perspective the author has lost, with specific reader-effect feedback?
- Does the coaching build the author's revision capacity, making editorial reasoning visible and teaching diagnostic patterns?
- Is the push for the work's potential calibrated to the author's real limits of time, skill, energy, and intent?
- Is motivation sustained through marked progress, honest acknowledgment, and direct attention to stalls?
- Are expectations about revision scope and effort set honestly from the start, avoiding both false optimism and unnecessary alarm?
- Is there a clear sense of when revision should stop, with diminishing returns recognized and completion guided?
- Has the diagnosis been shared with and confirmed by the author, so the revision path is shared rather than imposed?; has the revision been broken into achievable steps that build momentum rather than an undifferentiated mass?
- Is the editor honest about both the work's potential and its current distance from that potential?; would the author leave the coaching with a clear, believable path forward and the confidence and skill to walk it?
