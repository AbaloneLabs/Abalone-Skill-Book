# Author Onboarding And Project Scoping

The editorial relationship is shaped decisively in its first encounters, often before any sentence is edited. How an editor onboards an author, defines the project, sets expectations, and establishes working norms determines whether the collaboration runs smoothly or collapses into misunderstanding. Many of the hardest problems in editing, scope disputes, missed deadlines, bruised egos, mismatched expectations about how much the editor will change, originate not in the work itself but in assumptions left unexamined at the start. Onboarding and scoping are where an editor prevents those problems by making the implicit explicit.

Use this skill when beginning work with a new author or contributor, launching a new project with an existing author, or resetting a relationship that has drifted into confusion. It covers establishing the working relationship, defining scope and deliverables, setting expectations about process and timeline, clarifying editorial involvement and decision rights, and agreeing on communication norms. The editor's task is to build shared understanding before substantive work begins, so that later disagreements are resolved by reference to an agreed framework rather than by renewed negotiation.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Relationship Before The Work

The single most valuable onboarding act is to treat the relationship itself as something to be established, not assumed. Two professionals beginning a collaboration bring different assumptions about roles, authority, communication, and what editing means, and those assumptions will collide eventually. Surfacing them early, in a conversation rather than a conflict, is far cheaper than resolving them later.

Begin with an explicit conversation about how the collaboration will work, not only what it will produce. Introduce your role and your editing philosophy, how involved you tend to be, what you see as the author's authority and your own, and how you handle disagreement. Ask the author about their prior editorial experiences, good and bad, since an author scarred by a heavy-handed editor needs different reassurance than one who has worked mainly alone. Establish how the author prefers to receive feedback, how often they want contact, and what their working rhythm is. This conversation is not a formality; it is the foundation on which everything else rests, and skipping it leaves the relationship to be defined by its first friction.

### Define Scope And Deliverables Explicitly

Scope ambiguity is the most common source of editorial conflict. "Edit my manuscript" can mean anything from a light proofread to a structural rebuild, and an author and editor who imagine different scopes will discover the gap only when work has already begun and expectations have been set. Scoping must make the project's boundaries, deliverables, and depth explicit.

Define what the editor will and will not do, at what level of edit, over what material, and producing what deliverables. Specify whether the engagement includes developmental feedback, line editing, copy-editing, fact-checking, or only some of these, and at what depth. Define the length and condition of the material to be edited, what counts as a complete draft, and whether additional research, rewriting, or restructuring is in scope. Put the scope in writing, even informally, so both parties can return to it when boundaries blur. Scope is not a constraint on the work; it is the agreement that lets the work proceed without constant renegotiation, and revisiting it deliberately when the project changes is part of maintaining it.

### Set Expectations About Timeline And Process

Timeline disputes often stem from mismatched assumptions about how long editing takes, how many rounds are included, and when the author must deliver. An author who expects a single pass in a week and an editor who plans three rounds over two months are set up for disappointment regardless of the work's quality. Process and timeline must be agreed, not assumed.

Lay out the expected process: how many rounds of editing, what happens in each, how long the editor needs, and how long the author will have to respond. Build in the realities of the author's other commitments and the editor's queue, rather than assuming ideal availability. Identify the critical path, the dependencies that determine the overall timeline, such as peer review, fact-checking, or design, and where slippage will cascade. For long projects, set interim milestones so progress is visible and delays surface early. Where the timeline is uncertain, say so, and agree on how changes will be communicated. A timeline that both parties actually believe in is more useful than an optimistic one that neither can meet.

### Clarify Decision Rights And Authority

Editing involves constant small decisions about what to change, what to query, and what to leave, and the rules governing those decisions are rarely stated. An editor who rewrites freely may be seen as overstepping; an editor who queries everything may be seen as timid. Clarifying decision rights, who decides what, and how disagreements are resolved, prevents these frictions from accumulating.

Agree on the editor's authority to make changes directly versus to query, and how changes will be visible to the author. Some authors want to see and approve every edit; others prefer the editor to fix obvious errors silently and reserve queries for judgment calls. Establish how substantive changes, to structure, argument, or voice, will be handled, since these are where authority is most contested. Clarify the author's final authority over their work, which decisions are the author's to make even if the editor disagrees, and which are the editor's or the publication's to enforce, such as legal or style requirements. Decision rights do not eliminate disagreement, but they give it a structure for resolution rather than leaving it to power dynamics.

### Agree On Communication Norms And Tools

The mechanics of communication, how often, through what channel, with what response time, are mundane but consequential. An author who expects same-day email replies and an editor who works in weekly batches will each feel let down by the other. Communication norms should be agreed, not discovered through frustration.

Establish the primary channel for different kinds of communication, queries during editing, broader discussions, urgent issues, and the expected response time for each. Agree on how queries will be delivered and tracked, whether in-document comments, a separate query log, or a shared tool, so that nothing is lost. Clarify working hours and time zones, especially for asynchronous collaboration across regions, so that neither party expects responses at unreasonable times. For long projects, schedule regular check-ins so issues surface in conversation rather than accumulating. Communication norms that match both parties' realities prevent the slow erosion of trust that comes from feeling ignored or overwhelmed.

### Document The Agreement And Revisit It

Onboarding conversations are valuable only if their content survives the passage of time and the pressure of work. Memory is unreliable, circumstances change, and an agreement that exists only in a conversation will be contested or forgotten when it matters most. Documentation converts a good start into a durable framework.

Record the key agreements, scope, deliverables, timeline, decision rights, and communication norms, in a brief written form that both parties can reference. This need not be a formal contract; a shared summary email or project brief often suffices. Refer back to it when the project changes, since scope, timeline, or process shifts should prompt a deliberate revision of the agreement rather than silent drift. Treat the documented agreement as a living framework, not a one-time artifact, and revisit it at milestones or when friction arises. The value of onboarding is not the initial conversation but the shared reference it creates for the work that follows.

## Common Traps

### Skipping The Relationship Conversation

Assuming alignment on roles and norms leads to collision later. Establish the relationship explicitly before editing begins.

### Leaving Scope Implicit

"Edit my manuscript" means different things to each party. Define scope, depth, and deliverables in writing.

### Optimistic Timelines Neither Party Believes

Unrealistic schedules guarantee disappointment. Build the timeline from real availability and dependencies.

### Unstated Decision Rights

Ambiguity about who decides what creates friction on every judgment call. Clarify authority and change visibility.

### Mismatched Communication Expectations

Different channels and response times erode trust silently. Agree on norms that match both parties' realities.

### Onboarding Treated As A One-Time Event

Circumstances change and agreements drift. Revisit and revise the documented framework at milestones.

### Assuming Prior Editorial Experience Generalizes

An author's past editors shape their expectations. Ask about good and bad experiences to calibrate your approach.

### Scope Drift Addressed Too Late

Silent expansion of scope creates resentment and overwork. Name scope changes explicitly and renegotiate.

## Self-Check

Before treating author onboarding and scoping as complete, verify:

- An explicit conversation about the working relationship, roles, and editing philosophy has occurred, not only a discussion of the work itself.
- The author's prior editorial experiences and preferences have been understood and used to calibrate the approach.
- Scope, deliverables, and depth of edit are defined explicitly and in writing, including what is not in scope.
- A realistic timeline has been built from actual availability and dependencies, with interim milestones for long projects.
- Decision rights are clarified, including what the editor may change directly, what must be queried, and where the author holds final authority.
- Communication norms, channels, response times, and query tracking, are agreed and match both parties' working realities.
- The key agreements are documented in a form both parties can reference and revisit.
- The agreement is treated as a living framework, with planned revisits at milestones or when scope and circumstances change.
- Both parties enter the substantive work with shared expectations that can resolve later disagreements by reference rather than renegotiation.
