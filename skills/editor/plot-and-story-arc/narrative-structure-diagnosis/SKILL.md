# Narrative Structure Diagnosis

Narrative structure is the framework that organizes a story: how events are sequenced, how time is handled, how scenes and chapters connect, and how the overall arc builds. Structure is not just the order of events but the architecture that gives a story its shape and momentum. A weak structure produces a story that sags, confuses, or fails to build, even when individual scenes are well written. Narrative structure diagnosis is the discipline of evaluating whether a story's framework serves its purpose. Editors who focus on prose without examining structure miss the architectural problems that underlie engagement failures.

Use this skill when editing fiction or narrative nonfiction to diagnose narrative structure. It covers evaluating the structural framework, sequencing and pacing, and identifying structural weaknesses. The goal is a story whose structure supports its purpose and sustains reader engagement.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Structural Framework

The first step is identifying the structural framework the story uses or aspires to. Structures vary: three-act, five-act, non-linear, frame story, in medias res, episodic, circular. Identifying the framework provides a lens for evaluation. Identify the framework.

Determine what structural framework the story employs. This may be explicit, as in a clearly three-act structure, or implicit, emerging from how events are arranged. Consider whether the framework is conventional or experimental, and whether it is consistently applied. Identifying the framework is not about forcing conformity to a template but about understanding the architecture the author has chosen, so you can evaluate whether it works. A story may use a hybrid or unconventional structure; the goal is to understand what it is trying to do structurally, then assess whether it succeeds.

### Evaluate Whether The Structure Serves The Story's Purpose

A structure is good or bad relative to what the story is trying to do. A structure that serves a thriller's suspense may not serve a literary character study. Evaluate fit between structure and purpose.

Consider what the story is trying to achieve: suspense, emotional depth, thematic exploration, intellectual provocation, entertainment. Assess whether the structure supports that purpose. Does the sequencing build the intended effect? Does the pacing sustain the intended engagement? Does the framework highlight what matters? A structure that works against the story's purpose, such as a slow, digressive structure for a story meant to be thrilling, is a structural problem even if it is well executed. The standard is fitness for purpose, not adherence to convention.

### Assess Event Sequencing And Causality

Story events should be sequenced in a way that builds meaning and momentum, with clear causal connections that make each event feel necessary rather than arbitrary. Assess sequencing and causality.

Trace the sequence of events. Does each event follow logically and causally from what came before, or do events feel arbitrary or coincidental? Strong narratives have a sense of causality, where one thing leads to the next, building momentum and meaning. Weak narratives string together incidents without causal connection, producing a flat or random feel. Assess whether the sequencing reveals information at the right pace, withholding and revealing to build interest. Flag sequences where causality is weak, where events feel convenient rather than earned, or where the order obscures rather than enhances the story.

### Evaluate Pacing And Momentum

Pacing is the rhythm at which the story unfolds: fast or slow, intense or reflective. Momentum is the forward pull that keeps readers reading. Both are structural, determined by scene length, event density, and information release. Evaluate pacing.

Assess the story's pacing across its arc. Does it vary appropriately, with faster and slower passages, or is it monotonous? Does the pacing match the content, with intensity in action and reflection in quieter moments? Does the story build momentum, pulling the reader forward, or does it stall in sections that drag? Common pacing problems include a slow opening that fails to hook, a sagging middle where momentum is lost, and a rushed ending that compresses what should be developed. Pacing diagnosis identifies where the story moves well and where it bogs down.

### Check Scene And Chapter Architecture

Scenes and chapters are the building blocks of structure. Each should have a purpose, advance the story, and connect to what surrounds it. Weak scenes or chapters weaken the whole. Check architecture.

Evaluate each scene and chapter for purpose and contribution. Does it advance the plot, develop character, build theme, or some combination? Scenes that accomplish nothing, that repeat what other scenes have done, or that exist without clear purpose are structural dead weight. Check transitions between scenes and chapters: do they connect, or are there gaps or jumps that confuse? Assess whether scene and chapter lengths serve their content. Scene and chapter architecture is where macro structure becomes micro execution; problems here manifest as a story that feels episodic, disjointed, or padded.

### Evaluate The Opening And Hook

The opening is where readers decide whether to continue. It must establish the story, introduce what matters, and create enough interest to pull the reader in. A weak opening loses readers before the story finds its footing. Evaluate the opening.

Assess the opening's effectiveness. Does it begin at the right point, with enough happening to engage, or does it start too slowly with backstory or setup? Does it establish the protagonist, world, and stakes efficiently? Does it create a question or tension that pulls the reader forward? Common opening problems include starting too early with exposition, starting with a scene that does not represent the story, or burying the hook under information. The opening need not be action; it needs to be engaging. Evaluate whether the opening earns the reader's commitment to continue.

### Evaluate The Ending And Resolution

The ending is where the story's structure pays off. It must resolve the central tension, deliver on what the structure built, and leave the reader satisfied, whether the ending is happy, tragic, or ambiguous. Evaluate the ending.

Assess the ending's effectiveness. Does it resolve the story's central question or conflict? Does it feel earned, growing from the structure and events, or does it feel arbitrary or unearned? Does it deliver emotional or thematic payoff? Common ending problems include resolutions that come too easily, endings that introduce new elements rather than resolving existing ones, and ambiguity that feels evasive rather than meaningful. The ending should fulfill the promise the structure made; an ending that betrays that promise, even if well written, leaves readers unsatisfied.

## Common Traps

### Ignoring Structure While Polishing Prose

Structure underlies engagement. Diagnose it, not just the sentences.

### Forcing A Conventional Template

Evaluate fitness for purpose, not conformity to a formula.

### Arbitrary Or Coincidental Event Sequencing

Events should feel causally connected and necessary, not random.

### Pacing Problems: Slow Open, Sagging Middle, Rushed End

Assess pacing across the arc. Identify where momentum stalls.

### Purposeless Scenes Or Chapters

Each unit should advance the story. Cut or revise dead weight.

### Openings That Fail To Hook

Start where engagement begins. Avoid burying the hook in exposition.

### Endings That Feel Unearned Or Evasive

Resolve the central tension through the structure built, not convenience.

## Self-Check

Before treating narrative structure diagnosis as complete, verify:

- The structural framework has been identified, whether conventional or experimental.
- The structure's fitness for the story's purpose has been assessed.
- Event sequencing has been traced for causality, momentum, and meaning.
- Pacing has been evaluated across the arc for variation and forward pull.
- Each scene and chapter has been assessed for purpose, contribution, and connection.
- The opening has been evaluated for its ability to hook and establish efficiently.
- The ending has been evaluated for earned resolution and payoff.
- Structural weaknesses, such as slow openings, sagging middles, or rushed endings, are identified.
- The diagnosis distinguishes structural problems from prose problems.
- Recommendations address the architecture, not just the surface.
