# Fair Representation

Citing a source accurately word for word is not the same as representing it fairly. A draft can quote correctly and still mislead by selecting the damaging passage while omitting the qualification, by framing a tentative source as confident, by stripping context that changes meaning, or by presenting a source's words in service of a claim the source would reject. Fair representation is the editorial discipline of ensuring that sources and subjects appear in a draft as they would recognize themselves, with their meaning, emphasis, and intent preserved. Editors who check only for accurate quotation but not for fair representation pass along work that is technically correct and substantively dishonest.

Use this skill when editing any work that draws on sources, quotes people, or characterizes positions, including reportage, nonfiction, academic, polemic, and instructional writing. It covers detecting selective quotation and cherry-picking, verifying that framing matches source intent, checking context preservation, and ensuring subjects are represented as they would recognize. The editor's job is to ensure the work does not distort the material it relies on, even inadvertently.

## Core Rules

### Check That Quotation Preserves Context And Meaning

A quotation removed from its context can mean something entirely different from what the speaker or author intended. The same words, in a different surrounding, can reverse implication. Verify that every quotation preserves the context that gives it its meaning.

For each significant quotation, ask what surrounds it in the original. Is the speaker being ironic, hypothetical, conditional, or quoting someone else? Does the preceding or following text qualify, limit, or reverse the quoted passage? Does the quotation omit a hedge, exception, or counterpoint that the source included? Flag quotations whose meaning shifts when context is restored, and recommend either restoring the context, marking the omission, or replacing the quotation. A quotation that reads differently in context than in the draft is a misrepresentation regardless of word accuracy.

### Detect Selective And Cherry-Picked Evidence

Selective quotation and cherry-picked evidence present a source as supporting a position by featuring the favorable parts and suppressing the unfavorable. The result is a portrait of the source that its author would not recognize. Detect this pattern, especially in polemic and advocacy writing.

Compare the draft's use of a source against the source as a whole. Does the draft feature the source's conclusions while omitting its caveats? Does it cite the data that supports its claim while ignoring the data the same source offers against it? Does it present a contested finding as if the source treated it as settled? Flag selective use and recommend either balanced representation or honest acknowledgment that the source is more nuanced than the draft suggests. Cherry-picking is detectable to readers who know the source and devastating to credibility when discovered.

### Verify That Framing Matches Source Intent

How a source is framed, the surrounding language that introduces and characterizes it, shapes how readers receive it. A neutral finding can be framed as alarming, a tentative conclusion as confident, a qualified claim as absolute. Verify that framing matches the source's own intent and emphasis.

Read the framing language around each source use. Does the draft's characterization match how the source presents itself? Does the draft impose an interpretation the source does not claim? Does it attribute certainty the source hedges, or hedge a claim the source asserts? Flag framing that diverges from source intent and recommend alignment. Honest framing lets the source speak on its own terms, even when the writer disagrees with it.

### Ensure Subjects Would Recognize Themselves

A strong test of fair representation is whether the subject, the person, group, or source being characterized, would recognize the portrait as accurate and fair, even if they disagree with the writer's conclusions. They need not agree with the interpretation, but they should not feel distorted or misrepresented. Apply this test to characterizations of people and positions.

Ask whether a reasonable member of the characterized group would accept the representation as fair. Would they recognize their views, their words, their actions, and their context as portrayed? If the portrait is one they would experience as a caricature, a distortion, or a hit, the representation likely fails fairness even if no individual fact is wrong. Flag representations that fail the recognition test and recommend adjustment toward fair portrayal.

### Watch For False Balance And Both-Sides Distortion

Fair representation is not the same as equal representation. Presenting every position as equally supported, regardless of evidence, creates false balance that misleads readers about where the weight of evidence lies. Conversely, presenting only one side as if no serious opposition exists misleads by omission. Judge representation against the actual state of evidence, not against a false evenhandedness.

When a draft presents opposing views, check whether the representation reflects their actual evidentiary weight. A fringe position presented alongside a well-supported one as equals distorts the landscape. A well-supported position dismissed without engagement distorts by omission. Recommend representation that matches where evidence and expert consensus actually stand, while still acknowledging genuine uncertainty or contestation where it exists.

### Check Attribution Of Motives And Intent

A draft that attributes motives, intentions, or beliefs to sources or subjects takes on a representational burden. Claiming to know why someone did something, what they really believe, or what they intend requires evidence, not inference. Verify that motive attribution is supported rather than assumed.

Flag passages that assert motives or intentions without evidence, that present speculation about intent as fact, or that attribute the worst possible interpretation to ambiguous actions. Recommend either supporting the motive claim with evidence, softening it to what is demonstrable, or removing it. Unfair motive attribution is a common form of misrepresentation that reads as analysis but is actually assertion.

### Preserve Qualifications And Uncertainty

Sources often include qualifications, hedges, and acknowledgments of uncertainty. A draft that strips these to present a cleaner, stronger claim misrepresents the source's actual position. Preserve the qualifications that affect how the claim should be received.

Check whether the draft retains the source's hedges, scope limits, and uncertainty acknowledgments, or whether it has converted a tentative finding into a confident one, a limited claim into a general one, a correlation into a causation. Flag stripped qualifications and recommend restoring them. Honest representation includes the source's own sense of what it does and does not establish.

## Common Traps

### Equating Accurate Quotation With Fair Representation

Correct words in misleading context still misrepresent. Check context, not just wording.

### Cherry-Picking The Favorable Parts Of A Source

Featuring support while suppressing caveats distorts the source. Represent balance.

### Imposing Framing The Source Would Reject

Characterizing a source against its own intent misleads readers. Align framing with source emphasis.

### False Balance Treating Unequal Positions As Equal

Equal representation of unequal evidence misleads. Match representation to evidentiary weight.

### Asserting Motives Without Evidence

Attributing intent without support is assertion posing as analysis. Require evidence or soften.

### Stripping Qualifications For A Cleaner Claim

Removing hedges and scope limits misrepresents the source's actual position. Preserve uncertainty.

### Dismissing The Recognition Test

If subjects would not recognize themselves, representation likely fails fairness. Apply the test.

## Self-Check

Before treating fair representation review as complete, verify:

- Each significant quotation has been checked for whether its context is preserved and meaning intact.
- Selective quotation and cherry-picked evidence have been detected by comparing draft use to the source as a whole.
- Framing language around sources matches the sources' own intent and emphasis.
- Characterizations of people and positions pass the recognition test, that subjects would see them as fair.
- Representation of opposing views reflects actual evidentiary weight, avoiding both false balance and one-sided omission.
- Attribution of motives and intentions is supported by evidence rather than assumed.
- Qualifications, hedges, and uncertainty acknowledgments from sources have been preserved, not stripped.
- No source is made to support a position it would reject through selective use or misleading framing.
- The work represents its sources and subjects as they would recognize themselves.
- Fairness has been judged against the actual state of evidence, not against convenient distortion or false evenhandedness.
