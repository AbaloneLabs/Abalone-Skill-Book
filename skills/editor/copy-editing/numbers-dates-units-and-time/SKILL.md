# Numbers Dates Units And Time

Numbers, dates, times, and units are the most fact-like elements in any text, and they are where copy-editing most directly protects accuracy. Unlike prose, where a awkward phrase merely reads poorly, a wrong number, a misformatted date, an inconsistent unit, or a confused time zone can mislead readers, break schedules, cause financial or safety errors, and destroy credibility. These elements follow strict conventions that vary by style guide, region, and field, and they are easy to get subtly wrong: a numeral where a word belongs, an ambiguous date format, a unit without its symbol, a range with the wrong separator, a time without a zone. Copy-editing for numbers and temporal elements is the discipline of applying the right convention consistently, verifying that each value is internally coherent, and catching the errors that readers and downstream systems will notice. It is meticulous work, and its failures are disproportionately visible.

Use this skill at the copy-editing stage, when the task is to govern the treatment of numerical and temporal elements. It covers numeral-versus-word rules, date and time formatting, units and measurement, ranges and quantities, currency, and consistency across the work. The editor's task is to make every number, date, and unit correct, consistent, and unambiguous, recorded in the style sheet so the choices hold across the work and across contributors.

## Core Rules

### Apply Numeral-And-Word Rules Per The Style Guide

Style guides prescribe when to use numerals and when to spell out numbers, and the rules differ enough that consistency requires following one guide throughout. Common patterns include spelling out numbers below ten and using numerals for ten and above, but exceptions abound for specific contexts, and different guides draw the line differently.

Determine the work's governing guide and apply its numeral rules consistently. Watch the common edge cases: numbers at the start of a sentence, which most guides require spelled out; large round numbers, which may be spelled or numeral depending on the guide; numbers in a series where consistency within the series matters; and contexts like technical or scientific writing where numerals are used more liberally. Record the threshold and exceptions in the style sheet. Inconsistency in numeral treatment reads as carelessness even when each instance is individually defensible.

### Standardize Date And Time Formats And Avoid Ambiguity

Date formats vary by region, and ambiguity here causes real confusion and missed deadlines. "03/04/2025" means March 4 in the US and April 3 in much of the rest of the world. Times without zones leave the reader guessing. Copy-editing must standardize formats and eliminate ambiguity, especially in any work crossing regions.

Choose the work's date format per the style guide and apply it consistently, preferring unambiguous formats like "4 March 2025" or ISO 8601 "2025-03-04" where international clarity matters. For times, include the time zone where relevant, and standardize its format, UTC, GMT, ET, PT, and whether it is labeled. Watch for inconsistent formats across the work and for dates that are internally contradictory, an event dated differently in two places. Record the chosen formats in the style sheet.

### Handle Units Of Measurement Consistently And Correctly

Units carry meaning and getting them wrong, using the wrong unit, mixing systems, omitting symbols, or formatting them inconsistently, can mislead readers and in technical or safety contexts cause harm. A dose in mg versus g, a distance in miles versus kilometers, a temperature in Celsius versus Fahrenheit, are not interchangeable.

Apply the unit conventions appropriate to the field and audience. In scientific and technical work, use SI units with correct symbols and formatting, including spacing between number and unit. For general audiences, match the region's customary system but be consistent. Where units are converted, verify the conversion and consider noting the original. Never let a quantity appear without its unit where the unit is needed for meaning. Check that unit symbols are correct, capitalized properly, and consistent throughout.

### Format Ranges, Quantities, And Ordinals Per Convention

Ranges, approximate quantities, and ordinals have conventional treatments that vary by guide, and inconsistency here is common. Ranges may use en dashes, hyphens, or the word "to." Approximate quantities may use "about," "approximately," "around," or a tilde, and redundancy like "approximately about" is an error. Ordinals have spelling and numeral conventions.

Apply the guide's conventions for ranges, using the correct dash and deciding whether to space it. Standardize approximate-quantity language and eliminate redundant hedges. Apply ordinal conventions, spelling out or using numerals per the guide and context. These seem minor, but their inconsistency accumulates into a careless impression, and range errors, a hyphen where an en dash belongs or a reversed range, can mislead.

### Verify Numerical Coherence And Cross-Check Totals

Numbers within a work should be internally coherent, and copy-editing can catch errors by cross-checking. If percentages in a breakdown should total one hundred, they should. If a total is given, the components should add up. If a quantity is stated in one place and restated in another, they should match. These checks catch errors that reading alone misses.

Where numbers relate to each other, check the relationships. Sum components against totals. Check that percentages are reasonable and that percentage versus percentage-point distinctions are correct. Compare repeated numbers across the work for consistency. Where a number appears wrong but you cannot verify it, flag it for fact-checking rather than silently correcting. Internal coherence checks are a powerful copy-editing tool because they catch errors without external sources.

### Distinguish Percentages, Percentage Points, And Ratios

Percentages and percentage points are commonly confused, and the error changes meaning. A rise from 10 percent to 15 percent is a 5 percentage-point increase, not a 5 percent increase; it is a 50 percent relative increase. Ratios, rates, and multiples have their own conventions and are easy to misstate.

Check each quantitative claim for the correct form. Where a change is described, confirm whether it is in percentage points or relative percentage and that the wording matches. Where ratios or rates are used, confirm they are stated correctly and consistently. These distinctions matter in any quantitative context and especially in data-heavy or financial work, where a confused percentage can materially mislead.

### Govern Currency Treatment

Currency has formatting conventions, symbol placement, decimal usage, and codes, that vary by currency and region, and inconsistency or error here can confuse readers and cause transaction mistakes. Amounts in different currencies must be distinguished, and conversions should be handled carefully.

Apply the currency conventions for the work's context: symbol versus code, decimal places, thousands separators, and symbol placement. Where multiple currencies appear, distinguish them clearly, using ISO codes where ambiguity is possible. For converted amounts, verify the conversion, note the rate or date if relevant, and avoid implying false precision. Record currency conventions in the style sheet.

### Record All Numerical And Temporal Decisions In The Style Sheet

Because numerical and temporal conventions have so many variables and exceptions, the style sheet is essential for consistency across the work and across contributors. Every threshold, format choice, and exception should be recorded so later edits and other editors apply the same standards.

Build the numerical and temporal section of the style sheet as you edit: numeral threshold and exceptions, date and time formats, unit system and conventions, range and ordinal treatment, currency conventions, and any field-specific rules. The style sheet turns isolated decisions into a coherent system and prevents the drift that produces inconsistency in the most fact-sensitive elements of the work.

## Common Traps

### Inconsistent Numeral-And-Word Treatment

Switching between numerals and words reads as carelessness. Apply the guide's threshold consistently and record it.

### Ambiguous Date And Time Formats

Regional date ambiguity and missing time zones cause real confusion. Use unambiguous formats and label zones.

### Wrong Or Inconsistent Units

Unit errors mislead and can harm. Apply correct symbols, match the system to the audience, and never omit needed units.

### Misformatted Ranges And Redundant Hedges

Range separators and approximate-quantity language vary by guide. Standardize and eliminate redundancy.

### Numbers That Don't Add Up

Internal incoherence, percentages that don't total, restatements that conflict, signals errors. Cross-check relationships.

### Confusing Percentages And Percentage Points

A 5 percentage-point increase is not a 5 percent increase. Confirm the form matches the meaning.

### Inconsistent Currency Formatting

Symbol placement, decimals, and codes vary. Distinguish currencies and verify conversions.

### Editing Numbers Without A Style Sheet

Untracked numerical decisions drift into inconsistency. Record all conventions for consistency and recoverability.

## Self-Check

Before treating numbers, dates, units, and time editing as complete, verify:

- Numeral-and-word treatment follows the style guide consistently, with thresholds and exceptions recorded.
- Date and time formats are standardized and unambiguous, with time zones labeled where relevant.
- Units of measurement are correct, consistently formatted, and matched to the field and audience.
- Ranges, approximate quantities, and ordinals follow convention, with redundancy eliminated.
- Numerical coherence has been checked, with components summing to totals and repeated numbers consistent.
- Percentages, percentage points, ratios, and rates are distinguished and stated correctly.
- Currency is formatted consistently, with currencies distinguished and conversions verified.
- All numerical and temporal decisions are recorded in the style sheet for consistency across the work.
- Every number, date, and unit in the work is correct, consistent, and unambiguous.
