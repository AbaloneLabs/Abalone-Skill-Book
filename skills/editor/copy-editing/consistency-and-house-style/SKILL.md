# Consistency And House Style

Consistency is what makes a publication feel like a single, trustworthy source rather than a patchwork. Readers may not know the rules, but they notice when names are spelled two ways, when numbers shift between figures and words, when headings follow different capitalization patterns, and when one chapter feels like a different publication from the next. House style is the system that prevents this. Enforcing it is editorial work that requires judgment, because style guides cannot anticipate every case and because rigid application sometimes conflicts with clarity, accuracy, or the writer's voice.

Use this skill when copy-editing or production-editing to enforce consistency and house style across a work or publication. It covers building and maintaining a style sheet, resolving the treatment of recurring elements, harmonizing across contributors, applying house style without erasing voice, and deciding when to override the guide. The editor's task is to make the work read as coherent and professional while preserving what makes each piece, and each writer, distinct.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Style Sheet As The Source Of Truth

A style guide, whether a published manual or an in-house document, gives default rules. A style sheet records how those rules are applied in this specific work, including all the cases the guide does not cover and all the deliberate deviations. The style sheet is what actually governs consistency.

Start or update the style sheet at the beginning of the project and maintain it throughout. Record the governing guide and dictionary, then log every decision: spelling variants, hyphenation, capitalization, number treatment, date and time formats, names and titles, recurring terms, abbreviations, and any deviations from the guide with their rationale. The style sheet turns isolated judgments into a coherent system, prevents drift, and lets anyone working on the project apply the same standards. A work edited without a maintained style sheet will be inconsistent, no matter how careful the editor.

### Resolve The Treatment Of Recurring Elements Systematically

Names, terms, numbers, dates, measurements, and other recurring elements are where inconsistency is most visible. Each must be resolved once and applied everywhere.

For names, determine the canonical form of people, organizations, places, and works, including capitalization, spacing, and diacritics, and apply it consistently, noting variants only where context requires. For terms, decide on preferred forms and avoid unhelpful synonym-swapping, especially in technical or legal writing where consistency aids comprehension. For numbers, follow the guide's threshold for numerals versus words and apply it uniformly. For dates and times, choose a format and stick to it. Resolve each category as a system, not case by case, and record the system in the style sheet.

### Harmonize Across Contributors Without Erasing Voice

Multi-author works and publications face a double challenge: the whole must feel consistent, but each contributor's voice must survive. Harmonizing toward house style can slide into homogenizing toward house voice, which strips the distinctiveness that justified multiple contributors in the first place.

Apply house style to the elements that should be uniform: mechanics, formatting, term treatment, structural conventions, and citation style. Preserve the elements that constitute voice: sentence rhythm, word choice within standard usage, stance toward the reader, and characteristic phrasing. Distinguish a style requirement, which overrides voice, from a style preference, which should yield. When in doubt, enforce consistency on surfaces the reader experiences as the publication's, and preserve variation on surfaces the reader experiences as the author's.

### Apply House Style With Judgment, Not Mechanically

Style guides are defaults, not commandments. They cannot anticipate every case, and rigid application sometimes produces awkward, ambiguous, or inaccurate results. The editor must apply the guide with judgment, knowing when to follow, when to interpret, and when to override.

Follow the guide as the default and document deviations. Override when the guide's rule creates ambiguity, conflicts with accuracy, contradicts an established convention in the work's field, or produces results that confuse the target reader. When overriding, record the decision and rationale in the style sheet so it is applied consistently and can be defended. Mechanical application of a guide produces consistency of a sort, but judgment produces consistency that serves the reader.

### Distinguish Style From Substance And From Voice

Three things can look alike but require different treatment: a style question, a substantive question, and a voice question. Conflating them leads to over-editing or under-editing.

A style question concerns consistent treatment of mechanics and formatting, such as whether to use the serial comma; this is the copy-editor's to decide per the guide. A substantive question concerns meaning, accuracy, or logic, such as whether a number is correct or a claim is supported; this may require the writer's or fact-checker's input. A voice question concerns the writer's distinctive choices, such as a characteristic sentence shape; this should usually be preserved. Sort each issue into the right category before acting, and route substantive questions to the right authority rather than resolving them as if they were style.

### Manage Capitalization And Heading Conventions Consistently

Capitalization is a frequent source of inconsistency, especially in headings, subheadings, titles, and specialized terms. Inconsistent capitalization reads as carelessness and can even create confusion about whether a term is a proper noun.

Choose a capitalization style for headings and titles, typically title case or sentence case, and apply it consistently with a defined rule for minor words. For specialized terms, decide which are capitalized and apply uniformly, recognizing that over-capitalization in some fields and under-capitalization in others are both conventions. For labels, buttons, and UI elements in technical or product writing, match the actual interface. Record all decisions, because capitalization cases multiply and memory is unreliable.

### Handle Citation And Reference Style Uniformly

Citation style is a high-visibility consistency surface in academic, scientific, and reported work. Inconsistent citations signal carelessness and can cause real problems for readers trying to locate sources.

Follow the required citation style exactly, including author name format, date placement, title capitalization, italicization, punctuation, and ordering. Apply it to in-text citations, reference lists, footnotes, and bibliographies uniformly. For works with multiple source types, ensure each is formatted per the style's rules for that type. Do not invent hybrid forms or apply one style's conventions within another. Where a source does not fit a standard pattern, find the style's guidance for unusual sources or follow the closest analogy consistently.

### Coordinate Cross-References And Internal Links

Cross-references to chapters, sections, figures, tables, and notes must remain accurate and consistent, and they break easily during revision. A reference to "Chapter 4" that now points to Chapter 5 because of reorganization is a consistency failure with real consequences.

Track all internal references and update them whenever structure changes. Decide on a consistent reference style, such as "Chapter 4" versus "chapter 4" versus "the next chapter," and apply it. For figures, tables, and notes, ensure numbering is sequential and references match. In digital works, check that links resolve and that anchor text is consistent. Cross-reference errors are easy to miss because they look fine in isolation; check them as a system.

### Reconcile Display Text With Body Text

Headlines, subheads, captions, pull quotes, callouts, and tables carry disproportionate weight because readers scan them first. Inconsistency or error in display text undermines the whole work's credibility, even when the body is clean.

Check display text separately and as a system. Ensure headlines and subheads follow the capitalization and formatting conventions, that captions accurately describe their images or figures, that pull quotes are verbatim from the body, and that table and figure labels match their references. Display text often survives from early drafts while the body is revised; reconcile the two so the surfaces readers trust most are correct.

## Common Traps

### Editing Without A Maintained Style Sheet

Untracked decisions drift into inconsistency. Build and maintain the style sheet throughout.

### Case-By-Case Instead Of Systematic Resolution

Recurring elements resolved ad hoc produce inconsistency. Resolve each category as a system.

### Harmonizing Into House Voice

Enforcing style on voice features strips distinctiveness. Apply style to surfaces, preserve voice.

### Mechanical Guide Application

Rigid rule-following can produce awkward or ambiguous results. Apply with judgment and document overrides.

### Conflating Style, Substance, And Voice

Treating a substantive or voice question as a style question causes over- or under-editing. Sort each issue first.

### Inconsistent Capitalization

Headings, titles, and terms drift. Choose conventions and apply them uniformly.

### Hybrid Or Sloppy Citations

Inconsistent citations signal carelessness. Follow the required style exactly across all source types.

### Stale Cross-References and unreconciled Display Text

Structural changes break internal references. Track and update them as a system.

Headlines and captions that survived from early drafts undermine credibility. Check display text separately.

## Self-Check

Before treating consistency and house style enforcement as complete, verify:

- A style sheet has been maintained throughout, recording the governing guide, dictionary, and all decisions with rationale.
- Recurring elements, names, terms, numbers, dates, and measurements, have been resolved as systems and applied everywhere.
- Contributors have been harmonized on surfaces without erasing voice on authorial choices.
- House style has been applied with judgment, with overrides documented and defensible.
- Each issue has been sorted into style, substance, or voice before action, and substantive questions routed appropriately.
- Capitalization of headings, titles, and specialized terms follows a consistent, recorded convention.
- Citation and reference style is applied exactly and uniformly across all source types and locations.
- Cross-references and internal links are accurate, sequentially numbered, and updated after any structural change.
- Display text, headlines, captions, pull quotes, and labels, has been reconciled with body text and checked as a system.
- The work reads as a single, coherent, professional publication while preserving the distinctiveness of its contributors; a reader scanning surfaces would find no unexplained inconsistency
