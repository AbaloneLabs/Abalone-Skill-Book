# Grammar And Mechanics Correction

Grammar and mechanics are the load-bearing conventions that let prose carry meaning without friction. Copy-editing for them is not pedantry; it is the work of removing the small failures that distract readers, undermine credibility, and create ambiguity. But it is also work that can destroy voice if applied without judgment. A copy-editor who "corrects" every fragment, every dialect form, and every deliberate departure from standard usage turns distinctive prose into generic prose. The craft is correcting what is genuinely wrong while preserving what is deliberately right.

Use this skill at the copy-editing stage, after structure and line are stable, when the task is to enforce grammar, spelling, punctuation, and mechanics consistently. It covers applying rules without erasing voice, handling agreement and tense consistency, managing punctuation for clarity, dealing with variant spellings and conventions, and knowing when a rule should yield. The editor's task is to make the prose mechanically clean and trustworthy without flattening it.

## Core Rules

### Correct Error, Preserve Choice

The foundational distinction in mechanics editing is between genuine error and deliberate choice. Error is unintended departure from convention that distracts or confuses. Choice is intentional departure that constitutes voice, emphasis, or style. The same surface feature can be either.

A fragment in a thriller's action scene is choice; a fragment in a legal contract is error. Nonstandard dialect in a memoir is choice; subject-verb disagreement in a formal report is error. Before correcting, ask whether the feature is a deliberate effect serving the work. Correct error automatically; preserve choice, or query if you are unsure. Mechanics editing that cannot tell the difference will sand away the very texture that makes the prose worth reading.

### Apply Rules Consistently Within The Work

Consistency is the copy-editor's primary obligation after correctness. A work that switches between spellings, capitalizations, or punctuation styles within itself reads as careless even when each instance is individually defensible. Internal consistency often matters more than which convention is chosen.

Choose a standard for each variable, the style guide's, the house's, or the work's own established pattern, and apply it throughout. Track decisions in a style sheet so they are applied consistently and can be defended. When a writer has already established a consistent pattern that deviates from the guide, weigh whether to override the guide for consistency or to align the work to the guide; document the decision either way. Inconsistency is the failure mode mechanics editing exists to prevent.

### Handle Subject-Verb And Pronoun Agreement Carefully

Agreement errors are common and often subtle, especially with complex subjects, collective nouns, indefinite pronouns, and intervening phrases that distance the verb from its subject. These errors create momentary confusion and signal carelessness.

Check agreement in sentences where the subject and verb are separated, where the subject is a collective noun whose number depends on meaning, where indefinite pronouns like each or none appear, and where compound subjects are joined by and, or, or nor. For pronouns, check antecedent clarity and agreement, especially with singular they, collective antecedents, and ambiguous references. Where a construction is genuinely ambiguous, revise for clarity rather than picking a reading silently.

### Manage Tense, Mood, And Sequence Consistently

Tense shifts that are not deliberate create confusion about when things happen and undermine credibility. Sequence of tenses in reported speech and conditional constructions is a frequent source of error that careful writers still miss.

Check that the work maintains a consistent tense unless a shift is deliberate, such as moving between narrative past and present analysis. In reported speech and thought, ensure the sequence of tenses is logical, that a present statement reported in past narrative shifts appropriately. Watch for the subjunctive in counterfactuals and wishes, where using the indicative is a common error. Where the writer shifts tense for effect, confirm the shift reads as deliberate rather than accidental.

### Punctuate For Clarity, Not Just For Rule-Following

Punctuation governs how the reader parses sentences. Correct punctuation prevents misreading; mechanical punctuation that follows rules but obscures meaning has failed. The copy-editor should punctuate so the reader groups the sentence the way the writer intends.

Pay attention to commas around introductory and concluding elements, around nonrestrictive versus restrictive clauses, in series where the final comma changes meaning, and around parentheticals. Check that dashes, parentheses, and commas signal the right level of interruption. Ensure colons and semicolons join elements that genuinely belong at those weights. When a sentence is technically punctuated but still misreads, revise the punctuation or the syntax until the meaning is unambiguous.

### Resolve Spelling And Variant Conventions Deliberately

Spelling seems simple but is full of judgment calls: variant spellings, regional differences, compound words, hyphenation, capitalization of specialized terms, and the treatment of numbers. Each must be resolved consistently.

Follow the chosen style guide and dictionary, and where they permit variants, choose one and apply it throughout. For regional variants, match the work's intended audience. For compounds and hyphenation, apply the guide's rules and record decisions for terms that recur. For capitalization, distinguish proper nouns from common usage and apply title case or sentence case per the work's convention. For numbers, follow the guide's threshold for numerals versus words. Record all decisions in the style sheet so the work stays consistent and the reasoning is recoverable.

### Handle Quotations And Dialogue Mechanics Correctly

Quoted material and dialogue have their own mechanics, and errors here are both common and consequential because they affect attribution and accuracy. Misplaced quotation marks, incorrect comma placement with tags, and altered quotations all create problems.

Ensure quotation marks enclose exactly what was said or written, with no words silently added or dropped except where marked with brackets or ellipses. Place commas and periods inside or outside quotation marks per the relevant convention, consistently. Format dialogue tags, action beats, and internal thought per convention. Do not "correct" the grammar or mechanics inside a quotation; quoted material is fixed and must be reproduced accurately, with errors marked [sic] only where necessary.

### Know When A Rule Should Yield

Rules are defaults, not absolute commands. Sometimes strict application of a rule produces awkward, ambiguous, or voice-destroying prose, and the rule should yield to clarity, convention, or the writer's intent. The copy-editor must recognize these cases and yield deliberately.

A rule yields when following it creates ambiguity, when it conflicts with a stronger principle like clarity or accuracy, when it overrides an established voice feature, or when convention in the work's genre or field favors a different usage. Document the yield so it is consistent and defensible. Yielding is not laziness; it is the recognition that mechanics serve meaning, not the reverse.

### Use A Style Sheet To Enforce And Document Decisions and query Rather Than Silently Change High-Stakes Or Ambiguous Material

A style sheet is the copy-editor's memory and authority. It records every variable decision so the work stays consistent and so the editor, the writer, and later stages can see and defend the choices made.

Build the style sheet as you edit, recording spelling variants, hyphenation, capitalization, number treatment, punctuation choices, names and terms, and any rule yields. Note the governing style guide and dictionary. The style sheet turns isolated decisions into a coherent system and prevents the drift that produces inconsistency. For collaborative work, it is also the document that lets others apply the same standards.

Some mechanics-adjacent decisions carry stakes that exceed the copy-editor's authority: a number that may be wrong, a name whose spelling matters, a technical term whose form affects meaning, a quotation that may be misquoted. These should be queried, not silently changed.

When a fact-like element, a proper noun, a technical term, or a quotation looks wrong, flag it for the writer or fact-checker rather than correcting to the apparently right form. Silent correction of high-stakes material can introduce errors and obscure problems that need verification. Reserve silent changes for clear, low-stakes mechanics; query everything where being wrong has consequences.

## Common Traps

### Correcting Choice As Error

Fragments, dialect, and deliberate departures are voice, not mistakes. Distinguish before correcting.

### Inconsistency Within The Work

Switching conventions reads as carelessness. Choose a standard and apply it throughout, tracked in a style sheet.

### Agreement Errors In Complex Sentences

Separated subjects and verbs, collectives, and indefinites hide disagreement. Check carefully.

### Accidental Tense Shifts

Unintended shifts confuse timing. Maintain tense unless the shift is deliberate.

### Rule-Following That Obscures Meaning

Technically correct punctuation that misreads has failed. Punctuate for clarity.

### Silent Correction Of Quotations

Quoted material is fixed. Reproduce accurately and never correct inside quotation marks.

### Rigid Rule Application That Kills Voice

Some rules should yield to clarity, convention, or intent. Recognize and document yields.

### Silent Changes To High-Stakes Material and editing Without A Style Sheet

Numbers, names, terms, and quotations that look wrong should be queried, not silently fixed.

Untracked decisions drift into inconsistency. Build and maintain a style sheet throughout.

## Self-Check

Before treating grammar and mechanics correction as complete, verify:

- Each potential correction has been classified as error or deliberate choice, and only error has been changed automatically.
- A consistent standard has been chosen for every variable and applied throughout, recorded in a style sheet.
- Subject-verb and pronoun agreement have been checked in complex constructions, with ambiguity revised rather than silently resolved.
- Tense, mood, and sequence are consistent unless shifts are deliberate.
- Punctuation serves clarity, with technically correct but misleading constructions revised.
- Spelling, hyphenation, capitalization, and number treatment follow the chosen guide consistently, with variants resolved and recorded.
- Quotations and dialogue are mechanically correct and reproduced accurately, with no silent internal correction.
- Cases where rules yielded to clarity, convention, or voice have been recognized and documented.
- High-stakes or ambiguous material has been queried rather than silently changed.
- A style sheet documents all decisions so the work is consistent and the reasoning recoverable; the prose is mechanically clean and trustworthy while still sounding like its author
