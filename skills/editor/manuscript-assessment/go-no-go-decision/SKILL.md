# Go No Go Decision

The culmination of a manuscript assessment is a decision: take the project on, take it on with conditions, ask for revision and resubmission, or decline. This decision commits budget, schedule, and editorial effort, and it shapes an author's career and a publisher's list. A hedged, vague, or unjustified recommendation wastes the acquisitions conversation and leaves the decision to someone less informed. The go-no-go skill is the editorial discipline of rendering a clear, committed recommendation with explicit conditions and documented reasoning, resisting the temptation to hedge when the evidence points one way. Editors who cannot commit produce assessments that help no one.

Use this skill when finalizing a manuscript assessment into a recommendation for an acquisitions meeting, agent, committee, or author conversation. It covers synthesizing craft, market, and revision judgments into a decision, stating conditions explicitly, committing rather than hedging, and documenting reasoning for the record. The editor's job is to render a defensible judgment that enables action.

## Core Rules

### Synthesize All Judgments Into One Recommendation

A go-no-go decision is not a single impression; it is the synthesis of the craft audit, the market analysis, the revision-distance estimate, the author-capability assessment, and the cost considerations. Each judgment contributes, and the recommendation must account for all of them, including where they conflict.

A manuscript with strong craft but weak market position, or strong market position but fundamental craft flaws, or close revision distance but an unreliable author, presents a mixed picture. The recommendation must weigh these factors and explain how they combine. Do not let one favorable factor override a disqualifying one, and do not let one unfavorable factor kill a project that is strong on the others. Name how the judgments combine and which factors are decisive.

### Use A Small, Clear Set Of Outcomes

The recommendation should fit a recognizable decision framework so the acquisitions conversation can act on it. Use a small, consistent set of outcomes rather than inventing a unique verdict for each project.

A workable set: acquire or commission outright; acquire with stated conditions; revise and resubmit; decline with specific guidance; decline outright. Each outcome implies different next steps and different resource commitments. Map the recommendation to one of these, and resist the urge to create hybrid verdicts that obscure the decision. Clarity of outcome is the point.

### Make Conditions Explicit And Testable

A conditional recommendation is only useful if the conditions are specific enough to evaluate. "Acquire if the author strengthens the argument" is not a condition; it is a hope. State conditions as concrete, testable requirements that, if met, would yield a yes.

Specify what would have to change: a rewritten opening that establishes the thesis by chapter two, a cut subplot, two added chapters of evidence, a tonal recalibration toward the stated audience, a demonstrated revision on a sample chapter. Then judge whether the author is likely to meet the conditions based on demonstrated capability. A conditional yes with conditions the author probably cannot meet is a no in disguise; say so. Conditions exist to make the path to a yes legible, not to defer a decision indefinitely.

### Commit Rather Than Hedge

The most common assessment failure is hedging into meaninglessness. A recommendation that says "promising but needs work" or "could go either way" forces someone else to make the decision you were asked to make. Commit to a recommendation based on the evidence, even under uncertainty.

Commitment does not require false certainty. You can recommend decline while noting genuine strengths, or recommend acquisition while naming real risks. The discipline is to render the best judgment the evidence supports rather than refusing to judge. If the evidence genuinely does not support a confident call, say what additional information would resolve it, but do not use uncertainty as a permanent excuse to avoid deciding. Acquisitions conversations need decisions, not essays.

### Distinguish Decline-With-Guidance From Decline-Outright

Not every decline is the same. A work may be declined because it is fundamentally wrong for the list or market, or it may be declined in its current form while offering the author guidance that could help elsewhere. Distinguish the two and tailor the decline accordingly.

Decline-with-guidance communicates what is strong, what would need to change, and where the author might take the work next, even if not with you. This respects the author's effort and may yield a stronger resubmission or a relationship for a future project. Decline-outright is appropriate when the work is unsuited to your list, the market, or your capacity regardless of revision. Both should be clear; neither should be cruel.

### Judge Author Capability As Part Of The Decision

The manuscript is not the only variable. The author's ability to deliver the required revision, meet deadlines, accept editorial direction, and promote the work significantly affects whether the project lands. Incorporate author capability into the go-no-go judgment.

A strong manuscript from an author who cannot revise to the required level, who has a history of missing deadlines, or who resists editorial input is a higher-risk project than the manuscript alone suggests. Conversely, a promising-but-imperfect manuscript from a capable, responsive author may be a better bet. Weigh capability honestly, even though it feels personal, because it is an editorial risk factor that determines whether the project succeeds in production.

### Factor In Cost And Opportunity Cost

Every acquisition or commission commits resources that cannot go to other projects. A project that is viable in isolation may be the wrong use of limited budget, schedule slots, or editorial capacity. Factor cost and opportunity cost into the recommendation.

Consider the editorial rounds required, production expense, marketing investment, and what the slot would otherwise hold. A project that requires heavy investment for modest return may be viable but unwise. A project that fits the list strategy and uses resources efficiently may be preferable to a stronger manuscript that strains capacity. The recommendation should account for whether the project is the right use of resources, not just whether it could succeed.

### Document Reasoning For The Record

Assessments outlive the moment. They inform acquisitions meetings, justify decisions to authors and agents, support post-mortems when projects succeed or fail, and create institutional memory for future similar projects. Document the reasoning so the decision is defensible and useful later.

Write the assessment so a colleague who did not read the manuscript can follow the judgment: the contract, the craft conclusion, the market conclusion, the revision distance, the author assessment, the cost, and the recommendation with conditions. A well-documented decision protects the editor and the institution and improves future assessments. Undocumented decisions cannot be learned from.

## Common Traps

### Hedging Into Meaninglessness

A recommendation that will not commit wastes the acquisitions conversation. Render judgment based on the evidence.

### Vague Conditions

"Acquire if it improves" is not a condition. Make conditions concrete, testable, and judged against author capability.

### Letting One Factor Override All Others

Enthusiasm for craft, market, or author can blind the editor to disqualifying factors in other dimensions. Synthesize all judgments.

### Confusing Conditional Yes With Soft No

A conditional yes with unmeetable conditions is a no. Be honest about whether the author can meet the conditions.

### Ignoring Author Capability

A strong manuscript from an unreliable author is a risk. Weigh capability as part of the decision.

### Cost Blindness

A viable project that strains resources may be the wrong acquisition. Factor cost and opportunity cost.

### Undocumented Decisions

A decision without recorded reasoning cannot be defended or learned from. Document for the record.

## Self-Check

Before treating a go-no-go recommendation as complete, verify:

- The recommendation synthesizes craft, market, revision-distance, author-capability, and cost judgments, accounting for conflicts among them.
- The outcome fits a small, clear set: acquire, acquire with conditions, revise and resubmit, decline with guidance, or decline outright.
- Any conditions are concrete, testable, and judged against the author's demonstrated ability to meet them.
- The recommendation commits to a judgment rather than hedging, even under uncertainty.
- Decline-with-guidance is distinguished from decline-outright, and each is delivered clearly.
- Author capability has been incorporated as an editorial risk factor.
- Cost and opportunity cost have been factored into whether the project is the right use of resources.
- The reasoning is documented so a colleague who did not read the manuscript can follow and defend the decision.
- A conditional yes has been honestly assessed as achievable, not used to defer a no.
- The recommendation enables action in the acquisitions conversation rather than requiring further indecision.
