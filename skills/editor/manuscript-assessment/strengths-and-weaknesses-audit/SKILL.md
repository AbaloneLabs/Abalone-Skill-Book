# Strengths And Weaknesses Audit

A manuscript assessment requires an honest inventory of what the work does well and what it does poorly, examined across the dimensions that determine whether it succeeds on its own terms. This audit is not a list of likes and dislikes; it is a disciplined craft evaluation that separates genuine strengths from superficial charm, real weaknesses from matters of taste, and fixable problems from structural flaws. Editors who audit superficially produce assessments that miss the work's actual value or its actual risk, leading to acquisitions that disappoint or rejections that discard promising work.

Use this skill when reading a submission or manuscript to form a craft evaluation for an assessment report, acquisitions meeting, or revision scoping. It covers evaluating craft across the relevant dimensions, judging strengths and weaknesses against the work's own contract, distinguishing fixable from fundamental problems, and weighting findings by consequence. The editor's job is to render an accurate craft picture that the rest of the assessment can rely on.

## Core Rules

### Judge Against The Work's Own Contract First

Craft quality is not absolute. A quiet literary novel judged by thriller standards will appear to lack pace; a practical guide judged by literary standards will appear to lack style. Before auditing strengths and weaknesses, establish what the work is trying to be, then judge it against that contract.

Determine the genre, audience, and promise from the title, opening, and structure. Then evaluate whether the work achieves what it attempts. A strength is something the work does well relative to its contract; a weakness is something that undermines its contract. Judging against the wrong standard produces false negatives and false positives. Name the contract explicitly so the audit is defensible.

### Evaluate Across The Relevant Craft Dimensions

A complete audit examines the dimensions that matter for the work's type. Different genres weight dimensions differently, but the audit should cover those that determine success for this kind of work. Do not audit only the dimensions you personally care about.

For nonfiction, examine structure and argument, evidence and support, clarity and accessibility, voice and authority, originality of angle, and completeness against scope. For fiction, examine narrative structure and arc, character motivation and consistency, dialogue and voice, prose craft, pacing and tension, and thematic coherence. For practical or instructional works, examine accuracy, clarity of instruction, completeness of process, and usability. Tailor the dimension set to the work, but be systematic rather than impressionistic.

### Separate Genuine Strengths From Superficial Charm

Not everything that reads well is a genuine strength. A manuscript can be charming, funny, or stylish while lacking the structural or evidentiary substance to succeed. Distinguish strengths that serve the work's purpose from surface qualities that mask deeper problems.

A genuine strength is one that advances the work's contract: a structure that delivers the promised argument, a voice that fits the audience, evidence that supports the claims, characters whose choices drive the plot. Superficial charm entertains in the moment but does not build toward the work's purpose. Name the genuine strengths specifically, because they are what the work can build on, and do not let charm inflate the craft assessment beyond what the substance supports.

### Distinguish Real Weaknesses From Taste Differences

Equally important is distinguishing weaknesses that harm the work from editorial preferences that do not. An editor's taste is not a craft standard. A weakness is something that undermines the work's ability to fulfill its contract; a taste difference is something the editor would have done differently but that is defensible as written.

Test each perceived weakness against the work's contract and audience. If a reasonable reader of this kind of work would be harmed, confused, or alienated, it is a real weakness. If only the editor objects on stylistic grounds, it is likely taste. Recording taste as weakness inflates the problem list and erodes the author's trust. Reserve weakness labels for issues with genuine consequence.

### Separate Fixable Problems From Fundamental Flaws

A weakness's severity depends partly on whether it can be fixed. A fixable problem in an otherwise strong manuscript is a revision task; a fundamental flaw may make the work unviable regardless of other strengths. Classify each weakness by whether it is fixable and what fixing it would require.

Fixable problems include weak transitions, under-developed secondary characters, thin evidence in a chapter, a buried thesis, or inconsistent register. These respond to revision. Fundamental flaws include a premise the evidence cannot support, a structure that cannot deliver the promise, a protagonist whose core motivation is incoherent, or a topic with no real audience. These may require re-conception rather than revision. The fixable-versus-fundamental distinction directly informs the revision-distance and go-no-go judgments.

### Weight Findings By Consequence

Not all strengths and weaknesses matter equally. A brilliant voice cannot save a work whose central argument is unsupported; a weak prose style may be tolerable in a work whose structure and evidence are exceptional. Weight findings by how much they affect the work's ability to succeed.

Identify the load-bearing dimensions for this kind of work, the ones where strength or weakness most determines success. For a thriller, pacing and tension are load-bearing. For an argument-driven nonfiction work, evidence and logic are load-bearing. Weight strengths and weaknesses in load-bearing dimensions more heavily than those in secondary dimensions. A weighted audit produces a more accurate craft picture than a flat list.

### Look For Patterns, Not Just Isolated Instances

A single weak scene may be an outlier; a pattern of weak scenes indicates a craft problem the author must address systemically. Similarly, a single strong passage may be accidental, while a pattern of strength indicates a genuine authorial gift. Look for patterns across the manuscript.

Note where weaknesses recur: do dialogue scenes consistently underperform, do chapter endings consistently fail to pull the reader forward, does evidence consistently appear after the claim it should support? Patterns reveal the author's craft habits, both strong and weak, and they tell you what revision will actually require. Isolated issues are local fixes; patterns are craft-development challenges.

### Connect The Audit To The Assessment's Other Judgments

The strengths and weaknesses audit does not stand alone. It feeds the revision-distance estimate, the market analysis, the author-capability assessment, and the final recommendation. Connect the craft findings to these judgments explicitly.

A manuscript with genuine strengths but fixable weaknesses supports a revise-and-resubmit or conditional acquisition. A manuscript with fundamental flaws despite surface charm supports a decline. A manuscript whose weaknesses cluster in dimensions the author has shown strength elsewhere may be closable; weaknesses in dimensions the author has never demonstrated are higher risk. The audit's findings should make the rest of the assessment more precise.

## Common Traps

### Judging Against The Wrong Contract

Assessing a work by standards that do not match its genre or promise produces false conclusions. Establish the contract first.

### Letting Charm Inflate The Assessment

Surface qualities that entertain momentarily do not constitute craft strength if they do not serve the work's purpose. Distinguish charm from substance.

### Recording Taste As Weakness

Editorial preference is not a craft standard. Reserve weakness labels for issues with genuine consequence for the work's contract.

### Treating All Findings As Equal Weight

A flat list of strengths and weaknesses hides which ones matter most. Weight by consequence and by whether the dimension is load-bearing.

### Missing Patterns In Favor Of Isolated Notes

Isolated issues are local; patterns reveal craft habits. Look for recurrence across the manuscript.

### Failing To Classify Fixable Versus Fundamental

A weakness's fixability directly determines revision distance and viability. Classify each weakness accordingly.

### Auditing Only Preferred Dimensions

Evaluating only the dimensions you care about produces an incomplete picture. Audit systematically across the relevant set.

## Self-Check

Before treating a strengths and weaknesses audit as complete, verify:

- The work's contract has been established, and findings are judged against that contract, not a generic ideal.
- Craft has been evaluated across the dimensions relevant to this kind of work, systematically rather than impressionistically.
- Genuine strengths have been distinguished from superficial charm that does not serve the work's purpose.
- Weaknesses have been tested against the contract and audience to separate real problems from taste differences.
- Each weakness has been classified as fixable through revision or fundamental to the work's viability.
- Findings have been weighted by consequence and by whether the dimension is load-bearing for this genre.
- Patterns of strength and weakness have been identified, not just isolated instances.
- The audit connects explicitly to the revision-distance, market, and author-capability judgments.
- Surface appeal has not inflated the craft assessment beyond what substance supports.
- The audit provides an accurate, defensible craft picture that the rest of the assessment can rely on.
