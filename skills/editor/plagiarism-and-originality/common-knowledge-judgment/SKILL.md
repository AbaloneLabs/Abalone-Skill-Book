# Common Knowledge Judgment

Not every fact in a work requires a citation. Common knowledge, information that the target audience can reasonably be expected to know or that is widely available in standard reference works, does not need attribution. Citing common knowledge clutters the text without adding value. But the line between common knowledge and specialized knowledge is not always clear, and misjudging it leads to two errors: citing the obvious unnecessarily, or failing to cite specialized information that readers cannot verify on their own. Common knowledge judgment is the discipline of deciding, for each factual claim, whether citation is required based on what the target audience knows. Editors who apply a fixed rule, either cite everything or cite nothing obvious, produce works that are either cluttered or under-documented.

Use this skill when editing works that contain factual claims, to judge which require citation and which are common knowledge. It covers assessing audience knowledge, distinguishing common from specialized information, and handling borderline cases. The goal is a citation practice that documents what needs documentation without cluttering the text with unnecessary citations.

## Core Rules

### Judge Common Knowledge Relative To The Audience

Common knowledge is not absolute; it depends on the audience. What is common knowledge for specialists in a field may be specialized for general readers, and what is obvious to general readers may be unknown to specialists in another field. Judge common knowledge relative to the work's target audience.

Determine the target audience's likely knowledge base. For specialist audiences, assume knowledge of standard concepts, established findings, and disciplinary commonplaces. For general audiences, assume only broad cultural and historical knowledge. For student audiences, consider what level of the discipline they have reached. A fact that is common knowledge for one audience may require citation for another. The judgment must be calibrated to who will read the work. A citation decision appropriate for a scholarly monograph may be inadequate for a popular work on the same topic.

### Identify Information That Is Genuinely Common Knowledge

Some information is genuinely common knowledge and does not require citation: basic historical facts, widely known scientific concepts, general cultural knowledge, and information available in any standard reference work. Identify these to avoid unnecessary citation.

Recognize categories of common knowledge: basic historical dates and events, fundamental scientific concepts, widely known geographical and biographical facts, and information found in general encyclopedias. These do not require citation because the audience either knows them or can easily find them in standard references. Citing them adds clutter without aiding verification. When information falls clearly into common knowledge, no citation is needed. The test is whether the target reader would know it or find it trivially in a standard reference.

### Identify Specialized Knowledge That Requires Citation

Specialized knowledge requires citation: specific facts, statistics, research findings, detailed historical or scientific information, contested claims, and information not available in standard references. Identify these to ensure documentation.

Recognize categories requiring citation: specific statistics and data, research findings and study results, detailed or specialized facts, contested or debatable claims, direct quotations, and distinctive ideas or arguments from sources. These require citation because the audience cannot be expected to know them, and verification requires tracing to a specific source. When information is specialized, citation is necessary regardless of how well-established it seems. The test is whether the target reader would need a source to verify or explore the claim. Specialized information without citation is a documentation failure.

### Handle Borderline Cases Deliberately

Many claims fall in the borderline between common and specialized knowledge. A fact may be well-known in a field but not to general readers, or widely discussed but not in standard references. Handle borderline cases deliberately rather than defaulting to one side.

For borderline claims, consider the factors: how widely known is the information to the target audience, how easily can it be verified without a specific source, is it contested, and would a reader reasonably want to trace it. When uncertain, the safer practice is usually to cite, because under-citation is a more serious error than over-citation. However, consider whether citation adds value or merely clutter. For genuinely borderline cases, document the reasoning so the decision can be reviewed. Borderline judgment is where common knowledge decisions require the most thought and where editorial judgment matters most.

### Distinguish Established Consensus From Specific Findings

Established consensus, the broadly accepted understanding in a field, may be common knowledge for specialist audiences, while specific findings that established that consensus require citation. Distinguishing the two affects citation decisions.

For claims about a field's understanding, determine whether the claim reflects established consensus or a specific finding. Established consensus may be stated without citation for specialist audiences, though general audiences may need a source. Specific findings, even well-known ones, should be cited to their originating source. The distinction matters because consensus is a body of knowledge while findings are specific contributions. Citing consensus to a single source misrepresents how knowledge develops, while failing to cite specific findings under-documents the work's basis.

### Consider Contested And Debated Claims

Contested or debated claims require citation even when they are widely discussed, because the reader needs to know which position the work adopts and where it comes from. Common knowledge status does not apply to contested information in the same way.

For claims that are subject to debate or disagreement, citation is generally required regardless of how widely known the debate is. The reader needs to trace the work's position to its source to understand the stance and evaluate it. Contested claims presented without citation appear to assert the work's view as established fact, which misleads. When a claim is debated, cite the source of the position the work presents, and consider acknowledging the debate. Contested information is not common knowledge in the sense that allows omitting citation.

### Verify Common Knowledge Claims When Uncertain

Sometimes what appears to be common knowledge is actually imprecise, contested, or more specialized than assumed. When uncertain about whether a claim is truly common knowledge, verify rather than assume. Verification prevents both unnecessary citation and missing citation.

For claims being judged as common knowledge, if there is any uncertainty, verify the claim. Check whether it is accurately stated, whether it is as widely known as assumed, and whether it is truly uncontested. Verification may reveal that a presumed-common fact is actually imprecise or specialized, requiring citation. It may also confirm that the fact is common knowledge, supporting the decision not to cite. Verification is especially important for specific facts, dates, and figures that feel familiar, because familiarity can mask imprecision. When in doubt, verify before deciding.

## Common Traps

### Applying A Fixed Citation Rule

Common knowledge depends on audience. Judge each claim relative to the target reader.

### Citing Genuinely Common Knowledge

Unnecessary citation clutters the text. Identify what the audience knows.

### Missing Citation For Specialized Information

Specialized facts require documentation. Do not assume familiarity equals common knowledge.

### Defaulting To One Side For Borderline Cases

Borderline claims require deliberate judgment. When uncertain, citing is usually safer.

### Treating Specific Findings As Common Knowledge

Even well-known findings should cite their source. Distinguish findings from consensus.

### Omitting Citation For Contested Claims

Debated information requires citation. Readers need to trace the work's position.

### Assuming Familiarity Means Accuracy

Familiar facts may be imprecise. Verify when uncertain about common knowledge status.

## Self-Check

Before treating common knowledge judgment as complete, verify:

- Common knowledge decisions are calibrated to the target audience's likely knowledge.
- Genuinely common knowledge is identified and not cited unnecessarily.
- Specialized knowledge is identified and cited appropriately.
- Borderline cases are handled deliberately, with citation as the safer default.
- Established consensus is distinguished from specific findings in citation decisions.
- Contested and debated claims are cited regardless of how widely discussed.
- Common knowledge claims are verified when there is uncertainty about their status.
- The citation practice documents what needs documentation without unnecessary clutter.
- Specific facts, dates, and figures are verified before being treated as common knowledge.
- The work's citation pattern is consistent with audience expectations and disciplinary norms.
