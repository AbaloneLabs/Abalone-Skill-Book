# Post-Publication Protocol

Discovering a significant error in a published work triggers a process that must be handled according to protocol. The response involves assessing the error, deciding on the appropriate action, communicating with stakeholders and readers, implementing the correction or retraction, and documenting the process. Each step has requirements, and mishandling any step, delaying assessment, deciding without proper input, communicating poorly, or implementing incompletely, compounds the original error with a failed response. Post-publication protocol is the discipline of following the proper process for handling published errors thoroughly and responsibly. Editors who improvise the response rather than following protocol produce inconsistent, delayed, or incomplete corrections that fail readers and damage credibility.

Use this skill when a significant error is discovered in a published work and a formal response is required. It covers the protocol steps from assessment through documentation, stakeholder coordination, and ethical standards. The goal is a response that is thorough, timely, consistent, and aligned with editorial and ethical responsibilities.

## Core Rules

### Follow An Established Assessment Process

When an error is reported or discovered, the first step is a structured assessment. This involves verifying the error, understanding its nature and scope, and classifying its severity. An ad hoc or skipped assessment leads to misjudged responses. Follow an established assessment process.

Initiate the assessment promptly upon error discovery. Verify that the reported error is actually an error, consulting the original sources. Determine the error's nature, scope, and severity using the established classification framework. Identify what the error affects: which claims, conclusions, or readers. The assessment should be thorough enough to support a sound decision but prompt enough to enable timely response. Document the assessment findings. An established assessment process ensures that errors are evaluated consistently and that responses are based on understanding rather than reaction.

### Decide On Action Through Proper Authority

Based on the assessment, a decision must be made about the appropriate action: correction, prominent correction, or retraction. This decision should be made through proper authority, involving the right people and following the established process. Decisions made unilaterally or by the wrong people lack legitimacy.

Route the decision to the appropriate authority based on the error's severity and the organization's structure. Minor corrections may be handled by the editor; significant corrections may require editorial leadership; retractions may require the highest editorial authority and consultation with ethics bodies. Involve the author in the decision where appropriate, and consult subject matter experts for technical errors. The decision should follow established criteria for correction versus retraction, not ad hoc judgment. Document the decision, who made it, and the basis. Proper authority ensures that significant responses have legitimacy and consistency.

### Communicate With Stakeholders Promptly And Transparently

Stakeholders, including the author, affected subjects, publishers, and partners, need to be informed about the error and the planned response. Communication should be prompt and transparent, keeping stakeholders aligned and preventing surprises. Poor stakeholder communication creates confusion and mistrust.

Identify the stakeholders who need to be informed: the author, whose work is affected; subjects who may be impacted; the publisher and distribution partners; and any other relevant parties. Communicate the error, the assessment, and the planned response promptly. Be transparent about what happened and what will be done. Keep stakeholders informed as the response progresses. For significant errors or retractions, coordinated communication ensures consistent messaging. Stakeholder communication respects those affected by the error and the response, and it prevents the confusion that arises from uncoordinated or delayed notification.

### Implement The Correction Or Retraction Completely

The decided action must be implemented completely. A correction that is drafted but not published, or a retraction that removes the work but does not notify readers, is incomplete. Implementation must address all the places the error exists and all the audiences affected.

Implement the correction or retraction across all relevant locations and formats. For corrections, update the original content where feasible and publish the correction notice in appropriate locations. For retractions, mark or remove the work per policy, with a retraction notice that remains accessible. Ensure the implementation reaches all audiences who encountered the original, considering digital and print, original and syndicated versions. Verify that the implementation is complete across all channels. Incomplete implementation leaves some readers with the erroneous version, which defeats the purpose of the response. Complete implementation ensures the correction or retraction reaches everyone it needs to reach.

### Coordinate Timing Across Channels

The response should be coordinated across channels so that the correction or retraction appears consistently and simultaneously where possible. Uncoordinated timing, where some channels show the correction and others do not, creates inconsistency and confusion. Coordinate timing across channels.

Plan the implementation timing across all channels. Where possible, publish corrections or retractions simultaneously across platforms to ensure consistency. For complex multi-channel publications, coordinate with distribution partners to align timing. If simultaneous publication is not feasible, prioritize the channels with the greatest reach or impact. Coordinate the public communication, such as press or social media notifications, with the implementation. Coordinated timing ensures that readers encounter a consistent response regardless of where they access the work, and it prevents the confusion of inconsistent versions.

### Document The Entire Process

The entire post-publication process, from error discovery through implementation, should be documented. Documentation creates a record for accountability, learning, and defense if the response is questioned. Undocumented processes cannot be reviewed or improved.

Document the full process: the error and how it was discovered, the assessment and classification, the decision and authority, stakeholder communications, the implementation across channels, and the timing. Include the correction or retraction notice itself. Maintain this documentation as part of the editorial record. Documentation allows the response to be reviewed for quality and consistency, supports organizational learning about error patterns and response effectiveness, and provides a defense if the handling is questioned. Thorough documentation is the accountability mechanism for post-publication response.

### Review And Improve Processes Post-Incident

After the response is complete, review the incident to identify how the error occurred and how the response could be improved. This review supports organizational learning and helps prevent recurrence. Skipping the review misses the opportunity to improve.

Conduct a post-incident review once the immediate response is complete. Examine how the error entered the work: was it a verification failure, a process gap, or an individual mistake? Examine how the response went: was it timely, complete, and well-communicated? Identify process improvements that could prevent similar errors or improve future responses. Implement the improvements. The post-incident review turns an error into a learning opportunity, strengthening the work's quality processes and its capacity to handle future errors. Without review, the same errors and response failures may recur.

## Common Traps

### Skipping Or Rushing The Assessment

Sound decisions require understanding. Follow the assessment process before deciding.

### Decisions By Improper Authority

Significant responses need proper authority. Route decisions appropriately.

### Poor Stakeholder Communication

Affected parties need prompt, transparent notification. Coordinate messaging.

### Incomplete Implementation

Corrections must reach all audiences and channels. Verify completeness.

### Uncoordinated Timing Across Channels

Inconsistent versions confuse readers. Coordinate implementation timing.

### Undocumented Response

Without documentation, the response cannot be reviewed or defended. Record the process.

### Missing The Post-Incident Review

Errors offer learning opportunities. Review and improve processes afterward.

## Self-Check

Before treating post-publication protocol as complete, verify:

- An established assessment process was followed to verify and classify the error.
- The action decision was made through proper authority with appropriate consultation.
- Stakeholders were communicated with promptly and transparently.
- The correction or retraction was implemented completely across all relevant locations and formats.
- Implementation reached all audiences who encountered the original.
- Timing was coordinated across channels for consistency.
- The entire process, from discovery through implementation, is documented.
- A post-incident review identified how the error occurred and how to prevent recurrence.
- Process improvements identified in the review have been implemented.
- The response was thorough, timely, consistent, and aligned with editorial and ethical standards.
