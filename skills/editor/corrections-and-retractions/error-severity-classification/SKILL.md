# Error Severity Classification

Errors are not all equal. A minor typo that does not affect meaning is different from a factual error that undermines a core claim, which is different from an error that causes harm to subjects or misleads readers in consequential ways. The appropriate response, from no action to a quiet correction to a prominent public correction to a full retraction, depends on the error's severity. Misclassifying severity leads to two failures: under-responding to serious errors, allowing harm to continue, or over-responding to minor errors, damaging credibility unnecessarily. Error severity classification is the discipline of assessing each error's impact and determining the appropriate response. Editors who treat all errors the same either alarm readers with corrections of trivial matters or fail to address errors that require serious response.

Use this skill when errors are discovered in a published work or one near publication, to classify severity and determine the response. It covers assessing impact on accuracy, integrity, and harm, and matching response to severity. The goal is an appropriate, calibrated response to each error that protects readers and the work's integrity without overreacting.

## Core Rules

### Assess Impact On Accuracy And Meaning

The first dimension of severity is the error's impact on the work's accuracy and meaning. A typo that does not change meaning has minimal impact. A factual error in a supporting detail has moderate impact. An error in a core claim or conclusion has severe impact. Assess accuracy impact to begin classifying severity.

For each error, determine what it affects. Does it change the meaning of a sentence, the accuracy of a claim, or the validity of a conclusion? Errors that affect core claims, key evidence, or main conclusions are high-severity because they undermine the work's substance. Errors that affect minor details without changing meaning are low-severity. Errors in between, affecting supporting points but not core arguments, are moderate. Accuracy impact is the foundation of severity assessment because it determines whether the error misleads readers about the work's substance.

### Assess Potential Harm To Readers And Subjects

Beyond accuracy, errors can cause harm. A medical error can lead to harmful decisions. A misidentification can damage a subject's reputation. A legal error can create liability. Assess potential harm to readers and subjects, because harm elevates severity regardless of the error's size.

For each error, consider its potential consequences. Could the error lead readers to make harmful decisions, such as in health, financial, or safety contexts? Could it damage the reputation or interests of subjects? Could it create legal or ethical problems? Errors with harm potential are high-severity even if they seem small in isolation, because their consequences are significant. Harm assessment is especially important for errors in consequential fields like medicine, law, finance, and safety, where small errors can have large effects. An error's severity is not just about its size but about the damage it could cause.

### Consider The Error's Visibility And Reach

An error's severity is also affected by its visibility and reach. An error in a widely read work has more impact than the same error in a little-read one. An error that has been picked up and spread has more impact than one contained to the original. Consider visibility and reach in severity assessment.

For each error, consider how widely it has been or will be encountered. An error in a high-visibility publication or one that has been widely shared has greater potential impact and may warrant a more prominent correction. An error caught before wide distribution may be handled more quietly. Errors that have influenced other works, decisions, or public understanding have compounded impact and require more thorough response. Visibility and reach do not change the error's nature, but they affect the appropriate response scale.

### Classify Into Severity Tiers

Based on accuracy impact, harm potential, and visibility, classify each error into severity tiers that correspond to response levels. A typical classification might range from negligible, requiring no action, through minor, requiring quiet correction, to significant, requiring prominent correction, to severe, requiring retraction. Classify consistently.

Establish severity tiers appropriate to the work and context. Classify each error into a tier based on the assessment. Negligible errors, like minor typos with no meaning impact, may require no action. Minor errors, like small factual mistakes in non-core content, may require quiet correction. Significant errors, affecting accuracy of important claims, may require prominent correction notifying readers. Severe errors, undermining core conclusions or causing serious harm, may require retraction. Consistent classification ensures that responses are calibrated and that similar errors receive similar treatment. Document the classification and its basis.

### Match Response To Severity

Once severity is classified, match the response to the severity level. Under-responding to a severe error allows harm to continue; over-responding to a minor error damages credibility. The response should fit the severity.

For negligible errors, no action may be needed, or a quiet fix in future editions. For minor errors, a correction in the standard corrections process or future edition. For significant errors, a prominent public correction that notifies readers of the error and the fix. For severe errors, retraction or fundamental correction with clear notification. The response scale should match the severity scale. Matching response to severity ensures that serious errors receive serious attention while minor errors do not create unnecessary alarm. The response should also consider timing: serious errors require prompt response, while minor ones can wait for standard processes.

### Consider The Work's Stage And Distribution

The appropriate response also depends on the work's stage and distribution. An error caught before publication can be fixed quietly. An error in a published print work requires different handling than one in a digital work that can be updated. Consider stage and distribution in determining response.

For errors caught pre-publication, fix them directly with no public response needed. For errors in published digital works, corrections can often be made to the work itself, with notification of the correction. For errors in published print works, corrections typically appear in subsequent issues or editions, or through separate correction notices. For widely distributed works, consider whether and how to notify existing readers. The stage and distribution constrain the available responses and affect what is feasible. Factor them into the response determination alongside severity.

### Document The Error And Response

Error classification and response should be documented for accountability and learning. Documentation creates a record of what was found, how it was assessed, and what was done. It supports consistency in handling and allows the process to be reviewed.

For each error classified, document the error, its severity classification and basis, the response chosen, and the implementation. Maintain this documentation as part of the work's editorial record. Documentation allows responses to be reviewed if questioned, supports consistent handling of similar errors, and creates a record for organizational learning about error patterns. Undocumented errors and responses cannot be audited or learned from. The documentation is the accountability mechanism for error handling.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Errors The Same

Errors vary in severity. Classify each based on impact, harm, and visibility.

### Under-Responding To Serious Errors

Severe errors require serious response. Do not minimize harm-causing errors.

### Over-Responding To Minor Errors

Prominent correction of trivial matters alarms readers unnecessarily. Match response to severity.

### Ignoring Harm Potential

Small errors in consequential fields can cause large harm. Assess consequences.

### Missing Compounded Impact

Errors that have spread or influenced others have greater impact. Consider reach.

### Inconsistent Classification

Similar errors should receive similar treatment. Classify consistently.

### Undocumented Error Handling

Without documentation, responses cannot be audited or learned from. Record errors and responses.

## Self-Check

Before treating error severity classification as complete, verify:

- Each error's impact on accuracy and meaning has been assessed.
- Potential harm to readers and subjects has been considered, especially in consequential fields.
- The error's visibility and reach have been factored into severity assessment.
- Errors have been classified into consistent severity tiers.
- The response matches the severity level, neither under- nor over-reacting.
- The work's stage and distribution have been considered in determining feasible response.
- Serious errors receive prompt and prominent response.
- Minor errors are handled without unnecessary alarm.
- Each error, its classification, and its response have been documented.
- The classification and response process can be reviewed and audited.
