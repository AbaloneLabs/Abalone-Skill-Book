# Public Correction Crafting

When a published work contains an error that warrants public correction, the correction itself becomes a piece of writing that must be crafted carefully. A correction that is vague, defensive, or unclear fails to serve readers who need to understand what was wrong and what is now correct. A correction that overstates or understates the error misleads in a new way. A correction that is tone-deaf to the error's seriousness damages credibility further. Public correction crafting is the discipline of writing corrections that are transparent, clear, and appropriately toned, so readers understand the error and the correction fully. Editors who treat corrections as legalistic formalities produce notices that fail their purpose of informing readers honestly.

Use this skill when drafting public corrections, editor's notes, or errata for published errors of any severity. It covers identifying what to state, writing clearly and transparently, and matching tone to the error's seriousness. The goal is a correction that honestly informs readers and maintains the work's credibility through transparent error handling.

## Core Rules

### Clearly Identify What Was Wrong

The first function of a correction is to tell readers what was wrong. This requires specificity: which statement, figure, or claim was incorrect, and what exactly was the error. Vague corrections that reference "an error" without identifying it leave readers unable to determine what they read that was wrong. Clearly identify what was wrong.

State specifically what the error was. Identify the location: the article, section, or page. Quote or closely paraphrase the incorrect statement so readers can recognize it. Describe the nature of the error: a factual mistake, a misattribution, a numerical error, a misrepresentation. Avoid vague language like "an error appeared" that does not tell readers what they need to know. The reader who encountered the error should be able to identify it from the correction, and the reader who did not should understand what was wrong. Specificity is the foundation of an honest correction.

### State What Is Correct

A correction must state not only what was wrong but what is correct. Readers need to know the accurate information to replace the error in their understanding. State what is correct clearly and completely.

Provide the correct information that replaces the error. Be as specific about the correct version as about the error. If a figure was wrong, state the correct figure. If a claim was inaccurate, state the accurate claim. If an attribution was wrong, provide the correct attribution. The correction should leave readers with accurate information, not just with knowledge that something was wrong. Stating what is correct completes the correction's function of fixing the reader's understanding. Ensure the correct version is itself verified and accurate, so the correction does not introduce a new error.

### Explain The Significance Of The Correction

For errors that affect the work's meaning or conclusions, the correction should explain the significance: how does the correction change what readers should understand? A corrected figure may change a conclusion; a corrected attribution may change an assessment. Explain significance so readers can update their understanding fully.

For corrections that affect meaning, explain what changes. Does the correction affect the work's conclusions, recommendations, or implications? Does it change how readers should interpret the affected content? Explain the significance so readers do not just know the correct fact but understand its impact on the work. For minor corrections that do not affect meaning, a brief note that the correction does not change conclusions may be appropriate. Explaining significance respects readers by helping them fully update their understanding, rather than leaving them to figure out implications on their own.

### Match Tone To The Error's Seriousness

The tone of a correction should match the error's seriousness. A minor typo correction can be brief and matter-of-fact. A significant factual error warrants a more substantial and serious correction. An error that caused harm may warrant apology or acknowledgment of responsibility. Match tone to seriousness.

Calibrate the correction's tone to the error's severity. For minor errors, a straightforward, brief correction is appropriate. For significant errors, a more detailed and serious correction that acknowledges the importance of the error. For errors that caused harm, the correction may need to acknowledge responsibility, apologize, or explain steps taken to prevent recurrence. A tone-deaf correction, either flippant about a serious error or grave about a minor one, undermines credibility. The tone signals the work's understanding of the error's significance and its respect for readers.

### Avoid Defensive Or Evasive Language

Corrections sometimes slip into defensive or evasive language that minimizes the error, deflects responsibility, or obscures what happened. This undermines the correction's purpose and damages credibility. Avoid defensive or evasive language.

Watch for language that minimizes, deflects, or obscures. Avoid phrasings like "it was incorrectly reported that" without saying who reported it or how, or "due to circumstances" without explaining. Avoid passive voice that obscures responsibility where responsibility matters. Avoid language that suggests the error was trivial when it was not. Honest corrections take responsibility, explain what happened directly, and do not hide behind evasive phrasing. Defensive corrections suggest the work cares more about its image than about honesty, which damages the credibility that the correction was meant to protect.

### Place And Prominence The Correction Appropriately

A correction's placement and prominence affect whether readers see it. A correction buried where readers will not find it fails to inform those who encountered the error. Placement and prominence should match the error's visibility and severity.

Place corrections where affected readers will encounter them. For digital works, this may mean a prominent correction notice at the top or in the corrected section. For print works, corrections typically appear in a standard corrections location, but significant errors may warrant more prominent placement. The prominence should match the error's severity and visibility: significant errors that many readers encountered warrant prominent correction; minor errors may be handled in standard correction sections. Burying a correction for a significant error is a form of evasion. Match prominence to the need to reach affected readers.

### Update The Original Content Where Feasible

Where feasible, the original erroneous content should be updated or marked, not just supplemented with a separate correction notice. Digital works can often be corrected in place, with a note indicating the correction. This ensures readers encountering the original location get the correct information. Update original content where feasible.

For digital works, correct the original content and add a correction note explaining what was changed. This ensures readers who find the original location see the correct version. For print works that cannot be updated in place, the correction notice must be clear enough that readers can identify and correct their understanding. Where original content is updated, transparency about the change is important: note that a correction was made, so readers know the content has been altered from its original form. In-place updating combined with transparent correction notes serves readers best.

## Common Traps

### Vague Corrections That Do Not Identify The Error

Readers need to know what was wrong. Be specific about the error and its location.

### Stating What Was Wrong But Not What Is Correct

Corrections must provide accurate replacement information. State the correct version.

### Tone-Deaf Corrections

Match tone to seriousness. Avoid flippant or disproportionately grave language.

### Defensive Or Evasive Language

Minimizing or deflecting undermines honesty. Take responsibility and explain directly.

### Burying Significant Corrections

Affected readers need to see corrections. Match prominence to severity and visibility.

### Correcting Without Explaining Significance

For errors affecting meaning, explain what changes. Help readers update understanding.

### Failing To Update Original Content

Where feasible, correct in place with transparent notes. Do not rely only on separate notices.

## Self-Check

Before treating public correction crafting as complete, verify:

- The correction clearly and specifically identifies what was wrong, including location.
- The correction states what is correct, providing verified accurate replacement information.
- For errors affecting meaning, the correction explains the significance of the change.
- The tone matches the error's seriousness, neither flippant nor disproportionately grave.
- The correction avoids defensive, evasive, or minimizing language.
- Responsibility is taken where appropriate, without deflecting.
- The correction is placed and promoted so affected readers will encounter it.
- Prominence matches the error's severity and visibility.
- Original content is updated where feasible, with transparent correction notes.
- The correction honestly informs readers and maintains the work's credibility.
