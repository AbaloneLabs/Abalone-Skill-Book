# Syntax And Agreement

Syntax and agreement are the grammatical load-bearing structures that make sentences intelligible. When they fail, the reader stumbles, misreads, or loses trust. Subject-verb agreement, pronoun-antecedent agreement, tense consistency, and structural soundness are not pedantic concerns; they are the mechanics that prevent ambiguity and signal care. Errors in these areas are common, often subtle, and frequently hidden in complex constructions where the subject and verb are separated, where collective nouns create ambiguity, or where intervening phrases distract from the true grammatical relationship. Editors who scan for obvious errors but miss the buried ones pass along prose that fails under careful reading.

Use this skill at the copy-editing stage to verify syntactic correctness and agreement throughout a draft. It covers checking agreement in complex constructions, maintaining pronoun clarity and antecedent agreement, ensuring tense consistency, and diagnosing structural problems. The editor's job is to make the grammar sound without flattening legitimate variation.

## Core Rules

### Check Subject-Verb Agreement In Complex Constructions

Simple subject-verb agreement is rarely the problem; the errors hide in complexity. When the subject and verb are separated by intervening phrases, when the subject is a collective noun, an indefinite pronoun, or a compound, agreement errors creep in. Check these constructions specifically.

For sentences where the subject and verb are separated by a long phrase or clause, identify the true subject and ensure the verb agrees with it, not with the nearest noun in the intervening material. For collective nouns, determine whether the context treats them as singular or plural and maintain consistency. For indefinite pronouns like each, none, either, and neither, apply the correct agreement based on meaning and style guidance. For compound subjects joined by and, or, or nor, apply the rules for compound agreement. These are the constructions where agreement fails, and they require deliberate checking, not skimming.

### Resolve Pronoun-Antecedent Ambiguity And Agreement

Pronouns create two problems: ambiguity, when the reader cannot tell which antecedent a pronoun refers to, and disagreement, when the pronoun does not match its antecedent in number or gender. Both undermine clarity, and both are common. Check pronouns for clear reference and correct agreement.

For each pronoun, identify its antecedent. If multiple plausible antecedents exist, the reference is ambiguous and needs resolution, usually by replacing the pronoun with a noun or restructuring. If the antecedent is missing or too distant, the pronoun dangles and needs a closer or explicit antecedent. Check that pronouns agree with their antecedents in number, and handle singular they according to the style guide and the author's intent. Ambiguous pronoun reference is one of the most common clarity failures in prose, and it often survives multiple readings because the writer knows what they meant.

### Maintain Tense Consistency And Handle Shifts

Tense should remain consistent within a passage unless a change is intentional and signaled. Unintentional tense shifts, drifting from past to present or vice versa, confuse the reader about when events occur and signal carelessness. Check tense consistency and diagnose the cause of any shifts.

For narrative prose, identify the governing tense and check for unintended shifts. In analytical and argumentative prose, check that tense reflects the appropriate relationship to the material: literary present for discussion of texts, past for historical events, present for general truths. When a shift occurs, determine whether it is intentional, for example moving from past narrative to present reflection, or unintentional. Flag unintentional shifts and recommend aligning to the governing tense. Note that some genres, such as certain fiction techniques, use tense shifts deliberately; preserve those.

### Diagnose Structural Problems: Danglers, Misplaced Modifiers, Incomplete Constructions

Beyond agreement, syntax fails in characteristic ways: dangling modifiers, where the modifier has no logical subject to attach to; misplaced modifiers, where the modifier attaches to the wrong word; squinting modifiers, where placement creates ambiguity about what is modified; and incomplete constructions, where a sentence lacks a subject, verb, or necessary complement. Check for these structural problems.

For each sentence, verify that modifiers attach to logical targets. An opening participial phrase must modify the sentence's subject; if it does not, it dangles. A modifier placed between two elements may attach to either, creating ambiguity. A sentence missing a main verb or necessary component is incomplete. Flag each structural problem and recommend repair. These errors often survive because the writer fills in the intended meaning mentally; the editor must read for what the sentence actually says, not what it means to say.

### Handle Tricky Agreement Cases By Meaning

Some agreement cases are genuinely ambiguous and resolved by meaning rather than rule. Collective nouns can be singular or plural depending on whether the group acts as a unit or as individuals. Fractions and percentages take agreement based on the noun they modify. Titles and terms treated as words can be singular even when they appear plural. Resolve these by the meaning in context, not by rote.

For each tricky case, determine what the sentence means and choose agreement that reflects that meaning. A collective noun acting as a unit takes singular agreement; the same noun referring to individual members takes plural. "A majority of voters were" versus "the majority was," depending on emphasis. Apply meaning-based agreement consistently and document decisions in the style sheet so they are applied uniformly. When the style guide specifies a preference, follow it unless the author has established a consistent contrary pattern worth preserving.

### Preserve Legitimate Variation And Stylistic Choice

Not every departure from a prescriptive rule is an error. Fragments can be deliberate, split infinitives are often clearer than alternatives, beginning sentences with conjunctions is legitimate, and some "agreement errors" reflect evolving usage. Distinguish genuine errors from legitimate variation and stylistic choice.

Before correcting, ask whether the construction is a deliberate choice serving clarity, voice, or emphasis. A fragment in stylistic prose is a feature; correct it only if it is unintentional or distracting. A split infinitive that improves clarity should be preserved. Sentence-initial conjunctions are standard in much modern prose. Apply rules to fix genuine errors while preserving choices that serve the work. Over-correction of legitimate variation flattens voice and can make prose worse, not better.

### Check Parallel Structure In Series And Correlatives

Parallel structure requires that elements in a series, list, or correlative construction take the same grammatical form. Broken parallelism, mixing verbs with nouns, infinitives with gerunds, or different prepositions, creates a bump the reader feels even when the meaning is clear. Check parallelism in series and correlatives.

For lists, series, and constructions using correlatives like either/or, neither/nor, not only/but also, verify that the parallel elements share grammatical form. Flag broken parallelism and recommend aligning the forms. Parallel structure is both a grammatical and a stylistic concern: it serves clarity and creates the balanced rhythm that makes series satisfying to read.

## Common Traps

### Missing Agreement Errors Hidden In Complex Constructions

The nearest noun is not always the subject. Check true subject-verb agreement across intervening material.

### Tolerating Ambiguous Pronoun Reference

Writers know what they meant; readers do not. Resolve ambiguous antecedents.

### Drifting Tense Without Noticing

Unintentional shifts confuse timing. Maintain governing tense unless change is intentional.

### Skipping Structural Problems Because Meaning Is Inferable

Danglers and misplaced modifiers survive because meaning can be guessed. Read for what is said.

### Over-Correcting Legitimate Variation

Fragments, split infinitives, and sentence-initial conjunctions can be deliberate. Preserve choice.

### Breaking Parallelism In Series

Mixed grammatical forms in lists create bumps. Align parallel elements.

### Applying Rules By Rote To Meaning-Based Cases

Some agreement depends on meaning, not rule. Resolve by context.

## Self-Check

Before treating syntax and agreement editing as complete, verify:

- Subject-verb agreement has been checked in complex constructions with separated subjects, collectives, indefinites, and compounds.
- Pronoun references are unambiguous, with antecedents clear and agreement correct.
- Tense is consistent within passages, with any shifts intentional and signaled.
- Structural problems, danglers, misplaced modifiers, incomplete constructions, have been identified and repaired.
- Tricky agreement cases have been resolved by meaning in context, with decisions documented.
- Legitimate variation and stylistic choice have been preserved rather than over-corrected.
- Parallel structure has been verified in series, lists, and correlative constructions.
- Singular they has been handled according to style guidance and author intent.
- Corrections address genuine errors without flattening voice or legitimate flexibility.
- The grammar is sound enough that careful readers will not stumble.
