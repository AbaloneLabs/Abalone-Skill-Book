# Stakeholder Sign-Off

Publication requires approval from multiple stakeholders, each holding a piece of the responsibility for the work's quality and risk. The author must confirm the work represents their intent. Subject matter experts must verify technical accuracy. Legal reviewers must clear liability. Sponsors or commissioners must confirm the work meets their requirements. Sign-off is the process of obtaining these approvals, and it fails when approvals are assumed rather than obtained, collected from the wrong person, given without genuine review, or treated as a formality. Stakeholder sign-off is the discipline of ensuring each approval is informed, explicit, and from the right person, so publication proceeds with genuine authorization. Editors who treat sign-off as a box to check publish works with unaddressed concerns from stakeholders who were never actually asked.

Use this skill when coordinating the final approvals before publication. It covers identifying who must sign off, ensuring informed review, obtaining explicit approval, and managing disagreements. The goal is publication backed by genuine authorization from all responsible parties.

## Core Rules

### Identify All Required Sign-Off Parties

The first task is identifying everyone whose approval is required before publication. This depends on the work type, its risks, and the organizational context. Missing a required sign-off party means publishing without authorization that was needed. Identify parties comprehensively.

Map the stakeholders whose approval is required. Typically includes: the author, who must confirm the work represents their intent and that they accept the edits. Subject matter experts, for technical or specialized content, who must verify accuracy. Legal or compliance reviewers, for content with liability, privacy, or regulatory implications. Sponsors or commissioners, if the work was commissioned. Editors and production leads, for quality and format. Diversity or sensitivity reviewers, for content touching identity. Add parties specific to the context. Do not assume a generic list is complete; identify what this specific work requires. A missed sign-off party is a gap in authorization.

### Ensure Informed Review Before Approval

Sign-off is meaningful only if the approver has genuinely reviewed the work they are approving. An approval given without review, or based on an earlier version, is not real authorization. Ensure each approver reviews the final version of the work, or the relevant portion, before signing off.

Provide each approver with the final version of the work, or the portion relevant to their expertise, with enough time for genuine review. Clarify what they are approving: the whole work, the technical accuracy, the legal clearance, and what specifically they should check. Confirm that the version they review is the version that will be published, with no changes after their review unless re-approved. An approval of an earlier version does not cover later changes. Informed review is the substance of sign-off; without it, the approval is hollow.

### Obtain Explicit And Documented Approval

Approval must be explicit and documented. A nod, a silence that is taken as consent, or an ambiguous email does not constitute sign-off. Obtain clear, explicit approval and record it. Explicit, documented approval is defensible; implicit or unrecorded approval is not.

Require each approver to provide explicit approval, in writing or a documented form, stating what they are approving. Record the approval with the approver's name, role, what they approved, the date, and the version reviewed. Do not infer approval from silence or casual communication. Where approval is ambiguous, seek clarification and explicit confirmation. The documentation should allow anyone reviewing the sign-off process to see exactly who approved what, when, and on what basis. Explicit, documented approval creates an accountable authorization trail.

### Route Sign-Off To The Right Person

Sign-off must come from the right person, someone with the authority and expertise to provide the approval being sought. A legal review from someone without legal authority, a technical sign-off from someone outside the relevant field, or an authorial approval from someone other than the author, these are not valid sign-offs. Route each approval to the appropriate person.

For each required sign-off, identify who has the authority and expertise to provide it. Verify the approver is the right person, not merely a convenient one. For organizational roles, confirm the current incumbent. For expert review, confirm the reviewer has relevant expertise. For authorial approval, confirm all authors or the designated lead author. Routing sign-off to the wrong person provides false authorization; routing to the right person provides genuine authorization. The distinction matters when the authorization is later questioned.

### Manage Disagreements And Conditions

Stakeholders may disagree with each other or attach conditions to their approval. An expert may flag a concern the author dismisses; a legal reviewer may require changes the author resists; sponsors may request revisions. These disagreements and conditions must be managed, not ignored or overridden. Address them explicitly before publication.

When a stakeholder raises a concern or condition, record it explicitly. Facilitate resolution among the parties: clarify the concern, consider options, seek consensus. Where consensus cannot be reached, escalate to a decision-maker with authority to resolve the conflict. Document the resolution. Do not publish over an unresolved objection from a required sign-off party without explicit escalation and decision. Conditions attached to approval must be met before publication or explicitly waived through proper process. Managing disagreements respects the authorization system rather than circumventing it.

### Reconfirm Sign-Off After Material Changes

If the work changes materially after a stakeholder has signed off, the sign-off may no longer be valid. An approval based on an earlier version does not necessarily cover later changes, especially changes to content the approver reviewed. Reconfirm sign-off after material changes.

After any material change following sign-off, assess whether the change falls within the scope of the existing approval or requires reconfirmation. Changes to technical content require expert reconfirmation; changes affecting legal risk require legal reconfirmation; changes affecting authorial intent require author reconfirmation. Provide the changes to the relevant approvers and obtain renewed approval. Treat post-sign-off changes as requiring fresh authorization, not as covered by prior approval. Reconfirmation ensures the published version carries current authorization, not stale approval.

### Maintain The Sign-Off Record

The sign-off record should be maintained as part of the publication documentation, persisting beyond the publication event. This record demonstrates that publication was authorized and allows the authorization to be reviewed if questions arise later. An unrecorded or discarded sign-off cannot be defended.

Maintain a complete sign-off record: each approver, their role, what they approved, when, the version reviewed, any conditions or concerns raised, and how they were resolved. Store the record where it can be retrieved and audited. This record is the evidence that publication was properly authorized. If an issue arises after publication, the sign-off record shows what was approved and by whom, which is essential for accountability and for defending the publication decision. The record is the persistent artifact of the authorization process.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Sign-Off Rather Than Obtaining It

Approvals must be explicitly obtained. Do not infer consent from silence.

### Missing Required Sign-Off Parties

Identify all required approvers comprehensively. A missed party is a gap in authorization.

### Approvals Without Genuine Review

Sign-off must be based on review of the final version. Uninformed approval is hollow.

### Routing To The Wrong Person

Approval must come from someone with authority and expertise. Verify the approver.

### Publishing Over Unresolved Objections

Disagreements must be managed and resolved. Do not override without escalation.

### Treating Prior Sign-Off As Covering Later Changes

Material changes require reconfirmation. Do not assume stale approval covers new content.

### Discarding The Sign-Off Record

The record must persist for accountability and defense. Maintain it as publication documentation.

## Self-Check

Before treating stakeholder sign-off as complete, verify:

- All required sign-off parties have been identified for this specific work.
- Each approver has reviewed the final version, or relevant portion, with enough time for genuine review.
- Each approver has provided explicit, documented approval of what they are approving.
- Each approval has been obtained from the right person with appropriate authority and expertise.
- Disagreements and conditions have been addressed and resolved or properly escalated.
- No publication proceeds over an unresolved objection from a required sign-off party.
- Material changes after sign-off have been reconfirmed by the relevant approvers.
- The complete sign-off record is maintained, including approvers, roles, dates, versions, and resolutions.
- The record is stored where it can be retrieved and audited.
- Publication is backed by genuine, current authorization from all responsible parties.
