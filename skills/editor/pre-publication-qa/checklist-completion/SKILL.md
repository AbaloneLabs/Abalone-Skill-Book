# Checklist Completion

Publication is a point of no return. Once a work is published, errors are public, corrections are costly, and some harms cannot be undone. The pre-publication checklist exists to ensure that every necessary check has been performed before this irreversible step. But checklists fail in characteristic ways: items are checked without being verified, assumptions replace evidence, items are skipped under time pressure, and the checklist itself is incomplete. Checklist completion is the discipline of ensuring every item is genuinely verified, documented, and resolved before publication. Editors who treat the checklist as a formality publish works with gaps that the checklist was meant to catch.

Use this skill when managing the final quality assurance before publication, for any type of work. It covers building a complete checklist, verifying each item with evidence, managing dependencies and blockers, and documenting completion. The goal is a publication decision based on verified completion, not assumed completion.

## Core Rules

### Build A Complete And Appropriate Checklist

The checklist must be complete, covering every check that the work requires, and appropriate, tailored to the work type and its specific risks. A generic checklist misses work-specific requirements; an incomplete checklist provides false confidence. Build the checklist deliberately based on what this work needs.

Start with standard checks applicable to the work type: copy edit, proofread, fact check, legal review, permissions, formatting, accessibility. Add checks specific to the work: specialized terminology verification for technical works, sensitivity review for works touching identity, regulatory compliance for regulated content. Add checks specific to the context: platform requirements for digital, printer specifications for print. Consult with stakeholders to identify checks they require. The checklist should be comprehensive enough to catch what matters without being so bloated that items get skipped. A complete, appropriate checklist is the foundation of reliable QA.

### Verify Each Item With Evidence

Checking a box is not the same as verifying completion. An item is complete only when there is evidence that the check was performed and passed. Require evidence for each item rather than accepting assertions. Evidence transforms the checklist from a formality into a verification tool.

For each checklist item, define what evidence demonstrates completion: a signed-off edit, a documented fact check, a permissions file, a passed accessibility test. Require that evidence before marking the item complete. Do not accept "done" without supporting evidence. Where evidence is ambiguous, investigate before checking. The discipline of evidence prevents the most common checklist failure, marking items complete based on assumption or intent rather than verified performance. Evidence-based completion is auditable and defensible.

### Sequence Items By Dependency

Checklist items have dependencies. Permissions must be cleared before publication; legal review must complete before final sign-off; the copy edit must complete before proofreading. A checklist that ignores dependencies creates bottlenecks and gaps. Sequence items by dependency to ensure smooth flow and complete coverage.

Map the dependencies among checklist items. Identify what must complete before each item can begin or finish. Sequence the checklist so dependencies are respected: foundational checks first, dependent checks after. Identify the critical path, the longest chain of dependencies, and monitor it closely. Flag items that block others and ensure they receive priority attention. Dependency-aware sequencing prevents the situation where late-completing items hold up publication or, worse, are skipped to meet deadlines.

### Track And Resolve Blockers

Some checklist items will encounter problems: a permission is denied, a fact cannot be verified, a legal issue arises. These blockers must be tracked and resolved, not allowed to silently drop the item. Unresolved blockers are a primary source of incomplete checklists passing as complete.

When an item encounters a blocker, record the blocker explicitly. Track blockers centrally so they are visible and cannot be forgotten. For each blocker, assign responsibility for resolution and a deadline. Determine whether the blocker can be resolved, worked around, or requires escalating the publication decision. Do not mark a blocked item complete; either resolve the blocker or explicitly decide to publish with the item incomplete, which is a decision that should be made deliberately and documented. Silent dropping of blocked items is how checklists fail.

### Distinguish Must-Pass From Should-Pass Items

Not all checklist items are equal. Some are must-pass: legal clearance, factual accuracy for core claims, permissions for copyrighted material. These block publication if incomplete. Others are should-pass: stylistic preferences, minor consistency issues, nice-to-have enhancements. These can be deferred if necessary. Distinguish must-pass from should-pass to focus effort and make sound publication decisions.

For each checklist item, categorize it as must-pass or should-pass based on the consequences of incompleteness. Must-pass items are those whose failure creates legal, factual, ethical, or reputational risk. Should-pass items are those whose failure creates quality concerns but not blocking risk. Focus verification effort on must-pass items. If publication must proceed with incomplete items, it should be should-pass items that are deferred, explicitly and with documentation, never must-pass items silently skipped. Clear categorization supports sound decisions under pressure.

### Document Completion For Accountability

Checklist completion should be documented so the publication decision is accountable and auditable. Documentation shows what was checked, by whom, when, and with what evidence. It allows the decision to be reviewed and defended. Undocumented completion is unaccountable.

Record for each checklist item: the check performed, who performed it, when it was completed, and the evidence of completion. Record blockers and their resolution or explicit deferral. Record the final publication decision and who made it. This documentation persists beyond the publication event and allows the QA process to be reviewed, improved, and defended if issues arise later. Accountability through documentation is what separates a rigorous process from a performative one.

### Review The Checklist Before Publication Decision

Before the publication decision is made, review the checklist as a whole. Confirm all must-pass items are complete with evidence. Confirm blockers are resolved or explicitly deferred. Confirm the work is ready based on verified completion, not assumed completion. This final review is the last gate.

Convene the review with relevant stakeholders. Walk through the checklist, confirming each item's status. Address any items that are ambiguous or lack evidence. Confirm that must-pass items are genuinely complete. Confirm that any deferrals are deliberate and documented. Make the publication decision explicitly based on the checklist status. This review prevents publication proceeding on momentum or assumption when items are actually incomplete. The checklist is the basis for the decision, and the decision should be made against it deliberately.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Checklist As A Formality

The checklist exists to catch gaps. Verify each item with evidence, not assertion.

### Incomplete Or Generic Checklists

Tailor the checklist to the work. Generic lists miss work-specific requirements.

### Marking Items Complete Without Evidence

Assertion is not verification. Require supporting evidence for each item.

### Ignoring Dependencies

Items have dependencies. Sequence the checklist to respect them and avoid bottlenecks.

### Silent Dropping Of Blocked Items

Blockers must be tracked and resolved or explicitly deferred. Never silently skipped.

### Treating All Items As Equal

Distinguish must-pass from should-pass. Focus effort and make sound decisions under pressure.

### Skipping The Final Review

Review the checklist before deciding to publish. Confirm verified completion.

## Self-Check

Before treating checklist completion as done, verify:

- The checklist is complete and appropriate, covering standard and work-specific checks.
- Each item is verified with evidence before being marked complete.
- Items are sequenced by dependency to ensure smooth flow and complete coverage.
- The critical path of dependencies is identified and monitored.
- Blockers are tracked centrally, assigned for resolution, and not silently dropped.
- Must-pass and should-pass items are distinguished and categorized.
- Verification effort is focused on must-pass items.
- Completion is documented with check, person, date, and evidence for accountability.
- Any deferrals are deliberate, documented, and limited to should-pass items.
- A final review of the complete checklist has informed the publication decision.
