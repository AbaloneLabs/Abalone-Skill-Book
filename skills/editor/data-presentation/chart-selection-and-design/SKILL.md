# Chart Selection And Design

Charts and graphs turn data into visual information, but only when the chart type fits the data and the design is clear and honest. A mismatched chart type, such as a pie chart for time series or a line chart for categorical comparisons, obscures patterns rather than revealing them. Misleading design, such as truncated axes or distorted proportions, can make data appear to show what it does not. Chart selection and design is the discipline of ensuring visuals serve comprehension and truth. Editors who accept charts as provided, without evaluating type fit or design honesty, miss visuals that confuse or mislead.

Use this skill when editing documents that contain charts or graphs to evaluate selection and design. It covers chart type appropriateness, design clarity, and honesty. The goal is visuals that reveal the data accurately and support reader understanding.

## Core Rules

### Verify The Chart Type Fits The Data And Message

Different chart types suit different data and messages. Bar charts compare categories; line charts show trends over time; scatter plots show relationships; pie charts show parts of a whole. Using the wrong type obscures the message. Verify type fit.

For each chart, assess whether the type fits the data and the message. Is the data categorical, temporal, relational, or compositional? Does the chart aim to compare, show trend, reveal relationship, or display proportion? Match the type to the purpose. Flag mismatches, such as a pie chart for trends, a line chart for unrelated categories, or a bar chart for continuous distribution. The right chart type makes the message visible; the wrong type hides it or distorts it. Type selection is the foundational decision in chart design, and getting it wrong undermines everything that follows.

### Check Axes For Honest And Clear Scaling

Axes determine how data appears. Truncated y-axes exaggerate differences; inconsistent scales mislead; unlabeled axes confuse. Axes must be honest and clear. Check scaling.

Examine each chart's axes. Does the y-axis start at zero where appropriate, or is it truncated in a way that exaggerates differences? Are scales consistent across comparable charts? Are axes labeled with units and clear titles? Flag axis choices that mislead, such as truncation that makes small differences look dramatic or compressed scales that hide significant variation. Also flag unclear axes that lack labels or units, leaving the reader unable to interpret values. Honest, clear axes let readers perceive the data accurately; misleading or unclear axes distort perception. The axis is where many chart deceptions, intentional or accidental, occur.

### Ensure Labels, Legends, And Titles Are Clear

Charts need clear labels, legends, and titles so readers can interpret them without guessing. Missing or unclear labeling forces readers to infer, which leads to misreading. Ensure clarity.

Assess each chart's labeling. Are data series labeled directly or through a clear legend? Are axes titled with what they measure and in what units? Does the chart have a title that states what it shows? Flag charts with missing labels, tiny or unclear legends, or titles that do not convey the chart's message. Recommend direct labeling where possible, which is clearer than legends, and titles that state the finding, not just the topic. Clear labeling lets readers interpret the chart correctly and quickly. A chart that requires the reader to guess what the axes or series represent has failed in communication.

### Verify The Chart Reveals Rather Than Distorts The Pattern

A good chart reveals the true pattern in the data; a bad one distorts it. Distortion can arise from type mismatch, scaling, or visual effects like 3D that mislead. Verify the chart reveals honestly.

Assess whether each chart reveals or distorts the data's pattern. Does the visual impression match the actual data? Flag distortions: 3D effects that exaggerate or minimize values through perspective, area-based charts where area does not match value proportionally, or color choices that imply patterns that do not exist. The chart should allow readers to perceive the data accurately, including its significant features and its noise. A chart that makes a random fluctuation look like a trend, or a real trend look like noise, misleads. Honesty in visual representation is the core obligation of chart design.

### Check That Color And Visual Encoding Aid Comprehension

Color and visual encoding should aid comprehension, not decorate or confuse. Color should distinguish data series or encode values meaningfully; misuse of color obscures or misleads. Check encoding.

Assess each chart's use of color and visual encoding. Does color distinguish series clearly, encode values logically, or highlight what matters? Flag color use that is merely decorative, that uses too many colors to distinguish, that relies on red-green distinctions inaccessible to colorblind readers, or that encodes ordinally with a rainbow palette that implies false ordering. Recommend color use that serves comprehension: distinct colors for distinct series, sequential or diverging palettes for ordered data, and accessibility-conscious choices. Visual encoding should carry meaning; encoding that carries no meaning, or that misleads, detracts from the chart's function.

### Ensure The Chart Serves The Document's Narrative

Charts do not stand alone; they serve the document's narrative and argument. A chart should illustrate a point the text makes, with the text and chart working together. Ensure integration.

Assess how each chart integrates with the document's narrative. Does the chart illustrate, support, or develop a point the text makes? Is the chart referenced and interpreted in the text, not just inserted? Flag charts that float without textual integration, that do not serve the argument, or that contradict the text. Recommend that the text reference each chart, state what it shows, and connect it to the argument. A chart integrated with narrative reinforces the message; a chart standing alone may be overlooked or misinterpreted. The chart and text should form a coherent communication, each supporting the other.

### Verify Data Integrity And Source Attribution

Charts present data, and that data must be accurate and attributed. A chart with wrong data or no source attribution undermines credibility and prevents verification. Verify integrity and attribution.

Check each chart for data integrity and source attribution. Does the chart present data consistent with its source? Are values, percentages, and totals correct? Is the data source cited, so readers can verify? Flag charts with data that seems inconsistent, totals that do not add correctly, or missing source attribution. Recommend citing the source for each chart's data, whether a study, a database, or original analysis. Data integrity and attribution are essential for credibility; a chart whose data cannot be verified or trusted fails its purpose, no matter how well designed.

## Common Traps

### Wrong Chart Type For The Data

Match the type to the data and message. Mismatches obscure or distort.

### Truncated Or Misleading Axes

Check axis scaling for honesty. Truncation can exaggerate differences.

### Missing Or Unclear Labels And Legends

Label clearly so readers interpret without guessing.

### 3D Effects Or Visual Choices That Distort

Avoid effects that mislead. The visual impression should match the data.

### Decorative Or Misleading Color Use

Use color to encode meaning, not decorate. Consider accessibility.

### Charts Floating Without Narrative Integration

Reference and interpret each chart in the text. Connect to the argument.

### Uncited Or Inconsistent Data

Verify data integrity and cite sources for credibility and verification.

## Self-Check

Before treating chart selection and design as adequate, verify:

- Each chart's type fits the data and the message it aims to convey.
- Axes are scaled honestly, without truncation or inconsistency that misleads.
- Axes are labeled with what they measure and in what units.
- Data series are labeled directly or through a clear legend.
- Each chart has a title stating what it shows or finds.
- The chart reveals the true pattern without distortion from effects or encoding.
- Color and visual encoding aid comprehension and are accessibility-conscious.
- Each chart is referenced and interpreted in the text, serving the narrative.
- Data is accurate, internally consistent, and attributed to a cited source.
- A reader could interpret each chart correctly and verify its data.
