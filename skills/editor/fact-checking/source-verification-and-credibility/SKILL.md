# Source Verification And Credibility

Fact-checking is the discipline of confirming that claims rest on sources that actually exist, actually say what is attributed, and are credible enough to bear the weight placed on them. It is not adversarial skepticism for its own sake; it is the systematic verification that protects a publication's accuracy and the trust of its readers. A claim can sound authoritative and be wrong because the source is misquoted, is secondary rather than primary, has a conflict of interest, or simply does not say what the writer believes it says. Source verification is the work of finding these failures before publication.

Use this skill when fact-checking reportage, nonfiction, analysis, or any work making verifiable claims. It covers tracing claims to primary sources, assessing credibility and independence, evaluating expert authority, handling secondary and anonymous sources, and confirming that sources support the claims built on them. The editor's task is to ensure every load-bearing claim can be traced to a source that genuinely bears its weight.

## Core Rules

### Trace Every Load-Bearing Claim To Its Primary Source

Not every sentence needs verification, but every claim the work depends on does. The first task is to identify load-bearing claims and trace each to its origin, ideally the primary source rather than a secondary report of it.

A primary source is the original document, dataset, study, interview, or record. A secondary source reports on or summarizes a primary source. Tracing to primary matters because each layer of summarization can drift, drop qualifiers, or sharpen claims beyond what the original supports. For each load-bearing claim, find the primary source, read it, and confirm it says what the draft says it says. Where the primary is unavailable, document the best available source and the gap. Claims resting on untraceable origins are claims the work cannot fully stand behind.

### Assess Credibility Against The Claim's Stakes

Source credibility is not binary; it is calibrated to the stakes of the claim it supports. A low-stakes background fact may rest on a reputable general reference. A claim that damages a reputation, asserts a crime, or contradicts expert consensus needs correspondingly stronger sourcing.

For each claim, weigh whether the source's credibility matches the stakes. Consider the source's expertise in the specific field, independence from interested parties, track record for accuracy, transparency about methods and data, and the recency of the information. A credentialed source speaking outside their expertise is weaker than their credentials suggest. An interested source, a company on its own product, an advocate on their cause, may be useful but must be labeled and ideally corroborated. Match source weight to claim stakes, and where the source cannot bear the weight, strengthen the sourcing or narrow the claim.

### Evaluate Expert Authority In The Relevant Field

Expertise is field-specific. A Nobel laureate in physics is not an authority on virology, and a respected clinician is not an authority on health policy economics. Fact-checking must evaluate whether a cited expert's authority actually covers the claim attributed to them.

Check the expert's credentials, publications, and standing in the specific subfield relevant to the claim. Watch for credential inflation, where a broad title is used to imply authority over a narrow question outside the expert's work. Watch for experts whose prominence comes from media rather than from peer recognition in their field. Where the claim is contested within the field, a single expert does not settle it; the check should reflect the range of expert opinion. Authority is real and valuable, but it must be authority in the right field and weighed against the field's consensus.

### Confirm The Source Actually Supports The Claim

The most common fact-checking failure is the source that exists and is credible but does not say what the draft claims. Writers summarize, paraphrase, and interpret, and each step can move the meaning. Verification requires comparing the claim to the source, not merely confirming the source exists.

For each cited claim, read the relevant portion of the source and compare. Common drifts include dropping a qualifier the original included, treating a hypothesis as a finding, treating a finding about a narrow sample as a general conclusion, converting a correlation into a causal claim, and citing a secondary summary that itself misreported the primary. Where the source supports less than the claim asserts, narrow the claim to what the source bears. Where the source says something different, correct the claim. Existing and credible is not the same as supportive.

### Handle Secondary Sources With Explicit Caution

Secondary sources, news reports, summaries, and reviews are often necessary, especially when primaries are inaccessible, but they carry greater drift risk and should be treated with explicit caution.

When relying on a secondary source, identify it as secondary and, where stakes are high, trace toward the primary it reports on. Note where the secondary is the best available source and where the primary should be sought. Be especially cautious with secondaries that themselves cite other secondaries, creating chains where errors compound. For high-stakes claims, a secondary is a lead, not a destination; use it to find the primary, and verify there. Document the level of sourcing so readers and later editors know what stands behind each claim.

### Scrutinize Anonymous And Unattributed Sources

Anonymous sources are sometimes necessary to obtain information that would not otherwise emerge, but they transfer the credibility burden from the reader to the publication. Fact-checking must scrutinize them more heavily, not less, because the reader cannot.

For anonymous sources, confirm the standard for anonymity is met: genuine risk to the source, direct knowledge of the claim, and no on-the-record alternative. Verify the source's proximity to the information and their possible motives. Where possible, corroborate the claim with a second independent source, especially for damaging or contested claims. Describe the source as specifically as safety allows, since "a senior official involved in the decision" is more credible than "sources said." Anonymous attack claims against identifiable people carry special risk and warrant the highest corroboration standard.

### Check For Corroboration On Contested Claims

A single source for a contested claim is fragile, regardless of that source's quality. Where claims are disputed, where they affect reputation or policy, or where the field itself is divided, corroboration is part of verification.

Seek independent corroboration, a second source that did not derive from the first, for contested or high-stakes claims. Distinguish true independence from circular sourcing, where multiple outlets repeat the same origin. Where corroboration is partial or absent, the claim should be hedged, attributed to its single source with that limitation visible, or withheld until better sourcing exists. Corroboration is not about majority rule; it is about whether the claim rests on a foundation robust enough to publish.

### Verify Recency Against The Claim's Time Sensitivity

A source can be credible, primary, and supportive, and still be wrong for the present if the claim is time-sensitive and the source is outdated. Data, law, regulation, technology, and any fast-moving field require recency checking.

For each claim, ask whether the relevant facts change over time. If so, confirm the source is current enough to support the claim as stated. Watch for data presented as current that is actually from an earlier period, laws cited without checking for amendment, and technology descriptions that predate significant changes. Where the field moves fast, prefer the most recent authoritative source and note the date. A correct citation to an outdated source is still an error if the claim presents the information as current.

### Document The Verification Trail

Fact-checking must be reconstructable. If a claim is challenged after publication, the publication needs to show what was checked, against what source, by whom, and when. Undocumented verification cannot be defended.

Maintain a verification record for each checked claim: the claim, the source, the level of sourcing, the result of the check, any corrections made, and the date. Keep links or copies of sources, since web sources change and disappear. For high-stakes claims, note the reasoning behind sourcing decisions. A complete verification trail converts a post-publication challenge from a crisis into a routine response and protects both the publication and the fact-checker.

## Common Traps

### Confirming The Source Exists Without Confirming It Supports

A credible source that does not say what is claimed is no support. Compare claim to source.

### Secondary Treated As Primary

Each layer of summarization drifts. Trace to primary where stakes warrant.

### Credential Authority Outside The Field

A broad title does not confer authority over a narrow question. Evaluate expertise in the relevant subfield.

### Single-Sourcing Contested Claims

One source for a disputed or damaging claim is fragile. Seek independent corroboration.

### Anonymous Claims Without Corroboration

Anonymity shifts credibility burden to the publication. Scrutinize and corroborate, especially for attacks.

### Outdated Sources For Time-Sensitive Claims

A correct citation to an old source is an error if the claim presents it as current. Check recency.

### Circular Sourcing

Multiple outlets repeating one origin is not corroboration. Verify true independence.

### Undocumented Verification and authority Treated As Settling Contest

Unreconstructable checking cannot be defended. Maintain the verification trail.

A single expert does not settle a divided field. Reflect the range of expert opinion.

## Self-Check

Before treating source verification as complete, verify:

- Every load-bearing claim has been traced to its primary source where possible, with gaps documented.
- Source credibility has been calibrated to each claim's stakes, with stronger sourcing for higher-stakes claims.
- Cited experts' authority has been evaluated in the specific relevant subfield, not just by broad credentials.
- Each cited source has been confirmed to actually support the claim attributed to it, not merely to exist.
- Secondary sources are identified as such and traced toward primary where stakes warrant.
- Anonymous sources meet the standard for anonymity and are corroborated where stakes are high.
- Contested and high-stakes claims have independent corroboration, not circular repetition.
- Recency has been checked for time-sensitive claims, with outdated sources replaced or flagged.
- A verification trail documents each checked claim, its source, the result, and the date.
- The work's load-bearing claims can each be defended from the sources behind them.
