# Names Dates And Proper Nouns

Proper nouns are the most visible errors in any published work. A misspelled name, a wrong date, an outdated title, or a mislocated institution is the kind of mistake readers notice instantly and treat as evidence of broader carelessness, because these are facts that can be checked and that the reader often knows. Unlike arguments or interpretations, names and dates are binary: they are either right or wrong, and a wrong one cannot be defended as a matter of judgment. The discipline of verifying proper nouns is therefore one of the highest-value, lowest-glamour tasks in fact-checking.

Use this skill when fact-checking any work containing personal names, organizational names, titles and honorifics, dates, place names, and other identifying details. It covers verifying spelling and forms of names, checking titles and affiliations, confirming biographical dates, validating place names and jurisdictions, and ensuring that historical and contemporary entities are named and dated consistently and correctly. The editor's task is to treat every proper noun as a claim requiring confirmation against an authoritative source.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Proper Noun As A Claim To Verify

The foundational discipline is to stop reading proper nouns as furniture and start reading them as assertions. Each name, title, date, and place is a factual claim that the work is asserting as true, and each must be confirmed against a source that is authoritative for that specific claim.

Build a list of every proper noun in the work and verify each against the best available source. For living people, prefer the person's own published form, their website, institutional page, or professional listing. For organizations, prefer the organization's own current materials. For historical figures and events, prefer scholarly reference works and primary documents over crowd-sourced summaries. The act of listing forces nothing to be skipped on the assumption that it is "obviously right." The most damaging errors are often the names the editor assumed were too familiar to check.

### Verify Name Spelling And Preferred Form Against The Subject

Names are not merely spelled correctly in an abstract sense; they are spelled and presented in the form the subject uses. Verification means confirming the subject's own preferred spelling, capitalization, hyphenation, and ordering, not the most common or most phonetic rendering.

Check the preferred form against the subject's own publications, institutional profile, or official biography. Watch for transliteration variants, where the same name has several accepted English spellings and the subject has a preference. Watch for name order, given name first versus family name first, which varies by culture and by the subject's own usage. Watch for middle initials that some bearers use consistently and others omit. For organizations, confirm the exact legal or operating name, since many institutions have both a formal name and a common short form, and the work should use the appropriate one for its context. Where the subject has changed their name, use the current form and note the prior name where relevant for identification.

### Confirm Titles Honorifics And Affiliations Are Current

Titles, honorifics, and institutional affiliations are time-sensitive in a way surnames are not. A person who held a title last year may not hold it now, and an error here misrepresents their authority and standing. Verification must check not only that a title was held but that it is correctly attributed for the time being described.

For each title or affiliation, confirm it against the subject's current institutional page or a contemporaneous source for the period described. Watch for inherited titles versus current roles, a former minister described as "minister" without a temporal qualifier misleads. Confirm academic and professional titles against the granting institution, since inflated or honorary titles are sometimes presented as earned credentials. For honorifics, follow the subject's preference and the publication's style, recognizing that the wrong honorific can read as disrespect or as unearned deference. Note the date of verification, because affiliations change and a check that was current at editing may be stale by publication.

### Verify Dates Against Authoritative Chronologies

Dates are the proper nouns of time, and they are checked the same way: against a source authoritative for that specific date. Birth and death dates, founding dates, event dates, and publication dates each have characteristic sources and characteristic failure modes.

For biographical dates, confirm against vital records, authoritative biographical dictionaries, or the subject's own published materials, and note where sources disagree. For founding and organizational dates, confirm against the institution's own records or regulatory filings. For event dates, confirm against contemporaneous reporting or primary documents, and be alert to calendar differences, different conventions for when a year begins, time zones affecting "the same day," and the distinction between the date something happened and the date it was announced. Where sources disagree on a date, prefer the most authoritative and note the uncertainty rather than silently choosing one. A date presented as exact when sources conflict is a claim stronger than the evidence supports.

### Validate Place Names And Jurisdictional Accuracy

Place names carry political and legal weight that a casual editor may miss. The name used for a territory, city, or region can imply a jurisdictional claim, an ethnic framing, or a historical position, and the wrong choice can offend, mislead, or expose the publication to dispute. Verification must check not only that a place exists but that it is named in the form appropriate to the context.

Confirm place names against current authoritative gazetteers and maps, and be alert to disputed or changed names. Territories that have changed sovereignty may have competing names; choose the one appropriate to the context and the publication's policy, and note the dispute where it matters. For jurisdictional claims, verify that the place named is actually the place with legal authority over the matter described; a city named where a state or nation has jurisdiction is a substantive error. Watch for places that share names, the Springfield problem, where the right name points to the wrong locality without a qualifier. Where a place has both a local and an exonym, follow the publication's style and the subject's preference, and be consistent.

### Check That Entities Are Named For The Right Period

Historical and contemporary entities must be named in the form correct for the period being described. Countries, institutions, and organizations change their names, borders, and structures over time, and using the current name for a past entity, or vice versa, is an anachronism that can distort meaning.

For each entity, confirm the name used matches the period described. A reference to a twentieth-century institution under its current name, when it had a different name then, misleads readers trying to trace it. Borders and compositions change; a reference to a country's territory should reflect its extent at the relevant time, not its modern extent. Where the entity no longer exists, name it in its historical form and note its successor where helpful. The discipline is to let the period govern the name, not to project the present onto the past or the past onto the present.

### Maintain Consistency Across The Work

A name or date verified correctly in one place can still appear wrong elsewhere through inconsistency. Verification is not complete when each instance is checked individually; it is complete when all instances agree.

After verifying the preferred forms, check that every appearance of each name, title, date, and place uses the same form throughout. Watch for variant spellings introduced by different draft sections, by translation, or by source drift. Watch for dates that shift between sections, a birth date correct in the biography but wrong in a later caption. Use the verified forms as a controlled vocabulary and reconcile the manuscript against it. Consistency is part of accuracy, because a reader encountering two forms of the same name reasonably assumes they refer to two different entities.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Familiar Names Need No Check

The most recognizable names are often the most misspelled, because they are assumed obvious. Verify every proper noun, especially the famous ones.

### Using The Common Form Over The Preferred Form

A name can be "correctly" spelled in a common variant and still wrong because it is not the subject's form. Check the subject's own usage.

### Stale Titles And Affiliations

A title held last year is not current now. Confirm affiliations against current sources and note the verification date.

### Projecting Current Names Onto Past Entities

Using a modern name for a historical institution or territory is an anachronism. Name entities for the period described.

### Disputed Place Names Chosen Casually

Place names carry political and jurisdictional weight. Choose deliberately and follow policy, noting disputes where relevant.

### Silent Disagreement Among Sources

When authorities conflict on a date, presenting one as exact hides the uncertainty. Note the disagreement or hedge.

### Inconsistent Forms Across Sections

A name verified in one chapter and misspelled in another reads as two entities. Reconcile the whole manuscript to verified forms.

### Calendar And Time-Zone Confusion

"The same day" differs across time zones and calendar conventions. Confirm what a date denotes in its source context.

## Self-Check

Before treating proper-noun verification as complete, verify:

- Every proper noun in the work has been listed and checked against an authoritative source, with none skipped as "obvious."
- Personal names use the subject's own preferred spelling, capitalization, hyphenation, and ordering, not merely a common variant.
- Titles, honorifics, and affiliations have been confirmed as current for the period described, with a verification date noted.
- Biographical, founding, and event dates have been confirmed against authoritative chronologies, with source disagreements noted rather than silently resolved.
- Place names have been validated against current gazetteers, with disputed names handled per policy and jurisdictional accuracy confirmed.
- Historical and contemporary entities are named in the form correct for the period described, avoiding anachronism.
- All appearances of each name and date are consistent across the manuscript, reconciled to a single verified form.
- Calendar, time-zone, and dating-convention issues have been considered for any date presented as exact.
- A reader who knew each subject would find every name, title, date, and place presented as the subject themselves would recognize and accept.
