# Data And Statistics Verification

Numbers carry a false authority. A statistic sounds definitive in a way prose does not, and readers, and writers, often accept figures that they would question if stated in words. This makes quantitative claims especially dangerous: a wrong or misleading number propagates faster and is corrected slower than a wrong sentence. Verifying data and statistics is not just arithmetic; it is checking that the right number is used, that it measures what is claimed, that the methodology supports the interpretation, and that the presentation does not mislead. A figure can be technically accurate and deeply misleading, and catching that is the fact-checker's job.

Use this skill when fact-checking any work containing statistics, data, percentages, charts, or quantitative claims. It covers verifying figures against sources, checking calculations and units, evaluating methodology and sample validity, spotting misleading visualizations, confirming denominator and base-rate correctness, and ensuring quantitative claims are presented in context. The editor's task is to ensure numbers are not just correct but honest.

## Core Rules

### Verify Every Number Against Its Authoritative Source

Every quantitative claim should be traceable to a source, and the source should be checked. Numbers drift through repetition, transcription, and rounding, and a figure that circulates widely can still be wrong.

For each number, identify the authoritative source, the original dataset, report, or study, and confirm the figure matches. Watch for common drifts: transposed digits, percentages converted from fractions incorrectly, figures from different years or geographies combined, and numbers cited from secondary summaries that misreported the primary. Where a number is rounded, confirm the rounding is consistent and labeled. A number that cannot be traced to a source is a number the work cannot stand behind, regardless of how plausible it sounds.

### Check Calculations, Units, And Conversions

Many numerical errors are arithmetic. Percentages that do not sum correctly, growth rates miscalculated, unit conversions wrong, and aggregations that double-count or omit categories are common and catchable.

Recompute derived figures: percentages from raw numbers, changes over time, totals from components, averages and medians. Check unit conversions, especially across metric and imperial, currency, and time periods. Verify that aggregations include the right items and exclude the right ones. For complex calculations, walk through the method rather than trusting the result. Arithmetic errors are the easiest to catch and the most embarrassing to publish, because they are objectively verifiable.

### Confirm The Statistic Measures What The Claim Says

A correct number can support the wrong claim if it measures something different from what the text asserts. This is the most common and most subtle quantitative error, and it requires understanding what the statistic actually represents.

For each statistical claim, confirm the definition behind the number. A "crime rate" may be per capita or absolute, may include or exclude certain categories, and may cover a different geography or period than the claim implies. An "unemployment" figure may use different definitions across sources. A "survival rate" may be five-year or overall, adjusted or crude. Match the statistic's actual definition to the claim's wording, and where they diverge, correct the claim or re-source the number. Definition mismatch turns accurate figures into false claims.

### Evaluate Methodology And Sample Validity

A statistic is only as trustworthy as the method that produced it. Fact-checking quantitative claims requires evaluating whether the methodology can support the claim, especially for surveys, polls, studies, and comparisons.

Check the sample size, composition, and selection method. A small or non-representative sample cannot support broad claims. Check for selection bias, self-selection, survivorship bias, and confounding variables. For comparisons, confirm the groups are comparable and the differences are not explained by other factors. For studies, consider whether the design, observational versus experimental, can support causal claims. Where the methodology is weak or opaque, the claim should be hedged, re-sourced, or cut. Methodology is where strong-sounding statistics often collapse.

### Check Denominators, Base Rates, And Comparisons

Many misleading statistics arise from missing or wrong denominators. An absolute number without a rate, a percentage without a base, or a comparison without a common denominator can distort dramatically.

For each quantitative claim, identify the denominator and confirm it is correct and stated. Watch for base-rate neglect, where a large absolute number obscures a small rate, or a small rate obscures a large absolute impact. Check comparisons for common baselines: comparing two rates with different denominators, different time periods, or different definitions produces false contrasts. Where a claim presents a number without its denominator or base rate, the omission may be misleading even if the number is accurate. Honest quantitative presentation requires the denominator visible.

### Spot Misleading Charts And Visualizations

Charts can mislead as powerfully as numbers, sometimes more so because readers trust the visual. Truncated axes, cherry-picked time ranges, inconsistent scales, and distorted proportions all create false impressions from accurate underlying data.

Examine each chart for honesty. Check that axes start at a baseline or clearly indicate they do not, that scales are consistent across compared charts, that time ranges are not cherry-picked to manufacture a trend, and that proportions in pie, bar, and area charts are accurate. Confirm that the chart's visual impression matches the data's actual story. Where a chart overstates a trend, minimizes a difference, or implies a correlation the data does not support, it must be revised regardless of the underlying numbers. Visual honesty is part of statistical honesty.

### Verify Trend, Change, And Causation Claims

Claims about trends, changes, and causes are higher-order statistical claims that require more than a correct number. They require that the comparison be valid and that the inferred relationship be supported.

For trend claims, confirm the time period, the baseline, and that the change is real and not an artifact of definition or measurement change. For comparison claims, confirm the entities are comparable and the metric is consistent. For causal claims, distinguish correlation from causation and check whether the method, or the source's method, can support causation. Causal claims from observational data require careful qualification. Trend, change, and causation claims are where statistics most often overreach, and they warrant the heaviest scrutiny.

### Ensure Context Accompanies The Number

A correct statistic in isolation can mislead. A number's meaning depends on its context: what it is compared to, whether it is high or low, what the trend is, and what the uncertainty is. Fact-checking includes verifying that the presentation gives the reader the context to interpret the figure.

For each quantitative claim, check whether the reader has enough context: a comparison point, a sense of scale, the direction of change, and the uncertainty or margin of error. A number presented without context invites misinterpretation, even when accurate. Where context is missing, add it; where context would change the impression, ensure it is present. Honest statistics inform; context-free statistics can manipulate regardless of intent.

### Check Uncertainty, Significance, And Confidence

Quantitative claims often present precision that the underlying data does not support. Margins of error, confidence intervals, and significance are part of what a number means, and omitting them can overstate certainty.

For survey and poll results, check that margins of error are stated and that claimed differences exceed them. For study results, check whether findings are statistically significant and whether confidence intervals are reported. For estimates and projections, check the uncertainty range. Where the draft presents a precise figure without the uncertainty that accompanies it, the presentation overstates what is known. Calibrate the certainty of the presentation to the certainty of the data.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Circulated Figures Without Tracing

Widely repeated numbers can still be wrong. Verify each against its authoritative source.

### Arithmetic And Conversion Errors

Percentages, growth rates, and unit conversions are common failure points. Recompute derived figures.

### Definition Mismatch

A correct number measuring the wrong thing supports a false claim. Match statistic definition to claim wording.

### Weak Methodology Treated As Strong

Small or biased samples cannot support broad claims. Evaluate methodology against the claim's scope.

### Missing Or Wrong Denominators

Absolute numbers without rates or bases distort. Make denominators visible and correct.

### Misleading Visualizations

Truncated axes and cherry-picked ranges distort from accurate data. Check visual honesty.

### Correlation Sold As Causation

Observational data cannot establish causation without qualification. Scrutinize causal claims.

### Context-Free Numbers and false Precision

Accurate figures without comparison or scale invite misinterpretation. Provide interpretive context.

Precise presentation of uncertain data overstates what is known. Calibrate certainty to the data.

## Self-Check

Before treating data and statistics verification as complete, verify:

- Every quantitative claim has been traced to its authoritative source and confirmed to match.
- Calculations, unit conversions, and aggregations have been recomputed and found correct.
- Each statistic's definition matches what the claim asserts, with no measurement mismatch.
- Methodology and sample validity have been evaluated against the scope of each claim.
- Denominators, base rates, and comparisons use correct and visible common baselines.
- Charts and visualizations have been checked for honest axes, scales, ranges, and proportions.
- Trend, change, and causation claims rest on valid comparisons and supported relationships.
- Each number is presented with enough context for the reader to interpret it honestly.
- Margins of error, confidence intervals, and significance are reflected in the presentation.
- The quantitative claims are not just technically accurate but honestly presented in context.
