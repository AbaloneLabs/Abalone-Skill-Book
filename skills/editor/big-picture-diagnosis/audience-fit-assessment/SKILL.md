# Audience Fit Assessment

A draft can be well written, well structured, and well evidenced, and still fail because it is aimed at the wrong reader, or at no reader in particular. Audience fit is the judgment of whether the work's register, depth, prior-knowledge assumptions, examples, and tone actually match the reader it needs to reach. When fit is wrong, the work either talks down to experts, over the heads of beginners, past practitioners who need action, or at a general reader who never asked for the topic. Editors who treat audience as a label rather than a set of concrete expectations produce work that satisfies no one.

Use this skill when reading any draft to identify its actual target reader, test whether the draft serves that reader, detect mismatches in register, depth, and assumptions, and recommend retargeting when the fit is wrong. It covers distinguishing primary from secondary audiences, judging whether the work's choices serve the primary reader, and deciding whether to change the work or change the stated audience. The editor's job is to make the work meet a real reader's real needs.

## Core Rules

### Name The Primary Reader Concretely

"General reader," "professionals," and "students" are not audiences; they are categories. A real audience is a specific person with specific prior knowledge, specific reasons for reading, and specific things they need the work to do. Force concreteness.

Describe the primary reader in operational terms: what they already know, what they do not know, why they would pick up this work, what they want to be able to do or decide after reading, and what would make them stop reading. A work aimed at "IT managers evaluating cloud migration vendors" is actionable; a work aimed at "business readers" is not. If the author cannot describe the primary reader concretely, the work has no audience, and that is the first problem.

### Test Assumptions Against The Named Reader

Every draft makes assumptions about what the reader already knows. Audience fit fails when those assumptions are wrong: too high, and the reader is lost; too low, and the expert is bored and insulted.

Walk through the opening and each major section asking what prior knowledge it assumes. Does it define key terms, or use them as if known? Does it assume familiarity with concepts, tools, debates, or jargon? Compare the assumed knowledge against what your concrete primary reader actually has. Where the work assumes too much, add context or definitions; where it assumes too little, cut the condescension. Mismatched assumptions are the most common and most damaging audience-fit failure.

### Match Register And Tone To The Relationship

Register is the formality and distance the work establishes with its reader. Different audiences expect different registers, and a mismatch reads as off-key even when the content is correct. A peer-reviewed paper that cracks jokes confuses its audience; a popular guide that reads like a statute alienates its audience.

Identify the register the work currently uses and judge it against the audience's expectation. Consider formality, use of first or second person, directness, humor, and emotional warmth. A practitioner audience often wants direct, second-person, action-oriented register; a scholarly audience often wants measured, evidence-led register; a general audience often wants warm, concrete, story-led register. Mismatches are fixable, but they must be fixed consistently, not sprinkled.

### Judge Whether Examples And Evidence Serve The Reader

Audience fit shows up sharply in examples. The same topic needs different illustrations for different readers. Examples that miss the audience's world feel irrelevant and make the work feel abstract even when it is concrete.

Check whether the examples, cases, analogies, and evidence are drawn from the primary reader's domain and experience. A guide for hospital administrators should use healthcare examples, not generic business cases. A book for teenagers should not illustrate with corporate scenarios. When examples come from a different world than the reader's, the work feels aimed elsewhere. Recommend replacing mismatched examples with ones that land in the reader's actual context.

### Detect The Two-Audience Trap

Many drafts try to serve two audiences at once, usually a primary expert audience and a secondary general audience. This almost always produces a work that fully satisfies neither, because the choices that serve one audience conflict with the choices that serve the other.

Look for signs of dual audiences: alternating depth levels, explanations that experts will skip and novices will need, tone shifts between sections, and framing that tries to be both authoritative and accessible. If the work serves two audiences, recommend committing to the primary one and treating the secondary as a bonus, or splitting into two works. Trying to optimize for both usually optimizes for neither.

### Check Whether The Work Delivers The Reader's Payoff

Audience fit is ultimately about whether the reader gets what they came for. Different readers want different payoffs: a decision, a skill, an understanding, an emotion, an argument. A work that does not deliver the payoff its audience seeks has failed fit regardless of its other qualities.

Identify what the primary reader wants from the work, then check whether the ending delivers it. A practical reader wants to be able to act; does the work end with usable guidance? A reader seeking understanding wants a clear synthesis; does the work provide one? A reader seeking an argument wants a defensible conclusion; does the work reach one? If the payoff mismatches the audience's need, the fit is broken at the most important point.

### Decide Whether To Retarget The Work Or The Audience

When audience fit is wrong, there are two cures. You can change the work to fit the stated audience, or you can change the stated audience to fit the work. The right choice depends on which is more fixed.

If the audience is fixed by the commission, the market, or the purpose, retarget the work: adjust assumptions, register, examples, and payoff to serve that reader. If the work is strong but aimed at the wrong stated audience, retarget the audience: reframe the title, introduction, and framing to attract the reader it actually serves. Misdiagnosing which to change leads to wasted revision. Judge which is more fixed before recommending.

### Respect Audience Diversity Within The Primary Group

Even a well-defined primary audience contains variation in expertise, background, and need. Strong audience fit acknowledges this without trying to serve everyone. Provide on-ramps for the less-prepared reader without burdening the core reader.

Use techniques like sidebars, glossaries, tiered explanations, and signposting that let readers self-select depth. The goal is not to flatten the audience but to make the work navigable for its real range. Do not, however, use this as an excuse to avoid committing to a primary reader.

## Common Traps

### Treating Audience As A Demographic Label

Age, profession, and education level are proxies, not audiences. Describe the reader's knowledge, motivation, and need, not just their category.

### Assuming The Author Is The Audience

Writers often unconsciously write for themselves or their peers. Check whether the work's assumptions match the stated reader or the author's own expertise.

### Confusing Accessibility With Dumbing Down

Making a work accessible to a broader audience is not the same as removing its rigor. The trap is refusing to add context out of fear of condescension, leaving the work inaccessible.

### Optimizing For The Author's Approval Rather Than The Reader's Need

When the author is the editor's client, it is tempting to please the author's preferences. Audience fit is judged against the reader, not the author's taste.

### Trying To Serve Everyone

A work aimed at everyone serves no one well. Commit to a primary reader and let secondary readers benefit where they can.

### Missing Register Inconsistency

A work that shifts register between sections feels unstable to the reader. Check register consistency, not just whether each section's register is individually defensible.

## Self-Check

Before treating an audience-fit assessment as complete, verify:

- The primary reader has been described concretely in terms of knowledge, motivation, and need, not just a demographic label.
- Prior-knowledge assumptions throughout the work have been tested against that reader, and mismatches flagged.
- Register and tone have been judged against the audience's expectation, and inconsistencies noted.
- Examples, cases, and evidence have been checked for whether they come from the reader's world.
- The two-audience trap has been checked for, and a primary audience commitment recommended if found.
- The work's payoff has been judged against what the primary reader actually wants.
- A decision has been made about whether to retarget the work or the stated audience, based on which is more fixed.
- Audience diversity within the primary group has been acknowledged with appropriate on-ramps, not used as an excuse to avoid commitment.
- The assessment resists the temptation to optimize for the author's preferences over the reader's needs.
- The feedback tells the author specifically how to retarget, not merely that the fit feels off.
