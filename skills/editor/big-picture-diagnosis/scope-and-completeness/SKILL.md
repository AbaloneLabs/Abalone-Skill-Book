# Scope And Completeness

Scope is the contract between a work and its reader about how much ground it will cover. Completeness is whether it actually covers that ground. These two judgments together determine whether a draft feels satisfying or hollow, authoritative or thin, focused or bloated. A work fails scope when it promises more than it can deliver, and it fails completeness when it delivers less than it promised. Editors who do not test scope explicitly end up with drafts that are either overreaching and shallow or under-built and unconvincing, and they often cannot tell which problem they are looking at.

Use this skill when reading any substantial draft to judge whether its scope is correctly sized for its length, evidence, and audience, and whether it is complete against its own promise. It covers sizing the claim to the evidence, identifying missing material that the promise requires, detecting overreach where the work strays beyond its competence, and deciding the right remedy among narrowing, expanding, cutting, or re-scoping. The editor's job is to align the work's promise with what it actually contains.

## Core Rules

### Read The Promise Before Judging The Coverage

Scope cannot be judged in the abstract. First determine what the work promises to cover, drawn from its title, introduction, framing, and stated audience. The promise defines the standard of completeness.

Write down the promise as a list of obligations. A guide titled "The Complete Guide to Home Solar" promises coverage of sizing, financing, installation, maintenance, incentives, and pitfalls. Once the obligations are explicit, you can check each one. If the promise is vague, the first scope problem is that the work has not committed to what it covers. Force the author to state the scope, then measure the draft against it.

### Size The Claim To The Evidence And Length

A common scope failure is the work that attempts a vast claim in a small space with thin evidence. The claim outruns the support, and the reader loses trust. Scope must be sized to what the evidence and length can actually carry.

For each major claim, ask whether the manuscript provides enough evidence, examples, data, or analysis to support it within the available length. If a 2,000-word article claims to explain an entire industry's dysfunction, the scope is almost certainly too broad. The remedy is usually narrowing: focus on one mechanism, one decision, one case, or one audience. Narrowing is almost always safer than padding. A smaller claim fully supported beats a large claim thinly gestured at.

### Detect Overreach Beyond Competence And Evidence

Overreach is scope that exceeds not just length but the author's actual evidence, expertise, or access. A work that strays into claims it cannot substantiate becomes unreliable exactly where it is most ambitious.

Watch for sections where the author generalizes beyond their data, opines on fields outside their expertise, or makes causal claims the evidence only correlatively supports. Overreach is especially dangerous because it often reads confidently. Flag each instance and recommend either backing the claim with evidence, softening it to what the evidence supports, or cutting it. A work that stays within its demonstrated competence is more authoritative than one that reaches beyond it.

### Identify Required Material The Promise Demands

Completeness fails when the promise implies material the work does not provide. The reader reaches for a section that should exist and finds a gap. These gaps are often invisible to the author, who knows the material and fills it in mentally.

Map the promise's implied obligations and check each against the table of contents or structure. Common gaps include missing definitions of key terms, absent counterarguments, skipped prerequisites the audience needs, missing safety warnings or caveats, unstated assumptions, and conclusions that require a decision the work never helps the reader make. For instructional or practical works, check whether every process is complete from start to finish, including setup, execution, troubleshooting, and verification. Report each gap as a specific missing piece, not a vague sense of thinness.

### Distinguish Missing Material From Intentional Omission

Not every absence is a gap. A well-scoped work deliberately excludes material outside its promise to stay focused. The editor must distinguish a completeness failure from a legitimate boundary.

Ask whether the omitted material falls inside or outside the stated promise. If the work promises a beginner's introduction, omitting advanced techniques is correct scoping, not a gap. If it promises comprehensive coverage, the same omission is a failure. Judge omissions against the explicit promise, and when the promise is ambiguous, recommend the author clarify the boundary rather than silently filling or cutting.

### Check For Competing Scopes Within One Work

Some drafts try to be two works at once: a practical guide and a theoretical argument, a scholarly monograph and a popular book, a reference and a narrative. Competing scopes produce a manuscript that satisfies neither audience fully.

Identify whether the work is trying to serve multiple distinct scopes simultaneously. If so, recommend committing to one primary scope and demoting the other to a secondary role or removing it. A work with one clear scope serving one primary audience is stronger than a work that half-serves several.

### Match Depth To Audience Need

Scope is not only about breadth but depth. The same topic requires different depth for different audiences. A scope that is correct in breadth can still fail by being too shallow or too deep for its stated reader.

Judge depth against the audience the work claims. A practitioner audience needs operational detail and worked examples; a general audience needs context and plain explanation; an expert audience needs novelty and rigor. If the depth mismatches the audience, the scope is wrong even if the breadth is right. Recommend adjusting depth, or adjusting the stated audience to match the depth the work actually provides.

### Choose The Remedy That Matches The Diagnosis

Scope and completeness problems have different cures, and applying the wrong one wastes effort. Match the remedy to the specific failure.

Narrow when the claim is too broad for the evidence; reduce the scope to what can be supported. Expand when the promise requires material that is missing; add the needed sections. Cut when the work contains material outside its promise; remove the stray sections. Re-scope when the audience or purpose is unclear; clarify the promise first, then measure against it. Diagnose precisely so the remedy fits.

## Common Traps

### Equating Length With Completeness

A long work can be incomplete if it pads the wrong areas while leaving required material absent. Measure completeness against the promise, not the page count.

### Treating Overreach As Ambition

Ambition that outruns evidence is a liability, not a virtue. A smaller, fully supported claim is more credible than a sweeping, under-supported one.

### Filling Every Gap Indiscriminately

Adding material to every perceived gap can bloat the work and blur its focus. Distinguish required material from material that belongs in a different work.

### Missing The Audience-Depth Mismatch

A scope that is correct in breadth but wrong in depth still fails. Always check depth against the stated audience.

### Judging Scope Without First Fixing The Promise

If you assess scope against an ambiguous promise, the diagnosis will be unreliable. Force the promise explicit before measuring completeness.

### Recommending Expansion When Narrowing Is The Cure

When a claim outruns its evidence, the safe remedy is usually narrowing the claim, not gathering more evidence under time pressure. Match the remedy to the failure.

## Self-Check

Before treating a scope and completeness assessment as complete, verify:

- The work's promise has been stated explicitly as a list of obligations drawn from title, introduction, and framing.
- Each major claim has been tested for whether the evidence and length can carry it.
- Overreach beyond competence or evidence has been flagged for each instance, with a recommended softening or cut.
- Required material implied by the promise has been checked section by section, and gaps reported specifically.
- Omissions have been distinguished as completeness failures versus intentional, defensible boundaries.
- Competing scopes within the work have been identified, with a recommended primary commitment.
- Depth has been judged against the stated audience, not just breadth.
- The recommended remedy for each problem matches the diagnosis: narrow, expand, cut, or re-scope.
- The assessment distinguishes thinness caused by overreach from thinness caused by missing material.
- The feedback tells the author exactly what to add, cut, or narrow rather than reporting a vague sense of imbalance.
