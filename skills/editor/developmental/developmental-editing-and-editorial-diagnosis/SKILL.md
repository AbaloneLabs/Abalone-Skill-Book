# Developmental Editing And Editorial Diagnosis

Developmental editing asks whether the piece is the right piece before asking whether the sentences are good. A draft can be grammatically clean and still fail because it serves the wrong reader, hides its main point, lacks evidence, spreads across too many topics, starts too late, ends without resolution, or promises something it never delivers.

Use this skill before detailed line editing, copyediting, publication review, or final proofreading. The editor's job at this stage is to diagnose the draft's underlying shape and give revision direction that makes the work more coherent, useful, and honest. Do not start by fixing style if the structure, purpose, or evidence is not yet ready.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Draft's Editorial Contract

First determine what the draft appears to promise the reader. This may differ from what the writer intended. Look at the title, opening, framing, genre, structure, and ending. Ask what a reasonable reader would expect after the first few paragraphs.

Clarify:

- who the apparent reader is;
- what the piece promises to explain, argue, reveal, or help with;
- what genre contract it invokes;
- what level of depth it implies;
- what emotional, practical, or intellectual payoff it seems to offer;
- whether the ending fulfills that promise.

If the promise is unclear or mismatched, revision should begin there. Do not merely tell the writer to "clarify." Name the exact contract problem: wrong reader, inflated promise, hidden purpose, missing payoff, genre mismatch, or unsupported scope.

### Diagnose Before Prescribing

A developmental editor should not throw a list of preferences at a draft. Diagnose the cause of the problem before proposing a fix.

For each significant issue, separate:

- symptom: what the reader experiences;
- cause: why it happens;
- effect: why it matters;
- revision direction: what kind of change would solve it.

For example, "the middle drags" is a symptom. The cause may be repeated claims, misplaced background, weak section jobs, too many examples doing the same work, or lack of escalation. Each cause requires a different edit.

### Find The Real Center

Many drafts discover their actual subject late. The editor should look for the live center: the claim, question, scene, tension, example, or decision that gives the piece force.

Search for:

- the strongest paragraph;
- the clearest articulation of the problem;
- the sentence that sounds like the real thesis;
- the most vivid scene or example;
- the most consequential objection;
- the moment where stakes become visible;
- the material that the rest of the draft seems to orbit.

Then decide whether the draft should be reorganized around that center. Do not preserve a weak introduction simply because it is already written. The editor may recommend beginning later, moving the true thesis earlier, reframing the title, or cutting an opening that belongs to an older version of the draft.

### Test Scope Against Evidence And Length

Scope is one of the editor's most important levers. A piece can fail by being too broad, too narrow, too shallow, or too scattered.

Ask:

- Is the topic narrow enough for the available evidence?
- Is the claim sized to the draft's length?
- Are there too many audiences?
- Are adjacent topics competing with the main question?
- Does the draft need more reporting, examples, data, or scene work?
- Does the draft need to cut claims it cannot support?

Editors should prefer narrowing over padding. If a 1,500-word draft tries to explain a global industry, it may become stronger by focusing on one decision, one mechanism, one audience, or one case.

### Evaluate Structure As Reader Movement

Do not judge structure only by whether it has an introduction, body, and conclusion. Judge whether the reader moves through a meaningful sequence.

Map the draft:

- opening promise;
- first turn;
- main sections;
- evidence or scene placement;
- strongest escalation;
- counterargument or complication;
- final movement;
- ending payoff.

Then ask whether each part arrives when the reader needs it. Background may appear too early. Evidence may arrive before the claim it supports. A caveat may appear too late and make the reader feel misled. A conclusion may repeat instead of transform.

### Preserve The Writer's Best Intent

Developmental editing should improve the draft without replacing the writer's purpose with the editor's ego. Identify what the draft is trying to become, then help it become that more fully.

Preserve:

- the intended reader relationship;
- the writer's distinctive angle;
- necessary complexity;
- the best images, examples, or insights;
- the ethical stakes;
- the emotional truth when supported by the piece.

Challenge:

- unsupported claims;
- unclear audience;
- evasive structure;
- sentimental shortcuts;
- clever but distracting material;
- a frame that undermines the piece's own evidence.

Do not make every draft sound like the editor's preferred style.

### Separate Must-Fix From Nice-To-Have

Developmental feedback can overwhelm writers. Prioritize the changes that determine whether the piece works.

Use severity:

- blocking issue: the piece cannot succeed until this changes;
- major issue: the piece works but is significantly weakened;
- moderate issue: revision would improve clarity, flow, or force;
- optional issue: stylistic preference or enhancement.

A writer should know what to do first. If everything is marked equally, the feedback is not editorially useful.

### Make Feedback Actionable

Vague feedback creates confusion. "Strengthen the argument" is less useful than "move the causal claim into the introduction, then add evidence showing that the budget change preceded the staffing reduction."

Good developmental feedback names:

- where the issue appears;
- what the reader currently experiences;
- what the draft needs instead;
- one or two plausible revision paths;
- what should be preserved.

Do not rewrite the whole piece in feedback unless the task is explicitly rewriting. Give the writer control over the solution when possible.

### Know When The Draft Needs More Material

Some weaknesses cannot be fixed by rearranging. The draft may need reporting, examples, data, interviews, scenes, definitions, citations, or a clearer decision from the writer.

Flag missing material:

- unsupported central claims;
- absent counterarguments;
- examples that do not represent the claim;
- missing stakes for the reader;
- undefined key terms;
- a conclusion that requires a decision not yet made;
- story scenes without enough concrete detail;
- technical sections that need expert verification.

Do not pretend an under-reported or under-thought draft can be solved by style edits.

## Common Traps

### Line Editing A Broken Structure

Fixing sentences before the draft has a coherent shape can waste time and make necessary cuts emotionally harder. Diagnose first. Polish later.

### Giving Taste As If It Were Law

Editors have preferences. A developmental editor must distinguish personal taste from reader need, genre obligation, evidence problem, or structural failure.

### Overloading The Writer

A ten-page note on every possible issue may be accurate but unusable. Prioritize the few changes that unlock the draft.

### Replacing The Writer's Voice With The Editor's Voice

Developmental editing is not authorship by stealth. Preserve the writer's voice and purpose unless they conflict with clarity, truth, ethics, or audience fit.

### Treating Confusion As The Reader's Fault

If a reasonable reader gets lost, the structure or context likely needs work. Do not assume the reader should simply try harder.

### Mistaking More For Better

Adding background, citations, examples, or explanation can make a draft worse if it does not solve the actual problem. Sometimes the strongest edit is narrowing and cutting.

## Self-Check

Before treating developmental editing as complete, verify:

- The draft's apparent reader contract has been identified.
- The feedback distinguishes writer intent from what the draft currently delivers.
- The real center of the draft has been found or the absence of one has been named.
- Scope has been tested against evidence, length, and reader need.
- Structural feedback is based on reader movement, not outline preference.
- Major issues are diagnosed with symptom, cause, effect, and revision direction.
- Blocking issues are clearly separated from optional improvements.
- Feedback names what to preserve as well as what to change; the editor has not begun line-level polishing before solving structural problems
- Unsupported claims, missing evidence, or missing scenes are flagged as material gaps; the revision path is actionable and not merely evaluative
- The writer can tell what to do first after reading the edit; the edit strengthens the draft's own best version rather than imposing a different piece
