# Genre And Form Contract

Every piece of writing carries an implicit contract with its reader: a promise about what kind of experience is coming, how it will be structured, how deep it will go, and how it will resolve. That contract is shaped by genre and form. A how-to guide that never delivers steps, a memoir that reads like a report, an argument that never argues, a thriller-paced chapter in a contemplative essay, a listicle that hides a single idea, a long-form feature told in inverted-pyramid brevity, all break their contract. Readers feel the break as dissatisfaction even when they cannot name the cause. Developmental editing must read for genre and form because they are not decorative labels; they are structural commitments that determine what the piece owes the reader and how its parts must be arranged to deliver on that promise.

Use this skill when the developmental question is not "is this well written" but "is this the right shape for what it is trying to be." It covers identifying the operative genre and form, testing whether the draft honors the expectations that genre creates, judging where a deliberate genre break serves the work versus where an accidental one betrays it, and helping the writer either fulfill the contract or consciously renegotiate it. The editor's task is to make the draft's form match its intent, so the reader gets the experience they were promised.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Operative Genre And Form Before Judging Structure

Before diagnosing structure, name what the draft is trying to be. Genre and form are the lens through which every structural choice is evaluated, and misidentifying them leads to misdiagnosis. A piece judged as a feature may fail when it is actually an essay; judged as an essay it may succeed.

Determine the operative contract from evidence in and around the text: the title and framing, the opening move, the publishing context, the stated or implied audience, the conventions the draft reaches for, and the writer's stated intent. Note that a draft may invoke a hybrid form or shift genres mid-piece; identify the dominant contract and any secondary ones. Record the identified genre and form explicitly so subsequent structural judgment is anchored rather than drifting with the editor's personal preferences.

### Map The Specific Structural Obligations Of That Genre

Each genre and form carries structural obligations, the moves a reader expects and needs. A how-to needs sequenced, actionable steps and recovery guidance. An argument needs a claim, evidence, counterargument, and a warranted conclusion. A narrative needs scene, tension, escalation, and resolution. A reference entry needs scannability and completeness. A list needs parallel structure and a coherent organizing logic.

Translate the identified genre into a concrete list of obligations, then check the draft against each. Missing obligations are structural gaps; obligations present but misordered are structural problems. This is more precise than asking "does the structure work," because it ties judgment to the specific promises the form makes. A draft can be internally coherent and still fail its genre by omitting an obligatory move.

### Test Whether The Draft Honors The Promise Its Opening Makes

The opening sets the contract. If the opening promises a personal story, the reader expects the piece to deliver through that story. If it promises a definitive answer, the reader expects resolution, not an open-ended meditation. A draft that opens in one mode and delivers in another creates a broken-promise feeling that no amount of polish can repair.

Read the opening and articulate the promise it makes: what experience, what payoff, what depth, what stance. Then read the body and ending and ask whether they deliver that promise. Where the promise and delivery diverge, the fix is usually to align them: change the opening to match what the piece actually delivers, or reshape the body to deliver what the opening promises. Do not assume the body is correct and the opening wrong; either may be the one to move.

### Distinguish Deliberate Genre Play From Accidental Genre Drift

Strong work sometimes breaks genre expectations on purpose: a memoir that dissolves into reportage, a how-to that withholds the answer to make a point, an argument that refuses closure. Deliberate genre play can be powerful, but it must be earned, signaled, and purposeful. Accidental genre drift, where the writer simply loses track of the form, reads as incompetence.

To tell them apart, ask whether the break is consistent with a recognizable artistic intent, whether it is set up so the reader can follow the move, and whether it pays off. A deliberate break usually has setup earlier and a reason that becomes clear. An accidental break feels arbitrary and leaves the reader confused rather than challenged. Coach the writer to either commit to and support a deliberate break, or repair the drift by returning to the form's obligations.

### Judge Depth And Scope Against Genre Norms

Genre sets reader expectations not only for structure but for depth and scope. A beginner tutorial that assumes expert knowledge, a definitive guide that stays at surface level, a think piece that makes sweeping claims without evidence, a reference doc that omits edge cases, all violate depth expectations tied to their form.

Calibrate the expected depth from the genre and audience, then test the draft against it. Where the draft is shallower than its form promises, flag the missing depth and identify what to add. Where it is deeper than useful, flag the excess and consider cutting or moving material. Depth mismatches are common because writers often write to their own level rather than the reader the genre implies.

### Evaluate Pacing And Information Release For The Form

Different forms distribute information differently. A mystery withholds and reveals; a reference exposes everything upfront for scanning; a how-to sequences for action; a narrative controls release for tension. Pacing that suits one form sabotages another.

Diagnose pacing against the form's logic. Is information released in the order the reader of this genre needs it? Is background front-loaded where it belongs, or withheld where withholding creates confusion? Does the form's typical rhythm, escalation in narrative, steady accumulation in argument, flat scannability in reference, match what the draft does? Pacing problems are often genre problems in disguise.

### Recognize When A Draft Is The Wrong Form For Its Content

Sometimes the diagnosis is not that the draft fails its form but that it is the wrong form entirely. A topic that wants narrative is trapped in exposition. An argument that needs evidence is told as opinion. A reference subject is stretched into a tutorial. The content is fighting the form, and no amount of in-form revision will resolve the tension.

When the content and form are mismatched, name the mismatch and propose a form change: convert the exposition to scene, the opinion to argued analysis, the tutorial to reference. This is a significant developmental call and should be made with the writer, because it implies substantial rework, but it is often the edit that unlocks a stuck draft. Do not keep polishing a draft in a form its content has outgrown.

### Renegotiate The Contract Consciously With The Writer

When the draft's contract needs to change, whether shifting genre, narrowing scope, or changing promised depth, the change should be conscious and agreed, not imposed silently through editing. The writer owns the work's intent.

Frame contract changes as options with tradeoffs: "If we keep this as a how-to, we must add steps; if we reframe it as an essay about the problem, the current material works." Let the writer choose, because the choice determines what revision follows. A silently renegotiated contract produces a draft that no longer matches what the writer thought they were writing.

## Common Traps

### Judging Structure Without Naming The Genre

Generic structural critique that ignores genre produces misdiagnosis. Name the operative form before evaluating its moves.

### Treating Genre Rules As Rigid Laws

Genre obligations are strong defaults, not prison walls. Deliberate, earned breaks can be the strongest moves. Judge breaks by intent and payoff, not by rule-following.

### Missing A Broken Opening Promise

A draft that opens in one mode and delivers in another feels wrong to readers. Always test the opening's promise against the body's delivery.

### Calling Accidental Drift Stylistic Choice

Genre drift caused by losing track of the form is not artistic experimentation. Distinguish drift from deliberate play before defending it.

### Keeping The Wrong Form And Polishing It

When content and form fight, in-form revision cannot resolve it. Be willing to propose a form change rather than endlessly polishing a mismatched draft.

### Silently Renegotiating The Contract

Changing the draft's promised form through editing alone leaves the writer disoriented. Make contract changes explicit and agreed.

### Ignoring Depth Expectations Tied To Genre

A form implies a depth. Shallow definitive guides and over-deep quick references both violate their contract. Calibrate depth to the genre's reader.

## Self-Check

Before treating genre and form contract review as complete, verify:

- The operative genre and form have been identified explicitly from evidence, not assumed.
- The genre's specific structural obligations have been mapped and the draft checked against each.
- The opening's promise has been tested against what the body and ending actually deliver.
- Deliberate genre breaks have been distinguished from accidental drift, with setup and payoff verified.
- Depth and scope have been calibrated against the genre's reader expectations.
- Pacing and information release match the logic of the form.
- Cases where content and form are mismatched have been identified, with a form change proposed rather than endless in-form polishing.
- Any contract renegotiation has been made consciously and agreed with the writer, not imposed silently.
- The draft, after revision direction, will deliver the experience its form promises its reader.
