# Developmental Feedback For Revision

Developmental editing produces a diagnosis, but the writer receives feedback. Those are different artifacts. A correct diagnosis can still become useless feedback when it is unordered, unkind, too dense, rewritten in the editor's voice, or delivered as a list of every flaw without any sense of what to do first. Revision feedback is the bridge between editorial judgment and the author actually changing the manuscript.

Use this skill when turning a developmental assessment into the notes, letter, queries, or annotated comments a writer will use to revise. It covers prioritization, sequencing, tone, scope of editorial intervention, query design, and the line between guiding revision and writing the book for the author. It applies to fiction and nonfiction, trade and academic, long-form and short-form. The editor's task here is not to diagnose again but to make the diagnosis usable.

## Core Rules

### Lead With The Few Changes That Unlock The Most

A manuscript note that lists forty issues treats every problem as equal, and the writer will either freeze or fix the easy ones first. Revision feedback must rank.

Separate the work into clear tiers before writing prose:

- blocking issues the manuscript cannot succeed without resolving, such as a missing central conflict, a false thesis, a structural collapse, or a fatal credibility gap;
- major issues that significantly weaken the work but do not sink it, such as a sagging middle, an underdeveloped antagonist, a chapter that repeats an earlier argument, or evidence that supports only half the claim;
- moderate issues that would improve clarity, force, or cohesion;
- optional polish that the editor noticed but that the writer may legitimately set aside.

Tell the writer explicitly which tier each note belongs to. A writer who knows that two blocking issues must come first will not waste a revision cycle on comma placement. If you cannot reduce the blocking tier to a small number of items, you have not yet found the real center of the problem.

### Sequence Feedback To Match The Order Of Revision

Writers revise in passes. Feedback delivered in the wrong order wastes those passes. If the editor tells a writer to sharpen dialogue in chapter twelve while the whole second act still needs to move, the dialogue work will be thrown away.

Order notes so that earlier revisions do not invalidate later ones. A useful sequence is usually:

1. premise, thesis, or central dramatic question;
2. structure, arc, and chapter architecture;
3. scene and section level work;
4. character, argument depth, and evidence;
5. voice, tone, and prose rhythm;
6. line and copy concerns, which belong to a later stage.

State the sequence explicitly. Tell the writer which pass each note belongs to and warn them when a note depends on an earlier decision. "Do not rewrite this scene until you have decided whether the protagonist knows the secret by this point" is more useful than any scene-level suggestion.

### Diagnose Cause Before Assigning Revision Tasks

A symptom note such as "the pacing drags here" gives the writer nothing to act on. The writer cannot fix pacing directly; they can only change what causes it. Each significant note should name the symptom, identify the likely cause, explain the effect on the reader, and offer at least one revision direction.

For example, "the middle slows" is a symptom. The cause might be repeated scenes that all prove the same point, a subplot that outlives its purpose, missing stakes, or a chapter that explains what an earlier chapter already showed. Each cause implies a different fix: cut, compress, raise stakes, or delete the redundant chapter. Give the writer the cause so they can choose the fix that fits their intent instead of mechanically applying yours.

### Give Options When The Path Is A Judgment Call

Many revision decisions are not right-versus-wrong; they are tradeoffs the author must own. When a note could be resolved in more than one defensible way, present two or three revision paths and explain what each costs.

Name what each path gains and loses. One resolution may preserve a character's mystery but weaken motivation. Another may clarify motivation but flatten tone. A third may require adding material the author did not plan to write. Let the author choose with the tradeoffs visible. Prescribing a single fix when the decision is genuinely authorial turns editing into ghostwriting.

### Preserve Authorial Ownership Of Voice And Meaning

Developmental feedback should make the manuscript more itself, not more like the editor's preferred book. Distinguish between changes the work needs and changes the editor would prefer.

Preserve the author's stance, moral frame, distinctive rhythm, regional or cultural idiom, genre commitments, and the awkward-but-loaded phrases that carry their voice. Challenge unsupported claims, evasive structure, sentimentality, contradictions, and promises the draft does not keep, but challenge them as problems to solve rather than as invitations to rewrite in your register.

When you suggest specific language, label it as an example of the kind of change, not the required wording. Writers who feel overwritten stop trusting the editor on the issues that actually matter.

### Make Queries Specific And Locatable

Vague global notes ("tighten throughout," "deepen character") are hard to act on. Tie each significant note to a location, a scene, a chapter, a section, or a quoted phrase the writer can find.

A strong query names where, what the reader currently experiences, what the manuscript seems to intend, and what is missing or off. "In chapter seven, the reader learns the company is failing only through summary; a scene showing the founder reading the numbers would let the reader feel the stakes" is actionable. "Add more emotion here" is not.

If you use marginal comments, keep global observations in a separate cover letter so the writer can distinguish recurring patterns from one-off fixes.

### Separate Editorial Needs From Author Decisions

Some notes describe what the manuscript needs; others describe decisions only the author can make. Conflating them confuses the writer about what is required.

Mark the difference. An editorial need is something the reader experience depends on, such as clarity of the central question, consistent timeline, or stakes the reader can feel. An author decision is a creative choice the editor can inform but not settle, such as which point of view to use, whether to keep a subplot, how sympathetic a character should be, or how explicit to make a theme. Frame author decisions as questions, not commands.

### Calibrate Honesty To The Manuscript's Potential

Feedback that overpraises to soften criticism leaves the writer unable to trust praise. Feedback that is uniformly harsh can make the writer abandon a fixable manuscript. Calibrate to what the manuscript can realistically become.

Praise what genuinely works and be specific about why, so the writer knows which instincts to protect. When the work has serious problems, say so directly but in proportion to the problem. Do not manufacture encouragement, and do not dramatize minor issues to seem rigorous. A writer can read the difference between honest assessment and performance.

### Decide How Much To Rewrite In The Feedback and plan For Revision Cycles

There is a real tension between showing and overwriting. A short illustrative rewrite can clarify the kind of change you mean. A long rewrite steals the author's work and trains them to wait for your prose.

Use small demonstrations, clearly labeled as examples, for genuinely confusing notes. Avoid rewriting whole passages unless the task is explicitly a rewrite or the author has asked for modeled prose. When you do demonstrate, show the structural move, not the polished sentence, so the author retains ownership of the final voice.

Single-pass feedback rarely produces a finished manuscript. Tell the writer what to expect after this round and what later rounds will address.

Identify which issues will only become visible after the structural pass, such as pacing problems hidden by a missing chapter, or voice inconsistencies that disappear once a subplot is cut. Tell the writer not to line-edit material that may not survive the next structural pass. Setting expectations about cycles prevents the writer from polishing work that will be deleted and prevents the editor from having to repeat notes that were premature.

## Common Traps

### The Note Avalanche

Delivering every observation at equal weight overwhelms the writer and hides the few changes that matter. Rank and tier before writing.

### Fixing In The Wrong Order

Urging scene-level polish before structure is settled wastes the author's revision labor. Sequence notes to match the order in which revision actually happens.

### Symptom Without Cause

"Pacing is slow" or "character is flat" names a feeling, not a fixable cause. Always connect the symptom to the structural or dramatic source.

### Prescribing Where The Author Should Decide

When a revision path is a creative judgment call, prescribing a single answer displaces the author. Offer paths and name the tradeoffs.

### Rewriting The Manuscript In The Notes

Long modeled rewrites teach the author to wait for your prose and blur authorship. Demonstrate the move, not the finished sentence.

### Praising To Soften

Inflated praise makes real praise unbelievable and leaves the writer unable to tell which parts to protect. Praise only what genuinely works, and be specific.

### Global Vagueness

"Tighten throughout" gives the writer nothing to locate or act on. Tie notes to scenes, chapters, or quoted phrases.

### Treating One Round As Final and confusing Needs With Preferences

Feedback that assumes this pass will finish the manuscript ignores the issues that only surface after structural changes. Set cycle expectations.

Notes that present the editor's taste as editorial necessity erode trust. Separate what the reader experience requires from what the editor would personally prefer.

## Self-Check

Before treating developmental revision feedback as complete, verify:

- Notes are tiered into blocking, major, moderate, and optional, and the writer can tell which is which.
- The sequence of notes matches the order in which revision should happen, and dependencies between notes are flagged.
- Each significant note names symptom, likely cause, reader effect, and at least one revision direction.
- Where a revision path is a judgment call, two or more options are offered with their tradeoffs.
- Authorial ownership of voice, stance, and meaning is preserved; suggested language is labeled as example, not requirement.
- Each note is tied to a locatable scene, chapter, section, or quoted phrase rather than left as a global abstraction.
- Editorial needs are distinguished from author decisions, and author decisions are framed as questions.
- Praise is specific and honest, and criticism is proportional rather than dramatized or softened.
- Any modeled rewrites are short, structural, and clearly labeled, not polished substitutions.
- The feedback sets expectations for later revision cycles and warns the writer against premature line editing; the writer, after reading the feedback, knows what to do first, what to defer, and what only they can decide
