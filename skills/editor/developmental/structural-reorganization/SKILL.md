# Structural Reorganization

Developmental diagnosis identifies that a draft is out of order; structural reorganization is the work of actually rebuilding it. This is the most consequential and most error-prone kind of editing, because moving parts of a draft breaks the invisible dependencies that hold it together. A paragraph moved without checking what it refers back to, a chapter reordered without remapping its cross-references, a section merged without reconciling its definitions, a climax repositioned without adjusting the setup that pays it off, all create drafts that are internally broken in ways that are hard to see and expensive to fix late. Reorganization is not cutting and pasting; it is surgery on a connected system, and it must be done with a map of what depends on what.

Use this skill when the developmental decision is to restructure rather than merely to advise restructuring, and whenever large-scale movement of material is involved. It covers mapping the draft's dependency graph before moving anything, re-sequencing for reader need, merging and splitting sections cleanly, preserving meaning across moves, and verifying that the reorganized draft still coheres. The editor's task is to rebuild the architecture so the result is stronger than the original and free of the broken connections that careless reorganization introduces.

## Core Rules

### Map The Draft Before Moving Anything

Before reorganizing, build a map of the draft as it exists: what each section does, what it depends on, and what depends on it. Reorganization without a map is blind surgery. The map reveals which moves are safe and which will break dependencies.

Create a structural outline that captures each section's job (orientation, background, claim, evidence, counterargument, scene, transition, resolution), its key terms and definitions, its forward and backward references, and the logical or narrative load it carries. Identify the spine, the main line of argument or story that everything else supports. Only with this map visible can you reorganize without orphaning material or breaking the chain the reader follows.

### Re-Sequence For Reader Need, Not For Writer Convenience

The default order of a draft is often the order in which the writer thought of things, which is rarely the order the reader needs. Reorganization should serve the reader's path: what must they know first to understand what comes next, where do they need a turn, where do they need evidence, where do they need rest.

Re-sequence by asking, at each point, what the reader needs to have in hand to make sense of the next section. Move background to precede the claims that require it. Move the strongest material to where it has maximum effect. Move digressions that interrupt the spine to where they support it or cut them. The reader's cognitive sequence, not the writer's compositional sequence, should govern the final order.

### Identify And Preserve Load-Bearing Dependencies

Many sections are load-bearing: other parts of the draft rely on a definition, a fact, a scene, or a claim they establish. Moving a load-bearing section without moving or re-establishing what it carries breaks everything that depended on it.

For each section you plan to move, trace its dependents: what later material references its content, assumes its definitions, builds on its claims, or pays off its setup. If dependents exist, either move the section to a point still before them, duplicate the essential load-bearing element where needed, or revise the dependents to no longer rely on it. Never move a section and leave its dependents pointing at empty space.

### Handle Cross-References And Setup-Payoff Pairs As A System

Reorganization breaks cross-references and setup-payoff pairs more than any other editing move. A "as discussed earlier" now points to the wrong place; a foreshadowed reveal now appears before its setup; a figure referenced in chapter three has moved to chapter seven.

Treat all internal references as a system to be remapped after any move. Catalogue cross-references, forward references, callbacks, foreshadowing, figure and table references, and chapter and section pointers. After reorganizing, walk each one and confirm it still resolves correctly. Setup-payoff pairs are especially fragile: verify that every payoff still has its setup preceding it and that no setup is left dangling without its payoff.

### Merge And Split Sections To Match Structural Jobs

Reorganization often reveals that two sections are doing one job and should merge, or that one section is doing two jobs and should split. Merges and splits must be done at the seams, the natural boundaries between jobs, not at arbitrary points.

When merging, reconcile the sections' terms, tone, and logic so the result reads as one piece, not two stitched together. Remove the redundancy where the two overlapped. When splitting, ensure each resulting section has its own complete job, a clear opening, and a clear close, and that the transition between them is rebuilt. Merges and splits change the draft's rhythm, so re-read the surrounding sections after the change.

### Rebuild Transitions At Every New Boundary

Every reorganization creates new boundaries between sections, and the transitions that existed at the old boundaries no longer fit. A moved section now abuts material it was not written to follow, and the reader feels the gap.

After any move, rebuild the transition into and out of the moved section so the reader is carried across the new boundary. This is not optional polish; it is structural repair. A correctly placed section with a missing transition reads as disjointed, and the editor may wrongly conclude the placement was wrong when only the transition was missing. Rebuild transitions before judging whether a move worked.

### Preserve Meaning And Voice Across The Cut

Reorganization often involves cutting material that no longer fits the new structure. Cutting is legitimate, but it must not silently remove meaning the draft still needs. A cut scene may carry the only evidence for a claim; a cut aside may contain a definition later sections rely on.

Before cutting, check what the cut material carries. If it carries needed meaning, preserve that meaning by moving it elsewhere, condensing it into a surviving section, or flagging to the writer that the claim it supported now needs new support. Preserve the writer's voice in any condensed material rather than rewriting it in the editor's register.

### Verify Coherence After Reorganization, Not During It

During reorganization the draft is necessarily in a broken state, and judging it mid-process leads to false conclusions. Coherence must be verified only after the reorganization is complete and transitions are rebuilt.

After restructuring, read the draft end to end as a reader would. Check that the spine holds, that each section earns its place, that dependencies resolve, that transitions carry the reader, and that no meaning was lost. Only this full read confirms that the reorganization improved the draft rather than merely rearranging its problems.

## Common Traps

### Reorganizing Without A Dependency Map

Moving sections without mapping what depends on them orphans material and breaks the chain the reader follows. Map first, move second.

### Sequencing For The Writer's Logic Instead Of The Reader's

The order things were written is rarely the order the reader needs. Re-sequence to the reader's cognitive path.

### Moving A Section And Leaving Dangling References

Cross-references and setup-payoff pairs break silently when material moves. Remap the reference system after every move.

### Forgetting To Rebuild Transitions

A correctly placed section with a missing transition reads as wrong. Rebuild boundaries before judging a move.

### Cutting Material That Carries Needed Meaning

A cut can remove the only support for a surviving claim. Check what cut material carries before removing it.

### Judging Coherence Mid-Reorganization

A draft is necessarily broken during restructuring. Verify coherence only after the work and transitions are complete.

### Merging Or Splitting At Arbitrary Points

Merges and splits must happen at structural seams. Arbitrary cuts produce sections with incomplete jobs.

## Self-Check

Before treating structural reorganization as complete, verify:

- A structural map was built before any material was moved, capturing each section's job, dependencies, and dependents.
- Re-sequencing served the reader's need rather than the writer's compositional order.
- Load-bearing sections were identified, and their dependents were preserved, moved, or revised rather than orphaned.
- All cross-references, callbacks, foreshadowing, and figure references were remapped and confirmed to resolve.
- Setup-payoff pairs were verified to remain in correct sequence after the move.
- Merges and splits were made at structural seams, with redundancy removed and complete jobs preserved.
- Transitions were rebuilt at every new boundary before the move was judged.
- Cut material was checked for meaning it carried, and needed meaning was preserved elsewhere.
- A full end-to-end reader's read confirmed the reorganized draft coheres and is stronger than the original.
- No broken dependencies, dangling references, or lost meaning remain in the restructured draft.
