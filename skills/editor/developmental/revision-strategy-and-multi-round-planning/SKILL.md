# Revision Strategy And Multi-Round Planning

A single draft rarely becomes publishable in one revision pass. Most serious work requires several rounds, and the order in which those rounds happen determines whether the draft converges on its best version or churns without improving. Revising line-level prose before structure is settled wastes effort, because passages get cut or rewritten. Addressing every issue in one overloaded pass overwhelms the writer and produces shallow fixes everywhere. Revising without a plan causes the draft to drift, with each round undoing part of the last. Revision strategy is the editorial discipline of sequencing passes so each builds on the previous, so the writer's effort compounds, and so the draft moves steadily toward readiness rather than circling. A writer given a clear multi-round plan revises better than a writer given one giant list of everything wrong.

Use this skill when the developmental task is to plan the revision process itself, not just to diagnose a single draft state. It covers sequencing revision rounds, separating structural from line-level work, deciding what each round should accomplish, managing scope and fatigue across rounds, and knowing when revision is converging versus when it has stalled. The editor's task is to design a revision path the writer can actually follow, so each pass moves the work closer to done.

## Core Rules

### Sequence Rounds So Each Builds On The Previous

Revision rounds have a natural order, and violating it wastes work. Structure must be settled before line editing, because restructuring cuts and rewrites prose. Substantive accuracy must be settled before copy-editing, because factual changes alter sentences. Voice and clarity work must precede final proofreading, because proofreading assumes the text is final.

Plan rounds in dependency order: developmental and structural first, then substantive and evidentiary, then line and voice, then copy-editing, then proofreading. Each round should assume the previous round's work is stable. Where a writer wants to jump ahead to polish, redirect them to the round that must come first, because polishing passages that will be cut or rewritten is effort thrown away.

### Give Each Round A Single Dominant Purpose

A round that tries to fix structure, facts, voice, and typos at once does none of them well. The writer's attention is finite, and a focused pass produces deeper fixes than a scattered one. Define each round by one dominant purpose and defer everything else.

Name the round's purpose explicitly: "this pass is only about structure and the argument spine; ignore sentences." Within that purpose, be specific about what to address and what to leave. Deferred issues should be recorded so they are not lost, not held in the writer's memory. A sequence of single-purpose rounds converges faster than one omnibus pass, even though it sounds slower.

### Separate Structural Fixes From Line Work Strictly

The single most common sequencing error is mixing structural and line-level revision. Once a writer starts polishing sentences, they become attached to the polished prose and resist the structural cuts that the draft still needs. Polishing also creates false confidence that the draft is further along than it is.

Enforce a strict boundary: no line-level work until structure is settled and agreed. If line issues are noticed during a structural pass, note them for a later round rather than fixing them. This protects the writer from wasted effort and keeps the structural decisions honest, because the writer is not defending prose they have already polished.

### Match Round Scope To The Writer's Capacity And Time

Each revision round costs the writer effort and time, and a plan that ignores capacity will not be followed. A round that asks for a full structural rewrite, new reporting, and a voice pass in one week is a plan that collapses. Scope each round to what the writer can realistically do well in the available time.

Estimate the effort each round demands and balance it against the writer's capacity, the deadline, and the number of rounds feasible. Where capacity is limited, prioritize the rounds that most determine whether the work succeeds, and accept that lower-priority rounds may be lighter. A realistic plan that is followed beats an ambitious plan that is abandoned.

### Manage Diminishing Returns And Know When To Stop

Revision has diminishing returns. The first structural pass transforms the draft; the tenth polish pass changes little and risks making the work worse through over-revision. A draft can be revised past its best point, becoming overworked, stiff, and stripped of the energy that made it alive.

Watch for the signs of diminishing returns: changes that swap one defensible choice for another without clear improvement, increasing attachment to minor preferences, prose losing freshness. Plan to stop before this point. Define what "done" looks like for each round and for the project, so revision ends when the criteria are met rather than continuing indefinitely. Knowing when to stop is as important as knowing what to fix.

### Detect And Break Revision Churn

Churn is revision that does not converge: each round undoes part of the previous one, the draft oscillates between versions, or the writer revises without a clear sense of whether it is improving. Churn wastes effort and demoralizes the writer.

Diagnose churn by comparing draft states across rounds: is the work moving toward a stable, stronger version, or cycling? Churn often signals an unresolved foundational decision, an unclear goal, or conflicting feedback. Break churn by returning to the foundational questions: what is this piece trying to be, what does done look like, and what single change would most move it forward. Resolve the underlying uncertainty before asking for more revision.

### Record Round Outcomes So Progress Is Visible

Across multiple rounds, it becomes hard to remember what was addressed, what remains, and whether the draft is improving. Invisible progress breeds discouragement and duplicate effort. Record what each round accomplished and what it deferred.

After each round, note what was resolved, what partially improved, what was consciously deferred, and any new issues that emerged. This record lets the next round focus on what remains, prevents re-litigating settled decisions, and gives the writer visible evidence of convergence. A revision log turns a long process into a trackable one.

### Keep The Writer Oriented To The Goal Across Rounds

In a multi-round revision, the writer can lose sight of the work's purpose and start revising to the feedback rather than to the reader. The editor keeps the goal, the piece's promise to its reader, visible throughout, so each round serves the work rather than the notes.

Periodically restate what the piece is trying to become and how the current round serves that. Connect each round's purpose to the reader's experience. A writer who revises toward a clear goal makes better decisions than one who revises toward a checklist.

## Common Traps

### Polishing Before Structure Is Settled

Line work on passages that will be cut or rewritten is wasted and creates resistance to needed cuts. Enforce structural-first sequencing.

### The Omnibus Pass That Fixes Everything At Once

A round trying to address every level produces shallow fixes everywhere. Give each round one dominant purpose.

### Mixing Structural And Line Revision

Once prose is polished, writers resist structural change. Keep the boundary strict until structure is agreed.

### Ignoring Writer Capacity And Deadline

A plan the writer cannot follow is not a plan. Scope rounds to realistic capacity and time.

### Revising Past The Best Point

Diminishing returns and over-revision can make the work worse. Define done and stop when criteria are met.

### Letting Revision Churn Without Convergence

Cycling between versions wastes effort and demoralizes. Diagnose churn and resolve the foundational uncertainty driving it.

### Losing Track Of Progress Across Rounds

Invisible progress breeds discouragement and duplicate effort. Record what each round accomplished and deferred.

### Revising To The Feedback Instead Of The Reader

A writer who loses the goal optimizes for notes, not readers. Keep the work's purpose visible across rounds.

## Self-Check

Before treating multi-round revision planning as complete, verify:

- Revision rounds are sequenced in dependency order, with structure before line work and substantive before copy work.
- Each round has a single dominant purpose, with other issues explicitly deferred and recorded.
- Structural and line-level work are strictly separated until structure is settled and agreed.
- Round scope is matched to the writer's realistic capacity and the available time.
- Diminishing returns are anticipated, with clear criteria for when each round and the project are done.
- Revision churn is detectable, with a plan to return to foundational questions if the draft stops converging.
- Round outcomes are recorded so progress is visible and settled decisions are not re-litigated.
- The work's goal and reader promise are kept visible so the writer revises toward the reader, not the notes.
- The plan is one the writer can follow, with each round moving the draft measurably closer to its best version.
