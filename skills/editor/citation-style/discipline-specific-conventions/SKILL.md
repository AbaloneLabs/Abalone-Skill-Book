# Discipline-Specific Conventions

Citation style is not universal. Different academic and professional disciplines have distinct conventions for how sources are cited, what sources are privileged, and how the citation apparatus is structured. A historian citing archival material follows conventions that differ from a psychologist citing empirical studies, which differ again from a lawyer citing case law. Applying a generic citation style without regard for discipline-specific conventions produces work that looks wrong to experts in the field. Discipline-specific conventions are the set of expectations, source types, and citation patterns that characterize a given field. Editors who know only the general style guide miss the conventions that make citation feel native to a discipline.

Use this skill when editing work intended for a specific academic or professional discipline, to ensure citation conventions match field norms. It covers recognizing discipline expectations, handling discipline-specific source types, and judging when departures are appropriate. The goal is citation that an expert in the field would recognize as correctly and conventionally done.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Disciplinary Context

The first step is identifying the discipline and its citation conventions. Disciplines vary in preferred style, source types, apparatus structure, and expectations for citation density. Identifying the context determines what conventions apply.

Determine the discipline of the work, whether from the subject matter, the target venue, or the author's affiliation. Identify the citation style the discipline typically uses. For example, psychology and social sciences commonly use APA; humanities commonly use MLA or Chicago; law uses Bluebook or OSCOLA; sciences use various numbered styles. Identify the venue's specific requirements if applicable, which may override general discipline conventions. Understanding the disciplinary context is the foundation for applying the right conventions. Do not assume a single style fits all fields.

### Match Citation Style To Discipline Norms

Once the discipline is identified, verify the citation style matches field norms. Using a style associated with a different discipline signals unfamiliarity with the field's conventions. Match the style to the discipline.

Check that the chosen citation style is appropriate for the discipline and venue. If the author has used a style common to another field, flag the mismatch. For interdisciplinary work, identify the primary discipline and use its conventions, or follow the target venue's requirements. If the venue specifies a style, that overrides general discipline norms. When no style is specified, use the convention most common in the discipline. A mismatch between style and discipline is a visible error to experts in the field.

### Handle Discipline-Specific Source Types

Disciplines cite source types that are characteristic of their field. Historians cite archives, manuscripts, and primary documents. Scientists cite datasets, software, and experimental methods. Lawyers cite cases, statutes, and regulations. Artists cite artworks and exhibitions. Each source type has conventions for how it is cited. Handle discipline-specific source types correctly.

Identify the source types characteristic of the discipline. For each, understand the citation conventions. Verify that discipline-specific sources are cited according to convention. For example, legal citations must follow case and statute citation formats; historical citations must properly identify archival collections; scientific citations must include dataset or software identifiers. Check that details specific to the source type, such as accession numbers, docket numbers, or version numbers, are included as the discipline expects. Mishandling discipline-specific source types is a common error that signals inattention to field conventions.

### Recognize Discipline-Specific Citation Patterns

Beyond source types, disciplines have patterns in how citations are used. Some fields cite heavily and densely; others cite more sparingly. Some integrate citations into the narrative; others use them as discrete references. Some fields emphasize recency; others value foundational older works. Recognize and respect these patterns.

Observe the citation density and integration typical of the discipline. Verify the work's citation pattern aligns with field norms. A work in a citation-heavy field that cites too sparsely may appear under-supported; a work in a citation-light field that cites excessively may appear derivative. Check that the balance of source types, such as primary versus secondary or empirical versus theoretical, matches disciplinary expectations. Recognize whether the field values recent sources, as in fast-moving sciences, or foundational sources, as in humanities. Aligning citation patterns with discipline norms makes the work feel native to the field.

### Verify Authority And Hierarchy Of Sources

Disciplines differ in what sources they consider authoritative and how source authority is hierarchized. In sciences, peer-reviewed journals typically rank above books. In humanities, scholarly monographs may rank equal to or above journals. In law, binding precedent ranks above persuasive authority. In journalism, primary reporting ranks above aggregation. Verify the work's source authority aligns with discipline expectations.

Assess the types of sources cited and their authority within the discipline. Flag works that rely on sources considered weak or inappropriate for the field, such as a scientific paper relying on non-peer-reviewed sources or a legal brief citing non-authoritative opinion. Verify the hierarchy of sources reflects disciplinary norms, with the most authoritative sources carrying the argument's weight. Source authority that misaligns with discipline expectations undermines the work's credibility with experts.

### Check For Discipline-Specific Formatting Details

Citation styles have formatting details that vary by discipline. Abbreviations for recurring terms, the inclusion or omission of certain elements, and the treatment of specific source types can all carry discipline-specific conventions. Check these details.

Verify that discipline-specific abbreviations are used correctly, such as legal abbreviations for reporters and statutes, scientific abbreviations for journals, or historical abbreviations for archival references. Check that elements specific to the discipline are included, such as digital object identifiers in sciences, parallel citations in law, or locator information in humanities. Verify the treatment of source types characteristic of the field follows convention. These details are small but visible to experts, and getting them right demonstrates command of the discipline's conventions.

### Judge Departures From Convention Deliberately

Not every departure from convention is an error. Authors may depart from discipline conventions for good reasons, such as interdisciplinary work, genre innovation, or audience considerations. The editor's role is to ensure departures are deliberate and justified, not accidental.

When a departure from convention is found, assess whether it is deliberate. Ask the author about the rationale. If the departure serves a purpose, such as reaching a broader audience or integrating disciplines, it may be appropriate. If the departure appears accidental or stems from unfamiliarity with conventions, flag it for correction. Document any deliberate departures so they are consistent throughout the work. The standard is not rigid conformity but informed, intentional use of conventions, with departures that are justified and consistent.

## Common Traps

### Assuming A Universal Citation Style

Styles vary by discipline. Match the style to the field.

### Mishandling Discipline-Specific Source Types

Each field has characteristic sources with specific citation conventions. Handle them correctly.

### Ignoring Citation Density And Pattern Norms

Disciplines differ in citation patterns. Align with field expectations.

### Relying On Sources Weak For The Discipline

Source authority varies by field. Use sources the discipline considers authoritative.

### Missing Discipline-Specific Formatting Details

Abbreviations and elements vary. Get the details right for the field.

### Treating All Departures As Errors

Some departures are deliberate. Distinguish intentional choices from mistakes.

### Forgetting Venue Requirements Override Discipline Norms

If the venue specifies a style, follow it over general conventions.

## Self-Check

Before treating discipline-specific conventions as correctly applied, verify:

- The disciplinary context has been identified and the appropriate citation style selected.
- The citation style matches the norms of the discipline and the target venue.
- Discipline-specific source types are cited according to their conventions.
- Citation density and integration patterns align with field expectations.
- The balance of source types reflects disciplinary norms.
- Source authority and hierarchy align with what the discipline considers credible.
- Discipline-specific abbreviations and formatting details are correct.
- Elements specific to the discipline, such as DOIs or parallel citations, are included as expected.
- Any departures from convention are deliberate, justified, and consistent.
- An expert in the field would recognize the citation as conventionally and correctly done.
