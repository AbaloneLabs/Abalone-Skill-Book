# Source Strength Hierarchy

Not all sources carry equal authority, and a claim is only as strong as the sources that back it. Source strength hierarchy is the editorial discipline of evaluating cited sources by their type, reliability, independence, and appropriateness for the claim they support. A peer-reviewed study, a government dataset, a respected journalist's reporting, a press release, a blog post, and a social media update occupy very different positions on the credibility ladder, and using a weak source where a strong one is required undermines the work's trustworthiness. Editors who treat all citations as equivalent pass along arguments that collapse when a reader checks the backing.

Use this skill when editing any cited nonfiction, academic, reportage, or instructional work to evaluate whether the sources are strong enough, independent enough, and appropriate for their claims. It covers ranking sources by type and reliability, detecting conflicts of interest and dependence, identifying claims that need stronger sourcing, and recommending source upgrades. The editor's job is to ensure the work's evidentiary foundation is built on material that can bear scrutiny.

## Core Rules

### Rank Sources By Type And Reliability

Different source types carry different default reliability, and the editor should know where each cited source falls on the hierarchy. Rank sources explicitly when evaluating a draft's evidentiary base, so weak sourcing becomes visible.

A general hierarchy, strongest to weakest: peer-reviewed primary research and systematic reviews; official government data and statistics from recognized agencies; reporting from established news organizations with editorial standards and correction practices; books from academic or reputable trade publishers; reports from independent research organizations; trade publications and professional sources; press releases and institutional communications; advocacy organization materials; blogs, podcasts, and self-published content; social media posts and user-generated content. This hierarchy is a starting point, not absolute; a strong source can be wrong and a weak source can be right. But when a load-bearing claim rests on a source near the bottom, that is a flag.

### Match Source Strength To Claim Weight

The strength of source required depends on the weight of the claim. A trivial factual claim may be supportable by a quick reference; a contested empirical claim requires strong, preferably primary, sourcing; a causal claim requires methodologically rigorous evidence. Match source strength to claim weight.

For each load-bearing or contestable claim, ask whether the source type is appropriate. A claim that a policy reduced crime rates should not rest on a think-tank blog post when government crime data and peer-reviewed studies exist. A claim about medical efficacy should not rest on a wellness influencer's social media when clinical trials are available. Where the source is weaker than the claim requires, flag it and recommend a stronger source type. The goal is not maximum sourcing for every claim but proportionate sourcing for each claim's weight.

### Check Source Independence And Conflicts Of Interest

A source's reliability depends not only on its type but on its independence from the interests affected by the claim. A study funded by an industry about that industry's product, a report from an advocacy organization about its own cause, or a press release from a company about its own performance all carry conflicts that weaken their evidentiary value regardless of source type.

For each source, consider who produced it, who funded it, and what interest they have in the claim's outcome. A peer-reviewed study funded by the industry whose product it evaluates is weaker than an independently funded one. A statistic from an advocacy group may be accurate but should be checked against an independent source. Flag sources with material conflicts of interest and recommend independent verification or, at minimum, disclosure of the conflict to the reader. Undisclosed conflict of interest is a credibility failure readers will discover.

### Distinguish Primary From Secondary Sources

Primary sources are original data, documents, witnesses, or research; secondary sources interpret, summarize, or report on primary material. Both have value, but they support different claims and carry different risks. Distinguish which type each citation is and judge its use accordingly.

Primary sources are strongest for establishing facts: the actual data, the original document, the direct witness. Secondary sources are useful for synthesis, interpretation, and context, but they introduce a layer of mediation that can distort. A draft that relies on secondary summaries when primary sources are accessible is weaker than one that goes to the original. A draft that presents a secondary source's interpretation as if it were the primary source's fact commits a category error. Recommend primary sourcing for load-bearing factual claims and reserve secondary sources for context and synthesis.

### Detect Recycled And Aggregated Sources

A common weakness is the draft that cites several sources which all ultimately trace back to one original source. The appearance of multiple citations creates a false impression of convergent evidence when the underlying support is singular. Trace sources to their origin.

When multiple sources support a claim, check whether they are independent of each other. Three news articles citing the same press release are not three sources; they are one. Two studies drawing on the same dataset and method are not independent confirmation. Apparent convergence that collapses to a single origin is weaker than it looks. Flag recycled sourcing and recommend genuinely independent corroboration for important claims.

### Evaluate Source Currency And Scope

A source's value depends on whether it is current enough and scoped appropriately for the claim. A decade-old statistic may no longer reflect reality; a study of one population may not generalize to another; a national figure may not support a local claim. Check currency and scope against the claim.

For each cited source, ask whether it is recent enough to be reliable for the claim, whether its scope matches the claim's scope, and whether its context matches the claim's context. A claim about current trends should not rest on pre-pandemic data without acknowledgment. A claim about a specific group should not rest on a study of a different group. Flag mismatches of currency and scope and recommend sources that fit the claim's time and population.

### Recommend Source Upgrades Where Needed

Identifying weak sourcing is only half the work; the editor should recommend what would strengthen it. For each flagged source, suggest the type of source that would better support the claim, even if the editor cannot locate the exact reference.

A claim resting on a blog post might be strengthened by a peer-reviewed study or government data. A claim resting on a press release might be strengthened by independent reporting. A claim resting on an old source might be strengthened by a current one. Concrete upgrade recommendations help the author find better support rather than leaving them to guess. Where no stronger source exists because the claim is genuinely unsettled, recommend that the author acknowledge the evidentiary limit honestly rather than implying certainty.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Citations As Equivalent

Source type and reliability vary widely. Rank sources rather than counting citations.

### Missing Conflicts Of Interest

A source's funding and affiliation affect its reliability. Check independence, not just type.

### Accepting Apparent Convergence At Face Value

Multiple citations may trace to one origin. Verify that sources are genuinely independent.

### Relying On Secondary Sources When Primary Are Accessible

Secondary mediation can distort. Go to primary sources for load-bearing factual claims.

### Ignoring Currency And Scope Mismatches

An otherwise strong source may be too old or too narrow for the claim. Check fit.

### Flagging Weakness Without Recommending Upgrades

Identifying weak sourcing helps more when paired with what would strengthen it. Recommend source types.

### Confusing Source Quantity With Source Quality

Five weak sources do not equal one strong one. Quality and independence matter more than count.

## Self-Check

Before treating a source strength evaluation as complete, verify:

- Cited sources have been ranked by type and reliability, making weak sourcing visible.
- Source strength has been matched to claim weight, with under-sourced claims flagged.
- Each source's independence and conflicts of interest have been considered.
- Primary and secondary sources have been distinguished and used appropriately.
- Apparent convergence has been traced to verify sources are genuinely independent.
- Source currency and scope have been checked against the claim's time and population.
- Weak sources have been paired with recommendations for stronger alternatives.
- Conflicts of interest have been flagged for verification or disclosure.
- The evidentiary foundation is built on sources that can bear scrutiny for the claims made.
- Where no stronger source exists, the author is advised to acknowledge the evidentiary limit honestly.
