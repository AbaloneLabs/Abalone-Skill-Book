# Authorial Voice Identification

Voice is what makes a writer's prose recognizably theirs. It is the composite of diction, syntax, rhythm, perspective, register, and habitual attention that distinguishes one author from another. When an editor revises without first identifying the author's voice, the edits tend to flatten it, replacing distinctive choices with generic correctness until the prose sounds like no one in particular. Voice identification is the discipline of describing an author's characteristic patterns before editing, so that revision strengthens the voice rather than erasing it. Editors who skip this step produce clean, correct, lifeless prose that the author experiences as a violation.

Use this skill before and during revision of any author's work, especially in developmental, line, and copy editing where the editor's hand touches the prose directly. It covers analyzing the components of voice, describing it concretely, distinguishing voice features from errors, and using the description to guide edits that preserve distinctiveness. The editor's job is to know the voice well enough to revise within it rather than against it.

## Core Rules

### Analyze Voice Across Its Component Dimensions

Voice is not a single quality but a composite. To identify it, break it into analyzable components and describe each. A vague sense that an author sounds "literary" or "conversational" is not actionable; specific description of the patterns is.

Examine diction, the author's characteristic vocabulary: formal or informal, abstract or concrete, Latinate or Anglo-Saxon, specialized or general. Examine syntax: long or short sentences, simple or complex, periodic or loose, varied or uniform. Examine rhythm: the cadence, the pacing, the use of repetition or fragmentation. Examine perspective and attention: what the author notices, what they linger on, what they skip. Examine register: the formality, the distance from the reader, the use of first or second person. Describe each component specifically, with examples from the text, so the voice becomes a concrete profile rather than an impression.

### Distinguish Voice Features From Errors

The central challenge of voice preservation is telling the difference between a feature and a flaw. The same surface pattern can be a deliberate voice choice or an unintentional error. A fragment is a feature in a stylist's prose and an error in a formal report. Nonstandard grammar is a feature in dialect writing and an error in standard prose. Before correcting anything, determine whether it is voice or mistake.

Test each pattern by asking whether it appears consistently and intentionally, whether it serves the work's effect, and whether removing it would weaken the prose's distinctiveness. A pattern that recurs deliberately and contributes to the voice is a feature to preserve. A pattern that appears inconsistently, distracts, or undermines the work is an error to correct. When uncertain, query the author rather than silently changing. Preserving voice requires the discipline of not "correcting" what is deliberate.

### Describe The Voice Before Editing

Before making edits, write a description of the author's voice in concrete terms. This description serves as a reference point throughout revision, helping you distinguish edits that serve the voice from edits that impose your own. A voice description also helps maintain consistency across a long edit and communicate with the author about what you are preserving.

Capture the voice's signature: its characteristic diction, syntax, rhythm, and perspective, with examples. Note what gives it force or distinctiveness. Note any patterns that sit near the boundary between feature and flaw. Refer back to this description as you edit, asking whether each proposed change aligns with or erodes the described voice. The description turns voice from an intuition into a standard you can apply consistently.

### Identify What Gives The Voice Its Force

Within an author's voice, certain elements carry more of its distinctive force than others. These are the load-bearing features: the patterns whose removal would most diminish the prose's recognizability and power. Identify them so revision protects them especially.

An author's force may live in their startling verbs, their rhythmic fragmentation, their plain-spoken directness, their eye for concrete detail, their wry asides, or their long, accumulating sentences. Whatever it is, name it, and ensure your edits preserve and even serve it. A revision that removes the load-bearing features, however correct in the abstract, destroys what makes the prose worth reading. Protect the source of the voice's power.

### Match Edits To The Voice's Own Logic

When you must revise, make edits that follow the voice's own logic rather than substituting a different logic. If the author favors short Anglo-Saxon words, do not replace them with Latinate formality. If the author builds meaning through accumulation, do not replace it with terse compression. Edit within the voice's grain.

This requires understanding not just what the author does but why, the logic behind their choices. A voice that favors plain words may do so for directness and trust; replacing them with fancier alternatives betrays that logic. A voice that fragments may do so for urgency; smoothing the fragments into complete sentences removes the urgency. When an edit is necessary, find a solution consistent with the voice's underlying logic rather than imposing a generic correction. The edit should look like something the author could have written.

### Preserve Deliberate Departures From Standard

Many distinctive voices depart deliberately from standard grammar, usage, or structure. These departures are often the most recognizable features of the voice, and they are also the patterns most likely to be "corrected" by an inattentive editor. Identify deliberate departures and preserve them.

A voice may use sentence fragments for impact, nonstandard dialect for authenticity, repeated structures for rhythm, or unconventional punctuation for effect. Each is a choice, not an error, and correcting it erases the voice. Flag deliberate departures explicitly in your voice description so they are not normalized away during editing. Where a departure genuinely interferes with clarity, recommend an alternative that preserves the effect while solving the problem, rather than reverting to standard.

### Check That Edits Have Not Shifted The Voice

After editing, compare the revised prose to the original voice description to verify that the voice has been preserved, not shifted toward the editor's own preferences or toward generic correctness. Voice drift is often gradual and cumulative, each small edit moving the prose slightly until the cumulative effect is a different voice.

Read edited passages against the original and ask whether they still sound like the author. If a passage now sounds more like the editor than the author, the edit has overstepped. This check is especially important in copy editing, where the temptation to regularize is strongest. The goal is prose that is cleaner and clearer but still unmistakably the author's. If the edit has made the prose generic, pull back.

## Common Traps

### Correcting Voice Features As If They Were Errors

Distinctive patterns are often misread as mistakes. Test whether a pattern is deliberate before correcting.

### Editing Without A Voice Description

Without a concrete reference, edits drift toward the editor's preferences. Describe the voice first.

### Removing Load-Bearing Features

Some patterns carry most of the voice's force. Identify and protect them especially.

### Substituting The Editor's Logic For The Author's

Edits should follow the voice's own logic, not impose a different one. Edit within the grain.

### Normalizing Deliberate Departures

Nonstandard choices that constitute voice are erased by correction. Preserve them.

### Gradual Voice Drift Through Small Edits

Cumulative small changes can shift the voice significantly. Check edited prose against the original.

### Treating Voice As Intuition Rather Than Analysis

Vague impressions are not actionable. Describe voice in concrete, component-level terms.

## Self-Check

Before treating voice identification and preservation as complete, verify:

- The author's voice has been analyzed across its component dimensions: diction, syntax, rhythm, perspective, register.
- Voice features have been distinguished from errors, with deliberate patterns preserved.
- A concrete voice description has been written before editing, with examples from the text.
- The load-bearing features that give the voice its force have been identified and protected.
- Edits follow the voice's own logic rather than substituting the editor's preferences.
- Deliberate departures from standard have been identified and preserved, not normalized.
- Edited prose has been checked against the original voice description to detect drift.
- The revision has made the prose cleaner and clearer without making it generic.
- Uncertain feature-versus-error cases have been queried rather than silently changed.
- The edited prose remains unmistakably the author's, not the editor's.
