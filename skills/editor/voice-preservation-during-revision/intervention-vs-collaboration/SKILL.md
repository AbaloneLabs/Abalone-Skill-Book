# Intervention Versus Collaboration

Revision exists on a spectrum from minimal intervention to substantive rewriting, and where an editor falls on that spectrum has ethical, relational, and craft consequences. Intervene too lightly and the work keeps problems the editor was hired to fix. Intervene too heavily and the editor crosses from editing into ghostwriting, appropriating the author's work and violating the implicit contract that the words remain the author's. The discipline of intervention versus collaboration is choosing the right level of editorial force for each situation, respecting authorial ownership while still doing the editorial job. Editors who do not think about this boundary either abandon the work or colonize it.

Use this skill when revising an author's prose at any level, from line editing to copy editing, especially where the editor's changes touch voice, structure, or meaning. It covers calibrating intervention to the task and relationship, choosing among queries, suggestions, and direct edits, preserving authorial ownership, and knowing when to escalate or step back. The editor's job is to do the work that is needed without taking over the work that is not.

## Core Rules

### Calibrate Intervention To The Task And Relationship

The appropriate level of intervention depends on the editing task, the work's stage, the author's experience, and the working relationship. A copy edit calls for lighter intervention than a developmental rewrite. An experienced author with a strong voice warrants more restraint than a developing author seeking heavy guidance. Calibrate your force to the situation.

Consider what you were asked to do. A copy edit should correct errors and enforce consistency, not restructure arguments or rewrite passages. A line edit may rework sentences for flow and emphasis but should not change meaning. A developmental edit may suggest substantial restructuring but typically through direction rather than imposed rewriting. Match your intervention to the brief. Over-intervening beyond the task scope violates the contract; under-intervening fails to deliver the service. Clarify the scope before editing if it is ambiguous.

### Prefer Queries Over Silent Rewrites For Judgment Calls

When a passage has a problem but the solution involves judgment about meaning, voice, or intent, prefer a query over a silent rewrite. A query respects the author's ownership of the decision while flagging the issue. A silent rewrite imposes the editor's judgment without giving the author a chance to disagree.

Use queries for changes that affect meaning, voice, emphasis, or factual content: "Did you mean X or Y here?" "This passage seems to contradict the earlier claim; which is your intent?" "I find this unclear; could you rephrase?" Reserve direct edits for clear errors, consistency matters, and improvements that do not touch the author's meaning or voice. The rule of thumb: if the change is mechanical or unambiguous, edit directly; if it involves judgment about what the author meant or wanted, query. Heavy silent rewriting teaches authors to distrust editors and erodes the collaboration.

### Use Suggested Rewrites To Show, Not Impose

When a passage needs substantial reworking, offering a suggested rewrite can be more useful than a vague query, but it should be framed as a suggestion, not a substitution. The author should see the proposed alternative and decide whether to accept, modify, or reject it.

Present suggested rewrites as options: "Consider revising to something like: [suggested text]." This shows the author what you have in mind and gives them a concrete starting point, while leaving the decision with them. Avoid presenting rewrites as fait accompli that the author must accept or feel guilty about rejecting. The suggested rewrite is a tool for communication, not a replacement of the author's work. When the author accepts a rewrite, ensure they understand and own the change.

### Preserve Authorial Ownership Of Meaning And Voice

The boundary between editing and ghostwriting is the ownership of meaning and voice. An editor may improve clarity, correctness, consistency, and flow, but the work's meaning, argument, and distinctive voice should remain the author's. Crossing this boundary turns editing into authorship.

Check your edits against this standard. Have you changed what the author meant, or only how clearly they said it? Have you substituted your voice for theirs, or only removed obstacles to their voice? Have you introduced ideas, arguments, or emphases that the author did not intend? Flag any edits that approach the meaning-and-voice boundary and recommend confirming with the author. When in doubt, less intervention preserves ownership better than more. The author should be able to read the edited work and recognize it as their own, improved but not replaced.

### Match Intervention Style To The Author's Experience And Preference

Different authors need different intervention styles. A developing author may want and benefit from heavier guidance, detailed rewrites, and explicit direction. An experienced author with a strong voice may want only error correction and light queries, and may experience heavier intervention as violation. Match your style to the author.

Assess the author's experience, the strength of their voice, their stated preferences, and how they have responded to feedback so far. For authors who want collaboration, engage more substantively. For authors who want a light touch, restrain your intervention and query rather than rewrite. When unsure, ask the author directly what level of intervention they prefer. Imposing a heavy hand on an author who wants restraint, or a light hand on an author who wants guidance, both fail the relationship.

### Know When To Escalate Structural Problems

Some problems cannot be solved at the sentence level, and continuing to edit prose when the underlying structure is broken wastes effort and risks imposing coherence on a flawed foundation. Know when to stop line-level intervention and escalate to the author that structural work is needed.

If you find yourself rewriting passage after passage to fix problems that originate in structure, scope, or missing material, pause and flag the structural issue to the author. Continuing to polish prose that may be cut or substantially reorganized is premature. Recommend that the structural problem be addressed first, then return to line-level editing once the foundation is stable. Escalation is not abandonment; it is recognizing the right level for the problem.

### Document Edits So The Author Can Review And Learn

Whatever level of intervention you choose, document your edits so the author can review them, understand the reasoning, and learn from the process. Invisible edits, where the author cannot tell what changed or why, deny the author ownership and the chance to improve. Make edits transparent.

Use tracked changes, comments, or an editorial summary that explains significant edits and their rationale. Group edits by type so the author can see patterns: consistency changes, clarity improvements, error corrections, queries. Transparency lets the author accept or reject changes informedly, learn from the editor's reasoning, and maintain ownership of the final work. An editing process the author cannot see is a process that disempowers them.

## Common Traps

### Over-Intervening Beyond The Task Scope

Heavy rewriting in a copy edit violates the brief. Match intervention to what was asked.

### Silent Rewrites On Judgment Calls

Imposing the editor's judgment without querying denies the author the decision. Query meaning and voice changes.

### Crossing Into Ghostwriting

Changing meaning, argument, or voice appropriates the author's work. Preserve ownership.

### One-Size-Fits-All Intervention

Authors need different levels of force. Calibrate to experience and preference.

### Polishing Prose Over A Broken Structure

Line-editing when structure is broken wastes effort. Escalate structural problems.

### Invisible Edits The Author Cannot Review

Undocumented changes deny ownership and learning. Make edits transparent.

### Confusing Heavy Intervention With Thoroughness

Doing more is not always better editing. Restraint can be the more skilled choice.

## Self-Check

Before treating intervention calibration as complete, verify:

- The level of intervention matches the editing task, the work's stage, and the working relationship.
- Judgment calls about meaning and voice have been queried rather than silently rewritten.
- Suggested rewrites are framed as options the author can accept, modify, or reject.
- Edits preserve the author's ownership of meaning, argument, and distinctive voice.
- Intervention style has been matched to the author's experience and stated preferences.
- Structural problems have been escalated rather than papered over with prose edits.
- Edits are documented and transparent so the author can review, understand, and learn.
- The scope of intervention stays within the brief, neither abandoning nor colonizing the work.
- The author can read the edited work and recognize it as their own, improved but not replaced.
- Restraint has been used where it serves the work better than heavy intervention.
