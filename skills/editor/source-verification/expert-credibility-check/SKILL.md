# Expert Credibility Check

Appeals to authority are common in nonfiction, journalism, and research-based writing. An expert is cited or quoted to lend weight to a claim, and the reader trusts the appeal based on the assumption that the expert is genuinely authoritative. But not all presented experts are equally credible: some lack relevant expertise, some have conflicts of interest that bias their positions, and some are presented as more authoritative than their credentials warrant. When an appeal to authority rests on a weak or conflicted expert, the claim is weaker than it appears. Expert credibility checking is the discipline of verifying that experts presented as authorities have the expertise, credentials, and independence their citation implies. Editors who accept expert citations at face value publish works whose authority claims may not withstand scrutiny.

Use this skill when editing works that cite or quote experts, including journalism, nonfiction, policy writing, and research-based content. It covers verifying relevant expertise, checking credentials, identifying conflicts of interest, and flagging authority that is overstated. The goal is a work whose appeals to authority rest on genuine, relevant, unconflicted expertise.

## Core Rules

### Verify Relevant Expertise For The Claim

Expertise is domain-specific. An expert in one field is not an expert in another, and authority does not transfer across domains. A Nobel laureate in physics is not an authority on public health; a distinguished historian is not an authority on economics. Verify that an expert's expertise is relevant to the specific claim they are cited to support.

For each cited expert, identify their actual area of expertise. Compare it to the claim they support. Flag cases where the expertise is in a different domain, where it is adjacent but not directly relevant, or where it is general rather than specific. An expert cited outside their domain is not an expert for that claim, regardless of their eminence in their own field. Relevant expertise is the foundation of credible authority; without it, the appeal is to reputation rather than knowledge.

### Check Credentials And Affiliations

Credentials and affiliations signal expertise, but they must be accurate and meaningful. An expert's degrees, positions, and institutional affiliations should be verified, because they are sometimes exaggerated, outdated, or from institutions of questionable standing. Check credentials and affiliations to confirm they support the authority claimed.

Verify the expert's stated credentials: degrees, fields, institutions, and dates. Verify current affiliations and positions. Be alert to credentials that are exaggerated, irrelevant, or from unaccredited or ideologically aligned institutions. Check whether the affiliation is current or past, and whether it is as presented. Misrepresented credentials undermine the authority they are meant to establish. Verification protects the work from relying on false authority and from the embarrassment of citing an expert whose credentials do not hold up.

### Identify Conflicts Of Interest

Conflicts of interest bias expert positions, and undisclosed conflicts undermine the credibility of an expert citation. An expert with a financial stake in a product, a political affiliation with a position, or a personal interest in an outcome may not be a neutral authority. Identify conflicts of interest and assess whether they affect the expert's reliability for the claim.

For each expert, consider potential conflicts: financial ties to companies or industries discussed, political or ideological affiliations, personal or professional stakes in the outcome. Where conflicts exist, assess their relevance to the claim and whether they are disclosed in the work. Undisclosed relevant conflicts are a serious credibility problem; they suggest the expert is not the neutral authority they appear to be. Flag conflicts for disclosure or for the author's consideration of whether the expert remains appropriate. Transparency about conflicts allows the reader to weigh the authority appropriately.

### Assess The Standing Of The Expert In Their Field

Beyond formal credentials, an expert's standing in their field, their reputation among peers, their publication record, their influence, affects their authority. An expert who is marginal, controversial within their field, or rejected by mainstream consensus carries different weight than one who is widely respected. Assess standing to calibrate the authority appropriately.

Consider the expert's standing: are they representative of mainstream views in their field, or are they an outlier? Are they widely cited and respected, or marginal? For controversial claims, is the expert a recognized authority or a fringe figure? Standing does not determine truth, and outliers can be right, but it affects how the expert's views should be presented. An outlier presented as mainstream consensus misleads the reader. Calibrate the presentation to the expert's actual standing, and where an expert is controversial, note the controversy rather than presenting them as unchallenged authority.

### Distinguish Expert Opinion From Established Consensus

A single expert's opinion, even from a credible expert, is not the same as established consensus in a field. Presenting an individual opinion as established fact overstates the authority. Distinguish between expert opinion, which is one view, and consensus, which is the field's collective position. Present each appropriately.

For claims supported by expert citation, determine whether the claim reflects individual opinion or field consensus. Individual expert opinion should be attributed as such, not presented as established fact. Consensus claims should be supported by evidence of consensus, such as major reviews or professional society positions, not merely one expert. Conflating opinion with consensus is a common authority distortion. Where experts disagree, present the disagreement rather than citing one side as definitive. Honest authority presentation distinguishes what is known from what is opined.

### Verify Quotations And Attributions To Experts

When experts are quoted, the quotations and attributions must be accurate. An expert misquoted or quoted out of context may dispute the citation, and the misrepresentation undermines the work. Verify quotations and attributions to experts as rigorously as any other source.

For each expert quotation, verify it against the original source, word for word. Check that the quotation is in context and not distorted through selective excerpting. Verify the attribution correctly identifies the expert and their affiliation at the time of the statement. Be alert to quotations that have been altered, truncated, or repurposed. Expert quotation errors are damaging because the expert can publicly dispute them, and because they put words in the mouth of an authority. Verify against primary sources where possible.

### Flag Authority That Is Overstated

A common problem is presenting an expert or source as more authoritative than warranted. A consultant presented as a leading researcher, a commentator presented as a scholar, a single study presented as established consensus, these overstate authority and mislead the reader. Flag overstated authority and recommend calibration.

Review how each expert and source is described. Flag descriptions that inflate authority: titles or affiliations that exaggerate, qualifications that overstate, or presentations that imply more consensus than exists. Recommend descriptions that accurately represent the expert's standing and the claim's support. Overstated authority is a form of intellectual dishonesty, even if unintentional, and it is exposed when readers check the sources. Accurate authority presentation builds trust; inflated authority destroys it when exposed.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Expert Citations At Face Value

Experts must be verified. Do not assume presented authority is genuine or relevant.

### Expertise Outside The Relevant Domain

Authority does not transfer across fields. Verify relevance to the specific claim.

### Unverified Credentials And Affiliations

Credentials can be exaggerated or misrepresented. Verify degrees, positions, and institutions.

### Undisclosed Conflicts Of Interest

Conflicts bias positions. Identify and assess relevance, and ensure disclosure.

### Presenting Outliers As Mainstream

Standing affects authority. Calibrate presentation to the expert's actual position in the field.

### Conflating Opinion With Consensus

Individual opinion is not consensus. Present each appropriately and note disagreement.

### Overstating Authority

Inflated titles and affiliations mislead. Recommend accurate authority description.

## Self-Check

Before treating expert credibility checking as complete, verify:

- Each cited expert's expertise has been verified as relevant to the specific claim supported.
- Credentials and affiliations have been checked for accuracy and meaningful standing.
- Conflicts of interest have been identified and assessed for relevance to the claim.
- Relevant conflicts are disclosed in the work or flagged for disclosure.
- Each expert's standing in their field has been assessed to calibrate their authority.
- Expert opinion is distinguished from established consensus in presentation.
- Disagreement among experts is presented rather than suppressed.
- Direct quotations and attributions to experts have been verified against original sources.
- Authority descriptions have been checked for inflation and calibrated to actual standing.
- The work's appeals to authority rest on genuine, relevant, and unconflicted expertise.
