# Primary Vs Secondary Assessment

Sources exist in a hierarchy of authority. Primary sources are original materials, direct evidence, firsthand accounts, original research, and raw data. Secondary sources interpret, analyze, or summarize primary sources, providing commentary rather than direct evidence. Both have legitimate uses, but they carry different authority, and a claim's credibility depends partly on whether it rests on the appropriate source type. A claim presented as established fact that actually rests on a secondary summary, when the primary source is available and tells a more nuanced story, is weaker than it appears. Primary versus secondary assessment is the discipline of evaluating whether each claim relies on the source type its authority requires. Editors who do not assess source type treat all citations as equal and miss claims resting on inadequate authority.

Use this skill when editing works that make factual or scholarly claims supported by sources, including journalism, academic writing, history, and research-based nonfiction. It covers distinguishing source types, matching claims to appropriate source authority, and flagging authority gaps. The goal is a work where each claim's sourcing matches the authority the claim asserts.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Primary, Secondary, And Tertiary Sources

The foundation of source assessment is correctly distinguishing source types. Primary sources are original evidence: research data, historical documents, firsthand interviews, original artworks, legislation. Secondary sources interpret or analyze primary sources: scholarly books and articles, journalism based on primary reporting, reviews and criticism. Tertiary sources compile and summarize: encyclopedias, textbooks, handbooks. Each type has a distinct relationship to the evidence.

For each cited source, determine its type. Is it original evidence, interpretation of evidence, or a compilation? Consider the source's relationship to what it reports: a research article presenting original data is primary for that data; a news article reporting on the research is secondary; a textbook summarizing the field is tertiary. The same work can be primary for some claims and secondary for others. Correct classification is necessary because the appropriate use and authority of a source depends on its type.

### Match Claim Authority To Source Type

Different claims require different levels of source authority. A claim of direct fact, what a document says or what data shows, requires a primary source. A claim of interpretation or significance can rest on secondary analysis. A claim of general background can rest on tertiary sources. The authority of a claim should match the authority of its source; mismatch creates claims that appear stronger or weaker than their sourcing supports.

For each claim, assess the level of authority it asserts. A claim stated as established fact requires primary sourcing or authoritative secondary consensus. An interpretive claim can rest on secondary analysis with attribution. A background claim can rest on tertiary sources for general, uncontested information. Flag claims where the asserted authority exceeds the source authority: a definitive factual claim resting on a secondary summary, or a contested interpretation presented as established fact. Match claim to source so the work's authority is honest.

### Flag Claims Resting On Secondary Where Primary Is Available

A common authority problem is a claim resting on a secondary source when the primary source is available and accessible. The secondary source may summarize, interpret, or even distort the primary, and the claim inherits the secondary's limitations. Where the primary source is available, important claims should consult it directly rather than relying on secondary mediation.

For important claims, those load-bearing for the argument or presented as established, check whether a primary source is available. If the claim rests on a secondary source discussing a primary, flag for the author to consult the primary directly. Primary consultation avoids the distortion of mediation and allows the claim to rest on direct evidence. This is especially important for controversial claims, precise quotations, or claims where the secondary source has a known perspective. Flagging these gaps directs the author to strengthen the work's foundation.

### Assess The Quality Within Each Source Type

Source type is the first assessment, but quality within type also matters. A primary source can be unreliable, biased, or incomplete. A secondary source can be authoritative or partisan. A tertiary source can be current or outdated. Assess quality within type, not just type itself, because a weak primary source may be less authoritative than a strong secondary one.

For each source, assess its quality within its type. For primary sources, consider provenance, completeness, and potential bias. For secondary sources, consider the author's expertise, the publisher's reputation, peer review status, and the source's perspective. For tertiary sources, consider currency, editorial process, and reputation. Quality assessment refines the type-based hierarchy: an authoritative secondary source may be preferred over a dubious primary. Combine type and quality to assess each source's actual authority for the claim it supports.

### Verify Direct Quotation And Attribution

When a work quotes or closely paraphrases a source, the quotation and attribution must be accurate. A quotation that is actually a paraphrase, an attribution that names the wrong source, or a quotation pulled out of context misrepresents the source. Verify direct quotations and attributions against the original.

For each direct quotation, verify it against the original source, word for word. Check that the quotation is not altered in meaning through selective excerpting or removed context. Verify the attribution names the correct source and that the source actually said or wrote what is attributed. For close paraphrases, verify the paraphrase is faithful and does not distort. Quotation and attribution errors are serious because they put words in sources' mouths and can be exposed. Verify against primary sources where possible, since secondary sources may themselves misquote.

### Check For Over-Reliance On Single Sources

A work that relies heavily on a single source for multiple claims is vulnerable to that source's limitations and perspective. Diversification of sources strengthens authority and reduces bias. Check for over-reliance on single sources, especially for contested or important claims.

Survey the source base and identify claims supported by single sources. For important or contested claims, flag single-sourcing and recommend corroboration. A claim resting on one source is only as strong as that source; corroboration across independent sources is much stronger. Single-sourcing is sometimes unavoidable, as with unique primary sources, but it should be acknowledged. Over-reliance on a single secondary source, in particular, means the work inherits that source's interpretation without independent verification. Flag over-reliance for the author's attention.

### Identify Sources That Need Updating

Sources have a shelf life. Research is superseded, statistics become outdated, interpretations evolve, and online sources change or disappear. A source that was authoritative when cited may no longer be current. Identify sources that may need updating, especially for claims presented as current.

For each source, consider its currency relative to the claim. Scientific and statistical sources may be superseded by newer research. Legal sources may be outdated by new legislation or rulings. Online sources may have changed since access. Flag sources that may be outdated for the author to verify or update. For claims presented as current state-of-the-art, currency is essential. An outdated source presented as current misleads the reader about the state of knowledge. Currency checking is especially important for works with long production timelines.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Citations As Equal

Sources differ in type and authority. Assess each source's appropriateness for its claim.

### Claims Stronger Than Their Sourcing

A definitive claim needs definitive sourcing. Flag authority mismatches.

### Relying On Secondary Where Primary Is Available

Important claims should consult primary sources directly. Flag secondary mediation.

### Ignoring Quality Within Source Type

Type is the first assessment, not the only one. Assess provenance, expertise, and currency.

### Unverified Quotations And Attributions

Quotation errors misrepresent sources. Verify against originals.

### Over-Reliance On Single Sources

Single-sourcing is vulnerable. Flag important claims lacking corroboration.

### Outdated Sources Presented As Current

Sources have a shelf life. Flag sources that may need updating.

## Self-Check

Before treating primary versus secondary assessment as complete, verify:

- Each cited source has been classified as primary, secondary, or tertiary.
- The authority asserted by each claim matches the authority of its supporting source.
- Claims resting on secondary sources where primary sources are available have been flagged.
- Quality within each source type has been assessed, including provenance, expertise, and bias.
- Direct quotations and attributions have been verified against original sources.
- Over-reliance on single sources for important or contested claims has been identified.
- Sources have been checked for currency relative to the claims they support.
- Sources presented as current are in fact current.
- The work's sourcing honestly represents the authority behind each claim.
- Authority gaps and sourcing concerns have been flagged for author attention.
