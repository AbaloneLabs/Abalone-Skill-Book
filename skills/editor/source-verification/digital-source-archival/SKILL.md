# Digital Source Archival

Digital sources are unstable. Web pages change, URLs break, sites reorganize, and content disappears. A citation to a digital source that is accurate today may be broken tomorrow, and a reader who follows the citation in a year may find nothing or something different. This instability undermines the verifiability that citations are meant to provide. Digital source archival is the discipline of ensuring digital citations remain retrievable over time, through access dates, permanent links, archived versions, and complete citation information. Editors who treat digital citations like print citations, assuming permanence, publish works whose sourcing decays. The work's credibility depends on citations that can be followed, and digital citations require active archival to remain followable.

Use this skill when editing works that cite digital sources, including web pages, online articles, datasets, social media, and digital media. It covers capturing access dates, verifying and using permanent links, archiving sources, and documenting digital citations completely. The goal is digital citations that remain verifiable across the work's lifespan.

## Core Rules

### Capture Access Dates For All Digital Sources

Unlike print sources, digital sources can change after citation. An access date documents when the cited content was retrieved, allowing readers to understand what version the author saw and to investigate changes. Capture access dates for all digital sources as standard practice.

For each digital source citation, verify an access date is recorded. The access date should reflect when the author accessed the content that supports the claim, not when the citation was compiled. Include the access date in the citation per the relevant style guide. Access dates are especially important for sources likely to change, such as news sites, wikis, and organizational pages. Without an access date, a reader who finds different content at the URL cannot tell whether the source changed or the citation was wrong. The access date anchors the citation to a point in time.

### Verify And Use Permanent Links Where Available

Many digital sources offer permanent or persistent links designed to remain stable: DOIs for scholarly articles, permanent URLs for archives, stable links for government documents. These are more reliable than ordinary URLs, which can break. Verify and use permanent links where available.

For each digital source, check whether a permanent link is available. For scholarly articles, use the DOI. For archival sources, use the permanent identifier. For government and institutional sources, prefer stable over temporary URLs. Where a permanent link exists, use it in preference to or alongside the ordinary URL. Permanent links significantly reduce link rot and improve long-term retrievability. Relying on ordinary URLs when permanent links are available is a missed opportunity to make citations durable.

### Archive Sources To Preserve Cited Versions

Even with access dates and permanent links, the cited version of a digital source may change or disappear. Archiving the source, through services that capture and store web pages at a point in time, preserves the exact version the author cited. Archive sources to ensure the cited content remains accessible regardless of what happens to the original.

For important or unstable digital sources, create an archived version using an archival service. Capture the archived URL and include it in the citation or maintain it in the source documentation. Archiving is especially valuable for sources likely to change or disappear, such as personal pages, organizational sites, news articles, and social media posts. The archived version allows readers to see exactly what the author saw, even if the original has changed or vanished. Archiving transforms ephemeral digital sources into stable references.

### Document Digital Citations Completely

Digital citations require complete documentation to be verifiable: author, title, site or publisher, date of publication, URL, access date, and any archival information. Incomplete digital citations, missing the publication date or site name, are hard to verify and hard to find if the URL breaks. Document digital citations completely.

For each digital source, verify the citation includes all required elements per the style guide. Check that author and title are identified, not just the organization. Check that the publication or update date is recorded, distinguishing it from the access date. Check that the site or publisher is named. Verify the URL is complete and correct. For sources without clear authorship or dates, document what is available and note the limitations. Complete documentation allows the source to be found through multiple paths, not just the URL, which is essential when links break.

### Verify Links Resolve To The Cited Content

A citation is only useful if the link resolves to the cited content. URLs can break, redirect to different content, or point to pages that have been substantially revised. Verify that each digital citation's link actually resolves to the content that supports the claim, not just to a homepage or a changed page.

For each digital citation, follow the link and verify it resolves to the cited content. Check that the content at the URL matches what is cited, not a revised version. Check that the link is not broken or redirected to a generic page. For links that resolve to homepages or landing pages rather than specific content, flag for a more specific URL. Link verification catches the common problem of citations that technically resolve but do not lead to the supporting content, which is functionally useless to a verifying reader.

### Handle Social Media And Ephemeral Sources Carefully

Social media posts, forum comments, and other ephemeral digital sources present particular archival challenges. They can be deleted, edited, or made private, and they may lack stable identifiers. Handle these sources with extra care to preserve them.

For social media and ephemeral sources, capture complete documentation: the author, the platform, the date and time, the full text or content, and the URL. Archive the source immediately, since it may disappear. Note the ephemeral nature in the citation if appropriate. For deleted or altered posts that are cited, verify the citation reflects what was there at the time of access, supported by the archive or access date. Ephemeral sources can be valuable evidence, but their instability requires active preservation to remain credible citations.

### Check For Source Migration And URL Changes

Even stable digital sources sometimes migrate to new URLs or platforms. A citation that was correct may break because the content moved, even though it still exists somewhere. Check for source migration and update citations to current locations.

During editing, verify cited URLs resolve. For broken links, search for the content at its current location; it may have migrated rather than disappeared. Update the citation to the current URL, noting the change if appropriate. For sources known to have migrated, such as government sites that reorganize, proactively check for current locations. Maintaining current URLs is an ongoing responsibility, and checking for migration during editing catches breaks before publication. A citation to migrated content is fixable; a citation to vanished content may not be.

## Common Traps

### Treating Digital Sources Like Print Sources

Digital sources are unstable. Capture access dates and archival information.

### Missing Access Dates

Without an access date, changes cannot be distinguished from errors. Record for all digital sources.

### Using Ordinary URLs When Permanent Links Exist

Permanent links reduce link rot. Use DOIs and stable identifiers where available.

### Not Archiving Unstable Sources

Cited versions can change or disappear. Archive important or unstable sources.

### Incomplete Digital Citations

Missing authors, dates, or site names make sources hard to verify. Document completely.

### Links That Resolve But Not To Cited Content

A resolving link to the wrong content is useless. Verify links point to cited material.

### Neglecting Ephemeral And Social Media Sources

These sources are especially unstable. Archive and document them carefully.

## Self-Check

Before treating digital source archival as complete, verify:

- Access dates are recorded for all digital source citations.
- Permanent links, such as DOIs and stable URLs, are used where available.
- Important or unstable sources have been archived, with archived URLs documented.
- Digital citations include complete documentation: author, title, site, dates, URL.
- Publication dates are distinguished from access dates in citations.
- All cited links have been verified to resolve to the cited content.
- Links that resolve to homepages or landing pages have been flagged for specificity.
- Social media and ephemeral sources are archived and completely documented.
- Broken links have been investigated for migration and updated to current locations.
- Digital citations remain verifiable across the work's expected lifespan.
