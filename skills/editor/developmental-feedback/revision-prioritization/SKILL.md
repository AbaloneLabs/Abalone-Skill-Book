# Revision Prioritization

A manuscript usually has more wrong with it than any author can address at once. Developmental feedback that lists every issue at equal weight is not editorial help; it is paralysis. Revision prioritization is the editorial craft of ranking problems so the author knows what must change for the work to function, what would significantly improve it, and what is optional polish. An editor who cannot prioritize forces the author to guess, and authors usually guess wrong, polishing prose while the structure remains broken or chasing optional tweaks while a blocking flaw persists.

Use this skill when turning a developmental diagnosis into an ordered revision plan. It covers severity classification, sequencing revisions so earlier work does not invalidate later work, separating blocking from non-blocking issues, and protecting the author from being overwhelmed. The editor's job is to give the author a clear path: what to do first, what to do next, and what can wait.

## Core Rules

### Classify Every Issue By Severity

Not all problems matter equally. Before handing feedback to an author, assign each issue a severity that reflects how much it affects the work's ability to succeed. Severity is not about how interesting the issue is to discuss; it is about consequence.

Use a small, consistent scale. Blocking issues prevent the work from functioning: a missing thesis, a broken structure, an unsupported central claim, a protagonist whose motivation never makes sense. The work cannot succeed until these change. Major issues significantly weaken the work but do not kill it: a sagging middle, an under-developed subplot, thin evidence in a key chapter. Moderate issues improve clarity, flow, or force when fixed. Optional issues are stylistic preference or enhancement. If everything is marked major, the author cannot tell what matters, and the feedback fails its purpose.

### Sequence So Earlier Revisions Do Not Invalidate Later Ones

Order matters as much as severity. Some revisions, if done first, make other planned revisions unnecessary or wrong. An author who line-edits a chapter that will later be cut has wasted effort. Sequence the plan so structural work precedes sectional work, and sectional work precedes line work.

Put structural and scope decisions first: thesis, organization, scope, what stays and what goes. Once the structure is stable, address sectional issues: chapter jobs, evidence within chapters, scene-level problems. Only after these are stable should the author polish sentences and prose. State this sequence explicitly. An author who follows severity alone may fix a blocking structural issue and a line-level issue in the same pass, then have to redo the line work after the structure shifts again.

### Separate Blocking Issues And State Them First

Blocking issues deserve their own category at the top of the feedback, stated plainly and unambiguously. The author must understand that until these are resolved, other revision is premature.

A blocking issue is one where the work cannot achieve its purpose regardless of how well other parts are executed. Examples include a thesis the evidence cannot support, a structure that hides the main point, a fatal factual error in a central claim, or a premise that contradicts the work's own evidence. Name blocking issues, explain why they block, and tell the author to address them before investing in other revision. Clarity here saves enormous wasted effort.

### Protect The Author From Overwhelm

A thorough developmental read can surface dozens of issues. Presenting them all at once, even prioritized, can crush an author's morale and stall revision. Prioritization is partly an act of editorial care.

Lead with the few issues that unlock the most improvement. Group related issues so the author sees patterns rather than a blizzard of separate notes. Consider delivering feedback in tiers across revision rounds rather than all at once, especially for less experienced authors. The goal is a revision the author can actually start and finish, not a complete catalog of imperfection. An overwhelmed author revises nothing.

### Distinguish Issues The Author Can Fix From Those Requiring New Material

Some revision tasks are editorial: reorganize, cut, sharpen, clarify. Others require the author to do new work: report, research, draft new scenes, gather evidence, interview sources. These place very different demands on the author and should be flagged distinctly.

Mark which issues are editorial fixes the author can do at the desk, and which require new material or new effort outside the existing draft. New-material tasks often take longer and may require decisions the author must make. Sequencing these correctly matters: if a chapter needs new reporting, that should start early because it cannot be rushed at the end.

### Tie Each Priority To The Work's Purpose

Prioritization is not abstract ranking; it is ranking against what the work is trying to achieve. An issue's severity depends on the work's contract. A pacing problem is blocking in a thriller and optional in a contemplative memoir.

State, for each major priority, why it matters for this work's specific purpose and audience. This helps the author understand the reasoning rather than following orders blindly, and it lets them make judgment calls when revisions create tradeoffs. Feedback that explains why is more useful and more respected than feedback that only says what.

### Identify Force Multipliers

Some revisions are force multipliers: fixing them resolves or reduces several other issues at once. Identifying these lets the author get disproportionate benefit from one change.

For example, clarifying the thesis often resolves scattered structure, weak transitions, and unclear audience simultaneously. Cutting a competing subplot may fix pacing, sharpen focus, and free word count for depth elsewhere. Point out force multipliers explicitly so the author tackles high-leverage changes first. This is one of the most valuable things an editor can offer.

### Leave Room For Authorial Judgment

Prioritization should guide, not commandeer. The author owns the work and may have reasons to address issues in a different order or to reject some feedback. Build the plan as a recommended sequence with clear rationale, not a mandate.

Where the author has legitimate discretion, say so. Distinguish issues where the editor is confident the work will fail without the change from issues where the editor is recommending a stronger path. Respecting authorial agency produces better collaboration and better revision than dictation.

## Common Traps

### Marking Everything As Major

When all issues carry equal weight, the author cannot prioritize and the feedback becomes noise. Reserve major and blocking labels for issues that truly warrant them.

### Sequencing By Severity Alone

Severity tells you what matters; it does not tell you what to do first. Always sequence structural before sectional before line, regardless of severity within those tiers.

### Delivering The Complete Catalog At Once

A comprehensive list of every flaw can paralyze. Curate and tier feedback so the author can act.

### Failing To Identify Force Multipliers

Without naming high-leverage changes, the author may grind through many small fixes while a single structural change would resolve them all.

### Treating Prioritization As The Editor's Taste

Severity must be tied to the work's purpose and audience, not to the editor's preferences. An issue is major because it harms the work, not because the editor dislikes it.

### Ignoring New-Material Versus Editorial-Fix Distinction

Grouping reporting tasks with reorganization tasks hides the real time and effort each requires. Flag the difference.

## Self-Check

Before treating a revision prioritization as complete, verify:

- Every issue has been assigned a severity on a consistent scale: blocking, major, moderate, or optional.
- Blocking issues are stated first and unambiguously, with explanation of why they block.
- The revision sequence puts structural before sectional before line-level work.
- Feedback has been curated or tiered to protect the author from overwhelm.
- Issues requiring new material are distinguished from editorial fixes the author can do at the desk.
- Each priority is tied to the work's specific purpose and audience, with reasoning given.
- Force multipliers, changes that resolve multiple issues at once, have been identified and highlighted.
- The plan respects authorial judgment where discretion is legitimate.
- Related issues are grouped so the author sees patterns, not a blizzard of notes.
- The author can read the plan and know exactly what to do first, next, and what can wait.
