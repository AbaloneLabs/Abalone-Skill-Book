# Actionable Feedback Craft

Feedback that the author cannot act on is not feedback; it is opinion. "The middle drags," "the argument needs work," and "I did not connect with the character" are reactions, not editorial guidance. Actionable feedback is the craft of translating a reader's experience into specific, located, diagnosable, and directional notes that tell the author what is happening, why it is happening, and what kind of change would resolve it. Editors who cannot make feedback actionable leave authors confused, defensive, or stuck, no matter how accurate the underlying diagnosis.

Use this skill when writing any developmental, structural, or substantive editorial feedback, whether in margin notes, an editorial letter, or a revision plan. It covers anchoring feedback to specific locations, diagnosing causes rather than listing symptoms, offering revision direction without commandeering the solution, and phrasing notes so they are useful rather than demoralizing. The editor's job is to convert editorial judgment into language the author can revise from.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Every Note To A Specific Location

Vague feedback floats free of the text and gives the author nothing to act on. "The pacing is off" names no scene, no chapter, no moment. The author cannot find the problem because the note has no address. Anchor every note to a specific location in the manuscript.

Reference the exact chapter, scene, paragraph, or passage. Quote the relevant line or describe the specific moment. "In chapter seven, the scene where the protagonist confronts the mentor, the emotional turn happens in one sentence after three pages of setup, so the payoff feels unearned" is actionable; "the emotional beats need work" is not. Location turns a reaction into a revision task.

### Diagnose Cause, Do Not Just Report Symptom

A symptom is what the reader experiences; a cause is why it happens. Feedback that reports only symptoms forces the author to diagnose the cause themselves, which is exactly the work they could not do when writing the draft. Diagnose the cause and connect it to the symptom.

For example, "the middle drags" is a symptom. The cause might be repeated scenes doing the same work, a subplot that adds no escalation, background delivered too late, or stakes that plateau. Each cause requires a different revision. Name the cause you have identified, explain how it produces the symptom, and the author can address the root rather than guessing. Symptom-only feedback generates guesswork revision that often misses the real problem.

### Give Revision Direction, Not Just Evaluation

Evaluation tells the author whether the work is good or bad; direction tells them what kind of change would help. Both have a place, but direction is what enables revision. Ensure every significant note includes direction.

Direction does not mean rewriting the passage for the author. It means naming the kind of change needed: move this earlier, cut the second example because it repeats the first, raise the stakes by introducing a consequence, define the term before using it, merge these two scenes because they serve the same function. Offer one or two plausible revision paths when possible, so the author sees options rather than a single imposed solution. Direction respects authorial ownership while making the path forward legible.

### Separate What Is Wrong From What To Do About It

Conflate diagnosis and prescription and the author cannot tell which is which. Keep the structure of each note clear: here is what I experienced, here is why, here is what I think would help.

This separation matters because the author may agree with the diagnosis but prefer a different remedy, or may disagree with the diagnosis and want to push back. When diagnosis and prescription are fused, the author cannot engage selectively. Separating them invites productive collaboration and lets the author bring their own solution to a correctly identified problem.

### Be Specific About The Reader's Experience

The author cannot see the work as a first-time reader sees it; that is the editor's value. Describe the reader's experience concretely enough that the author can picture it. Avoid generic reactions that could apply to any draft.

Instead of "this is confusing," describe the confusion: "by the end of this paragraph I was not sure whether the policy applied to current employees or only new hires, because the antecedent of 'they' is ambiguous." Instead of "I did not believe the decision," explain: "the protagonist agrees to the risky plan immediately after stating strong objections, with no scene showing what changed her mind, so the reversal reads as plot convenience." Specific experience descriptions are inherently more useful and more respectful than blanket judgments.

### Quantify And Give Scale

Authors need to know whether a note concerns a line, a paragraph, a scene, a chapter, or the whole work. Feedback without scale leaves the author unsure how much to revise. Indicate the scale of each issue.

Note whether a problem is localized to one passage, recurring across several sections, or structural and pervasive. A localized issue needs a local fix; a recurring issue suggests a pattern the author must address at its source; a structural issue requires rethinking. Scale tells the author how much effort and what kind of effort each note demands.

### Preserve What Works, Not Just What Fails

Feedback that lists only problems skews the author's sense of their own work and can erode the confidence needed to revise well. Identify what is working and should be preserved, so the author does not cut strong material while fixing weak material.

Name specific passages, techniques, scenes, or arguments that succeed, and explain why they work. This is not flattery; it is calibration. The author needs to know what to keep and build on, not only what to change. Preservation notes also help the author understand the editor's standards, making the critique more credible.

### Phrase For Utility, Not Ego

The tone and phrasing of feedback affects whether the author can hear it. Harsh, dismissive, or cleverly cruel phrasing triggers defensiveness and stalls revision, however accurate the note. Phrasing is an editorial skill, not a courtesy afterthought.

Be direct but respectful. Avoid sarcasm, contempt, or phrasing that makes the author feel incompetent. Frame notes around the work and the reader's experience, not around the author's ability. "This scene currently reads as X because Y; consider Z" is more useful and more humane than "this scene does not work." Utility and respect are not in tension; clear, kind, specific feedback is the most useful kind.

## Common Traps

### Offering Reactions Instead Of Direction

"I loved this" or "this fell flat" tells the author how you felt but not what to do. Always add direction.

### Fusing Diagnosis And Prescription

When the problem and the fix are inseparable, the author cannot engage with either selectively. Keep them distinct.

### Vague Location References

"Somewhere in the middle" or "early on" gives the author no address. Anchor notes to specific passages.

### Symptom-Only Notes

Reporting what felt wrong without diagnosing why forces the author to do the diagnostic work themselves. Name the cause.

### Over-Prescribing A Single Solution

Dictating exactly how to rewrite a passage steals the author's ownership and may miss their better solution. Offer direction and options.

### Omitting Preservation Notes

Critique without acknowledgment of strengths distorts the author's view of the work and risks losing good material. Calibrate with what works.

### Clever Cruelty Mistaken For Honesty

Harsh phrasing does not make feedback more accurate; it makes it less usable. Direct and respectful is not weak.

## Self-Check

Before treating actionable feedback as complete, verify:

- Every significant note is anchored to a specific location: chapter, scene, paragraph, or quoted passage.
- Each note diagnoses a cause and connects it to the symptom, not just reporting the symptom.
- Each note includes revision direction or plausible paths, not only evaluation.
- Diagnosis and prescription are separated so the author can engage with each.
- The reader's experience is described concretely, not with generic reactions applicable to any draft.
- The scale of each issue is indicated: localized, recurring, or structural.
- What is working is named and explained, not only what fails.
- Phrasing is direct, specific, and respectful, avoiding sarcasm or contempt.
- The author could read each note and know what to do without guessing.
- The feedback invites collaboration rather than commanding compliance.
