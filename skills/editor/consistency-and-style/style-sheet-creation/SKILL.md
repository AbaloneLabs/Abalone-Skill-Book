# Style Sheet Creation

A style sheet is the record of every editorial decision made on a project. Without one, consistency is a matter of memory and luck, and on any work longer than a few pages, both fail. The editor who decides in chapter one that the term is "decision-making" hyphenated and then encounters "decision making" unhyphenated in chapter ten has no record to consult, and the inconsistency stands. A style sheet turns scattered decisions into a reference that ensures consistency, enables collaboration, and provides a defensible record of why choices were made. Editors who do not maintain a style sheet produce internally inconsistent work and cannot explain their choices when challenged.

Use this skill when editing any substantial work to create and maintain a project style sheet. It covers what to record, how to organize the sheet, how to use it during editing, and how to hand it off. The editor's job is to make consistency systematic rather than incidental.

## Core Rules

### Record Every Nonstandard Decision

A style sheet exists to capture decisions that are not automatic, either because the style guide does not address them, because the author has established a nonstandard preference, or because the work requires a choice among legitimate options. Record every such decision the moment it is made.

Capture spelling variants the author prefers, capitalization choices for recurring terms, hyphenation of compounds, number formatting, date and time formats, treatment of names and titles, abbreviations and acronyms, and any usage choice that could recur. If you make a decision, record it. If you defer to the author's established pattern, record that too. The principle is that any decision applied more than once should be documented so it is applied the same way every time. Undocumented decisions lead to inconsistency because later instances are decided fresh, without memory of the earlier choice.

### Organize The Sheet For Quick Reference

A style sheet is a tool, and a tool is only useful if you can find what you need quickly. Organize the sheet so decisions are retrievable during editing, not buried in a chronological log. Structure it by category so related decisions cluster together.

Use clear categories: spelling and hyphenation, capitalization, numbers and dates, names and terms, abbreviations, punctuation, and any project-specific categories. Within categories, alphabetize entries so they can be found by scanning. Include the governing style guide and dictionary at the top, then record only deviations and specific decisions. A well-organized style sheet lets you check a decision in seconds; a disorganized one wastes time and gets ignored, which defeats its purpose.

### Record Decisions As They Arise, Not Retroactively

The most common style sheet failure is trying to reconstruct decisions after the fact, when memory is unreliable and inconsistencies have already crept in. Record decisions as they arise during editing, while the context is fresh and the reasoning is clear.

When you encounter a term or construction that requires a decision, make the decision, apply it, and record it immediately. Do not plan to "add it to the style sheet later," because later you will forget or the decision will seem less clear. The discipline of real-time recording is what makes a style sheet accurate. If you use editing software, add entries as you work. If you edit on paper or in a separate document, update the sheet in the same session as the edit.

### Note The Authority Behind Each Decision

A defensible style sheet records not just what was decided but why, the authority or reasoning behind the choice. This matters when decisions are questioned, when collaborators need to understand the logic, and when future editors take over the project. Note the basis for each decision.

For each entry, indicate the source: the style guide's ruling, the dictionary's preferred form, the author's established preference, a house convention, or an editorial judgment call. "Decision-making, hyphenated, per Merriam-Webster" is more defensible than "decision-making" alone. When you override a style guide for a specific reason, note the reason. This transforms the style sheet from a list of arbitrary choices into a reasoned record that can be defended and understood.

### Use The Sheet Actively During Editing

A style sheet that is created but not consulted during editing provides no consistency benefit. The sheet must be used actively: consulted whenever a recurring term or construction appears, and applied to ensure each instance matches the recorded decision. Make consultation a habit.

When you encounter a term, name, or construction that appears in the style sheet, verify the current instance matches the recorded decision. When you encounter one that is not yet in the sheet but recurs, decide and add it. The style sheet is a working document, not an archive. Editors who create a sheet and then edit without consulting it defeat the purpose. Active use is what produces consistency.

### Coordinate With Collaborators Through The Sheet

On projects with multiple editors, proofreaders, or contributors, the style sheet is the coordination mechanism that prevents each party from making independent, conflicting decisions. Share the sheet, keep it current, and ensure all parties use it. A shared style sheet is how a team produces consistent work.

Distribute the style sheet to everyone touching the prose. Update it as new decisions arise and communicate updates. When a collaborator raises a question the sheet answers, point them to it. When a collaborator makes a decision not yet recorded, add it. The style sheet becomes the single source of truth that aligns the team. Without it, multiple editors will make conflicting choices that no one notices until the inconsistencies appear in proof.

### Hand Off The Sheet With The Project

A style sheet's value extends beyond the current edit. It accompanies the project through proofreading, production, and future editions, and it informs related works in a series. Ensure the style sheet is handed off with the manuscript so downstream stages can maintain consistency.

Include the style sheet with the edited manuscript when passing to proofreading, production, or the author. A proofreader without the style sheet may "correct" decisions back to the default style guide, undoing deliberate choices. Production may need term and capitalization decisions for design. Future editions or series volumes benefit from the established decisions. The style sheet is part of the project's editorial record, not a disposable working document.

## Common Traps

### Trying To Reconstruct Decisions Retroactively

Memory is unreliable. Record decisions as they arise, not after the fact.

### Creating A Sheet But Not Consulting It

An unused style sheet provides no consistency. Consult it actively during editing.

### Recording Decisions Without Their Authority

A list of choices without reasoning cannot be defended. Note the source for each.

### Disorganized Sheets That Cannot Be Searched

A style sheet is a tool. Organize by category and alphabetize for quick reference.

### Failing To Share The Sheet With Collaborators

Independent decisions by multiple editors create conflicts. Coordinate through the sheet.

### Not Handing The Sheet To Downstream Stages

Proofreading and production need the decisions. Include the sheet with the handoff.

### Treating The Sheet As Archive Rather Than Working Document

The style sheet evolves with the project. Keep it current throughout.

## Self-Check

Before treating style sheet creation as complete, verify:

- Every nonstandard decision has been recorded, including deviations from the style guide and author preferences.
- The sheet is organized by category with alphabetized entries for quick reference.
- Decisions have been recorded as they arose, not reconstructed retroactively.
- Each entry notes the authority or reasoning behind the decision.
- The sheet has been actively consulted during editing to ensure consistency.
- Collaborators have received the sheet and updates are communicated.
- The governing style guide and dictionary are noted at the top.
- Project-specific categories have been included for recurring terms and decisions.
- The sheet is prepared for handoff to proofreading, production, and future use.
- The sheet makes consistency systematic rather than dependent on memory.
