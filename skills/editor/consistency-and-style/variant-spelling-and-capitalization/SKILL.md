# Variant Spelling And Capitalization

Many words have more than one legitimate spelling or capitalization. "Organize" and "organise," "adviser" and "advisor," "internet" and "Internet," "the President" and "the president," each pair is defensible, and style guides differ on which is preferred. Within a single work, however, only one form should appear. When variants coexist, the reader perceives inconsistency even when each instance is individually correct, and the work reads as careless. Resolving variant spelling and capitalization is the discipline of choosing a standard for each variable and applying it without exception, so the work presents a unified surface. Editors who tolerate coexisting variants produce work that looks unpolished regardless of its content quality.

Use this skill at the copy-editing stage to resolve spelling and capitalization variants throughout a work. It covers identifying variants, choosing the appropriate standard, and applying it consistently, with attention to the genuinely ambiguous cases. The editor's job is to eliminate variation that reads as inconsistency.

## Core Rules

### Identify All Spelling And Capitalization Variables

The first task is recognizing where variants exist. Some are obvious, British versus American spellings, but others are subtle and easy to miss: words with multiple accepted spellings, terms whose capitalization is genuinely optional, compounds that can be open or hyphenated, and names or titles with variable treatment. Scan for these variables rather than assuming the draft is already consistent.

Common variable areas include: regional spellings like -ize/-ise, -or/-our, -er/-re; words with multiple accepted forms like "adviser/advisor," "ambiance/ambience," "artifact/artefact"; technological terms whose capitalization is evolving like "internet," "web," "email"; titles and offices where capitalization depends on context; compass directions where regional versus directional use differs; and terms derived from proper nouns where the connection may or may not warrant capitalization. Compile a list of variables found in the draft so each can be resolved systematically.

### Choose The Standard By Authority And Appropriateness

Once variables are identified, choose the standard for each. The choice should follow a clear hierarchy of authority: the governing style guide first, then the dictionary, then the author's established preference, then house convention, then editorial judgment. Do not choose arbitrarily.

Determine the applicable style guide and follow its preferences. Where the style guide is silent, consult the dictionary's preferred form. Where the author has established a consistent nonstandard preference, weigh whether to preserve it for voice or align to standard; document the decision. For terms not covered by any authority, choose the form most appropriate to the work's audience and context, and record the choice. The standard should be defensible, meaning you can explain why it was chosen, not merely that it was chosen. Note the authority behind each decision in the style sheet.

### Resolve Regional Spelling Variants Deliberately

Regional spelling variants, primarily British versus American English, are the most visible spelling variable. A work should commit to one regional standard throughout. Mixing British and American spellings is a glaring inconsistency that readers notice immediately. Resolve the regional standard deliberately based on the work's audience and market.

Determine which regional standard the work should follow based on its primary audience, publisher location, or author preference. Apply that standard's spelling conventions consistently: -ize or -ise, -or or -our, -er or -re, -ense or -ence, single or double l in inflections. Check that the standard applies not just to obvious variants but to less visible ones like "judgment/judgement," "dialogue/dialog," and "catalog/catalogue." Where a work must serve multiple regional audiences, choose one primary standard and note any necessary accommodations. Mixing is never acceptable; commit to one.

### Capitalize According To Rules And Consistency

Capitalization involves both rule-governed decisions and genuine choices. Proper nouns are always capitalized; common nouns are not; but many terms sit in between, where capitalization depends on context, usage, or evolving convention. Resolve capitalization by rule where rules apply and by consistent choice where they do not.

Check proper nouns and ensure they are consistently capitalized, including names of people, places, organizations, and specific entities. Check titles and offices: titles before names are typically capitalized, titles after names or used generically are not, but verify against the style guide. Check terms derived from proper nouns: some retain capitalization, others have become generic. Check technological and cultural terms whose capitalization is evolving and follow current standard or style guide. For each capitalization variable, choose the standard and apply it consistently. Capitalization inconsistency is highly visible because the eye catches the shift between forms.

### Handle Hyphenation And Compounding As Variables

Hyphenation is both a consistency and a spelling matter, because many compounds can be open, hyphenated, or solid, and the choice is a variable to resolve. A work that treats the same compound differently in different places reads as inconsistent. Resolve compound treatment and apply it consistently.

For each recurring compound, determine its treatment: open, hyphenated, or solid. Follow the dictionary for established compounds, applying part-of-speech and position rules where they affect hyphenation. For compounds not in the dictionary, choose a treatment based on clarity and convention, and record it. Apply the chosen treatment consistently whenever the compound appears. Because hyphenation depends on usage context, document not just the compound but the rule applied to it, so variations in context are handled consistently rather than ad hoc.

### Apply The Standard Without Exception

The most common consistency failure is allowing "exceptions" that are really lapses. Once a standard is chosen for a variable, it applies in every instance, including those where the alternative form feels natural or where the context slightly differs. Apply the standard without exception, or the variation will read as error.

After choosing standards, do a dedicated pass to verify each variable is applied consistently throughout. Search for variant forms to catch any instances that were missed during general editing. Check that the standard holds in all contexts, including quotations, headings, captions, and front and back matter, unless a specific exception is documented. Consistency means no exceptions except deliberate, documented ones. A single lapse can undermine the perception of care that consistency is meant to create.

### Document Decisions For Verification And Handoff

Resolved variants and their standards belong in the style sheet so they can be verified, defended, and maintained by downstream editors. Documentation turns one-time decisions into a reusable standard. Record each resolved variable with its chosen form and authority.

For each variant resolved, record the term, the chosen form, and the authority or reasoning. Organize the entries alphabetically for quick reference. This documentation allows a proofreader to verify consistency, allows the choice to be defended if questioned, and allows future editions or related works to maintain the same standard. Undocumented decisions are lost and must be remade, often inconsistently. The style sheet is the persistence mechanism for resolved variants.

## Common Traps

### Tolerating Coexisting Variants As Individually Correct

Each instance may be defensible, but coexistence reads as inconsistency. Choose one standard.

### Mixing Regional Spellings

British and American forms must not coexist. Commit to one regional standard.

### Choosing Standards Arbitrarily

Each choice should follow authority: style guide, dictionary, author preference, or documented judgment.

### Allowing Exceptions That Are Really Lapses

Once a standard is chosen, it applies everywhere. Document only deliberate exceptions.

### Missing Capitalization Variables In Edge Cases

Titles, directions, and evolving terms have subtle rules. Check and apply consistently.

### Forgetting Hyphenation As A Variable

Compounds treated differently in different places read as inconsistent. Resolve and apply.

### Failing To Document Resolved Variants

Undocumented decisions are lost and remade inconsistently. Record in the style sheet.

## Self-Check

Before treating variant spelling and capitalization resolution as complete, verify:

- All spelling and capitalization variables in the work have been identified.
- A standard has been chosen for each variable by a clear hierarchy of authority.
- Regional spelling variants have been resolved to one consistent standard throughout.
- Capitalization follows rules where they apply and consistent choice where they do not.
- Hyphenation and compound treatment have been resolved and applied consistently.
- The chosen standards are applied without exception except deliberate, documented ones.
- A dedicated pass has verified each variable across the entire work, including front and back matter.
- Variant forms have been searched to catch missed instances.
- Each resolved variant is documented in the style sheet with its form and authority.
- The work presents a unified spelling and capitalization surface with no coexisting variants.
