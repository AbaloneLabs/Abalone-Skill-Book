# Logical Fallacy Detection

An argument can feel persuasive and still be logically broken. Logical fallacies are patterns of reasoning that fail to establish their conclusions even when their premises are accepted, and they appear constantly in argumentative writing, often unintentionally. A draft that relies on fallacious reasoning persuades only readers who do not notice the flaw, and it collapses before readers who do. Fallacy detection is the editorial discipline of identifying these patterns so the author can repair the reasoning rather than merely strengthen the rhetoric. Editors who cannot detect fallacies pass along arguments that are rhetorically effective and logically indefensible.

Use this skill when editing argumentative nonfiction, academic work, opinion, polemic, or any draft that advances reasoning toward conclusions. It covers recognizing common formal and informal fallacies, distinguishing genuine flaws from defensible reasoning, and recommending repairs that address the logic rather than masking it. The editor's job is to ensure the argument's reasoning can withstand logical scrutiny.

## Core Rules

### Read For The Inference, Not Just The Premises

A fallacy lives in the step from premises to conclusion, not necessarily in the premises themselves. Premises can be true and the conclusion still unsupported if the inference is invalid. When reading an argument, focus on whether the conclusion actually follows from the premises, not just on whether the premises are plausible.

For each significant conclusion, reconstruct the argument: what premises are offered, what inference connects them to the conclusion, and does that inference hold? A draft may present accurate facts and reach a conclusion they do not warrant. The editorial task is to find where the reasoning breaks, not just where the facts are wrong. Premise-checking is fact-checking; inference-checking is logic-checking, and both matter.

### Recognize The Common Informal Fallacies

Certain fallacy patterns recur constantly in argumentative writing, and recognizing them by sight is a core editorial skill. Learn the common informal fallacies and watch for them.

Ad hominem attacks the person rather than the argument. Strawman misrepresents an opposing position to refute the distortion. False dilemma presents only two options when more exist. Hasty generalization draws a sweeping conclusion from insufficient examples. Post hoc assumes causation from sequence. Slippery slope claims one step inevitably leads to extreme outcomes without showing the chain. Appeal to authority cites authority in place of evidence. Appeal to emotion substitutes feeling for reasoning. Equivocation shifts the meaning of a key term mid-argument. Tu quoque deflects criticism by accusing the critic of the same. Bandwagon claims truth from popularity. These are starting points; name the specific flaw when you find it, not just the label.

### Detect Causal Reasoning Errors

Causal claims are especially prone to fallacy because causation is hard to establish and easy to assert. A draft that claims one thing caused another must show the causal connection, not just coincidence, correlation, or sequence. Watch for causal reasoning that the evidence does not support.

Post hoc, assuming that because B followed A, A caused B, is the most common causal fallacy. Correlation treated as causation is its cousin. Single-cause fallacy attributes a complex outcome to one factor while ignoring others. Reverse causation gets the direction wrong, claiming A causes B when B causes A. Common-cause error attributes a relationship between A and B to one causing the other when both result from C. For each causal claim, ask what would establish causation, whether the evidence provides it, and whether alternative explanations have been ruled out. Flag unsupported causal claims and recommend either establishing the mechanism or softening to what the evidence shows.

### Watch For Generalization And Sample Errors

Arguments often move from specific cases to general conclusions, and this move is valid only when the cases are representative and sufficient. A draft that generalizes from unrepresentative or too few examples commits a generalization fallacy. Check the basis for every generalization.

Hasty generalization draws a broad conclusion from a small sample. Biased sample draws a conclusion from a sample that does not represent the population, such as relying on self-selected respondents. Anecdotal evidence generalizes from vivid individual cases. For each generalization, ask how many cases support it, whether they are representative, and whether the conclusion's breadth matches the evidence's scope. Recommend either narrowing the generalization to what the sample supports or gathering more representative evidence.

### Identify Fallacies Of Relevance And Distraction

Some fallacies work by introducing material that is emotionally or rhetorically compelling but logically irrelevant, distracting from whether the conclusion actually follows. These are often the most persuasive-sounding and thus the most dangerous.

Red herring introduces an irrelevant topic to divert attention. Whataboutism deflects criticism by pointing to others' faults. Appeal to consequences argues that a claim must be false because believing it would have bad consequences. Genetic fallacy judges a claim by its origin rather than its merit. For each, ask whether the material offered actually bears on the conclusion. If it does not, flag it as a distraction and recommend removing it or connecting it to the argument's actual logic.

### Distinguish Fallacy From Defensible Reasoning

Not every pattern that resembles a fallacy is one. Informal fallacies often have legitimate counterparts: appeal to legitimate authority is valid when the authority is qualified and the field is one where expertise matters; arguing from analogy is valid when the analogy is relevant; pointing out hypocrisy can be relevant to credibility in some contexts. Avoid crying fallacy where reasoning is defensible.

Before flagging, ask whether the reasoning could be defended on its own terms. Is the authority genuinely qualified and the question within their expertise? Is the analogy genuinely parallel in relevant respects? Is the generalization supported by adequate representative evidence? Reserve fallacy flags for reasoning that genuinely fails, and for borderline cases, recommend strengthening the reasoning rather than dismissing it. Over-flagging defensible reasoning erodes the author's trust and produces weaker arguments, not stronger ones.

### Recommend Repair, Not Just Detection

Detecting a fallacy is only useful if the editor recommends how to repair the reasoning. A fallacy flag without a repair leaves the author to fix the logic themselves, which is the work they could not do when writing. For each fallacy, suggest what would make the reasoning valid.

Repairs vary: supply the missing premise that would make the inference valid, narrow the conclusion to what the premises support, replace a faulty analogy with a relevant one, gather the evidence a generalization requires, or remove the irrelevant material a distraction fallacy introduces. Sometimes the repair is acknowledging that the conclusion does not follow and treating it as a hypothesis or a position to argue further rather than an established result. Concrete repairs turn fallacy detection into improved argument.

## Common Traps

### Checking Premises But Missing Invalid Inference

True premises do not guarantee a valid conclusion. Check the inference step, not just the facts.

### Crying Fallacy On Defensible Reasoning

Some patterns that resemble fallacies are legitimate. Verify the reasoning actually fails before flagging.

### Missing Causal Claims Disguised As Description

Causal assertions often hide in descriptive language. Surface and test causal implications.

### Accepting Correlation As Causation

Correlation alone does not establish causation. Require mechanism or additional evidence.

### Generalizing From Vivid But Unrepresentative Cases

Anecdotes and small samples do not warrant broad conclusions. Check sample size and representativeness.

### Flagging Without Recommending Repair

Detection without repair leaves the argument broken. Suggest what would make the reasoning valid.

### Letting Rhetorical Force Mask Logical Gaps

Persuasive prose can hide invalid inference. Judge the logic, not the eloquence.

## Self-Check

Before treating fallacy detection as complete, verify:

- You have read for the inference from premises to conclusion, not just the premises themselves.
- Common informal fallacies have been checked for, including ad hominem, strawman, false dilemma, and slippery slope.
- Causal claims have been tested for whether the evidence establishes causation rather than correlation or sequence.
- Generalizations have been checked for sample size and representativeness.
- Fallacies of relevance and distraction have been identified where material does not bear on the conclusion.
- Defensible reasoning has been distinguished from genuine fallacy, avoiding over-flagging.
- Each fallacy flag includes a recommended repair that would make the reasoning valid.
- Conclusions have been checked for whether they follow from their premises or need narrowing.
- Rhetorically forceful passages have been examined for underlying logical gaps.
- The argument's reasoning can withstand logical scrutiny, not just stylistic appreciation.
