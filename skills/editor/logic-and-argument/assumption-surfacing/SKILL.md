# Assumption Surfacing

Every argument rests on assumptions it does not state. Some assumptions are reasonable and shared; others are contestable, hidden, or load-bearing in ways the author has not examined. Assumption surfacing is the editorial discipline of identifying the unstated premises an argument depends on, judging whether they are defensible, and ensuring the author acknowledges the ones that, if wrong, would undermine the conclusion. Arguments that do not surface their assumptions persuade only readers who happen to share them, and they fail before readers who notice the hidden dependency. Editors who do not surface assumptions pass along reasoning that stands on foundations no one has inspected.

Use this skill when editing argumentative nonfiction, academic work, policy analysis, opinion, or any draft that builds reasoning toward conclusions. It covers reconstructing the unstated premises an argument requires, judging their defensibility, and recommending where acknowledgment or defense is needed. The editor's job is to ensure the argument's hidden dependencies are visible and examined.

## Core Rules

### Reconstruct The Argument To Find Its Hidden Premises

An argument's stated premises rarely suffice to establish its conclusion. Between the stated reasons and the conclusion sit unstated assumptions that the reasoning requires but does not voice. To find them, reconstruct the argument and ask what additional premise would be needed to make the inference valid.

For each significant conclusion, list the stated premises and then ask: what must also be true for these premises to support this conclusion? If the argument moves from a statistic about one population to a conclusion about another, the hidden assumption is that the populations are comparable. If it moves from a historical case to a present prediction, the hidden assumption is that the situations are relevantly similar. If it moves from a study's finding to a policy recommendation, the hidden assumption is that the finding translates into effective policy. Surface these dependencies explicitly so they can be examined.

### Distinguish Reasonable From Contestable Assumptions

Not all assumptions need to be defended. Some are reasonable because they are widely shared, well-established, or trivially true. Others are contestable and require acknowledgment or defense. The editorial task is to sort assumptions by how much weight they carry and how contestable they are.

An assumption is reasonable when it rests on established knowledge, common experience, or definitions. It is contestable when reasonable people disagree, when it depends on values or interpretations, when evidence cuts both ways, or when it is central to the argument's force. Focus attention on contestable and load-bearing assumptions, the ones that, if false, would seriously weaken or collapse the conclusion. Trivial assumptions can remain unstated; load-bearing contestable ones should not.

### Test Load-Bearing Assumptions Against Counterexamples

For each load-bearing assumption, ask what would happen if it were false. If the conclusion collapses or seriously weakens when the assumption fails, the assumption is load-bearing and deserves explicit treatment. This counterfactual test reveals which hidden premises truly matter.

Construct the case where the assumption does not hold. If the comparable populations are not actually comparable, does the conclusion still follow? If the historical situation differs in relevant ways, does the prediction still hold? If the finding does not translate into policy, does the recommendation stand? When the conclusion depends on the assumption holding, flag it for the author and recommend either stating and defending it, hedging the conclusion to acknowledge the dependency, or finding evidence that supports the assumption directly.

### Check Whether Assumptions Are Acknowledged In The Draft

A defensible argument acknowledges its contestable assumptions rather than hiding them. Check whether the draft names the assumptions it depends on, or whether they remain implicit. Unacknowledged contestable assumptions are a weakness informed readers will notice.

Read for explicit acknowledgment of the premises the argument requires. Does the draft note that it is assuming comparability, that it is treating a finding as generalizable, that it is relying on a value judgment? Or does it proceed as if the assumptions are obviously true? Flag contestable load-bearing assumptions that go unacknowledged and recommend stating them. Acknowledgment signals intellectual honesty and lets readers evaluate the assumption rather than being misled that none exists.

### Verify Assumptions Are Defended Where They Need Defense

Acknowledging an assumption is not enough if it is contestable and central; it should also be defended. Check whether the draft offers support for its load-bearing assumptions or merely asserts them. An undefended contestable assumption is a gap in the argument.

For each acknowledged contestable assumption, ask whether the draft provides reason to accept it. Does it cite evidence that the populations are comparable, argue that the historical situations are parallel, establish that the finding generalizes? If the assumption is merely stated as if obvious, flag it for defense. Where defense is not possible because the assumption is genuinely uncertain, recommend hedging the conclusion to reflect that uncertainty rather than presenting it as established.

### Watch For Ideological And Value Assumptions

Some assumptions are not empirical but ideological or value-based: assumptions about what matters, what is good, what counts as fair, what risks are acceptable. These are often the most hidden because they feel obvious to the author and invisible to sympathetic readers. Surface them.

Identify where the argument depends on a value judgment presented as fact: that economic growth is the primary good, that individual choice trumps collective welfare, that security justifies restriction, that tradition carries authority. These assumptions may be defensible, but they are not universal, and readers who do not share them will reject conclusions that depend on them unacknowledged. Flag value assumptions and recommend either acknowledging them as the framework the argument operates within or arguing for them explicitly.

### Check For Assumptions Shared With The Audience But Not Universal

Arguments often rely on assumptions the intended audience shares but a broader readership does not. Within a discipline, community, or political tendency, certain premises go without saying. When the work reaches beyond that audience, those premises become gaps. Judge assumptions against the work's actual readership.

If the draft assumes specialist knowledge, shared values, or common frameworks that its broader audience may not hold, flag the mismatch. An argument persuasive to insiders may be opaque or unpersuasive to general readers because the shared assumptions are absent. Recommend either stating the assumptions for the broader audience or clarifying that the argument addresses readers who share its framework.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Stated Premises As The Whole Argument

The inference usually requires unstated premises. Reconstruct to find them.

### Treating All Assumptions As Equal

Trivial assumptions can stay implicit; load-bearing contestable ones must be surfaced. Focus attention where it matters.

### Missing Value And Ideological Assumptions

Non-empirical assumptions are the most hidden and often the most consequential. Look for them.

### Flagging Reasonable Shared Assumptions

Not every unstated premise needs defense. Reserve flags for contestable load-bearing ones.

### Acknowledging Without Defending

Naming an assumption is not the same as supporting it. Contestable central assumptions need defense.

### Assuming The Audience Shares The Author's Premises

What goes without saying for insiders may be a gap for broader readers. Judge against actual readership.

### Letting Conclusions Rest On Unexamined Comparability

Arguments from analogy, precedent, or generalization depend on comparability assumptions. Test them.

## Self-Check

Before treating assumption surfacing as complete, verify:

- You have reconstructed the argument to identify the unstated premises the inference requires.
- Assumptions have been sorted by whether they are reasonable and shared or contestable and load-bearing.
- Load-bearing assumptions have been tested against the counterfactual of their being false.
- The draft's acknowledgment of contestable assumptions has been checked, with unacknowledged ones flagged.
- Acknowledged contestable assumptions have been checked for whether they are defended, not just stated.
- Ideological and value assumptions presented as fact have been identified.
- Assumptions shared with the intended audience but not universal have been checked against broader readership.
- Conclusions resting on unexamined comparability, generalizability, or precedent have been flagged.
- Recommendations distinguish assumptions that need acknowledgment, defense, or hedging.
- The argument's hidden dependencies are visible and examined, not buried beneath confident prose.
