# Evidence Evaluation And Strength

A claim is only as good as what backs it. Editors who focus on prose can miss the moment when a confident sentence rests on no support, on a single anecdote, on a source that does not say what the writer thinks it says, or on evidence that proves a narrower point than the claim asserts. Evidence evaluation is the substantive work of asking, for each load-bearing claim, whether the support actually carries the weight, and what kind of support the claim requires before it can stand in print.

Use this skill when reviewing nonfiction, argument, reportage, analysis, opinion, or any draft where truth claims matter. It covers classifying claim strength, matching evidence type to claim type, spotting the gap between what the evidence shows and what the writer concludes, judging source quality and recency, recognizing when an example is illustrative versus probative, and deciding what additional reporting or citation the piece needs. The editor's task is to make sure the draft does not overclaim what its evidence can carry.

## Core Rules

### Classify Each Load-Bearing Claim By Strength

Not every sentence needs equal scrutiny. Identify the claims the argument depends on, the ones that, if false, would collapse the piece. Then classify each by the strength it requires.

Useful categories include universal claims, statistical or probabilistic claims, causal claims, definitional claims, interpretive claims, and illustrative or anecdotal claims. A universal claim needs far stronger support than a probabilistic one. A causal claim needs more than correlation. An interpretive claim needs reasoned argument and acknowledgment of alternatives. Sort the load-bearing claims first so effort goes where failure would be most damaging.

### Match Evidence Type To Claim Type

Evidence types are not interchangeable. A claim about prevalence needs data, not an anecdote. A claim about causation needs a method that can support causation. A claim about meaning needs interpretation grounded in the text. A claim about what someone intended needs their account or strong contextual evidence.

Check the fit. Common mismatches include using a single vivid example to imply a widespread pattern, using correlation to assert causation, using an authority's opinion to settle a factual question, using old data to describe a current state, and using a study on one population to draw conclusions about another. When the evidence type cannot support the claim type, the claim must be narrowed, re-hedged, or backed by different evidence.

### Distinguish Illustrative From Probative Evidence

An example can show what something looks like, or it can prove that something happens. These are different jobs, and conflating them is a frequent substantive error.

An illustrative example gives the reader a concrete instance of a pattern the writer has established by other means. A probative example is itself the evidence that the pattern exists. A draft that uses one striking story to imply a broad trend is treating an illustration as proof. Either gather enough instances to support the generalization, or reframe the example as illustration and back the general claim with data or a cited source. Make clear to the reader which role each example plays.

### Check The Distance Between Source And Conclusion

Writers summarize, paraphrase, and interpret sources, and each step can drift. The substantive editor must test whether the source actually says what the draft claims it says.

For important citations, look at the original and compare. Common drifts include citing a secondary summary of a primary study, treating a hypothesis as a finding, treating a finding about a narrow sample as a general conclusion, dropping a qualifier the original included, and converting a correlation into a causal statement. Where you cannot check the original, flag the claim for verification rather than assuming the writer read it correctly. The distance between source and conclusion is where overclaiming lives.

### Weigh Source Quality, Independence, And Recency

Not all sources carry equal weight. A claim is only as trustworthy as the source, and the editor must judge whether the source is appropriate to the stakes.

Consider whether the source is primary or secondary, independent or interested, peer-reviewed or self-published, expert in the relevant field or merely credentialed, current or outdated for a fast-moving topic, and corroborated or standing alone. An interested source, such as a company reporting on its own product, may still be useful but must be labeled. A single source for a contested claim is fragile. Recency matters most for data, regulation, technology, and any field where the state of knowledge changes. Match source weight to claim stakes.

### Recognize Cherry-Picking And Selective Evidence

A draft can be entirely accurate in each citation and still misleading as a whole if it cites only the evidence that supports the claim and omits the evidence that complicates it.

Look for the missing counter-evidence. If studies exist on both sides, does the draft acknowledge the range? If examples cut against the thesis, are they addressed or ignored? If the writer found the one favorable statistic in a report whose overall finding is unfavorable, that is cherry-picking. The fix is not always to abandon the claim; often it is to acknowledge the range of evidence and explain why the claim still holds, or to narrow the claim to what the full evidence supports.

### Identify The Claim The Evidence Actually Supports

Sometimes the right move is not to find new evidence but to revise the claim to match the evidence the writer already has. A draft may overclaim because the writer reached for a bigger conclusion than the material warrants.

For each load-bearing claim, ask what the strongest defensible version is, given the evidence in hand. If the evidence supports "in these three cities, under these conditions, this intervention correlated with a measurable improvement," the claim should say that, not "this intervention works." Narrowing a claim to what the evidence supports is often the most honest and most credible substantive edit.

### Decide What Evidence Is Still Missing

Substantive review should produce a list of what the piece needs before it can responsibly make its claims. This is editorial direction, not just criticism.

Flag specific gaps: a causal claim that needs a methodological source, a prevalence claim that needs a dataset, a historical claim that needs a primary document, a contested claim that needs a counter-source addressed, a technical claim that needs expert verification. Distinguish gaps that block publication from gaps that would strengthen the piece. Give the writer a clear reporting or research list rather than a vague instruction to "support your claims better."

### Separate Factual Claims From Interpretive And Opinion Claims and preserve Honest Uncertainty

Factual claims are checkable; interpretive and opinion claims are judged by reasoning and fairness. The two require different editorial treatment and should not be blurred.

Factual claims should be verifiable and cited where stakes are high. Interpretive claims should show the reasoning that links evidence to conclusion and should acknowledge reasonable alternative readings. Opinion claims should be clearly marked as the writer's judgment and should not masquerade as fact. When a draft presents opinion as fact or fact as opinion, the substantive editor must realign the framing so the reader knows what kind of claim is being made and how to weigh it.

Strong writing is not the same as certain writing. A draft that hedges appropriately where evidence is incomplete is more trustworthy than one that asserts beyond its support.

Allow hedging where hedging is honest: "the evidence suggests," "in the studies available," "among this population," "at the time of writing." Remove hedging only where the evidence actually warrants certainty. The substantive editor's job is to calibrate the confidence of each claim to the strength of its support, not to inflate certainty for impact or to flatten all claims into the same tentative register.

## Common Traps

### The Anecdote As Proof

One vivid story feels persuasive but cannot establish a pattern. Use anecdotes to illustrate, and back general claims with data or multiple instances.

### Correlation Sold As Causation

A relationship between two variables does not show that one causes the other. Causal claims need a method or source that supports causation.

### Drift Between Source And Citation

Each step of summarizing can move the meaning. Check important citations against the original, especially when the conclusion is stronger than the source.

### Cherry-Picked Evidence

Accurate individual citations can still mislead if contrary evidence is omitted. Look for what the draft leaves out.

### Overclaiming Beyond The Evidence

The strongest defensible claim is often narrower than the one the writer reached for. Revise the claim to fit the evidence when the evidence cannot be expanded.

### Wrong Evidence Type For The Claim

Authority cannot settle fact, anecdote cannot establish prevalence, and old data cannot describe the present. Match evidence type to claim type.

### Single-Source Contested Claims

A contested claim resting on one source is fragile. Corroborate or acknowledge the contest.

### Inflating Certainty For Impact and treating Secondary Summary As Primary

Confident prose that outruns its evidence reads as authoritative until a reader checks. Calibrate confidence to support.

A press summary of a study is not the study. For high-stakes claims, trace to the primary source.

## Self-Check

Before treating evidence evaluation as complete, verify:

- Load-bearing claims have been identified and classified by the strength they require.
- Each load-bearing claim is backed by an evidence type that can support that kind of claim.
- Examples are clearly used as illustration or as proof, not silently swapped between the two roles.
- Important citations have been checked against their sources for drift, dropped qualifiers, and overclaiming.
- Source quality, independence, expertise, recency, and corroboration have been weighed against claim stakes.
- The draft has been checked for cherry-picking, and missing counter-evidence has been identified.
- Claims have been narrowed where the evidence supports less than the draft asserts.
- A specific list of missing evidence has been produced, distinguishing blocking gaps from strengthening gaps.
- Factual, interpretive, and opinion claims are clearly distinguished and treated with the appropriate standard.
- Hedging reflects honest uncertainty rather than inflating certainty for impact or flattening all claims into timidity; the draft's strongest claims are ones its evidence can actually carry to publication
