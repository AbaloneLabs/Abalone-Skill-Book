# Attribution Granularity And Fairness

Attribution is how a draft accounts for where its information, quotes, ideas, and characterizations come from, and how fairly it treats the people and institutions it describes. These are not formatting details. Granularity determines whether a reader can judge the reliability of a claim, and fairness determines whether the draft describes its subjects honestly or distorts them through selective quotation, missing context, or loaded framing. Editors who treat attribution as a copyediting concern miss the substantive judgments underneath.

Use this skill when reviewing reportage, profiles, criticism, investigation, opinion, or any draft that names or describes real people, organizations, data, or ideas. It covers choosing how precisely to attribute, handling anonymous and composite sources, quoting fairly and in context, characterizing people and institutions without distortion, giving subjects a fair chance to respond, and avoiding misattribution of ideas and data. The editor's task is to make the draft both traceable and fair.

## Core Rules

### Match Attribution Granularity To Claim Stakes

Attribution is not uniform. A passing background fact needs less precision than a damaging allegation, a contested scientific claim, or a direct quote. Calibrate granularity to what the reader needs to weigh the claim.

For low-stakes context, a general attribution may suffice. For claims that affect reputation, safety, money, or public understanding, attribute specifically: name the source, note their position and interest, indicate whether they are primary or secondary, and show whether the claim is corroborated. When a draft attributes a serious allegation vaguely to "sources" or "critics," ask whether the reader can actually judge the claim's weight. If not, the attribution is too coarse for the stakes.

### Make The Source's Position And Interest Visible

A source is not just a name; it is a position and an interest. The reader needs to know not only who said something but why their perspective matters and what they stand to gain or lose.

When attributing, include the relevant context: official role, institutional affiliation, relationship to the subject, financial interest, ideological stance, or history of conflict. "A competitor said the product is unsafe" reads differently from "an independent safety researcher said the product is unsafe." Omitting interest does not make reporting neutral; it hides the lens. Where space allows, make the position visible; where it does not, choose a level of attribution that does not mislead by omission.

### Quote Accurately And In Context

A quotation is a contract with the reader that the words were said and that the surrounding context does not reverse their meaning. Both halves matter.

Check that quotes are verbatim where presented as verbatim, that ellipses and brackets do not alter meaning, that the surrounding context has not been stripped away to make a quote appear to say something it did not, and that the speaker's intent and tone are preserved. A quote that is technically word-for-word but contextually misleading is still unfair. When a writer paraphrases a quote, ensure the paraphrase does not sharpen or soften the original into a different claim.

### Avoid Selective Quotation And Character Distortion

Choosing the most damaging or most flattering sentence from a longer statement can misrepresent the speaker even when each word is accurate. The substantive editor must read the full source and compare.

Watch for quotes that omit qualifying clauses, that pull a provocative line from a measured statement, that combine sentences spoken apart to imply a continuous thought, or that present irony or hypothetical as earnest. Where selective quotation has occurred, restore context, rebalance with other quotes from the same source, or revise the characterization. The test is whether a fair-minded reader of the full original would recognize the draft's portrayal.

### Handle Anonymous Sources With Discipline

Anonymity is sometimes necessary and always risky. It removes the reader's ability to judge the source and shifts that burden to the editor. Use it only when the standard for anonymity is met.

Before accepting an anonymous source, confirm that the source faces genuine risk of harm, that the information cannot be obtained on the record, that the source has direct knowledge, and that the claim is corroborated where stakes are high. Describe the source as specifically as safety allows, such as "a senior official directly involved in the decision," rather than the meaningless "sources said." Anonymous attack quotes against identifiable people carry special unfairness and should be held to a higher corroboration standard.

### Reject Composite Sources And Blended Quotes

Combining multiple people into a composite "source," or stitching quotes from different speakers into one apparent quote, misleads the reader about the evidence. These practices are not shortcuts; they are fabrication of provenance.

If two sources said similar things, attribute each separately or describe the group precisely. If a quote must be assembled from a conversation, use brackets and attribution to show the assembly. Never present a composite as a real individual, and never present blended quotes as a single utterance. When you find these practices in a draft, treat them as integrity failures requiring correction, not as style choices.

### Give Subjects Fair Notice And A Chance To Respond

People named in a damaging or contested way should have a fair opportunity to respond before publication. This is both an ethical obligation and a substantive accuracy check.

Confirm that the writer contacted the subjects, gave them specific notice of the claims or characterizations, allowed reasonable time to respond, and represented their responses accurately in the piece. Where a subject declined or did not respond, say so. Where a response changes the picture, integrate it. Fairness is not neutrality; it is giving the subject's side a genuine place in the record so the reader can weigh it.

### Attribute Ideas, Not Just Quotes

Plagiarism and misattribution are not only about copied words. Ideas, frameworks, analyses, and distinctive arguments also belong to their originators and must be credited.

When a draft presents a concept, interpretation, or analytical frame, check whether it originated with the writer or with someone whose work should be cited. This is especially important in essays, criticism, and scholarship, where the intellectual contribution is the work. Credit the originator even when the wording is the writer's own. When in doubt, attribute; the cost of unnecessary credit is small, and the cost of stolen credit is large.

### Separate Reporting From The Writer's Characterization and weigh Fairness To Institutions And Groups, Not Only Individuals

A draft can blur the line between what sources said and what the writer concludes. Readers need to know which is which to judge the argument.

Mark where the draft reports a source's view and where it asserts the writer's own. Use attribution phrases, framing, and structure so that characterizations are visibly the writer's analysis and factual claims are visibly sourced. When a writer's opinion is presented as if it were reported fact, the reader is misled about the basis of the claim. Clarity here protects both the reader and the subjects.

Fairness obligations extend to organizations, professions, communities, and demographic groups described in the draft. Stereotyping and loaded collective characterization distort as much as unfair treatment of an individual.

Check generalizations about groups for evidentiary support, watch for loaded collective nouns that carry judgment, and ensure that criticism aimed at an institution is grounded in specific conduct rather than diffuse suspicion. A draft can unfairly tar a group by association with a few members, or by quoting only its critics. Balance and precision matter at the collective level too.

## Common Traps

### Coarse Attribution For High-Stakes Claims

"Sources said" is too thin for a damaging allegation. Match granularity to stakes and describe the source as specifically as safety allows.

### Hidden Source Interest

Omitting a source's role or stake does not create neutrality; it conceals the lens. Make position and interest visible.

### Technically Accurate But Contextually Misleading Quotes

Word-for-word accuracy is not enough if context is stripped. Read the full source and compare.

### Selective Quotation To Sharpen A Portrayal

Pulling the most damaging line from a measured statement distorts the speaker. Restore balance or revise the characterization.

### Anonymous Attack Quotes Without Corroboration

Anonymity plus attack plus single source is a recipe for unfairness. Hold these to a higher corroboration standard.

### Composite Sources And Blended Quotes

Combining people or stitching quotes misrepresents provenance. Treat as an integrity failure.

### Skipped Or Token Response Requests and uncredited Ideas And Frameworks

Naming someone damagingly without genuine notice and response time is unfair and weakens accuracy. Confirm the writer gave subjects a real chance.

Words are not the only thing that belongs to an originator. Credit ideas, interpretations, and analytical frames.

### Blurred Reporting And Opinion and unfair Collective Characterization

When reported fact and writer assertion look identical, the reader cannot judge the basis. Keep the line visible.

Generalizations about groups need the same evidentiary care as claims about individuals.

## Self-Check

Before treating attribution and fairness review as complete, verify:

- Attribution granularity has been calibrated to the stakes of each claim, with serious or damaging claims attributed specifically.
- Source position, role, affiliation, and interest are visible enough for the reader to judge the claim.
- Quotes are verbatim where presented as such, and context has not been stripped to alter meaning.
- Selective quotation has been checked against the full source and corrected where it distorts.
- Anonymous sources meet the standard for genuine risk, direct knowledge, and corroboration, and are described as specifically as safety allows.
- No composite sources or blended quotes are presented as real individuals or single utterances.
- Named subjects received specific notice and a genuine chance to respond, and responses are represented accurately.
- Ideas, frameworks, and distinctive arguments are credited to their originators, not only direct quotes.
- The line between reported source material and the writer's own characterization is visible to the reader.
- Generalizations about institutions and groups are supported and free of loaded, unsupported collective judgment; a fair-minded reader of the full underlying sources would recognize the draft's portrayal as honest
