# Factual Logic And Attribution Review

Substantive editing protects the reader from unsupported meaning. It checks whether claims are true enough, evidenced enough, attributed enough, and logically connected enough for the draft's purpose. This applies beyond journalism: essays, reports, proposals, white papers, speeches, marketing copy, academic work, internal memos, and guides can all mislead through confident but weak claims.

Use this skill when reviewing a draft for accuracy, evidence, attribution, logic, data, quotations, citations, or risk. The editor should not assume that polished prose is reliable. Every factual, causal, comparative, legal, medical, financial, historical, or technical claim should be treated according to its stakes.

## Core Rules

### Classify Claims By Verification Need

Not every sentence needs the same level of checking. Classify claims so effort goes where risk is highest.

Claim types include:

- simple observable facts;
- names, dates, titles, and locations;
- statistics and quantities;
- historical claims;
- causal claims;
- comparative claims;
- superlatives and firsts;
- predictions;
- legal, medical, financial, or safety claims;
- quotes and paraphrases;
- interpretations;
- value judgments;
- recommendations.

High-stakes claims require stronger evidence and clearer attribution. A casual metaphor may need style review. A medical recommendation needs authoritative verification or removal. A claim that a person committed wrongdoing needs careful evidence, response opportunity, and wording.

### Match Claim Strength To Evidence Strength

The most common substantive error is overclaiming. Evidence may suggest, illustrate, indicate, support, or prove. Those are not the same.

Ask:

- Does the evidence directly support the claim?
- Is the evidence current enough?
- Is it primary, secondary, anecdotal, expert interpretation, or marketing material?
- Is the sample representative?
- Are limitations disclosed?
- Does the claim imply causation when evidence shows only correlation?
- Does a single example stand in for a trend?
- Does an expert quote support the whole claim or only a narrow part?

Revise claim language to match the evidence. Replace "shows" with "suggests" when needed. Replace "caused" with "was associated with" when causality is not established. Replace "always" with a bounded claim.

### Check Attribution Granularity

Attribution should tell the reader how the writer knows. Vague attribution weakens trust and can hide risk.

Improve attribution by specifying:

- who said it;
- in what capacity;
- where the information appears;
- whether it comes from a report, interview, dataset, court filing, press release, study, internal document, or observation;
- whether the source has conflicts or incentives;
- whether the source is primary or secondary;
- whether the source directly supports the wording.

Do not allow "experts say," "studies show," "research proves," "critics argue," or "some believe" unless the draft identifies enough basis for the reader to judge.

### Verify Quotes And Paraphrases

Quotes carry special responsibility. Check that quoted words are exact, contextual, and not misleading. Paraphrases must preserve meaning without pretending to be quotation.

Review:

- exact wording;
- speaker identity;
- source date;
- context of the quote;
- whether ellipses change meaning;
- whether brackets are necessary and accurate;
- whether translation choices are faithful;
- whether a paraphrase overstates what the source said;
- whether the quote is being used as evidence for a claim it does not support.

If the source material is unavailable, flag the quote for verification rather than assuming accuracy.

### Test Logic Between Paragraphs

A draft can have true statements arranged into a false implication. Check the reasoning chain.

Look for:

- causal leaps;
- false dichotomies;
- straw-man summaries;
- circular reasoning;
- missing premises;
- equivocation in key terms;
- anecdote-to-trend jumps;
- chronology mistaken for cause;
- comparisons with unequal baselines;
- recommendations that do not follow from the evidence.

When logic is weak, edit the connection, not only the sentence. Add a missing premise if evidence exists. Narrow the claim if it does not.

### Review Data Claims Carefully

Numbers are persuasive and easy to misuse. Check both the number and the interpretation.

Ask:

- What is the source?
- What period does it cover?
- What is the denominator?
- Is it nominal or adjusted?
- Is it preliminary, revised, sampled, modeled, or complete?
- Are categories defined consistently?
- Are percentages and percentage points distinguished?
- Does a chart start at an appropriate baseline?
- Are outliers or missing data disclosed?
- Is the comparison fair?

A statistic without context can mislead more efficiently than a vague sentence.

### Check Names, Titles, Dates, And Terminology

Small errors signal carelessness. Verify proper nouns, titles, organization names, product names, laws, place names, dates, version numbers, units, currency, spelling, pronouns, and acronyms.

Be especially careful with:

- people whose names are often misspelled;
- changed titles or organizations;
- jurisdictions;
- academic or professional credentials;
- legal terms;
- medical terms;
- product and model names;
- historical dates;
- translated names.

Do not rely on memory for details that can be checked.

### Identify Claims That Need Subject Response Or Expert Review

Some claims require more than textual editing. A person or institution criticized in a serious way may need a chance to respond. Technical, legal, medical, financial, scientific, or safety-sensitive claims may require expert review.

Flag:

- accusations of wrongdoing;
- claims about motive or intent;
- health or treatment advice;
- investment or legal recommendations;
- public safety instructions;
- claims based on confidential or anonymous sources;
- complex data methodology;
- specialized technical descriptions;
- statements that could affect reputation, money, liberty, or safety.

If qualified review is not available, narrow or remove the claim.

### Preserve Uncertainty

Editing should not erase uncertainty that is true. A sentence may sound stronger after removing hedges, but the result may become inaccurate.

Keep or add uncertainty markers when needed:

- alleged;
- according to;
- estimated;
- preliminary;
- not independently verified;
- appears to;
- may;
- could;
- associated with;
- based on available records;
- the evidence does not show.

Remove hedges only when they are habitual or weaken a well-supported claim.

## Common Traps

### Treating Citations As Proof Without Reading Fit

A citation proves nothing unless it supports the exact claim. Check claim-source fit. A source may be credible and still irrelevant to the sentence.

### Allowing Marketing Language To Masquerade As Fact

"Industry-leading," "revolutionary," "trusted," and "best-in-class" are claims, not facts, unless defined and supported. Treat promotional language skeptically.

### Letting A Polished Draft Create Confidence

Good style can make weak evidence feel stronger. Edit substance independently from prose quality.

### Confusing Legal Safety With Truth

A claim may be legally safe but still misleading. Conversely, a truthful claim may need careful wording and evidence. Check both accuracy and risk.

### Overcorrecting Into False Neutrality

Do not weaken well-supported claims merely to avoid taking a position. If evidence is strong, clear language serves the reader.

### Missing Risk In Captions, Charts, And Bullets

Factual errors often hide outside the main paragraphs. Review tables, captions, labels, footnotes, pull quotes, summaries, and headlines.

## Self-Check

Before treating substantive review as complete, verify:

- Central claims have been classified by verification need and risk.
- Claim strength matches evidence strength.
- Causal, comparative, superlative, predictive, legal, medical, financial, and safety claims receive heightened scrutiny.
- Attribution explains how the writer knows, not merely that someone said something.
- Vague source phrases have been replaced or flagged.
- Quotes and paraphrases preserve source meaning and context.
- Numbers, percentages, dates, names, titles, units, and terminology have been checked; data claims include enough context for fair interpretation
- Logical links between evidence and conclusion have been tested; unsupported leaps have been narrowed, sourced, or flagged
- Serious criticism, technical complexity, or high-stakes advice has been flagged for response or expert review; necessary uncertainty remains visible
- Captions, charts, headings, bullets, summaries, and display text were reviewed for factual accuracy; the editor did not let polished prose substitute for evidence
