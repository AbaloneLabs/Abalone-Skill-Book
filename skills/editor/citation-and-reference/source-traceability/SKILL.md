# Source Traceability

Citation serves a fundamental purpose beyond formatting: it makes a work's claims traceable to their sources. A reader who wants to verify a claim, examine the evidence, or explore further should be able to follow the citation to the source. When claims lack citations, quotations are unattributed, or borrowed ideas are presented without acknowledgment, the work fails in traceability. Readers cannot verify, and the boundary between the author's original contribution and borrowed material blurs, which is both a scholarly and an ethical failure. Source traceability is the discipline of ensuring every claim, quotation, and borrowed idea is attributed to a traceable source. Editors who focus on citation format but not citation completeness produce works that look properly cited but leave gaps in the evidentiary trail.

Use this skill when editing any work that makes claims, uses sources, or builds on others' ideas, including academic, journalistic, and nonfiction writing. It covers identifying what needs citation, verifying quotations and paraphrases are attributed, and ensuring citations are complete enough to trace. The goal is a work where every non-original element is traceable to its source.

## Core Rules

### Identify What Requires Citation

Not every statement needs a citation, but many do, and the line is not always obvious. Factual claims, especially specific or contested ones, require citation. Direct quotations require citation. Paraphrased ideas and arguments require citation. Statistics and data require citation. Common knowledge generally does not. Identify what requires citation by assessing each claim's nature and provenance.

Read the work assessing each substantive claim. Flag factual claims, especially specific figures, dates, or assertions, for citation verification. Flag direct quotations for attribution. Flag paraphrased ideas that derive from sources. Flag specialized knowledge that is not common knowledge. Distinguish common knowledge, which does not require citation, from claims that do. The default is to cite when in doubt, because under-citation is a more serious error than over-citation. Systematic identification ensures nothing requiring citation is missed.

### Verify Direct Quotations Are Attributed

Direct quotations must be attributed to their source, with citation that allows the reader to find the original. Unattributed quotations are both a traceability failure and a potential plagiarism issue. Verify every direct quotation has a clear attribution and citation.

For each direct quotation, verify it has a citation to the source. Check that the citation is specific enough to locate the quotation, including page numbers where appropriate. Verify that the attribution correctly identifies the speaker or author. Check that the quotation is presented as a quotation, with quotation marks or block format, so the reader knows it is verbatim. Unattributed or misattributed quotations are serious errors that undermine the work's integrity and can constitute plagiarism. Verify all quotations are properly marked and cited.

### Verify Paraphrases And Borrowed Ideas Are Attributed

Paraphrased ideas and arguments require attribution just as quotations do, even though the words are the author's. Presenting a paraphrased idea without attribution passes off another's thinking as original. Verify paraphrases and borrowed ideas are attributed to their sources.

For each passage that presents ideas, arguments, or analysis, consider whether the content derives from a source. If the idea is borrowed, even if restated, verify it is attributed. Check that paraphrases are cited to their sources. Be alert to passages that present specialized knowledge, specific arguments, or distinctive analysis without attribution, as these may be unattributed paraphrases. The distinction between original synthesis and borrowed ideas is not always clear, but when the author is building on or responding to a source, that source should be acknowledged. Unattributed paraphrase is a common form of inadvertent plagiarism.

### Check Citation Specificity And Locatability

A citation must be specific enough that the reader can locate the cited material. A citation to a 500-page book without page numbers is barely traceable. A citation to a website without a URL or access date may be untraceable. Check citation specificity and locatability.

For each citation, assess whether it is specific enough to locate the cited material. For books and long sources, check that page numbers or sections are cited where the citation supports a specific claim or quotation. For digital sources, verify URLs and access dates. For sources with multiple editions or versions, verify the specific edition is identifiable. Flag citations that are too general to trace and recommend greater specificity. A citation that cannot be followed fails its purpose, even if it is formatted correctly.

### Verify Citations Match The Claims They Support

A citation should support the specific claim it is attached to. Sometimes citations are mismatched: a citation supports a different claim than the one it appears with, or it supports a weaker version of the claim. Verify that citations match the claims they support.

For cited claims, verify the cited source actually supports the claim as stated. Check that the citation is not supporting a weaker claim while the text asserts a stronger one. Check that the citation is not attached to the wrong claim through editing error. Where a claim rests on a source, verify the source says what the claim says it says. Mismatched citations undermine the work's evidentiary structure and can be exposed when readers follow them. Verify the citation-claim relationship throughout.

### Check For Over-Citation And Citation Clutter

While under-citation is the more serious error, over-citation can also be a problem. Citing common knowledge, citing redundantly, or cluttering the text with unnecessary citations disrupts reading without adding traceability. Check for over-citation and recommend trimming where appropriate.

For each citation, assess whether it is necessary. Flag citations to common knowledge, which do not add traceability. Flag redundant citations where the same source is cited repeatedly for the same point. Flag citation clutter that disrupts the prose without serving verification. Recommend removing or consolidating unnecessary citations. The goal is a citation apparatus that provides traceability where needed without burdening the text. Over-citation, while less harmful than under-citation, still requires editorial attention.

### Verify The Boundary Between Original And Borrowed Is Clear

Throughout the work, the reader should be able to tell what is the author's original contribution and what is borrowed from sources. When this boundary is unclear, the work's originality is ambiguous, and traceability fails. Verify the boundary between original and borrowed material is clear.

Read the work attending to the flow between cited and uncited material. Check that when the author transitions from discussing sources to presenting their own analysis, the transition is clear. Check that original arguments are distinguishable from summaries of sources. Check that the author's voice and contribution are identifiable amid the sourced material. Where the boundary is unclear, recommend clarification through signaling language or citation. Clear boundary marking protects the author's originality and ensures borrowed material is properly traceable.

## Common Traps

### Focusing On Format But Not Completeness

Correctly formatted citations may still leave traceability gaps. Check completeness.

### Missing Citations For Factual Claims

Specific and contested claims require citation. Identify what needs attribution.

### Unattributed Quotations

Direct quotations must be marked and cited. Verify attribution throughout.

### Unattributed Paraphrases

Borrowed ideas need attribution even when restated. Flag specialized knowledge without citation.

### Citations Too General To Trace

A 500-page book without pages is barely traceable. Check specificity and locatability.

### Citation-Claim Mismatch

Citations must support the claims they are attached to. Verify the relationship.

### Unclear Boundary Between Original And Borrowed

Readers should tell what is the author's contribution. Clarify transitions.

## Self-Check

Before treating source traceability as complete, verify:

- Every factual claim, especially specific or contested ones, has been checked for citation.
- All direct quotations are marked as quotations and attributed with specific citations.
- All paraphrased ideas and borrowed arguments are attributed to their sources.
- Common knowledge is distinguished from claims requiring citation.
- Citations are specific enough to locate the cited material, including page numbers where needed.
- Digital citations include URLs and access dates for locatability.
- Each citation actually supports the specific claim it is attached to.
- Over-citation and citation clutter have been identified for trimming.
- The boundary between the author's original contribution and borrowed material is clear.
- Every non-original element in the work is traceable to its source.
