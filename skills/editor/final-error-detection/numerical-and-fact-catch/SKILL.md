# Numerical And Fact Catch

Numbers, dates, names, titles, and other factual details are easy to overlook in editing because they sit within prose and the editor's attention is on language. But factual errors are among the most damaging because they are verifiable and undermine the work's credibility. A misstated date, a transposed digit in a statistic, an incorrect title or name, or a figure in the text that contradicts a table all signal carelessness or worse. Final fact checking catches these errors by verifying numbers against their sources, checking internal consistency, and flagging claims that seem implausible. Editors who treat factual verification as someone else's responsibility publish works with avoidable errors.

Use this skill at the copy-editing or proofreading stage to catch numerical and factual errors. It covers verifying figures against sources, checking internal consistency, cross-referencing text with tables and charts, and flagging implausible claims for author verification. The goal is a work where numbers and facts are internally consistent and traceable.

## Core Rules

### Verify Numbers Against Authoritative Sources

Every number in a work should be traceable to a source, and the final pass verifies that the number matches its source. This includes statistics, dates, quantities, measurements, and any quantitative claim. A number that cannot be traced or that does not match its source is an error waiting to be caught by readers.

For each number, identify its source: a study, a dataset, a reference work, or author-provided data. Verify the number matches the source exactly, including units and decimal places. Check that the source is current and authoritative. Where a number is attributed, verify the attribution is correct. Where a number is derived through calculation, verify the calculation. Flag any number that cannot be traced or that mismatches its source for author attention. Numbers that are unverified at publication are liabilities.

### Check Internal Consistency Of Figures

Even when individual numbers are correct against their sources, they may be inconsistent with each other within the work. A total in one place may not match the sum of its parts stated elsewhere. A percentage may not match the underlying count. A date in the text may contradict a date in a timeline. Internal consistency checking catches these contradictions.

Scan for numbers that should relate to each other and verify they do. Check that totals equal the sum of their components. Check that percentages match their base figures. Check that dates are consistent across mentions. Check that quantities and measurements are consistent when the same figure appears in multiple places. Check that figures in summaries match figures in the detailed discussion. Internal contradictions are common because different sections may be written or revised at different times, and they are highly visible to attentive readers.

### Cross-Reference Text With Tables And Charts

Numbers often appear both in the running text and in tables or charts, and the two must agree. A text that says "43 percent" while the table shows "34 percent" is a glaring inconsistency. Cross-referencing catches mismatches between prose and visual data presentations.

For each table and chart, verify that figures cited in the text match the table or chart exactly. Check that the text describes the data accurately, not just that the numbers match but that the characterization is correct. Check that table and chart titles, labels, and notes are consistent with the text discussion. Check that the same data presented in multiple formats, such as a table and a chart, is consistent across formats. Text-table-chart consistency is a high-yield area for catching errors because the same data appears in multiple places where drift can occur.

### Verify Names, Titles, And Proper Nouns

Names, titles, and proper nouns are factual details that are easy to get wrong and damaging when wrong. A misspelled name, an incorrect title, or a wrong affiliation disrespects the subject and undermines credibility. Verify these details as rigorously as numbers.

For each person named, verify the spelling of their name and their correct title or affiliation. For organizations, verify the official name and any required capitalization or styling. For places, verify current and correct names, especially where names have changed. For works cited, verify titles, authors, and publication details. Check that names and titles are consistent across all mentions. Errors in names and titles are often caught by the subjects themselves or their associates, and they are embarrassing because they suggest the author did not care enough to verify.

### Flag Implausible Claims For Author Verification

Some factual errors are not mismatches against a source but claims that are implausible on their face. A date that is out of range, a statistic that seems too extreme, a claim that contradicts general knowledge, these may be errors even without a specific source to check against. Flag implausible claims for the author to verify rather than passing them silently.

Read with a skeptical eye for claims that seem off. A date outside the plausible range for the context, a percentage that seems too high or low, a quantitative claim that contradicts common understanding, these warrant a flag. Note the concern and ask the author to verify. Do not change the claim unilaterally, because it may be correct and surprising, but do not let it pass without verification. Implausibility flags catch errors that source-checking misses because the error is in the claim itself, not its transcription.

### Check Units, Scales, And Decimal Places

Numerical errors often involve units, scales, and decimal places rather than the digits themselves. A figure stated in millions that should be billions, a decimal point in the wrong place, a unit confusion between metric and imperial, these are common and consequential errors. Check units and scales explicitly.

For each number, verify the unit is correct and consistently stated. Check that the scale is appropriate and consistent, millions versus billions, thousands versus millions. Verify decimal places are correct and consistent with the source. Check for unit conversions and verify they are correct. Check that units are stated explicitly where needed for clarity. Unit and scale errors can change meaning by orders of magnitude and are easy to miss because the digits look right.

### Verify Dates And Temporal Claims

Dates involve their own error patterns: wrong year, wrong month, day-month transposition, and inconsistencies across the work. They are also often load-bearing for narrative or argument. Verify dates systematically.

For each date, verify it is correct against the source and internally consistent. Check date format consistency throughout the work. Verify that dates make sense in context, that events are in the right sequence and that durations are plausible. Check that dates in timelines, chronologies, and tables match dates in the text. For historical dates, verify calendar conventions if relevant. Date errors are common because dates are easy to mistype and are often revised during editing without full consistency checks.

## Common Traps

### Treating Factual Verification As Someone Else's Job

Numbers and facts are editorial responsibilities. Verify or flag for verification.

### Missing Internal Inconsistencies

Numbers correct against sources may contradict each other within the work. Check consistency.

### Overlooking Text-Table-Chart Mismatches

The same data in multiple places drifts. Cross-reference text with tables and charts.

### Trusting Names And Titles Without Verification

Misspelled names and wrong titles undermine credibility. Verify all proper nouns.

### Passing Implausible Claims Silently

Claims that seem off may be errors. Flag for author verification.

### Missing Unit And Scale Errors

Right digits with wrong units or scales change meaning drastically. Check explicitly.

### Inconsistent Date Formats And Sequences

Dates are easy to mistype and revise inconsistently. Verify dates systematically.

## Self-Check

Before treating numerical and fact catching as complete, verify:

- Every number has been traced to a source and verified to match, including units and decimals.
- Internal consistency of figures has been checked, including totals, percentages, and repeated figures.
- Text figures have been cross-referenced with tables and charts for exact agreement.
- Names, titles, affiliations, and proper nouns have been verified for spelling and accuracy.
- Implausible claims have been flagged for author verification rather than passed silently.
- Units, scales, and decimal places have been checked for correctness and consistency.
- Dates have been verified for accuracy, format consistency, and contextual plausibility.
- Figures in summaries match figures in detailed discussion.
- The same data presented in multiple formats is consistent across formats.
- Factual details are consistent across all mentions throughout the work.
