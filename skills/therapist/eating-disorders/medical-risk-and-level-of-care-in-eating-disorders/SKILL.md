# Medical Risk And Level Of Care In Eating Disorders

Eating disorders are among the most medically dangerous psychiatric conditions, with mortality rates driven by cardiac arrest, electrolyte collapse, and suicide, and the therapist is often the first and most consistent clinician in a position to detect medical instability. The therapist's central judgment is to assess medical risk continuously rather than once at intake, to know when outpatient care is unsafe and a higher level of care is required, to coordinate with medical and nutritional providers as core members of the team rather than optional adjuncts, and to recognize that the disorder itself will resist the very interventions that would save the client's life. The recurring failures are treating medical monitoring as someone else's job, accepting the client's self-report of stability when objective signs say otherwise, keeping a medically unstable client in outpatient care because the alliance feels strong, and underestimating how aggressively the eating disorder will undermine treatment.

Use this skill when assessing medical risk in an eating disorder, deciding on level of care, coordinating medical and nutritional care, or when an outpatient client is not progressing or is deteriorating.

## Core Rules

### Assess Medical Risk Continuously, Not Only At Intake

Medical risk in eating disorders is dynamic, not static: a client who was stable at intake can become medically unstable within weeks through weight loss, purging escalation, electrolyte disturbance, or co-occurring substance use. Build medical risk assessment into ongoing care, not only the initial evaluation. Track weight trends, vital signs when available, purging frequency, restrictive patterns, exercise intensity, laxative and diuretic use, and any signs of cardiovascular compromise such as dizziness, fainting, chest pain, or palpitations. Coordinate with a medical provider for laboratory monitoring, including electrolytes, especially potassium, and an ECG when indicated. A single "medically cleared" note at intake does not establish ongoing safety, because the client can deteriorate between visits.

### Recognize The Signs That Outpatient Care Is Unsafe

Certain clinical features indicate that outpatient care is unsafe and a higher level of care, intensive outpatient, partial hospitalization, residential, or inpatient, is required. These include significantly low body weight or rapid weight loss, purging severe enough to cause electrolyte disturbance, vital sign instability, syncope or near-synapse, medical comorbidity such as diabetes interacting with the eating disorder, co-occurring suicidality or severe self-harm, and failure to progress in outpatient care with ongoing deterioration. The client's motivation, insight, or therapeutic alliance does not override medical instability; a motivated, insightful client who is medically unstable still needs a higher level of care. Know the criteria used by programs in your area, and refer early rather than late.

### Coordinate With Medical And Nutritional Providers As Core Team Members

Eating disorder treatment requires a team: a therapist, a medical provider, a registered dietitian, often a psychiatrist, and sometimes a family therapist. These are not optional referrals; they are core members of the treatment. Establish the team early, with releases for communication, and maintain regular contact. The medical provider monitors labs and vital signs; the dietitian addresses the nutritional rehabilitation that the therapist is not qualified to direct; the psychiatrist addresses comorbid conditions and medication. A therapist treating an eating disorder without medical and nutritional support is practicing below the standard of care, because the medical and nutritional dimensions are central to the condition, not adjunctive.

### Understand Refeeding Risk And Why Medical Supervision Is Non-Negotiable

Refeeding a severely malnourished client can cause refeeding syndrome, a potentially fatal shift in fluids and electrolytes, particularly phosphorus, magnesium, and potassium, as metabolism shifts. Refeeding requires medical supervision, gradual caloric increase, and laboratory monitoring, and is not something outpatient therapy can manage. This is one reason a severely underweight client cannot be safely treated in outpatient care alone. Understand the risk so you can explain to the client and family why medical and nutritional supervision is required, why a higher level of care is indicated, and why "just eating more" is medically dangerous without supervision. The eating disorder will use the discomfort of refeeding as evidence that recovery is harmful; the therapist must be able to distinguish normal refeeding distress from medical danger.

### Anticipate And Work With The Disorder's Resistance To Treatment

Eating disorders are ambivalent about recovery by their nature: the disorder provides something, control, soothing, identity, protection from other problems, that the client does not want to lose, and the client will often resist the interventions that would help. Anticipate this resistance rather than being surprised or offended by it. Expect minimization of symptoms, concealment of weight or purging, reluctance to gain weight, sabotage of meal plans, and ambivalence about higher levels of care. Use motivational and harm-reduction approaches alongside the medical framework, work with the ambivalence rather than against it, and do not interpret resistance as a reason to lower the level of care when medical risk is present. The disorder will argue for outpatient care; the clinician must weigh medical risk, not the client's preference alone.

### Use Objective Measures Because Self-Report Is Unreliable

Clients with eating disorders, particularly restrictive and purging disorders, often minimize or conceal symptoms, not from dishonesty but from the disorder itself. Do not rely on self-report alone for the indicators that drive level of care: obtain weights taken consistently, ideally in a medical setting with the client in a gown to prevent hidden weights; review laboratory results directly rather than accepting "normal"; track vital signs; and corroborate purging frequency through collateral where possible. A client who reports "doing better" while objectively deteriorating is showing the disorder at work, and the objective data must drive the clinical decision. Document the objective measures and the reasoning based on them.

### Address Co-Occurring Conditions That Affect Medical Risk

Eating disorders frequently co-occur with depression, anxiety, obsessive-compulsive disorder, substance use, trauma, and personality vulnerabilities, and these affect both medical risk and treatment. Type 1 diabetes with an eating disorder, sometimes called "diabulimia," is particularly dangerous and requires specialized care; substance use, especially stimulants, worsens medical risk; suicidality and self-harm add to the risk picture. Assess co-occurring conditions, integrate them into the formulation and the level-of-care decision, and ensure the team is addressing them. An eating disorder does not exist in isolation, and treating it as if it does misses the conditions that drive and complicate it.

## Common Traps

### Treating Medical Monitoring As Someone Else's Job

A therapist may assume that the medical provider is monitoring risk and that the therapist's job is purely psychological, when in practice the therapist often sees the client most frequently and is best positioned to notice deterioration, and medical follow-up may be sporadic. The mechanism of harm is that everyone assumes someone else is watching the medical risk, and no one is, and the client deteriorates between visits without anyone catching it. The harm is that medical instability progresses to crisis, sometimes to cardiac arrest or suicide, because the monitoring was assumed rather than arranged. Establishing the team, communicating regularly, and treating medical risk monitoring as a shared, explicit responsibility prevents this gap.

### Accepting Self-Report Of Stability Against Objective Signs

A therapist may accept a client's report that they are eating, not purging, and feeling better, especially when the alliance is strong and the client is articulate, while objective signs, weight trend, labs, purging evidence, or vital signs, tell a different story. The mechanism of harm is that the alliance and the client's narrative are weighted over the objective data, and the disorder's minimization is mistaken for clinical improvement. The harm is that a deteriorating client is treated as stable, the level of care is not escalated, and the client progresses toward medical crisis. Using objective measures, corroborating self-report, and letting the data drive the decision, even when it contradicts the client's report, is essential.

### Keeping A Medically Unstable Client In Outpatient Care Because The Alliance Feels Strong

A therapist may keep a client who meets criteria for a higher level of care in outpatient treatment because the therapeutic alliance is strong, the client is engaged, and the therapist believes they can help, when medical instability makes outpatient care unsafe regardless of the alliance. The mechanism of harm is that the strength of the relationship is mistaken for the adequacy of the level of care, and the therapist, reluctant to disrupt the alliance, delays the referral that the client's medical condition requires. The harm is that the client deteriorates further, sometimes irreversibly, while the therapist continues a treatment that cannot meet the medical need. Escalating the level of care based on medical criteria, even at the cost of the alliance, is the standard of care.

### Underestimating The Disorder's Capacity To Undermine Treatment

A therapist may underestimate how aggressively an eating disorder will sabotage treatment, expecting that insight and alliance will translate into behavioral change, when the disorder will use minimization, concealment, sabotage of meal plans, and resistance to weight restoration to protect itself. The mechanism of harm is that the therapist treats the eating disorder as a symptom the client can control with insight, rather than as a process that actively resists recovery, and is repeatedly surprised by setbacks framed as the client's choice. The harm is that the treatment plan underestimates the intensity of intervention required, and the client does not receive the structure, monitoring, or level of care that the disorder demands. Anticipating resistance, using objective measures, and matching the intensity of care to the disorder's severity prevents this mismatch.

### Failing To Establish A Full Team And Practicing In Isolation

A therapist may treat an eating disorder with therapy alone, referring to a medical provider or dietitian only nominally or not at all, when the condition requires an integrated team and the therapist alone cannot manage the medical and nutritional dimensions. The mechanism of harm is that the therapist practices below the standard of care, the medical and nutritional dimensions go unaddressed, and the client's risk is not monitored by the people qualified to monitor it. The harm is that the client receives incomplete care, medical instability goes undetected, and the treatment fails for reasons that were preventable. Establishing and actively coordinating a full team is a prerequisite for treating eating disorders.

- [ ] Did I establish and coordinate a full team, including medical provider, dietitian, and psychiatrist, with releases for communication?
