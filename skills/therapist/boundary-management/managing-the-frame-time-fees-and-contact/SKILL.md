# Managing The Frame: Time, Fees, And Contact

The frame, the set of boundaries that structure the therapy, session length, frequency, scheduling, fees, cancellation policy, and the rules for contact between sessions, is not an administrative detail; it is a clinical structure that holds the work. A consistent frame lets the client trust the relationship, brings relational material into the room where it can be worked with, and protects both client and therapist from the ambiguity of a relationship without boundaries. The therapist's judgment is to establish the frame clearly, to maintain it consistently, to examine frame disruptions as clinical material rather than merely enforcing rules, and to adjust the frame deliberately when adjustment serves the client. The recurring failures are inconsistent enforcement, frame adjustments that serve the therapist, treating frame issues as administrative rather than clinical, and rigid enforcement that ignores the client's circumstances.

Use this skill when establishing the frame, when frame disruptions arise, when considering a frame adjustment, or when the frame has eroded.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Frame Clearly At The Outset

At the start of treatment, establish and explain the frame: session length and frequency, scheduling process, the fee and billing, the cancellation policy, the policy on contact between sessions and after hours, and the expectations on both sides. Put the key elements in writing where appropriate. A frame the client understands from the start is one they can work within; a frame that is discovered piecemeal through violations creates confusion and resentment.

### Maintain The Frame Consistently

Consistency is what makes the frame hold. Start and end sessions on time, charge the agreed fee consistently, apply the cancellation policy consistently, and respond to between-session contact according to the agreed policy. Inconsistency, waiving the fee for one client but not another, running over for some sessions but not others, answering some after-hours contacts but not others, creates ambiguity that the client must navigate and that erodes trust. The frame's consistency, not its specific terms, is what makes it therapeutic.

### Treat Frame Disruptions As Clinical Material

Frame disruptions, chronic lateness, missed sessions, late payment, excessive between-session contact, requests for exceptions, are not merely administrative problems; they are clinical material that often reflects the client's relational patterns. A client who is always late may be expressing resistance, anxiety, or a pattern of self-sabotage; a client who does not pay may be testing the frame or expressing anger indirectly; a client who seeks excessive contact may be struggling with boundaries or with dependency. Explore the disruption as material, while also addressing it as a frame issue. The two are not opposed; the frame is maintained and the disruption is understood.

### Examine Your Own Frame Behavior

The therapist's frame behavior matters as much as the client's. Do you start and end on time? Do you follow through on what you say? Do you find yourself making exceptions for some clients but not others, and why? Do you avoid addressing frame disruptions because the conversation is uncomfortable? Examine your own consistency and your own patterns, because frame erosion often begins with the therapist's exceptions, which then shape what the client expects.

### Adjust The Frame Deliberately When It Serves The Client

The frame is not immutable; it can be adjusted deliberately when adjustment serves the client's treatment. Reducing frequency as the client stabilizes, adjusting the fee for a documented financial hardship, increasing contact during a crisis, or modifying the cancellation policy for a client with a chronic illness are all legitimate adjustments when made deliberately, discussed with the client, documented, and consistent with the client's welfare. The distinction between deliberate adjustment and frame erosion is whether the adjustment is conscious, discussed, and client-serving.

### Address Frame Issues Directly And Early

Frame disruptions that are not addressed early accumulate and become harder to address. A client who is occasionally late becomes chronically late; an unpaid bill becomes a pattern; an after-hours text becomes a stream of contact. Address disruptions early, non-judgmentally, and curiously, exploring the meaning while re-establishing the frame. Early address prevents the accumulation that eventually requires a confrontation that damages the alliance.

### Distinguish Frame Flexibility That Serves The Client From Flexibility That Serves The Therapist

Frame flexibility is appropriate when it serves the client's treatment, and inappropriate when it serves the therapist's needs. Waiving a fee because the client genuinely cannot pay and needs continued care serves the client; waiving a fee because the therapist is uncomfortable discussing money serves the therapist. Extending a session because the client is in crisis serves the client; extending because the therapist enjoys the client's company serves the therapist. Examine each flexibility for whose need it meets, and consult when the distinction is unclear.

## Common Traps

### Inconsistent Enforcement

When the therapist applies the frame inconsistently, waiving fees sometimes, running over sometimes, answering some after-hours contacts, the client cannot predict the boundaries and must navigate ambiguity in every interaction. The mechanism is that each individual exception feels caring or expedient, so the inconsistency is invisible to the therapist, while the client experiences the frame as unstable. The harm is that the client cannot trust the structure, relational material leaks into the ambiguity, and the frame's therapeutic function is lost. Consistent application, with deliberate and discussed exceptions, is what makes the frame hold.

### Frame Adjustments That Serve The Therapist

A therapist may adjust the frame in ways that serve their own needs, extending sessions with clients they enjoy, reducing sessions with clients they find difficult, waiving fees to avoid money conversations, or allowing after-hours contact to feel needed, while framing the adjustments as client-serving. The mechanism is that the therapist's needs are often invisible to the therapist, so self-serving adjustments feel like good practice. The harm is a frame shaped by the therapist's welfare rather than the client's, a relationship that has drifted from its therapeutic purpose, and a client whose treatment is compromised. Examining each adjustment for whose need it meets is the discipline that prevents this.

### Treating Frame Issues As Purely Administrative

When frame disruptions are treated as administrative problems to be managed rather than as clinical material to be understood, the therapy loses the relational information the disruptions carry. The mechanism is that addressing the disruption as a billing or scheduling issue feels efficient, so the therapist does not explore its meaning. The harm is that the client's relational pattern, expressed in the frame disruption, is not addressed, and the pattern continues, often in the client's life outside therapy as well. Treating frame disruptions as both an administrative issue to manage and clinical material to explore is what captures their full value.

### Avoiding Frame Conversations Because They Are Uncomfortable

A therapist who is uncomfortable with conflict may avoid addressing frame disruptions, letting lateness, missed sessions, late payment, or excessive contact continue unaddressed, while resentment builds and the frame erodes. The mechanism is that the conversation feels confrontational, so the therapist hopes the disruption will resolve on its own, when it rarely does. The harm is that the disruption continues, the therapist's resentment leaks into the therapy, and the eventual confrontation is larger and more damaging than an early, calm address would have been. Addressing frame issues early and non-judgmentally is what prevents the accumulation.

### Rigid Enforcement That Ignores Circumstances

A therapist who enforces the frame rigidly, without considering the client's circumstances, may apply rules in ways that are technically consistent but clinically harmful, charging a cancellation fee to a client hospitalized for a medical emergency, refusing any fee adjustment for a client who has lost their income, or enforcing punctuality with a client whose transportation is unreliable due to poverty. The mechanism is that consistency is treated as the highest value, so circumstances are ignored in its name. The harm is a frame that is consistent but cruel, that prioritizes the structure over the client, and that can drive vulnerable clients out of care. Deliberate, discussed, documented adjustment for genuine circumstances is what distinguishes a therapeutic frame from a bureaucratic one.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I establish the frame clearly at the outset, explaining and documenting the key elements?
- [ ] Am I maintaining the frame consistently, without ad hoc exceptions that create ambiguity?
- [ ] Am I treating frame disruptions as clinical material, exploring their meaning while re-establishing the frame?
- [ ] Do I examine my own frame behavior, including my consistency and my patterns of exception?
- [ ] When adjusting the frame, did I do so deliberately, with discussion, documentation, and the client's welfare as the criterion?
- [ ] Do I address frame disruptions early and non-judgmentally, before they accumulate?
- [ ] Do I distinguish frame flexibility that serves the client from flexibility that serves me, consulting when unclear?
- [ ] Do I avoid inconsistent enforcement, self-serving adjustments, treating frame issues as purely administrative, avoiding frame conversations, and rigid enforcement that ignores circumstances?
