# Dual Relationships And Outside Contact

A dual relationship, where the therapist has another role with the client in addition to the therapeutic one, is one of the most common boundary dilemmas, and it is rarely as simple as a rule against it. In small communities, rural settings, tight professional networks, and specific populations, dual relationships are sometimes unavoidable, and the question is not whether to avoid them but how to manage them to protect the client. The therapist's judgment is to recognize dual relationships when they arise or are anticipated, to assess the risk to the client and the therapy, to avoid those that can be avoided, and to manage those that cannot with explicit discussion, consultation, and documentation. The recurring failures are entering dual relationships casually without recognizing them, justifying exploitative relationships as unavoidable, and refusing all dual relationships so rigidly that clients in small communities cannot access care.

Use this skill when a potential dual relationship arises, when working in a small community, when an outside encounter with a client occurs, or when an existing relationship becomes a therapy relationship.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Dual Relationships Early, Including Subtle Ones

Dual relationships are not only the dramatic cases of romance or business. They include the therapist whose client is their child's teacher, whose client attends the same church, whose client's spouse they know professionally, or whose client they encounter regularly in a small town. Recognize the dual relationship as soon as it is anticipated or arises, because the failure to recognize is the failure to manage. Subtle dual relationships, if unaddressed, can shape the therapy invisibly.

### Assess The Risk To The Client And The Therapy

Not all dual relationships carry the same risk. Assess:

- the power differential in the other role, with greater power for the therapist increasing risk;
- the intimacy of the other role, with greater intimacy increasing risk;
- the duration and unavoidability of the other role;
- the potential for the other role to compromise objectivity, confidentiality, or the therapeutic frame;
- the client's vulnerability and history, which may heighten the impact of any boundary issue.

A dual relationship that is brief, public, low-intimacy, and unavoidable in a small community may be manageable; one that is intimate, ongoing, and avoidable is high-risk and should be avoided.

### Avoid Dual Relationships That Can Be Avoided

When a dual relationship can be avoided, avoid it. Do not provide therapy to friends, family, employees, colleagues, or others with whom you have a pre-existing relationship that would compromise objectivity or create a power imbalance. Do not enter into business, romantic, or social relationships with current clients. The avoidance is not bureaucratic; it protects the client from the distortion of the therapeutic relationship that dual roles introduce.

### Manage Unavoidable Dual Relationships Explicitly

In small communities, rural settings, and specific populations, some dual relationships cannot be avoided, and refusing to see the client would deny them care. When a dual relationship is unavoidable:

- name it explicitly with the client, acknowledging the dual roles;
- discuss how it will be managed, including how to handle encounters outside therapy, how confidentiality will be protected, and how the other role will not intrude into the therapy;
- document the discussion and the plan;
- consult, especially when the dual relationship is significant;
- monitor the impact on the therapy and revisit the plan as needed.

Explicit management, not silent accommodation, is what protects the client when dual relationships are unavoidable.

### Handle Chance Encounters With Discretion

Chance encounters with clients outside the session, in a store, a restaurant, a social event, are common and require discretion. Follow the client's lead on acknowledgment, do not disclose the therapeutic relationship, do not introduce the client to your companions by their client status, and keep the interaction brief. Discuss at the next session how the client would like such encounters handled, so the understanding is explicit rather than improvised.

### Do Not Enter Romantic Or Sexual Relationships With Clients, Ever

Romantic or sexual relationships with current clients are always prohibited, regardless of the client's initiative or the therapist's feelings, because the power differential makes genuine consent impossible and the harm to the client is well-documented. Relationships with former clients are prohibited for a defined period after termination under most ethics codes, often two years or permanently, and even after the period are generally considered high-risk and ethically fraught. This is not a boundary to manage; it is a boundary to observe absolutely.

### Be Cautious With Bartering, Gifts, And Outside Contact

Other boundary issues require judgment rather than absolute prohibition. Bartering, accepting goods or services for therapy, is permitted in some ethics codes under specific conditions but carries risks of exploitation and conflict. Gifts may be acceptable when modest and culturally expected, but require assessment of the meaning and the impact on the frame. Outside contact, calls, emails, texts between sessions, should be governed by a clear understanding of their purpose and limits. Each of these is assessed in context, with the client's interest and the frame's integrity as the criteria.

## Common Traps

### Entering Dual Relationships Casually

A therapist who agrees to see a friend, a colleague, a neighbor, or an acquaintance, rationalizing that the relationship is not significant or that the client really needs help, enters a dual relationship without recognizing the risk. The mechanism is that the request feels reasonable and the prior relationship feels manageable, so the therapist proceeds without assessing the distortion the dual role will introduce. The harm is that the therapy is compromised from the start: objectivity is impaired, confidentiality is at risk, the frame is blurred, and the client receives inferior care. Recognizing the dual relationship before agreeing to treat, and referring when it is significant, is what prevents the harm.

### Justifying Exploitative Relationships As Unavoidable

In small communities, the genuine unavoidability of some dual relationships can become a justification for relationships that are avoidable and exploitative, such as business ventures, social intimacies, or romantic connections with clients, rationalized as part of community life. The mechanism is that the real constraints of small communities blur into a license for relationships that serve the therapist rather than the client. The harm is exploitation of the power differential, harm to the client, and violation of the ethics code. Distinguishing genuinely unavoidable dual relationships, which are managed explicitly, from avoidable and exploitative ones, which are refused, is the discipline that protects clients.

### Refusing All Dual Relationships So Rigidly That Care Is Denied

A therapist who applies the rule against dual relationships absolutely, refusing to see anyone with any prior connection, may deny care to clients in small communities where some connection is inevitable. The mechanism is that the rule is applied without judgment, treating all dual relationships as equally prohibited regardless of their nature or unavoidability. The harm is that clients who need care cannot access it, because no therapist in their community can meet a standard of zero prior connection. The ethics codes anticipate this and permit management of unavoidable dual relationships; rigid refusal exceeds the standard and harms clients.

### The Unaddressed Chance Encounter

When the therapist and client encounter each other outside the session and the encounter is not discussed, it can create ambiguity and anxiety for the client, who does not know how to interpret the therapist's behavior or what it means for confidentiality. The mechanism is that the encounter feels awkward to raise, so neither party does, and the ambiguity festers. The harm is that the client's uncertainty about the frame, and about how their therapist relates to them outside the room, erodes the alliance. Discussing chance encounters at the next session, and agreeing on how future encounters will be handled, removes the ambiguity.

### Sliding Boundary Creep

A boundary that is extended once, a slightly longer session, a waived fee, a personal exchange, an after-hours contact, is easier to extend again, and the gradual accumulation can move the relationship well outside the therapeutic frame before either party recognizes it. The mechanism is that each individual extension feels minor and caring, so the pattern of creep is invisible, and the therapist, having extended once, has reduced the friction to extending again. The harm is a frame that has eroded to the point of being unclear, with the client navigating ambiguity and the therapist's objectivity compromised. Noticing the pattern of extensions, consulting when the frame is shifting, and re-establishing the frame when it has eroded, are what prevent creep from accumulating.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I recognize the dual relationship as soon as it arose or was anticipated, including subtle ones?
- [ ] Did I assess the risk to the client and the therapy, considering power, intimacy, duration, unavoidability, and client vulnerability?
- [ ] Did I avoid dual relationships that could be avoided, including therapy with friends, family, employees, and colleagues?
- [ ] For unavoidable dual relationships, did I name them explicitly, discuss management, document, and consult?
- [ ] Do I handle chance encounters with discretion, following the client's lead and not disclosing the relationship?
- [ ] Am I observing the absolute prohibition on romantic or sexual relationships with current clients, and the restrictions on former clients?
- [ ] For bartering, gifts, and outside contact, did I assess the meaning and impact on the frame, rather than accepting or refusing reflexively?
- [ ] Did I avoid entering dual relationships casually, justifying exploitative ones as unavoidable, and refusing all dual relationships so rigidly that care is denied?
