# Applying DBT And Emotion Regulation Skills

Dialectical behavior therapy was developed for clients with chronic emotion dysregulation, self-harm, and borderline personality patterns, populations that often fail in traditional therapy and that strain the therapist. DBT's distinctive contribution is the dialectic, the simultaneous stance of acceptance and change, validated as the client is while supporting them in changing what causes suffering. The therapist's judgment is to hold the dialectic, to deliver the skills components with fidelity, to use validation strategically rather than reflexively, and to manage the dysregulation that can derail the therapy. The recurring failures are validating without change, changing without validation, delivering skills without the individual therapy that makes them usable, and the therapist becoming dysregulated by the client's crises.

Use this skill when treating clients with emotion dysregulation, self-harm, or borderline patterns; when teaching DBT skills; or when the client's dysregulation is threatening the therapy.

## Core Rules

### Hold The Dialectic Of Acceptance And Change

The core of DBT is the dialectic between acceptance and change. Acceptance alone validates the client but offers no path out of suffering; change alone invalidates the client and activates resistance. The dialectic holds both: the client is accepted exactly as they are, and the client is supported in changing the patterns that cause suffering. Move between validation and problem-solving within sessions and across treatment, never staying too long in one pole. The dialectic is a stance, not a technique; it pervades the therapy.

### Use Validation Strategically, Not Reflexively

Validation communicates that the client's responses make sense given their history and current circumstances, and it is one of DBT's most powerful tools. But validation is strategic: it is used to validate what is valid, the understandable response, not to validate what is maladaptive, the behavior that causes suffering. Distinguish the valid emotion or perception from the unhelpful response, validate the former, and address the latter. Reflexive validation, validating everything, removes the change pole of the dialectic and leaves the client accepted but unchanged.

### Teach The Four Skills Modules With Attention To Function

DBT skills are organized into four modules, each addressing a function:

- mindfulness, the capacity to observe and describe experience without judgment, which underlies the other skills;
- distress tolerance, skills for surviving crisis without making it worse, including distraction, self-soothing, and radical acceptance;
- emotion regulation, skills for understanding and changing emotional responses, including identifying emotions, reducing vulnerability, and increasing positive emotions;
- interpersonal effectiveness, skills for asserting needs, managing conflict, and maintaining relationships and self-respect.

Teach skills with attention to the function they serve for this client, not as a curriculum to complete. A client whose crisis is self-harm needs distress tolerance first; a client whose relationships are chaotic needs interpersonal effectiveness; the sequencing follows the client's needs.

### Target Behaviors In A Clear Hierarchy

DBT organizes treatment around a clear target hierarchy: life-threatening behaviors first, therapy-interfering behaviors second, quality-of-life-interfering behaviors third. Within each category, the most recent or most severe is addressed first. The hierarchy keeps the therapy focused on what matters most and prevents the session from being captured by whatever crisis the client brings in, which may not be the most important target. Address self-harm and suicidality before addressing therapy-interfering behaviors, and therapy-interfering behaviors before addressing relationship problems.

### Use Phone Coaching To Support Skills Generalization

DBT includes phone coaching between sessions, brief calls focused on applying skills in the moment, not on therapy processing. The purpose is to help the client use skills in real-life situations where they are most needed, before the crisis escalates to self-harm. Phone coaching is skills-focused, time-limited, and distinct from therapy. If you offer it, define its purpose and limits clearly, and use it to support generalization rather than as a crisis line.

### Manage Therapy-Interfering Behaviors As Targets

Therapy-interfering behaviors, missed sessions, lateness, non-completion of homework, angry or threatening behavior toward the therapist, are themselves targets in DBT, because they threaten the treatment that the client needs. Address therapy-interfering behaviors directly, explore their function, and work to change them, because a therapy that the client cannot engage in will not help them. Do not let therapy-interfering behaviors continue unaddressed while pursuing other targets.

### Use The Consultation Team To Support The Therapist

DBT recognizes that treating this population is demanding and that therapists need support to sustain the work. A consultation team provides peer support, keeps the therapist in the dialectic, prevents burnout, and offers perspective on difficult cases. If you are doing DBT-informed work without a formal team, build consultation into your practice, because treating dysregulated clients without support risks the therapist's own dysregulation and compromised care.

## Common Traps

### Validation Without Change

A therapist who validates skillfully but does not move to the change pole leaves the client feeling understood but unchanged, and the therapy becomes supportive without being transformative. The mechanism is that validation feels good and reduces the client's distress in the moment, so the therapist, relieved, stays in validation, when the relief is temporary and the suffering continues. The harm is a therapy that the client enjoys but that does not address the patterns causing the suffering, sometimes for years. Moving from validation to problem-solving, holding the dialectic, is what makes the validation therapeutic rather than merely soothing.

### Change Without Validation

The opposite error is pushing change without validating, confronting the client's maladaptive behaviors without first communicating that their responses make sense, which activates resistance and ruptures the alliance. The mechanism is that the therapist, focused on what needs to change, addresses it directly without the validation that would make the change acceptable to the client. The harm is that the client experiences the therapy as invalidating and critical, the alliance ruptures, and the client disengages or drops out. Validation before change, the dialectic, is what makes change acceptable.

### Skills Without The Individual Therapy

A therapist who teaches DBT skills in a group or in session without the individual therapy that helps the client apply them may produce a client who knows the skills intellectually but cannot use them in crisis. The mechanism is that skills knowledge is necessary but not sufficient; the client needs the individual therapy to identify the obstacles to using the skills, to practice in the therapeutic relationship, and to generalize to life. The harm is a client who can recite the skills but whose behavior does not change, because the skills were taught without the context that makes them usable. Integrating skills training with individual therapy, and addressing the obstacles to skills use, is what makes the skills effective.

### The Session Captured By The Crisis

A client who brings a crisis to every session can capture the session with crisis management, week after week, while the underlying patterns are never addressed. The mechanism is that the crisis is urgent and compelling, so addressing it feels like the priority, when the recurring crises are themselves the pattern that the therapy needs to address. The harm is that the therapy becomes reactive crisis management, the underlying patterns continue, and the client does not improve. Using the target hierarchy, addressing therapy-interfering patterns, and connecting the recurring crises to the skills the client needs, are what prevent the session from being captured.

### The Therapist Becoming Dysregulated

Treating clients with chronic dysregulation, self-harm, and crisis is demanding, and the therapist can become dysregulated in response, anxious, overwhelmed, reactive, or burnt out, which compromises the therapy. The mechanism is that the client's dysregulation is contagious, and the therapist, without support, begins to mirror it. The harm is a therapist whose reactivity shapes clinical decisions, whose burnout reduces the quality of care, and whose own dysregulation models the opposite of what the therapy teaches. Using consultation, recognizing one's own dysregulation, and sustaining self-care are what allow the therapist to do this work over time.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Am I holding the dialectic of acceptance and change, moving between validation and problem-solving?
- [ ] Am I using validation strategically, validating the valid response while addressing the maladaptive behavior?
- [ ] Am I teaching the four skills modules with attention to the function each serves for this client?
- [ ] Am I targeting behaviors in the hierarchy: life-threatening, therapy-interfering, quality-of-life-interfering?
- [ ] If offering phone coaching, is it skills-focused, time-limited, and distinct from therapy processing?
- [ ] Am I addressing therapy-interfering behaviors as targets, rather than letting them continue unaddressed?
- [ ] Am I using consultation or a team to support myself in this demanding work?
- [ ] Did I avoid validation without change, change without validation, skills without individual therapy, session capture by crisis, and my own dysregulation?
