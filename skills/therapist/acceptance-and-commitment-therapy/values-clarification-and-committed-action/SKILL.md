# Values Clarification and Committed Action

Values clarification and committed action are the directional heart of Acceptance and Commitment Therapy, and the judgment problem is that values work is often delivered as a pleasant values-card exercise that produces a list on a page but no change in behavior. A value, in ACT, is a freely chosen, verbally constructed, ongoing pattern of action that reveals what a person wants to stand for. It is not a goal to be reached and finished, not a feeling to be achieved, not an obligation imposed by others, and not a moral rule. The therapist must distinguish values from goals, must detect when a stated value is actually plused (socially coerced, guilt-driven, or fused with a rigid self-concept), and must convert clarified values into concrete, repeatable, observable committed actions that the client performs between sessions. The deeper challenge is that committed action almost always requires willingness toward the discomfort that valued living activates, so values work cannot be separated from acceptance and defusion work without collapsing into a goal-setting exercise. The therapist must also watch for values drift, perfectionism, all-or-nothing committed action, and the use of values as a weapon of self-criticism. The therapist's freedom is real, but the work must remain grounded in choice, in behavioral specificity, and in safety. Given the clinical stakes, this guidance is conservative: clarify freely chosen values, convert them to small repeatable actions, link action to willingness, and protect the client from values becoming another source of avoidance or self-punishment.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Values From Goals, Feelings, Rules, and Obligations

The therapist must teach and repeatedly reinforce the structural difference between a value and its common imposters, because clients will otherwise convert every values conversation into a goal to achieve or a feeling to attain. A value is an ongoing direction, like heading west; a goal is a destination that can be completed, like reaching a city. A value can be enacted now, in this session, regardless of outcome; a goal is reached only in the future. The therapist should test any candidate value with the "ongoing pattern" question: can this be enacted today and again tomorrow and again next year without ever being finished? If the answer is no, the candidate is a goal. The therapist must also distinguish values from desired feelings (wanting to feel confident is not a value), from moral rules (being a good person is too abstract and often fused with approval-seeking), and from obligations imposed by family, culture, or religion that the client has never actually chosen.

The tradeoff is that goals are concrete and motivating in the short term, while values are abstract and require translation to become actionable. The resolution is to use values as the direction and goals as the milestones along that direction, and to ensure every goal is explicitly in service of a value so that achieving or failing the goal does not collapse the value.

### Detect Chosen Versus Plused Values and Fused Self-Concept Values

Not every value a client states is freely chosen. A plused value is one the client has absorbed from others and performs to avoid guilt, shame, or rejection, and it produces a constrained, resentful, or perfectionistic quality of action rather than vitality. A fused self-concept value is one attached to a rigid identity rule ("I am the strong one," "I am the helper"), and it collapses the moment the identity is threatened. The therapist must assess for these by asking origin questions (where did this come from, who taught you this mattered, what would happen if you did not pursue it), by tracking the emotional quality when the value is discussed (vitality versus constriction), and by testing willingness to revise (could this value change if your life changed). Plused and fused values are not discarded; they are loosened, examined, and either freely re-chosen or released.

The decision point is how hard to push on a plused value. Pushing too hard can feel like the therapist is attacking the client's identity or community; not pushing at all colludes with avoidance. The resolution is to use defusion and perspective-taking rather than confrontation, and to let the client discover the quality of their own engagement.

### Convert Clarified Values Into Small, Repeatable, Observable Committed Actions

A clarified value that is not translated into behavior produces no change. The therapist must convert each value into a committed action that is specific, observable, small enough to be performed this week, repeatable, and linked to the willingness the action will require. "Being a loving parent" becomes "ten minutes of phone-free play with my child on Tuesday and Thursday, while making room for the urge to check my phone." The therapist should avoid grand, all-or-nothing actions that the client cannot sustain, should co-design the action with the client rather than prescribing it, and should build in a what-will-get-in-the-way forecast that names the avoidance that will arise and the acceptance move that will meet it.

The tradeoff is that small actions can feel trivial to a client who wants dramatic change, while large actions are more exciting but rarely completed. The resolution is to frame small actions as the practice of a direction rather than as the solution to a problem, and to build the action size gradually as the client's willingness repertoire grows.

### Link Committed Action to Willingness and Defusion

Committed action toward a meaningful value almost always activates the avoided private content that acceptance work targets, so the therapist must pair every committed action with a willingness plan. The therapist should forecast what thoughts, feelings, memories, or urges the action will stir up, should confirm the client has the defusion and acceptance skills to meet them, and should treat the homework as a willingness exercise in service of the value rather than as a behavior to be graded by outcome. When the client returns having avoided the action, the therapist should treat this as data about the avoidance barrier rather than as noncompliance, and should adjust the willingness support rather than exhorting harder.

### Track Values Drift, Perfectionism, and Self-Criticism Over the Course of Treatment

Values work can degrade in predictable ways. Values drift occurs when the client's stated values slowly shift to match what is comfortable or what others approve, and the therapist must periodically re-examine values rather than assuming they are stable. Perfectionism converts committed action into a performance to be judged, and the therapist must catch and defuse the self-evaluative frame. Self-criticism weaponizes values, turning "what matters to me" into "what I am failing at," and the therapist must explicitly teach the difference between values as a compass and values as a stick. The therapist should monitor the client's relationship to their own values across sessions and intervene when the relationship becomes punitive.

### Integrate Values Work With Safety, Capacity, and Scope

Values work is not appropriate as the primary intervention for clients in acute crisis, active suicidality, severe substance withdrawal, or acute psychosis, because these require stabilization and safety first. For clients with severe depression, the therapist may need to begin with very small, externally cued actions and with behavioral activation principles before the value itself can be felt as vital. The therapist must coordinate with prescribers and higher levels of care when committed action is beyond the client's current capacity, and must not use values as a rationale for pushing a fragile client past their window of tolerance.

## Common Traps

### Producing a Values List Without Behavioral Translation

The therapist runs a values clarification exercise, the client produces a thoughtful list of values, and both treat the completed list as the therapeutic product, because listing values feels meaningful and is much easier than designing and reviewing behavioral homework. The mechanism is that the value has been clarified but never operationalized, so it remains a verbal construction with no contact with the contingencies of the client's life, and nothing changes. The false signal is the client's articulate engagement with values language, which looks like progress but actually indicates the work has stalled at the level of content rather than function. The harm is a client who can talk fluently about what matters while living the same avoided life, leading to demoralization and the conclusion that therapy is just talk. The defense is to require that every clarified value be converted into a specific, small, repeatable committed action with a willingness plan, and to review homework every session.

### Confusing Goals for Values and Then Collapsing Direction on Achievement

The therapist accepts a goal as a value ("I want to get married," "I want the promotion") and builds committed action toward the goal, because goals are concrete and measurable and feel like real work, but once the goal is achieved or abandoned the direction disappears. The mechanism is that a goal is finite and outcome-dependent, so it cannot sustain an ongoing pattern of valued living, and the client who reaches the goal finds the value was never actually clarified and experiences the familiar post-achievement emptiness. The false signal is measurable progress toward the goal, which looks like effective therapy but is actually motion without direction. The harm is that the client's life narrows back to avoidance once the goal is resolved, and the underlying experiential avoidance is never addressed. The defense is to test every candidate value with the ongoing-pattern question and to articulate the value that the goal serves, so the direction survives the goal.

### Accepting Plused Values as Chosen and Reinforcing Avoidance

The therapist takes the client's stated values at face value and builds committed action around them, because the values sound admirable and the client endorses them, but the values are actually plused by family, culture, or guilt and the client experiences them as constriction rather than vitality. The mechanism is that plused values are maintained by avoidance of shame or rejection, so pursuing them reinforces the avoidance repertoire and the client becomes more rigid and exhausted rather than more flexible. The false signal is the client's compliance and the social respectability of the values, which look like engagement but actually indicate the work is serving avoidance. The harm is increased constriction, resentment, burnout, and a life that looks admirable on the outside and feels dead on the inside. The defense is to assess the origin and quality of each value, to track vitality versus constriction, and to use defusion to loosen plused values so they can be freely re-chosen or released.

### Allowing All-or-Nothing Committed Action That Guarantees Failure

The therapist and client design a committed action that is large, ambitious, and all-or-nothing ("I will exercise every day," "I will never avoid my partner again"), because dramatic commitments feel meaningful in session and the client is motivated in the moment, but the action is unsustainable and its failure becomes evidence that the value is impossible. The mechanism is that all-or-nothing actions exceed the client's willingness repertoire, so the first missed day is interpreted as total failure, the value is abandoned, and the avoidance repertoire is reinforced by the experience of failure. The false signal is the client's enthusiastic agreement to a big commitment, which looks like motivation but actually sets up the failure. The harm is that the client concludes they cannot change, the value is poisoned by the failed attempt, and the alliance is strained. The defense is to co-design small, repeatable, observable actions that the client can perform even on a hard day, to normalize imperfect engagement, and to build the action size gradually.

### Weaponizing Values as Self-Criticism

The therapist teaches values work and the client converts the values into a measuring stick for their own inadequacy, because the gap between valued living and current behavior is obvious and the client's self-critical repertoire is strong, so the value becomes a source of suffering rather than vitality. The mechanism is that values have been fused with a self-evaluative rule, so the value now functions as a stick rather than a compass, and the client avoids values work because it triggers shame. The false signal is the client's intense engagement with values language, which looks like commitment but actually indicates escalating self-punishment. The harm is increased shame, avoidance of the values that matter most, and sometimes worsening depression or self-harm. The defense is to explicitly teach the compass-versus-stick distinction, to defuse the self-critical voice, and to frame values as a direction to be approached with willingness rather than a standard to be met.

## Self-Check

- Did I distinguish values from goals, feelings, rules, and obligations, and did I test candidate values with the ongoing-pattern question?
- Did I assess whether each stated value is freely chosen versus plused by others or fused with a rigid self-concept, and did I track vitality versus constriction?
- Did I convert every clarified value into a specific, small, repeatable, observable committed action co-designed with the client?
- Did I pair each committed action with a willingness plan that forecasts the avoidance it will activate and the acceptance move that will meet it?
- Did I review committed action homework every session and treat avoidance of homework as data rather than as noncompliance?
- Did I monitor for values drift, perfectionism, and the weaponization of values as self-criticism, and did I intervene when the relationship to values became punitive?
- Did I sequence values work appropriately for the client's capacity, beginning with small externally cued actions for severely depressed clients?
- Did I stay within scope, prioritize stabilization and safety for clients in crisis, and coordinate with medical or higher levels of care when indicated?
