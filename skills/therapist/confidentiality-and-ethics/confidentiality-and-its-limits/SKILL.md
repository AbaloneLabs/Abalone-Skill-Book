# Confidentiality And Its Limits

Confidentiality is the foundation of the therapeutic relationship: clients will not disclose the material that therapy requires unless they trust that it will be held in confidence. But confidentiality is not absolute. It is limited by mandatory reporting duties, by duties to warn and protect, by court orders, by coordinated care arrangements, and by the involvement of parents and guardians in the treatment of minors. The therapist's judgment is to protect confidentiality as the default, to know and explain its limits in advance, to disclose only what is required and no more, and to handle the inevitable breaches in a way that minimizes harm to the client and the alliance. The recurring failures are failing to explain limits in advance, over-disclosing when a breach is required, treating coordinated care as a license for casual sharing, and mishandling the confidentiality of minors.

Use this skill when explaining confidentiality at intake, when a disclosure question arises, when handling third-party requests, when coordinating care, or when working with minors and families.

## Core Rules

### Explain Confidentiality And Its Limits At The Outset, In Plain Language

At intake, explain what confidentiality means, what it protects, and what its limits are, in language the client can understand. Cover the exceptions that apply in your jurisdiction: mandatory reporting of abuse and neglect, duty to warn and protect when there is a serious threat to an identifiable victim, responses to court orders, and coordination with other providers with consent. Invite questions. Do not bury the limits in fine print or minimize them, because a client who learns of a limit only when it is invoked experiences it as betrayal. Document that the explanation was given.

### Protect Confidentiality As The Default

Confidentiality is the default; disclosure requires a basis. Do not discuss clients outside consultation and supervision, do not share information with family members without consent, do not confirm or deny that a person is a client, do not leave messages that disclose content, and do not discuss cases in settings where they can be overheard. Treat the default as strong: the question is always whether there is a basis to disclose, not whether there is a basis to keep confidence.

### Disclose Only What Is Required, No More

When disclosure is required, by mandatory reporting, by duty to protect, by a court order, or by consent, disclose only what is necessary to fulfill the purpose. A report to child protective services includes the facts that support the suspicion, not the client's entire history. A coordination call with a prescriber includes what is clinically relevant, not the client's whole narrative. A response to a records request includes what is covered by the request and the authorization, not the whole record. Over-disclosure breaches confidentiality beyond what is required and harms the client.

### Obtain Specific, Informed Consent For Coordinated Care

Coordinated care with prescribers, primary care, schools, or other providers requires the client's specific, informed consent, documented in writing. Explain what will be shared, with whom, for what purpose, and that the client can revoke consent at any time. Do not treat a blanket release as license for casual sharing; share what is clinically relevant, document what was shared, and keep the client informed. Casual sharing among providers, without specific consent and without clinical relevance, erodes confidentiality and the alliance.

### Handle Third-Party Requests Carefully

Requests for client information from insurers, attorneys, employers, schools, or family members must be handled carefully. Verify the legal basis for the request, whether a signed authorization covers it, whether a court order compels it, and what the scope of the permitted disclosure is. When in doubt, consult before disclosing. Notify the client when a request is received, when possible, so they are aware and can consent or object. Do not disclose under pressure without verifying the legal basis.

### Manage Confidentiality With Minors And Families Carefully

Confidentiality with minors is layered: minors have confidentiality, but parents or guardians have rights to information, and the balance depends on the minor's age, maturity, the jurisdiction, and the type of treatment. At the outset, explain to both the minor and the parents what will be shared and what will be held in confidence, and renegotiate the understanding as treatment proceeds. Hold the minor's confidences except where safety requires disclosure, and when disclosure is necessary, tell the minor what will be shared and why, before sharing it. Do not let parents' requests for information override the minor's confidentiality without a basis, because doing so destroys the minor's trust and the therapy.

### Use The Least Identifying Information In Consultation And Supervision

Consultation and supervision are part of clinical care and do not require client consent, but they require discretion. Share the clinical material needed for the consultation, disguise identifying details where possible, and avoid sharing more than the consult requires. Supervision and consultation are not exceptions to confidentiality so much as bounded extensions of it, and the same principle of minimum necessary disclosure applies.

## Common Traps

### Failing To Explain Limits In Advance

When the therapist explains confidentiality at intake but glosses over or omits the limits, the client proceeds believing confidentiality is absolute, and the first time a limit is invoked, by a mandatory report, a duty to protect, or a records request, the client experiences it as betrayal and the alliance is damaged. The mechanism is that the therapist, wanting to reassure the client and to build trust, softens the limits, presenting confidentiality as stronger than it is. The harm is that the client's trust is built on a false premise, and the eventual disclosure, which could have been anticipated and explained, lands as a violation. Explaining the limits clearly at the outset, and documenting the explanation, is what allows the client to make informed decisions and what preserves the alliance when a limit is invoked.

### Over-Disclosing When A Breach Is Required

When a disclosure is required, the therapist, anxious about the duty and wanting to be thorough, may disclose more than is necessary, sharing the client's whole history or narrative when only the relevant facts are required. The mechanism is that the duty feels absolute and the therapist, wanting to comply fully, over-shares, treating the breach as license for comprehensive disclosure. The harm is that the client's confidentiality is breached beyond what the law required, more people know more than they needed to, and the alliance is damaged more than the necessary disclosure would have caused. Disclosing only what is required to fulfill the purpose, and documenting the basis for each piece of information shared, is what limits the harm.

### Casual Sharing In Coordinated Care

In settings where providers work together, a team, a clinic, a coordinated care arrangement, the culture may normalize casual discussion of clients, sharing details beyond what is clinically relevant, with people beyond those who need to know. The mechanism is that the team setting makes sharing feel like collaboration, and the routine erodes the discipline of minimum necessary disclosure. The harm is that confidentiality is breached routinely and invisibly, clients' details circulate beyond their consent, and the alliance is undermined if the client learns the extent of sharing. Maintaining the discipline of specific consent, clinical relevance, and minimum necessary disclosure, even in team settings, is what protects confidentiality.

### Mishandling Minor Confidentiality

When working with minors, the therapist may err in either direction: disclosing too much to parents, destroying the minor's trust and the therapy, or disclosing too little, withholding safety-relevant information that parents need. The mechanism is that the balance is genuinely difficult and the therapist, pressured by parents who want information or by the minor who wants secrecy, makes ad hoc decisions without a clear framework. The harm is either a minor who stops trusting the therapist and stops disclosing, or parents who are blindsided by a crisis they could have helped prevent. Establishing the framework at the outset, with both minor and parents, and renegotiating it as treatment proceeds, is what allows the therapy to serve the minor while keeping safety information flowing to those responsible for the minor's welfare.

### Confirming Or Denying Client Status

In social or professional settings, the therapist may be asked whether someone is a client, by a friend, a family member, or another professional, and the reflexive response, confirming or denying, breaches confidentiality even in the denial. The mechanism is that the question seems innocuous and the response seems harmless, but confirming that someone is a client discloses their status, and even a denial, "no, I'm not seeing them," can disclose by implication when the therapist would otherwise not comment. The harm is that the client's status, which they expected to be confidential, is disclosed without their consent. The standard response is to not confirm or deny, stating that confidentiality prevents comment on whether anyone is or is not a client.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I explain confidentiality and its limits at the outset, in plain language, and document the explanation?
- [ ] Am I treating confidentiality as the default, disclosing only when there is a clear basis?
- [ ] When disclosure is required, am I disclosing only what is necessary to fulfill the purpose, not more?
- [ ] Did I obtain specific, informed, written consent for coordinated care, and share only clinically relevant information?
- [ ] Am I handling third-party requests by verifying the legal basis, scope, and authorization before disclosing, and notifying the client?
- [ ] With minors, did I establish a clear framework with both minor and parents about what is shared and what is held, and renegotiate it as needed?
- [ ] Am I using the least identifying information in consultation and supervision, disguising details where possible?
- [ ] Am I avoiding confirming or denying client status in any setting?
