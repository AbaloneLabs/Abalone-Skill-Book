# Ethical Decision Making In Therapy

Therapy is dense with ethical decisions, and most of them are not the dramatic, headline cases but the everyday judgments where duties pull in different directions: confidentiality versus safety, client autonomy versus beneficence, the alliance versus the frame, the client's interests versus third-party demands. The therapist's judgment is not to find a rule that resolves each dilemma mechanically, because genuine dilemmas have no rule that resolves them cleanly, but to reason through the dilemma using a structured process, to consult, to document the reasoning, and to own the decision and its consequences. The recurring failures are resolving dilemmas by reflex or convenience rather than reasoning, failing to consult, failing to document the reasoning, and confusing the therapist's comfort with the client's interest.

Use this skill when facing an ethical dilemma, when duties conflict, when a decision feels uncomfortable or unclear, or when reviewing a past decision.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Ethical Dimension Of Ordinary Decisions

Most ethical failures are not in the dramatic cases but in ordinary decisions made without recognizing their ethical content. A decision to extend a session, to waive a fee, to accept a gift, to disclose to a family member, to continue treatment that is not working, to accept a client who is a friend of another client, each carries ethical weight. The first task is to notice the ethical dimension, to pause when a decision feels slightly off, and to ask what duties are at stake before acting.

### Identify The Duties And Interests In Tension

When a decision has ethical content, identify what is at stake. Common tensions include:

- confidentiality versus the duty to protect, when disclosure could prevent harm;
- client autonomy versus beneficence, when the client's choice conflicts with their welfare;
- the alliance versus the frame, when accommodating the client would blur a boundary;
- the client's interest versus third-party demands, such as insurers, courts, or employers;
- the therapist's values versus the client's values, when they diverge;
- duties to the profession, to other clients, to the public, and to the therapist's own welfare.

Name the tension explicitly, because a decision made without naming what is at stake is a decision made blindly.

### Use A Structured Reasoning Process

Ethical reasoning benefits from structure, not because structure guarantees the right answer but because it ensures the relevant considerations are weighed. A useful process:

- describe the situation factually, separating facts from assumptions;
- identify the duties, principles, and interests in tension;
- consult the relevant ethics code, law, and agency policy;
- consider the options, not only the obvious two, and the consequences of each;
- weigh the options against the duties, giving priority to the duties that cannot be compromised, such as safety;
- decide, act, and document the reasoning;
- review the outcome and learn from it.

Structure prevents the decision from being driven by reflex, convenience, or the loudest pressure.

### Consult Before Deciding On Hard Cases

Ethical decisions made alone are more likely to err and are harder to defend. Consultation with a supervisor, colleague, or ethics committee provides perspective, surfaces considerations the therapist missed, and shares the weight of the decision. Consultation is not a sign of uncertainty; it is the standard of care for difficult decisions. Document the consultation, who was consulted, what was discussed, and how it informed the decision.

### Give Safety Priority When Duties Conflict

When duties conflict, the duty to protect safety, the client's or others', generally takes priority over confidentiality, autonomy, and the alliance. This is the principle behind mandatory reporting, duty to warn, and involuntary hospitalization. The priority is not absolute and does not license over-riding other duties casually, but when a serious safety risk is present, the protective action is required even at the cost of other duties. Document the safety risk and the reasoning that justified the priority.

### Distinguish The Therapist's Comfort From The Client's Interest

A recurring source of ethical error is confusing the therapist's comfort, convenience, or needs with the client's interest. A decision to extend treatment that is not working may serve the therapist's financial or emotional needs; a decision to terminate a difficult client may serve the therapist's comfort; a decision to disclose may relieve the therapist's anxiety. Examine decisions for the therapist's stake, consult when the therapist's interests are engaged, and keep the client's interest as the primary criterion.

### Document The Reasoning, Not Just The Decision

In the event of a complaint or a review, the documentation that protects the therapist is the reasoning, not just the decision. Document the situation, the duties in tension, the consultation, the options considered, the decision, and the rationale. A note that records the decision without the reasoning looks arbitrary; a note that records the reasoning looks like a clinician doing the work.

### Know And Use Your Ethics Code And Law

Familiarity with your profession's ethics code and the relevant law is foundational, not because they resolve every dilemma but because they define the boundaries within which reasoning occurs. Know the code, know the law, consult them when dilemmas arise, and document the relevant provisions when they inform a decision. Ignorance of the code or the law is not a defense.

## Common Traps

### Resolving Dilemmas By Reflex Or Convenience

When a decision has ethical content and the therapist resolves it by the first reflex, by what is easiest, or by what avoids discomfort, the decision is shaped by convenience rather than reasoning. The mechanism is that genuine ethical reasoning is effortful and uncomfortable, so the path of least resistance, accommodating the client, avoiding the conflict, deferring the decision, presents itself as the natural response. The harm is that decisions made by convenience erode the frame, compromise duties, and accumulate into patterns that serve the therapist rather than the client. The corrective is to pause when a decision feels slightly off, to name the ethical dimension, and to reason through it.

### Failing To Consult On Hard Cases

A therapist who carries ethical decisions alone, either from confidence or from a sense that consulting is an admission of uncertainty, makes worse decisions and has no record of due diligence. The mechanism is that consulting feels like a delay or a concession, and the therapist, wanting to act decisively, decides alone. The harm is that the second perspective that would have caught an error is absent, and the decision, if challenged, has no consultation to support it. Consultation is the standard of care for difficult decisions, and documenting it is part of the decision.

### Confusing The Therapist's Needs With The Client's Interest

A therapist whose decisions consistently serve their own needs, financial, emotional, or reputational, while framed as client-centered, has confused their comfort with the client's interest. The mechanism is that the therapist's needs are often invisible to the therapist, so decisions that serve them feel like good practice. The harm is that the therapy is shaped by the therapist's welfare rather than the client's, the frame erodes, and the client's interests are subordinated. Examining decisions for the therapist's stake, and using consultation to surface what the therapist cannot see, are what prevent this.

### Under-Documenting The Reasoning

A therapist who documents the decision, "terminated treatment," "disclosed to family," without the reasoning, has a record that looks arbitrary and that provides no protection if the decision is challenged. The mechanism is that documenting the reasoning takes time and feels redundant when the decision is clear to the therapist, so the note records the action without the thought. The harm is that, in a complaint or review, the bare decision cannot be defended, because the reasoning that justified it is not in the record. Documenting the situation, the duties, the consultation, the options, and the rationale is what makes the decision defensible.

### Treating The Ethics Code As A Shield Rather Than A Guide

A therapist who consults the ethics code only to find a provision that justifies a preferred decision, or who treats compliance with the letter of the code as sufficient regardless of the spirit, uses the code as a shield rather than a guide. The mechanism is that the code's provisions can be read selectively to support a desired outcome, and compliance with the letter can coexist with violation of the spirit. The harm is that decisions that technically comply but ethically fail, because they serve the therapist's interest or compromise the client's, are defended as compliant. The code guides reasoning; it does not replace it, and the spirit of the principles, not just their letter, is what ethical practice requires.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I recognize the ethical dimension of the decision, rather than treating it as routine?
- [ ] Did I identify the duties, principles, and interests in tension, naming them explicitly?
- [ ] Did I use a structured reasoning process, considering multiple options and their consequences?
- [ ] Did I consult on the hard case, and document who was consulted and what was discussed?
- [ ] When duties conflicted, did I give safety priority and document the reasoning?
- [ ] Did I examine the decision for my own stake, distinguishing my comfort from the client's interest?
- [ ] Did I document the situation, the duties, the consultation, the options, and the rationale, not just the decision?
- [ ] Did I consult the ethics code and the law as guides to reasoning, not as shields for a preferred outcome?
