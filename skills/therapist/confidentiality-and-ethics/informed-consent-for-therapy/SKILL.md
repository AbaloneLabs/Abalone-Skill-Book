# Informed Consent For Therapy

Informed consent is often treated as a form signed at intake and filed, but genuine informed consent is an ongoing conversation in which the client understands what they are agreeing to, why, what the alternatives are, and what the risks and limits are. A signed form without understanding is procedural compliance, not informed consent, and it leaves the client unprepared for the difficulties of therapy, the boundaries of confidentiality, or the nature of the specific approach. The therapist's judgment is to make consent a genuine dialogue, to tailor it to the client's capacity and the treatment's specifics, to revisit it when the treatment changes, and to document not just the signature but the conversation.

Use this skill at intake, when starting a new modality or phase of treatment, when consent was incomplete, or when the client's capacity to consent is in question.

## Core Rules

### Cover The Elements Of Informed Consent Genuinely

Genuine informed consent covers the nature of the treatment, its expected benefits, its risks, the alternatives, and the right to withdraw. For therapy specifically, cover:

- the nature of the approach, what the therapy involves, how it works, and what a session looks like;
- the expected benefits, what the evidence suggests about outcomes for this problem, and the timeframe;
- the risks, including the likelihood of temporary discomfort, the possibility of feeling worse before feeling better, the limits of confidentiality, and the possibility that the treatment may not work;
- the alternatives, including other modalities, medication, no treatment, and referral;
- the practical frame, including frequency, duration, fee, cancellation policy, and contact between sessions;
- the right to withdraw, to ask questions, to seek a second opinion, and to refuse any intervention.

Cover these in conversation, not just in a form, and check the client's understanding.

### Tailor Consent To The Specific Modality And Phase

Different modalities and phases carry different risks and require different consent. Exposure-based treatment for anxiety or trauma involves deliberate, supervised experience of distress, and the client must understand and agree to that. Trauma processing carries a risk of temporary destabilization. Group therapy involves disclosure to other clients. Telehealth involves limits to privacy and to crisis response. EMDR, hypnosis, and other specific techniques have their own consent considerations. Consent for each distinct element of treatment, beyond the general consent for therapy, ensures the client knows what they are agreeing to.

### Revisit Consent When The Treatment Changes

Consent obtained at intake covers the treatment as initially understood. When the treatment changes, a new modality, a new focus, a shift from individual to group, a medication recommendation, a change in the frame, the consent should be revisited. The client who agreed to supportive therapy for depression has not thereby agreed to trauma processing; the client who agreed to weekly sessions has not thereby agreed to twice-weekly intensification. Re-consent at meaningful transitions, and document the conversation.

### Ensure Consent Is Genuine, Not Coerced

Consent is genuine when the client understands, is free to choose, and is not under undue pressure. Several factors can compromise genuineness: the power imbalance inherent in the therapeutic relationship, the client's acute distress at intake, pressure from family or courts to attend therapy, and the therapist's authority. Mitigate these by presenting alternatives honestly, including no treatment, by emphasizing the client's right to refuse, by checking understanding rather than assuming it, and by being alert to signs that the client is agreeing to please rather than from genuine choice. A client who consents to please the therapist has not genuinely consented.

### Address Capacity To Consent

Some clients have reduced capacity to consent, due to cognitive impairment, severe acute illness, intoxication, developmental disability, or being a minor. Capacity is decision-specific and time-specific: a client may lack capacity to consent during an acute crisis but regain it when stable. Assess capacity for the decision at hand, involve guardians or legally authorized representatives where appropriate, defer non-urgent treatment when capacity is temporarily impaired, and re-consent when capacity returns. Document the capacity assessment and the basis for the consent obtained.

### Document The Conversation, Not Just The Signature

The signed consent form is evidence that a form was signed, not that informed consent occurred. Document the conversation: what was explained, what questions the client asked, what concerns they raised, what alternatives were discussed, and how understanding was checked. In the event of a dispute, the documentation of the conversation, not the form, demonstrates that consent was genuine.

### Attend To Cultural And Linguistic Factors In Consent

Consent assumes a particular cultural model of the patient-clinician relationship, one in which the patient is informed and decides. Clients from cultures with different models, where the family decides, where the clinician's authority is expected to be followed, or where direct discussion of risks is taboo, may experience the consent process differently. Use professional interpretation when language is a barrier, attend to who the decision-maker is in the client's cultural framework, and adapt the consent process without abandoning its substance.

## Common Traps

### The Form Replacing The Conversation

When the therapist hands the client a consent form to sign, witnesses the signature, and files it, the consent is procedural but not informed, because the form's content was never discussed and the client's understanding was never checked. The mechanism is that the form satisfies the documentation requirement, so it reads as compliance, when it actually bypasses the conversation that makes consent genuine. The harm is that the client proceeds without understanding the treatment's risks, limits, or alternatives, and is unprepared for difficulties that arise, such as the limits of confidentiality or the discomfort of exposure. The corrective is to treat the form as documenting a conversation that occurred, not as a substitute for it.

### Glossing Over Risks To Reassure The Client

A therapist who wants to reassure a client at intake may minimize the risks of therapy, presenting it as uniformly beneficial and omitting the possibilities of temporary worsening, of uncovering difficult material, or of treatment not working. The mechanism is that reassurance feels caring and supports engagement, so minimizing risks reads as good practice. The harm is that the client, unprepared for the difficulties, experiences them as betrayal or as evidence the therapy is failing, and may drop out. Honest discussion of risks, framed within the overall benefit, prepares the client and strengthens rather than weakens engagement.

### One-Time Consent Treated As Permanent

Consent obtained at intake is sometimes treated as covering everything that follows, so that when the treatment changes, a new modality, a new focus, a frame change, the therapist proceeds without revisiting consent. The mechanism is that the initial consent feels sufficient and re-consenting feels redundant, so the therapist does not recognize that the treatment has moved beyond what was agreed. The harm is that the client undergoes interventions they did not consent to, which may feel violating, and the alliance is damaged when the client realizes they were not asked. Re-consenting at meaningful transitions is what keeps consent aligned with the actual treatment.

### Consent Under Pressure Or To Please

A client who consents because they feel they should, because the therapist recommended it, because family pressured them, or because they want to please the therapist, has not genuinely consented, and the consent may not hold when the treatment becomes difficult. The mechanism is that the power imbalance and the client's distress at intake make acquiescence easy, and the therapist, wanting engagement, may accept acquiescence as consent. The harm is that the client's agreement is fragile, withdrawal is likely when difficulty arises, and the client may undergo treatment they did not genuinely choose. Checking understanding, emphasizing the right to refuse and to withdraw, and presenting alternatives honestly are what support genuine consent.

### Failing To Consent Specific Modalities

A therapist who obtained general consent for therapy may begin a specific modality, exposure, EMDR, group therapy, without specific consent for that modality's distinct demands. The mechanism is that the general consent feels like it covers the treatment, and the specific modality feels like a technique within it, so the therapist does not recognize that the modality has its own consent requirements. The harm is that the client undergoes a demanding intervention, such as trauma exposure, without understanding and agreeing to its specific risks, which can feel violating and can cause harm. Specific consent for distinct modalities, beyond the general consent for therapy, is what ensures the client knows what they are agreeing to.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I cover the nature, benefits, risks, alternatives, practical frame, and right to withdraw in conversation, not just in a form?
- [ ] Did I tailor consent to the specific modality and phase, providing specific consent for distinct interventions?
- [ ] Did I revisit consent when the treatment changed, modality, focus, frame, or level of care?
- [ ] Did I ensure the consent was genuine, checking understanding, emphasizing the right to refuse, and presenting alternatives honestly?
- [ ] Did I assess the client's capacity to consent, involving guardians where appropriate and deferring non-urgent treatment when capacity was impaired?
- [ ] Did I document the consent conversation, including what was explained and what questions were asked, not just the signature?
- [ ] Did I attend to cultural and linguistic factors, using interpretation and respecting the client's decision-making framework?
- [ ] Did I avoid glossing over risks to reassure, and avoid treating one-time consent as permanent?
