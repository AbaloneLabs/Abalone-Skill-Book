# Initial Interview And History Taking

The first session does two jobs at once that often pull in opposite directions: it must gather enough structured information to build a safe formulation and detect risk, and it must begin a therapeutic relationship that the client experiences as human rather than interrogational. Therapists lean toward one failure or the other depending on temperament and training. The over-structured intake reads like an insurance form, leaves the client feeling unheard, and suppresses exactly the spontaneous material that would have revealed the real problem. The under-structured intake feels warm and rapport-rich but misses a hidden suicidality, a medication interaction, a trauma history, or a substance pattern that will surface later as a crisis or a treatment failure. The skilled intake is not a middle ground; it is a deliberate sequencing of safety-critical screening first, story-building second, and deferred detail later, all while explicitly contracting the client into the process.

Use this skill before and during the first one to three sessions with a new client, when completing an intake evaluation, or when revisiting history because earlier collection was incomplete.

## Core Rules

### Screen For Acute Risk And Medical Red Flags Before Everything Else

Before investing in a full narrative, establish that the client is safe enough to wait until the next session. A client who is actively suicidal, acutely psychotic, medically unstable, intoxicated, or in an unsafe living situation needs a different first-session path than history-taking.

Screen first for:

- current suicidal ideation, plan, intent, means, and recent attempts or rehearsals;
- current homicidal ideation or risk to identifiable others;
- acute psychosis, command hallucinations, or severe disorganization that prevents basic safety;
- medical conditions that could be causing or worsening the presentation, including head injury, thyroid disease, seizure, delirium, or recent overdose;
- intoxication or withdrawal that requires medical rather than psychological management;
- active abuse, exploitation, or trafficking, especially in minors, elders, or dependent adults.

If any of these are present, the intake pivots to risk management, safety planning, referral, or crisis response, and the full history is deferred until the client is stable. Document the screen and the rationale for deferral.

### Tell The Client What You Are Doing And Why

An intake that the client does not understand as a collaborative process becomes a one-sided interrogation. Explain, in plain language, what the intake is for, what you will ask, how long it will take, how confidentiality works, and what will happen with the information. Invite the client to ask questions, to decline to answer anything, and to correct the record. This contracting reduces the power imbalance, increases the accuracy of disclosure, and is itself the beginning of the therapeutic alliance.

### Prioritize The Presenting Problem And The Client's Own Words

Begin with the client's reason for seeking help now, in their own language. The presenting problem is the entry point to the formulation, the strongest predictor of what the client will engage with, and the basis for treatment goals. Map the presenting problem in its current form: onset, course, triggers, severity, what makes it better or worse, what the client has already tried, and what the client hopes will change. Resist the pull to jump from presenting complaint to a diagnostic label before you have heard the phenomenology.

### Collect History Across Predictable Domains, But Sequence It

A complete history covers identifiable domains, but trying to cover all of them in one session overwhelms the client and produces thin, unreliable answers. Know your domains and decide what is essential now versus what can be gathered over the first few sessions.

Core domains include:

- presenting problem and current symptoms, with timeline and functional impact;
- psychiatric history, including prior diagnoses, hospitalizations, medication, therapy, and response;
- substance use, including alcohol, cannabis, opioids, stimulants, prescription misuse, and caffeine and nicotine;
- medical history, current medications, allergies, head injuries, and recent medical care;
- developmental history, especially for children and for adults with suspected developmental or early-onset conditions;
- family psychiatric and medical history;
- social history, including relationships, living situation, employment, finances, education, and legal involvement;
- trauma and adverse experiences, across the lifespan, gathered with pacing and consent;
- cultural, religious, and spiritual identity and how these relate to the problem and to help-seeking;
- strengths, coping, protective factors, and prior successes.

Gather trauma history with care. Trauma disclosure on demand, early in the relationship, can destabilize a client who is not yet resourced to process it. It is often clinically wiser to establish safety and rapport, note that you will return to difficult material, and screen for trauma broadly without forcing detail.

### Use A Mix Of Open And Structured Questions

Open questions elicit the client's phenomenology and story; structured questions ensure you do not miss specific, high-stakes items. A useful rhythm is to open each domain with a broad question, follow the client's lead, then fill gaps with direct questions before moving on. Avoid leading questions that telegraph the answer you want, especially about trauma, substance use, and suicidality, where leading questions suppress accurate disclosure.

### Triangulate History When Reliability Is In Doubt

Self-report at intake is often incomplete, not because clients are dishonest, but because shame, memory, intoxication, dissociation, or a desire to present well all distort recall. When the presentation is complex, high-risk, or inconsistent, seek collateral with the client's written consent: prior treaters, family, schools, or other providers. Explain to the client why collateral helps and what it will and will not change. For minors and dependent adults, collateral is often essential rather than optional.

### Close The Intake With A Shared Summary And Next Steps

Do not end the first session having only collected data. Summarize what you heard, in the client's words where possible, reflect it back, and check for accuracy. Share your tentative impression without over-committing to a diagnosis, describe the next steps, and give the client a chance to respond, correct, or add. This closing converts the intake from an extraction into a shared understanding and sets up collaborative treatment planning.

### Recognize When The Client Does Not Fit Your Setting Or Competence

Intake is also a gatekeeping function. Some clients need a higher level of care, a different modality, or a clinician with specific expertise. If the client is outside your competence or the setting's capacity, say so honestly, explain why, and provide a warm referral rather than starting treatment you cannot sustain. Document the rationale.

## Common Traps

### The Questionnaire Intake

When the therapist works through a standardized intake form item by item, reading questions and recording answers, the session produces a complete-looking record but no therapeutic relationship and often no real understanding of the client. The mechanism of harm is that the form's structure, designed for completeness and billing, displaces the clinician's curiosity, so the therapist never asks the follow-up questions that would have revealed the actual problem. The client experiences the session as cold and administrative, decides the therapist does not care, and either does not return or returns but withholds the very material the therapy needs. Worse, the completed form creates a false sense of thoroughness: the record looks comprehensive, so no one flags that the high-stakes items, suicidality, substance use, trauma, were checked off with one-word answers that were never clinically explored. The form is a scaffold, not the session; the session is the conversation the form is meant to support.

### Missing Risk Because The Narrative Was Compelling

A client who tells a vivid, engaging story about a relationship breakup or workplace conflict can lead the therapist deep into that narrative for an entire session, leaving no room for the structured risk screen. The compelling story feels like good rapport-building, and the therapist leaves the session satisfied, having missed that the client also described hopeless ideation, a recent attempt, or access to means, embedded in the narrative and never followed up. The harm is that the very clients most in need of a risk assessment are often the ones whose storytelling most effectively distracts from it, and the warm, attentive therapist is the one most vulnerable to being drawn in. Risk screening must be scheduled into the session structure, not left to whatever time remains after the client finishes talking, because the client will rarely volunteer the most dangerous material unprompted.

### Forcing Trauma Disclosure Too Early

In a well-intentioned effort to be thorough, a therapist who screens for trauma at the first session and then presses for detail can re-traumatize a client who has no coping resources yet established. The client discloses under perceived pressure, experiences flooding or dissociation in or after the session, and associates the therapy itself with loss of control. The harm is twofold: the client may not return, and if they do, they have learned that this therapist is not safe with the hardest material. The trap is seductive because thorough trauma history is genuinely important, so pressing for it feels clinically diligent rather than harmful. The correct stance is to screen for the presence and broad nature of trauma, acknowledge it, and explicitly defer detail until safety, coping, and alliance are established, telling the client why you are not asking more now.

### Anchoring On The First Diagnosis Suggested

When a prior provider, a referral source, or the client themselves offers a diagnosis at intake, the therapist can anchor on it and then gather history selectively to confirm it, while discounting inconsistent evidence. A client referred with "depression" may also have undetected bipolar disorder, substance use, or an underlying medical condition, each of which would change the treatment substantially. The mechanism is confirmation bias dressed as efficiency: the label is already there, so the therapist spends the session filling in its supporting details rather than testing it. The harm is a treatment plan built on an unverified assumption that may be wrong, sometimes dangerously so, as when antidepressants are prescribed or recommended for a client whose real diagnosis is bipolar depression. Treat every intake diagnosis, including your own first impression, as a hypothesis to be tested against the full history, not a conclusion.

### Treating Intake As A One-Time Event

Intake is often treated as a discrete first session after which the history is "done," but reliable history emerges over the first several sessions as trust builds and as the client's state fluctuates. A client who minimized substance use at session one because they were ashamed may disclose it at session four; a client who was guarded about family may reveal abuse once they trust the therapist. The trap is that the intake note, written once, becomes the authoritative record, and later material is never integrated into the formulation. The harm is a treatment plan that stays frozen around an incomplete picture. Treat intake as a process across the first several sessions, and update the formulation and plan as new history emerges.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I screen for acute suicide risk, violence risk, psychosis, medical red flags, intoxication or withdrawal, and active abuse before proceeding with the full history?
- [ ] Did I explain the purpose, structure, confidentiality limits, and client rights of the intake before asking questions?
- [ ] Did I begin with and prioritize the client's presenting problem in their own words, rather than leading with diagnosis?
- [ ] Did I cover the essential domains, and explicitly defer non-urgent domains to later sessions rather than rushing or skipping them?
- [ ] Did I gather trauma history with pacing and consent, screening for presence without forcing destabilizing detail?
- [ ] Did I use open questions to elicit phenomenology and structured questions to ensure high-stakes items were not missed, avoiding leading questions on sensitive topics?
- [ ] Did I seek collateral with consent when reliability was in doubt or the presentation was complex or high-risk?
- [ ] Did I close with a shared summary, a tentative impression, and clear next steps, giving the client a chance to correct or add?
- [ ] Did I assess whether the client fits my competence and setting, and arrange a warm referral if not?
- [ ] Did I treat intake as an ongoing process across the first several sessions, planning to update the formulation as new history emerges?
