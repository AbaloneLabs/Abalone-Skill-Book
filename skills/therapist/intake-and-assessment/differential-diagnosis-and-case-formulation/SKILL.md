# Differential Diagnosis And Case Formulation

Diagnosis and formulation are different acts that agents often conflate. A diagnosis is a categorical label drawn from a classification system, useful for communication, billing, and selecting evidence-based treatments, but it is a reduction that hides as much as it reveals. A formulation is the clinician's working model of why this person, with this history, in this context, is suffering in this way now, and what would help. A diagnosis without a formulation leads to protocol-driven treatment that ignores the individual; a formulation without a diagnosis risks missing a condition that requires specific, evidence-based care. The therapist's judgment is to hold both, keep both provisional, and revise both as new data arrives, while never letting the label substitute for understanding the person in front of them.

Use this skill when building a diagnostic impression or clinical formulation after intake, when a client's presentation does not fit the obvious category, when treatment is not working and the formulation may be wrong, or when deciding whether to refer for specialized evaluation.

## Core Rules

### Build The Formulation Across Multiple Explanatory Levels

A formulation that explains a client's depression only as "chemical imbalance" or only as "childhood trauma" is incomplete and leads to narrow treatment. A strong formulation integrates several levels of explanation and shows how they interact.

Cover at minimum:

- predisposing factors, the vulnerabilities that made this problem more likely, including genetics, temperament, early adversity, and family history;
- precipitating factors, what triggered the current episode or presentation, the stressors or losses or changes that shifted the client from coping to not coping;
- perpetuating factors, what is keeping the problem going now, including ongoing stressors, maladaptive coping, substance use, relationship patterns, avoidance, and unhelpful beliefs;
- protective factors, the strengths, relationships, values, and past successes that buffer the client and can be leveraged in treatment;
- the functional analysis, what the symptom does for the client, what need it meets, what it protects against, because symptoms often persist because they serve a purpose.

This five-factor structure, the 5 Ps, is one framework among several; the point is to think across levels rather than locating the cause at one.

### Generate A Genuine Differential, Not A Single Hypothesis

Before settling on a working diagnosis, list the plausible alternatives and what would distinguish them. A differential forces you to consider what else this could be and protects against premature closure.

Common differential considerations include:

- whether symptoms reflect a primary mood disorder, a substance-induced condition, or a mood disorder secondary to a medical condition, each of which demands different treatment;
- whether anxiety symptoms reflect an anxiety disorder, trauma, hyperthyroidism, caffeine or stimulant use, or a withdrawal state;
- whether psychotic symptoms reflect a primary psychotic disorder, severe depression or mania with psychotic features, substance intoxication or withdrawal, or an organic cause;
- whether attention and concentration problems reflect ADHD, depression, anxiety, sleep deprivation, trauma, or substance effects;
- whether personality patterns reflect a personality disorder, an untreated mood or anxiety disorder, a trauma response, or a culturally normative style.

For each alternative, note the evidence for and against, and what further information would resolve the question.

### Rule Out Medical And Substance Causes Before Settling On A Primary Psychiatric Diagnosis

Many psychiatric presentations have medical or substance causes or contributors that, if missed, lead to treating a symptom while the underlying condition worsens. A client whose "depression" is hypothyroidism, whose "panic" is hyperthyroidism or cardiac arrhythmia, whose "psychosis" is an autoimmune encephalitis, or whose "anxiety" is untreated sleep apnea will not improve with therapy alone.

Ask about and consider:

- recent medical workup, current medical conditions, and current medications including over-the-counter and herbal supplements;
- substance use and its timing relative to symptom onset and fluctuation;
- the need for medical referral or laboratory work when a medical contributor is plausible;
- whether symptoms began after starting or stopping a medication or substance.

When in doubt, refer for medical evaluation. It is better to over-refer for a medical workup than to treat a medical condition as psychiatric for months.

### Distinguish State From Trait

A client who appears chronically angry, dependent, or distrustful during a crisis may be showing a state, a temporary reaction to acute distress, rather than a trait, a stable personality pattern. Diagnosing a personality disorder during an acute crisis, an active mood episode, or early in treatment pathologizes a temporary state and can damage the alliance. Observe the client across time and contexts, look for evidence that the pattern is stable and pervasive rather than situational, and defer personality diagnosis until the acute picture has settled, unless the pattern is clearly long-standing and central to the formulation.

### Match The Formulation To The Client's Readiness And Language

A formulation is a clinical tool, but it is also something you share with the client, and how you frame it matters. A client who hears their suffering explained as a "personality disorder" may feel blamed and labeled; the same formulation, framed as long-standing patterns of relating that developed for understandable reasons and can be changed, may be received as validating and hopeful. Decide how much of the formulation to share, in what language, and when, based on the client's readiness, the strength of the alliance, and the client's own framework for understanding their experience. Some clients want a diagnostic label and find it relieving; others find labels reductive and alienating. Ask.

### Keep The Formulation Living And Revisable

A formulation written at intake and never revisited becomes a fossil. As treatment progresses, new history emerges, the client's state changes, the response to intervention provides diagnostic information, and the formulation should evolve. Build in regular points to revisit the formulation: when treatment is not progressing, when new information contradicts the working model, when the client's presentation changes substantially, and at planned intervals such as every several sessions. A formulation that cannot be revised when the evidence changes is not a formulation but a fixed belief.

### Know When To Refer For Specialized Evaluation

Some diagnostic questions exceed generalist competence and require specialized evaluation: suspected autism spectrum disorder in adults, complex dissociative disorders, neurocognitive disorders, bipolar disorder when the diagnosis would change medication strategy, and eating disorders requiring medical monitoring. Recognize the limits of your training, refer for psychological testing, psychiatric evaluation, or specialty consultation when the stakes are high or the picture is unclear, and integrate the specialist's findings into your formulation rather than treating referral as abandonment of the diagnostic task.

## Common Traps

### Premature Closure On The Obvious Diagnosis

When a client presents with classic symptoms of a common condition, the therapist may settle on the diagnosis quickly and then stop looking, missing co-occurring or underlying conditions that are driving the presentation. A client who meets criteria for depression may also have untreated bipolar disorder, active substance use, an eating disorder, or a medical condition, any of which would be worsened by standard depression treatment. The mechanism of harm is that the obvious diagnosis is partially correct, so it confirms itself: the symptoms do fit, the therapist feels they have understood the case, and the search stops before the less obvious but more important diagnosis is found. The treatment then targets a symptom of the real problem while the real problem progresses. Generating a genuine differential, even when the first diagnosis seems obvious, is what prevents this, because it forces the therapist to actively test and rule out alternatives rather than assume them away.

### Treating Substance Use As A Symptom Rather Than A Cause

When a client presents with depression or anxiety and also drinks heavily or uses substances, the therapist may treat the mood symptoms as primary and the substance use as self-medication, assuming that treating the mood disorder will resolve the use. But substance use can be the primary driver of the mood symptoms, through intoxication, withdrawal, and the life consequences of use, and mood will not improve while use continues. The harm is that weeks or months of therapy are spent processing emotions while the physiological and behavioral engine of the symptoms, the substance use, is never directly addressed. The trap is seductive because the self-medication narrative is often partly true and is empathic rather than punitive, so it feels like good clinical formulation. The correct approach is to assess the temporal and causal relationship between use and symptoms, consider a period of sustained abstinence as a diagnostic test, and address substance use directly rather than assuming it will resolve as a downstream effect.

### Pathologizing Normal Cultural Or Developmental Variation

A client whose experiences fall outside the therapist's own cultural or developmental frame can be misdiagnosed when normative variation is read as pathology. Spiritual or religious experiences, culturally normative expressions of distress, grief responses, or the effects of systemic oppression can be misread as symptoms of psychosis, a mood disorder, or a personality disorder. The mechanism is that the therapist's diagnostic categories are built on a particular cultural baseline, and presentations that deviate from that baseline trigger diagnostic labels even when they are understood and normative within the client's own context. The harm is a stigmatizing misdiagnosis that pathologizes the client's identity and culture, drives them out of treatment, and may follow them in their record. The corrective is to assess whether the experience is normative within the client's cultural, religious, and developmental context before labeling it pathological, and to seek consultation when cultural distance is significant.

### Confusing Formulation With Excuse

A rich formulation that explains why a client developed a pattern can slide into a stance that excuses the pattern's current harm, especially in areas like interpersonal aggression, substance use, or neglect of responsibilities. The therapist, having understood the developmental origins of the behavior, may become reluctant to challenge it, colluding with the client's view that the behavior is understandable and therefore acceptable. The harm is that the therapy becomes validating but not change-oriented, the client's harmful patterns continue, and others affected by them, partners, children, colleagues, are not protected. Understanding why a pattern exists does not mean endorsing it; the formulation should explain the behavior's origins and function while still supporting the client in changing it, holding both compassion and accountability.

### The Static Formulation

A formulation built at intake and treated as fixed becomes increasingly wrong as the client changes and as new information emerges, yet it continues to guide treatment because no one revisits it. The mechanism is that the original formulation is documented, shared with the care team, and treated as the case's official understanding, so subsequent data is interpreted to fit it rather than to test it. Treatment that is not working is explained as client non-compliance rather than as evidence that the formulation is wrong. The harm is months of ineffective treatment directed at the wrong target, while the client's actual condition, which may have evolved or which was mischaracterized from the start, goes unaddressed. Building in scheduled formulation reviews, and treating treatment non-response as a signal to re-examine the formulation rather than to blame the client, is what keeps the formulation honest.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the formulation integrate predisposing, precipitating, perpetuating, and protective factors, plus a functional analysis, rather than locating cause at a single level?
- [ ] Did I generate a genuine differential with evidence for and against each alternative, rather than settling on the first plausible diagnosis?
- [ ] Did I actively consider and rule out medical and substance causes before settling on a primary psychiatric diagnosis, and refer for medical evaluation when indicated?
- [ ] Did I distinguish state from trait, and defer personality diagnosis during acute crisis unless the pattern is clearly long-standing?
- [ ] Did I consider how to share the formulation with the client in language that fits their readiness and framework, rather than imposing diagnostic labels unhelpfully?
- [ ] Is the formulation explicitly provisional, with planned points for revision when new data arrives or treatment does not progress?
- [ ] Did I recognize when the diagnostic question exceeds my competence and refer for specialized evaluation rather than guessing?
- [ ] Did I assess whether experiences I am considering pathological are normative within the client's cultural, religious, or developmental context?
