# Screening For Comorbid Medical And Psychiatric Conditions

Therapists are not physicians, but they are often the first clinician to see a client whose psychological symptoms have a medical cause, whose psychiatric presentation has medical comorbidity, or whose primary need is a different level or type of care. The therapist's job is not to diagnose medical conditions but to screen broadly, recognize the signals that something lies outside psychotherapy's scope, and route the client to the right evaluation without abandoning them. The recurring failure is narrow screening: the therapist screens for the condition they treat and misses the conditions they do not, leaving medical illness, untreated bipolar disorder, sleep disorders, eating disorders, and neurodevelopmental conditions to worsen while psychotherapy proceeds against the wrong target.

Use this skill during intake and early treatment when selecting screens, interpreting results, coordinating care with other providers, or deciding when a referral is needed.

## Core Rules

### Screen Broadly, Not Only For What You Treat

A therapist whose practice is anxiety should still screen for depression, substance use, trauma, bipolar disorder, eating pathology, sleep disorders, and risk, because comorbidity is the rule, not the exception, and an un-screened comorbidity will undermine the treatment of the presenting problem. Build a standard screening set into intake that covers the common and high-stakes comorbidities, regardless of the presenting complaint.

A reasonable baseline screen set includes:

- depression, with a validated instrument such as the PHQ-9, including the suicide item;
- generalized anxiety, with the GAD-7, which also screens for panic and PTSD;
- bipolar disorder, with the MDQ, because missed bipolar disorder is dangerous when antidepressants are involved;
- substance use, with the AUDIT or AUDIT-C for alcohol and a drug use screen;
- trauma and PTSD, with the PCL-5 or a trauma exposure checklist;
- eating pathology, with the SCOFF or EAT-26, especially in adolescents and young adults;
- suicide risk, with the C-SSRS or a structured risk assessment.

Adapt the set to the population and setting, but do not narrow it to your specialty.

### Interpret Screens As Signals, Not Diagnoses

A screening instrument produces a score that indicates probability, not a diagnosis. A positive screen means the area deserves clinical follow-up; a negative screen does not rule out the condition, especially when the clinical picture suggests it. A client who scores below cutoff on a depression screen but describes anhedonia, hopelessness, and recent weight loss is depressed regardless of the score. Interpret every screen in conjunction with the clinical interview, never in isolation, and document the reasoning when you override a screen result.

### Recognize The Medical Conditions That Mimic Or Worsen Psychiatric Symptoms

Certain medical conditions present with, worsen, or mimic psychiatric symptoms and must be considered when the presentation is atypical, new, or treatment-resistant:

- thyroid disease, hyper and hypo, presenting as anxiety, depression, or mood instability;
- vitamin deficiencies, especially B12 and D, contributing to depression and cognitive symptoms;
- anemia and chronic infection presenting as fatigue and depression;
- sleep disorders, especially obstructive sleep apnea, presenting as depression, anxiety, and cognitive impairment;
- neurological conditions, including seizure disorders, multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's, and early dementia, presenting with mood, anxiety, or cognitive changes;
- autoimmune and post-infectious conditions, including autoimmune encephalitis and long COVID, presenting with new psychiatric symptoms;
- medication side effects and interactions, including steroid-induced mood changes and psychiatric symptoms from many common drugs.

When the presentation is new in onset, atypical, late-onset, or not responding to appropriate treatment, raise the question of a medical contributor and recommend medical evaluation.

### Coordinate With Prescribers And Primary Care Actively

When a client has a prescriber or primary care provider, the therapist is part of a team, not a parallel silo. With the client's written consent, establish communication: share your clinical observations, especially changes in mood, risk, side effects, and response, and request the prescriber's view on diagnosis and medication strategy. Medication response and side effects are clinical data that shape the formulation, and the therapist often sees the client more frequently than the prescriber and is best positioned to notice early changes. Do not assume the prescriber knows what you know; coordinate deliberately.

### Refer For Specialty Evaluation When The Stakes Are High

Some conditions warrant referral for specialized evaluation rather than generalist management:

- suspected bipolar disorder, because the diagnosis changes medication strategy and misdiagnosis carries risk;
- suspected autism or ADHD in adults, where specialized assessment improves accuracy;
- complex dissociative disorders, which require specific expertise;
- eating disorders with medical instability, which require medical and nutritional management;
- neurocognitive disorders and suspected dementia, requiring neurological and neuropsychological evaluation;
- sleep disorders, requiring sleep study and medical management.

A referral is not abandonment. Maintain the therapeutic relationship during the evaluation, integrate the specialist's findings into your formulation, and clarify roles so the client experiences coordinated care rather than being bounced between providers.

### Re-Screen At Intervals, Not Only At Intake

Conditions emerge over the course of treatment. A client who did not have substance use at intake may develop it; bipolar disorder may reveal itself through a hypomanic episode triggered by antidepressant treatment; an eating disorder may emerge under new stress. Build re-screening into routine care at planned intervals and whenever the clinical picture changes, not only at intake.

## Common Traps

### The Single-Condition Screen

When the therapist screens only for the presenting complaint, comorbid conditions that would change the treatment plan go undetected and undermine care. A client presenting with anxiety who is also drinking heavily, has untreated bipolar traits, and is in an abusive relationship receives anxiety treatment that cannot work because the comorbidities are driving the presentation. The mechanism of harm is that the narrow screen feels efficient and focused, so it reads as good clinical practice, while the un-screened conditions silently shape the outcome. The client does not improve, the therapist attributes this to treatment resistance or client factors, and the real drivers are never identified. Broad screening at intake, even for conditions the therapist does not treat, is what prevents this, because the goal is to understand the whole case, not only the part that fits the referral.

### Treating A Positive Screen As A Diagnosis

A high score on a screening instrument can be treated as a confirmed diagnosis, leading the therapist to build a treatment plan around a condition that the full clinical picture does not support. A client who scores high on a bipolar screen due to substance-induced mood instability, borderline traits, or simple endorsement of common experiences receives a bipolar label that shapes medication recommendations and the client's self-understanding, sometimes incorrectly. The harm is a misdiagnosis propagated through the record and the treatment system, with the screen's probabilistic result treated as certainty. Screens generate hypotheses to be confirmed or disconfirmed by clinical interview and longitudinal observation; they do not diagnose on their own.

### Missing Bipolar Disorder Behind Depression

A client who presents with depression may have bipolar disorder that is missed because the screening focused on current depressive symptoms and never asked about past hypomanic or manic episodes. The danger is specific and severe: antidepressant treatment without a mood stabilizer can trigger manic switches, mixed states, and rapid cycling, worsening the very condition being treated. The mechanism is that hypomania often feels good to the client, who does not report it as a symptom, and the therapist, focused on the depression, does not ask. The harm is iatrogenic worsening through well-intentioned treatment. Screening every depressed client for past hypomanic and manic episodes, and communicating positive screens to the prescriber, is what prevents this avoidable harm.

### Failing To Route Medical Symptoms To Medical Care

When a client describes symptoms with a plausible medical cause, fatigue, weight change, neurological symptoms, cognitive change, the therapist may integrate them into a psychological formulation rather than recommending medical evaluation, because the symptoms fit the psychological narrative. The mechanism is that psychological explanations are available and satisfying, so the therapist does not feel the gap that would trigger a medical referral. The harm is that a treatable medical condition progresses while the client receives therapy for a symptom of it. Any new, atypical, or treatment-resistant symptom with a plausible medical cause warrants a recommendation for medical evaluation, documented clearly, even when a psychological explanation is also available, because both can be true and the medical cause may be the more dangerous one.

### The Referral That Becomes Abandonment

When the therapist refers a client for specialty evaluation and does not maintain contact, the client may experience the referral as being passed off, especially if the specialist has a long wait, the client has to repeat their story, or the two providers do not communicate. The client may not follow through on the referral, may drop out of care entirely, or may return to the therapist only after a crisis. The harm is a gap in care during a vulnerable period and the erosion of the alliance. A warm referral includes explaining why the evaluation helps, what to expect, maintaining the therapy relationship during the wait, obtaining consent to coordinate with the specialist, and integrating the findings, so the client experiences continuity rather than handoff.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I screen broadly at intake for common and high-stakes comorbidities, not only for the presenting complaint?
- [ ] Did I interpret screen results as signals to be confirmed by clinical interview, not as diagnoses, and document reasoning when overriding a screen?
- [ ] Did I consider medical conditions that mimic or worsen psychiatric symptoms, especially when the presentation is new, atypical, late-onset, or treatment-resistant?
- [ ] Did I recommend medical evaluation for symptoms with a plausible medical cause, rather than absorbing them into a psychological formulation?
- [ ] Did I screen every depressed client for past hypomanic and manic episodes and communicate positive screens to the prescriber?
- [ ] Did I establish coordinated communication with the prescriber and primary care provider, with the client's consent?
- [ ] Did I refer for specialty evaluation when the stakes were high or the picture unclear, while maintaining the therapeutic relationship?
- [ ] Did I build re-screening into routine care at planned intervals and when the clinical picture changes, not only at intake?
