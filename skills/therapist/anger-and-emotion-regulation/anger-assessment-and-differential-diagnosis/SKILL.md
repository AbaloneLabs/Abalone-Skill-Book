# Anger Assessment and Differential Diagnosis

Anger assessment and differential diagnosis require the therapist to resist the most common and most damaging error in this work: treating anger as a unitary problem to be managed rather than as a symptom whose meaning, cause, and risk vary enormously across presentations. The judgment problem is that the angry client is often the mandated, the distressed, or the defensive, and the visible anger crowds out the assessment, so the therapist reaches for the anger management protocol before understanding what the anger is, where it comes from, and what it is doing. Anger may be the primary presentation (the intermittent explosive disorder, the chronic hostility), the symptom of another condition (the depression, the anxiety, the trauma, the substance use, the bipolar, the personality disorder, the medical condition), the expression of the unmet need or the injustice, or the behavior of the risk. The failure to differentiate produces the treatment of the symptom while the cause worsens, the under-assessment of the violence risk, and the harm to the client and the others. The therapist's freedom is real, but the differential diagnosis and the risk assessment are required, and the high-risk presentations require the escalation. This skill covers the assessment, the differential, the risk, and the referral. Given the safety and the clinical stakes, this guidance is conservative: assess thoroughly, differentiate the cause, assess the risk, and escalate the high-risk.

## Core Rules

### Treat Anger as a Symptom, Not a Diagnosis

The anger must be treated as the symptom, not the diagnosis, because the anger is the presentation that may reflect the many causes (the depression, the anxiety, the trauma, the substance, the bipolar, the personality, the medical), and the treatment of the anger without the differential diagnosis treats the symptom while the cause worsens. The therapist must conduct the thorough differential assessment (the onset, the course, the triggers, the context, the associated symptoms, the substance use, the medical history, the trauma, the family history), and must formulate the anger in the context of the whole presentation. The anger that is the symptom of the depression requires the depression treatment; the anger that is the symptom of the trauma requires the trauma treatment; the anger that is the symptom of the substance requires the substance treatment. The therapist must not deliver the generic anger management without the differential.

The tradeoff is the symptom-focused approach (the efficient, the protocol-driven, but the ineffective for the underlying cause) versus the differential approach (the thorough, but the time-consuming). The resolution is to conduct the differential, to formulate, and to treat the cause.

### Differentiate Anger From Aggression and Hostility

The anger (the emotional state), the aggression (the behavior intended to harm), and the hostility (the attitudinal set) must be differentiated, because the treatment differs: the anger (the emotion) requires the emotion regulation, the aggression (the behavior) requires the behavior management and the risk assessment, and the hostility (the attitude) requires the cognitive work. The therapist must assess the each: the anger (the frequency, the intensity, the duration, the triggers), the aggression (the verbal, the physical, the property, the targets, the consequences), and the hostility (the cynicism, the attribution of the hostile intent, the grudge-holding). The aggression requires the immediate risk assessment and the safety planning, and the anger and the hostility require the targeted treatment.

The trap is the conflation (the treating the anger when the aggression is the concern), which under-assesses the risk. The defense is to differentiate the anger, the aggression, and the hostility, to assess the each, and to prioritize the risk.

### Assess the Violence Risk Systematically

The violence risk must be assessed systematically, because the anger presentation may involve the risk to the others, and the unassessed risk endangers the identified targets and the therapist. The therapist must assess the risk factors (the history of the violence, the substance use, the access to the weapons, the threats, the stalking, the psychosis, the personality disorder, the stressors, the targets), the warning behaviors (the escalation, the planning, the rehearsal, the leaking), and the protective factors. The therapist must use the structured tools when available, must assess the specific targets (the partner, the family, the public, the therapist), and must develop the safety plan and the risk management. The high-risk presentations require the escalation (the warning, the hospitalization, the law enforcement), consistent with the duty to warn and the jurisdiction laws.

The trap is the under-assessment of the risk (the assumption of the safety), which endangers the targets. The defense is to assess the violence risk systematically, to use the tools, to develop the safety plan, and to escalate the high-risk.

### Screen for the Trauma and the Adversity

The trauma and the adversity (the childhood abuse, the domestic violence, the combat, the community violence) must be screened, because the trauma is the common cause of the anger (the hyperarousal, the reactivity, the mistrust), and the untreated trauma drives the anger, and the anger treatment without the trauma work fails. The therapist must screen for the trauma (the childhood, the domestic, the combat, the community), must assess the trauma-related symptoms (the hyperarousal, the reactivity, the avoidance, the intrusion), and must formulate the anger in the context of the trauma. The trauma-focused treatment may be the indicated, and the anger management may be the adjunctive.

The trap is the trauma omission (the no screening), which misses the cause. The defense is to screen for the trauma and the adversity, to assess the symptoms, and to integrate the trauma work.

### Assess the Substance Use as the Contributor

The substance use (the alcohol, the stimulants, the other) must be assessed as the contributor, because the substance use is the common cause and the exacerbator of the anger and the aggression, and the anger treatment without the substance treatment fails. The therapist must assess the substance use (the type, the amount, the frequency, the pattern, the correlation with the anger), the substance-induced aggression, and the use as the coping. The substance treatment may be the primary, and the anger management may be the adjunctive. The therapist must coordinate with the substance specialist when the substance use is the significant.

The trap is the substance omission (the no assessment), which misses the contributor. The defense is to assess the substance use, to correlate with the anger, and to coordinate the treatment.

### Rule Out the Medical and the Medication Causes

The medical conditions (the traumatic brain injury, the dementia, the hormonal, the neurological, the pain) and the medications (the steroids, the stimulants, the withdrawal) must be ruled out as the causes, because the medical and the medication causes of the anger are common, and the psychological treatment of the medical anger fails. The therapist must assess the medical history, the medications, the onset correlation, and the neurological symptoms, and must coordinate with the physician for the medical evaluation. The traumatic brain injury and the dementia are the particularly important in the specific populations (the veterans, the elderly), and the medication review is the essential.

The trap is the psychological treatment of the medical anger, which fails. The defense is to rule out the medical and the medication causes, to coordinate with the physician, and to treat the psychological contributors.

## Common Traps

### The Generic Anger Management Without the Differential

The therapist delivers the generic anger management (the relaxation, the assertiveness, the cognitive restructuring) without the differential diagnosis, because the anger is the visible and the protocol is the known, so the underlying cause (the depression, the trauma, the substance) goes untreated and the anger persists. The mechanism of the trap is that the anger is the symptom, and the generic treatment without the differential treats the symptom while the cause worsens, because the therapist, in the response to the visible anger, delivers the known protocol without the assessment of the cause, and the underlying condition (the depression, the trauma) goes untreated. The false signal is the visible anger and the known protocol, which suggest the treatment but mask the missing differential. The harm is the persistent anger, the worsened cause, and the failed treatment. The defense is to conduct the differential, to formulate, and to treat the cause.

### The Conflation of Anger and Aggression

The therapist conflates the anger (the emotion) with the aggression (the behavior), treating the emotion when the behavior is the concern, so the violence risk is under-assessed and the targets are endangered. The mechanism of the trap is that the anger and the aggression are the distinct, and the conflation under-assesses the risk, because the therapist, in the focus on the emotional regulation, does not assess the aggressive behavior (the verbal, the physical, the targets), and the risk to the others is missed. The false signal is the emotional presentation, which suggests the regulation work but masks the aggressive behavior. The harm is the under-assessed risk, the endangered targets, and the potential violence. The defense is to differentiate the anger from the aggression, to assess the behavior, and to prioritize the risk.

### The Under-Assessment of the Violence Risk

The therapist under-assesses the violence risk (the assumes the safety, the no structured assessment), because the client seems the manageable and the risk seems the low, so the violence occurs and the targets are harmed. The mechanism of the trap is that the violence risk requires the systematic assessment, and the under-assessment endangers the targets, because the therapist, in the assumption of the safety or the discomfort with the risk assessment, does not assess the risk factors (the history, the weapons, the threats, the targets), and the violence occurs. The false signal is the manageable presentation, which suggests the safety but masks the risk. The harm is the violence, the harmed targets, and the legal and the ethical liability. The defense is to assess the violence risk systematically, to use the tools, and to escalate the high-risk.

### The Trauma Omission

The therapist does not screen for the trauma, because the anger is the visible and the trauma is not the presenting, so the trauma-driven anger goes untreated and the anger management fails. The mechanism of the trap is that the trauma is the common cause of the anger, and the trauma omission misses the cause, because the therapist, in the focus on the visible anger, does not screen for the trauma (the childhood, the domestic, the combat), and the trauma-driven anger (the hyperarousal, the reactivity) is treated with the generic anger management that fails. The false signal is the anger presentation, which suggests the anger management but masks the trauma. The harm is the failed treatment, the untreated trauma, and the persistent anger. The defense is to screen for the trauma, to assess the symptoms, and to integrate the trauma work.

### The Substance Omission

The therapist does not assess the substance use, because the client minimizes and the anger is the presenting, so the substance-driven anger goes untreated and the anger management fails. The mechanism of the trap is that the substance use is the common contributor, and the omission misses the contributor, because the therapist, in the focus on the anger and the client's minimization, does not assess the substance use (the alcohol, the stimulants), and the substance-driven aggression is treated with the anger management that fails. The false signal is the client's minimization, which suggests the absence but masks the substance use. The harm is the failed treatment, the untreated substance use, and the continued aggression. The defense is to assess the substance use, to correlate with the anger, and to coordinate the treatment.

### The Psychological Treatment of the Medical Anger

The therapist treats the anger psychologically, without the medical rule-out, so the medical cause (the traumatic brain injury, the dementia, the hormonal) goes undiagnosed and the psychological treatment fails. The mechanism of the trap is that the medical causes of the anger are common, and the psychological treatment of the medical anger fails, because the therapist does not coordinate with the physician, assumes the psychological cause, and the medical condition goes undiagnosed. The false signal is the psychological presentation, which suggests the psychological cause but masks the medical contributor. The harm is the failed treatment, the undiagnosed medical condition, and the potential harm. The defense is to rule out the medical and the medication causes, to coordinate with the physician, and to treat the psychological contributors.

## Self-Check

- Did I treat the anger as the symptom, conduct the thorough differential assessment, and formulate the anger in the context of the whole presentation?
- Did I differentiate the anger (the emotion) from the aggression (the behavior) and the hostility (the attitude), and assess the each?
- Did I assess the violence risk systematically (the history, the weapons, the threats, the targets, the warning behaviors), use the structured tools, and develop the safety plan?
- Did I screen for the trauma and the adversity, assess the trauma-related symptoms, and integrate the trauma work?
- Did I assess the substance use as the contributor, correlate with the anger, and coordinate with the substance specialist?
- Did I rule out the medical and the medication causes, coordinate with the physician, and treat the psychological contributors?
- Did I assess the risk to the therapist and the others, and develop the office safety procedures?
- Did I practice within the scope, escalate the high-risk (the duty to warn, the hospitalization), and refer beyond the scope?
