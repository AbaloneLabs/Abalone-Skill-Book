# Session Structure and Termination

IPT is a time-limited, structured therapy with distinct phases (initial, middle, termination), and the judgment problem is that the structure and frame are essential to IPT's effectiveness (they create the focus, the urgency, and the consolidation that make brief work productive), yet they are often eroded by the therapist's discomfort with limits, the client's wish for longer treatment, or the pull toward open-ended exploration. The initial phase (sessions 1 to 3) involves assessment, the interpersonal inventory, psychiatric and medical review, identifying the problem area, setting the contract, and establishing the frame. The middle phase involves working the identified problem area with the area-specific methods, maintaining the focus, and monitoring symptoms. The termination phase involves consolidating gains, addressing the ending, planning for the future, and recognizing that the time-limited frame itself is therapeutic. The therapist must structure the treatment across the phases, must maintain the time-limited frame, must use the IPT session structure, must plan termination deliberately, and must resist the erosion of the frame. The deeper traps are drifting into open-ended therapy, failing to use the session structure, neglecting termination planning, and allowing the frame to erode without recognizing the cost. The therapist's freedom is real, but IPT's structure and frame are part of the treatment and must be maintained. Given the clinical stakes, this guidance is conservative: structure the treatment across phases, maintain the time-limited frame, use the session structure, plan termination deliberately, and never let the frame erode or termination become an afterthought.

## Core Rules

### Structure the Treatment Across the Three Phases

IPT has three phases, and the therapist must structure the treatment across them, with each phase having its own tasks. The initial phase (typically sessions 1 to 3) involves completing the assessment (diagnosis, symptoms, psychiatric and medical review), conducting the interpersonal inventory (reviewing current and past relationships), identifying the primary problem area, establishing the treatment contract (agreeing on the focus and the frame), and psychoeducation about the connection between symptoms and interpersonal context. The middle phase (the majority of sessions) involves working the identified problem area with the area-specific methods, maintaining the focus, monitoring symptoms, and adjusting as needed. The termination phase (typically the last 1 to 3 sessions) involves consolidating gains, addressing the ending, planning for the future, and recognizing that the time-limited frame itself is therapeutic. The therapist should know which phase they are in and should conduct the phase-appropriate tasks.

### Maintain the Time-Limited Frame as a Therapeutic Element

The time-limited frame is not merely a constraint but a therapeutic element of IPT, and the therapist must maintain it. The frame creates focus (limited time requires prioritization), urgency (the time is finite, so the work must proceed), and consolidation (the ending requires integration of gains), and these elements contribute to IPT's effectiveness. The therapist should establish the frame at the outset (we will work for approximately N sessions), should reference it throughout (we are at session 5 of our planned 12), should resist the erosion of the frame (extending without good reason, drifting into open-ended work), and should recognize that the frame's limits are part of what makes the treatment work. The therapist should distinguish between appropriate flexibility (extending for a client who needs a few more sessions to consolidate) and frame erosion (drifting into indefinite treatment).

### Use the IPT Session Structure in Each Session

Each IPT session has a structure that the therapist should use, because the structure maintains the focus and the frame. A typical IPT session opens with a symptom check (how have your symptoms been this week), proceeds to connecting symptoms to interpersonal events (what happened interpersonally this week, how does it relate to the focus), works on the identified problem area (using the area-specific methods), and closes with connecting the work to the upcoming week and the frame. The structure is not rigid, but it provides the organization that keeps the sessions focused on the interpersonal context rather than drifting into symptom management, open-ended exploration, or content that is not related to the focus. The therapist should use the structure while remaining responsive to the client's presentation.

### Plan Termination Deliberately From the Beginning

Termination in IPT is planned from the beginning, not deferred to the end, because the time-limited frame means the ending is known from the outset. The therapist should reference the termination throughout (we have 4 sessions left, let us think about consolidating), should begin explicit termination planning in the final phase, should consolidate gains (reviewing what was accomplished, how the symptoms have changed, what the client learned), should address the ending relationally (what is it like to end, what has the relationship meant), and should plan for the future (what the client will watch for, what they will do if symptoms recur, whether they will return). Termination is a phase of work, not merely the stopping of treatment.

### Consolidate Gains and Establish the Client's Independent Capacity

The termination phase involves consolidating the gains and establishing the client's capacity to continue independently, and the therapist should do this deliberately. The therapist should review the progress (where you started, where you are now), should help the client identify what they learned and what worked, should connect the gains to the client's own efforts and resources, should discuss what the client will watch for (early signs of recurrence) and what they will do, and should make clear the door is open for return if needed. The consolidation makes the gains explicit and owned, and it establishes the client's independent capacity to maintain the change.

### Address the Relational Ending and the Frame's Meaning

Termination is a relational ending, and the therapist should address it, especially given that IPT's time-limited frame makes the ending a defined event. The therapist should invite the client's feelings about ending (what is it like to be ending, what will you miss), should acknowledge the meaning of the relationship, should recognize that for some clients (especially the deficit client, for whom the therapy relationship may have been significant) the ending may be painful, and should handle the ending with care. The therapist should also recognize that the time-limited frame's ending itself carries meaning (the client can form a meaningful relationship that has a defined end, the client can tolerate a loss and continue), and should use the termination therapeutically.

## Common Traps

### Drifting Into Open-Ended Therapy and Eroding the Frame

The therapist, uncomfortable with the time limit or responsive to the client's wish for longer treatment, allows the frame to erode, because the limit feels harsh and the client seems to need more, and the sessions extend beyond the planned number without clear rationale, and the treatment drifts into open-ended therapy, losing the focus, urgency, and consolidation that the time-limited frame provides. The mechanism is that the frame has eroded, so the time-limited structure has become open-ended, and the therapeutic elements of the frame (focus, urgency, consolidation) are lost. The false signal is the responsiveness to the client's need, which looks like compassionate practice but actually indicates the erosion of the frame. The harm is the loss of the frame's therapeutic elements, the drift into unfocused treatment, the extended duration without the structure that makes IPT effective, and the potential for dependency rather than independence. The defense is to maintain the time-limited frame, to distinguish appropriate flexibility from erosion, to reference the frame throughout, and to recognize that the frame is a therapeutic element, not merely a constraint.

### Failing to Use the Session Structure and Drifting in Sessions

The therapist, preferring a more open or client-led session flow, does not use the IPT session structure, because the structure feels rigid and the therapist wants to follow the client, and the sessions drift into open-ended exploration, symptom management, or content unrelated to the focus, and the organization that keeps IPT focused is lost. The mechanism is that the session structure has not been used, so the sessions lack the organization that maintains the focus, and the work drifts from the interpersonal context into other territory. The false signal is the responsive, client-led session flow, which looks like engaged practice but actually indicates the loss of structure. The harm is unfocused sessions, drift from the interpersonal focus, reduced effectiveness because the structure is not used, and the therapy becoming generic rather than specifically IPT. The defense is to use the IPT session structure (symptom check, connection to interpersonal events, work on the focus, connection to the upcoming week), to recognize that the structure maintains the focus, and to balance structure with responsiveness.

### Neglecting Termination Planning Until the Final Session

The therapist, focused on the middle phase work and not wanting to foreground the ending, defers termination planning until the final session, because the work seems more important and the ending feels premature to address, and the termination arrives without preparation, and the consolidation, the relational ending, and the future planning are rushed or omitted in the last session. The mechanism is that termination planning has been deferred, so the ending arrives without the deliberate work it requires, and the consolidation and relational ending are rushed or omitted. The false signal is the focus on the middle phase work, which looks like productive practice but actually indicates the neglect of termination planning. The harm is unconsolidated gains, an unworked relational ending, the client's unpreparedness for independence, and the missed opportunity to use termination therapeutically. The defense is to plan termination from the beginning, to reference it throughout, to begin explicit termination planning in the final phase, and to recognize that termination is a phase of work requiring deliberate attention.

### Extending Treatment Without Clear Rationale and Undermining the Frame

The therapist, encountering a client who has not improved as much as hoped, extends the treatment beyond the planned frame, because the client needs more and the therapist does not want to terminate with unresolved symptoms, but extends without a clear rationale (how many more sessions, what specifically will be accomplished, when will the extension end), and the frame becomes indefinite, and the focus and urgency are lost. The mechanism is that the treatment has been extended without clear rationale, so the frame has become indefinite, and the therapeutic elements of the time limit are lost. The false signal is the responsiveness to the client's continued need, which looks like responsible practice but actually indicates frame erosion without rationale. The harm is the loss of focus and urgency, the indefinite extension that undermines the frame's therapeutic elements, and the potential for dependency. The defense is to distinguish appropriate extension (with clear rationale, defined additional sessions, and specific goals) from indefinite drift, to recognize when the client needs a different or longer treatment than IPT can provide, and to refer rather than extend indefinitely.

### Failing to Consolidate Gains and Establish Independent Capacity

The therapist, reaching the termination phase and having accomplished the work, moves to end without consolidating the gains, because the goals are met and the work seems done, but does not review the progress, does not help the client identify what they learned, does not establish the client's capacity to maintain the change independently, and the client leaves with gains that are not explicit or owned and without clear capacity for independent maintenance. The mechanism is that consolidation has been omitted, so the gains have not been made explicit and owned, and the client's independent capacity has not been established. The false signal is the achievement of the goals, which looks like completed work but actually indicates gains that have not been consolidated. The harm is gains that are fragile because they are not owned, the client's uncertainty about their capacity, and the increased risk of recurrence because the client does not know how they produced the change or how to maintain it. The defense is to consolidate gains explicitly, to review progress, to help the client identify what they learned, to connect gains to their own efforts, and to establish their independent capacity to maintain the change.

## Self-Check

- Did I structure the treatment across the three phases (initial, middle, termination), conducting the phase-appropriate tasks?
- Did I maintain the time-limited frame as a therapeutic element, referencing it throughout and resisting erosion?
- Did I use the IPT session structure (symptom check, connection to interpersonal events, work on the focus, connection to the upcoming week) in each session?
- Did I plan termination from the beginning, referencing it throughout and beginning explicit planning in the final phase?
- Did I consolidate gains by reviewing progress, helping the client identify what they learned, and connecting gains to their own efforts?
- Did I establish the client's independent capacity, discussing what they will watch for and what they will do, and making clear the door is open for return?
- Did I address the relational ending, inviting the client's feelings and acknowledging the meaning of the relationship?
- Did I avoid drifting into open-ended therapy, failing to use the session structure, neglecting termination planning, extending without rationale, and failing to consolidate gains?
