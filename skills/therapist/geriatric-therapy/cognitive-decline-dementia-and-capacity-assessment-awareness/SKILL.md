# Cognitive Decline, Dementia, And Capacity Assessment Awareness

Working with older adults means working with cognitive change. Some decline is normal aging, some is a progressive dementia, some is a reversible medical condition, and some is delirium signaling acute illness. The therapist's central judgment is to recognize cognitive changes when they appear, to distinguish the patterns, to refer for medical and neuropsychological evaluation rather than diagnosing dementia from the therapy chair, to support families through the losses that dementia brings, and to navigate capacity questions clinically without usurping the formal legal or medical evaluation. The recurring failures are attributing cognitive changes to aging or depression and missing a reversible cause, accepting a dementia label without understanding the type or course, treating cognitive symptoms as motivational, and confusing a clinical impression of capacity with a formal capacity determination.

Use this skill when an older client shows cognitive changes, when distinguishing dementia from depression, delirium, or normal aging, when supporting a family through cognitive decline, or when capacity questions arise in treatment.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Cognitive Change And Distinguish The Major Patterns

Cognitive change in older adults falls into several patterns that require different responses. Normal aging involves slower processing and mild retrieval difficulty but intact function. Mild cognitive impairment involves measurable change beyond age norms without loss of functional independence. Dementia involves progressive decline in multiple domains that eventually impairs function. Delirium is an acute, fluctuating confusion usually driven by medical illness, medication, or infection, and it is a medical emergency. Depression can mimic dementia, producing "pseudodementia," and is treatable. Recognize the pattern from history and observation, and let the pattern drive the next step: delirium needs urgent medical evaluation, depression needs treatment, and progressive cognitive loss needs medical and neuropsychological workup.

### Refer For Medical And Neuropsychological Evaluation Rather Than Diagnosing

A therapist cannot diagnose dementia type, exclude reversible causes, or determine capacity from clinical interview alone. Refer the client to their physician for medical workup, because reversible causes, vitamin deficiency, thyroid dysfunction, medication effects, infection, subdural hematoma, normal pressure hydrocephalus, must be identified and treated. Refer for neuropsychological evaluation when the question is the type, severity, or trajectory of cognitive loss, because formal testing characterizes the pattern far better than bedside impression. Frame your role as recognizing the change, supporting the client and family, and coordinating care, not as making the neurocognitive diagnosis. Document the referrals and the rationale.

### Screen For And Distinguish Depression From Dementia

Depression is common in older adults, can present with cognitive complaints that mimic dementia, and is treatable, making the distinction consequential. Screen for depression with validated instruments, and attend to the pattern: depressive "pseudodementia" often has abrupt onset, prominent motivational loss, and variable performance, while dementia has insidious onset and consistent deficits. Ask about the timeline, the client's effort and concern, and the specific cognitive domains affected. A client who gives up on tasks and answers "I don't know" differs from one who confabulates or is unaware of deficits. When uncertain, refer for evaluation, because treating depression can reverse cognitive impairment, and untreated depression worsens outcomes in dementia.

### Identify Reversible Causes And Advocate For Medical Workup

A significant fraction of cognitive decline in older adults has a reversible component, and the therapist can be the one who notices and advocates for the workup. Ask about recent medication changes, especially anticholinergics, sedatives, and polypharmacy; about recent illness or hospitalization; about sensory loss, which can mimic or worsen cognitive impairment; and about sleep, pain, and nutrition. Encourage the client and family to pursue medical evaluation, and coordinate with the physician, with authorization. Untreated hearing or vision loss, medication effects, and untreated medical conditions can present as, or worsen, cognitive decline, and addressing them can restore function.

### Support The Family Through Diagnosis And Progression

Dementia affects the whole family, and the therapist often supports spouses and adult children as much as the identified client, especially as the disease progresses. Provide education about the type and expected course, normalize grief for the losses that precede death, address caregiver burden and burnout, and help the family plan for safety, finances, driving, and future care. Connect families to community resources, support groups, and respite care. Recognize that family members may disagree about the diagnosis and the plan, and that old family dynamics reemerge under the stress. Hold a space for both the client's remaining autonomy and the family's need for safety and planning.

### Address Capacity As A Clinical Question And Refer For Formal Determination

Capacity is decision-specific, time-specific, and context-specific: a client may have capacity to make a treatment decision today but not to manage finances, or may regain capacity when delirium resolves. The therapist can offer a clinical impression of the client's understanding, appreciation, reasoning, and choice for a specific decision, but formal capacity determinations for legal purposes are made by physicians, neuropsychologists, or the courts. Do not opine on legal capacity as if it were a settled clinical fact. When capacity is in question, document your observations, refer for formal evaluation, and work with the family and any legal agents, such as a healthcare proxy or power of attorney, consistent with the client's actual authority structure.

### Adapt Treatment To Cognitive Capacity

Therapy with cognitively impaired older adults must adapt to the client's remaining capacities. Use shorter sessions, concrete language, repetition, structure, and compensatory strategies such as notes and reminders. Focus on emotional quality of life, preserved strengths, behavioral symptoms, and family support rather than insight-oriented work that the client can no longer use. For advanced dementia, the work shifts to the family and the care environment, with the client's comfort and dignity as the goal. Match the modality to the capacity, and revise the plan as the disease progresses.

## Common Traps

### Attributing Cognitive Change To Aging Or Depression And Missing A Reversible Cause

A therapist may observe cognitive slowing or forgetfulness in an older client and attribute it to normal aging or to depression, when the change reflects a reversible medical condition such as medication toxicity, thyroid dysfunction, vitamin deficiency, infection, or sensory loss. The mechanism of harm is that a treatable cause is filed under "expected for age" or "depression," and the medical workup that would have reversed the decline is never pursued. The harm is that a potentially reversible condition progresses, sometimes irreversibly, because the window for treatment closed while the therapist treated the wrong problem. Recognizing cognitive change as a signal requiring medical workup, rather than an explanation in itself, prevents this error.

### Treating Cognitive Symptoms As Motivational Or Willful

A therapist may interpret the apathy, repetition, or difficulty initiating tasks in a cognitively impaired client as lack of motivation, resistance, or noncompliance, when these are symptoms of the brain disease. The mechanism of harm is that neurological symptoms are read through a psychological or moral lens, and the client is blamed, pressured, or treated as choosing not to engage. The harm is that the client experiences failure and shame for symptoms they cannot control, the family becomes frustrated, and interventions target motivation when the actual need is compensation, structure, and acceptance of the loss. Understanding cognitive symptoms as neurological, and adapting the work accordingly, is essential.

### Accepting A Dementia Label Without Understanding Type Or Course

A therapist may accept a family's or even a clinician's label of "dementia" or "Alzheimer's" without understanding the type, the stage, or the expected course, when different dementias, Alzheimer's, vascular, Lewy body, frontotemporal, have very different symptom profiles, trajectories, and management needs. The mechanism of harm is that a generic label produces a generic plan that misses the specific risks, for example, the motor and hallucination profile of Lewy body dementia or the behavioral and language changes of frontotemporal dementia. The harm is that the client and family are unprepared for the actual course, and interventions that help one type may worsen another. Seeking and using specific diagnostic information, and educating the family accordingly, improves care.

### Confusing A Clinical Impression Of Capacity With A Formal Determination

A therapist may form an opinion that a client "lacks capacity" or "has capacity" and communicate it to the family as a determination, when formal legal capacity is decision-specific and is determined by physicians, neuropsychologists, or courts using specific criteria. The mechanism of harm is that a clinical impression is mistaken for a legal finding, and the family acts on it, sometimes restricting the client's autonomy or, conversely, allowing unsafe decisions, on the basis of an opinion that carries no legal weight. The harm is that the client's rights are improperly curtailed or improperly left unprotected, and the therapist exceeds their role. Offering clinical observations about specific decisions, referring for formal evaluation, and respecting the legal framework prevents overreach.

### Conducting Insight-Oriented Work A Client Can No Longer Use

A therapist may continue an insight-oriented or exploratory approach with a client whose cognitive capacity has declined, expecting reflection, self-monitoring, and carryover that the client can no longer provide. The mechanism of harm is that the modality assumes cognitive capacities, abstract reasoning, working memory, new learning, that the disease has eroded, and the sessions produce frustration and a sense of failure rather than benefit. The harm is that the client's limited energy is spent on work they cannot use, while their actual needs, emotional support, behavioral management, family education, and quality of life, go unmet. Adapting the modality to the capacity, and shifting the focus as the disease progresses, serves the client.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.
