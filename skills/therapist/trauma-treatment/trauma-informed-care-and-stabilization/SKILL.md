# Trauma-Informed Care And Stabilization

Trauma-informed care is the foundational stance for working with trauma survivors: it recognizes the prevalence and impact of trauma, prioritizes safety, trust, choice, collaboration, and empowerment, and understands that behaviors and symptoms often make sense as adaptations to traumatic experience. Before any trauma processing, the client must be stabilized, meaning they have the skills, supports, and stability to manage the affect that processing will activate. The therapist's judgment is to apply trauma-informed principles across all phases of treatment, to establish stabilization thoroughly before processing, to recognize when a client is and is not ready for processing, and to avoid the recurring failure of rushing into processing before the client can tolerate it. The most common serious error in trauma treatment is moving to processing before stabilization is adequate, which can destabilize the client, worsen symptoms, and re-traumatize.

Use this skill when beginning or conducting trauma treatment, when establishing safety and stabilization, when applying trauma-informed principles, or when deciding if a client is ready for processing.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Trauma-Informed Principles Across All Phases

The trauma-informed principles, safety, trustworthiness, choice, collaboration, and empowerment, should guide all interactions with trauma survivors, not just the trauma-specific interventions. Safety includes physical, psychological, and emotional safety, and is established through predictability, transparency, and respect for the client's pace. Trustworthiness is built through reliability, honesty, and follow-through. Choice is honored by offering options and respecting the client's decisions about their treatment. Collaboration positions the client as a partner, and empowerment supports the client's agency and strengths. Apply these principles in assessment, in the frame, in interventions, and in the relationship, because they create the conditions that allow trauma work to proceed.

### Establish Stabilization Thoroughly Before Processing

The first phase of trauma treatment, often called safety and stabilization, must be established thoroughly before any trauma processing begins. Stabilization includes the client's ability to manage affect, to ground and self-soothe, to stay present rather than dissociate, to maintain basic life stability, housing, safety from ongoing abuse, and to access support. Assess stabilization directly: can the client use grounding skills when distressed? Do they have a safe environment? Are they free from ongoing trauma? Is their life stable enough to support the demands of processing? If stabilization is not adequate, the work is stabilization, not processing, and moving to processing prematurely risks destabilization and re-traumatization.

### Respect The Client's Pace And Window Of Tolerance

Trauma processing must stay within the client's window of tolerance, the zone of arousal where the client can process experience without becoming hyperaroused, flooded, or hypoaroused, dissociated. Respect the client's pace, do not push processing faster than the client can tolerate, and monitor the client's arousal throughout. If the client moves outside their window, slow down, ground, and return to stabilization. The window of tolerance expands with stabilization and skills, but at any given moment, processing must stay within it. Pushing beyond the window produces flooding or dissociation, which is re-traumatizing, not therapeutic.

### Assess Readiness For Processing Explicitly

Before beginning trauma processing, assess readiness explicitly, using clear criteria: the client is stabilized, has affect regulation skills, has adequate support, is free from ongoing trauma, has a therapeutic alliance strong enough to support the work, and wants to process. If any criterion is not met, address it before processing. Readiness is not a guess; it is an assessment, and documenting the readiness assessment protects the client and the clinician. If you are unsure whether the client is ready, err toward continued stabilization, because the cost of premature processing is high and the cost of delayed processing is low.

### Use Evidence-Based Trauma Processing When Ready

When the client is stabilized and ready, use evidence-based trauma processing approaches, such as trauma-focused CBT, EMDR, prolonged exposure, or narrative exposure therapy, chosen based on the client's presentation, the trauma type, and the therapist's training. These approaches have evidence for their effectiveness and their procedures are designed to process trauma while managing arousal. Do not improvise trauma processing; use a trained, evidence-based approach, and stay within your training. If you are not trained in trauma processing, refer to a clinician who is, rather than attempting processing outside your competency.

### Address Ongoing Safety And Re-Traumatization Risks

Throughout trauma treatment, address ongoing safety, because a client who is currently being abused, in an unsafe relationship, or in unsafe circumstances cannot safely process past trauma. Assess for ongoing abuse, domestic violence, and unsafe environments, and address these before or alongside trauma processing. Re-traumatization can also occur within the therapy, through premature processing, insensitive interventions, or boundary violations, and the therapist must be vigilant to avoid re-creating traumatic dynamics in the therapeutic relationship. Ongoing safety is the precondition for trauma work, and it must be maintained throughout.

### Integrate The Trauma And Rebuild Meaning

The final phase of trauma treatment involves integration, making meaning of the traumatic experience, and rebuilding a life that incorporates the trauma without being defined by it. Help the client integrate the processed material into their narrative, construct a coherent meaning of what happened, and reconnect with values, relationships, and identity that the trauma disrupted. Integration is not forgetting or moving on; it is incorporating the experience into a life that is meaningful and whole. Attend to integration as a distinct phase, because processing without integration leaves the trauma processed but not incorporated.

## Common Traps

### Rushing To Processing Before Stabilization

A therapist may rush to trauma processing before the client is stabilized, eager to address the trauma and confident in the processing approach, which can destabilize the client and re-traumatize them. The mechanism is that processing feels like the "real" work, and stabilization feels like delay, so the therapist moves to processing before the client has the skills, support, and stability to tolerate it. The harm is that the client is flooded, dissociates, decompensates, or worsens, because the processing activated material they could not manage. Establishing stabilization thoroughly, and assessing readiness explicitly before processing, is what prevents this serious and common error.

### Ignoring The Window Of Tolerance

A therapist may push processing beyond the client's window of tolerance, pursuing the protocol or the client's stated desire to "get through it," while the client becomes hyperaroused or dissociated, which is re-traumatizing. The mechanism is that the protocol has steps, and the client wants to process, so the therapist continues, when the client's arousal signals that they have moved outside the zone where processing is therapeutic. The harm is that the client is flooded or dissociated, the processing is re-traumatizing rather than therapeutic, and the client may worsen or drop out. Monitoring arousal, staying within the window, and grounding when the client moves outside it, is what keeps processing therapeutic.

### Re-Creating Traumatic Dynamics In The Therapy

A therapist may inadvertently re-create traumatic dynamics in the therapeutic relationship, through pressure, boundary violations, dismissiveness, or the power differential, re-traumatizing the client within the therapy. The mechanism is that trauma often involves betrayal of trust, violation of boundaries, or abuse of power, and therapeutic behaviors that echo these dynamics, even subtly, can activate the trauma. The harm is that the client is re-traumatized in the very relationship that was meant to heal, the alliance ruptures, and the client may worsen or leave. Applying trauma-informed principles, attending to the power differential, and being vigilant to dynamics that echo the trauma, is what prevents re-traumatization in the therapy.

### Processing When There Is Ongoing Trauma

A therapist may process past trauma while the client is currently being abused, in an unsafe relationship, or in unsafe circumstances, when ongoing trauma is a contraindication for processing past trauma. The mechanism is that the past trauma is identified and the therapist begins processing, without adequately assessing whether the client is currently safe, when processing past trauma while currently being traumatized can overwhelm the client's capacity. The harm is that the client, already managing ongoing trauma, is further overwhelmed by processing, and the processing cannot integrate while the current trauma continues. Assessing for and addressing ongoing safety before processing is what prevents this error.

### Improvising Trauma Processing Outside Evidence-Based Approaches

A therapist may improvise trauma processing, using unstructured exploration of traumatic memories without a trained, evidence-based approach, which can be ineffective or harmful. The mechanism is that the therapist recognizes the trauma and wants to address it, but is not trained in a specific processing approach, so they explore the memories conversationally, when unstructured exploration can flood the client without the structure that makes processing therapeutic. The harm is that the client is exposed to traumatic material without the containment, and the processing is ineffective or re-traumatizing. Using a trained, evidence-based approach, and referring when not trained, is what keeps trauma processing safe and effective.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Am I applying the trauma-informed principles, safety, trust, choice, collaboration, empowerment, across all phases?
- [ ] Have I established stabilization thoroughly, including affect regulation, support, and life stability, before processing?
- [ ] Am I respecting the client's pace and keeping processing within the window of tolerance?
- [ ] Did I assess readiness for processing explicitly, against clear criteria?
- [ ] Am I using an evidence-based processing approach within my training, or referring?
- [ ] Have I assessed for and addressed ongoing safety and re-traumatization risks?
- [ ] Am I attending to integration and meaning-making as a distinct phase?
- [ ] Did I avoid rushing to processing, ignoring the window, re-creating trauma dynamics, processing during ongoing trauma, and improvising processing?
