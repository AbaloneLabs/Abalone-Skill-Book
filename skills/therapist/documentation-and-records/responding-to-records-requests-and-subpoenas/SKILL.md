# Responding To Records Requests And Subpoenas

Requests for therapy records come from multiple sources: clients exercising their right to access their records, attorneys issuing subpoenas in legal proceedings, insurers requesting records to justify payment, and other providers requesting records for continuity of care. Each type of request carries different obligations, and the therapist's judgment is to respond appropriately to each, protecting the client's confidentiality and the therapist's legal interests while complying with legitimate requests. The recurring failures are releasing records without proper authorization, releasing more than is required, failing to obtain the client's authorization when it is needed, treating a subpoena as a court order, and releasing psychotherapy notes without the heightened authorization they require. The therapist must know their jurisdiction's rules and must not navigate these requests alone when the stakes are high.

Use this skill when receiving any request for therapy records, when a client asks to see their record, when a subpoena arrives, or when a third party seeks client information.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish The Types Of Requests And Their Requirements

Different requests carry different obligations. A client's request to access their own record is a right under HIPAA and most state laws, with limited exceptions, and must be honored. A subpoena from an attorney is a legal demand but, in many jurisdictions, is not sufficient by itself to compel release of therapy records without the client's authorization or a court order. A court order is a judicial command that must be complied with, though the therapist may seek to limit its scope. An insurer request for records to justify payment is governed by the insurance contract and the client's authorization. Identify the type of request, and apply the corresponding requirements.

### Obtain The Client's Authorization For Most Releases

For most releases of records to third parties, obtain the client's written, informed authorization, which specifies what is to be released, to whom, for what purpose, and with an expiration. The authorization must be informed, meaning the client understands what they are authorizing and the potential consequences. Do not release records based on a third party's request alone, even if the third party is an attorney, family member, or insurer, without the client's authorization or a legal mandate. If the client declines to authorize a release that a third party is requesting, inform the third party that the client has declined.

### Do Not Treat A Subpoena As A Court Order

A subpoena from an attorney is a legal demand, but in many jurisdictions, it is not sufficient by itself to compel release of therapy records, which are protected by privilege. A court order, issued by a judge, is a judicial command that must be complied with. Do not treat a subpoena as the equivalent of a court order: verify whether the client has authorized the release, whether privilege has been waived, and whether a court order exists. If the subpoena is not accompanied by authorization or a court order, the therapist may need to assert privilege on the client's behalf, seek to quash the subpoena, or obtain the client's authorization before releasing.

### Protect Psychotherapy Notes With Heightened Authorization

Under HIPAA, psychotherapy notes, the therapist's private process notes, have heightened protection and require a separate, specific authorization for release, distinct from the authorization for the rest of the medical record. Most authorizations for the medical record do not cover psychotherapy notes, and most third-party requests do not entitle the requester to them. Do not release psychotherapy notes without a specific authorization that meets HIPAA's heightened requirements, and in most cases, do not release them at all. The distinction between the progress note and the psychotherapy note is critical here.

### Release The Minimum Necessary

When releasing records, release the minimum necessary to satisfy the request, not the entire record. Review the records before release, and if they contain information about third parties, such as family members or partners, redact or obtain authorization for that material. If the records contain sensitive material not relevant to the request, consider whether it must be included. The minimum necessary standard protects the client's confidentiality and the privacy of third parties, and it reduces the risk that irrelevant sensitive material is used in legal proceedings.

### Seek Consultation Or Legal Counsel For High-Stakes Requests

For high-stakes requests, such as subpoenas in contentious legal proceedings, court orders, requests involving minor custody, or requests where the client's authorization is unclear, seek consultation or legal counsel before responding. Do not navigate these requests alone, because the legal and ethical obligations are complex and the consequences of error are significant. Professional liability insurers often provide legal guidance, and professional associations offer consultation. The cost of consultation is far less than the cost of an improper release or an improper refusal.

### Document The Request And The Response

Document every records request and the response, including the date of the request, the requester, the type of request, the authorization or legal basis, what was reviewed, what was released or withheld, and the rationale. This documentation protects the clinician in the event of a later challenge and provides a record of the decision-making. If records are released under a court order or over the client's objection, document the legal basis and the consultation obtained. Documentation of the request and response is part of sound record-keeping.

## Common Traps

### Releasing Records Without Proper Authorization

A therapist may receive a request for records from an attorney, insurer, or family member and release them without obtaining the client's authorization or verifying the legal basis, a serious breach of confidentiality. The mechanism is that the request appears official, on letterhead or accompanied by a subpoena, and the therapist, intimidated or uninformed, complies without checking the authorization requirements. The harm is that confidential therapy records are released to a third party without the client's consent, which is a breach of confidentiality, a potential ethics violation, and a basis for legal liability. Verifying the authorization or legal basis before any release, and obtaining the client's written authorization when it is required, is what prevents this breach.

### Treating A Subpoena As A Court Order

A therapist may receive a subpoena and treat it as a command to release records immediately, without recognizing that a subpoena from an attorney is not, in many jurisdictions, sufficient to compel release of privileged therapy records. The mechanism is that a subpoena looks authoritative, and the therapist, lacking legal knowledge, assumes it must be obeyed, when the records may be protected by privilege and the client's authorization or a court order is needed. The harm is that privileged records are released without proper legal basis, breaching confidentiality and potentially waiving privilege. Distinguishing a subpoena from a court order, verifying authorization, and seeking legal counsel when unsure, is what protects the client's privilege.

### Releasing Psychotherapy Notes Without Heightened Authorization

A therapist may release psychotherapy notes, the private process notes, along with the rest of the record, without recognizing that HIPAA requires a separate, specific authorization for them. The mechanism is that the therapist does not maintain the distinction between the progress note and the psychotherapy note, or does not know the heightened authorization requirement, and includes the psychotherapy notes in the release. The harm is that the therapist's most private clinical thinking, hypotheses, and personal reactions are released to third parties, which can harm the client and the therapy, and which HIPAA specifically protects against. Maintaining the distinction and requiring heightened authorization for psychotherapy notes is what protects them.

### Releasing More Than The Minimum Necessary

A therapist may release the entire record in response to a request, without reviewing for relevance or third-party information, when the minimum necessary standard requires releasing only what is needed. The mechanism is that reviewing and redacting is time-consuming, and the therapist, pressed, releases the whole record rather than the relevant portion. The harm is that irrelevant sensitive material, and information about third parties, is released and potentially used in legal proceedings or otherwise disclosed, harming the client and others. Reviewing records before release, redacting third-party information, and releasing only the minimum necessary, is what protects confidentiality.

### Navigating High-Stakes Requests Without Legal Counsel

A therapist may attempt to navigate a high-stakes request, a subpoena in a custody dispute, a court order, a request where the client's wishes are unclear, without seeking legal counsel, and make an error with significant consequences. The mechanism is that the therapist wants to handle it themselves, or is concerned about the cost of counsel, when the legal and ethical obligations are complex and the consequences of error, improper release or improper refusal, are significant. The harm is that the therapist breaches confidentiality, violates privilege, or fails to comply with a legitimate legal demand, any of which can produce ethics complaints, liability, or contempt. Seeking consultation or legal counsel for high-stakes requests is what prevents navigating them alone.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I identify the type of request, client access, subpoena, court order, insurer, and its requirements?
- [ ] Did I obtain the client's written, informed authorization for releases to third parties?
- [ ] Did I distinguish the subpoena from a court order and verify the legal basis for release?
- [ ] Did I protect psychotherapy notes with heightened authorization and not release them with the record?
- [ ] Did I release the minimum necessary, reviewing and redacting third-party information?
- [ ] Did I seek consultation or legal counsel for high-stakes requests?
- [ ] Did I document the request, the response, and the rationale?
- [ ] Did I avoid releasing without authorization, treating subpoenas as court orders, releasing psychotherapy notes improperly, over-releasing, and navigating high-stakes requests alone?
