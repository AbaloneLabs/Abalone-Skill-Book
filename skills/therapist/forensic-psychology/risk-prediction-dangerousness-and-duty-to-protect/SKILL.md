# Risk Prediction, Dangerousness, And Duty To Protect

Assessing risk of harm to others is among the most consequential judgments a therapist makes, and it sits at the intersection of clinical assessment, ethical obligation, and legal duty. The therapist's central judgment is to assess threats and risk factors directly, to understand the duty to protect as it applies in the jurisdiction, to take protective actions when a identifiable target is at serious risk, to document the assessment and the actions, and to hold the limits of prediction honestly, neither overclaiming the ability to predict violence nor using uncertainty as a reason for inaction. The recurring failures are failing to assess threats directly, confusing clinical risk assessment with prediction of dangerousness, misapplying or ignoring the duty to protect, and failing to document the reasoning behind protective decisions.

Use this skill when assessing a client's risk of harm to others, when threats are made toward an identifiable target, when duty-to-protect obligations may apply, or when documenting risk and protective actions.

## Core Rules

### Assess Threats, Intent, Plan, And Access Directly

As with suicide risk, the assessment of risk to others requires direct, specific inquiry. Ask about threats: toward whom, what kind of harm, how often, and how seriously the client intends it. Ask about plan and means: has the client thought about how, do they have access to the means, have they taken preparatory steps, have they approached or surveilled the target. Ask about history: prior violence, prior threats, what has stopped them before. Ask about the target: who is it, what is the relationship, what is the perceived grievance. Asking directly does not increase risk; it is the only way to assess it. Do not avoid the questions because they are uncomfortable or because you assume the client is "just venting."

### Understand The Duty To Protect As It Applies In Your Jurisdiction

The duty to protect, often associated with the Tarasoff case, varies by jurisdiction in its scope and specifics, and you must know the law as it applies where you practice. In many jurisdictions, the duty is triggered when there is a serious threat of physical violence against a reasonably identifiable victim, and it may require protective actions such as warning the target, notifying law enforcement, taking reasonable steps to commit or stabilize the client, or some combination. Some jurisdictions have a mandatory duty, some a permissive one, and the specific actions required differ. Know your jurisdiction's standard, and seek consultation when the situation may trigger the duty. Do not assume the duty is the same everywhere or that it requires the same actions.

### Take Protective Actions When A Target Is At Serious Risk

When a serious threat against an identifiable target is present, take protective actions consistent with your jurisdiction's duty and with clinical judgment. Protective actions may include warning the identified target directly, notifying law enforcement, initiating hospitalization, voluntary or involuntary, increasing the level of care, and revising the treatment plan to address the risk. The specific actions depend on the severity and immediacy of the threat, the client's history, the target's vulnerability, and the legal standard. Act on the side of protection when the threat is serious, because the cost of unnecessary protective action is repairable while the cost of a missed serious threat is irreversible. Document the threat, the assessment, the actions taken, and the consultation sought.

### Distinguish Clinical Risk Assessment From Prediction Of Dangerousness

Clinical risk assessment identifies risk factors, threats, and the current clinical picture, and it guides action; it does not predict whether the client will actually be violent, because individual violence cannot be reliably predicted. Hold this distinction honestly: you can assess risk and take appropriate action without claiming to predict the outcome, and you can acknowledge uncertainty while still acting on serious threats. Do not present risk assessment as prediction, because overclaiming predictive ability misleads the legal system and the public, and it sets up the therapist for failure when violence occurs despite a low-risk assessment. Frame the work as risk assessment and management, not as prediction, and document it accordingly.

### Identify The Target And The Specificity Of The Threat

The duty to protect and the clinical response depend on whether there is an identifiable target and a specific threat. A vague statement of anger at a category of people, "I hate my boss," differs from a specific threat against an identifiable person, "I'm going to kill my boss, John Smith, on Friday." Assess the specificity: who is the target, how identifiable are they, how specific is the plan, how immediate is the threat. The more specific and identifiable, the higher the obligation to act. When the target is identifiable and the threat is serious, the duty is more clearly engaged; when the threat is vague, the clinical response may focus on risk management within the treatment. Document the specificity assessment.

### Document The Assessment, The Decision, And The Actions

Documentation in risk-to-others cases must show the clinical reasoning, not only the conclusion. Document what you assessed, what threats and risk factors you found, how you weighed them, what duty you determined applied, what actions you took, what consultation you sought, and what follow-up you arranged. A note that says "denied homicidal ideation" without the assessment behind it provides no protection if the outcome is bad. Document the reasoning that supports the decision, including the factors you weighed, the jurisdictional standard you applied, and the consultation that informed the action. Thorough documentation protects the client, the target, and the therapist, and it is part of the standard of care in these cases.

### Seek Consultation On Cases Involving Risk To Others

No therapist should carry a case involving serious risk to others alone. Consultation with a supervisor, colleague, attorney, or the liability insurer provides a second perspective, clarifies the legal duty, and shares the weight of the decision. Consultation is part of the standard of care in high-risk cases, not a sign of weakness, and it should be sought whenever a serious threat is present, whenever the duty may be triggered, and whenever the clinical decision is difficult. Document the consultation and how it informed the plan. Carrying these decisions alone increases the risk of error and of isolation under the stress.

## Common Traps

### Failing To Assess Threats Directly Because Of Discomfort

A therapist may avoid asking directly about threats, intent, plan, and targets because the questions are uncomfortable or because the client's anger is assumed to be venting, when direct assessment is the only way to identify serious risk. The mechanism of harm is that the discomfort is mistaken for clinical judgment, and the assessment omits the information most consequential to the safety of a potential victim. The harm is that a serious threat goes undetected, the target is not warned or protected, and harm occurs that assessment and action could have prevented. Asking directly, specifically, and repeatedly, and treating anger and threats as data requiring assessment, is the standard of care.

### Confusing Clinical Risk Assessment With Prediction Of Dangerousness

A therapist may treat risk assessment as prediction, believing that a low-risk assessment means the client will not be violent or that a high-risk assessment means they will, when individual violence cannot be reliably predicted and the task is risk assessment and management, not prediction. The mechanism of harm is that the conflation leads to overconfidence in low-risk assessments, with insufficient protective action, or to defensive over-action on high-risk assessments that may not be warranted. The harm is that targets are inadequately protected when risk is underestimated, or clients are over-restricted when risk is overestimated. Holding the distinction honestly, and framing the work as assessment and management, prevents both errors.

### Misapplying Or Ignoring The Duty To Protect

A therapist may misapply the duty to protect, warning when the legal standard is not met or failing to act when it is, because the jurisdictional standard is misunderstood or the clinical situation is ambiguous. The mechanism of harm is that the wrong action, or inaction, is taken: unnecessary warnings that breach confidentiality and damage the treatment, or failures to warn that leave a target unprotected. The harm is that confidentiality is breached without justification, the therapeutic relationship is damaged, or a victim is harmed who could have been protected. Knowing the jurisdictional standard, seeking consultation, and acting consistently with both law and clinical judgment prevents misapplication.

### Using Uncertainty As A Reason For Inaction

A therapist, faced with uncertainty about whether a threat is serious or whether the duty applies, may use the uncertainty as a reason for inaction, waiting for clarity that may come only with harm, when uncertainty in the face of a serious threat warrants protective action and consultation. The mechanism of harm is that the discomfort of uncertainty leads to paralysis, and a serious threat is managed as if it were ambiguous, with no protective action taken. The harm is that a target is harmed who protection could have prevented, and the therapist, having rationalized inaction, faces the consequences. Acting on the side of protection when the threat is serious, and seeking consultation to share the decision, is the defensible path.

### Failing To Document The Assessment And The Reasoning

A therapist may conduct a risk assessment and make a decision but document only the conclusion, "denied homicidal ideation, no duty triggered," without the assessment, the factors weighed, and the reasoning, when the documentation must show the clinical thinking to be defensible. The mechanism of harm is that the note provides no protection if the outcome is bad, because it does not show that the assessment was competent or that the decision was justified. The harm is that the therapist is exposed professionally and legally, and the record does not reflect the care that was actually provided. Documenting the full assessment, the factors weighed, the duty analysis, the actions, and the consultation protects everyone involved.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.
