# Treatment Progress Review And Plan Revision

Therapists are trained to start treatment but less often trained to evaluate whether it is working and to revise it when it is not. The result is therapy that continues past the point where it should have changed, with both therapist and client attributing non-response to the client's resistance or the difficulty of the work, when the real issue is a formulation or plan that no longer fits. Progress review is not a bureaucratic checkpoint; it is the feedback loop that keeps treatment honest. The recurring failures are relying on impression rather than measurement, attributing non-response to the client, continuing the same approach longer than warranted, and failing to recognize deterioration as a signal to act.

Use this skill at planned intervals, when progress stalls, when the client reports no change, when symptoms worsen, or when reconsidering the whole approach.

## Core Rules

### Measure Progress Routinely, Not By Impression Alone

Clinical impression of progress is biased: therapists tend to overestimate improvement, clients tend to report improvement to please the therapist, and both collude in a narrative of progress that may not match the data. Routine outcome measurement, using validated symptom scales, the client's own rating of target problems, or behavioral markers, corrects the bias and identifies non-response early. Administer measures at planned intervals, review them with the client, and use them to guide decisions. Measurement is not a substitute for clinical judgment; it is an input to it.

### Define What Counts As An Adequate Trial Before Deciding Non-Response

Non-response should not be declared too early, before the treatment has had a fair chance, nor too late, after months of ineffective work. Define in advance what an adequate trial looks like for the modality and problem, such as a sufficient number of sessions, adequate frequency, client engagement, and competent delivery. For many CBT protocols, eight to twelve sessions of engaged work without improvement warrants reconsideration; for psychodynamic work the timeframe may be longer but should still be defined. Document the trial parameters and the response, so the decision to revise is evidence-based.

### Distinguish Non-Response From Deterioration And Act On Detergation Urgently

Non-response, the client is not improving, and deterioration, the client is getting worse, require different responses. Non-response warrants formulation review and plan revision; deterioration warrants urgent attention, because a client who is worsening in treatment may be experiencing a harm from the treatment itself, an escalation of the underlying condition, or an external stressor. When a client deteriorates, assess the cause, consider whether the treatment is contributing, adjust or pause the intervention, and increase monitoring. Deterioration is never something to wait out.

### Reconsider The Formulation When The Plan Is Not Working

When treatment is not working, the first place to look is the formulation. A plan built on an incorrect or incomplete formulation will not produce change regardless of how well it is delivered. Revisit the diagnosis, the hypothesized mechanisms, the precipitating and perpetuating factors, and the functional analysis. Ask what has been missed: an undetected substance use, a medical condition, an unreported trauma, a bipolar diathesis, an unaddressed relationship problem. New history that emerged during treatment may shift the formulation. Revise the formulation, then revise the plan to match.

### Engage The Client In The Review Honestly

The client is the expert on whether the treatment is helping, and engaging them in the review surfaces information the therapist may miss. Ask directly: "Is this working? Are you getting what you need? What has changed, and what has not?" Frame the review as collaborative, not as an indictment of the client or the therapist. A client who has been holding back dissatisfaction will often share it when asked directly, and the conversation itself can redirect the work.

### Consider The Full Range Of Revisions

When revision is needed, consider the full range, not only the familiar one of "more sessions." Options include:

- changing the modality, when the evidence suggests a different approach;
- changing the frequency or intensity, such as moving to twice weekly or to a higher level of care;
- addressing a comorbidity or external factor that has been blocking progress;
- adding a component, such as medication evaluation, group therapy, or skills training;
- adjusting the focus, when the original goals were wrong or have been overtaken by events;
- consulting or seeking supervision to gain a new perspective;
- referring to a different clinician when the fit or competence is the issue.

Choose the revision that fits the reason the plan is not working, and document the reasoning.

### Recognize When The Therapy Itself May Be Causing Harm

A small proportion of clients deteriorate in therapy, and some of those deteriorations are caused by the therapy: an exposure pushed too fast, a trauma processing undertaken without stabilization, a confrontation that ruptured the alliance, a transference interpretation that was inaccurate and alienating. When a client deteriorates, consider honestly whether the treatment is contributing, and be willing to pause, adjust, or change the approach. Defensive loyalty to the modality, in the face of a client getting worse, is a clinical failure.

## Common Traps

### Impression-Based Progress Assessment

When progress is assessed by the therapist's general sense and the client's verbal report, both parties are biased toward perceiving improvement, and non-response or deterioration goes unrecognized until a crisis. The mechanism is that the therapist wants the work to succeed, the client wants to please the therapist and to believe they are improving, and the absence of measurement removes any corrective feedback. The harm is that ineffective treatment continues, deterioration is missed, and the client eventually drops out or worsens without the therapist having identified the problem. Routine measurement, even a brief session rating or symptom scale, breaks the collusion and surfaces non-response early.

### Attributing Non-Response To The Client

When treatment is not working, the therapist may attribute the failure to the client's resistance, motivation, or pathology, rather than reconsidering the formulation or plan. The mechanism is that the therapist, confident in the approach, locates the problem in the client, because reconsidering the formulation would mean admitting the plan was wrong. The harm is that the client is blamed for the treatment's failure, the formulation is never revised, and the same ineffective approach continues, sometimes with increasing frustration on both sides. The corrective is to treat non-response as information about the formulation and plan, not as a verdict on the client, and to revise accordingly.

### Continuing Too Long Out Of Inertia Or Hope

A treatment that is not working may continue for months or years past the point where revision was warranted, because switching feels difficult, the investment feels substantial, and the therapist hopes the next session will be the breakthrough. The mechanism is that each session feels like it might be the one, and the cumulative cost of the ineffective treatment is invisible because it accrues session by session. The harm is the client's time, money, and suffering, and the opportunity cost of not receiving an effective treatment. Defining an adequate trial in advance, and committing to revise when the trial has not produced response, prevents inertia from extending ineffective treatment.

### Missing Deterioration As A Signal

A client who is getting worse in treatment may be assessed as "still working through things" or "in a difficult phase," when the deterioration is a signal that the treatment is causing harm or that the condition is escalating. The mechanism is that deterioration can look like expected therapeutic struggle, especially in approaches that involve processing difficult material, so the therapist normalizes it rather than investigating it. The harm is that a client who is being harmed by the treatment, or whose condition is escalating, continues to receive the same intervention, and the deterioration worsens. Any deterioration warrants assessment of cause, consideration of whether the treatment is contributing, and adjustment; it is never something to wait out.

### Loyalty To The Modality Over The Client's Outcome

A therapist strongly identified with a modality may continue it in the face of non-response, because abandoning it feels like betraying the model or admitting its limits. The mechanism is that professional identity is tied to the modality, so revising away from it feels like a personal and theoretical failure. The harm is that the client's outcome is subordinated to the therapist's allegiance, and an effective alternative is not pursued. The corrective is to remember that the goal is the client's improvement, not the vindication of the modality, and that switching when the evidence warrants it is clinical competence, not betrayal.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Am I measuring progress routinely with validated instruments or concrete behavioral indicators, not impression alone?
- [ ] Did I define what counts as an adequate trial for this modality and problem, and base the non-response decision on it?
- [ ] Did I distinguish non-response from deterioration, and respond to deterioration urgently rather than waiting?
- [ ] Did I reconsider the formulation when the plan is not working, looking for what was missed?
- [ ] Did I engage the client honestly in the review, asking directly whether the work is helping?
- [ ] Did I consider the full range of revisions, including modality change, intensity change, addressing comorbidity, adding components, consulting, or referring?
- [ ] Did I consider honestly whether the treatment itself may be contributing to deterioration, and adjust accordingly?
- [ ] Did I avoid attributing non-response to the client, and avoid continuing an ineffective approach out of inertia or loyalty?
