# Group Formation, Screening, And Composition

The formation of a therapy group, the selection and screening of members and the composition of the group, determines much of what the group can become, and errors in formation are difficult to correct once the group is running. The therapist's judgment is to screen members carefully for suitability and fit, to compose the group with attention to the balance of homogeneity and heterogeneity, to exclude clients who cannot safely or effectively participate, and to prepare members for the group experience. The recurring failures are forming groups too quickly without adequate screening, composing groups without attention to balance, including clients who are not suitable or who destabilize the group, and failing to prepare members for the group's demands. A well-formed group can do powerful work; a poorly formed group may never cohere or may become destructive.

Use this skill when forming a therapy group, screening prospective members, deciding on composition, or determining a client's suitability for group therapy.

## Core Rules

### Screen Each Member For Suitability And Fit

Screen each prospective member individually before offering a place in the group, assessing their suitability for group therapy in general and for this group in particular. Suitability includes the capacity for interpersonal engagement, willingness to give and receive feedback, ability to tolerate the affect and conflict that groups generate, stability of functioning, and absence of contraindications. Fit includes whether the member's presentation, goals, and dynamics match the group's purpose and composition. Use the screening to inform the member about the group, to assess their readiness, and to begin the preparation that group membership requires. Screening is not a formality; it is the foundation of the group's safety and effectiveness.

### Compose The Group With Attention To Balance

Group composition, the balance of homogeneity and heterogeneity among members, affects the group's dynamics and its therapeutic potential. Homogeneity, in presenting concerns, demographics, or level of functioning, helps members connect and feel understood; heterogeneity, in personality, perspective, or coping style, provides the variety that generates learning and feedback. Compose the group with attention to this balance: enough homogeneity that members feel they belong, enough heterogeneity that the group offers new perspectives. Avoid extreme imbalance, such as a single member who is markedly different from the rest, which can isolate that member and distort the group dynamics.

### Exclude Clients Who Cannot Safely Or Effectively Participate

Certain clients are not suitable for group therapy, at least at a given time, and excluding them protects both the client and the group. Contraindications include active suicidality that requires individual management, active psychosis, severe paranoia, current substance intoxication, a history of violating boundaries or monopolizing groups, inability to maintain confidentiality, or a presentation that would isolate the client in the group. Excluding a client is a clinical decision, made kindly and clearly, with referral to more appropriate care. Including an unsuitable client, out of reluctance to exclude, can harm the client and destabilize the group.

### Prepare Members For The Group Experience

Prepare each member for the group experience before they attend, because the group's demands, interpersonal engagement, feedback, conflict, and emotional intensity, are unfamiliar and can be overwhelming. Explain the group's purpose, structure, and norms; describe what to expect, including the discomfort that is part of the work; and discuss how to participate effectively. Address the member's concerns and questions, and establish a preliminary agreement about participation. Preparation reduces the anxiety and dropout that unprepared members experience, and it sets the expectations that allow the group to function.

### Establish Clear Group Agreements And Norms

Establish clear group agreements and norms, covering confidentiality, attendance, punctuality, participation, feedback, outside contact between members, and the handling of crisis. The agreements create the structure and safety that allow the group to do its work, and they are established collaboratively, with the group's input, in the early sessions. Review the agreements regularly, and address violations directly, because unaddressed violations erode the group's safety and norms. The agreements are not rules imposed from above; they are the shared understandings that the group agrees to uphold, and they are central to the group's functioning.

### Manage The Group Size And Life Cycle

Manage the group size to support the work: most therapy groups function best with six to eight members, enough for rich interaction without becoming unwieldy, and small enough for each member to be seen. Plan for the group's life cycle, including the addition of new members as others leave, and the eventual termination of the group. A group that is too small cannot generate sufficient interaction; a group that is too large leaves members unseen. Plan the size and the life cycle proactively, rather than allowing the group to drift.

### Use Pre-Group Screening To Begin The Therapeutic Work

The pre-group screening is not only selection; it is the beginning of the therapeutic work. Use the screening to understand the member's interpersonal patterns, to begin the alliance, and to identify the dynamics that will emerge in the group. The screening information guides the leader's understanding of the member and informs how to support them in the group. Treat the screening as a clinical intervention, not merely an administrative step, because the relationship and understanding established in the screening support the member's engagement.

## Common Traps

### Forming Groups Too Quickly Without Adequate Screening

A therapist may form a group too quickly, recruiting members with minimal screening, because of pressure to fill the group, eagerness to start, or underestimating the importance of composition, and the group suffers from the inclusion of unsuitable or poorly matched members. The mechanism is that the group needs members, and the therapist, wanting to start, accepts members without thorough screening, when the screening is what ensures the group can function. The harm is that the group includes members who cannot participate effectively, who destabilize the group, or who drop out, and the group may never cohere. Screening each member thoroughly, even if it delays the start, is what forms a viable group.

### Including Clients Who Are Not Suitable

A therapist may include a client who is not suitable for the group, out of reluctance to exclude, a desire to help the client, or pressure to fill the group, and the client's participation harms both the client and the group. The mechanism is that exclusion feels unkind, and the therapist, wanting to help, includes the client, when the client's presentation is a contraindication. The harm is that the unsuitable client may be isolated, overwhelmed, or destabilized in the group, and their participation may disrupt the group's dynamics, safety, or cohesion. Excluding unsuitable clients, with kindness and referral, is what protects both the client and the group.

### Composing Without Attention To Balance

A therapist may compose the group without attention to the balance of homogeneity and heterogeneity, accepting members in the order they present, and the group's dynamics are distorted by imbalance. The mechanism is that the therapist fills the group without considering composition, when the balance affects how members connect and what the group offers. The harm is that a group with extreme imbalance, a single member who is markedly different, or a group that is too homogeneous to generate learning, does not function well. Composing with attention to balance, and adjusting the composition as needed, is what supports the group's dynamics.

### Failing To Prepare Members For The Group's Demands

A therapist may fail to prepare members for the group's demands, sending them into the group with minimal orientation, and the members are overwhelmed, anxious, or drop out. The mechanism is that the therapist assumes the members will learn the norms in the group, when the group's demands are unfamiliar and the early sessions are critical. The harm is that unprepared members experience the group as confusing or threatening, do not engage, or drop out, and the group's early sessions are disrupted. Preparing each member for the group's purpose, structure, norms, and demands, before they attend, is what supports engagement and retention.

### Ignoring Violations Of Group Agreements

A therapist may ignore violations of the group agreements, such as lateness, absenteeism, breaking confidentiality, or outside contact, hoping they will resolve, and the violations erode the group's safety and norms. The mechanism is that addressing violations is uncomfortable, and the therapist, avoiding the discomfort, lets them pass, when unaddressed violations signal that the agreements do not matter. The harm is that the group's safety erodes, members become cynical or anxious, and the norms that allow the work break down. Addressing violations directly, early and consistently, is what maintains the group's safety and norms.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I screen each member individually for suitability and fit before offering a place?
- [ ] Did I compose the group with attention to the balance of homogeneity and heterogeneity?
- [ ] Did I exclude clients who could not safely or effectively participate, with referral?
- [ ] Did I prepare each member for the group's purpose, structure, norms, and demands?
- [ ] Did I establish clear group agreements and norms, collaboratively, in the early sessions?
- [ ] Is the group size appropriate, and have I planned for the life cycle?
- [ ] Did I use the pre-group screening as the beginning of the therapeutic work?
- [ ] Did I avoid rushing formation, including unsuitable clients, ignoring composition, failing to prepare members, and ignoring agreement violations?
