# Bipolar Disorder Management

Bipolar disorder presents the therapist with a condition defined by oscillation — between depression and mania, between insight and its loss, between the client's desire for the energy and creativity of hypomania and the devastation of the crashes that follow. The therapeutic work is not eliminating the oscillation, which is often not achievable, but helping the client recognize, anticipate, and manage the cycles, maintain adherence to a treatment that they will be tempted to abandon when they feel well, and build a life that can survive the illness. The therapist who treats bipolar disorder like unipolar depression will harm the client, because the interventions that help depression — activating strategies, encouraging goal-pursuit, reframing negative thoughts — can precipitate or worsen mania.

The judgment problem is that bipolar disorder requires the therapist to work against the client's preferences at key moments. The client in depression wants to withdraw and the therapist must encourage activation; the client in hypomania wants to pursue ambitious goals and the therapist must counsel caution; the client who feels well wants to stop medication and the therapist must support continuation. The therapeutic alliance must be strong enough to sustain these tensions, and the therapist must distinguish between the client's authentic preferences and the preferences generated by the illness.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Baseline Pattern and Identify Early Warning Signs

Begin with a thorough history of the client's mood episodes — the number, type, duration, triggers, and consequences of each, going back to adolescence if possible. Identify the prodromal signs that precede mania (decreased need for sleep, increased energy, racing thoughts, increased goal-directed activity, grandiosity) and depression (fatigue, anhedonia, hypersomnia, withdrawal, negative cognitions). These signs are individual; one client's first sign of mania is buying things, another's is calling old friends at 3 AM, another's is starting ambitious projects.

Create a written relapse signature with the client — a personalized list of their early warning signs, developed collaboratively and shared with their support system. This document becomes the reference point for early intervention: when the client or a family member notices the signs, the response is not panic but a pre-planned set of actions — contact the prescriber, prioritize sleep, reduce stimulation, postpone major decisions. The client who recognizes mania at the prodromal stage can often prevent a full episode; the client who recognizes it at full mania usually cannot.

### Prioritize Sleep and Rhythm as Clinical Interventions

Sleep disruption is both a symptom and a trigger of bipolar episodes — decreased need for sleep is one of the earliest and most reliable signs of mania, and sleep deprivation can precipitate mania in vulnerable individuals. Treat sleep regulation as a clinical intervention, not a lifestyle preference. Assess sleep at every session — hours, quality, timing, and the client's sense of whether they need it (the client who feels great on three hours of sleep is not thriving, they are cycling).

Social rhythm therapy — stabilizing the timing of daily activities including sleep, meals, exercise, and social interaction — has evidence for reducing episode frequency. Help the client build a consistent daily routine, with regular sleep and wake times even on weekends, and a plan for maintaining rhythm during travel, shift work, or life transitions. The client who protects their sleep protects their stability; the client who sacrifices sleep for productivity, socializing, or because they "feel fine without it" is courting an episode.

### Support Medication Adherence Through the Cycle of Conviction

Medication non-adherence in bipolar disorder is driven by a predictable cycle: the client feels well, concludes the medication is unnecessary or is holding them back, stops the medication, and relapses. The therapist's role is to anticipate this cycle and work with the client during stable periods to build a commitment to continuation that can withstand the temptation to stop. Explore the client's experience of medication — the side effects, the sense of being "numbed" or "held back," the loss of the hypomanic edge — and acknowledge that these are real costs, not signs of resistance.

Develop an advance directive with the client during a stable period — a written plan for what should happen if they become manic, including who can contact the prescriber, what medication changes are authorized, and what decisions should be postponed. The advance directive allows the client's stable self to govern their ill self, and it gives the support system permission to act when the client lacks insight. Without it, the support system is paralyzed by the client's insistence that they are fine, and the episode escalates.

### Distinguish Authentic Preferences from Illness-Generated Preferences

The client in hypomania announces they are starting a business, ending their relationship, and moving to another city, all in the same week. Are these authentic choices or symptoms? The distinction matters because acting on illness-generated preferences during an episode leads to decisions the stable client will regret — financial ruin, relationship destruction, career damage that persists long after the episode resolves.

Develop with the client, during a stable period, a rule about major decisions during mood episodes: no major financial, relational, or career decisions during mania or depression, or a requirement to consult a designated person before acting. The client who has agreed to this rule in advance has authorized their support system to say "not now" when they are most certain they are right. The therapist's role is not to prevent the client from pursuing their goals but to ensure that the goals are pursued from a stable baseline, not from the transient conviction of an episode.

### Use Psychoeducation to Build the Client's Expertise in Their Own Illness

Psychoeducation is not simply providing information; it is building the client's capacity to manage their own condition. Teach the client about the course of bipolar disorder, the role of medication, the triggers and warning signs, the relationship between sleep and episodes, and the impact of substance use. Use the client's own history as the curriculum — "the last time you stopped sleeping, what happened next?" — so the learning is personal rather than abstract.

Involve family and support persons in psychoeducation, with the client's consent. Family members who understand the illness can recognize early warning signs, support adherence, and respond to episodes without escalating conflict. They are also the people most affected by the client's illness, and they need support to sustain their own wellbeing while caring for someone with a chronic, relapsing condition.

### Address Co-Occurring Substance Use as a Primary Target

Substance use is common in bipolar disorder, often begins as self-medication, and is the single strongest predictor of poor outcome. Substances destabilize mood, interact dangerously with psychiatric medications, disrupt sleep, and impair the judgment needed for medication adherence and early episode recognition. Assess substance use at every session, and integrate treatment rather than referring it out.

The client who uses alcohol to manage depression, cannabis to manage anxiety, or stimulants to extend hypomanic productivity is treating symptoms with substances that worsen the underlying illness. Use motivational interviewing to explore the functions of the substance use, develop alternative coping strategies, and build motivation for change. Harm reduction is appropriate when abstinence is not the immediate goal; the client who reduces use, uses less frequently, or avoids use during vulnerable periods has reduced their risk.

### Navigate the Relational and Occupational Consequences

Bipolar disorder damages relationships and careers in characteristic ways — manic episodes produce conflict, financial decisions, infidelity, and abandonment; depressive episodes produce withdrawal, unmet obligations, and disappointment. The client in stable periods faces the work of repair, and the therapist helps them acknowledge the impact without drowning in shame, make amends where possible, and build structures that reduce future harm.

Work with the client on disclosure decisions — who to tell about the diagnosis, what to say, and how to manage stigma. Employment is a particular challenge; the client may need accommodations, may have gaps in their work history from episodes, and may struggle with whether to disclose to employers. Support the client in building a vocational path that is sustainable, which may mean adjusting ambitions to match the reality of a chronic illness rather than repeating the cycle of overcommitting during hypomania and crashing during depression.

## Common Traps

### Treating Bipolar Depression with Unipolar Interventions

The client presents in depression and the therapist, missing the bipolar diagnosis or treating the depression in isolation, uses activating interventions — behavioral activation, goal-setting, cognitive restructuring of negative thoughts — that are effective for unipolar depression but can precipitate mania in bipolar disorder. The trap is that bipolar depression is not unipolar depression, and antidepressants without a mood stabilizer, or activating therapy without attention to the cycle, can flip the client into mania, hypomania, or mixed states that are more dangerous than the depression. Always assess for bipolarity before treating depression, and coordinate with the prescriber about medication.

### Validating Hypomanic Goals as Authentic Choices

The client in hypomania is energetic, optimistic, and full of plans, and the therapist — relieved to see the client engaged and productive — supports the new business, the relationship change, the major purchase. The trap is that hypomanic goals are illness-generated preferences that the stable client will not endorse, and the therapist who validates them has aligned with the illness against the client's long-term interests. The decision to support or defer major life changes must be governed by the mood state, and the default during hypomania is to defer, not to validate.

### Abandoning Medication Work When the Client Is Stable

The client is stable, adherent, and functioning well, and the therapist shifts focus to other issues — relationships, work, personal growth — and stops addressing medication. The trap is that stability is the result of medication, and the client is most at risk of stopping medication precisely when they feel well, because the illness is invisible and the medication feels unnecessary. The therapist who stops discussing medication during stable periods has abandoned the intervention that is producing the stability, and the relapse that follows will be attributed to the illness rather than to the therapeutic neglect.

### Failing to Recognize Mixed States and Their Suicide Risk

The client appears depressed but agitated, with racing thoughts, severe insomnia, and intense psychic pain, and the therapist treats it as worsening depression. The trap is that this is a mixed state — depressive symptoms with manic features — and mixed states carry the highest suicide risk of any mood presentation. The client in a mixed state has the despair of depression combined with the energy and impulsivity of mania, and the risk of lethal self-harm is acute. Recognize mixed states, communicate urgently with the prescriber, and assess suicide risk at every contact until the state resolves.

### Letting the Support System Flounder Without Education

The therapist works with the client individually and never involves the family, who are left to cope with episodes without understanding the illness, the warning signs, or how to respond. The trap is that the family is the client's primary early warning system and the people most affected by the illness, and an uneducated family will escalate conflict during mania, enable withdrawal during depression, and miss the warning signs that could trigger early intervention. Family psychoeducation reduces relapse rates and improves outcomes; the therapist who does not involve the support system is undertreating the illness.

### Avoiding the Trauma and Comorbidity That Maintain the Illness

The therapist focuses on mood management and avoids the trauma history, the anxiety disorder, the ADHD, or the personality features that coexist with the bipolar disorder, because "we need to stabilize the mood first." The trap is that comorbid conditions maintain the mood instability — trauma drives substance use, anxiety disrupts sleep, ADHD undermines the routine needed for stability — and treating the mood without treating the comorbidity produces incomplete and fragile recovery. Integrated treatment of the whole presentation is more effective than sequential treatment that defers everything until mood is stable.

## Self-Check

- Have I established the client's personalized relapse signature — their individual early warning signs for mania and depression — and shared it with their support system?
- Am I assessing sleep at every session and treating sleep regulation as a clinical intervention, not a lifestyle preference?
- Have I developed an advance directive with the client during a stable period, authorizing the support system to act during episodes?
- Am I distinguishing authentic preferences from illness-generated preferences, and deferring major decisions during mood episodes?
- Have I assessed for bipolarity before using unipolar depression interventions, and am I coordinating with the prescriber about medication?
- Am I continuing to address medication adherence during stable periods, when the risk of discontinuation is highest?
- Do I recognize mixed states and their elevated suicide risk, and am I assessing safety at every contact during these periods?
- Have I involved the support system in psychoeducation, and am I addressing comorbid trauma, anxiety, and substance use rather than deferring them?
