# Working with Psychosis and Schizophrenia

Working with clients who experience psychosis requires the therapist to hold a position that is difficult to sustain: taking the client's subjective experience seriously without validating the content of delusions, building alliance with a person whose reality testing is impaired, and supporting recovery in a condition that is chronic, stigmatized, and often interleaved with trauma, substance use, and social marginalization. The client with schizophrenia may be the most clinically complex person a therapist will see, and the therapeutic errors that are tolerable in other populations — confrontation, intellectualized interpretation, rigid adherence to a single modality — can cause rupture, disengagement, and decompensation here.

The judgment problem is that psychosis fundamentally disrupts the shared reality that therapy usually depends on. The therapist cannot simply "meet the client where they are" if where they are is a delusional system, and cannot challenge the delusion without rupturing the alliance. The work requires a nuanced stance — acknowledging the client's experience and distress without confirming or disputing the content, focusing on the functional and emotional impact rather than the truth value, and building the client's capacity to cope with symptoms rather than eliminate them. The therapist who treats psychosis like anxiety or depression will fail, and the failure will reinforce the client's belief that therapy does not work for them.

## Core Rules

### Build Alliance Through the Shared Experience of Symptoms, Not Reality Testing

The therapeutic alliance with a client in psychosis is not built on agreement about what is real; it is built on the therapist's genuine interest in the client's experience and their shared work on the distress and functional impairment the symptoms cause. Ask about the voices — what they say, when they are loudest, what makes them better or worse — not to assess or challenge, but to understand the client's daily reality. Acknowledge how frightening, exhausting, or demoralizing it must be to live with these experiences, and let the client feel heard in a way they rarely do.

Do not dispute delusional content. The client who believes they are being monitored has arrived at that belief through a process that feels logical to them, and presenting evidence to the contrary will be experienced as either naivety (you cannot see what is obvious) or complicity (you are part of it). At the same time, do not confirm or collude with the delusion — saying "that must be scary" acknowledges the emotion without endorsing the content. The stance is: I do not share your interpretation, but I take your suffering seriously, and I am here to help with it.

### Use Recovery-Oriented, Strengths-Based Goals, Not Symptom Elimination

The historical model of schizophrenia treatment was stabilization and maintenance — keep the client out of the hospital, on medication, and managed. The recovery model, supported by decades of outcome research, holds that clients with serious mental illness can and do achieve meaningful lives that include work, relationships, and self-determination, even with ongoing symptoms. Set goals that are meaningful to the client — returning to school, repairing a relationship, managing their own money, living independently — rather than goals defined by symptom reduction alone.

The distinction matters because symptom elimination is often not achievable and framing it as the goal sets the client up for a lifetime of failure. The client who still hears voices but has returned to part-time work, has a friend, and manages their own appointments has achieved recovery by any meaningful definition. Help the client identify their values and build a life that includes the illness rather than waiting for the illness to resolve before life can begin.

### Integrate Cognitive-Behavioral Approaches for Persistent Psychosis

Cognitive-behavioral therapy for psychosis (CBTp) is an evidence-based approach that helps clients examine the relationship between their thoughts, beliefs, and distress, and develop alternative interpretations and coping strategies. CBTp does not aim to convince the client that their beliefs are wrong; it aims to help the client consider alternative explanations, reduce the distress and behavioral impact of the beliefs, and build coping skills. The work is collaborative and Socratic, not directive or argumentative.

A client who believes neighbors are entering their apartment at night might be helped to consider: what is the evidence for and against this, what are other possible explanations for the sounds they hear, what would they tell a friend who had this belief, and what coping strategies reduce the distress enough to function. The goal is not to eliminate the belief but to loosen its grip and reduce the behavioral consequences — checking locks 50 times, confronting neighbors, stopping sleep. The therapist who attempts to argue the client out of the belief will lose the alliance and achieve nothing.

### Address Negative Symptoms with Patience and Activation, Not Pressure

Negative symptoms — blunted affect, alogia, avolition, anhedonia, social withdrawal — are often more disabling than positive symptoms and more resistant to medication. They are easily misread as depression, laziness, lack of motivation, or therapeutic non-engagement, and the therapist who pushes too hard will overwhelm a client whose capacity for initiation and social engagement is genuinely reduced.

Work with negative symptoms through graded activation, environmental structure, and behavioral activation rather than insight-oriented exploration. Structure sessions with clear, small, achievable tasks. Use behavioral activation principles — schedule small pleasurable or mastery activities, build routine, reduce the cognitive load of decision-making. Recognize that improvement is slow and measured in small increments, and that the client who attends a session, makes eye contact, and speaks a few sentences has made meaningful effort. Patience is not passivity; it is calibrated pacing.

### Support Medication Adherence as a Collaborative, Ongoing Process

Medication non-adherence in schizophrenia is the rule, not the exception, and it is the most common cause of relapse and rehospitalization. The therapist's role is not to enforce adherence but to support the client's informed, autonomous relationship with medication. Explore the client's experience with medication — what helps, what causes side effects, what they dislike, what they fear — and help them weigh the benefits and costs in the context of their goals.

Do not dismiss the client's complaints about side effects, even if they seem minor to you. Weight gain, sedation, sexual dysfunction, tremor, and emotional numbing are real and distressing, and they are legitimate reasons a person might choose to stop medication. Help the client communicate with their prescriber about side effects rather than stopping unilaterally, and support them through the trial-and-error process of finding a regimen that works. The client who feels heard about medication is more likely to adhere than the client who feels lectured.

### Coordinate Care with the Psychiatric Team and Support System

Clients with serious mental illness are typically served by a team — psychiatrist, case manager, vocational specialist, family members, and sometimes a community support worker. The therapist is one node in this network, and care that is not coordinated will be fragmented and ineffective. With the client's consent, communicate regularly with the prescriber about symptom changes, functional progress, and any concerns about medication. Share information that helps the team serve the client better, and receive information that helps you understand the client's context.

Involve family and support persons when the client consents and when the involvement is therapeutic. Family psychoeducation — helping family understand the illness, respond to symptoms without escalation, and support the client's autonomy — reduces relapse rates significantly. Do not assume the family is part of the problem; many families are the client's primary support and want to help but do not know how.

### Assess and Address Co-Occurring Substance Use

Substance use is extremely common in serious mental illness, often begins as self-medication for symptoms or side effects, and worsens the course of the illness, interferes with medication, and increases the risk of relapse, homelessness, and incarceration. Assess substance use directly and without judgment, and integrate substance use treatment into the therapeutic work rather than treating it as a separate problem to be resolved before therapy can proceed.

Integrated treatment — addressing both the mental illness and the substance use in the same setting, with the same team — is more effective than sequential or parallel treatment. Use motivational interviewing to explore the client's relationship with substances, help them identify the functions the substance serves (sleep, anxiety, social connection, symptom relief), and develop alternative strategies. Harm reduction is appropriate when abstinence is not the client's goal; the client who reduces use, uses more safely, or uses less frequently has made meaningful progress.

## Common Traps

### Confronting or Disputing Delusional Content

The client describes a delusional belief, and the therapist — trained to challenge cognitive distortions — presents evidence, offers alternative explanations, or directly disputes the belief. The trap is that this approach, which works with anxiety and depression, ruptures the alliance with a psychotic client. The client experiences the challenge as proof that the therapist is naive, uninformed, or part of the system they believe is persecuting them, and they disengage from therapy entirely. The delusion is not a cognitive distortion to be corrected; it is a fixed belief held with high conviction, and the therapeutic work is on the distress and function, not the content.

### Misreading Negative Symptoms as Resistance or Depression

The client sits silently, shows no emotion, does not complete homework, and seems disengaged — and the therapist interprets this as resistance, lack of motivation, or depression, and responds with confrontation, pressure, or a referral for depression treatment. The trap is that these are negative symptoms of schizophrenia — blunted affect, alogia, avolition — which are neurological in origin and do not respond to the approaches used for resistance or depression. Confronting negative symptoms as if they were motivational problems overwhelms the client and confirms their sense of failure; the correct response is graded activation, structure, and patience calibrated to genuinely reduced capacity.

### Framing Recovery as Symptom Elimination

The therapist sets goals with the client around eliminating voices, ending delusional beliefs, or achieving a "normal" mental state, and every session becomes a measure of how far the client is from symptom-free. The trap is that schizophrenia is typically chronic, symptoms often persist despite treatment, and framing recovery as symptom elimination sets the client up for a lifetime of perceived failure. The client who still hears voices but has rebuilt a social life, returned to part-time work, and manages their own treatment has recovered by any meaningful definition — but not if the therapist has defined recovery as silence.

### Abandoning the Client During Relapse

The client decompensates — becomes more disorganized, paranoid, or symptomatic — and the therapist, feeling unable to help or fearing the decompensation, reduces contact or refers elsewhere. The trap is that relapse is part of the course of serious mental illness, and the client needs their therapist most during these periods. The work during relapse is not intensive insight-oriented therapy; it is practical support, coordination with the psychiatric team, safety assessment, and continuity of the relationship. The therapist who stays engaged through relapse, adjusted to the client's capacity, builds a trust that no symptom-free period can establish.

### Treating the Illness Without Addressing the Trauma

The client has schizophrenia and a history of severe trauma — childhood abuse, assault, homelessness, institutional betrayal — and the therapist focuses exclusively on symptom management, never addressing the trauma because "they cannot handle it." The trap is that trauma is often the context in which the illness developed and the lens through which symptoms are interpreted, and untreated trauma maintains symptoms, undermines functioning, and drives substance use. Trauma-informed care, and when appropriate trauma-focused treatment adapted for psychosis, is not contraindicated — it is necessary, and the therapist who avoids it because of the diagnosis is undertreating the whole person.

### Failing to Coordinate with the Prescriber

The therapist notices the client is increasingly paranoid, sleeping poorly, and stopping medication, but does not communicate with the prescriber because "that is not my role" or because consent was never formally obtained. The trap is that the therapist often sees the client more frequently than the prescriber and is the first to detect early warning signs of relapse, and the information that could trigger a medication adjustment or an earlier appointment is lost. Establish consent for coordination at the outset, and communicate proactively — the prescriber cannot act on information they do not have.

## Self-Check

- Am I building alliance through shared understanding of the client's experience rather than reality testing or disputing delusional content?
- Are my goals recovery-oriented — focused on the client's values, functioning, and self-determination — rather than symptom elimination?
- Am I using CBTp principles to help the client examine and cope with beliefs, not to argue them out of them?
- When I see negative symptoms, am I responding with graded activation and patience rather than confrontation or a depression referral?
- Am I supporting medication adherence collaboratively — exploring the client's experience, validating side effect concerns, and helping them communicate with their prescriber?
- Have I established consent for and am I maintaining coordination with the psychiatric team and support system?
- Have I assessed co-occurring substance use and integrated it into the treatment rather than treating it as a separate prerequisite?
- Am I addressing trauma history in a trauma-informed way, not avoiding it because of the psychosis diagnosis?
