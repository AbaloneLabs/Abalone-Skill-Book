# Sexual Dysfunction Assessment and Treatment

Sexual dysfunction assessment and treatment require the therapist to hold a problem that sits at the intersection of the biological, the psychological, the relational, and the cultural, and the failure modes are the over-attribution to any single domain. The judgment problem is that the sexual concerns are common, the distress is real, and the therapist's discomfort or the enthusiasm can each produce the harm: the therapist who is uncomfortable may avoid the assessment, minimize the concern, or refer prematurely; the therapist who is enthusiastic may pathologize the normal variation, medicalize the relational problem, or deliver the technique without the assessment. The sexual response is the biopsychosocial, and the dysfunction may be the medical (the hormonal, the vascular, the neurological, the medication-induced), the psychological (the anxiety, the depression, the trauma, the body image), the relational (the conflict, the communication, the desire discrepancy), or the contextual (the stress, the fatigue, the cultural). The therapist must conduct the thorough biopsychosocial assessment, rule out the medical causes through the physician coordination, and deliver the sex therapy (the sensate focus, the behavioral techniques, the cognitive restructuring) within the scope. The therapist's freedom is real, but the medical coordination is essential, and the scope of the practice (the assessment and the therapy, not the medical diagnosis or the physical examination) must be maintained. This skill covers the assessment, the differential, the treatment, and the medical coordination. Given the clinical and the ethical stakes, this guidance is conservative: assess the biopsychosocial, coordinate with the physician, treat within the scope, and refer beyond it.

## Core Rules

### Conduct the Thorough Biopsychosocial Sexual Assessment

The assessment must be the thorough biopsychosocial, covering the sexual concern (the onset, the course, the duration, the context, the partner-specific or the generalized), the medical history (the conditions, the medications, the surgeries, the hormonal status), the psychological factors (the mood, the anxiety, the trauma, the body image, the self-esteem), the relational factors (the satisfaction, the communication, the conflict, the desire discrepancy), and the contextual factors (the stress, the fatigue, the privacy, the cultural and the religious context), because the sexual dysfunction is the multifactorial, and the incomplete assessment produces the incorrect formulation and the failed treatment. The assessment must use the specific sexual history (the sexual behaviors, the orientation, the gender, the practices, the safety), and the therapist must be the comfortable, the nonjudgmental, and the specific. The assessment must include the partner when the dysfunction is the relational, and must assess the partner's perspective and the satisfaction.

The tradeoff is the thorough assessment (the complete, but the time-consuming and the potentially uncomfortable) versus the focused assessment (the efficient, but the incomplete). The resolution is to conduct the thorough biopsychosocial assessment, to be the comfortable and the specific, and to use the validated measures when available.

### Rule Out the Medical Causes Through the Physician Coordination

The medical causes of the sexual dysfunction (the hormonal, the vascular, the neurological, the medication-induced, the surgical, the disease-related) must be ruled out through the physician coordination, because the medical causes are common and the psychological treatment of the medical dysfunction fails, and the untreated medical cause may be the serious (the cardiovascular, the endocrine). The therapist must assess the medical factors (the conditions, the medications, the onset correlation), refer the client for the medical evaluation (the primary care, the urology, the gynecology, the endocrinology), and coordinate with the physician (with the client's consent). The common medication causes (the SSRIs, the antihypertensives, the hormonal contraceptives) must be assessed, and the medication effect must be distinguished from the psychological. The therapist must not assume the psychological cause without the medical rule-out.

The trap is the psychological treatment of the medical dysfunction, which fails and may delay the medical diagnosis. The defense is to rule out the medical causes through the physician coordination, to assess the medications, and to coordinate the care.

### Apply the Sensate Focus and the Behavioral Techniques Within the Scope

The sex therapy techniques (the sensate focus, the directed masturbation, the stop-start, the squeeze, the systematic desensitization, the cognitive restructuring) must be applied within the scope of the licensed therapist, and the therapist must be trained in the sex therapy, because the techniques are the specific and the effective, but the untrained application may be the ineffective or the harmful. The sensate focus (the graduated, the non-demand, the sensual touch exercises) is the foundational technique for the anxiety-based and the relational dysfunction, and the behavioral techniques (the directed masturbation for the anorgasmia, the stop-start for the premature ejaculation) are the specific. The therapist must apply the techniques with the clear rationale, the homework, the processing, and the adjustment, and must work with the individual or the couple as the formulation indicates.

The tradeoff is the technique-focused approach (the efficient, but the mechanistic) versus the formulation-based approach (the effective, but the complex). The resolution is to base the treatment on the formulation, to apply the techniques within the scope, and to process the relational and the psychological material.

### Distinguish the Lifelong From the Acquired and the Generalized From the Situational

The sexual dysfunction must be distinguished as the lifelong (the present since the sexual activity began) versus the acquired (the developed after the period of the normal function), and the generalized (the across the partners and the situations) versus the situational (the specific partner or the situation), because the distinction informs the formulation and the treatment, and the lifelong and the generalized suggest the more biological or the deep-seated factors, and the acquired and the situational suggest the more relational or the contextual. The therapist must assess the onset (the lifelong, the acquired), the context (the generalized, the situational), the course, and the partner-specific factors, and must formulate the treatment accordingly. The acquired change requires the medical and the psychological assessment, and the situational dysfunction requires the relational assessment.

The trap is the failure to distinguish, which produces the undifferentiated treatment. The defense is to assess the onset and the context, to distinguish the lifelong from the acquired and the generalized from the situational, and to formulate the treatment.

### Address the Relational and the Contextual Contributors

The relational and the contextual contributors (the conflict, the communication, the desire discrepancy, the stress, the fatigue, the cultural context) must be addressed, because the sexual dysfunction is rarely the purely individual, and the relational work is often the essential, and the contextual contributors (the stress, the fatigue) may be the primary. The therapist must assess the relational satisfaction, the communication, the conflict, and the desire discrepancy, and must address these through the couples work (the communication skills, the conflict resolution, the desire negotiation) when indicated. The contextual contributors must be assessed and the addressed (the stress management, the lifestyle). The therapist must work with the couple when the dysfunction is the relational, and must not treat the individual in the relational vacuum.

The trap is the individual treatment of the relational dysfunction, which fails. The defense is to address the relational and the contextual contributors, to work with the couple, and to integrate the relational work.

### Maintain the Nonjudgmental and the Inclusive Stance

The therapist must maintain the nonjudgmental and the inclusive stance toward the sexual orientation, the gender, the relationship structure, the sexual practices, and the cultural context, because the judgment or the assumption produces the harm (the shame, the disengagement, the pathologizing the normal variation), and the inclusive stance produces the accurate assessment and the effective treatment. The therapist must assess the client's sexual values and the cultural context, must not impose the therapist's values, and must be the knowledgeable about the diverse sexual practices and the relationship structures. The therapist must distinguish the distress from the variation (the low desire that is not the distress, the atypical interest that is not the disorder), and must treat the dysfunction only when it causes the distress.

The trap is the pathologizing the normal variation, which produces the shame and the harm. The defense is to maintain the nonjudgmental and the inclusive stance, to assess the client's values, and to distinguish the distress from the variation.

## Common Traps

### The Psychological Treatment of the Medical Dysfunction

The therapist assumes the psychological cause and delivers the sex therapy, without the medical rule-out, so the underlying medical cause (the hormonal, the vascular, the medication-induced) goes untreated, and the psychological treatment fails. The mechanism of the trap is that the medical causes are common and the psychological treatment of the medical dysfunction fails, because the therapist does not coordinate with the physician, assumes the psychological cause, and the medical cause (the cardiovascular, the endocrine) goes undiagnosed. The false signal is the psychological presentation (the anxiety, the relational stress), which suggests the psychological cause but masks the medical contributor. The harm is the failed treatment, the delayed diagnosis, and the untreated medical condition. The defense is to rule out the medical causes through the physician coordination, to assess the medications, and to coordinate the care.

### The Pathologizing the Normal Variation

The therapist pathologizes the normal variation (the low desire that is not the distress, the atypical interest that is not the disorder, the orientation or the practice that is outside the therapist's experience), so the client feels the shame and the pathologized. The mechanism of the trap is that the sexual response varies widely, and the pathologizing the normal variation (the low desire without the distress, the atypical interest without the harm) produces the shame, because the therapist applies the normative standard that does not fit the client, or the therapist's discomfort is interpreted as the judgment. The false signal is the deviation from the norm, which suggests the disorder but masks the normal variation. The harm is the shame, the disengagement, and the harm to the client. The defense is to maintain the nonjudgmental and the inclusive stance, to assess the distress, and to distinguish the variation from the dysfunction.

### The Technique Without the Assessment

The therapist delivers the sex therapy technique (the sensate focus, the behavioral technique) without the thorough assessment, because the technique is the known and the efficient, so the technique is applied to the wrong formulation and fails. The mechanism of the trap is that the technique must be based on the formulation, and the technique without the assessment is the undifferentiated application, because the therapist applies the known technique without the assessment of the cause, and the wrong cause produces the failed technique. The false signal is the known technique, which suggests the treatment but masks the missing assessment. The harm is the failed treatment, the wasted time, and the client discouragement. The defense is to conduct the thorough assessment, to formulate, and to apply the technique based on the formulation.

### The Individual Treatment of the Relational Dysfunction

The therapist treats the individual for the sexual dysfunction that is the relational (the conflict, the desire discrepancy, the communication), without the partner involvement, so the relational cause goes unaddressed and the treatment fails. The mechanism of the trap is that the sexual dysfunction is often the relational, and the individual treatment of the relational dysfunction fails, because the therapist works with the individual without the partner, and the relational cause (the conflict, the desire discrepancy) goes unaddressed. The false signal is the individual presentation (the client's distress), which suggests the individual treatment but masks the relational cause. The harm is the failed treatment and the unaddressed relational cause. The defense is to assess the relational factors, to involve the partner, and to work with the couple.

### The Avoidance of the Sexual Assessment

The therapist is uncomfortable with the sexual content and avoids the thorough sexual assessment, so the dysfunction is under-assessed, the formulation is incomplete, and the treatment is the generic. The mechanism of the trap is that the sexual assessment requires the comfort and the specificity, and the uncomfortable therapist avoids the assessment, because the therapist's discomfort leads to the avoidance, the vague questions, and the incomplete history, and the dysfunction is under-assessed. The false signal is the client's initial vagueness, which suggests the comfort but masks the therapist's avoidance. The harm is the incomplete assessment, the incorrect formulation, and the failed treatment. The defense is to be the comfortable, the specific, and the thorough, to use the structured assessment, and to seek the sex therapy training.

### The Boundary Violation Through the Technique

The therapist, in the enthusiasm or the inexperience, crosses the boundary (the physical touch, the explicit demonstration, the dual relationship), so the client is harmed and the therapy is compromised. The mechanism of the trap is that the sex therapy involves the explicit content, and the boundary violation (the touch, the demonstration, the dual relationship) is the risk, because the therapist confuses the sex therapy with the physical practice, or the enthusiasm leads to the boundary crossing, and the client is harmed. The false signal is the therapeutic intent, which suggests the help but masks the boundary violation. The harm is the client harm, the ethical violation, and the legal liability. The defense is to maintain the clear boundaries (the sex therapy is the talk therapy, not the physical practice), to seek the supervision, and to refer the physical concerns.
