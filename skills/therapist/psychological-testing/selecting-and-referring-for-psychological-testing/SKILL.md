# Selecting And Referring For Psychological Testing

Psychological and neuropsychological testing can answer questions that clinical interview cannot, but only when the right evaluation is requested for the right question. The therapist's central judgment is to recognize when a clinical question requires formal testing rather than interview alone, to select the type of evaluation that fits the question, to write a referral that gives the evaluator the specific information and questions that will shape the assessment, and to integrate the results into the treatment rather than filing them away. The recurring failures are referring generically without specifying the question, choosing the wrong type of evaluation for the question, treating testing as a substitute for clinical judgment, and failing to integrate results into the ongoing care.

Use this skill when deciding whether to refer for testing, selecting the type of evaluation, writing a testing referral, or deciding among psychoeducational, neuropsychological, personality, and diagnostic evaluations.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Specific Clinical Question That Testing Should Answer

A useful testing referral begins with a specific clinical question, not a general request for "testing." Identify what you need to know that interview alone has not answered: Is this ADHD, anxiety, or both, and how do they interact? What is the cognitive profile after a head injury or in suspected dementia? What is the personality structure underlying treatment-resistant symptoms? What is the learning profile affecting school performance? Does this client have an autism spectrum condition that has been missed? A specific question guides the evaluator to the right instruments and the right interpretation, and it makes the results useful. Vague referrals produce vague reports that do not answer the question that drove the referral.

### Match The Type Of Evaluation To The Question

Different evaluations answer different questions, and matching the type to the question is essential. Psychoeducational evaluation addresses learning disabilities, academic skills, and school-related cognitive functioning, and supports IEP and 504 determinations. Neuropsychological evaluation provides a comprehensive map of cognitive domains, attention, memory, executive function, language, visuospatial, and motor, and is indicated for head injury, dementia, neurological conditions, and complex cognitive presentations. Personality assessment, structured and projective, addresses personality structure, diagnostic clarification, and treatment planning for complex clinical presentations. Diagnostic clarification evaluation addresses questions where the diagnosis is uncertain and the diagnosis matters for treatment. Choose the evaluation that fits the question, and consult the evaluator if unsure which type is appropriate.

### Write A Referral That Gives The Evaluator The Questions And Context

A good referral gives the evaluator the specific questions, the relevant history, the presenting concerns, what has been tried, and what you hope to gain from the evaluation. Include the history that bears on the question: developmental, educational, medical, psychiatric, and family history; prior evaluations and their dates; current medications; and the specific observations or symptoms that prompted the referral. State the questions clearly, in order of priority, and note what you need from the report to guide treatment. A referral that says "evaluate for ADHD" with no context produces a report that may not address your question; a referral that says "question ADHD versus anxiety, given these specific observations and this history, with implications for medication and accommodation decisions" produces a useful report.

### Recognize When Testing Is Indicated And When It Is Not

Testing is indicated when the clinical question cannot be answered adequately by interview and history, when the answer will change the treatment plan, when diagnostic clarification is needed for medication or accommodation decisions, when cognitive or learning profiles are suspected, or when the course of treatment has stalled and a deeper understanding may help. Testing is not indicated when the clinical picture is already clear, when the results would not change the plan, when the client is in acute crisis that precludes valid assessment, or when the question is motivational rather than diagnostic. Do not refer reflexively for testing when interview is sufficient, and do not avoid testing when it would clarify a question that is stalling the treatment. Match the referral to the clinical need.

### Consider Validity, Effort, And Context In Interpreting Results

Test results are only as valid as the conditions under which they were obtained, and the therapist integrating the results must consider factors that affect validity. Was the client's effort adequate, or were there signs of insufficient effort or malingering, particularly in forensic, disability, or litigation contexts? Were there cultural, linguistic, or educational factors that affect the validity of the norms applied? Were there acute state factors, anxiety, depression, fatigue, medication effects, or recent substance use, that affected performance? A good report addresses these factors; if it does not, raise them with the evaluator. Do not treat test scores as transparent truth; treat them as data obtained under specific conditions, and integrate them with the clinical picture.

### Integrate Testing Results Into The Treatment Plan

Testing that is not integrated into the treatment is wasted. Read the full report, not only the summary, and attend to the recommendations. Translate the findings into implications for the therapy: a neuropsychological profile revealing executive function weaknesses suggests specific compensatory strategies; a personality assessment revealing specific structure suggests specific treatment approaches; a diagnostic clarification changes the formulation. Discuss the results with the client in understandable terms, use them to refine the goals and methods, and coordinate with the prescriber or school as indicated. A testing report that sits in the chart has not helped the client; one that shapes the treatment has.

### Coordinate With The Evaluator Before And After The Assessment

With authorization, speak with the evaluator before the assessment to clarify the questions and the context, and after the assessment to discuss the findings and their implications. The pre-assessment conversation helps the evaluator tailor the battery and the interpretation to your questions; the post-assessment conversation lets you ask about findings that bear on treatment, about factors affecting validity, and about recommendations. A report is a poor substitute for a conversation with the clinician who wrote it, and the coordination improves the integration. Document the communications and how they informed the plan.

## Common Traps

### Referring Generically Without Specifying The Question

A therapist may refer for "psychological testing" or "an evaluation" without specifying the question, assuming the evaluator will figure out what is needed, when a generic referral produces a generic report that does not answer the specific question that drove the referral. The mechanism of harm is that the absence of a specific question leaves the evaluator to guess, the battery may not address the actual question, and the report, however thorough, does not help the treatment. The harm is that the client undergoes an expensive, time-consuming evaluation that does not inform the care, and the question remains unanswered. Writing a referral with specific, prioritized questions, and the relevant context, ensures the evaluation serves the treatment.

### Choosing The Wrong Type Of Evaluation For The Question

A therapist may choose the wrong type of evaluation, referring for psychoeducational testing when the question is neuropsychological, or for personality assessment when the question is diagnostic, because the types overlap in name but differ in focus and instruments. The mechanism of harm is that the evaluation addresses adjacent but not the right domains, the report does not answer the question, and the client has undergone the wrong assessment. The harm is wasted time and resources, an unanswered question, and sometimes a misleading conclusion based on the wrong instruments. Matching the type of evaluation to the question, and consulting the evaluator when unsure, prevents this mismatch.

### Treating Testing As A Substitute For Clinical Judgment

A therapist may treat testing results as definitive truth that replaces clinical judgment, applying the scores and labels directly to the treatment without integrating them with the clinical picture, when test results are data obtained under specific conditions and require clinical integration. The mechanism of harm is that the test is privileged over the relationship and the ongoing observation, and the treatment is driven by scores rather than by the client's actual presentation and course. The harm is that the treatment becomes rigid, the client's changing presentation is not reflected, and the testing, however valid at the time, ossifies into a fixed picture. Integrating results with clinical judgment, and revising as the picture evolves, keeps the treatment alive.

### Failing To Integrate Results Into The Ongoing Care

A therapist may receive the testing report, file it in the chart, and continue the treatment unchanged, treating the evaluation as a completed task rather than as information that should shape the care. The mechanism of harm is that the findings and recommendations are never translated into the treatment, the client underwent the evaluation for nothing, and the care continues as if the question had never been raised. The harm is wasted resources, an unanswered question, and a missed opportunity to refine the treatment. Reading the full report, discussing it with the client, translating findings into the plan, and coordinating with the evaluator integrates the results.

### Ignoring Validity Factors When Interpreting Results

A therapist may accept test scores at face value without considering factors that affect validity, effort, cultural and linguistic fit, acute state, or context, when these factors can substantially affect the meaning of the scores. The mechanism of harm is that scores obtained under compromised conditions are treated as accurate, and the conclusions, and the treatment based on them, are wrong. The harm is that the client receives a diagnosis or plan based on invalid data, the actual issues are missed, and the care is misdirected. Considering validity factors, raising them with the evaluator, and integrating them into the interpretation protects against this error.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I identify the specific clinical question that testing should answer, rather than referring generically?
- [ ] Did I match the type of evaluation, psychoeducational, neuropsychological, personality, or diagnostic, to the question?
- [ ] Did I write a referral with specific, prioritized questions and the relevant history and context?
- [ ] Did I recognize when testing is indicated and when it is not, matching the referral to the clinical need?
- [ ] Did I consider validity factors, including effort, cultural and linguistic fit, and acute state, in interpreting results?
- [ ] Did I integrate the testing results into the treatment plan, reading the full report and translating findings into the care?
- [ ] Did I coordinate with the evaluator before and after the assessment, with authorization and documentation?
- [ ] Did I avoid generic referrals, wrong evaluation types, substituting testing for judgment, failing to integrate, and ignoring validity?
