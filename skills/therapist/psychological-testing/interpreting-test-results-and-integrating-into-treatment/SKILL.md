# Interpreting Test Results And Integrating Into Treatment

A testing report is not a verdict; it is data, obtained under specific conditions, that must be read critically, integrated with clinical knowledge of the client, and translated into treatment. The therapist's central judgment is to read the full report and understand the findings in context, to weigh the results against the client's history and presentation, to translate technical findings into treatment implications, to explain results to the client in terms that support understanding without overwhelming, and to hold the results as one source among several rather than as definitive truth. The recurring failures are reading only the summary, accepting conclusions that do not follow from the data, over-interpreting single scores, treating results as fixed, and failing to explain results to the client in usable terms.

Use this skill when interpreting test results, reading a testing report, translating findings into treatment, explaining results to a client, or integrating assessment data with clinical observation.

## Core Rules

### Read The Full Report, Not Only The Summary

The summary of a testing report condenses the findings, but the data, the reasoning, and the qualifications are in the body, and reading only the summary risks missing important nuances, caveats, and alternative interpretations. Read the full report: the tests administered, the scores with confidence intervals, the qualitative observations, the validity indicators, the interpretation, and the recommendations. Attend to the confidence intervals around scores, because a single number is a point estimate with a range, and overlapping ranges may not represent real differences. Attend to the qualitative observations, which often carry information the scores do not. A thorough reading catches what the summary compresses away, and it lets you weigh the conclusions against the data.

### Weigh Results Against History, Presentation, And Other Data

Test results are one source of information, and they must be weighed against the client's history, current presentation, prior evaluations, and the ongoing clinical observation. A result that fits the clinical picture strengthens the formulation; a result that conflicts with the clinical picture requires reconciliation, not automatic acceptance of either source. Consider whether the testing captured the client on a representative day, whether acute state factors affected performance, whether the results are consistent with the developmental and historical picture, and whether prior data converge or diverge. Integration, not selection of one source, produces the most accurate formulation. Bring apparent conflicts to the evaluator in a follow-up conversation.

### Translate Technical Findings Into Treatment Implications

Translate the testing findings into specific implications for the treatment. A neuropsychological profile revealing working memory and executive function weaknesses suggests compensatory strategies, environmental supports, and accommodation referrals; a personality assessment revealing specific structure suggests particular treatment approaches and anticipated challenges; a diagnostic clarification may change the formulation and the medication plan. Work out what the findings mean for the goals, the methods, the pace, and the anticipated course of the therapy. The translation is the therapist's contribution; the report provides the data, but the treatment implications are worked out in the integration. Document the integration in the treatment plan.

### Explain Results To The Client In Understandable And Usable Terms

Clients have a right to understand their assessment results, and the explanation supports their engagement in the treatment. Explain the findings in plain language, avoiding jargon, using concrete examples, and connecting the findings to the client's experience and the reasons they sought assessment. Address what the results mean for them, what they do not mean, what the implications are for treatment, school, or work, and what the next steps are. Be honest about limitations and uncertainties, and avoid both overstating certainty and burying the client in caveats. A client who understands their assessment can use it; one who is confused or overwhelmed cannot. The explanation is a clinical intervention in itself.

### Hold Results As One Source, Not As Definitive Or Fixed

Test results represent a snapshot obtained under specific conditions, and they should be held as one source of information, not as definitive truth or as a fixed picture of the client. Conditions on the testing day, acute state, effort, rapport with the evaluator, and the limitations of the instruments all affect the results, and the client's presentation evolves over time. Use the results to inform the formulation, but continue to observe and revise. Do not treat a diagnosis or profile from testing as permanent when the clinical picture changes, and do not ignore subsequent clinical observation because the testing said otherwise. The results inform; they do not replace ongoing clinical judgment.

### Use Results To Anticipate Challenges And Tailor The Approach

Testing results often reveal patterns that let the therapist anticipate challenges and tailor the approach. A profile revealing emotional dysregulation and specific personality features suggests anticipated ruptures and the need for a structured frame; cognitive weaknesses suggest the need to adjust the pace, the materials, and the homework demands; learning differences suggest specific accommodations; autism spectrum features suggest specific adjustments to communication and sensory considerations. Use the results proactively, to anticipate what the treatment will need, rather than reactively, after the challenges have derailed the work. The assessment is most useful when it shapes the treatment before problems arise, not only after.

### Address Discrepancies And Limitations Honestly

Reports have limitations and sometimes discrepancies: scores that conflict within the report, conclusions that seem stronger than the data support, validity indicators that raise questions, or findings that conflict with the clinical picture. Address these honestly rather than glossing them. Raise discrepancies with the evaluator, consider alternative interpretations, and qualify the conclusions in the formulation. A formulation that acknowledges the limitations of its data is more accurate and more defensible than one that presents testing as settled truth. Document the qualifications and the reasoning, so that the treatment plan reflects the actual strength of the evidence.

## Common Traps

### Reading Only The Summary And Missing The Data

A therapist may read only the summary and recommendations of a testing report, trusting the evaluator's synthesis, and miss the data, the caveats, the validity indicators, and the alternative interpretations in the body of the report. The mechanism of harm is that the summary compresses away the qualifications, and the therapist applies conclusions whose limitations were never appreciated. The harm is that the treatment is based on an incomplete reading, discrepancies are missed, and the integration is shallow. Reading the full report, attending to the data and the caveats, and weighing the conclusions against the evidence prevents this error.

### Accepting Conclusions That Do Not Follow From The Data

A therapist may accept the conclusions of a report at face value without checking whether they follow from the data presented, when evaluators vary in quality and sometimes overstate their conclusions or rely on instruments inappropriate to the question. The mechanism of harm is that the authority of the report substitutes for critical reading, and conclusions that the data do not fully support are applied to the treatment. The harm is that the client receives a diagnosis or plan based on overstated findings, the actual issues may be missed, and the care is misdirected. Reading the data critically, checking whether the conclusions follow, and raising questions with the evaluator protects against this error.

### Over-Interpreting Single Scores Or Subtest Differences

A therapist may over-interpret a single score or a difference between subtests, treating a point estimate as a definitive finding, when individual scores have confidence intervals and subtest differences are often not clinically meaningful within the range of measurement error. The mechanism of harm is that normal variation is treated as significant, and a profile is built on differences that may reflect noise rather than signal. The harm is that the client receives a formulation based on over-interpreted data, the treatment targets "weaknesses" that are not real, and the client is mislabeled. Attending to confidence intervals, to the reliability of differences, and to the convergence of multiple data points prevents over-interpretation.

### Treating Results As Fixed Rather Than As A Snapshot

A therapist may treat test results as a fixed picture of the client, applying the diagnosis or profile permanently and ignoring subsequent clinical observation, when the results represent a snapshot obtained under specific conditions and the client's presentation evolves. The mechanism of harm is that the testing ossifies into a fixed formulation, and the treatment does not track the client's actual changing presentation. The harm is that the treatment becomes rigid, the client's evolution is not reflected, and care is driven by an outdated picture. Holding results as one source, and revising the formulation as the clinical picture changes, keeps the treatment responsive.

### Failing To Explain Results To The Client In Usable Terms

A therapist may receive the report, integrate it into the treatment plan, and fail to explain the results to the client in terms they can understand and use, when the client has a right to understand their assessment and the explanation supports engagement. The mechanism of harm is that the client is excluded from their own assessment, the findings remain mysterious, and the client cannot use them in their life, school, or work decisions. The harm is that the client loses the benefit of understanding themselves, the engagement suffers, and the assessment becomes something done to them rather than with them. Explaining results in plain, usable terms, with honesty about limitations, is a clinical responsibility.
