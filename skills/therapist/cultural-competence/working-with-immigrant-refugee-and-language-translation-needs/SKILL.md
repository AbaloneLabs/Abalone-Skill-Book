# Working With Immigrant, Refugee, And Language Translation Needs

Immigrant and refugee clients bring specific clinical considerations that extend beyond general cultural competence: migration histories that may include trauma, loss, and danger; acculturation stresses as they navigate a new culture; resettlement challenges including legal, economic, and social pressures; and often the need to work across a language difference, through an interpreter. The therapist's judgment is to understand the client's migration context and its clinical implications, to address acculturation and identity dimensions, to work effectively with interpreters when needed, and to adapt therapy for clients whose cultural and linguistic context differs from the majority. The recurring failures are assuming that standard therapy applies without adaptation, failing to recognize migration trauma and acculturation stress, working poorly with interpreters, and neglecting the practical and legal stressors that shape the client's presentation.

Use this skill when working with immigrant or refugee clients, addressing migration or acculturation issues, or working with interpreters.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Migration Context And Its Clinical Implications

Understand the client's migration context: whether they are an immigrant, by choice or necessity, or a refugee, who fled persecution or danger; their migration history, including trauma, loss, separation from family, and danger; their pre-migration, migration, and post-migration experiences; and their current immigration status and its implications. The migration context shapes the client's presentation, their stressors, their trauma history, and their resources, and understanding it is essential to accurate formulation. Do not assume that all immigrants have the same experience or that migration is the presenting issue; understand the specific context and its clinical implications for this client.

### Address Acculturation, Identity, And Intergenerational Dynamics

Acculturation, the process of adapting to a new culture while maintaining the culture of origin, is a significant dimension for immigrant clients, and it involves identity, values, family roles, and intergenerational dynamics. Explore the client's acculturation experience, including the tensions between cultures, the identity questions, and the family dynamics, especially intergenerational differences between immigrant parents and children raised in the new culture. Acculturation stress is a real clinical phenomenon, and addressing it supports the client's adjustment. Do not assume that acculturation is linear or that more assimilation is better; explore the client's own navigation of their cultural identities.

### Work Effectively With Interpreters When Needed

When the client's primary language differs from the therapist's, work with a trained interpreter, because effective communication is foundational to therapy and because using family members or untrained interpreters is clinically and ethically problematic. Arrange a trained, professional interpreter, ideally one trained in mental health interpretation, and brief the interpreter before the session on the goals and the content. During the session, speak directly to the client, not to the interpreter, use short, clear segments, and allow time for interpretation. Debrief with the interpreter after the session. Recognize that the interpreter is a member of the clinical team, and that working through an interpreter changes the therapy's dynamics, requiring adaptation and patience.

### Recognize And Address Migration And Refugee Trauma

Many immigrants and most refugees have experienced trauma, in their country of origin, during migration, or in resettlement, and this trauma may be central to their presentation. Assess for migration and refugee trauma, including war, persecution, torture, trafficking, loss, and separation, and address it using trauma-informed principles, with stabilization before processing. Recognize that refugee trauma is often complex and political, involving systematic persecution, and that the client may have valid distrust of authorities, including mental health providers. Address the trauma at the client's pace, with appropriate stabilization, and recognize that the trauma and the migration are intertwined with the current presentation.

### Address Practical, Legal, And Resettlement Stressors

Immigrant and refugee clients often face significant practical, legal, and resettlement stressors: immigration proceedings, documentation, employment, housing, language, discrimination, and access to services, and these stressors shape the presentation and the therapy. Address the practical stressors, recognizing that they may be the primary concern and that they affect mental health. Connect the client with resources, including legal services, resettlement agencies, and community organizations, and recognize that addressing practical stressors is part of the care. Do not focus only on the psychological, when the practical stressors are pressing and interconnected with the mental health.

### Adapt Therapy For Cultural And Linguistic Context

Standard therapy approaches, developed in majority-culture, English-speaking contexts, may need adaptation for immigrant and refugee clients, to fit their cultural values, communication styles, and experiences. Adapt the therapy: consider the client's preferences for directiveness, their comfort with emotional expression, their understanding of mental health, and their cultural frameworks for distress and healing. Use culturally adapted approaches when available, such as culturally adapted CBT, and integrate the client's cultural resources, including family, community, religion, and traditional healing. Adaptation is not dilution; it is making the therapy accessible and effective for the client's context.

### Recognize The Role Of Racism, Discrimination, And Xenophobia

Immigrant and refugee clients often experience racism, discrimination, and xenophobia, in employment, housing, healthcare, and daily life, and these experiences are clinically significant, contributing to stress, trauma, and mistrust. Address discrimination and its impact, validate the client's experiences, and recognize that the client may have encountered discrimination from providers, including mental health providers, that affects their trust. Create a safe space for discussing discrimination, and do not minimize or dismiss it. The client's experience of discrimination is part of their context, and addressing it is part of the care.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Standard Therapy Applies Without Adaptation

A therapist may assume that standard therapy, developed in majority-culture contexts, applies universally, and conduct it without adaptation for the immigrant or refugee client's cultural and linguistic context. The mechanism is that the therapist's training presents the approach as universal, and the therapist, not recognizing the cultural assumptions embedded in it, applies it unchanged. The harm is that the therapy does not fit the client's values, communication style, or experience, the client does not engage, and the therapy is ineffective. Adapting the therapy for the client's cultural and linguistic context is what makes it accessible and effective.

### Failing To Recognize Migration Trauma And Acculturation Stress

A therapist may fail to recognize migration trauma and acculturation stress, treating the client's symptoms as a standard mental health condition without understanding the migration context that shapes them. The mechanism is that the therapist focuses on the symptoms, which look familiar, and does not explore the migration context, when the context is central to the formulation. The harm is that the formulation is incomplete, the treatment does not address the trauma or the acculturation stress, and the client does not improve. Understanding the migration context, and recognizing its clinical implications, is what supports accurate formulation and effective treatment.

### Working Poorly With Interpreters

A therapist may work poorly with interpreters, using family members, speaking to the interpreter instead of the client, using long complex statements, or not allowing time for interpretation, which undermines the communication and the therapy. The mechanism is that working with interpreters is a skill the therapist may not have, and the therapist, untrained, makes errors that compromise the communication. The harm is that the communication is inaccurate or incomplete, the client is not fully understood or engaged, and the therapy is ineffective or harmful, especially if family members are used as interpreters for sensitive material. Working effectively with trained interpreters, with briefing, direct address, and debriefing, is what supports the communication.

### Neglecting Practical And Legal Stressors

A therapist may neglect the practical and legal stressors the client faces, focusing on the psychological while the client is preoccupied with immigration proceedings, housing, employment, or documentation, and the therapy feels irrelevant. The mechanism is that the therapist focuses on the clinical, their area of expertise, and does not address the practical stressors, when those stressors are pressing and interconnected with the mental health. The harm is that the client experiences the therapy as irrelevant to their most pressing concerns, disengages, and the mental health does not improve because the stressors persist. Addressing practical and legal stressors, and connecting the client with resources, is what makes the care relevant.

### Minimizing The Client's Experience Of Discrimination

A therapist may minimize or not ask about the client's experience of discrimination, racism, and xenophobia, either because it feels uncomfortable or because the therapist does not recognize its clinical significance, and the client's experience is not addressed. The mechanism is that discrimination is uncomfortable to discuss, and the therapist, avoiding the discomfort, does not raise it, when it is a significant stressor for many immigrant and refugee clients. The harm is that the client's experience is not validated or addressed, the client feels unseen, and a significant stressor is omitted from the formulation. Addressing discrimination, validating the experience, and recognizing its clinical significance, is what makes the care complete.

- [ ] Am I addressing acculturation, identity, and intergenerational dynamics?
- [ ] When an interpreter is needed, am I using a trained professional, briefing, speaking to the client, and debriefing?
- [ ] Am I recognizing and addressing migration and refugee trauma, with stabilization before processing?
- [ ] Am I addressing practical, legal, and resettlement stressors, and connecting the client with resources?
- [ ] Have I adapted the therapy for the client's cultural and linguistic context?
- [ ] Am I recognizing and addressing the client's experience of racism, discrimination, and xenophobia?
- [ ] Did I avoid assuming universality, missing migration context, poor interpreter use, neglecting practical stressors, and minimizing discrimination?
