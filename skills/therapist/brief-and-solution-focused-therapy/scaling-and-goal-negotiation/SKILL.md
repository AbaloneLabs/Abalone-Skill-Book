# Scaling and Goal Negotiation

Scaling questions and goal negotiation are the methods by which solution-focused and brief therapies convert vague complaints into concrete, measurable, collaboratively negotiated change targets, and the judgment problem is that goals are often left vague (the client wants to feel better), imposed by the therapist (the therapist decides what the goal should be), or set without the client's genuine ownership, all of which undermine the focus and the measurement that make brief therapy work. Scaling questions ask the client to rate their position on a scale (typically 0 to 10, where 10 is the preferred future and 0 is the worst), and they serve to make progress visible, to identify what a one-point change would look like, and to locate the client's existing position as a starting point rather than a zero. Goal negotiation is the collaborative process of defining what the client wants to achieve in terms that are concrete, behavioral, achievable, and genuinely the client's. The therapist must negotiate goals collaboratively (the client's goals, not the therapist's), must make goals concrete and behavioral (what specifically would be different), must use scaling to track progress and to identify small next steps, must avoid imposing goals or accepting vague goals, and must revisit goals as understanding deepens. The deeper traps are accepting vague goals, imposing the therapist's goals, using scaling as a measurement ritual rather than as a clinical tool, and failing to connect scales to concrete action. The therapist's freedom is real, but goals must be collaboratively owned, concrete, and tracked. Given the clinical stakes, this guidance is conservative: negotiate goals collaboratively, make them concrete and behavioral, use scaling to track progress and identify next steps, and never impose goals or accept vagueness.

## Core Rules

### Negotiate Goals Collaboratively and Ensure Client Ownership

Goals must be the client's, negotiated collaboratively, and the therapist must ensure genuine client ownership rather than acquiescence. The therapist should elicit the client's goals in their own terms, should explore what the client actually wants (rather than what they think the therapist wants to hear), should negotiate when goals are unclear or conflicting, and should check ownership (does the client genuinely want this, or are they agreeing to please the therapist or others). The therapist should distinguish the client's goals from goals imposed by external pressure (a court, a spouse, a school), should work with the client's own motivation where possible, and should be transparent when external requirements constrain the goals. Goals that the client does not own will not be pursued, regardless of how clinically appropriate they are.

### Make Goals Concrete, Behavioral, and Observable

Vague goals (feel better, be happier, have better relationships) cannot guide action or measure progress, and the therapist must help the client convert them into concrete, behavioral, observable terms. The therapist should ask what specifically would be different (what would you be doing, what would others notice, what would a good day look like), should help the client define the goal in terms of behavior rather than feeling (because behavior can be tracked and chosen while feeling often cannot), and should ensure the goal is specific enough that progress can be recognized. Concrete goals enable both action (the client knows what to do) and measurement (the client and therapist can tell whether progress is occurring), and the conversion from vague to concrete is a core therapeutic act.

### Use Scaling to Track Progress and Identify Small Next Steps

Scaling questions make progress visible and identify small next steps, and the therapist should use them as a clinical tool, not as a measurement ritual. The therapist should establish the scale collaboratively (what would 10 look like, what would 0 look like, where are you now), should explore the current rating in detail (what makes it a 4 rather than a 3, what is already present), should identify what a one-point increase would look like (what would be different at a 5), and should connect the one-point change to a small actionable step. The scale reveals existing resources (why are you at 4 and not 0, what is keeping you from 0) and makes the next step concrete and achievable. The therapist should use scaling across sessions to track progress, should attend to both improvement and stagnation, and should adjust the work based on what the scale reveals.

### Ensure Goals Are Achievable and Appropriately Ambitious

Goals must be achievable (so the client experiences success and builds self-efficacy) and appropriately ambitious (so the goals are meaningful and represent real change), and the therapist must balance these. Goals that are too ambitious set the client up for failure and demoralization, and goals that are too trivial do not represent meaningful change and can feel patronizing. The therapist should help the client set goals that stretch them without overwhelming them, should break large goals into smaller achievable steps, should celebrate progress toward the goal (not only full achievement), and should adjust the goal if it proves too easy or too hard. The balance is collaborative and ongoing.

### Revisit Goals as Understanding Deepens

The initial goals are a starting point, and they should be revisited as the therapy progresses and as the client's understanding deepens. The client may discover that their initial goal was not what they really wanted, or that a different goal has become more important, or that the goal needs refinement as they learn more about what change looks like. The therapist should check in about the goals regularly, should be open to revision, and should not treat the initial goals as fixed. Goal revision is a sign of engagement and deepening understanding, not of instability.

### Distinguish the Client's Goals From the Therapist's Clinical Agenda

The therapist will often have a clinical sense of what would help (the client should reduce drinking, the client should leave the abusive relationship, the client should take the medication), and this clinical agenda may differ from the client's goals. The therapist must distinguish their clinical agenda from the client's goals, must work with the client's goals as the primary frame, must be transparent about concerns (sharing clinical concerns honestly rather than covertly steering), and must respect the client's right to set their own goals even when the therapist disagrees. The therapist can share their perspective, can work with the client's readiness, and can revisit concerns, but cannot substitute their goals for the client's without undermining ownership and agency.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Vague Goals Without Converting Them to Concrete Terms

The therapist accepts the client's vague goal (to feel better, to be happier), because the client stated it and the therapist does not want to push too hard, and the therapy proceeds toward a goal that cannot guide action or measure progress, so neither the client nor the therapist can tell whether change is occurring, and the work drifts without a concrete target. The mechanism is that the vague goal has been accepted without conversion, so the goal cannot function as a guide (the client does not know what to do) or as a measure (the client and therapist cannot tell whether they are progressing), and the therapy lacks the focus that makes brief therapy effective. The false signal is the client's stated goal, which looks like a negotiated target but actually indicates a goal too vague to guide work. The harm is unfocused therapy, inability to track progress, client and therapist uncertainty about whether change is occurring, and the therapy becoming longer and less effective. The defense is to convert vague goals into concrete, behavioral, observable terms, to ask what specifically would be different, and to ensure the goal is specific enough to guide action and measure progress.

### Imposing the Therapist's Goals and Mistaking Acquiescence for Ownership

The therapist, knowing what would clinically help, shapes the goals toward the clinical agenda, because the therapist's goals are evidence-based and in the client's interest, and the client acquiesces (agrees without genuine ownership), because the therapist is the expert and the client wants to cooperate, and the therapy proceeds toward goals the client does not own, which they will not pursue outside the session. The mechanism is that the therapist's goals have been substituted for the client's, so the goals reflect the clinical agenda rather than the client's actual wants, and the client's acquiescence has been mistaken for ownership. The false signal is the client's agreement, which looks like goal ownership but actually indicates compliance without commitment. The harm is goals the client does not pursue, the undermining of client agency, and the failure of change because the goals are not owned. The defense is to elicit the client's goals in their own terms, to check ownership (does the client genuinely want this), to distinguish the clinical agenda from the client's goals, to be transparent about concerns rather than covertly steering, and to work with the client's own motivation.

### Using Scaling as a Measurement Ritual Rather Than a Clinical Tool

The therapist asks the scaling question each session and records the number, because scaling is part of the model and tracking numbers feels rigorous, but does not explore what the number means (what makes it a 4, what would a 5 look like), so the scale becomes a measurement ritual that produces numbers without clinical information, and the opportunity to use the scale to identify resources and next steps is lost. The mechanism is that scaling has been used as measurement rather than as clinical exploration, so the number has been recorded without the detail that makes it useful, and the scale produces data without insight. The false signal is the regular collection of scaling data, which looks like rigorous tracking but actually indicates a ritual without clinical value. The harm is the missed opportunity to identify existing resources (why 4 and not 0), to define the next step concretely (what would 5 look like), and to use the scale as a tool for change rather than merely a record of it. The defense is to explore each rating in detail, to identify what is already present, to define the one-point change concretely, and to use the scale as a clinical tool for identifying resources and next steps.

### Setting Goals That Are Too Ambitious and Producing Demoralization

The therapist, wanting to support meaningful change and confident in the client's potential, collaborates on goals that are large and ambitious, because the goals are important and the client seems capable, but the goals exceed what the client can currently achieve, and the client experiences repeated failure to meet them, producing demoralization and the erosion of self-efficacy, and the client may disengage from the work. The mechanism is that the goals have been set too ambitiously, so the gap between current capacity and the goal produces repeated failure, and each failure undermines the confidence the goals were meant to build. The false signal is the ambition of the goals, which looks like high expectations and confidence in the client but actually indicates a mismatch with current capacity. The harm is demoralization, erosion of self-efficacy, and disengagement, the opposite of what goal-setting is meant to produce. The defense is to balance ambition with achievability, to break large goals into smaller achievable steps, to celebrate progress toward the goal, and to adjust the goal if it proves too hard.

### Failing to Revisit Goals and Pursuing Outdated Targets

The therapist negotiates goals at the start of therapy, because goals are needed to focus the work, and proceeds toward them for the duration, because the goals were set and the therapy is following the plan, but does not revisit the goals as understanding deepens, and the client may have shifted (a different goal has become more important, the initial goal was not what they really wanted), and the therapy pursues outdated targets that no longer reflect the client's current needs. The mechanism is that the goals have been treated as fixed rather than revisited, so the initial negotiation has not been updated, and the therapy pursues goals the client may no longer hold. The false signal is the consistency of pursuing the original goals, which looks like focused work but actually indicates failure to track the client's evolving understanding. The harm is work toward goals the client no longer owns, wasted effort, and the client's sense that the therapy is not tracking their actual needs. The defense is to revisit goals regularly, to be open to revision, to check whether the goals still reflect the client's current wants, and to treat goal revision as a sign of engagement rather than instability.

## Self-Check

- Did I negotiate goals collaboratively and ensure genuine client ownership, distinguishing the client's goals from my clinical agenda and from external pressure?
- Did I convert vague goals into concrete, behavioral, observable terms that can guide action and measure progress?
- Did I use scaling as a clinical tool, exploring each rating in detail to identify existing resources and define the next step, rather than as a measurement ritual?
- Did I ensure goals were both achievable and appropriately ambitious, breaking large goals into smaller steps and adjusting if they proved too hard?
- Did I revisit goals as understanding deepened, checking whether they still reflected the client's current wants, and treating revision as engagement?
- Did I distinguish my clinical agenda from the client's goals, share concerns transparently rather than covertly steering, and respect the client's right to set their own goals?
- Did I celebrate progress toward goals rather than only full achievement, building self-efficacy through recognized success?
- Did I avoid accepting vague goals, imposing my goals, or pursuing outdated targets without checking continued ownership?
