# Dual Relationships And Managed Care

When a third party pays for therapy, the therapist acquires a second relationship that can pull against the client's interest: an obligation to the insurer or managed care organization to justify care, assign diagnoses, document medical necessity, and sometimes limit or extend treatment based on coverage rather than clinical need. This is a structural dual relationship, less dramatic than a social entanglement but pervasive, and it creates recurring ethical tensions. The therapist's judgment is to serve the client's clinical interest as the primary criterion while meeting legitimate third-party obligations honestly, to assign diagnoses that are clinically accurate even when coverage pressures distort them, to document medical necessity truthfully and specifically, and to recognize when the demands of the payer conflict with the client's welfare and resolve that conflict in the client's favor through honest means. The recurring failures are assigning a more severe diagnosis to secure coverage, padding medical necessity to extend sessions, accepting payer limits as clinical limits without question, and treating the insurer's demands as overriding the client's interest. This skill addresses the third-party payer relationship specifically; the social and outside-contact forms of dual relationship are covered separately.

Use this skill when accepting insurance, assigning a diagnosis for billing, writing medical necessity or treatment plan justifications, responding to utilization review or payer audits, deciding whether continued sessions are clinically warranted, or navigating a conflict between coverage rules and client welfare.

## Core Rules

### Make The Client's Clinical Interest The Primary Criterion

The therapist's primary obligation is to the client's welfare, and the third-party payer relationship is secondary to it. When the demands of managed care, session limits, authorization requirements, or documentation requests conflict with the client's clinical interest, the client's interest governs, and the therapist resolves the conflict through honest means: appealing adverse determinations, advocating for medical necessity, discussing self-pay options, or referring to a higher level of care when the client needs it. Do not let coverage rules become clinical rules unexamined; a session limit is a payment limit, not a clinical judgment about how many sessions the client needs. Keep the client informed about coverage constraints that affect their care, so they understand the forces shaping the treatment and can participate in decisions about them.

### Assign Diagnoses That Are Clinically Accurate, Not Coverage-Optimized

A diagnosis submitted for billing must be a clinically accurate diagnosis supported by the assessment, not a diagnosis chosen because it is reimbursable, covers more sessions, or avoids a stigma the client prefers to avoid. Assigning a more severe or different diagnosis than the clinical picture supports, in order to secure coverage, constitutes fraud and falsifies the clinical record in ways that follow the client. Assigning no diagnosis, or a milder one, to avoid stigma when the clinical picture warrants the more accurate diagnosis, compromises care and may deny the client appropriate coverage. Discuss the diagnostic reasoning with the client, explain that a diagnosis is required for insurance billing, address their concerns about the label, and document the diagnosis with the supporting clinical evidence. When a clinically accurate diagnosis is not covered, that is a coverage problem to solve honestly, not a reason to diagnose dishonestly.

### Document Medical Necessity Truthfully And Specifically

Medical necessity documentation must truthfully describe the client's diagnosis, symptoms, functional impairment, the treatment plan, and the expected benefit, in the specific terms the payer requires, because this documentation justifies payment and demonstrates the care was warranted. Write medical necessity that reflects the actual client: the specific symptoms and their severity, the functional impact on work, relationships, or self-care, the interventions used and their rationale, and the progress or continued need. Avoid templated, generic medical necessity that could describe any client, which both fails to justify care and signals carelessness to auditors. Do not exaggerate impairment to secure authorization, and do not minimize it to shorten treatment; document the clinical reality. Accurate, specific medical necessity protects the client's access to care, the legitimacy of the claim, and the clinician in an audit.

### Distinguish Session Limits From Clinical Decisions

Insurers often limit the number of sessions they will pay for, and utilization review may authorize or deny further sessions, but these are payment determinations, not clinical judgments about the client's need. Distinguish clearly between what the insurer will cover and what the client clinically needs, communicate both to the client, and do not collapse the two by treating the session limit as the endpoint of treatment. When the client needs more sessions than are covered, discuss the options honestly: appealing the denial with supporting medical necessity, self-pay for additional sessions, a modified treatment plan, or referral. Ending treatment at the coverage limit, without examining whether the client still needs care, conflates a payment rule with a clinical decision and can abandon a client who is not yet stable.

### Advocate Through Appeals And Honest Means

When an insurer denies coverage that the client clinically needs, advocate through the formal processes available: submit a reconsideration with supporting medical necessity, request a peer-to-peer review, escalate through the appeals process, and involve the client in the appeal. Advocacy is part of the care when the client's access to treatment depends on it, and clinicians who accept insurance have an obligation to use the system's mechanisms on the client's behalf. Document the denial, the appeal, and the clinical rationale, and keep the client informed. Do not accept an adverse determination as final without exhausting the honest avenues for reversal, and do not resort to dishonest means, such as altered documentation, to overcome a denial.

### Be Transparent With The Client About Coverage And Its Limits

Clients often do not understand their coverage, its limits, or the forces it exerts on their care, and the therapist has an obligation to be transparent. Explain at the outset whether you accept their insurance, what it covers and does not, the session limits and authorization process, the diagnoses that will be submitted and why, and the client's financial responsibility. When coverage constraints arise during treatment, such as a denial or a session limit approaching, discuss them with the client rather than absorbing them silently or ending treatment without explanation. Transparency allows the client to participate in decisions shaped by coverage and prevents the client from experiencing coverage-driven decisions as clinical judgments or personal rejections.

### Decide Insurance Participation Deliberately

Whether to participate with insurance panels, and which ones, is a decision with clinical and ethical dimensions, not only a financial one. Panel participation expands access for clients who could not otherwise afford care, but it imposes the documentation, authorization, and limit pressures described above, and it may create pressure to see more clients at lower rates to sustain income. Decide participation deliberately, weighing access, the administrative burden, the impact on the frame and the care, and your capacity to meet the obligations honestly. If you do not accept insurance, be clear about that and about the client's options for out-of-network reimbursement, and do not imply coverage that does not exist. Whatever the decision, recognize that the structure of payment shapes the care and manage it consciously.

### Maintain Confidentiality And Minimum Necessary Disclosure To Payers

Information submitted to insurers, in claims, treatment plans, or utilization review, must be the minimum necessary to justify the care, not the client's full clinical record, unless specifically authorized. Submit the diagnosis, the treatment code, the medical necessity justification, and any specific documentation the payer requires, with the client's authorization, and avoid disclosing more detail than the purpose requires. Payer requests for records should be scoped to what is needed for the determination, and broader releases should be questioned. Recognize that information submitted to a payer enters a system the therapist does not control, and that over-disclosure to a payer is a confidentiality breach as much as over-disclosure to any third party.

## Common Traps

### Assigning A More Severe Diagnosis To Secure Coverage

A clinician may assign a more severe diagnosis than the clinical picture supports, or a different diagnosis that is reimbursable, in order to secure coverage or more sessions for a client they believe needs care. The mechanism is that the client needs care and the accurate diagnosis is not covered, and the clinician, wanting to help, "upcodes" to access the benefit, when the false diagnosis constitutes fraud and falsifies the client's permanent record. The harm is that the client carries a diagnostic label they do not have, which can affect future insurability, employment, and their own self-understanding, the clinician commits billing fraud, and the clinical record is corrupted in ways that misdirect future care. Diagnosing accurately, and solving coverage problems through appeals and honest means, is what protects both client and clinician.

### Padding Medical Necessity To Extend Sessions

A clinician may exaggerate the client's symptoms or functional impairment in medical necessity documentation, or describe interventions more intensively than they occur, in order to secure authorization for sessions the client wants or the clinician believes beneficial. The mechanism is that the client values the sessions and the clinician, wanting to provide them, strengthens the documentation beyond the clinical reality, when the inflated documentation is falsification. The harm is that the claim is fraudulent, the medical record misrepresents the client's condition in ways that affect future care and coverage, and an audit will expose the discrepancy between the documentation and the actual sessions. Documenting medical necessity truthfully and specifically, and addressing session limits through appeals and transparent options, is what keeps the documentation honest.

### Treating Session Limits As Clinical Endpoints

A clinician may treat an insurer's session limit as the natural endpoint of treatment, ending care when coverage ends without examining whether the client still needs treatment, conflating the payment rule with a clinical judgment. The mechanism is that the limit feels authoritative, and the clinician, accepting it as the structure of care, does not distinguish what is covered from what is needed, when the limit is a payment determination that may not match the clinical need. The harm is that a client who still needs care is discharged prematurely, symptoms may recur or worsen, and the client experiences the ending as abandonment dressed up as completion. Distinguishing coverage from clinical need, communicating both to the client, and exploring options for continued care, is what prevents premature termination.

### Accepting Payer Demands As Overriding Client Welfare

A clinician may accept a payer's denial, limitation, or documentation demand as authoritative, even when it conflicts with the client's welfare, treating the insurer's determination as binding on the clinical care. The mechanism is that the payer feels powerful, and the clinician, wanting to comply and avoid conflict, defers, when the payer's determination is a payment decision that the clinician can and should challenge when it harms the client. The harm is that the client loses needed care or has care shaped by the payer's interests rather than their own, and the clinician has allowed a third party to override their primary obligation. Advocating through appeals, and resolving conflicts in the client's favor through honest means, is what keeps the client's interest primary.

### Over-Disclosing To Payers Beyond Minimum Necessary

A clinician may submit the client's full clinical record to a payer, or include extensive personal detail in treatment plans or utilization review, beyond what is needed to justify the care, treating the payer request as a license for comprehensive disclosure. The mechanism is that payers request records and the clinician, wanting to satisfy the request fully, sends more than necessary, when the obligation to the payer is bounded by minimum necessary disclosure. The harm is that the client's confidential material enters a system beyond the therapist's control, disclosed beyond what the purpose required, which is a confidentiality breach. Submitting only the minimum necessary to justify the care, with authorization, and scoping broader requests, is what protects confidentiality within the payer relationship.

### Failing To Be Transparent With The Client About Coverage Forces

A clinician may absorb coverage constraints silently, ending sessions at the limit or modifying the treatment in response to payer demands, without explaining to the client what is happening or why, leaving the client to experience coverage-driven decisions as clinical judgments. The mechanism is that explaining the constraints is awkward and time-consuming, and the clinician, wanting to protect the relationship, absorbs them, when the client has a right to understand the forces shaping their care. The harm is that the client cannot participate in decisions shaped by coverage, experiences changes as arbitrary or personal, and loses trust when the forces are later discovered. Being transparent about coverage, its limits, and the constraints as they arise, is what allows the client to participate and preserves trust.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Am I making the client's clinical interest the primary criterion, and resolving conflicts with payer demands in the client's favor through honest means?
- [ ] Is the diagnosis I submitted clinically accurate and supported by the assessment, not chosen to optimize coverage?
- [ ] Is my medical necessity documentation truthful, specific, and reflective of the actual client, not templated, exaggerated, or minimized?
- [ ] Am I distinguishing session limits and coverage determinations from clinical decisions about the client's need, and communicating both to the client?
- [ ] When coverage is denied for needed care, am I appealing with supporting medical necessity, pursuing peer-to-peer review, and exhausting honest avenues?
- [ ] Am I being transparent with the client about coverage, its limits, the diagnoses submitted, and the constraints as they arise?
- [ ] Am I submitting only the minimum necessary information to payers, with authorization, and scoping broader record requests?
- [ ] Did I decide insurance participation deliberately, weighing access, administrative burden, and impact on care, and am I clear about coverage status?
