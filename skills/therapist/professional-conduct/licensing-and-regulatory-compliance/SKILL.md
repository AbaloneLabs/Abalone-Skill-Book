# Licensing And Regulatory Compliance

A license to practice is not a permanent credential earned once; it is an active legal status maintained through continuing education, renewal, adherence to scope of practice, and compliance with the regulations of the licensing board, and its loss can end a career. The regulations that govern practice are detailed, jurisdiction-specific, and unforgiving of ignorance: they define who you may treat, what you may call yourself, what supervision you must provide and document, what records you must keep and for how long, what advertising you may do, and what conduct will trigger discipline. The therapist's judgment is to treat compliance not as bureaucratic overhead but as the legal floor beneath clinical work, to know the specific rules of the jurisdiction and board, to maintain the records and continuing education that sustain the license, and to respond to board inquiries with the seriousness they require. The recurring failures are letting continuing education lapse, practicing across state lines without checking telehealth rules, supervising associates without documenting to board standards, advertising in ways that mislead, and treating a board complaint as a misunderstanding rather than a legal proceeding.

Use this skill when renewing a license, tracking CE, supervising pre-licensed clinicians, advertising services, practicing telehealth across jurisdictions, interpreting scope of practice, or responding to a board complaint, audit, or investigation.

## Core Rules

### Know The Specific Rules Of Your Board And Jurisdiction

Licensing rules are jurisdiction-specific: each state or region sets its own requirements for renewal, continuing education, supervision, scope of practice, telehealth, advertising, and discipline, and what is permissible in one jurisdiction may be prohibited in another. Know the rules of every jurisdiction in which you practice, including the specific statutes, board regulations, and any advisory opinions that bear on your work. Pay attention to the details that clinicians often overlook: required supervisor training, the ratio and hour limits for supervision, the specific CE categories (ethics, suicide prevention, cultural competence, substance use) and their hour minimums, the titles you may and may not use, and the rules for advertising and public statements. Treat board websites and newsletters as required reading, because the board's communications define current expectations, and ignorance of a rule is not a defense to its violation.

### Maintain Continuing Education Proactively, Not At Renewal Panic

Continuing education is a renewal requirement in nearly every jurisdiction, and it typically includes specific categories: a minimum number of total hours, a required subset in ethics, and often additional required hours in suicide risk assessment, cultural competence, substance use, or supervision. Track CE continuously against the specific requirements, retain certificates of completion, and complete required categories deliberately rather than scrambling at renewal. Do not assume that any course counts toward any requirement; verify that each course is approved by the relevant board or an accepted provider, and that it satisfies the specific category. Lapsed or non-compliant CE can delay renewal, trigger fines, or result in discipline, and the clinician who discovers the gap at the renewal deadline has few good options. Build a system that tracks CE as it is completed, not as a deadline approaches.

### Practice Within Your Scope Of Practice And Use Titles Accurately

Scope of practice defines what your license permits you to do, and it differs by license type: a licensed clinical social worker, a psychologist, a licensed marriage and family therapist, a licensed professional counselor, and a psychiatrist have overlapping but distinct scopes, and the boundaries matter legally and clinically. Practice within your specific scope, do not hold yourself out as having a credential you do not have, and use titles precisely, because misrepresenting credentials, even loosely, is a common basis for board discipline. Be especially careful with the term "psychologist," which is legally protected in most jurisdictions and requires specific licensure, and with medical terms such as "diagnosis" or "medical necessity" that may exceed a non-medical license. When in doubt about whether an activity is within your scope, consult the board's scope-of-practice guidance before acting.

### Document Supervision To Board Standards

If you supervise pre-licensed clinicians, you bear specific documentation and oversight obligations defined by the board: the supervision agreement, the hours and content of supervision, the cases discussed, and the supervisor's attestation of the supervisee's competence, often in a specific format the board requires. Establish a written supervision agreement at the outset, document each supervision meeting with the date, duration, and content, and retain the records for the period the board specifies. Verify that you meet the board's requirements to act as a supervisor, which often include a minimum number of years licensed and completion of supervisor training, and do not supervise without them. Supervision that is undocumented, or documented to a standard below the board's, exposes both supervisor and supervisee to discipline and can invalidate the supervisee's hours toward licensure.

### Practice Telehealth Only Where You Are Licensed, After Checking The Rules

Telehealth practice is governed by the location of the client, not the therapist, which means that treating a client located in a jurisdiction where you are not licensed generally constitutes unlicensed practice in that jurisdiction. Before providing telehealth to a client who is or may be located in another state or country, verify whether you are licensed there, whether that jurisdiction offers a temporary telehealth registration or interstate compact privilege (such as PSYPACT for psychologists), and what its specific telehealth requirements are, which often include informed consent for telehealth and a documented plan for local emergency care. Do not assume that an existing client traveling temporarily changes nothing; even temporary presence in another jurisdiction can trigger its rules. Track client locations and have a clear policy for what happens when a client relocates, including referral when you cannot lawfully continue.

### Advertise Honestly And Avoid Misleading Claims

Advertising, including websites, directories, social media, and public statements, must be honest, must not guarantee outcomes, must not use testimonials where prohibited, and must not claim credentials or specialties the clinician does not hold. Be accurate about your license, training, specializations, and the evidence base for your approach; avoid implying guarantees of cure or specific results; and verify board rules on testimonials, which many prohibit. Pay attention to specialty claims: holding yourself out as a specialist in an area often requires specific board-recognized credentials or training, and unsupported specialty claims are a common basis for discipline. Honest advertising protects the public and the clinician, and the temptation to stand out in a crowded market must not override accuracy.

### Respond To Board Complaints And Audits With Utmost Seriousness

A board complaint, audit, or investigation is a legal proceeding, not a misunderstanding to be talked through, and the response can determine whether the license survives. If you receive notice of a complaint or audit, do not contact the complainant, do not alter records, do not respond substantively without consulting an attorney experienced in licensing defense, and do not assume that good intentions will resolve the matter. Gather the relevant records and documentation, retain qualified legal counsel, and respond through counsel in the format and timeframe the board specifies. Cooperate with the investigation while protecting your rights, and recognize that even unfounded complaints require a serious, documented response. Treating a board inquiry casually is among the most damaging errors a clinician can make.

### Maintain Records Consistent With Board Requirements

Board regulations specify what records you must keep, what they must contain, how long you must retain them, and how you must store and protect them. Know the retention period in your jurisdiction, which commonly ranges from five to ten years after the last service or after a minor reaches majority, and maintain records securely for the full period. Maintain records that meet the board's content expectations: identifying information, informed consent, assessment, treatment plan, progress notes, consultations, and termination, in a form that demonstrates competent care. Records that are incomplete, retained for too short a period, or stored insecurely are themselves violations, independent of the quality of care, and they leave the clinician undefended if care is questioned.

## Common Traps

### Letting Continuing Education Lapse Or Miscounting Categories

A clinician may let continuing education accumulate without tracking it against the specific requirements, then discover at renewal that they lack the required ethics hours, the suicide prevention hours, or the total, and scramble or miscount to fill the gap. The mechanism is that CE feels like an ongoing background task, and the clinician, attending courses of interest, does not verify that they satisfy the specific categories, when the board requires both a total and specific minimums. The harm is that renewal is delayed or denied, the clinician may practice with a lapsed license, and the miscounting, if discovered, can itself trigger discipline. Tracking CE continuously against the specific category requirements, and retaining certificates, is what keeps the license in good standing.

### Practicing Telehealth Across State Lines Without Checking The Rules

A clinician may continue treating an established client by telehealth after the client moves to another state, or accept a telehealth client in a state where they are not licensed, assuming the existing relationship or the virtual medium exempts them. The mechanism is that telehealth feels continuous with in-person care, and the clinician, focused on the relationship, does not recognize that the client's new location triggers a different jurisdiction's rules, when unlicensed practice in that jurisdiction is a serious violation. The harm is that the clinician commits unlicensed practice, exposes themselves to board discipline in both jurisdictions, and may have to terminate care abruptly when the issue is discovered. Verifying licensure and telehealth rules for the client's location before providing care, and having a relocation policy, is what prevents cross-jurisdictional violations.

### Supervising Without Meeting Board Requirements Or Documenting To Standard

A clinician may supervise pre-licensed associates without having completed required supervisor training, or supervise without the written agreements, hour logs, and content documentation the board requires, treating supervision as an informal mentorship. The mechanism is that supervision feels like a natural extension of clinical work, and the clinician, wanting to support juniors, supervises without attending to the regulatory scaffolding, when the board sets specific requirements for who may supervise and how it must be documented. The harm is that the supervision may be invalid, the supervisee's hours toward licensure may not count, and both parties face discipline, with the supervisor bearing the greater responsibility. Meeting board supervisor requirements and documenting to standard is what makes supervision valid and protective.

### Advertising With Unsupported Specialty Or Outcome Claims

A clinician may advertise using testimonials where prohibited, claim a specialty without the supporting credential, or imply guarantees of outcome, because the marketing feels effective and the claims feel harmless. The mechanism is that standing out in a competitive market pressures clinicians toward stronger claims, and the clinician, wanting to attract clients, overstates credentials or outcomes, when the board regulates advertising precisely to prevent public deception. The harm is that the public is misled, competitors and clients can file complaints, and the unsupported claims, especially specialty and outcome claims, are common bases for discipline. Advertising honestly, verifying specialty claims against board-recognized credentials, and avoiding outcome guarantees, is what keeps marketing compliant.

### Treating A Board Complaint As A Misunderstanding Rather Than A Legal Proceeding

A clinician who receives notice of a board complaint may treat it as a misunderstanding to be clarified, responding informally, contacting the complainant, or failing to retain counsel, assuming that good intentions and an explanation will resolve it. The mechanism is that the complaint feels unjust and the clinician, trusting in their own good faith, responds as they would to a colleague, when a board complaint is an adversarial legal proceeding in which informal responses can be used against them. The harm is that the clinician's unguarded responses create additional grounds for discipline, the opportunity for a careful, documented defense is lost, and the license is endangered by the response as much as the original complaint. Retaining licensing-defense counsel immediately, gathering records without alteration, and responding formally through counsel is what protects the license.

### Practicing With A Lapsed License Or During A Suspension

A clinician may continue practicing after a license has lapsed through failure to renew, or during a period of suspension, sometimes without realizing the status, because renewal felt routine or notice was missed. The mechanism is that licensure feels like a permanent state, and the clinician, distracted by practice, does not track the renewal date or respond to board communications, when practicing with a lapsed or suspended license is unlicensed practice. The harm is that every session delivered during the lapse is unlicensed practice, a serious violation that compounds the original lapse and can trigger severe discipline. Tracking renewal dates, confirming license status, and ceasing practice immediately if the license lapses or is suspended, is what prevents the compounding violation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Do I know the specific rules of my board and jurisdiction, including renewal, CE categories, supervision, scope, telehealth, advertising, and records retention?
- [ ] Am I tracking continuing education continuously against the specific category requirements, and retaining certificates?
- [ ] Am I practicing within my scope of practice and using titles and specialty claims accurately?
- [ ] If I supervise, do I meet board supervisor requirements, and do I document supervision in the format and detail the board requires?
- [ ] Before providing telehealth, have I verified I am licensed or authorized in the client's jurisdiction, and do I have a relocation policy?
- [ ] Is my advertising honest, free of prohibited testimonials, and accurate about credentials, specialties, and outcomes?
- [ ] If I receive a board complaint or audit, will I retain licensing-defense counsel, avoid contacting the complainant, avoid altering records, and respond formally within the deadline?
- [ ] Are my records maintained in content, security, and retention period consistent with board requirements, and am I certain my license is currently active?
