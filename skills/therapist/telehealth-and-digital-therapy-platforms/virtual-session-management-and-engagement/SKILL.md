# Virtual Session Management and Engagement

Virtual session management is the adaptation of therapeutic practice to the video or phone medium, and the judgment problem is that telehealth is often treated as equivalent to in-person therapy delivered through a screen (the same work, just remote) when in fact the medium changes the therapeutic relationship, the available clinical information, the engagement dynamics, and the suitability of specific techniques in ways that require deliberate adaptation. The video medium reduces available information (the therapist sees a framed face rather than a whole body, misses micro-expressions, cannot read the room), changes the relational dynamics (screen-mediated presence is different from embodied presence, eye contact is altered, the therapeutic alliance must be built and maintained differently), introduces technical disruptions (frozen video, audio lag, dropped connections) that rupture the session, and creates access dynamics (the client's environment is uncontrolled, privacy and safety at the client's end cannot be assured). The therapist must adapt their presence and engagement for the medium, must address technology and access barriers, must modify techniques that do not transfer well, must manage the disruptions that the medium introduces, and must recognize when telehealth is not appropriate. The deeper traps are assuming equivalence with in-person work, neglecting the altered relational dynamics, failing to adapt techniques, and ignoring the client's environment. The therapist's freedom to use telehealth is real, but the medium is a consequential dimension that requires deliberate adaptation. Given the clinical stakes, this guidance is conservative: adapt for the medium, address access, modify techniques, manage disruptions, and assess appropriateness.

## Core Rules

### Adapt Therapeutic Presence and Engagement for the Video Medium

Therapeutic presence in telehealth must be adapted for the video medium, not assumed to transfer automatically. The therapist should attend to their own presence (framing themselves at appropriate distance, maintaining eye contact through the camera rather than the screen image, managing their visible affect and engagement), should attend to the client's presence (what is visible in the frame, the client's engagement level, signs of distraction or dissociation that the medium may amplify), should build and maintain the alliance deliberately (telehealth can feel less personal, and the alliance requires explicit attention), and should use verbal acknowledgment of presence and connection more than in-person work (where presence is communicated nonverbally). The therapist should recognize that screen-mediated presence is different from embodied presence, and that the adaptation is a clinical skill, not merely a technical adjustment.

### Address Technology and Access Barriers Systematically

Technology and access barriers can prevent clients from benefiting from telehealth, and the therapist should address them systematically. The therapist should assess the client's technology access (device, internet bandwidth, data limits, private space for sessions), should provide technical support or referral (how to use the platform, what to do if the connection fails), should consider equity issues (not all clients have adequate technology, and telehealth may disadvantage clients with fewer resources), should have low-tech alternatives (phone sessions when video is not available), and should not assume that all clients can access telehealth equally. The therapist should also address their own technology (reliable platform, adequate bandwidth, backup connection, private space), because the therapist's technical failures disrupt the client's therapy as much as the client's.

### Modify Techniques That Do Not Transfer Well to the Medium

Some therapeutic techniques do not transfer well to the video medium, and the therapist should modify or avoid them. The therapist should assess which techniques work in telehealth (verbal-based approaches, many cognitive and behavioral techniques, mindfulness exercises that can be guided verbally) and which are compromised (techniques that rely on physical presence or movement, some experiential and body-based work, techniques that require close observation of the whole body), should modify techniques where possible (adapting experiential work to the verbal channel, using the client's environment for grounding exercises), and should recognize when a technique that would be appropriate in person is not appropriate in telehealth. The therapist should not force techniques into the medium where they do not fit, and should consider whether in-person work is needed for specific therapeutic tasks.

### Manage Technical Disruptions and Session Ruptures

Technical disruptions (frozen video, audio lag, dropped connections) rupture the session and must be managed. The therapist should establish a plan at the outset (if we lose connection, I will call you at this number, we will resume on this platform), should handle disruptions calmly and without making the client feel responsible, should recognize the relational impact of disruptions (they can feel like rejection or disconnection, especially for clients with attachment concerns), should repair the rupture after a disruption (that was frustrating, are we okay, where were we), and should recognize that frequent disruptions may indicate that telehealth is not workable for this client and that alternatives should be considered. The therapist should not treat disruptions as merely technical, because their relational impact is clinical.

### Assess and Manage the Client's Environment

The client's environment in telehealth is uncontrolled by the therapist, and it must be assessed and managed. The therapist should assess the client's privacy (are they alone, can they speak freely, are they in a safe space), should address privacy problems (can you use headphones, can you move to a more private space, would another time be better), should attend to safety (is the client at risk in their environment, is there domestic violence risk that the remote setting cannot address), should recognize that the client may be multitasking or distracted, and should adapt the session to the environment (shorter sessions if the client cannot sustain privacy, different content if the environment is not conducive to deep work). The therapist should not assume that the client's environment supports therapy, and should recognize that the uncontrolled environment is a significant difference from in-person work.

### Recognize When Telehealth Is Not Appropriate

Telehealth is not appropriate for all clients or all presentations, and the therapist should recognize its limits. The therapist should assess whether telehealth serves the client's needs (some presentations require in-person work, some clients cannot engage through the medium, some safety concerns require in-person management), should consider the client's preference (some clients prefer telehealth, others need in-person presence), should recognize crisis situations that may exceed telehealth's capacity (acute suicidality, psychosis, situations requiring hands-on intervention), and should refer to in-person services when telehealth is not appropriate. The therapist should not default to telehealth for convenience, and should make the modality decision based on clinical appropriateness.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Telehealth Is Equivalent to In-Person Therapy

The therapist, trained in in-person practice and now conducting telehealth, assumes that the work is equivalent, because the therapeutic content is the same and the medium is just a delivery channel, but the medium changes the therapeutic relationship, the available information, the engagement dynamics, and the suitability of techniques, and the assumption of equivalence leaves these changes unaddressed, producing telehealth that is less effective than it could be. The mechanism is that the medium has been treated as a neutral delivery channel rather than as a consequential dimension, so the adaptations that would optimize telehealth have not been made, and the work proceeds as in-person therapy through a screen, with its limitations unaddressed. The false signal is the apparent continuity of the therapeutic content, which looks like equivalent work but actually indicates a failure to adapt for the medium. The harm is the reduced effectiveness of telehealth, the missed clinical information, the weakened therapeutic alliance, and the client's experience of a less engaging or less effective therapy. The defense is to adapt therapeutic presence and engagement for the medium, to modify techniques, to manage disruptions, and to recognize that telehealth requires deliberate adaptation rather than assumed equivalence.

### Neglecting the Altered Relational Dynamics of Screen-Mediated Presence

The therapist, focused on the therapeutic content and confident in their relational skills, neglects the altered relational dynamics of screen-mediated presence, because the relationship feels present and the content is the focus, but screen-mediated presence is different from embodied presence (eye contact is altered, the whole person is not visible, the felt sense of being-with is reduced), and the neglect leaves the alliance weaker than it would be with deliberate attention. The mechanism is that the altered relational dynamics have been neglected, so the alliance has not been built and maintained with the deliberate attention that the medium requires, and the client may feel less connected or less engaged. The false signal is the apparent presence of the relationship, which looks like adequate alliance but actually indicates a relationship that is weaker than it appears. The harm is the weakened therapeutic alliance, the client's reduced engagement, the potential for the client to discontinue therapy, and the reduced effectiveness of the work. The defense is to attend to presence and engagement deliberately, to use verbal acknowledgment of connection, to build and maintain the alliance explicitly, and to recognize that screen-mediated presence requires deliberate attention.

### Failing to Adapt Techniques That Do Not Transfer Well

The therapist, using their standard techniques and wanting to provide the same treatment remotely, fails to adapt techniques that do not transfer well, because the techniques are effective in person and the client needs them, but some techniques rely on physical presence, close observation, or environmental control that the video medium does not provide, and the unadapted techniques are less effective or ineffective in telehealth. The mechanism is that techniques have been used without adaptation, so techniques that rely on in-person dimensions have been compromised in the medium, and the client has received less effective versions of the techniques. The false signal is the apparent transferability of the techniques, which looks like effective treatment but actually indicates techniques that are compromised in the medium. The harm is the reduced effectiveness of the techniques, the client's experience of therapy that does not work as well, and the potential for the client to conclude that therapy is ineffective when the problem is the medium-specific adaptation. The defense is to assess which techniques work in telehealth, to modify techniques where possible, to recognize when techniques do not fit the medium, and to consider in-person work for techniques that require it.

### Ignoring the Client's Environment and Its Impact on the Session

The therapist, focused on the therapeutic content and viewing the client's environment as the client's business, ignores the client's environment, because the client has chosen their space and the therapy is the focus, but the uncontrolled environment may lack privacy, safety, or the conditions for therapeutic work, and the client may be unable to engage fully or safely. The mechanism is that the client's environment has been ignored, so privacy, safety, and engagement problems that originate in the environment have not been addressed, and the client may be conducting therapy in conditions that undermine the work. The false signal is the client's apparent presence in the session, which looks like adequate engagement but actually indicates a client who may be in an environment that does not support therapy. The harm is the compromised privacy and confidentiality, the potential safety risks, the client's reduced engagement, and the ethical failure of conducting therapy in conditions that do not support it. The defense is to assess and manage the client's environment, to address privacy and safety, to adapt the session to the environment, and to recognize that the uncontrolled environment is a significant clinical dimension.

### Defaulting to Telehealth for Convenience Without Clinical Justification

The therapist, finding telehealth convenient and efficient, defaults to telehealth for convenience, because it saves time and the client seems to manage, but telehealth may not be clinically appropriate for the client's presentation, and the default to convenience rather than clinical appropriateness may compromise the client's care. The mechanism is that telehealth has been chosen for convenience rather than clinical appropriateness, so the client may be receiving a modality that does not serve their needs, and the clinical decision has been made on non-clinical grounds. The false signal is the convenience and efficiency of telehealth, which looks like a good choice but actually indicates a decision based on the therapist's rather than the client's needs. The harm is the compromised care, the client's needs not being met by the modality, and the ethical failure of choosing a modality on non-clinical grounds. The defense is to assess whether telehealth is clinically appropriate, to consider the client's presentation and preference, to recognize crisis situations that exceed telehealth's capacity, and to make the modality decision based on clinical appropriateness rather than convenience.

## Self-Check

- Did I adapt my therapeutic presence and engagement for the video medium, attending to framing, eye contact, and verbal acknowledgment of connection?
- Did I address technology and access barriers systematically, including the client's device, bandwidth, private space, and equity considerations?
- Did I modify techniques that do not transfer well to the medium, and avoid forcing techniques where they do not fit?
- Did I manage technical disruptions and repair the relational ruptures they caused?
- Did I assess and manage the client's environment, including privacy, safety, and conditions for therapeutic work?
- Did I recognize when telehealth is not appropriate for this client or presentation, and refer to in-person services when needed?
- Did I avoid assuming equivalence with in-person work, neglecting relational dynamics, failing to adapt techniques, ignoring the environment, and defaulting to telehealth for convenience?
- Did I recognize that the medium is a consequential dimension that requires deliberate adaptation rather than assumed equivalence?
