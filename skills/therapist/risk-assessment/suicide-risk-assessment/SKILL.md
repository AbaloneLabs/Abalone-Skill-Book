# Suicide Risk Assessment

Suicide risk assessment is the highest-stakes judgment a therapist makes, and it is the area where overconfidence and formulaic approaches do the most damage. The core difficulty is that suicide risk cannot be predicted at the individual level; no combination of risk factors, instruments, or clinical interviews reliably identifies who will die by suicide. What the therapist can do is assess the current clinical picture thoroughly, identify modifiable risk factors, build a collaborative safety plan, arrange the appropriate level of care, and document the reasoning. The recurring failures are treating risk assessment as a checkbox that absolves responsibility, relying on no-harm contracts that provide no protection, being reassured by a client's denial of ideation while ignoring the risk factors, and failing to act on elevated risk because hospitalization feels drastic.

Use this skill whenever a client expresses suicidal ideation or shows elevated risk factors, when conducting routine risk screening, when deciding on level of care, or when documenting a risk assessment.

## Core Rules

### Assess Ideation, Plan, Intent, And Means Directly And Specifically

Asking directly about suicide does not increase risk; it is the only way to assess it. Assess each component separately and specifically.

Ask about:

- ideation: presence, frequency, intensity, duration, controllability, and whether it is passive, "I wish I wouldn't wake up," or active, "I think about killing myself";
- plan: specificity, lethality, method, time, place, and preparatory acts such as researching methods, acquiring means, writing notes, giving away possessions, or rehearsing;
- intent: how close the client is to acting, what has stopped them so far, and what would increase the likelihood of acting;
- means: access to firearms, medications, and other lethal methods, because means access is one of the strongest predictors of death.

Asking about method and means access directly is non-negotiable. Firearms are the most lethal and most common method of suicide death, and means restriction is one of the few interventions shown to reduce suicide death. Ask about firearms specifically, not just "weapons."

### Weigh Static And Dynamic Risk Factors, But Prioritize The Dynamic

Static risk factors, prior attempts, family history, male sex, older age, chronic illness, are stable and cannot be changed; they establish a baseline of vulnerability. Dynamic risk factors, acute stressors, recent loss, intoxication, access to means, command hallucinations, hopelessness, recent discharge from psychiatric care, are changeable and are where intervention can reduce risk. A client with high static risk who is currently stable differs from a client with high static risk who has just experienced a loss and acquired means. Prioritize the dynamic picture because it is what you can act on and what changes the acute risk level.

### Treat Recent Attempt And Preparatory Behavior As Acute High Risk

A recent suicide attempt, an aborted or interrupted attempt, or evidence of preparatory acts, acquiring means, writing a note, saying goodbye, rehearsing, represents acute high risk that almost always warrants a higher level of care than outpatient management. Do not manage recent attempt or active preparation with an outpatient safety plan alone; arrange emergency evaluation. The clinical question after a recent attempt is not whether the client is currently suicidal but whether they are safe to leave the session.

### Build A Collaborative Safety Plan, Not A No-Harm Contract

A safety plan is a collaboratively written, specific document that the client helps create and agrees to use; it is not a no-harm contract in which the client promises not to kill themselves. No-harm contracts have no demonstrated protective effect, can create false reassurance, and have no legal value. A safety plan, in contrast, is a practical tool.

A safety plan includes:

- personal warning signs that a crisis is developing;
- internal coping strategies the client can use alone;
- people and social settings that provide distraction or support;
- people the client can ask for help, with names and numbers;
- professionals and agencies to contact, including the therapist's crisis line and emergency numbers;
- means restriction, the specific steps to make the environment safer, including removing or securing firearms and medications.

The safety plan must be written, given to the client, and reviewed and updated as risk changes. Means restriction is part of the plan, not separate from it.

### Address Means Restriction Directly And Concretely

Means restriction is one of the most effective suicide prevention interventions, because suicide attempts using highly lethal means, especially firearms, are far more likely to be fatal, and most suicide attempts are impulsive and method-specific. Reducing access to the preferred method during a high-risk period saves lives.

Address means restriction by:

- asking specifically about firearms in the home and asking the client or family to remove or secure them off-site during the risk period;
- asking about medication stockpiles and working with prescribers and family to limit quantities;
- discussing other accessible lethal means specific to the client's plan;
- involving family or supports, with consent, to hold or secure means;
- documenting the discussion, the plan, and who is responsible.

Do not accept vague assurances that means are "secure" without specifics. A firearm in a locked safe with the client holding the key is not secured.

### Match The Level Of Care To The Risk Level

Risk level should drive the level of care, and the decision must be defensible.

- low risk: ideation without plan or intent, no recent attempt, protective factors; outpatient care with safety plan and close follow-up;
- moderate risk: ideation with some plan features, significant risk factors, but no intent or preparatory behavior; intensified outpatient care, more frequent sessions, safety plan, means restriction, consider partial hospitalization;
- high risk: plan with intent, preparatory acts, recent attempt, or severe risk factors with acute destabilization; emergency psychiatric evaluation, likely hospitalization, do not leave the client alone.

When uncertain, err toward a higher level of care and seek consultation. A decision to hospitalize involuntarily is grave and should follow the legal criteria in your jurisdiction, but the threshold is danger to self, not the therapist's comfort.

### Document The Assessment, The Decision, And The Rationale

Risk documentation must show the clinical reasoning, not just the conclusion. Document what you assessed, what you found, what risk level you assigned and why, what you did, what the client agreed to, what means restriction was arranged, what follow-up was set, and what consultation you sought. A note that says "denies suicidal ideation, low risk" without the assessment behind it provides no protection if the outcome is bad. Document the reasoning that supports the decision, including the factors you weighed.

### Seek Consultation On High-Risk Cases

No therapist should carry a high-risk suicide case alone. Consultation with a supervisor, colleague, or psychiatrist provides a second perspective, reduces the cognitive load on the primary clinician, and improves the quality of the decision. Consultation is not a sign of weakness; in high-risk cases it is the standard of care. Document the consultation and how it informed the plan.

## Common Traps

### The Checkbox Risk Assessment

When the therapist asks "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" and, on hearing "no," documents "denies SI" and moves on, the assessment has the form of risk evaluation without its substance. The mechanism of harm is that a denial, especially from a client who has reason to minimize, ends the inquiry prematurely, and the risk factors that would have elevated concern are never connected to the clinical picture. The client who denies ideation but has a recent loss, a firearm at home, increasing substance use, and a prior attempt is at elevated risk regardless of the denial, and a checkbox assessment misses every one of those signals. The harm is that the highest-risk clients are often the ones who deny ideation most firmly, because ambivalence about living coexists with a wish not to be stopped. A denial is one data point, not the end of the assessment; the full risk picture must be evaluated regardless of the verbal response.

### The No-Harm Contract As Protection

A no-harm contract, in which the client agrees in writing not to harm themselves for a period, creates an illusion of safety that has no empirical support and no legal standing. The mechanism of harm is that the therapist, having obtained the contract, lowers their vigilance and the level of care, treating a piece of paper as if it restrained the client's behavior. Clients who signed no-harm contracts have died by suicide; the contract provided no protection and may have reduced the clinician's urgency. The trap is seductive because the contract feels like a concrete agreement and gives the therapist something to point to. A collaborative safety plan, which is a practical tool the client uses during a crisis, is a different and effective intervention; a no-harm contract is a substitute for one.

### Reassurance From The Client's Presentation

A client who appears calm, cooperative, and verbally committed to safety can reassure the therapist into underestimating risk, especially when the therapist is relieved not to face a hospitalization decision. The mechanism is that calm presentation is read as evidence of low risk, when in fact some clients become calm after deciding to die, because the decision has resolved their internal conflict. The harm is that the period of greatest danger, after a decision is made, is the period when the client looks most at peace, and the reassured therapist reduces monitoring or sends the client home. Presentation is one factor, weighted against ideation, plan, intent, means, and risk factors; a calm presentation never overrides a plan with intent and means access.

### Ignoring Means Access Because The Conversation Is Uncomfortable

Asking about firearms, and asking a family to remove them, is uncomfortable, and therapists often avoid the specific conversation, accepting vague assurances or skipping the question. The mechanism of harm is that means access is one of the strongest predictors of suicide death, and firearms in particular are so lethal that their presence multiplies risk during a crisis. Avoiding the conversation because it is awkward leaves the most modifiable risk factor unaddressed. The harm is concrete and measurable: a significant proportion of suicide deaths occur with firearms already in the home, and securing or removing them during high-risk periods prevents deaths. The discomfort of the conversation is not a clinical justification for omitting it; ask directly, ask specifically, and document the plan.

### Failing To Act Because Hospitalization Feels Drastic

When a client is at high risk, the therapist may delay or avoid emergency evaluation or involuntary hospitalization because the decision feels drastic, damaging to the alliance, or disruptive to the client's life. The mechanism is that the therapist weighs the cost of an unnecessary hospitalization against the cost of an adverse outcome and, under the discomfort of the decision, biases toward the less disruptive option. The harm is that an acute high-risk client is managed as an outpatient during the period of greatest danger, sometimes with fatal outcome. The corrective is to weigh the decision correctly: the cost of an unnecessary evaluation is inconvenience and alliance strain, both repairable; the cost of a missed high-risk case is irreversible. Erring toward evaluation when risk is high, and seeking consultation to share the decision, is the defensible path.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I assess ideation, plan, intent, and means directly and specifically, including asking about firearms by name?
- [ ] Did I weigh both static and dynamic risk factors, prioritizing the modifiable dynamic picture?
- [ ] Did I treat any recent attempt, aborted attempt, or preparatory acts as acute high risk requiring emergency evaluation?
- [ ] Did I build a collaborative written safety plan with the client, including warning signs, coping strategies, supports, professional contacts, and means restriction?
- [ ] Did I address means restriction concretely, with specific steps and a responsible person, rather than accepting vague assurances?
- [ ] Did I match the level of care to the assessed risk level, erring toward higher care when uncertain?
- [ ] Did I document the full assessment, the risk level and rationale, the interventions, the means restriction plan, and the follow-up?
- [ ] Did I seek consultation on high-risk cases rather than carrying the decision alone?
- [ ] Did I avoid relying on a no-harm contract, and avoid being reassured by calm presentation when other factors indicate high risk?
