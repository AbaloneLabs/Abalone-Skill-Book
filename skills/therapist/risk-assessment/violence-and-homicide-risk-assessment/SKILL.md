# Violence And Homicide Risk Assessment

Violence risk assessment and the duty to protect third parties present a dual obligation that is easy to get wrong in either direction: failing to act on a credible threat and leaving a potential victim unprotected, or over-disclosing and breaching confidentiality in ways that are clinically harmful and legally unjustified. The judgment problem is that the therapist holds a simultaneous duty of confidentiality to the client and a duty to protect identifiable victims when a serious threat is credible, and the law (Tarasoff and its state-by-state variants) requires a fact-specific judgment about whether a duty to protect has been triggered and what actions discharge it. The skilled therapist assesses violence risk using structured professional judgment (not unstructured intuition), distinguishes threats that trigger the duty to protect from those that do not, takes the specific actions that discharge the duty when it is triggered (warning the victim, notifying law enforcement, hospitalizing the client), documents the assessment and the actions, and manages the client's treatment in a way that addresses the violence risk while preserving the therapeutic alliance to the extent possible. The recurring failures are relying on intuition over structured assessment, failing to act on a credible threat, breaching confidentiality without a triggering threat, the unilateral discharge that damages the alliance and drives the client from treatment, and the failure to document the assessment and the rationale. This is a high-stakes domain in which the therapist's role is to assess the risk in a structured way, to determine whether the duty to protect is triggered, to take the specific actions that discharge it, and to document the assessment and the rationale for the actions taken.

## Core Rules

### Assess Violence Risk With Structured Professional Judgment

Violence risk is not assessed by intuition; it is assessed by structured professional judgment that weighs the evidence-based risk factors for violence, the protective factors, and the specific threat. The assessment is dynamic, repeated as circumstances change, and documented.

- Assess the static risk factors: history of violence (the strongest single predictor), age and gender, early onset of conduct problems, antisocial personality traits, substance use disorders, prior incarceration.
- Assess the dynamic risk factors: current substance use, acute psychosis with persecutory delusions or command hallucinations, acute stressors (loss, humiliation, conflict), access to weapons, homicidal ideation with plan and intent, and recent escalation.
- Assess the specific threat: is there an identifiable victim, a specific plan, intent, means access, and a timeline? Threats that are specific, plausible, and toward an identifiable victim carry the highest risk and the clearest duty.
- Assess the protective factors: treatment engagement, social support, employment, stable housing, future orientation, and the client's capacity to collaborate on a safety plan.
- Use a structured approach (such as the HCR-20 or comparable structured professional judgment tools) where appropriate, and document the factors weighed, not merely the conclusion.

### Determine Whether The Duty To Protect Is Triggered

The duty to protect (Tarasoff and its state-by-state variants) is triggered by a credible threat of serious harm toward a reasonably identifiable victim, and the threshold and the required actions vary by jurisdiction. The therapist must know the law of the jurisdiction in which they practice and must determine, on the facts of the case, whether the duty is triggered.

- The duty is generally triggered by a serious threat of physical harm toward a reasonably identifiable victim; some jurisdictions require a specific threat, others require a foreseeable victim, and the specifics vary.
- Determine whether the threat is credible: the client's history of violence, the specificity of the threat, the plan, the means, the intent, and the recent escalation all bear on credibility.
- Determine whether the victim is identifiable: a named individual, a readily identifiable class (a spouse, a specific coworker, a public figure with whom the client has a parasitic relationship), or a person the client is stalking.
- When uncertain whether the duty is triggered, consult immediately — with a supervisor, a risk committee, or counsel — because the consequences of error in either direction are serious, and consultation both improves the judgment and documents the diligence.

### Take The Specific Actions That Discharge The Duty

When the duty to protect is triggered, the therapist must take reasonable steps to protect the victim, and the specific actions vary by jurisdiction but generally include one or more of: warning the identifiable victim, notifying law enforcement, initiating involuntary hospitalization if the client meets criteria, and taking other reasonable steps. The actions must be taken promptly, documented, and matched to the threat.

- Warn the identifiable victim: communicate the threat and the risk directly, in a way that allows the victim to take protective action.
- Notify law enforcement: provide the information necessary for them to act, and document the notification.
- Initiate hospitalization if the client meets criteria: a client with acute mental illness and a serious threat may meet involuntary commitment criteria, and hospitalization both protects the victim and addresses the client's condition.
- Take other reasonable steps: adjusting the treatment (increased monitoring, medication evaluation), involving supports, or other steps proportionate to the threat.
- The actions are documented: what was done, when, to whom, and the rationale, because the documentation is the evidence that the duty was discharged.

### Manage The Client's Treatment To Address The Violence Risk

The duty to protect and the duty of confidentiality to the client coexist, and the management of the client's treatment must address the violence risk while preserving the therapeutic alliance to the extent possible. A client who is unilaterally discharged after a threat is a client driven from treatment, and the treatment engagement is itself a protective factor.

- Discuss the duty to protect with the client: when the duty is triggered, explain to the client what the therapist is required to do, and to the extent possible, involve the client in the actions (the client may agree to hospitalization, may agree to the warning, may collaborate on the plan).
- Address the violence risk in the treatment: the substance use, the acute psychosis, the stressors, the weapon access, and the dynamic factors that elevate the risk.
- Preserve the alliance to the extent possible: the client who feels betrayed by the disclosure may be angry, and the therapist acknowledges the rupture, explains the obligation, and works to repair the alliance, because a client who remains in treatment is safer than one who has left it.
- Do not discharge the client unilaterally as a substitute for the protective actions: discharging the client does not protect the victim, removes a protective factor, and may escalate the risk; the protective actions are the warning, the law enforcement notification, and the hospitalization, not the termination of treatment.

### Know The Jurisdiction's Law And Consult

The duty to protect varies by jurisdiction: some states impose a duty to warn, others a duty to protect (which may be discharged by actions other than warning), some impose the duty only for identifiable victims, and the specifics of what triggers the duty and what discharges it differ. The therapist must know the law of the jurisdiction and must consult when the application is uncertain.

- Know the jurisdiction's statute and case law on the duty to protect, including what triggers the duty and what actions discharge it.
- Consult with a supervisor, a risk committee, or counsel when the application is uncertain, because the consultation improves the judgment and documents the diligence.
- Document the consultation: who was consulted, when, and what was recommended, because the documentation is part of the evidence that the duty was handled with diligence.

### Document The Assessment And The Actions

The documentation of the violence risk assessment and the actions taken is the evidence that the duty was handled appropriately, and it is the record on which the therapist's conduct will be evaluated if the case is later scrutinized. Document the assessment, the factors weighed, the determination of whether the duty was triggered, the consultation, and the actions taken.

- Document the assessment: the risk factors weighed, the protective factors, the specific threat, and the conclusion about the level of risk.
- Document the determination: whether the duty to protect was triggered, and the rationale.
- Document the consultation: who was consulted, when, and what was recommended.
- Document the actions: what was done, when, to whom, and the response, because the documentation is the evidence that the duty was discharged.

## Common Traps

### Relying On Intuition Over Structured Assessment

When the therapist assesses violence risk by clinical impression rather than by a structured weighing of the evidence-based risk and protective factors, the assessment is driven by the client's presentation in the session (cooperative, remorseful, minimizing) rather than by the factors that actually predict violence (history of violence, substance use, weapon access, plan and intent), and the threat is underestimated. The mechanism is that the client's in-session presentation reads as the relevant data, so the impression feels grounded, while the strongest predictors of violence — the history, the substance use, the weapon access — are not systematically weighed, and the impression is shaped by the client's momentary demeanor rather than by the risk profile. The harm is that a client whose presentation is calm but whose risk profile is high is assessed as low risk, the protective actions are not taken, and the victim is left unprotected, while the therapist believed the assessment was grounded. The defense is to assess violence risk with structured professional judgment, weighing the static and dynamic risk factors and the protective factors systematically, to use a structured tool where appropriate, and to recognize that the in-session presentation is one data point among many, not the assessment, because the strongest predictor of violence is the history of violence, and it must be weighed regardless of the presentation.

### Failing To Act On A Credible Threat

When a client makes a credible threat toward an identifiable victim and the therapist, reluctant to breach confidentiality or damage the alliance, does not take the protective actions, the victim is left unprotected during the highest-risk period, and the duty to protect is not discharged. The mechanism is that the breach of confidentiality and the alliance rupture feel like harms to be avoided, so the reluctance reads as clinical caution, while the credible threat toward an identifiable victim triggers the duty to protect, and the reluctance leaves the victim unprotected and the duty undischarged. The harm is that the victim, unprotected, is harmed by the threat the therapist knew of, and the therapist faces liability and the moral weight of the failure to act, while the therapist believed they were preserving the alliance. The defense is to determine whether the duty to protect is triggered when a credible threat toward an identifiable victim is made, to take the specific actions that discharge the duty (warning the victim, notifying law enforcement, hospitalizing the client), to consult when uncertain, and to recognize that the duty to protect overrides confidentiality when a serious threat toward an identifiable victim is credible, because the victim's safety is the priority the duty exists to protect.

### Breaching Confidentiality Without A Triggering Threat

When the therapist, anxious about violence risk, discloses a client's violent ideation or general hostility without a credible threat toward an identifiable victim, the disclosure breaches confidentiality without a triggering threat, and the breach is clinically harmful and legally unjustified, because the duty to protect is triggered by a credible threat toward an identifiable victim, not by general hostility or vague ideation. The mechanism is that the anxiety about violence reads as responsible caution, so the disclosure feels protective, while the threshold for the duty has not been met, and the disclosure breaches confidentiality without justification, damaging the alliance and potentially the client's life (employment, relationships, reputation). The harm is that the client's confidentiality is breached without legal justification, the alliance is ruptured, the client may leave treatment, and the therapist faces a complaint or liability for the unjustified breach, while the therapist believed they were acting responsibly. The defense is to determine whether the duty is triggered by the specific facts (a credible threat toward an identifiable victim), to consult when uncertain, and to recognize that general hostility or vague ideation does not trigger the duty, because the duty to protect is triggered by specific threats toward identifiable victims, and over-disclosure is a breach of confidentiality, not a discharge of the duty.

### The Unilateral Discharge That Drives The Client From Treatment

When the therapist, faced with a client who has made a threat, unilaterally discharges the client from treatment as a substitute for the protective actions, the client is driven from treatment, the protective factor of the treatment engagement is removed, and the risk may escalate, because the discharge does not protect the victim and removes the structure that was managing the risk. The mechanism is that the discharge feels like a responsible termination of a risky case, so it reads as prudent practice, while the discharge does not protect the victim (the warning and the law enforcement notification do), removes a protective factor, and may escalate the risk by abandoning the client at the moment of highest risk. The harm is that the client, abandoned at the moment of highest risk, re-escalates without the treatment that was managing the risk, the victim is no more protected than before, and the therapist has substituted termination for the protective actions the duty required. The defense is to take the protective actions the duty requires (warning, law enforcement notification, hospitalization), to address the violence risk in the treatment, to preserve the alliance to the extent possible, and to recognize that discharging the client is not a protective action but the removal of one, because a client who remains in treatment is safer than one who has been driven from it.

### Failing To Document The Assessment And Actions

When the therapist assesses the violence risk and takes (or does not take) the protective actions but does not document the assessment, the factors weighed, the determination, the consultation, and the actions, the record does not reflect the diligence of the assessment, and if the case is later scrutinized, the therapist has no evidence of the judgment exercised. The mechanism is that the assessment and the actions feel like the work, so the documentation feels secondary, while the documentation is the evidence on which the therapist's conduct will be evaluated, and its absence leaves the therapist unable to demonstrate the diligence of the judgment. The harm is that, if the case is scrutinized (a complaint, a lawsuit, a licensing board inquiry), the therapist cannot demonstrate the assessment, the factors weighed, the consultation, or the rationale for the actions, and the absence of documentation reads as the absence of diligence, regardless of the actual care exercised. The defense is to document the assessment (the risk and protective factors weighed), the determination (whether the duty was triggered and the rationale), the consultation (who, when, what was recommended), and the actions (what was done, when, to whom), because the documentation is the evidence that the duty was handled with diligence, and its absence is indistinguishable from its absence of diligence.
