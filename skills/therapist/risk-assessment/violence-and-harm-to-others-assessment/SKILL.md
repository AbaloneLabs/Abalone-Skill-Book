# Violence And Harm To Others Assessment

Therapists are trained to think about suicide risk but often less prepared for violence risk toward others, even though the legal and ethical obligations are equally serious and the decisions more tangled. Violence risk assessment shares methods with suicide assessment, ideation, plan, intent, means, history, but it adds a distinctive complication: the duty to protect identifiable victims, which requires the therapist to breach confidentiality and sometimes contact the victim or law enforcement. The recurring failures are failing to ask about violent ideation at all, missing the duty to protect because the threat was not recognized as triggering it, over-disclosing in ways that violate confidentiality beyond what the law requires, and carrying the decision alone without consultation or legal guidance.

Use this skill when a client expresses violent ideation, when a potential victim is identifiable, when assessing history of violence, or when deciding on protective action and confidentiality boundaries.

## Core Rules

### Assess Violent Ideation, Plan, Intent, And Target As Specifically As Suicide

Ask directly about thoughts of harming others, using the same structured approach as suicide assessment. Vague reassurance that a client "wouldn't hurt anyone" is not an assessment.

Assess:

- ideation: presence, frequency, intensity, who the thoughts involve, passive wishes versus active plans;
- target: whether there is an identifiable victim or class of victims, such as a partner, a former employer, a public figure, or a group;
- plan: specificity, method, time, place, preparatory acts such as surveillance, acquiring weapons, or researching the target;
- intent: how close to acting, what has prevented action so far, what would trigger action;
- means: access to weapons, especially firearms, and other methods relevant to the plan;
- history: past violence, arrests, restraining orders, weapon-carrying, and the escalation pattern over time.

Identifiability of the victim is the threshold question for the duty to protect. A general statement of anger is different from a named target with a plan.

### Know And Apply Your Jurisdiction's Duty To Warn And Protect

The duty to warn and protect originated in the Tarasoff case and has been codified differently across jurisdictions. Know the specific legal standard where you practice: some states impose a duty to warn the identifiable victim, some impose a duty to protect that may be satisfied by steps short of warning, some protect therapists who warn in good faith, and the thresholds and required actions differ. The duty is generally triggered by a serious threat of physical violence against a reasonably identifiable victim.

When the duty may apply:

- identify the specific legal standard in your jurisdiction before acting, not after;
- consult immediately with a supervisor, risk management, or legal counsel if available;
- take the protective action the standard requires, which may include warning the victim, notifying law enforcement, initiating hospitalization, or a combination;
- document the threat, the consultation, the legal basis, the action taken, and the reasoning.

Do not improvise the legal standard under pressure; know it in advance, and consult when the situation is live.

### Distinguish Threats That Trigger The Duty From General Anger

Not all expressions of anger or violent fantasy trigger the duty to protect. A client who says "I'm so angry at my boss I could kill him" in the context of venting, with no plan, no intent, and no preparatory acts, is different from a client who names a specific target, describes a method, and has acquired means. The clinical task is to assess the specificity and seriousness of the threat and to determine whether it crosses the threshold that the legal standard sets. When the threshold is unclear, consult; do not guess, because both over-disclosure and under-disclosure carry harm.

### Address Firearm And Weapon Access As A Modifiable Risk Factor

As with suicide, access to firearms dramatically increases the lethality of violence, and means restriction is a modifiable risk factor. Ask about weapon access directly, including firearms in the home or accessible elsewhere, and discuss securing or removing them during the risk period. Involve family or supports with consent where possible. Document the discussion and the plan.

### Consider involuntary Hold Or Hospitalization For Acute Danger To Others

When a client presents an acute danger to others that cannot be managed with outpatient means, the threshold for involuntary evaluation or hospitalization is met in most jurisdictions under criteria for danger to others. The decision is grave and should follow the legal process in your jurisdiction, but the clinical threshold is a serious, specific threat that the client cannot or will not refrain from acting on. Arrange emergency evaluation; do not send the client home to "see if they calm down."

### Maintain The Therapeutic Relationship While Taking Protective Action

Taking protective action, warning a victim, notifying law enforcement, initiating hospitalization, will strain or damage the alliance, but the duty is not optional. The clinical task is to take the action while being as honest with the client as the situation allows: tell the client what you are required to do and why, unless doing so would increase the danger, and frame the action as protective of both the potential victim and the client, who faces legal consequences if they act. A client may terminate, but the duty is not contingent on the client's agreement.

### Document The Threat, The Assessment, The Consultation, And The Action

Violence risk documentation must show the threat, the assessment of specificity and seriousness, the identifiability of the victim, the consultation sought, the legal basis for action, the action taken, and the follow-up. In the event of an adverse outcome or a confidentiality complaint, the documentation is what demonstrates that the therapist met the duty. Document contemporaneously and specifically.

## Common Traps

### Never Asking About Violent Ideation

Many therapists screen routinely for suicide but never ask about thoughts of harming others, because the question feels intrusive or because violence is assumed not to arise in "ordinary" outpatient caseloads. The mechanism of harm is that violent ideation goes undetected until it becomes behavior, and the therapist, having never asked, has neither the assessment nor the documentation to support protective action. The harm is that the duty to protect is never triggered in the therapist's mind because the threat was never surfaced in session. Asking about violent ideation, especially in clients with relevant risk factors such as recent loss, persecution beliefs, relationship rupture, or weapon access, is part of routine risk assessment, not a special intervention for forensic populations.

### Missing The Duty Because The Threat Was Indirect

A threat may not come as a direct statement of intent but as a plan, a fantasy, a grievance narrative, or a rehearsal described in detail, and the therapist may not recognize that the specificity and identifiability cross the threshold for the duty to protect. The mechanism is that indirect or narrative forms of threat feel less serious than a direct statement, so the therapist treats them as processing rather than planning, and the duty is not considered. The harm is that the very clients most at risk of acting, those who have built a detailed grievance and plan, are the ones whose threats sound like storytelling. Assess the content of what is described, not the stylistic form; a detailed plan described calmly is more dangerous than an angry outburst with no plan.

### Over-Disclosure Beyond What The Duty Requires

In anxiety about the duty, a therapist may disclose more than the legal standard requires, breaching confidentiality broadly, notifying employers, family members not at risk, or agencies with no role, in ways that harm the client beyond what protection demands. The mechanism is that the duty feels absolute and the therapist, wanting to be safe, over-applies it, treating any violence-related content as license to disclose everything. The harm is unnecessary breaches that damage the client, the alliance, and the therapist's credibility, and that may themselves create liability for over-disclosure. The duty is specific: it requires protective action toward identifiable victims, not wholesale disclosure. Consult to calibrate the action to the legal standard, and disclose only what the standard requires to protect the victim.

### Carrying The Decision Alone

Violence risk decisions, especially those involving the duty to protect, are high-stakes and legally fraught, and a therapist who decides alone, without consultation, is more likely to err in either direction and has no record of due diligence. The mechanism is that the decision feels urgent and the therapist acts under time pressure without pausing to consult, or feels that consulting is an admission of uncertainty. The harm is a decision made without the second perspective that would have caught an error, and no documentation that the therapist sought guidance. Consultation with a supervisor, colleague, risk management, or legal counsel is the standard of care in duty-to-protect cases, and the consultation itself is part of the protective action; document it.

### Confusing Venting With Planning, In Either Direction

The therapist must distinguish venting from planning, and errors in both directions are harmful. Reading genuine planning as mere venting leads to missed duty and an un-warned victim; reading ordinary venting as planning leads to unnecessary disclosure that breaches confidentiality and damages the client. The mechanism is that the distinction rests on specificity, intent, preparatory acts, and weapon access, not on the emotional tone, and a therapist who attends only to tone will misclassify. The corrective is to assess the content systematically: is there a target, a method, a timeline, preparatory behavior, means access, and stated intent? Venting lacks these; planning has them. When the distinction is genuinely unclear, consult before acting.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I assess violent ideation, target identifiability, plan, intent, means, and history of violence directly and specifically?
- [ ] Do I know the specific duty-to-warn and duty-to-protect standard in my jurisdiction, and did I apply it rather than improvising?
- [ ] Did I distinguish threats that trigger the duty from general anger or venting, based on specificity, intent, preparatory acts, and means, not on emotional tone?
- [ ] Did I address firearm and weapon access as a modifiable risk factor, with specific steps to secure or remove means?
- [ ] Did I arrange emergency evaluation or hospitalization for acute danger to others rather than managing as outpatient?
- [ ] Did I take the required protective action while being honest with the client about what I am required to do and why?
- [ ] Did I disclose only what the legal standard requires to protect the identifiable victim, avoiding over-disclosure beyond the duty?
- [ ] Did I seek consultation on the threat assessment and the protective action, and document the consultation?
- [ ] Did I document the threat, the assessment, the legal basis, the consultation, the action taken, and the follow-up contemporaneously?
