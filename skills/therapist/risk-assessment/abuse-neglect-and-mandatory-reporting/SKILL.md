# Abuse, Neglect, And Mandatory Reporting

Mandatory reporting creates a structural tension unique to therapy: the therapist is bound to confidentiality, yet the law requires breaching it when abuse or neglect of a protected person is suspected. The therapist's judgment is exercised not in deciding whether to report, when the legal threshold is met the report is mandatory, but in recognizing when the threshold is met, gathering the assessment without re-traumatizing the client, making the report in a way that minimizes harm, and maintaining the relationship through a breach the client did not consent to. The recurring failures are failing to screen for abuse at all, missing the reporting threshold because the evidence is not "certain," delaying the report while gathering more information, and damaging the alliance by making the report without preparing the client or by failing to follow up.

Use this skill when screening for abuse, when abuse is disclosed or suspected, when deciding whether a report is required, when making a report, or when managing the relationship after a report.

## Core Rules

### Screen For Abuse Across Protected Populations As Part Of Routine Assessment

Abuse is common, often hidden, and rarely volunteered without being asked. Build screening into routine assessment for child abuse and neglect, elder abuse and neglect, dependent adult abuse, and intimate partner violence, using direct, specific questions rather than waiting for disclosure.

Screen for:

- child abuse and neglect in any client who is a minor, who has children, or who has contact with children;
- elder and dependent adult abuse in clients who are older or who have cognitive or physical disabilities, or whose family members do;
- intimate partner violence in all adult clients, regardless of gender or presentation, using validated screening questions;
- abuse of the client themselves, because a client's own history of abuse may involve an ongoing protected victim, such as a sibling or a child now in the home.

Screen privately, away from partners or family members, and frame the questions as routine, not as suspicion.

### Know Your Jurisdiction's Mandatory Reporting Law In Detail

Mandatory reporting laws differ by jurisdiction in who is a mandated reporter, what must be reported, the standard of suspicion required, the timeframe, and the agency to report to. Know the specifics where you practice:

- what categories of abuse and neglect are covered, physical, sexual, emotional, neglect, exploitation;
- who is a protected person, children, elders, dependent adults, and the age and capacity definitions;
- the threshold, typically reasonable suspicion or reasonable cause, not certainty or proof;
- the timeframe, often immediately or within a short window such as 24 to 72 hours;
- the agency, child protective services, adult protective services, or law enforcement;
- whether reports of intimate partner violence against an adult are mandatory, because in most jurisdictions they are not, though exceptions exist.

The threshold is suspicion, not certainty. You do not need to investigate, prove, or confirm the abuse before reporting; you need a reasonable basis to suspect it.

### Report On Suspicion, Do Not Investigate Before Reporting

A common error is delaying the report while gathering more evidence to be "sure." The mandated reporter's role is not investigator; investigating can contaminate evidence, re-traumatize the victim, and delay protection. When the threshold of reasonable suspicion is met, report. Document the basis for the suspicion, make the report within the required timeframe, and let the investigating agency do the investigation. If you need to gather more to reach the threshold of suspicion, do so efficiently, but do not treat the report as contingent on certainty.

### Make The Report Even When It Will Damage The Alliance

A mandatory report may damage or end the therapeutic relationship, especially when the client is the abuser or when the report is against the client's wishes. The duty is not optional and is not contingent on the client's consent. The clinical task is to make the report and to manage the relationship as well as possible: tell the client what you are required to do and why, unless doing so would endanger the victim, frame the report honestly, and offer to continue the therapeutic work. Some clients will leave; many, with honest handling, will stay. Do not avoid the report to preserve the alliance, because the unprotected victim's safety outweighs the alliance, and failure to report is both a legal violation and a professional ethics violation.

### Distinguish Mandatory Reports From Permissive Disclosures

Not all abuse-related disclosures trigger a mandatory report. Intimate partner violence against a competent adult is generally not mandatorily reportable in most jurisdictions, though the victim may consent to disclosure and may need help accessing protection. A client's disclosure of their own childhood abuse, where the perpetrator no longer has access to children, may not trigger a report, though the therapist should assess whether the perpetrator currently has access to minors. Know the difference between what you must report and what you may disclose only with consent, and do not over-report in ways that breach confidentiality unnecessarily and re-traumatize the client.

### Assess Safety And Develop A Safety Plan For The Victim

Alongside the report, assess the immediate safety of the victim and develop a safety plan. For intimate partner violence, this includes assessing lethality risk, escape planning, and connecting to domestic violence resources. For child and elder abuse, the investigating agency handles removal and protection, but the therapist can support the family and the victim through the process. Document the safety assessment and the resources provided.

### Document The Suspicion, The Report, And The Follow-Up

Document the basis for the suspicion, the agency contacted, the date and time of the report, the person spoken to, the content of the report, and any follow-up. Mandatory reporting documentation protects the therapist legally, demonstrates compliance with the duty, and provides a record for the investigating agency. Do not document speculative or inflammatory content beyond what supports the suspicion; document factually.

## Common Traps

### Failing To Screen Because Abuse Is Assumed Not To Arise

Therapists who do not routinely screen for abuse miss it because abuse is rarely volunteered unprompted, and the clients who experience it are often the least able to disclose spontaneously. The mechanism of harm is that the absence of screening reads as professional restraint, when it is actually a failure to detect a high-stakes, common problem. A client who is being abused by a partner, whose child is being abused, or who is abusing a child in the home will not raise it unless asked, and the therapist who never asks will never know. The harm is that abuse continues undetected and unreported, sometimes for the duration of treatment, while the therapist treats the downstream symptoms. Routine, direct screening of all relevant clients is the standard of care, not an intrusion.

### Waiting For Certainty Before Reporting

The mandated reporter's threshold is reasonable suspicion, but therapists often delay reporting until they feel certain, treating the report as a grave accusation that requires proof. The mechanism is that making a report feels like accusing a client or a family of a serious wrong, and the therapist, wanting to avoid a false report, waits for more evidence. The harm is twofold: the victim remains unprotected during the delay, and the therapist may never reach certainty because the evidence is inherently hidden, so the report is never made. The legal standard does not require certainty; it requires reasonable suspicion. Documenting the basis for the suspicion and reporting within the timeframe is compliance; waiting for proof is a failure of the duty.

### Investigating Before Reporting

A therapist who learns of possible abuse may try to investigate, interviewing the client or family in detail, seeking confirmation, or corroborating with other sources, before reporting. The mechanism is that investigating feels like thorough clinical work, but it risks contaminating the evidence the investigating agency needs, re-traumatizing the victim through repeated questioning, and delaying protection. The harm is that the investigation undermines the agency's ability to assess the case and may itself be used to discredit the victim's account. The mandated reporter gathers enough to reach reasonable suspicion and reports; the investigation is the agency's role, not the therapist's.

### Avoiding The Report To Preserve The Alliance

When the client is the abuser or strongly opposes the report, the therapist may avoid or delay reporting to preserve the therapeutic relationship, rationalizing that the relationship is the best protection for the victim. The mechanism is that the alliance genuinely matters and the therapist values it, so breaching it feels like harming the client. The harm is that the unprotected victim remains at risk, the legal duty is violated, and the therapist has made a unilateral decision to prioritize the alliance over a legal and ethical obligation that is not theirs to weigh. The duty is mandatory; the alliance is managed around it, not traded against it. Honest communication with the client about the requirement, and ongoing offer of therapeutic support, is how the alliance is preserved through a necessary breach.

### Over-Reporting Or Reporting Without A Reasonable Basis

In anxiety about the duty, a therapist may report on thin suspicion, or report disclosures that do not meet the threshold, such as a client's own adult childhood abuse by a perpetrator with no current access to children, or intimate partner violence against a competent adult where reporting is not mandatory. The mechanism is that the duty feels absolute and the therapist, wanting to be safe, reports whenever abuse is mentioned, without assessing whether the legal threshold is met. The harm is unnecessary breaches of confidentiality that damage clients, sometimes re-traumatize them, and erode trust in the therapeutic relationship and in the reporting system. The threshold is reasonable suspicion of abuse of a protected person; assess whether the disclosure meets it, consult when unclear, and do not report reflexively.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I screen routinely for child abuse, elder and dependent adult abuse, and intimate partner violence, using direct questions in private?
- [ ] Do I know my jurisdiction's mandatory reporting law, including who is protected, the threshold of suspicion, the timeframe, and the agency?
- [ ] Did I report on reasonable suspicion rather than waiting for certainty or completing an investigation?
- [ ] Did I make the report within the required timeframe, and avoid investigating beyond what was needed to reach the threshold?
- [ ] Did I make the report even when it would damage the alliance, and communicate honestly with the client about the requirement?
- [ ] Did I distinguish mandatory reports from permissive disclosures, and avoid over-reporting disclosures that do not meet the threshold?
- [ ] Did I assess the immediate safety of the victim and develop or support a safety plan, including connecting to relevant resources?
- [ ] Did I document the basis for suspicion, the agency contacted, the date, time, and content of the report, and the follow-up?
- [ ] Did I seek consultation when the reporting threshold or the protected status was unclear?
