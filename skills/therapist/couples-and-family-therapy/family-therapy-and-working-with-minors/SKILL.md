# Family Therapy And Working With Minors

Family therapy works with the family system, and when the family includes minors, the therapy involves additional complexities: parental consent and access, the minor's confidentiality and autonomy, the competing interests of parents, children, and the system, and the developmental considerations of including children. The therapist's judgment is to assess and intervene at the family system level, to navigate parental authority and minor confidentiality thoughtfully, to include children and adolescents in age-appropriate ways, and to manage the competing interests without aligning with one family member against others. The recurring failures are aligning with the parents or the identified patient, failing to establish clear confidentiality expectations for minors, including children in ways that are developmentally inappropriate or harmful, and neglecting the system in favor of individual dynamics. Family therapy with minors requires holding the family's welfare alongside each member's, especially the most vulnerable.

Use this skill when conducting family therapy, working with families that include minors, navigating parental consent and minor confidentiality, or including children and adolescents in therapy.

## Core Rules

### Assess And Intervene At The Family System Level

The client in family therapy is the family system, its structure, patterns, communication, roles, and history, and the assessment and intervention must focus on the system, not only on the identified patient, the member whose symptoms brought the family to therapy. Assess the family's structure, the patterns of interaction, the roles and alliances, the communication, the conflict, and the family's history and development. Understand how the identified patient's symptoms function in the system, what they express or maintain, and intervene at the system level. Treating only the identified patient, while the system that maintains the symptoms continues, is unlikely to produce lasting change.

### Navigate Parental Consent And Access Thoughtfully

When working with families that include minors, navigate parental consent and access thoughtfully, because parents have legal authority over their minor children, including consent to treatment and access to records, but minors also have developing confidentiality and autonomy interests. Establish at the outset who has consent authority, which is typically the parents or legal guardians, and what access they have to the minor's records and information. Clarify with parents and minor what will and will not be shared, recognizing that some confidentiality supports the minor's engagement, while parental access is a legal right. Know your jurisdiction's rules about minor consent and access, which vary, and establish clear expectations.

### Establish Clear Confidentiality Expectations For Minors

Confidentiality for minors in family therapy is complex, because parents have access rights, the family is the client, and the minor has confidentiality interests. Establish clear expectations at the outset: what the minor can expect to be confidential, what will be shared with parents, and how information will be handled. Recognize that some confidentiality supports the minor's engagement and honesty, while absolute confidentiality is not possible given parental rights and the family context. Address the limits of confidentiality, including mandated reporting and risk, and revisit the expectations as the therapy evolves. Clear expectations prevent the binds that ambiguity creates.

### Include Children And Adolescents In Age-Appropriate Ways

Include children and adolescents in family therapy in age-appropriate ways, because their participation is important, but the format and content must match their developmental level. Young children may participate through play, drawing, or structured activities, rather than adult conversation. Adolescents may participate more fully but need their perspective and autonomy respected. Do not include children in adult conflict or material that is inappropriate for them, and do not use them as informants against their parents. Tailor the inclusion to the child's developmental level, and protect them from material or dynamics that could harm them.

### Manage Competing Interests Without Aligning Against A Member

Family therapy involves competing interests: parents' authority and concerns, children's needs and autonomy, the identified patient's symptoms, and the family's welfare. Manage these competing interests without aligning with one member against others, which would replicate the family's problematic dynamics and undermine the therapy. Maintain neutrality among family members, attend to each member's perspective and needs, and keep the family's welfare as the overarching goal. Recognize the power differentials, especially between adults and children, and protect vulnerable members, including children, from being scapegoated or burdened. The therapist holds the family's welfare alongside each member's.

### Recognize And Address Power Differentials And Safety

Family systems involve power differentials, between adults and children, between partners, and among siblings, and the therapist must recognize and address these, especially when safety is at risk. Assess for abuse, neglect, and domestic violence, and address safety before proceeding with family work. Do not conduct family therapy that includes a perpetrator and victim together when ongoing abuse is present, because the joint format can endanger the victim. Protect vulnerable members, especially children, from being coerced, scapegoated, or harmed in the therapy, and recognize that the therapy room can re-create the family's power dynamics. Safety and the protection of vulnerable members are foundational.

### Coordinate With Other Providers And Systems

Families in therapy are often involved with other systems, schools, child welfare, medical providers, or individual therapists, and coordinating with these systems, with appropriate authorization, supports the family's care. Understand the family's involvement with other systems, obtain authorization for coordination, and share information that supports the family's treatment. Recognize that other systems may have their own goals and perspectives, and navigate any conflicts thoughtfully. Coordination supports the family's care, especially when the family is complex or when multiple providers are involved.

## Common Traps

### Aligning With The Parents Or The Identified Patient

A therapist may align with the parents, who often bring the family to therapy and who hold authority, or with the identified patient, whose symptoms are salient, and lose the system view that family therapy requires. The mechanism is that the parents' or the identified patient's framing is compelling, and the therapist, without recognizing it, adopts that perspective. The harm is that the other members experience the therapist as partisan, the alliance ruptures, and the therapy cannot work with the system. Maintaining neutrality among members, and attending to the system rather than aligning with a member, is what keeps the work at the family level.

### Failing To Establish Clear Confidentiality For Minors

A therapist may fail to establish clear confidentiality expectations for minors, leaving the minor and the parents uncertain about what will be shared, which can create binds and undermine the minor's engagement. The mechanism is that the confidentiality rules are complex, and the therapist, avoiding the complexity, does not clarify them, when the ambiguity creates problems. The harm is that the minor does not trust the confidentiality and does not engage honestly, or that parents feel excluded and demand information, creating conflict. Establishing clear expectations at the outset, and revisiting them, is what prevents the binds.

### Including Children In Developmentally Inappropriate Ways

A therapist may include children in family therapy in ways that are developmentally inappropriate, expecting young children to participate in adult conversation, exposing them to adult conflict, or using them as informants against their parents, which can harm the children. The mechanism is that the therapist includes children without tailoring to their developmental level, or uses the family format without protecting the children. The harm is that children are exposed to material or dynamics they cannot handle, are burdened or scapegoated, and may be harmed by the experience. Tailoring inclusion to developmental level, and protecting children from inappropriate material, is what keeps their participation safe and useful.

### Neglecting The System For Individual Dynamics

A therapist may focus on individual dynamics, the identified patient's symptoms or an individual member's issues, and neglect the family system, which is the actual client. The mechanism is that individual dynamics are familiar and visible, and the therapist, comfortable with individual work, addresses them, when the family's difficulties are in the system. The harm is that the system patterns are not addressed, the symptoms persist or shift to another member, and the family does not improve. Maintaining the focus on the system, and using the patterns and interactions as the material, is what makes family therapy effective.

### Conducting Family Therapy Despite Abuse Or Unsafe Dynamics

A therapist may conduct family therapy despite the presence of abuse, neglect, or unsafe dynamics, failing to recognize the danger or rationalizing the dynamics as treatable, which can endanger vulnerable members. The mechanism is that the unsafe dynamics are not identified, or are minimized as "family conflict," and the therapist, wanting to help, proceeds, when family therapy in the presence of ongoing abuse is contraindicated. The harm is that vulnerable members, especially children, are endangered, the abuse is reinforced or normalized, and the therapy may worsen the danger. Assessing for and addressing abuse and safety before proceeding, and not conducting joint therapy when ongoing abuse is present, is what protects vulnerable members.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.
