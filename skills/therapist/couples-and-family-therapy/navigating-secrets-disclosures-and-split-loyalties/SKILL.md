# Navigating Secrets, Disclosures, And Split Loyalties

Secrets, undisclosed information that one family member holds from others, are common in couples and family therapy: infidelity, hidden substance use, financial secrets, health information, or past events. Secrets create binds for the therapist who learns them, affect the family system even when undisclosed, and carry complex implications for disclosure. Split loyalties, triangulation, and competing alliances, where family members are pulled to align with one member against another, are common dynamics that the therapist must navigate. The therapist's judgment is to anticipate secrets and establish policies before they arise, to navigate discovered secrets thoughtfully with attention to safety and the system, to avoid being triangulated into family alliances, and to work with split loyalties as system dynamics rather than personal failures. The recurring failures are being caught unprepared by secrets, disclosing or withholding without clinical rationale, being triangulated into family conflicts, and neglecting the system function of secrets and loyalties.

Use this skill when navigating secrets or undisclosed information in couples or family therapy, managing disclosures, or working with triangulation and split loyalties.

## Core Rules

### Anticipate Secrets And Establish Policies Before They Arise

Secrets are common enough in couples and family therapy that the therapist should anticipate them and establish policies before they arise. At the outset, explain how the therapist will handle information shared by one member, whether secrets will be kept, and the expectation that significant information affecting the system will be brought into the therapy. A common policy is that the therapist does not keep secrets that affect the couple or family, and that members are encouraged to disclose in the therapy, with the therapist's support. Establishing the policy before a secret arises prevents the bind of discovering one unprepared.

### Navigate Discovered Secrets Thoughtfully

When a secret is discovered, typically in an individual session, navigate it thoughtfully, with attention to safety, the system, and the therapeutic goals. Assess the secret's implications: does it involve ongoing risk, such as hidden substance use or affairs that may expose partners to harm? Does it maintain a dysfunctional system pattern? What would disclosure or continued secrecy mean for the system? Avoid automatic disclosure or automatic secrecy; instead, explore with the member who holds the secret their readiness to disclose, the obstacles, and the supports needed. Use consultation, because secrets are clinically and ethically complex, and document the decision-making.

### Avoid Automatic Disclosure Or Automatic Secrecy

Neither automatic disclosure nor automatic secrecy is the right approach to secrets; each must be navigated individually. Automatic disclosure, revealing the secret without the holder's involvement, betrays the holder's trust and may endanger them. Automatic secrecy, keeping the secret indefinitely, may maintain a dysfunctional pattern, leave other members at risk, or compromise the therapy. The thoughtful approach explores the secret's function and implications, works with the holder toward disclosure when appropriate, and uses the therapy to support the disclosure and its aftermath. The decision is clinical, made with consultation, and documented.

### Do Not Be Triangulated Into Family Alliances

Family members may attempt to triangulate the therapist into their alliances, seeking the therapist's agreement against another member, using the therapist to deliver messages, or positioning the therapist as an ally in a conflict. Recognize triangulation attempts and resist them, maintaining neutrality and keeping the communication and conflict in the family, where it belongs. Do not carry messages, do not agree with one member against another, and do not allow yourself to be used as a weapon in family conflicts. Address triangulation as a dynamic, illuminating it for the family and using it as material, rather than participating in it. The therapist who is triangulated loses the system view and becomes part of the problem.

### Work With Split Loyalties As System Dynamics

Split loyalties, where family members are pulled to align with one member against another, are common in families, especially those with conflict, divorce, or estrangement, and they are system dynamics, not personal failings. Work with split loyalties as expressions of the family's structure and patterns, exploring how they function, what they protect or maintain, and how they affect members. Help family members recognize and navigate the loyalties they feel, without judgment, and work toward more flexible, less polarized alignments. In families with children, protect children from being pulled into parental loyalties, which burdens them and distorts their development.

### Address The System Function Of Secrets And Loyalties

Secrets and loyalties serve functions in family systems: they may protect members, maintain stability, avoid conflict, or preserve alliances, and understanding the function is key to working with them. Explore what the secret or the loyalty protects, what it costs, and what would change if it were addressed. A secret that maintains stability by avoiding a feared conflict may also prevent intimacy and maintain dysfunction. A loyalty that protects a vulnerable member may also polarize the family. Address the function, rather than simply pushing for disclosure or realignment, because the function explains why the pattern persists and what would need to change for it to shift.

### Use Consultation For Complex Secret And Loyalty Dynamics

Secrets and loyalties are clinically and ethically complex, and the therapist should use consultation for difficult cases, because the dynamics can be hard to see clearly from inside. Consultation provides perspective on the secret's implications, the decision about disclosure, the triangulation dynamics, and the therapist's own reactions, which may be strong. Do not navigate complex secrets or loyalty dynamics alone, because the risks of error, to the family and to the therapy, are significant. Consultation is part of sound practice in couples and family therapy, especially when secrets or loyalties are involved.

## Common Traps

### Being Caught Unprepared By Secrets

A therapist may be caught unprepared when a secret emerges, without an established policy, and may make a hasty decision about disclosure or secrecy that is not clinically sound. The mechanism is that secrets seem exceptional, and the therapist, not anticipating them, does not establish a policy, when the first decision is made in the moment, under pressure. The harm is that the decision may be wrong, the holder's trust may be betrayed or the system may be maintained in dysfunction, and the therapy is compromised. Anticipating secrets and establishing policies before they arise is what prevents being caught unprepared.

### Disclosing Or Withholding Without Clinical Rationale

A therapist may disclose a secret automatically, believing the partner or family has a right to know, or withhold it automatically, believing the holder's confidentiality is paramount, without a clinical rationale specific to this secret and this system. The mechanism is that the therapist applies a general principle, disclosure or confidentiality, without exploring the specific implications, when each secret requires individual navigation. The harm is that automatic disclosure may betray the holder and endanger them, while automatic secrecy may maintain dysfunction and risk. Navigating each secret thoughtfully, with attention to safety, system function, and therapeutic goals, is what guides sound decisions.

### Being Triangulated Into Family Alliances

A therapist may be triangulated into family alliances, agreeing with one member against another, carrying messages, or becoming an ally in a conflict, which compromises the therapist's neutrality and effectiveness. The mechanism is that family members seek allies, and the therapist, wanting to help or uncomfortable with conflict, is drawn in, when the triangulation makes the therapist part of the problem. The harm is that the therapist loses the system view, other members experience the therapist as partisan, and the family's patterns are reinforced rather than addressed. Recognizing triangulation and resisting it, keeping conflict in the family, is what maintains the therapist's effectiveness.

### Neglecting The System Function Of Secrets And Loyalties

A therapist may focus on the content of a secret or a loyalty, what is hidden or who is aligned with whom, and neglect the function it serves in the system, which is key to understanding and changing it. The mechanism is that the content is salient, and the therapist, focused on it, misses the function, when the function explains why the pattern persists. The harm is that the pattern is addressed at the content level, perhaps through disclosure or realignment, without understanding what it protects or maintains, and it persists or re-emerges. Exploring the function, what the secret or loyalty protects and costs, is what allows lasting change.

### Navigating Complex Secrets Without Consultation

A therapist may navigate a complex secret, such as infidelity, hidden substance use, or abuse history, without consultation, relying on their own judgment in a high-stakes, complex situation. The mechanism is that the therapist wants to handle it, or is concerned about confidentiality, and does not seek consultation, when the dynamics are complex and the therapist's judgment may be clouded. The harm is that the decision about disclosure or secrecy may be wrong, the system may be harmed, and the therapy compromised. Using consultation for complex secrets and loyalties is part of sound practice, and prevents navigating them alone.
