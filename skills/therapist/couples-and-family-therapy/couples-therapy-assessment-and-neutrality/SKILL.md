# Couples Therapy Assessment And Neutrality

Couples therapy introduces a fundamentally different clinical frame from individual therapy: the client is the relationship, the system, not the individuals, and the therapist must assess and intervene at the system level while maintaining neutrality between partners. The therapist's judgment is to assess the relationship system, its patterns, cycles, and history, rather than only the individuals; to maintain therapeutic neutrality, avoiding alignment with one partner; to navigate the complications of individual sessions within couples work; and to recognize when couples therapy is contraindicated, such as in the presence of ongoing domestic violence. The recurring failures are aligning with one partner and losing the system view, conducting individual therapy with partners in each other's presence, using individual sessions in ways that compromise the couples work, and proceeding with couples therapy when individual safety is at risk.

Use this skill when conducting couples therapy, assessing a relationship, maintaining neutrality, or deciding whether couples therapy is appropriate.

## Core Rules

### Assess The Relationship System, Not Only The Individuals

The client in couples therapy is the relationship, the system, and the assessment must focus on the system, its patterns, cycles, communication, conflict, history, and strengths, as well as the individuals. Assess the couple's interactional patterns, how they communicate, how they conflict, how they repair, and how they connect. Understand each partner's contribution to the patterns, without assigning blame, and understand the system's history, how the patterns developed. The assessment includes the individuals, their histories, presentations, and goals, but it centers on the system they form together. Assessing only the individuals misses the relationship, which is the client.

### Maintain Therapeutic Neutrality Between Partners

Therapeutic neutrality, maintaining a balanced alliance with both partners, is foundational to couples therapy, because a therapist who aligns with one partner loses the ability to work with the system and may be experienced as a partisan by the other. Neutrality does not mean having no reactions; it means managing your reactions so they do not produce alignment, and attending to both partners' perspectives, feelings, and contributions. Monitor yourself for alignment, which can be subtle, through who you empathize with more, who you challenge more, or whose framing you adopt. If you recognize alignment, correct it, and use consultation to understand it. Neutrality is actively maintained, not passively assumed.

### Navigate Individual Sessions Within Couples Work Carefully

Individual sessions within couples therapy, whether for assessment or for addressing individual issues, introduce complications because they can create secrets, alliances, or imbalances that undermine the couples work. If you hold individual sessions, establish clear expectations about what will and will not be shared with the partner, and prefer a policy of transparency, where significant content is brought back to the couple. Use individual sessions sparingly and purposefully, and recognize that what is learned individually must be integrated into the couples work. Be cautious about secrets, which place the therapist in a bind and can damage the couples therapy, and prefer to address sensitive material in the couples session when possible.

### Screen For And Address Domestic Violence Before Couples Therapy

Couples therapy is contraindicated in the presence of ongoing domestic violence, because the violence creates a power imbalance and a safety risk that the couples frame cannot address, and the therapy may endanger the victim or blame them for the violence. Screen for domestic violence, through individual sessions or validated screening, before beginning couples therapy, and if violence is present, address it through individual work, safety planning, and referral, not couples therapy. Distinguish situational couple violence, which may be addressed in couples work with caution, from intimate terrorism, which requires individual intervention. Do not proceed with couples therapy when ongoing violence creates risk, because the couples frame can endanger the victim.

### Establish Clear Confidentiality Policies For The Couple

Confidentiality in couples therapy has unique features, because the clients are the couple, and the therapist must establish clear policies about what is confidential within the couple and how information will be shared. Establish whether the therapist will keep individual secrets, the standard recommendation is not to, and explain the policy at the outset. Clarify how records will be handled, how requests for information from one partner will be managed, and how the couple's confidentiality differs from individual confidentiality. Address these policies in the informed consent, and revisit them as needed, because ambiguity about confidentiality can create binds and undermine the therapy.

### Use Evidence-Based Couples Therapy Approaches

Use evidence-based couples therapy approaches, such as emotionally focused therapy, the Gottman method, or behavioral couples therapy, chosen based on the couple's presentation and the therapist's training. These approaches have evidence for their effectiveness and provide frameworks for assessing and intervening at the system level. Do not improvise couples therapy or conduct individual therapy with both partners present; use a trained approach that addresses the relationship system. If you are not trained in couples therapy, refer to a clinician who is, because couples therapy is a distinct competency from individual therapy.

### Recognize When Individual Therapy Is Needed Alongside Or Instead

Some couples present with individual issues, such as active substance use, untreated mental illness, or individual trauma, that require individual therapy alongside or instead of couples therapy. Recognize when individual treatment is needed, recommend it, and coordinate with the individual therapist if you are providing the couples work. Couples therapy cannot substitute for individual treatment of serious individual issues, and proceeding without addressing them may be ineffective or harmful. Distinguish what is the couples work from what is individual work, and ensure both are provided.

## Common Traps

### Aligning With One Partner And Losing The System View

A therapist may align with one partner, often the one who is more articulate, more sympathetic, or whose perspective the therapist shares, and lose the system view that couples therapy requires. The mechanism is that one partner's framing is compelling, or the therapist's own values or reactions favor one partner, and the therapist, without recognizing it, adopts that partner's perspective. The harm is that the other partner experiences the therapist as partisan, the alliance ruptures, and the therapy cannot work with the system. Monitoring for alignment, managing reactions, and attending to both partners' perspectives, is what maintains the neutrality the work requires.

### Conducting Individual Therapy With Partners Present

A therapist may conduct individual therapy with one partner while the other observes, focusing on one partner's individual issues, history, or symptoms, rather than the relationship system, which wastes the couples format and may alienate the observing partner. The mechanism is that the therapist is trained in individual therapy and defaults to that mode, working with the partner who presents individual material. The harm is that the relationship, the actual client, is not addressed, the observing partner disengages, and the couples therapy is ineffective. Maintaining the focus on the system, the patterns and interactions, rather than individual issues, is what keeps the work at the couples level.

### Individual Sessions That Create Secrets Or Imbalances

A therapist may hold individual sessions that create secrets or imbalances, learning material from one partner that is not shared, which places the therapist in a bind and can undermine the couples work. The mechanism is that individual sessions can elicit sensitive material, and the therapist, wanting to respect the individual's privacy, keeps it, when the secret creates a split that damages the couples therapy. The harm is that the therapist holds information that affects the couple but cannot share it, the therapy is distorted by the secret, and if the secret emerges, the trust is damaged. Establishing clear policies about sharing, preferring transparency, and addressing sensitive material in the couples session, is what prevents secrets from undermining the work.

### Proceeding With Couples Therapy Despite Domestic Violence

A therapist may proceed with couples therapy despite the presence of ongoing domestic violence, failing to screen adequately or rationalizing the violence as mutual, which can endanger the victim and blame them for the violence. The mechanism is that the violence is not identified, or is minimized as "conflict," and the therapist, wanting to help the couple, proceeds, when couples therapy in the presence of ongoing violence is contraindicated. The harm is that the victim is endangered, the violence is reinforced or blamed on the victim, and the therapy may worsen the danger. Screening for violence, and addressing it through individual work and safety, not couples therapy, is what protects the victim.

### Treating Couples Therapy As Individual Therapy For Both

A therapist may treat couples therapy as individual therapy for both partners, addressing each partner's individual issues in turn, rather than the relationship system, which does not address the couple's actual difficulties. The mechanism is that individual issues are visible and familiar, and the therapist, comfortable with individual work, addresses them, when the couple's difficulties are in the system. The harm is that the relationship patterns are not addressed, the couple does not improve, and they may conclude that therapy does not work. Maintaining the focus on the system, and using the relationship patterns as the material, is what makes couples therapy effective.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Am I assessing the relationship system, its patterns, cycles, and history, not only the individuals?
- [ ] Am I maintaining therapeutic neutrality, monitoring for and correcting alignment?
- [ ] If holding individual sessions, have I established clear sharing policies, preferring transparency?
- [ ] Did I screen for domestic violence, and am I avoiding couples therapy when ongoing violence is present?
- [ ] Have I established clear confidentiality policies for the couple, in the informed consent?
- [ ] Am I using an evidence-based couples therapy approach within my training?
- [ ] Am I recognizing when individual therapy is needed alongside or instead of couples work?
- [ ] Did I avoid aligning with one partner, individual therapy in couples format, secret-creating sessions, proceeding despite violence, and treating couples as two individuals?
