# Sleep Assessment and Differential Diagnosis

The sleep assessment is the clinical foundation on which the entire treatment rests, and it is the point at which the therapist most often fails by collecting a presenting-complaint insomnia narrative and then proceeding to the behavioral protocol without the differential work that would reveal the sleep apnea, the restless legs, the circadian disorder, or the medication-induced disturbance that the behavioral protocol will not resolve. The judgment problem is that insomnia is the most common sleep complaint, it is the complaint therapists are trained to treat, and the behavioral protocol is effective for it, so the pull toward the rapid protocol initiation is strong, and the differential diagnosis feels like the medical task outside the scope. In fact, the differential is the therapist's responsibility at the assessment stage, because the therapist is often the first clinician to evaluate the sleep complaint, and the missed medical sleep disorder produces the treatment failure, the wasted months, and the worsened condition. A patient whose sleep apnea is treated with the sleep restriction alone deteriorates; a patient whose restless legs is treated with the sleep hygiene never receives the iron evaluation; a patient whose circadian disorder is treated with the standard wake time worsens the phase mismatch; a patient whose insomnia is actually the medication effect never has the medication adjusted. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the sleep assessment and the differential are correct and complete, including the comprehensive interview, the tools, the differential, and the referral. Given the clinical stakes, this guidance is conservative: assess comprehensively, use the tools, differentiate the insomnia from the medical sleep disorders, and refer for the polysomnography when the red flags appear.

## Core Rules

### Conduct the Comprehensive Sleep Interview Across the Domains

The comprehensive sleep interview must cover the sleep complaint across the domains: the sleep schedule (the bedtime, the sleep-onset latency, the awakenings, the wake time, the naps), the sleep quality (the restfulness, the depth, the fragmentation), the daytime functioning (the sleepiness, the fatigue, the napping, the cognitive and the mood impact), the sleep environment (the bed, the partner, the noise, the light, the temperature), the sleep behaviors (the pre-sleep routine, the screen use, the caffeine, the alcohol, the exercise), the medical and the psychiatric history (the conditions, the medications, the substance use), and the specific sleep-disorder symptoms (the snoring, the apneas, the leg movements, the parasomnias). The interview must use the open-ended questions followed by the specific probes, and it must establish the temporal pattern (the onset, the course, the triggers, the fluctuations). The comprehensive interview is the foundation of the differential, and the shortcut (the "how are you sleeping?") misses the critical findings.

The tradeoff is the focused complaint interview (efficient, but misses the differential) versus the comprehensive interview (thorough, but takes time). The resolution is to conduct the comprehensive interview across the domains, to use the probes, and to establish the pattern.

### Use the Sleep Diary and the Validated Tools for the Objective Baseline

The sleep assessment must use the sleep diary (the daily record of the bedtime, the sleep-onset latency, the awakenings, the wake time, the sleep quality, the daytime functioning, kept for at least two weeks) and the validated tools (the Insomnia Severity Index for the insomnia severity, the Epworth Sleepiness Scale for the daytime sleepiness, the STOP-BANG or the Berlin Questionnaire for the apnea risk, the PHQ-9 and the GAD-7 for the comorbid mood and anxiety), because the diary provides the objective baseline (the actual sleep patterns, free of the recall bias), and the tools provide the standardized, the quantified, and the comparable measures that guide the differential and the treatment. The sleep diary must be reviewed with the patient (the patterns, the discrepancies, the insights), and the tools must be interpreted in the clinical context. The subjective report alone (the "I sleep poorly") is insufficient for the differential and the treatment.

The trap is the assessment without the diary or the tools, which relies on the subjective recall and misses the patterns. The defense is to use the sleep diary for the baseline, the tools for the quantification, and to review both with the patient.

### Differentiate the Insomnia From the Sleep Apnea, the RLS, and the Circadian Disorders

The differential diagnosis must differentiate the insomnia disorder from the medical sleep disorders that mimic or coexist with it, including the obstructive sleep apnea (the snoring, the witnessed apneas, the gasping, the obesity, the large neck, the morning headaches, the residual sleepiness despite the adequate sleep time, the treatment failure), the restless legs syndrome (the urge to move the legs, the evening worsening, the relief with the movement, the family history, the iron deficiency), the periodic limb movement disorder (the kicking, the disrupted sleep, the daytime sleepiness), the circadian rhythm disorders (the delayed sleep phase in the young, the advanced sleep phase in the older, the shift work, the jet lag), and the parasomnias (the sleepwalking, the REM sleep behavior disorder, the sleep-related eating), because the behavioral protocol alone will not resolve these, and the differential determines the correct treatment and the referral. The differential must use the interview, the tools, and the clinical reasoning, and the uncertain cases must be referred.

The trap is the insomnia diagnosis without the differential, which misses the medical sleep disorder. The defense is to differentiate the insomnia from the apnea, the RLS, the circadian disorders, and the parasomnias, and to refer the uncertain cases.

### Identify the Comorbid Mental Health, Medical, and Substance Causes

The sleep assessment must identify the comorbid mental health (the depression, the anxiety, the PTSD, the bipolar, the psychotic disorders), the medical (the chronic pain, the nocturia, the thyroid disease, the menopause, the heart failure, the COPD, the GERD), and the substance (the caffeine, the alcohol, the nicotine, the recreational drugs, the withdrawal) causes of the sleep disturbance, because these are the common and the treatable causes, and the behavioral protocol must account for them. The identification must use the interview, the screening tools, and the coordination with the medical providers, and the comorbid causes must be addressed alongside the sleep therapy. The therapist must evaluate the medication effects (the psychotropics, the cardiovascular, the respiratory) on the sleep, and coordinate with the prescriber.

The trap is the sleep assessment without the comorbid evaluation, which misses the common causes. The defense is to identify the mental health, the medical, and the substance causes, and to address them alongside the sleep therapy.

### Refer for the Polysomnography When the Red Flags Appear

The therapist must refer the patient for the polysomnography (the sleep study) when the red flags appear, including the suspected sleep apnea (the snoring, the witnessed apneas, the gasping, the obesity, the residual sleepiness), the suspected periodic limb movement disorder (the kicking, the disrupted sleep, the daytime sleepiness), the parasomnias (the sleepwalking, the REM sleep behavior disorder, the sleep-related eating, the injurious behaviors), the uncertain diagnosis (the atypical presentation), and the treatment failure (the CBT-I non-response), because the polysomnography is the diagnostic test for these disorders, and the behavioral protocol alone will not resolve them. The referral must be to the sleep specialist or the sleep center, with the documented rationale, and the therapist may continue the behavioral work alongside the evaluation if the insomnia is the confirmed component.

The trap is the continued behavioral treatment for the red flags, without the referral, which misses the medical sleep disorder. The defense is to refer for the polysomnography when the red flags appear, and to document the rationale.

### Establish the Sleep Diagnosis and the Treatment Plan on the Evidence

The sleep diagnosis and the treatment plan must be established on the evidence (the comprehensive interview, the sleep diary, the validated tools, the differential, the comorbid evaluation), not on the assumption or the convenience, because the correct diagnosis drives the correct treatment, and the incorrect diagnosis drives the incorrect and the ineffective treatment. The diagnosis must be the specific (the insomnia disorder, the comorbid insomnia, the circadian rhythm disorder, the suspected apnea for the referral), and the treatment plan must match the diagnosis (the CBT-I for the insomnia, the trauma-focused therapy for the PTSD nightmares, the referral for the apnea, the circadian regulation for the phase disorder). The diagnosis and the plan must be documented, with the rationale, and communicated to the patient and the care team.

The trap is the diagnosis and the plan on the assumption (the "it's insomnia"), which drives the incorrect treatment. The defense is to establish the diagnosis and the plan on the evidence, to document the rationale, and to communicate with the team.

## Common Traps

### The Complaint-Based Assessment Without the Differential

The therapist collects the insomnia complaint, conducts the brief interview, and proceeds to the CBT-I, without the differential work that would reveal the apnea, the RLS, the circadian disorder, or the parasomnia, so the medical sleep disorder is missed and the behavioral protocol fails. The mechanism of the trap is that the differential is the foundation of the correct treatment, and the complaint-based assessment without the differential misses the medical sleep disorder, because the therapist focuses on the insomnia complaint and does not probe the apnea, the legs, the circadian, or the parasomnia symptoms. The false signal is the clear insomnia complaint, which suggests the diagnosis but masks the differential. The harm is the treatment failure, the wasted months, and the worsened condition. The defense is to conduct the differential, to probe the specific sleep-disorder symptoms, and to refer the uncertain cases.

### The Assessment Without the Sleep Diary or the Tools

The assessment relies on the subjective recall ("how are you sleeping?"), without the sleep diary or the validated tools, so the actual sleep patterns, the severity, and the apnea risk are not captured, and the treatment is based on the unreliable recall. The mechanism of the trap is that the sleep diary and the tools provide the objective and the quantified data, and the assessment without them relies on the recall bias, because the patient overestimates or underestimates the sleep, and the patterns are missed. The false signal is the patient's report, which suggests the data but masks the recall bias. The harm is the inaccurate baseline, the incorrect treatment, and the unreliable progress monitoring. The defense is to use the sleep diary for the baseline, the tools for the quantification, and to review both with the patient.

### The Sleep Apnea Missed in the Insomnia Presentation

The patient presents with the insomnia (the difficulty staying asleep, the early awakening, the daytime fatigue), but the underlying sleep apnea (the snoring, the witnessed apneas, the obesity) is not probed, because the presentation is framed as the insomnia, so the apnea is missed and the behavioral protocol worsens the sleep deprivation. The mechanism of the trap is that the sleep apnea can present as the insomnia (the sleep-maintenance difficulty, the fragmented sleep, the non-restorative sleep), and the insomnia framing misses the apnea, because the therapist does not probe the apnea symptoms in the insomnia presentation. The false signal is the insomnia complaint, which suggests the behavioral treatment but masks the apnea. The harm is the missed apnea, the worsened sleep deprivation, and the cardiovascular risk. The defense is to probe the apnea symptoms in every insomnia presentation, and to refer the positive findings.

### The Circadian Disorder Treated as the Insomnia

The patient with the delayed sleep phase (the late sleep onset, the late wake time, the difficulty with the early schedule) is treated with the standard CBT-I (the consistent early wake time, the sleep restriction), without the circadian assessment, so the phase mismatch is worsened and the patient cannot adhere. The mechanism of the trap is that the circadian rhythm disorder (the delayed phase) requires the circadian-specific treatment (the morning light, the melatonin timing, the gradual phase shift), and the standard CBT-I without the circadian assessment worsens the mismatch, because the early wake time is set against the delayed phase, and the patient cannot adhere. The false signal is the sleep-onset difficulty, which suggests the insomnia but masks the circadian disorder. The harm is the non-adherence, the treatment failure, and the patient discouragement. The defense is to assess the circadian pattern, and to use the circadian-specific treatment for the phase disorder.

### The Medication or the Substance Cause Missed

The patient's sleep disturbance is caused by the medication (the new SSRI, the stimulant, the beta-blocker) or the substance (the caffeine, the alcohol, the nicotine), but the assessment does not evaluate these, because the disturbance is attributed to the insomnia or the mental health, so the cause is missed and not addressed. The mechanism of the trap is that the medications and the substances are the common and the treatable causes, and the assessment without the evaluation misses them, because the therapist does not review the medications, the substances, and their timing, and attributes the disturbance to the condition. The false signal is the mental health condition, which suggests the cause but masks the medication or the substance effect. The harm is the persistent disturbance, the medication non-adherence, and the compromised outcome. The defense is to evaluate the medication and the substance causes, and to coordinate with the prescriber.

### The Continued Behavioral Treatment for the Red Flags

The red flags (the snoring, the witnessed apneas, the leg movements, the parasomnias, the treatment failure) are present, but the therapist continues the behavioral treatment without the referral, because the flags are attributed to the adherence or the complexity, so the medical sleep disorder is missed. The mechanism of the trap is that the red flags indicate the medical sleep disorder, and the continued behavioral treatment without the referral misses the disorder, because the therapist attributes the flags to the behavioral factors and does not refer for the polysomnography. The false signal is the adherence or the complexity attribution, which suggests the explanation but masks the missed disorder. The harm is the continued suffering, the missed medical diagnosis, and the compromised care. The defense is to refer for the polysomnography when the red flags appear, and to document the rationale.

## Self-Check

- Did I conduct the comprehensive sleep interview across the domains (the schedule, the quality, the daytime, the environment, the behaviors, the medical and psychiatric, the specific sleep-disorder symptoms)?
- Did I use the sleep diary (at least two weeks) and the validated tools (the ISI, the Epworth, the STOP-BANG, the PHQ-9, the GAD-7) for the objective baseline and the quantification?
- Did I differentiate the insomnia from the sleep apnea, the restless legs, the periodic limb movement, the circadian disorders, and the parasomnias, using the interview and the tools?
- Did I identify the comorbid mental health, the medical, and the substance causes of the sleep disturbance, and coordinate with the medical providers?
- Did I refer for the polysomnography when the red flags appeared (the apnea signs, the limb movements, the parasomnias, the treatment failure), and document the rationale?
- Did I establish the specific sleep diagnosis and the matched treatment plan on the evidence, document the rationale, and communicate with the patient and the care team?
- Did I use the ongoing assessment (the diary, the tools, the clinical interview) to monitor the progress, evaluate the outcome, and adjust the plan?
- Did I practice within the scope of the licensed therapist, coordinate with the medical providers, and refer beyond the scope?
