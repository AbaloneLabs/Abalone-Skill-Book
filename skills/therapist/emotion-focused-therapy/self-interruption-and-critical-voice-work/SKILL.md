# Self-Interruption and Critical Voice Work

Self-interruption and self-criticism are two of the most important markers in emotion-focused therapy, and the judgment problem is that both are often treated as content to be discussed (the client describes their self-criticism, the therapist offers cognitive reframing) rather than as live processes to be enacted and worked with experientially, which leaves the underlying structures unchanged. Self-interruption is the client's observable interruption of their own experience (a visible blocking, a self-silencing, a physical or verbal self-stopping), and it signals an internal process that interrupts emotional experience, often to protect against vulnerability. Self-criticism is a harsh critical voice the client directs at themselves, and it signals an internalized critical structure that attacks the self. Both are worked with through two-chair tasks (the client enacts the two parts in separate chairs, externalizing the internal process), and the work is experiential and enactive rather than cognitive and discussive. The therapist must recognize the markers, must use the two-chair task to externalize and enact the internal process, must access the underlying emotion and need beneath the criticism or interruption (the critical voice often covers fear or unmet need), must work toward softening the criticism or understanding the interruption's protective function, and must avoid collapsing into cognitive discussion or enacting the therapist's agenda. The deeper traps are discussing rather than enacting, attacking the critical voice rather than understanding it, accessing only one side of the dialogue, and pushing for resolution before the underlying material is worked. The therapist's freedom is real, but the work must be experiential, enactive, and attentive to both sides. Given the clinical stakes, this guidance is conservative: recognize the markers, use two-chair tasks to enact the internal process, access the underlying emotion and need, work toward softening and understanding, and never collapse into discussion or attack the critical voice.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Self-Interruption and Self-Criticism Markers as Live Processes

The therapist must recognize self-interruption and self-criticism as they occur live in the session, not merely as content the client reports. A self-interruption marker appears as an observable self-blocking (the client begins to feel something and visibly stops themselves, changes the subject, dismisses the feeling, physically tightens), and the marker is the moment of interruption, not the report of a tendency to interrupt. A self-criticism marker appears as a harsh internal voice expressed in the client's speech (you are so stupid, you always ruin things, you do not deserve that), and the marker is the live criticism, not the report of being self-critical. The therapist should catch these markers as they occur, should mark them for the client (I notice you just stopped yourself, I hear a critical voice there), and should introduce the two-chair task while the marker is live.

### Use the Two-Chair Task to Externalize and Enact the Internal Process

The two-chair task externalizes the internal process by having the client enact the two parts (critical and criticized, or interrupting and interrupted) from separate chairs, and this enactment is the core of the work. The therapist should set up the task clearly (this chair is the critical voice, this chair is the part that receives it, speak from each in turn), should have the client speak from each part in the first person (not report what the voice says, but speak as the voice), should facilitate the dialogue between the parts, and should keep the enactment experiential rather than allowing it to collapse into discussion about the parts. The externalization makes the internal structure visible and workable, and the enactment accesses the live emotion and process that discussion does not reach.

### Access the Emotion and Need Beneath the Criticism or Interruption

The critical voice and the self-interruption are not arbitrary; they serve functions and cover underlying emotion and need, and the therapist must access these. The critical voice often covers fear (if I do not criticize you, you will fail and be rejected), unmet need (I am trying to make you good enough to be loved), or an internalized other (the voice of a critical parent). The self-interruption often protects against vulnerability (if you feel this, it will be too much). The therapist should explore what the criticism or interruption is trying to accomplish, what it is protecting against, what it needs, and should access the underlying emotion (the fear beneath the criticism, the pain beneath the interruption). Accessing the underlying material transforms the work from managing the surface structure to understanding and softening the deeper process.

### Work Toward Softening the Criticism and Understanding the Interruption's Function

The goal is not to eliminate the critical voice or the self-interruption (which often cannot be simply removed) but to soften the criticism and to understand and renegotiate the interruption's function. For self-criticism, the work moves toward the critical voice softening as it recognizes the pain it causes and the fear it operates from, and toward the criticized part expressing its hurt and asserting its need for compassion rather than attack. For self-interruption, the work moves toward understanding what the interruption protects against, acknowledging its protective function, and renegotiating it so that it allows rather than blocks necessary experiencing. The softening and renegotiation emerge from the dialogue between the parts, not from the therapist's imposition, and they require that both sides have been fully heard.

### Keep Both Sides of the Dialogue Alive and Fully Expressed

Both sides of the internal dialogue must be kept alive and fully expressed, because the transformation emerges from the encounter between them. The therapist should ensure that the critical voice is fully expressed (not shut down prematurely) and that the criticized part is fully expressed (not silenced by the criticism), should balance the dialogue so that neither side dominates, and should facilitate genuine encounter between the parts. If only one side is expressed (the criticism without the response, or the hurt without the critical voice's perspective), the dialogue does not produce transformation. The therapist must hold the space for both sides to be heard.

### Avoid Collapsing Into Cognitive Discussion of the Process

The constant risk is that the experiential enactment collapses into cognitive discussion about the process (the client stops speaking as the voice and starts talking about the voice), because discussion feels safer and more controlled than enactment, and the therapist must actively maintain the experiential mode. The therapist should redirect the client back to speaking from the parts (can you say that from the chair, as the voice), should notice when the enactment has collapsed into discussion, and should re-establish the enactment. The experiential mode is what accesses the live emotion and process; cognitive discussion about the process does not transform it.

## Common Traps

### Discussing the Self-Criticism or Self-Interruption Rather Than Enacting It

The therapist recognizes the self-criticism or self-interruption and discusses it with the client, because discussion is safer and more familiar than enactment and the therapist is more comfortable in the cognitive mode, but the discussion stays at the level of talking about the process rather than enacting it, and the underlying structure is not accessed or transformed, because cognitive discussion does not reach the experiential level where the change occurs. The mechanism is that the marker has been discussed rather than enacted, so the work has stayed at the cognitive level, and the internal process has not been externalized and worked with experientially. The false signal is the productive discussion of the self-criticism, which looks like engaged therapy but actually indicates the collapse into the cognitive mode. The harm is work that does not transform the underlying structure, the client's intellectual understanding without experiential change, and the reduced effectiveness of EFT conducted cognitively. The defense is to use the two-chair task to enact the internal process, to keep the work experiential, to redirect the client back to speaking from the parts when discussion intrudes, and to recognize that cognitive discussion does not transform experiential structures.

### Attacking the Critical Voice Rather Than Understanding It

The therapist, recognizing the harm the critical voice causes and wanting to protect the client, attacks or dismisses the critical voice, because the voice is harsh and the client is hurt by it and the therapist wants to stop the harm, but the critical voice serves a function (protection, motivation, internalized other) and attacking it without understanding its function drives it underground or intensifies its defensiveness, and the voice does not soften because it has not been understood. The mechanism is that the critical voice has been attacked rather than understood, so its function has not been accessed, and the voice defends rather than softens because it is treated as an enemy rather than as a part with a purpose. The false signal is the therapist's protective stance, which looks like advocacy for the client but actually indicates the attack on a part that needs understanding. The harm is the voice driven underground or intensified, the missed opportunity for softening through understanding, and the internal war that the attack perpetuates. The defense is to explore what the criticism is trying to accomplish, what it protects against, what it needs, and to work toward softening through understanding rather than through attack.

### Accessing Only One Side of the Dialogue

The therapist facilitates the two-chair task but allows only one side to be fully expressed, because one side is more sympathetic (the hurt criticized part) or more dominant (the loud critical voice), and the other side is underdeveloped or silenced, and the dialogue does not produce transformation because genuine encounter requires both sides to be fully heard and to respond to each other. The mechanism is that one side has been privileged and the other neglected, so the encounter between the parts has not occurred, and the transformation that emerges from their dialogue cannot happen. The false signal is the emotional intensity of the expressed side, which looks like productive work but actually indicates an incomplete dialogue. The harm is a one-sided expression that does not transform the internal structure, the reinforced dominance of one part, and the missed encounter that would produce softening and renegotiation. The defense is to keep both sides alive and fully expressed, to balance the dialogue, to ensure each side responds to the other, and to recognize that transformation requires genuine encounter between the parts.

### Pushing for Resolution Before the Underlying Material Is Worked

The therapist, eager to reach a softening or resolution and wanting the client to feel better, pushes for resolution before the underlying emotion and need have been fully accessed and worked, because the resolution is the goal and the therapist wants to achieve it, but the critical voice has not been understood, the criticized part has not been fully heard, and the premature resolution is inauthentic and does not hold, because it was imposed before the work was done. The mechanism is that resolution has been pushed for before the underlying material was worked, so the softening or renegotiation is premature and inauthentic, and it does not integrate because it was not earned through the full dialogue. The false signal is the achievement of a resolution, which looks like successful work but actually indicates a premature conclusion. The harm is an inauthentic resolution that does not hold, the client's sense of false peace that dissolves, and the underlying structure unchanged because the work was rushed. The defense is to allow the underlying material to be fully accessed and worked, to let the resolution emerge from the dialogue rather than imposing it, and to recognize that authentic softening requires that both sides have been fully heard.

### Failing to Access the Underlying Need and Operating Only at the Surface

The therapist conducts the two-chair task at the surface level (the criticism and the response), because the surface dialogue is visible and the task is proceeding, but does not access the underlying need and emotion that the criticism or interruption serves, because the underlying material is less accessible and requires deeper exploration, and the work stays at the surface, producing a dialogue that does not reach the level where transformation occurs. The mechanism is that the underlying need has not been accessed, so the work has stayed at the surface, and the critical voice or self-interruption is engaged without understanding its function or the emotion it covers. The false signal is the proceeding two-chair task, which looks like productive EFT but actually indicates surface work without depth. The harm is a dialogue that does not transform because it does not reach the underlying structure, and the unchanged pattern because the function and need were not accessed. The defense is to explore what the criticism or interruption is trying to accomplish, what it protects against, what it needs, to access the underlying emotion, and to recognize that surface dialogue without underlying access does not transform.

## Self-Check

- Did I recognize the self-interruption or self-criticism marker as a live process in the session, and did I introduce the two-chair task while the marker was live?
- Did I use the two-chair task to externalize and enact the internal process, keeping the work experiential rather than allowing it to collapse into cognitive discussion?
- Did I access the emotion and need beneath the criticism or interruption (the fear, the unmet need, the protective function), rather than operating only at the surface?
- Did I work toward softening the criticism and understanding the interruption's function, rather than attacking or dismissing the critical voice?
- Did I keep both sides of the dialogue alive and fully expressed, ensuring genuine encounter between the parts rather than privileging one side?
- Did I avoid pushing for resolution before the underlying material was fully accessed and worked, allowing the resolution to emerge from the dialogue?
- Did I maintain the experiential mode, redirecting the client back to speaking from the parts when discussion intruded?
- Did I recognize that transformation requires both the enactment and the access to underlying need, and that neither alone is sufficient?
