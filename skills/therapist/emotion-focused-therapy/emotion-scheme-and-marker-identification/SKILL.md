# Emotion Scheme and Marker Identification

Emotion-focused therapy (EFT) is organized around the identification of in-session markers (observable client states that indicate a specific emotion process is active and that a specific therapeutic task is appropriate), and the judgment problem is that markers are often missed, misidentified, or treated as content rather than as signals, and without accurate marker identification the therapist cannot select the appropriate task and the therapy loses its specificity. An emotion scheme is the client's implicit organization of emotion, cognition, and meaning (the underlying structure that produces emotional responses), and markers are the surface signals that reveal the scheme and the point at which intervention is possible. The therapist must recognize the major marker types (unfinished business, self-interruption, self-criticism, problematic reaction points, vague or unclear feeling, conflict splits), must distinguish them accurately (each calls for a different task), must select the appropriate task based on the marker, must assess the client's capacity for the affective work the task requires, and must avoid applying tasks without marker identification or pushing affective work past the client's capacity. The deeper traps are missing markers because they are subtle, misidentifying markers and applying the wrong task, applying tasks procedurally without genuine marker identification, and pushing affective work without assessing capacity. The therapist's freedom is real, but EFT is marker-driven, and task selection must follow accurate marker identification. Given the clinical stakes, this guidance is conservative: recognize the major marker types, distinguish them accurately, select the appropriate task, assess capacity, and never apply tasks without marker identification or push past the client's window of tolerance.

## Core Rules

### Recognize the Major Marker Types and What They Signal

EFT identifies specific markers that signal specific emotion processes, and the therapist must recognize the major types. A problematic reaction point marker is a client's puzzlement or frustration about their own disproportionate emotional reaction to a situation, and it signals an exploration of the underlying scheme. An unfinished business marker is a persistent unresolved feeling toward a significant other, and it signals an empty-chair or enactment task. A self-interruption marker is the client's observable interruption of their own experience (a visible blocking, a self-silencing), and it signals a self-interruption task. A self-criticism marker is a harsh critical voice that the client directs at themselves, and it signals a two-chair task for self-criticism. A vague feeling marker is a diffuse, unclear bodily or emotional sense that the client cannot articulate, and it signals a focusing task. A conflict split marker is a felt division within the self (one part wants this, another wants that), and it signals a two-chair dialogue. The therapist must learn to recognize these markers as they appear in session.

### Distinguish Markers Accurately Because Each Calls for a Different Task

Accurate marker identification matters because each marker calls for a different task, and applying the wrong task is ineffective or counterproductive. The therapist must distinguish, for example, a self-criticism marker (harsh internal voice, calls for a two-chair task where the client speaks from both the critical and the criticized parts) from a self-interruption marker (observable self-blocking, calls for a self-interruption task that explores what the interruption is protecting against), because the two can look similar (both involve an internal constraint on the client's experience) but require different interventions. The therapist should attend to the specific quality of the marker, should verify the identification (is this self-criticism or self-interruption, is this unfinished business or a problematic reaction point), and should not assume the marker from a superficial resemblance.

### Select the Appropriate Task Based on the Marker

Once the marker is identified, the therapist selects the appropriate task, and the selection must follow the marker rather than the therapist's preference. The therapist should know the task appropriate to each marker (empty-chair for unfinished business, two-chair for self-criticism and for conflict splits, focusing for vague feelings, systematic evocative unfolding for problematic reaction points, self-interruption work for self-interruption markers), should introduce the task when the marker is live, and should be prepared to shift if the marker shifts (the client may move from a self-criticism marker to an unfinished business marker within a session). The task is in service of the marker, and the therapist should not force a task when the marker is not present or has passed.

### Assess the Client's Capacity for the Affective Work the Task Requires

EFT tasks require affective engagement, and the therapist must assess the client's capacity for the specific demands of the task before proceeding. Some tasks require high affective intensity (empty-chair work with unfinished business), some require tolerance for internal conflict (two-chair work), and some require the ability to attend to subtle bodily experience (focusing). The therapist must assess whether the client can currently engage at the required level, must consider the client's window of tolerance and their history (trauma, affective regulation difficulties), must build capacity if needed before proceeding, and must recognize when a task is beyond the client's current capacity and modify or defer it. Pushing affective work past the client's capacity produces flooding, dissociation, or shutdown rather than productive processing.

### Identify the Underlying Emotion Scheme Through the Task

The marker signals the presence of an emotion scheme, and the task is the means of accessing and processing it. The therapist should use the task to help the client access the underlying scheme (the implicit organization of emotion, meaning, and belief that produces the surface response), should help the client articulate the core emotion and its meaning, and should work toward the transformation of maladaptive core emotions (through the activation of adaptive emotions that can reorganize the scheme). The scheme is the target, the marker is the signal, and the task is the method, and the therapist must keep all three in view.

### Monitor for Marker Shifts Within and Across Sessions

Markers are not stable; they shift within sessions (a client may move from self-criticism to unfinished business as the work deepens) and across sessions (the marker that was live last session may not be live this session), and the therapist must monitor for shifts and adjust the task accordingly. The therapist should not assume that the marker from the previous session is still active, should check what is live in the current moment, and should be prepared to shift tasks as the marker shifts. Flexibility and present-moment attention are essential, because EFT follows the live marker rather than a fixed plan.

## Common Traps

### Missing Markers Because They Are Subtle or Embedded in Content

The therapist focuses on the content of what the client is saying, because the content is interesting and the therapist wants to understand the client's story, and misses the marker (the moment of self-interruption, the flash of self-criticism, the vague feeling that could be focused on) because the marker is embedded in the content and is briefer and subtler than the narrative, and the opportunity for a marker-driven task is lost. The mechanism is that the marker has been missed because attention was on content rather than on the moment-to-moment process, so the signal that would have indicated a task has been overlooked, and the therapy proceeds as content discussion without accessing the emotion scheme. The false signal is the productive content discussion, which looks like engaged therapy but actually indicates the missing of the marker that would have enabled deeper work. The harm is therapy that stays at the content level without accessing the emotion schemes that drive the client's responses, and the reduced effectiveness that comes from missing the marker-driven tasks. The defense is to attend to the moment-to-moment process, to watch for markers as they appear, and to recognize that markers are often briefer and subtler than the content they are embedded in.

### Misidentifying Markers and Applying the Wrong Task

The therapist identifies a marker but misidentifies it, because two markers can look similar (self-criticism and self-interruption both involve internal constraint) and the therapist has not attended to the specific quality, and applies the task appropriate to the misidentified marker, which does not fit the actual process and is ineffective or counterproductive. The mechanism is that the marker has been misidentified, so the task appropriate to the wrong marker has been applied, and the intervention does not match the actual emotion process. The false signal is the apparent appropriateness of applying a task to an identified marker, which looks like competent EFT but actually indicates a misidentification. The harm is an ineffective or counterproductive intervention, the client's confusion or frustration, and the wasted session time on a task that does not fit. The defense is to attend to the specific quality of the marker, to verify the identification before applying the task, and to recognize that similar-looking markers require different tasks.

### Applying Tasks Procedurally Without Genuine Marker Identification

The therapist applies EFT tasks (two-chair, empty-chair, focusing) because the tasks are known and the client's presentation resembles the marker, but applies them procedurally without genuine marker identification, because the tasks are the visible method and the marker identification feels less concrete, and the tasks are applied when the marker is not genuinely live, producing mechanical interventions that do not access the emotion scheme. The mechanism is that the task has been applied without marker identification, so the intervention is procedural rather than responsive, and the task does not fit the live process because no live marker was confirmed. The false signal is the correct performance of the task technique, which looks like competent EFT but actually indicates a technique without its marker basis. The harm is mechanical interventions that feel inauthentic, the client's disengagement from tasks that do not fit, and the reduced effectiveness of EFT applied without marker-driven specificity. The defense is to identify the marker genuinely before applying the task, to introduce the task only when the marker is live, and to recognize that EFT is marker-driven rather than task-driven.

### Pushing Affective Work Past the Client's Window of Tolerance

The therapist, having identified a marker and selected a task, pushes the affective work past the client's capacity, because the task requires affective engagement and the therapist wants to access the core emotion, but the client's window of tolerance is narrower than the task demands, and the client floods, dissociates, or shuts down, and the affective work produces harm rather than processing. The mechanism is that the affective work has been pushed past the client's capacity, so the task has exceeded the window of tolerance, and the client's response (flooding, dissociation, shutdown) is a protective reaction rather than productive processing. The false signal is the intensity of the affective engagement, which looks like productive EFT but actually indicates work past the client's capacity. The harm is flooding, dissociation, shutdown, re-traumatization for clients with trauma history, and the erosion of the alliance. The defense is to assess the client's capacity before proceeding, to consider the window of tolerance and trauma history, to build capacity if needed, to monitor the client's state during the task, and to recognize when a task is beyond current capacity.

### Treating Markers as Stable Rather Than Monitoring for Shifts

The therapist identifies a marker at the start of a session or from the previous session, because the marker was clear and the task was selected, and proceeds with the task throughout without monitoring for shifts, because the marker was identified and the plan was set, but the marker has shifted (the client moved from self-criticism to unfinished business, or the previous session's marker is no longer live), and the task no longer fits the current process. The mechanism is that the marker has been treated as stable, so the task has continued without checking whether the marker is still live, and the intervention is applied to a process that has moved. The false signal is the consistency of pursuing the identified task, which looks like focused work but actually indicates failure to track the live marker. The harm is tasks applied to non-live markers, wasted session time, and the client's sense that the therapist is not tracking their actual process. The defense is to monitor for marker shifts within and across sessions, to check what is live in the current moment, and to shift tasks as the marker shifts.

## Self-Check

- Did I recognize the major marker types (unfinished business, self-interruption, self-criticism, problematic reaction points, vague feeling, conflict splits) as they appeared in session?
- Did I distinguish similar-looking markers accurately, attending to the specific quality of each before selecting a task?
- Did I select the appropriate task based on the identified marker, and did I introduce the task only when the marker was genuinely live?
- Did I assess the client's capacity for the affective work the task requires, considering their window of tolerance and trauma history?
- Did I use the task to access and process the underlying emotion scheme, working toward the transformation of maladaptive core emotions?
- Did I monitor for marker shifts within and across sessions, and did I shift tasks as the marker shifted?
- Did I avoid missing markers due to content focus, misidentifying markers, applying tasks procedurally, pushing past capacity, and treating markers as stable?
- Did I attend to the moment-to-moment process rather than only the content, watching for the subtle and brief markers embedded in the narrative?
