# Externalization and Reauthoring

Externalization and reauthoring are the foundational practices of narrative therapy, and the judgment problem is that the problem-saturated story (the dominant narrative that defines the client by their problem) is so taken for granted by the client and often by the therapist that it is treated as reality rather than as one story among possible stories, and the work of creating distance from the problem and authoring alternative stories is done superficially or not at all. Externalization is the practice of separating the person from the problem through language and framing (the person is not the problem; the problem is the problem), which creates space for the client to relate to the problem rather than being defined by it. Reauthoring is the practice of developing alternative stories (alternative narratives that foreground the client's values, skills, intentions, and actions that the dominant problem-saturated story has obscured), which gives the client a different basis for identity and action. The therapist must externalize the problem consistently through language (naming the problem as an entity, mapping its influence, tracing its effects), must identify and thicken alternative stories (finding the unique outcomes and exceptions that contradict the dominant story), must co-author the alternative stories collaboratively (the client's stories, not the therapist's), and must avoid re-inscribing the problem-saturated story through carelessness. The deeper traps are using externalization as a technique without genuine adoption of the stance, failing to thicken thin alternative stories, imposing the therapist's preferred narrative, and lapsing back into internalizing language. The therapist's freedom is real, but externalization and reauthoring must be adopted as a consistent stance, not deployed as occasional techniques. Given the clinical stakes, this guidance is conservative: externalize the problem consistently, identify and thicken alternative stories collaboratively, use the person-is-not-the-problem language throughout, and never re-inscribe the problem-saturated story.

## Core Rules

### Externalize the Problem Consistently Through Language and Framing

Externalization separates the person from the problem through language, and it must be used consistently, not occasionally. The therapist should name the problem as an entity distinct from the person (what shall we call this thing, the anxiety, the critical voice, the temper), should use language that locates the problem outside the person (the anxiety is bothering you, rather than you are anxious), should map the problem's influence (how does the anxiety affect your work, your relationships, your sense of yourself), and should trace the person's influence on the problem (when do you push back against the anxiety, when does it lose ground). The consistency matters because lapsing back into internalizing language (you are depressed, you are anxious) re-inscribes the problem-saturated story and undermines the externalizing stance. Externalization is not a denial of suffering but a reframing that creates relational distance from the problem.

### Map the Influence of the Problem and the Person's Influence on It

Externalization includes mapping the bidirectional influence between the person and the problem: how the problem affects the person (its strategies, tactics, effects on the person's life and identity) and how the person affects the problem (when they resist it, when they limit it, when they act against it). The therapist should explore both directions in detail, should identify the problem's typical strategies and the moments when the person has resisted them, and should use the mapping to locate the person's agency within the relationship with the problem. The mapping makes the problem's influence visible (rather than totalizing and taken for granted) and makes the person's counter-influence visible (rather than invisible), and it sets up the identification of unique outcomes.

### Identify and Thicken Alternative Stories

The dominant problem-saturated story has obscured alternative stories, and the therapist must identify and thicken them. Identification begins with unique outcomes (moments when the problem's influence was reduced, resisted, or absent) and exceptions (times that contradict the dominant story). Thickening means developing these thin hints into rich, detailed, multi-stranded alternative stories: exploring the unique outcome in detail (what happened, what did you do, what does it mean), connecting it to the client's values and intentions (what does this tell us about what you care about), tracing its history (have there been other times like this), and linking it to other events that support the alternative story. A thin alternative story (a single exception noted and dropped) does not provide a viable alternative identity; a thick alternative story (multi-stranded, detailed, connected to values and history) can support a different basis for identity and action.

### Co-Author Alternative Stories Collaboratively

The alternative stories must be the client's, co-authored collaboratively, and the therapist must guard against imposing their own preferred narrative. The therapist should follow the client's experiences, values, and language, should ask questions that elaborate the client's alternative story rather than directing it, should use the client's own words and images, and should not introduce the therapist's vision of what the alternative story should be. The therapist provides the structure and the curiosity (the questions that elicit and thicken), the client provides the content (their experiences, values, and meanings). If the therapist's vision of the alternative story differs from the client's, the therapist should follow the client, because an imposed alternative story is just another dominant narrative replacing the first.

### Use Externalizing Language Throughout and Avoid Re-Inscribing the Problem Story

The externalizing stance must be maintained throughout, and the therapist must avoid lapsing back into internalizing language that re-inscribes the problem-saturated story. The therapist should monitor their own language (am I saying you are anxious or the anxiety is bothering you), should gently reframe the client's internalizing language (when the client says I am lazy, the therapist might ask what the laziness is doing), and should recognize that the dominant story is constantly reasserted by the client's own language and by the culture, and that the externalizing stance requires ongoing attention. Consistency is the discipline that makes externalization effective.

### Respect the Client's Relationship With the Problem and Avoid Premature Resolution

The client's relationship with the problem is complex, and the therapist must respect it rather than pushing for premature resolution. The problem may serve functions (protection, identity, familiarity), the client may be ambivalent about changing it, and the alternative story may be threatening because it requires a different identity. The therapist should explore the client's relationship with the problem, should respect the ambivalence, and should not push the alternative story faster than the client can own it. Externalization and reauthoring open possibilities; they do not impose solutions.

## Common Traps

### Using Externalization as a Technique Without Adopting the Stance

The therapist uses externalizing language in some moments, because the technique was learned and it seems useful, but lapses back into internalizing language in others, because the dominant problem-saturated story is the default and the internalizing frame is easier, and the inconsistency undermines the externalization, leaving the client confused about whether the problem is them or separate from them. The mechanism is that externalization has been used as a technique rather than adopted as a stance, so the externalizing language appears occasionally but the internalizing frame remains dominant, and the client receives mixed messages about the relationship between themselves and the problem. The false signal is the occasional use of externalizing language, which looks like narrative practice but actually indicates a technique without the underlying stance. The harm is confusion, the undermining of the externalizing frame, and the continued dominance of the problem-saturated story because the internalizing language reasserts it. The defense is to adopt externalization as a consistent stance, to monitor one's own language, to gently reframe the client's internalizing language, and to recognize that consistency is the discipline that makes externalization effective.

### Failing to Thicken Thin Alternative Stories

The therapist identifies a unique outcome (a moment when the problem's influence was reduced), because the exception question was asked and an exception was found, but notes it and moves on without thickening it, because the exception was identified and the work seems done, and the alternative story remains a thin hint that cannot support an alternative identity, and the dominant problem-saturated story reasserts itself because the alternative was never developed. The mechanism is that the alternative story has been left thin, so the unique outcome has been noted without the thickening that would make it a viable alternative narrative, and the dominant story reasserts itself because the thin alternative cannot compete with it. The false signal is the identification of the unique outcome, which looks like narrative practice but actually indicates an exception noted without development. The harm is the reassertion of the problem-saturated story, the missed opportunity to build an alternative identity, and the client's continued definition by the problem. The defense is to thicken alternative stories by exploring unique outcomes in detail, connecting them to values and intentions, tracing their history, and linking them to other supporting events, producing a multi-stranded alternative narrative.

### Imposing the Therapist's Preferred Narrative

The therapist, recognizing an alternative story direction that seems healthy and positive, shapes the reauthoring toward the therapist's preferred narrative, because the therapist knows what a good outcome looks like and the client's alternative story seems incomplete, and the client adjusts toward the therapist's direction, producing an alternative story that is the therapist's vision dressed in the client's words, which the client does not own and will not inhabit. The mechanism is that the therapist's preferred narrative has been substituted for the client's, so the co-authoring has become covert direction, and the alternative story reflects the therapist's values rather than the client's. The false signal is the apparent collaboration, which looks like narrative practice but actually indicates therapist direction. The harm is an alternative story the client does not own, the undermining of client agency, and the replacement of one imposed narrative (the problem story) with another (the therapist's story). The defense is to follow the client's experiences, values, and language, to ask questions that elaborate rather than direct, and to recognize that an imposed alternative story is just another dominant narrative.

### Lapsing Back Into Internalizing Language That Re-Inscribes the Problem

The therapist, despite intending to maintain the externalizing stance, lapses back into internalizing language (you are depressed, you are anxious, you are avoidant), because the internalizing frame is the cultural default and it is easier, and each lapse re-inscribes the problem-saturated story, telling the client that they are the problem, and the externalizing work is undermined by the inconsistency. The mechanism is that the internalizing language has been used without monitoring, so the dominant story has been reasserted through the therapist's own speech, and the externalizing frame is eroded by the lapses. The false signal is the therapist's intention to externalize, which feels like the stance but is undermined by the actual language used. The harm is the re-inscription of the problem-saturated story, the confusion of mixed messages, and the undermining of the externalizing work. The defense is to monitor one's own language consistently, to reframe the client's internalizing language gently, and to recognize that the dominant story is constantly reasserted and requires ongoing resistance.

### Pushing the Alternative Story Faster Than the Client Can Own It

The therapist, excited by the alternative story direction and wanting the client to inhabit it, pushes the reauthoring faster than the client can own it, because the alternative story is positive and the therapist wants the client to claim it, but the client's relationship with the problem is complex and the alternative identity is threatening because it requires a different sense of self, and the push produces resistance or compliant performance rather than genuine ownership. The mechanism is that the reauthoring has been paced too fast, so the alternative story has been pushed before the client can integrate it, and the client responds with resistance or performance rather than ownership. The false signal is the positive direction of the alternative story, which looks like productive reauthoring but actually indicates pacing that exceeds the client's readiness. The harm is resistance, compliant performance of a story the client does not own, and the undermining of the genuine reauthoring that slower pacing could support. The defense is to respect the client's relationship with the problem, to explore the ambivalence, to pace the reauthoring to the client's readiness, and to recognize that externalization and reauthoring open possibilities rather than impose solutions.

- Did I monitor my own language and avoid lapsing back into internalizing language that re-inscribes the problem-saturated story?
- Did I respect the client's relationship with the problem and avoid pushing the alternative story faster than the client could own it?
