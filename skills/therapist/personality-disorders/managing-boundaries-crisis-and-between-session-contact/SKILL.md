# Managing Boundaries, Crisis, And Between-Session Contact

Clients with personality pathology, particularly borderline, stress the frame of treatment in characteristic ways: frequent between-session contact, crises that seem to arise just after sessions or before breaks, threats to drop out when the work gets hard, boundary tests that escalate when limits are set, and patterns of idealization and devaluation that target the therapist's sense of competence and care. The therapist's central judgment is to build a frame that is consistent and humane, to respond to crises within the frame rather than abandoning it, to set limits that protect the treatment without being punitive, and to recognize that the testing of the frame is itself the pathology expressing itself, not a problem to be eliminated. The recurring failures are abandoning the frame in response to crisis, setting limits punitively, rescuing in ways that reinforce the crisis behavior, and personalizing the client's frame-testing as a statement about the therapist.

Use this skill when managing the treatment frame, between-session contact, crises, boundary tests, dropout threats, or idealization and deactivation with personality pathology.

## Core Rules

### Build A Frame That Is Consistent, Predictable, And Humane

The frame for personality pathology must be consistent, predictable, and humane, because the client's internal world is inconsistent and unpredictable, and the frame provides the external structure the client cannot generate internally. Establish the frame explicitly at the outset: session frequency, length, start and end times, the policy for between-session contact, the procedure for crises, the missed-session policy, the boundaries of the therapist's role, and the conditions for higher levels of care. Discuss the frame as part of the treatment, not as bureaucracy, and explain that its consistency is therapeutic. Return to the frame when it is tested, because it will be, and use the testing as material for the work rather than as a problem to suppress.

### Define Between-Session Contact Clearly And Use It Strategically

Between-session contact is where the frame is most often tested and most often breaks down. Define the policy clearly: what contact is for, how the client should reach you, what the response time is, what constitutes an emergency requiring emergency services rather than the therapist, and what is not the purpose of contact, such as ongoing therapy by text. Within evidence-based models, between-session contact has specific roles: in DBT, phone coaching is a structured intervention for skills generalization; in other models, contact may be more limited. Use the contact strategically, as part of the model, rather than reactively. Unlimited, unstructured contact reinforces the crisis pattern; no contact at all leaves the client without support between sessions. Define the policy and use it consistently.

### Respond To Crises Within The Frame, Not By Abandoning It

Crises will occur, and the temptation is to abandon the frame to address them: extending sessions, adding sessions on demand, being available at all hours, rescuing the client from consequences. This is often countertherapeutic, because it reinforces the crisis as the route to care and prevents the client from developing the skills to manage distress within the frame. Respond to crises within the frame: use the established procedures, apply the skills the treatment is teaching, involve emergency services when safety requires it, and process the crisis in the next session. Distinguish genuine emergencies, which require escalation, from therapeutic crises, which require the frame to hold. Abandoning the frame teaches the client that only crisis produces care; holding the frame teaches that care is available without crisis.

### Set Limits That Protect The Treatment Without Being Punitive

Limits are necessary and therapeutic when they protect the treatment and the client; they are harmful when they are punitive, retaliatory, or driven by the therapist's frustration. Set limits proactively, as part of the frame, rather than reactively, in response to a violation, because reactive limits are often experienced as punishment. When a limit is violated, address it directly, explore what happened, reaffirm the limit, and use the violation as material for understanding the client's patterns. Explain the clinical rationale for limits, so they are understood as therapeutic rather than arbitrary. A limit such as "I cannot respond to non-urgent contact after hours, and here is what to do instead" is therapeutic; a limit such as "if you contact me again I will terminate" is punitive and often harmful.

### Distinguish Chronic Self-Harm From Acute Suicide Risk

Personality pathology, particularly borderline, often involves chronic self-harm that serves an affective regulatory function, and also carries elevated suicide risk, and the two must be distinguished because the responses differ. Chronic self-harm, low-lethality cutting, burning, or hitting that the client uses to regulate affect, is addressed within the treatment through skills, functional understanding, and harm reduction, not treated as a series of emergencies. Acute suicide risk, elevated ideation with plan, intent, or preparatory behavior, requires escalation, safety planning, means restriction, and possibly higher levels of care. Conflating the two leads either to chronic emergency-room visits that do not help or to missed acute risk. Assess each episode for its function and lethality, and respond accordingly.

### Recognize And Work With Idealization And Devaluation

Idealization and devaluation are characteristic of several personality pathologies and reflect the difficulty tolerating ambivalence, the splitting of good and bad, and the terror of depending on a flawed other. Recognize the pattern: the client who idealizes you as the first therapist who ever understood them may later devalue you as incompetent or harmful, often in response to a limit, a break, or a rupture. Do not personalize either the idealization or the devaluation; both are expressions of the pathology, not accurate assessments of you. Use the pattern as material: examine it as it occurs, mentalize the interaction, and help the client develop the capacity to hold ambivalence. Stay consistent through both phases, because the consistency itself is therapeutic.

### Use Consultation And Supervision To Maintain The Frame

Personality pathology reliably stirs the therapist, and maintaining the frame in the face of intense testing requires consultation and supervision. Use consultation to recognize enactments, to calibrate limits, to manage the countertransference that arises with idealization, devaluation, and crisis, and to sustain the consistency the treatment requires. A therapist treating personality pathology without consultation is at risk of enactments, burnout, and frame breakdown. Consultation is not optional; it is part of the standard of care for this work. Document significant consultations and how they informed the plan.

## Common Traps

### Abandoning The Frame In Response To Crisis

A therapist, faced with a client in acute distress between sessions, may abandon the frame, extending sessions, adding contact, or rescuing the client from consequences, believing that flexibility in crisis builds trust, when it actually reinforces the crisis as the route to care. The mechanism of harm is that the abandonment of the frame teaches the client that only escalation produces responsiveness, and the crisis pattern intensifies because it works. The harm is that the client becomes more dysregulated, the treatment becomes chaotic, and the therapist burns out, because the frame that would have held the work was dismantled by the crisis. Holding the frame, using the established procedures, and processing the crisis in session is what produces change.

### Setting Limits Punitively Rather Than Therapeutically

A therapist, frustrated by repeated boundary violations, may set limits reactively and punitively, threatening termination or withdrawing responsiveness as punishment, when the client experiences this as the abandonment they have always expected. The mechanism of harm is that the punitive limit confirms the client's core fear, ruptures the alliance, and often escalates the very behavior the limit was meant to stop, because the client reacts to perceived abandonment with increased dysregulation. The harm is that the treatment ruptures, the client drops out or worsens, and the therapist's frustration, not the client's needs, drove the intervention. Setting limits proactively, with clinical rationale, and using violations as material rather than punishment, protects the alliance.

### Rescuing In Ways That Reinforce The Crisis Behavior

A therapist, moved by a client's distress and wanting to help, may rescue repeatedly, solving the client's problems, extending the frame, or intervening in the client's life, when the rescue reinforces the helplessness and the crisis pattern that maintains the pathology. The mechanism of harm is that the rescue teaches the client that they cannot manage without the therapist's intervention, prevents the development of skills, and intensifies the dependence that the treatment should be reducing. The harm is that the client becomes more reliant on crisis and rescue, the treatment produces no growth in capacity, and the therapist becomes increasingly responsible for the client's functioning. Supporting the client in managing their own distress, within the frame, builds the capacity the pathology has arrested.

### Personalizing Idealization And Devaluation

A therapist may personalize the client's idealization, believing they are uniquely skilled, and then be devastated when the devaluation arrives, or may personalize the devaluation, believing they have failed or harmed the client, when both are expressions of the pathology rather than accurate assessments of the therapist. The mechanism of harm is that the personalization destabilizes the therapist, who then responds from their own need, rescuing to restore the idealization or withdrawing to escape the devaluation, rather than from the client's needs. The harm is that the therapy becomes about the therapist's state, the frame becomes inconsistent, and the client's pathology is reinforced rather than examined. Recognizing both idealization and devaluation as data, and using consultation to maintain perspective, sustains the work.

### Conflating Chronic Self-Harm With Acute Suicide Risk

A therapist may treat every self-harm episode as an emergency requiring escalation, hospitalizing a client for chronic low-lethality cutting that serves an affective function, or may minimize genuine acute suicide risk by assuming it is "just" the chronic pattern. The mechanism of harm is that the conflation leads to the wrong response: chronic emergency visits that disrupt the treatment and reinforce the behavior, or missed acute risk that ends in suicide. The harm is that the chronic client is hospitalized repeatedly without benefit and the acute risk is not addressed. Assessing each episode for function and lethality, and responding with the appropriate level of care, prevents both errors.
