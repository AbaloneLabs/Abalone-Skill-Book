# Hospitalization And Higher Level Of Care Decisions

The decision to move a client to a higher level of care is one of the gravest in outpatient therapy, and it is fraught because the right decision protects the client and the wrong one, in either direction, causes harm. Hospitalize too readily and you disrupt the client's life, stigmatize them, expose them to the risks of inpatient settings, and damage the alliance; hospitalize too reluctantly and a client who needed inpatient protection deteriorates or dies. Between routine outpatient and inpatient there is a spectrum, emergency evaluation, partial hospitalization, intensive outpatient, that the therapist must navigate. The recurring failures are delaying hospitalization because it feels drastic, hospitalizing to relieve the therapist's anxiety rather than the client's need, failing to use intermediate levels of care, and abandoning the relationship during the transition.

Use this skill when a client's needs may exceed outpatient management, when arranging hospitalization, when coordinating with higher levels of care, or when managing the return from hospitalization.

## Core Rules

### Match The Level Of Care To The Clinical Need

The level of care should fit the clinical need, and there is a spectrum to choose from, not only outpatient or inpatient:

- routine outpatient, for stable clients managing their conditions;
- intensified outpatient, more frequent sessions, for clients whose risk or symptoms are rising but who remain safe;
- intensive outpatient programs, several hours several days a week, for clients needing more structure than weekly therapy;
- partial hospitalization, full days, five days a week, for clients needing daily structure and monitoring but not 24-hour care;
- inpatient hospitalization, 24-hour care, for clients who cannot be kept safe or stabilized at a lower level.

Choose the least restrictive level that can safely meet the need, recognizing that choosing too low a level leaves the client unsafe and choosing too high a level imposes unnecessary cost and disruption.

### Use Emergency Evaluation When Acute Risk Is Present

When a client is acutely at risk, current suicidal intent with plan and means, acute psychosis with dangerous content, inability to maintain basic safety, the appropriate response is emergency psychiatric evaluation, not an intensified outpatient plan. Emergency evaluation can occur through an emergency department, a mobile crisis team, or a crisis stabilization unit, depending on what is available. Arrange the evaluation, do not send the client to arrange it themselves if they are at acute risk, and do not leave them alone while it is arranged.

### Pursue Voluntary Admission When The Client Can Agree

Voluntary hospitalization is preferable to involuntary when the client can agree, because it preserves autonomy, is less traumatic, and often leads to better engagement with treatment. When the client recognizes the need and can consent, pursue voluntary admission: explain why hospitalization is indicated, what it will involve, and arrange the admission through the emergency department or directly with a facility. A client who agrees to voluntary admission may still need accompaniment and support through the process.

### Use Involuntary Hold When The Criteria Are Met And The Client Cannot Agree

When the client meets the criteria for danger to self or others, or grave disability, and cannot or will not agree to voluntary admission, involuntary hold is indicated. Know the specific legal criteria and process in your jurisdiction, including who can initiate the hold, the duration, and the review process. Involuntary hospitalization is a serious intervention that should follow the law precisely, be documented carefully, and be accompanied by honest communication with the client about what is happening and why. The alliance may be damaged, but the duty to protect is not contingent on the client's agreement.

### Coordinate With The Receiving Facility And Maintain The Relationship

Hospitalization is not a handoff; the outpatient therapist remains the client's therapist and should coordinate with the inpatient team. With the client's consent, share the relevant clinical information, the formulation, the current risk picture, and the recent course. Maintain contact during the hospitalization where possible, and arrange the return to outpatient care before discharge, so the client experiences continuity rather than abandonment. The transition out of the hospital is a high-risk period, and the outpatient therapist's involvement reduces that risk.

### Plan The Transition Back To Outpatient Care

The period immediately after hospitalization is one of elevated risk, and the return to outpatient care must be planned, not assumed. Before discharge, arrange a prompt outpatient appointment, ideally within a few days, coordinate with the prescriber about medication, confirm the safety plan and means restriction, and identify the supports and the warning signs. Intensify outpatient care initially, then step down as the client stabilizes. A client discharged to routine outpatient care without a structured transition is at elevated risk in the gap.

### Address The Client's Experience Of Hospitalization

Hospitalization is often frightening, stigmatizing, and sometimes traumatic for the client, and the experience, positive or negative, becomes part of the treatment. After discharge, process the experience: what helped, what did not, what the client learned, how they feel about having been hospitalized, and what they need going forward. A client who feels heard about their hospitalization is more engaged in the subsequent care; a client whose experience is not addressed may disengage.

## Common Traps

### Delaying Hospitalization Because It Feels Drastic

When a client meets the criteria for hospitalization, the therapist may delay, hoping that intensified outpatient care will suffice, because hospitalization feels drastic, disruptive, and damaging to the alliance. The mechanism is that the costs of hospitalization are visible and immediate, while the cost of not hospitalizing, the risk of an adverse outcome, is probabilistic and easier to minimize. The harm is that a client who needed inpatient protection remains at acute risk during the delay, sometimes with fatal outcome. The decision rests on the assessed risk; when the risk meets the threshold, hospitalization is indicated regardless of its disruption, and delaying to avoid the disruption is a failure of the duty to protect.

### Hospitalizing To Relieve The Therapist's Anxiety

The opposite error is hospitalizing a client whose risk does not meet the threshold, because the therapist is anxious and hospitalization relieves the therapist's distress, even when the client could be managed outpatient. The mechanism is that the therapist's anxiety, in the face of elevated but manageable risk, drives a decision that serves the therapist's affect regulation rather than the client's need. The harm is unnecessary hospitalization that disrupts the client's life, exposes them to the risks of inpatient settings, stigmatizes them, and damages the alliance. The decision rests on the assessed risk, not on the therapist's comfort; when the risk does not meet the threshold, outpatient management with a safety plan is the appropriate response, and the therapist's anxiety is managed through consultation, not through over-hospitalization.

### Failing To Use Intermediate Levels Of Care

When a client needs more than routine outpatient but does not meet inpatient criteria, the therapist may default to one or the other, either continuing inadequate outpatient care or hospitalizing unnecessarily, missing the intermediate options. The mechanism is that intermediate levels, partial hospitalization and intensive outpatient, are less familiar and require referral and coordination, so the binary of outpatient or inpatient feels simpler. The harm is that clients who would benefit from structured day programs either receive too little care or too much, with the disruption and cost of unnecessary hospitalization. Knowing the local resources and using the intermediate levels is what allows the care to fit the need.

### Abandoning The Relationship During Hospitalization

When a client is hospitalized, the outpatient therapist may step back, treating the inpatient team as taking over, and fail to coordinate or maintain contact, so the client experiences the hospitalization as abandonment by the therapist they trusted. The mechanism is that the inpatient team is now responsible, so the outpatient therapist's role feels redundant, and the coordination feels like extra work. The harm is a loss of continuity at a vulnerable moment, a client who feels dropped, and a higher-risk transition back to outpatient care. The outpatient therapist remains the client's therapist during hospitalization, coordinates with the inpatient team, and arranges the return, so the client experiences continuity.

### No Structured Transition Back To Outpatient

A client discharged from the hospital to routine outpatient care, without a prompt appointment, coordinated medication, or updated safety plan, is at elevated risk in the gap between discharge and the next outpatient contact. The mechanism is that discharge is treated as the end of the crisis, so the follow-up is assumed rather than arranged. The harm is that the highest-risk period, the days after discharge, is managed at routine intensity, and recurrence or deterioration is missed. A structured transition, prompt appointment, coordination, updated safety plan, and intensified initial follow-up, is what reduces the post-discharge risk.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I match the level of care to the clinical need, choosing the least restrictive level that can safely meet it?
- [ ] Did I use emergency evaluation when acute risk was present, rather than managing as outpatient?
- [ ] Did I pursue voluntary admission when the client could agree, preserving autonomy?
- [ ] When involuntary hold was indicated, did I follow the legal criteria and process precisely, and communicate honestly with the client?
- [ ] Did I coordinate with the receiving facility, sharing relevant clinical information with consent?
- [ ] Did I maintain the therapeutic relationship during hospitalization, rather than treating it as a handoff?
- [ ] Did I plan a structured transition back to outpatient care, with a prompt appointment, coordination, and updated safety plan?
- [ ] Did I process the client's experience of hospitalization after discharge?
- [ ] Did I avoid delaying needed hospitalization, avoid hospitalizing to relieve my own anxiety, and use intermediate levels of care where appropriate?
