# Safety Planning And Means Restriction

A safety plan is the single most practical tool a therapist has for managing suicide risk between sessions, and it is consistently confused with two things it is not: a no-harm contract, which has no demonstrated efficacy, and a generic handout, which the client does not own and will not use. A genuine safety plan is collaboratively written, specific to the client, includes means restriction as a core component, and is reviewed and updated as risk changes. The therapist's judgment is to build the plan collaboratively, to address means access concretely even when the conversation is uncomfortable, to involve supports where possible, and to treat the plan as a living tool rather than a one-time document.

Use this skill when building a safety plan, when arranging means restriction, when a client's risk level changes, or when reviewing an existing plan.

## Core Rules

### Build The Plan Collaboratively, Not As A Handout

A safety plan the therapist writes and hands to the client is not owned by the client and will not be used in crisis. Build the plan collaboratively: elicit the client's own warning signs, their own coping strategies, their own supports, and their own words. The client's authorship is what makes the plan usable, because in a crisis the client will reach for what is theirs, not for what was given to them. Write the plan with the client, give them a copy, and review it together.

### Include Each Component, In The Order The Client Will Use Them

The standard safety plan structure, adapted from the Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention, includes components in the order a client would use them during escalating distress:

- warning signs, the thoughts, images, moods, situations, or behaviors that signal a crisis is developing;
- internal coping strategies, things the client can do alone to distract or regulate, such as exercise, music, or breathing;
- people and social settings that provide distraction or support, with names;
- people the client can ask for help, with names and numbers;
- professionals and agencies the client can contact, including the therapist's number, a crisis line, and emergency numbers;
- means restriction, the specific steps to make the environment safer.

Each component should be specific, with names, numbers, and concrete actions, not generic placeholders.

### Address Means Restriction Concretely And Specifically

Means restriction is not a separate intervention; it is part of the safety plan, and it is one of the most effective. The plan should specify, for the means relevant to this client's risk:

- firearms: who will hold or store them off-site during the risk period, with a specific person and timeframe, not a vague assurance of "secured";
- medications: working with prescribers and family to limit quantities, using lockboxes, and removing stockpiles;
- other means specific to the client's plan or history.

Do not accept "they're locked up" without specifics. A firearm in a safe the client can open is not restricted. A medication supply the client controls is not restricted. Means restriction requires that the client does not have ready access during the risk period.

### Involve Supports With Consent Where Possible

A safety plan is stronger when supports are involved. With the client's consent, identify one or more family members, friends, or partners who can be part of the plan, who can hold means, check in, or be called during escalation. Involve them with the client's knowledge and agreement, share the relevant parts of the plan, and clarify their role. For some clients, no safe support is available, and the plan relies on professionals and crisis lines; for others, supports are central.

### Match Monitoring To The Risk Level

The safety plan includes the level of monitoring between sessions, matched to risk: more frequent sessions, check-in calls if within your frame, coordination with a prescriber or case manager, or a higher level of care such as intensive outpatient or partial hospitalization. The monitoring should be concrete and agreed, not aspirational.

### Review And Update The Plan As Risk Changes

A safety plan written once and never reviewed becomes stale. Review the plan at each session while risk is elevated, update it as circumstances change, add or remove supports, adjust means restriction, and confirm the client still has the copy and can use it. After a crisis, review what was used, what worked, what did not, and revise. A living plan is what helps; a static document does not.

### Give The Plan To The Client In A Usable Form

The plan does no good in the chart; it must be with the client in a crisis. Give the client a physical copy, encourage them to photograph it, save it in their phone, or keep it where they will find it. Discuss where they will keep it and how they will access it in crisis. A plan the client cannot find in crisis is not a plan.

## Common Traps

### The Handout Plan The Client Does Not Own

When the therapist hands the client a pre-printed safety plan, or writes one and gives it to the client without collaboration, the plan is the therapist's document, not the client's, and the client is unlikely to use it in crisis. The mechanism is that the pre-written plan satisfies the documentation requirement efficiently, so it reads as good practice, when it actually bypasses the collaboration that makes the plan usable. The harm is that the client, in crisis, does not reach for a document they did not help create, and the plan fails at the moment it was needed. Collaborative authorship, using the client's own words, warning signs, and supports, is what makes the plan usable.

### Means Restriction Treated As A Checkbox

When the safety plan includes a line about means restriction that is never concretely addressed, "client will secure firearms," with no specific person, timeframe, or verification, the means access remains unchanged and the risk is unmodified. The mechanism is that the conversation about firearms and medications is uncomfortable, and the therapist, wanting to complete the plan, accepts a vague entry that documents the topic without accomplishing the restriction. The harm is that the most modifiable risk factor, means access, is left in place during the highest-risk period, and the plan provides false reassurance. Asking specifically, naming a responsible person and timeframe, and verifying the arrangement, is what makes means restriction real.

### The Static Plan Never Reviewed

A safety plan written at intake and never reviewed becomes increasingly irrelevant as the client's circumstances, supports, and risk level change, but it continues to sit in the chart as if it were current. The mechanism is that the plan satisfies the requirement once written, so it is treated as complete, while the client's actual situation diverges from it. The harm is that the plan the client would reach for in crisis no longer matches their life, the supports listed may no longer be available, and the means restriction may have lapsed. Reviewing and updating the plan at each session while risk is elevated, and after any crisis, is what keeps it usable.

### Relying On The Therapist's Availability Instead Of A Plan

A safety plan that amounts to "call me if you need to" places the entire burden on the therapist's availability, which is not continuous, and provides no path for the hours the therapist cannot be reached. The mechanism is that the offer feels caring and responsive, so it reads as a plan, when it actually provides no structure for the times the therapist is unavailable. The harm is that the client, in crisis at a time the therapist cannot be reached, has no other path and no plan to follow. A genuine plan includes crisis lines, emergency numbers, supports, and coping steps the client can use without the therapist, with the therapist as one contact among several.

### No Follow-Up On Whether The Plan Was Used

After a client experiences an escalation, the therapist may not review whether the safety plan was used, what worked, and what did not, treating the plan as written rather than as tested. The mechanism is that the plan's existence is treated as sufficient, so its actual use is not examined. The harm is that a plan that did not work, because a support was unreachable or a coping step was ineffective, continues unchanged, and the next crisis finds the client with the same inadequate plan. Reviewing the plan's use after any escalation, and revising based on what was learned, is what makes the plan improve over time.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I build the plan collaboratively, using the client's own warning signs, coping strategies, supports, and words?
- [ ] Does the plan include each component in order: warning signs, internal coping, people and settings, people to ask for help, professionals and agencies, and means restriction?
- [ ] Did I address means restriction concretely, with specific persons, timeframes, and verification, especially for firearms and medications?
- [ ] Did I involve supports with the client's consent, and clarify their role in the plan?
- [ ] Does the plan include a level of monitoring matched to the risk, with concrete and agreed arrangements?
- [ ] Did I review and update the plan at each session while risk is elevated, and after any crisis?
- [ ] Did I give the client a usable copy and discuss where they will keep and access it in crisis?
- [ ] Did I avoid relying on my availability alone, and avoid treating means restriction as a checkbox?
