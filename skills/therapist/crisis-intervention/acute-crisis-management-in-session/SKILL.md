# Acute Crisis Management In Session

A client who becomes acutely destabilized during a session, suicidal, panicked, dissociating, psychotic, or agitated, requires the therapist to shift immediately from the usual therapeutic posture to a crisis management posture, and the shift is disorienting precisely because the usual frame has just been operating. The therapist's judgment is to recognize the crisis early, to de-escalate and stabilize before making decisions, to assess whether the client can be managed outpatient or needs emergency care, and to manage the aftermath, including the client's experience of the crisis and the therapy's continuation. The recurring failures are missing the escalation until it is severe, trying to process the crisis instead of managing it, sending an unsafe client home to avoid hospitalization, and failing to follow up adequately after the crisis.

Use this skill when a client destabilizes during a session, when managing acute distress, psychosis, panic, or suicidality in the room, or when deciding on emergency referral.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Escalation Early And Shift Posture

Crises rarely appear without warning; the signs, rising agitation, shifting affect, dissociation cues, escalating ideation, disorganized speech, are usually visible before the crisis is full. Recognize the signs early and shift from the therapeutic posture to the crisis posture: slow your pace, lower your voice, simplify your language, reduce stimulation, and focus on the immediate present. The shift is not abandoning the therapy; it is protecting the client during an acute state.

### Stabilize Before You Assess Or Decide

A client in acute crisis cannot participate reliably in assessment or planning. Before asking what happened or what to do, stabilize: help the client regulate their affect, ground them if they are dissociating, reduce the intensity of the stimulation, and restore enough calm for the client to engage. Stabilization techniques include grounding, slow breathing, orienting to the room, and simple, calming presence. Do not attempt to process the crisis material while the client is acutely dysregulated; that can worsen the state.

### Assess Safety Once The Client Can Engage

Once the client is stable enough to engage, assess the immediate safety questions: current suicidal or homicidal ideation, intent, plan, means access; current psychotic symptoms, especially command hallucinations; current intoxication or medical instability; the presence of supports who can be with the client. The assessment determines whether the client can be managed with an outpatient plan or needs emergency evaluation. Do not skip the assessment because the client seems calmer; the calm may be temporary.

### Decide Outpatient Management Versus Emergency Care Based On Risk

The decision to send a client home versus to arrange emergency evaluation rests on the assessed risk, not on the client's or the therapist's preference. If the client has current intent to act, access to means, recent preparatory behavior, acute psychosis with dangerous content, or cannot contract to a workable safety plan, emergency evaluation is indicated. If the client is stable, has released the acute intent, has supports and means restriction, and can engage a safety plan, outpatient management with intensified follow-up may be appropriate. When uncertain, err toward emergency evaluation and consult.

### Do Not Leave An Unsafe Client Alone

If the client is at acute risk and emergency evaluation is being arranged, do not leave the client alone. Stay with them, or arrange for a responsible support person to stay, until the evaluation occurs or the risk has demonstrably reduced. Leaving an acutely suicidal client alone in a waiting room or sending them to an emergency department unaccompanied is a failure of the duty to protect.

### Build A Concrete Safety Plan Before The Client Leaves

If the client is to be managed outpatient, build a concrete, written safety plan before they leave the session: warning signs, coping steps, supports to contact, professional contacts including crisis lines, means restriction steps, and the next appointment. Give the plan to the client, review it, and confirm the client can use it. A verbal "call me if you need to" is not a safety plan.

### Arrange Intensified Follow-Up After A Crisis

A client who has been in crisis needs intensified follow-up in the immediate aftermath: a next appointment within a day or two, a check-in call if appropriate within your frame, coordination with supports and prescribers, and a plan for what to do if the crisis recurs. Do not return to the pre-crisis schedule as if nothing happened; the period immediately after a crisis is a period of elevated risk.

### Process The Crisis Therapeutically Once Stable

Once the acute crisis has passed and the client is stable, the crisis becomes therapeutic material: what triggered it, what the warning signs were, what helped and what did not, what the client needs to reduce the likelihood of recurrence. This processing, done later and when the client can engage, turns the crisis into learning that strengthens the treatment. Do not process the crisis while it is acute.

## Common Traps

### Trying To Process The Crisis Instead Of Managing It

A therapist who continues the therapeutic posture during an acute crisis, exploring the feelings, interpreting the dynamics, or working through the material, may worsen the crisis by increasing the intensity when the client needs reduction. The mechanism is that the therapeutic frame is the therapist's default, so the reflex in the face of distress is to go deeper into it, when the crisis requires the opposite. The harm is that the dysregulated client becomes more dysregulated, the assessment is delayed, and the risk increases. The corrective is to recognize the crisis, shift to stabilization, and defer processing until the client is stable.

### Sending An Unsafe Client Home To Avoid Hospitalization

When a client is at acute risk but the therapist, the client, or both want to avoid emergency evaluation or hospitalization, the therapist may send the client home with a safety plan that is inadequate to the risk. The mechanism is that hospitalization feels drastic, disruptive, and damaging to the alliance, so the therapist, under pressure, manages as outpatient what should be managed as emergency. The harm is that an acutely at-risk client leaves the session without the level of care the risk requires, and the outcome can be fatal. The decision rests on assessed risk, not on preference; when risk is high, emergency evaluation is indicated regardless of the disruption.

### Missing Escalation Until It Is Severe

A therapist focused on the session's content may miss the signs of escalation, rising agitation, shifting affect, dissociation, until the crisis is severe and harder to manage. The mechanism is that the signs are subtle and the therapist, absorbed in the material, does not track the client's state as a separate stream. The harm is that the crisis is harder to manage when it is severe, the stabilization takes longer, and the risk is higher. Tracking the client's state throughout the session, and shifting posture at the first signs of escalation, is what allows early management.

### Inadequate Follow-Up After The Crisis

A client who has been stabilized and sent home with a safety plan may not be followed up adequately, with the therapy returning to its pre-crisis pace as if the crisis were resolved. The mechanism is that the crisis feels handled once the client leaves the session stable, so the elevated risk of the aftermath is not recognized. The harm is that the period of highest risk, the days after a crisis, is managed at routine intensity, and recurrence is missed. Intensified follow-up, a next appointment within a day or two, coordination, and a clear plan for recurrence, is what the aftermath requires.

### Leaving The Client Alone During Arrangement

When emergency evaluation is being arranged, the therapist may step out to make calls, leaving the acutely at-risk client alone in the office or waiting room. The mechanism is that the arrangement requires the therapist's attention, and the client, seeming calmer, appears safe to leave. The harm is that the calm may be temporary, and an unattended client at acute risk can act in the brief window of being alone. Staying with the client, or arranging a responsible support person to stay, until the evaluation occurs, is the standard of care.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I recognize the signs of escalation early and shift to a crisis posture before the crisis became severe?
- [ ] Did I stabilize the client, through grounding and affect regulation, before assessing or deciding?
- [ ] Once the client could engage, did I assess current ideation, intent, plan, means, psychosis, intoxication, and supports?
- [ ] Did I decide outpatient management versus emergency care based on assessed risk, not on preference, erring toward evaluation when uncertain?
- [ ] Did I avoid leaving an unsafe client alone, staying or arranging a support person until evaluation occurred?
- [ ] If outpatient, did I build a concrete, written safety plan with means restriction before the client left?
- [ ] Did I arrange intensified follow-up, a next appointment within a day or two, coordination, and a recurrence plan?
- [ ] Did I defer processing the crisis until the client was stable, and then use it as therapeutic material?
