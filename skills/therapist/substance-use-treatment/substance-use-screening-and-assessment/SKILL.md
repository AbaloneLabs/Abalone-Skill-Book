# Substance Use Screening And Assessment

Substance use is common among therapy clients, frequently co-occurring with mental health conditions, and often underdetected when therapists do not screen systematically. The therapist's judgment is to screen every client for substance use, not only those who present with addiction, to assess the severity and impact using validated tools and clinical interview, to determine the appropriate level of care based on the severity and the client's circumstances, and to recognize when integrated therapy is sufficient and when specialized addiction treatment is needed. The recurring failures are not screening because the client does not "look like" an addict, accepting the client's minimization without exploration, underestimating severity and the need for specialized care, and missing co-occurring mental health and substance use interactions. Substance use assessment is not a specialty add-on; it is a core competency for therapists.

Use this skill when screening or assessing substance use, determining severity or level of care, or deciding on integrated versus specialized treatment.

## Core Rules

### Screen Every Client, Not Only Those Who Present With Addiction

Screen every therapy client for substance use, regardless of the presenting concern, because substance use is common, often co-occurring, and frequently undisclosed unless directly asked. Use a validated screening tool, such as the AUDIT for alcohol or the DAST for drugs, supplemented by clinical questions about frequency, quantity, and impact. Normalize the screening as part of the standard assessment, so the client does not feel singled out. Screening is brief, non-judgmental, and universal, and it detects substance use that would otherwise be missed until it becomes a crisis. Do not rely on the client to raise substance use, because shame, minimization, or lack of concern often prevent disclosure.

### Assess Severity And Impact Using Validated Tools And Clinical Interview

When screening indicates substance use, assess the severity and impact using validated tools and a thorough clinical interview. Assess the pattern of use, frequency, quantity, route, and consequences; the impact on the client's functioning, relationships, health, and mental health; the presence of tolerance, withdrawal, and loss of control; and the client's perception of the use and their readiness to change. Use the assessment to determine whether the use meets criteria for a substance use disorder and, if so, the severity, mild, moderate, or severe. The assessment guides the treatment, and a thorough assessment is the foundation of sound care.

### Determine The Appropriate Level Of Care Based On Severity And Circumstances

Substance use disorders span a range of severity, and the appropriate level of care ranges from outpatient therapy to medically managed inpatient treatment, determined by the severity, the client's medical and psychological status, their support system, and their history. Use the ASAM criteria or a similar framework to assess the needed level of care, considering intoxication and withdrawal risk, medical and psychiatric conditions, readiness to change, relapse potential, and recovery environment. Clients with severe use, medical complications, co-occurring conditions, or unstable environments may need intensive outpatient, partial hospitalization, residential, or inpatient care. Do not assume outpatient therapy is sufficient; match the level of care to the severity.

### Recognize When Specialized Addiction Treatment Is Needed

Some clients need specialized addiction treatment, in addition to or instead of general therapy: clients with severe substance use disorders, those who have not responded to outpatient treatment, those with complex medical or psychiatric needs, or those who need the structure and peer support of specialized programs. Recognize when the client's needs exceed what integrated outpatient therapy can provide, and refer to specialized treatment, including medication-assisted treatment, which is effective for opioid and alcohol use disorders and which many therapists cannot provide. Maintain a network of referral relationships with addiction treatment providers, so referrals are timely and appropriate.

### Assess For Co-Occurring Mental Health And Substance Use Interactions

Substance use and mental health conditions frequently co-occur, and the interactions are complex: substance use can cause, worsen, or mimic mental health symptoms, and mental health conditions can drive substance use as self-medication. Assess the relationship between the substance use and the mental health symptoms, including the temporal pattern, which came first, the client's reasons for use, and the effect of use on symptoms. Do not assume that treating the mental health condition will resolve the substance use, or vice versa; both need attention, and the interactions need to be understood. Integrated treatment, addressing both, is the standard for co-occurring disorders.

### Assess Readiness To Change And Match Intervention To Stage

Substance use clients vary in their readiness to change, from precontemplative to action-oriented, and the intervention should match the stage. A precontemplative client needs motivational interviewing to consider change; a contemplative client needs to resolve ambivalence; a client in preparation needs a plan; a client in action needs support and skills. Assess the stage, using the client's statements and behaviors, and match the intervention, using motivational interviewing for early stages and structured treatment for later stages. Pushing action on a precontemplative client fails; waiting for action on a client who is ready misses the opportunity.

### Assess For Withdrawal Risk And Medical Needs

Some substances, alcohol, benzodiazepines, and opioids, carry significant withdrawal risks, and withdrawal can be dangerous or life-threatening. Assess the client's withdrawal risk based on the substance, the level of use, the duration, and the history, and refer for medical evaluation when withdrawal is possible. Do not advise abrupt cessation of substances with dangerous withdrawal, and coordinate with medical providers for supervised withdrawal when needed. Recognize that withdrawal is a medical matter, and the therapist's role is to refer and coordinate, not to manage withdrawal directly.

## Common Traps

### Not Screening Because The Client Does Not Look Like An Addict

A therapist may not screen for substance use because the client does not fit the therapist's stereotype of addiction, presenting as high-functioning, employed, or socially conventional, and the substance use is missed. The mechanism is that the therapist holds a stereotype of what an addict looks like, and the client, not fitting it, is not screened, when substance use disorders occur across all demographics and presentations. The harm is that the substance use is missed, the co-occurring conditions are treated without addressing the use, and the treatment fails or worsens the situation. Screening every client, regardless of presentation, is what prevents this error.

### Accepting Minimization Without Exploration

A therapist may accept the client's minimization of their substance use, "I only drink on weekends," "It's not a problem," without exploring the pattern, the quantity, or the impact, and the severity is underestimated. The mechanism is that clients minimize substance use, often honestly, due to shame, lack of concern, or denial, and the therapist, wanting to respect the client's framing, does not explore further. The harm is that a significant substance use disorder is underestimated, the treatment does not address it, and the client does not get the care they need. Exploring minimization, with specific questions and collateral information, is what detects the actual severity.

### Underestimating Severity And The Need For Specialized Care

A therapist may underestimate the severity of a substance use disorder and attempt to treat it in outpatient therapy when the client needs specialized or intensive treatment, because the therapist does not use a validated framework or wants to keep the client. The mechanism is that the therapist assesses severity informally, or is reluctant to refer, and proceeds with outpatient treatment when the severity warrants more. The harm is that the client does not get the level of care they need, the treatment fails, and the client may worsen or drop out. Using validated criteria to determine the level of care, and referring when needed, is what matches care to severity.

### Missing Co-Occurring Interactions

A therapist may treat a co-occurring mental health condition without addressing the substance use, or vice versa, missing the interactions between them, and the treatment of one fails because the other is unaddressed. The mechanism is that the conditions are treated separately, or one is missed, when substance use and mental health conditions interact in complex ways. The harm is that the treatment is ineffective, because the unaddressed condition maintains the other, and the client does not improve. Assessing and addressing both conditions, and their interactions, through integrated treatment, is what produces improvement.

### Advising Abrupt Cessation Of Substances With Withdrawal Risk

A therapist may advise a client to stop using a substance abruptly, not recognizing the withdrawal risk for substances like alcohol or benzodiazepines, which can be dangerous or life-threatening. The mechanism is that the therapist recognizes the substance use and wants the client to stop, without assessing the withdrawal risk or coordinating medical care. The harm is that the client undergoes dangerous withdrawal, which can include seizures or delirium tremens, and the therapist has endangered the client by advising cessation without medical oversight. Assessing withdrawal risk and referring for medical management when needed is what prevents this dangerous error.

- [ ] Am I screening every client for substance use, regardless of presentation?
- [ ] Am I assessing severity and impact using validated tools and a thorough clinical interview?
- [ ] Am I determining the level of care using validated criteria, such as ASAM, based on severity and circumstances?
- [ ] Am I recognizing when specialized addiction treatment, including medication-assisted treatment, is needed?

- [ ] Am I assessing readiness to change and matching the intervention to the stage?
