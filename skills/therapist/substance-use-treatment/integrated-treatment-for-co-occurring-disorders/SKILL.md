# Integrated Treatment For Co-Occurring Disorders

Co-occurring disorders, the presence of both a mental health condition and a substance use disorder, are the norm rather than the exception among clients with either condition, and integrated treatment, addressing both conditions together, is the evidence-based standard. The therapist's judgment is to treat both conditions and their interactions, to sequence the treatment thoughtfully, to coordinate with other providers including addiction medicine and psychiatry, and to recognize when one condition must be stabilized before the other can be addressed. The recurring failures are treating one condition and neglecting the other, sequencing treatment rigidly when flexibility is needed, failing to coordinate with medical providers, and not recognizing how the conditions maintain each other. Integrated treatment requires the therapist to hold both conditions in mind and to address the system they form.

Use this skill when treating a client with co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders, integrating care, or coordinating with other providers.

## Core Rules

### Treat Both Conditions And Their Interactions

Integrated treatment addresses both the mental health condition and the substance use disorder, and the interactions between them, rather than treating one and expecting the other to resolve. Assess how the conditions interact: does the client use substances to manage mental health symptoms? Does the substance use worsen the mental health condition? Does one condition trigger relapse in the other? Treat the interactions directly, helping the client understand the patterns and develop strategies for both. Integrated treatment is not treating two conditions in parallel; it is treating the system they form, and the interactions are central.

### Sequence The Treatment Thoughtfully

While both conditions are treated, the sequencing matters, and at different points, one condition may need priority. Early in treatment, if the substance use is active and severe, stabilization of the use, through detox, reduced use, or abstinence, may be the priority, because active severe use undermines mental health treatment. If the mental health condition is acute, such as severe depression or psychosis, stabilizing it may be the priority, because the symptoms undermine substance use treatment. Sequence thoughtfully, based on the client's current status, and revisit the sequencing as the treatment evolves. The sequencing is dynamic, not fixed, and it follows the client's needs.

### Coordinate With Medical And Psychiatric Providers

Co-occurring disorders often require medical and psychiatric care, including medication-assisted treatment for substance use, psychiatric medication for mental health conditions, and medical management of withdrawal or complications. Coordinate with these providers, with the client's authorization, sharing information that supports integrated care and receiving information that informs the therapy. Recognize that medication-assisted treatment, such as buprenorphine for opioids or naltrexone for alcohol, is effective and that many therapists cannot prescribe it, making referral essential. Build relationships with addiction medicine and psychiatric providers, so coordination is smooth and timely.

### Address The Mutual Maintenance Of The Conditions

Co-occurring conditions often maintain each other: the mental health symptoms drive substance use as self-medication, and the substance use worsens the mental health symptoms, creating a cycle that perpetuates both. Address the mutual maintenance directly, helping the client understand the cycle and develop strategies for breaking it. For example, a client who drinks to manage social anxiety finds that the drinking worsens the anxiety, leading to more drinking; the therapy addresses both the anxiety and the drinking, and the cycle between them. Addressing only one condition leaves the cycle intact, and the unaddressed condition maintains the other.

### Use Integrated Evidence-Based Approaches

Use evidence-based approaches that integrate treatment for co-occurring disorders, such as integrated dual disorder treatment, CBT adapted for co-occurring disorders, or DBT with substance use adaptations. These approaches address both conditions and their interactions, within a coherent framework. Do not treat the conditions with unrelated or conflicting approaches, which can confuse the client and undermine the care. If you are not trained in integrated treatment, seek training or refer to a provider who is, because co-occurring disorders require specific competencies beyond treating each condition separately.

### Recognize And Manage Relapse Risk For Both Conditions

Both mental health conditions and substance use disorders have relapse risk, and a relapse in one often triggers relapse in the other, so relapse prevention must address both. Develop a relapse prevention plan that includes early warning signs for both conditions, triggers that cross over, and strategies for managing setbacks in either. Recognize that a substance use relapse may trigger a mental health relapse, and vice versa, and address the crossover in the plan. Monitor both conditions throughout treatment, and address emerging signs of relapse early, in either condition.

### Address The Client's Stage Of Change For Each Condition

The client may be at different stages of change for each condition, ready to address their depression but precontemplative about their drinking, or vice versa, and the treatment must respect each stage. Assess the stage for each condition, and match the intervention, using motivational interviewing for the condition the client is less ready to address and active treatment for the one they are ready to address. Do not assume that readiness for one condition implies readiness for the other, and do not push change in a condition the client is not ready to address. Respecting the stage for each condition supports engagement and progress in both.

## Common Traps

### Treating One Condition And Neglecting The Other

A therapist may treat one condition, the one the client presents with or the therapist is most comfortable with, and neglect the other, expecting it to resolve, and the treatment fails because the unaddressed condition maintains the treated one. The mechanism is that one condition is salient, and the therapist, focused on it, does not address the other, when the conditions interact. The harm is that the treated condition does not improve, or improves temporarily, because the unaddressed condition maintains it, and the client does not recover. Treating both conditions and their interactions is what produces lasting improvement.

### Sequencing Treatment Rigidly When Flexibility Is Needed

A therapist may sequence treatment rigidly, insisting that the substance use be fully resolved before addressing the mental health condition, or vice versa, when the client's needs require flexibility. The mechanism is that the therapist holds a fixed sequence, often based on a model, when the client's status may require addressing both, or prioritizing the other condition. The harm is that the client's needs are not met, the untreated condition undermines the treatment, and the client may disengage. Sequencing thoughtfully, based on the client's current status, and revisiting the sequencing, is what keeps the treatment responsive.

### Failing To Coordinate With Medical Providers

A therapist may fail to coordinate with medical and psychiatric providers, treating the co-occurring disorders in isolation, when the client needs medication-assisted treatment, psychiatric medication, or medical management. The mechanism is that the therapist works independently, and does not build referral and coordination relationships, when co-occurring disorders often require medical care. The harm is that the client does not receive the medical care that would support recovery, the therapy is less effective, and the client's medical and psychiatric needs go unaddressed. Coordinating with medical providers, with authorization, is what supports integrated care.

### Not Recognizing How The Conditions Maintain Each Other

A therapist may treat both conditions but not recognize how they maintain each other, addressing each in isolation, and the mutual maintenance persists, undermining the treatment of both. The mechanism is that the conditions are treated as separate, and the interactions between them are not addressed, when the interactions are what perpetuate the cycle. The harm is that both conditions persist, despite treatment, because the cycle that maintains them is intact. Addressing the mutual maintenance, the cycle between the conditions, is what breaks the cycle and allows recovery.

### Pushing Change In A Condition The Client Is Not Ready To Address

A therapist may push change in a condition the client is not ready to address, insisting on abstinence when the client is precontemplative about their use, or pushing trauma work when the client is not stable, which activates resistance and disengagement. The mechanism is that the therapist wants to address the condition, and the client's readiness is not assessed or respected, when pushing change before readiness fails. The harm is that the client resists, the alliance ruptures, and the client may drop out. Assessing and respecting the stage of change for each condition, and matching the intervention, is what supports engagement and progress.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

- [ ] Am I coordinating with medical and psychiatric providers, with authorization?
- [ ] Am I addressing the mutual maintenance, the cycle between the conditions?
- [ ] Am I using an integrated, evidence-based approach within my training?
- [ ] Does the relapse prevention plan address both conditions and their crossover?
- [ ] Am I assessing and respecting the stage of change for each condition?
