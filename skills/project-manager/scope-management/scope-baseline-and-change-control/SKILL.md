# Scope Baseline And Change Control

Scope is protected by a baseline and a change process, not by good intentions. Without a baseline, there is no stable reference against which to judge whether new work belongs. Without a controlled change process, scope expands through small accumulative additions that each look reasonable and together break the schedule, budget, or quality. The project manager must establish what is in scope, make change visible, and force every addition through an honest assessment of its cost.

Use this skill before baselining scope, setting up a change control process, evaluating a change request, or diagnosing scope creep. The goal is to prevent the agent from allowing scope to drift silently until the project can no longer meet its commitments.

## Core Rules

### Establish A Scope Baseline Before Execution

A baseline is the approved version of scope against which performance and change are measured. It typically includes the scope statement, the work breakdown structure, and the work breakdown dictionary that defines each package.

The baseline must be approved by the accountable sponsor before execution commitments harden. Once baselined, the baseline is the reference point. Work outside the baseline is either a change to be controlled or out of scope to be refused.

Without a baseline, every discussion about what belongs becomes a renegotiation.

### Define Explicit Change Control

Change control is the process by which requested changes are documented, evaluated, approved or rejected, and reflected in the baseline. It exists to make change deliberate rather than accidental.

A functioning change control process includes:

- a single intake for change requests;
- impact analysis on scope, schedule, cost, quality, and risk;
- a defined approver or change authority;
- a decision recorded with rationale;
- update of the baseline and related plans when approved;
- communication to affected stakeholders.

The process should be proportional. Large changes deserve formal review; small ones can use a lightweight path. But no change should enter the work silently.

### Evaluate Impact Before Approving Change

A change request is not just a new feature. It is a tradeoff against existing commitments. Before approval, assess what the change costs in effort, duration, cost, risk, quality, and dependencies.

Ask:

- What work does this add, modify, or remove?
- What is the effort and duration impact?
- Does it affect the critical path?
- Does it require additional resources or budget?
- Does it introduce new risk or quality exposure?
- What is the cost of not doing it?

Present the tradeoff to the decision maker. A change approved without understanding its impact is scope creep wearing a formal label.

### Distinguish Scope Creep From Scope Discovery

Not every addition is creep. Scope discovery is the legitimate refinement of scope as understanding grows within the agreed boundaries. Scope creep is uncontrolled growth beyond the agreed boundaries, often through incremental additions that escape formal review.

The test is whether the addition is within the spirit of the baseline and whether it goes through the change process. Discovery is managed; creep is accidental. Both should be visible, but only creep is a failure of control.

### Manage Gold Plating

Gold plating is the addition of features or polish that were not requested, usually by a delivery team trying to exceed expectations. It feels generous but consumes effort, adds complexity, increases test surface, and can introduce defects.

Make clear that exceeding scope without approval is not a virtue. Unrequested additions should go through the same change evaluation as any other addition.

### Keep The Baseline Current And Versioned

A baseline that is never updated becomes useless as a reference. When a change is approved, update the baseline, the schedule, the budget, and the related plans, and record the version history.

Stakeholders should always be able to see the current approved scope and the history of changes that got there. A stale baseline is worse than none because it creates false confidence.

### Connect Scope Control To Schedule, Cost, And Quality

Scope does not change in isolation. A scope addition almost always affects schedule, cost, or quality, or requires a compensating reduction elsewhere. Change control must trace these effects.

When scope grows without schedule, budget, or quality adjustments, the project is silently accepting a tradeoff it has not acknowledged. Make the connection explicit in every change evaluation.

## Common Traps

### No Baseline, So No Reference

Without an approved baseline, scope is whatever the loudest stakeholder currently wants, and there is no way to detect drift.

### Silent Acceptance Of Small Additions

Each small request looks harmless. Accumulated, they break commitments. Size does not determine whether change control applies.

### Approving Change Without Impact Analysis

A change approved on enthusiasm or pressure, without understanding its cost, becomes a future failure.

### Confusing Formality With Control

A heavy change form that is rubber-stamped provides the appearance of control without the substance. Impact analysis and honest tradeoffs matter more than paperwork.

### Allowing Gold Plating

Unrequested extras consume effort and add risk. Exceeding expectations without approval is not generosity.

### Stale Baseline

A baseline frozen at kickoff and never updated no longer reflects reality and stops being a useful reference.

### Ignoring Out-Of-Scope Refusals

Failing to clearly refuse out-of-scope work, or accepting it quietly to avoid conflict, erodes the baseline and overloads the team.

### Disconnecting Scope From Schedule And Budget

Approving scope growth without adjusting schedule or budget pretends the project can absorb infinite work for free.

## Self-Check

- [ ] An approved scope baseline exists and includes the scope statement, work breakdown structure, and package definitions.
- [ ] A defined change control process exists with a single intake, impact analysis, an approver, recorded decisions, and baseline updates.
- [ ] Every change request is evaluated for impact on scope, schedule, cost, quality, risk, and dependencies before approval.
- [ ] Scope creep is distinguished from legitimate scope discovery, and both are made visible.
- [ ] Gold plating and unrequested additions are treated as changes requiring evaluation.
- [ ] The baseline is versioned and kept current as approved changes are incorporated.
- [ ] Scope changes are connected to corresponding schedule, cost, and quality adjustments.
- [ ] Out-of-scope requests are refused or processed through change control rather than accepted silently.
- [ ] Change formality is proportional to change size, but no change bypasses evaluation.
- [ ] Stakeholders can see the current approved scope and the history of changes.
