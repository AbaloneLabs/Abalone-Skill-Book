# Scope Verification And Acceptance

Completing work is not the same as having it accepted. Scope verification is the formal process of comparing completed deliverables against the defined requirements and acceptance criteria and obtaining stakeholder acceptance. The judgment problem is that acceptance is often treated as a formality at the end, when in reality it is a process that must be set up from the beginning through clear criteria, staged verification, and stakeholder involvement. The most common failure is a deliverable that the team considers complete but the customer rejects, because the acceptance criteria were never explicitly agreed, or because verification was deferred to the end when correction is most expensive.

Use this skill before defining acceptance criteria, before planning deliverable verification, before a handover or acceptance review, or when deliverables have been rejected and the cause is unclear. The goal is to prevent the agent from deferring verification to the end, from leaving acceptance criteria implicit, from surprising stakeholders at handover, or from proceeding without formal acceptance.

## Core Rules

### [ ] Define Acceptance Criteria Before Work Begins

Acceptance criteria specify the measurable conditions a deliverable must meet to be accepted. Defined before work begins, they align the team and the customer on what "done" means and prevent disputes at handover. Criteria defined after the work is complete are contested.

- [ ] Define measurable, testable acceptance criteria for each deliverable before work starts.
- [ ] Agree criteria with the customer or accepting stakeholder and document them.
- [ ] Ensure criteria are specific enough to determine acceptance objectively.
- [ ] Avoid vague criteria like "meets expectations" that invite dispute.

### [ ] Verify Deliverables Staged, Not Only At The End

Deferring all verification to project end concentrates risk: defects are found late when correction is most expensive, and rejection at the end can invalidate months of work. Stage verification throughout the project so issues surface early and incrementally.

- [ ] Verify deliverables at milestones and phase gates, not only at project end.
- [ ] Obtain intermediate acceptance for components and sub-deliverables.
- [ ] Use incremental verification to catch defects early when correction is cheap.
- [ ] Avoid a single end-of-project acceptance event for the entire scope.

### [ ] Involve The Accepting Stakeholder Throughout

The stakeholder who will accept the deliverable should be involved throughout, not only at the final review. Involvement during development ensures the deliverable evolves toward acceptance and prevents surprise rejection. A stakeholder seeing the deliverable for the first time at handover is a setup for rejection.

- [ ] Engage the accepting stakeholder during development, not only at handover.
- [ ] Share intermediate versions for feedback and course correction.
- [ ] Use reviews and demos to build toward acceptance incrementally.
- [ ] Avoid presenting a finished deliverable to a stakeholder who has not seen it evolve.

### [ ] Verify Against The Requirements Baseline

Verification compares the deliverable against the approved requirements and acceptance criteria, not against subjective judgment or shifting expectations. The requirements baseline is the reference; deviation from it, whether through change control, is documented and traceable.

- [ ] Verify deliverables against the approved requirements and acceptance criteria.
- [ ] Use the requirements baseline as the objective reference.
- [ ] Document any deviations and their disposition.
- [ ] Avoid verifying against undocumented or shifting expectations.

### [ ] Distinguish Verification (Quality) From Acceptance (Scope)

Verification is an internal quality check: does the deliverable meet its specifications? Acceptance is a stakeholder decision: does the customer formally accept it? Both are needed; verification precedes and supports acceptance. Confusing them leads to assuming internal quality equals customer acceptance.

- [ ] Perform internal verification (quality control) before seeking acceptance.
- [ ] Treat acceptance as a formal stakeholder decision, separate from verification.
- [ ] Do not assume a verified deliverable will automatically be accepted.
- [ ] Document both verification results and acceptance decisions.

### [ ] Document Acceptance Decisions Formally

Acceptance should be documented in writing, with the deliverable, the criteria, the result, and signatures. Informal verbal acceptance is contested later. Formal documentation creates a record and supports project closure.

- [ ] Document acceptance decisions in writing with criteria and signatures.
- [ ] Specify the deliverable, version, criteria, and result for each acceptance.
- [ ] Obtain formal sign-off from the authorized accepting stakeholder.
- [ ] Maintain acceptance records for project closure and audit.

### [ ] Manage Rejection Constructively

Rejection is not failure; it is information. When a deliverable is rejected, analyze the cause: were the criteria unclear, was the work deficient, or did expectations shift? Use rejection to improve the process and the deliverable, not to assign blame.

- [ ] Treat rejection as diagnostic information, not just failure.
- [ ] Analyze the cause: unclear criteria, deficient work, or shifted expectations.
- [ ] Define corrective action and re-verification.
- [ ] Use rejection patterns to improve the acceptance process.

### [ ] Handle Scope Changes Through Change Control Before Acceptance

If acceptance reveals that requirements have changed, the change must flow through change control, not be absorbed silently. Accepting changed expectations without change control leads to scope creep and erodes the baseline.

- [ ] Route requirement changes discovered during acceptance through change control.
- [ ] Avoid absorbing changed expectations without updating the baseline.
- [ ] Document the change, its impact, and its approval.
- [ ] Re-baseline acceptance criteria when scope changes are approved.

### [ ] Plan Acceptance Activities And Resources

Acceptance is not free; it requires reviewer time, test environments, and documentation. Plan acceptance activities, resources, and timeframes as part of the project plan, not as an afterthought at the end.

- [ ] Include acceptance activities, resources, and timeframes in the project plan.
- [ ] Schedule acceptance reviews and allocate reviewer availability.
- [ ] Prepare verification evidence (test results, documentation) in advance.
- [ ] Avoid leaving acceptance unplanned and rushed at project end.

### [ ] Connect Acceptance To Project Closure

Formal acceptance of all deliverables is a prerequisite for project closure. Without acceptance, the project cannot be closed cleanly. Track acceptance status across all deliverables and ensure all are accepted before closure.

- [ ] Track acceptance status for all deliverables.
- [ ] Ensure all deliverables are formally accepted before project closure.
- [ ] Resolve any unaccepted deliverables before closure.
- [ ] Use acceptance records as closure evidence.

## Common Traps

### [ ] Implicit Acceptance Criteria

Leaving "done" undefined until handover, inviting dispute.

### [ ] End-Only Verification

Deferring all verification to the end when correction is most expensive.

### [ ] Surprise At Handover

Presenting a finished deliverable to a stakeholder who has not been involved.

### [ ] Verifying Against Shifting Expectations

Comparing against undocumented expectations rather than the requirements baseline.

### [ ] Confusing Quality With Acceptance

Assuming internal verification equals customer acceptance.

### [ ] Informal Acceptance

Verbal or undocumented acceptance that is later contested.

### [ ] Silent Scope Changes

Absorbing changed expectations without change control, causing scope creep.

### [ ] Unplanned Acceptance

Leaving acceptance activities, resources, and timeframes unplanned until the end.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are acceptance criteria defined, measurable, and agreed before work begins?
- [ ] Is deliverable verification staged throughout the project, not only at the end?
- [ ] Is the accepting stakeholder involved throughout development, not only at handover?
- [ ] Are deliverables verified against the approved requirements baseline?
- [ ] Are internal verification (quality) and formal acceptance (scope) distinguished?
- [ ] Are acceptance decisions documented formally with criteria and signatures?
- [ ] Is rejection analyzed constructively to improve the deliverable and process?
- [ ] Are scope changes discovered during acceptance routed through change control?
- [ ] Are acceptance activities, resources, and timeframes planned as part of the project?
- [ ] Is acceptance status tracked and all deliverables accepted before project closure?
