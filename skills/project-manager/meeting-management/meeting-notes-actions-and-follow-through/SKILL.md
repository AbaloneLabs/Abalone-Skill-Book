# Meeting Notes, Actions, and Follow-Through

A meeting ends, and within an hour its value begins to decay. Without a record, the decisions dissolve, the action items lose their owners, and the commitments made in the room quietly evaporate. By the next meeting, half the actions were never done, no one is sure who owed what, and the same decisions get reopened because nothing was captured to defend them. The note-taking and follow-through discipline is what converts a meeting from an expensive conversation into a project asset. A meeting that produced a great decision but left no trace might as well not have happened.

The judgment problem is not "take minutes." It is how to capture the decisions, actions, and open questions in a form that survives the week, how to assign each action to a single accountable owner with a real deadline, and how to close the loop so that what was decided actually gets done. Agents tend to capture discussion rather than outcomes, to leave actions with vague ownership, and to treat note distribution as the end of the process rather than the start of tracking.

## Core Rules

### Capture Outcomes, Not Transcript

Notes that summarize what was said are low-value; notes that record what was decided, what actions resulted, and what remains open are high-value. Distinguish between the discussion (which is ephemeral) and the decisions and actions (which are the durable output). Capture each decision with its rationale and each action with its owner and due date. Skip the narrative of who said what unless it carries a commitment or a risk.

### Assign Every Action to a Single Owner With a Real Date

An action item must have exactly one owner and a specific due date. "The team will follow up" is not an action; it is a wish that no one is accountable for. When an action is shared, name a single primary owner who is responsible for ensuring it happens, even if others contribute. The due date must be a real date, not "soon" or "next week," and it must be agreed by the owner, not imposed silently.

### Record Decisions With Rationale and Rejected Alternatives

A decision recorded as a bare outcome invites re-litigation, because a future reader cannot tell why it was made or what else was considered. Capture the decision, the key reason it was chosen, and the alternatives that were rejected, along with any dissent or accepted risk. This is what lets the decision hold when it is later questioned, and what prevents the team from reopening a settled question.

### Distribute Notes While Context Is Fresh

Notes lose value every hour they sit undistributed, as memory drifts and the window for correction closes. Distribute the notes within the same day, ideally within an hour, while participants can still catch errors and omissions. Prompt distribution also signals to action owners that their commitments are now recorded and expected, which dramatically improves completion rates.

### Make Notes Searchable and Linked to the Decision Record

Meeting notes buried in individual inboxes are lost; notes in a shared, searchable location become a project memory. Store notes where the team can find them, link them to the relevant work items or decision records, and keep a consistent structure so they can be scanned quickly. The decision log and the meeting notes should reference each other so a decision can be traced back to where and why it was made.

### Track Actions to Completion, Not Just to Assignment

Recording an action is the start, not the end. Each action must be tracked until it is done, with status visible to the owner and the project manager. Review open actions at the start of the next relevant meeting, so owners know their commitments will be checked. An action that is never followed up on teaches the team that actions are optional, which collapses the entire follow-through system.

### Distinguish Decision, Action, Issue, and Parking Lot

Not everything in a meeting is the same kind of output. A decision is a commitment that is now in force. An action is work someone will do. An issue is a problem that needs resolution. A parking-lot item is something raised but deferred, which still needs an owner to revisit it. Label each item by type so that decisions are not confused with actions and deferred items are not silently dropped.

### Close the Loop on Decisions and Communicate Them Outward

A decision made in a meeting but never communicated to those not present does not take effect. Identify who needs to know each decision and ensure it reaches them through the right channel. Track that decisions are actually implemented, not just recorded, because a decision that exists only on paper erodes trust in the whole governance process.

## Common Traps

### Transcript Notes Without Outcomes

Notes that summarize the discussion capture the ephemeral and drop the durable, so the decisions and actions that actually mattered are missing. The trap is that the notes look thorough because they are long. Capture outcomes, decisions, and actions explicitly, and let the discussion go unrecorded unless it carries a commitment.

### Shared or Vague Action Ownership

Actions assigned to "the team," to a group, or with no named owner have no accountability and rarely get done. The trap is that group ownership feels collaborative but actually diffuses responsibility to zero. Assign every action to a single primary owner, even when the work is collaborative.

### Due Dates That Are Not Dates

"ASAP," "soon," or "next sprint" are not deadlines; they are aspirations with no forcing function. The trap is that vague dates feel less confrontational but guarantee the action slips indefinitely. Every action needs a specific calendar date, agreed with the owner.

### Notes Distributed Too Late

Notes sent days after the meeting arrive after memory has drifted and corrections are no longer possible, so errors become permanent and momentum is lost. The trap is treating notes as a cleanup task rather than a time-sensitive output. Distribute within the day, while the context is still fresh.

### Actions Recorded But Never Tracked

The action is captured diligently and then never followed up, so owners learn that recording an action carries no expectation of completion. The trap is that the tracking system exists but is inert. Review open actions in the next meeting and close the loop, or the system loses credibility.

### Decisions Without Rationale Inviting Re-Litigation

A decision recorded as a bare outcome gives a future challenger nothing to engage with, so the question gets reopened and re-debated at full cost. The trap is that the bare record looks sufficient. Capture the rationale and rejected alternatives so the decision can be defended rather than relitigated.

### Parking-Lot Items That Silently Disappear

Items deferred to the parking lot are raised but never assigned an owner or a revisit date, so they vanish into a black hole and resurface later as surprises. The trap is that "parking" feels like handling them. Every parked item needs an owner and a trigger to revisit it.

### Decisions Made but Never Communicated

The decision is recorded in the notes but never reaches the people who must act on it, so it never takes effect and the team continues the old behavior. The trap is confusing recording with communicating. Identify the audience for each decision and send it to them through a channel they actually read.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Do the notes capture decisions, actions, and open questions rather than a transcript of discussion?
- [ ] Does every action item have a single primary owner and a specific calendar due date?
- [ ] Are decisions recorded with rationale and the alternatives that were rejected?
- [ ] Are notes distributed the same day, while participants can still correct errors?
- [ ] Are notes stored in a shared, searchable location and linked to the decision record and work items?
- [ ] Are open actions tracked to completion and reviewed at the start of the next relevant meeting?
- [ ] Is each item labeled as decision, action, issue, or parking lot so types are not confused?
- [ ] Does every parking-lot item have an owner and a trigger to revisit it?
- [ ] Have decisions been communicated to the people who must act on them, not just recorded?
- [ ] Is there a mechanism confirming decisions are actually implemented, not just documented?
