# Meeting Cadence and Communication Architecture

Individual meetings are easy to critique; the meeting system is what actually consumes a team's week. Most projects accumulate meetings reactively: a standup here, a status meeting there, a steering committee, a working group, a sync, a check-in, until the calendar is full and no one has uninterrupted time to do the work the meetings are meant to coordinate. Communication architecture is the discipline of designing the whole set of interactions as a system, so that each meeting has a distinct purpose, the cadences fit together without redundancy, and the team retains the focus time the work requires.

The judgment problem is not "schedule a standup." It is how to choose the right cadences for the project's rhythm, how to assign each communication need to the cheapest effective channel, and how to keep the meeting set lean enough that the remaining meetings are worth attending. Agents tend to add meetings to solve every coordination problem, to let cadences proliferate without a map, and to treat the calendar as a given rather than as something designed and periodically pruned.

## Core Rules

### Map the Communication Needs Before Designing the Cadence

Start from what the project needs to communicate, not from a template of meetings. Identify the recurring needs: tactical coordination, status visibility, decision-making, risk and issue surfacing, stakeholder reporting, and reflective improvement. Each need has a natural frequency and a cheapest effective channel. Designing the cadence from needs prevents the default of copying another project's meeting set, which rarely fits.

### Assign Each Need to the Cheapest Effective Channel

Not every need requires a meeting. Status visibility is often best served by a written dashboard or async update; tactical coordination by a chat channel or a short standup; decision-making by a focused synchronous session; reflective improvement by a periodic retrospective. Match the channel to the need, and resist the reflex to make everything a meeting. The goal is the fewest meetings that still meet every real need.

### Choose Cadence Frequency to Match the Rate of Change

A standup that meets daily suits work where priorities shift daily; one that meets weekly suits slower-moving work. Match cadence to the half-life of the information being exchanged. A steering committee that meets monthly cannot govern a project that changes weekly; a daily standup for a project that moves monthly wastes time. Calibrate frequency to how fast the relevant decisions and risks actually evolve.

### Give Each Meeting a Distinct, Non-Overlapping Purpose

When two meetings cover the same ground, both degrade: attendance splits, information fragments, and people stop engaging because the content repeats. Ensure each meeting in the architecture owns a distinct purpose that no other meeting covers. The standup handles tactical blockers; the status report handles visibility; the steering committee handles governance decisions; the retrospective handles improvement. If purposes overlap, merge or kill one.

### Sequence Cadences So Output Feeds Input

A communication architecture is a flow, not just a set. The standup's surfaced blockers should feed the issue review; the status report should feed the steering committee; the retrospective's improvements should feed the next planning cycle. Sequence meetings so that each one's output is the input to the next, rather than running them as independent events that each gather information from scratch.

### Protect Focus Time as a First-Class Constraint

Every meeting hour is an hour of focus time removed, and the cost is non-linear because meetings fragment the day into slots too short for deep work. Treat the team's available focus time as a constrained resource and budget the meeting load against it. A team in meetings thirty hours a week cannot do the work the meetings are coordinating. Set a ceiling on meeting load and design within it.

### Separate Operational, Governance, and Improvement Loops

Three different rhythms serve different functions and should not be mashed into one meeting. Operational loops (standups, syncs) handle in-flight coordination at high frequency. Governance loops (steering, sponsor reviews) handle direction and decisions at lower frequency. Improvement loops (retrospectives, lessons learned) handle learning at the end of cycles. Conflating them produces meetings that try to do everything and accomplish nothing well.

### Prune the Architecture Periodically

Meeting sets accumulate and rarely get cut. Run a periodic review of the whole architecture: which meetings are still earning their time, which overlap, which have outlived their purpose, which are missing. Treat the calendar like a backlog that needs grooming, not a permanent fixture. The health signal is whether the team can name, for each meeting, the distinct outcome it produces that no other meeting covers.

## Common Traps

### Meeting Proliferation Without a Map

Each new coordination problem spawns a new recurring meeting, and no one holds the map of the whole set, so redundancy compounds. The trap is that each addition seemed reasonable in isolation. Maintain the architecture as a visible map and require every new meeting to justify its distinct purpose against the existing set.

### Copying Another Project's Cadence Template

A cadence that worked elsewhere is imported wholesale, but the project's rhythm, size, and risk profile differ, so the meetings do not fit. The trap is that the template feels safe and professional. Design the cadence from this project's communication needs and rate of change, not from a borrowed blueprint.

### Frequency Mismatched to Rate of Change

A daily standup for slow-moving work burns time on nothing new; a monthly steering review for fast-moving work governs after the fact. The trap is setting cadence by convention rather than by the half-life of the information. Recalibrate frequency to how fast decisions and risks actually evolve.

### Overlapping Purposes Across Meetings

Two or three meetings cover the same status, so attendance fragments and engagement drops because the content repeats. The trap is redundancy disguised as thoroughness. Enforce distinct, non-overlapping purposes, and merge or kill meetings whose purposes collide.

### Meetings That Gather Instead of Flow

Each meeting independently collects information from participants, with no flow between cadences, so the same data gets reported repeatedly in different rooms. The trap is treating meetings as isolated events rather than as a pipeline. Sequence cadences so outputs feed inputs and information is reported once, then flows.

### Ignoring the Focus-Time Cost

Meetings are added without accounting for the focus time they consume, until the team has no uninterrupted blocks left and throughput collapses. The trap is counting meeting hours linearly when the real cost is the fragmentation of the day. Budget meeting load against available focus time as a hard constraint.

### The Permanent Recurring Meeting

A meeting created for a temporary need becomes permanent by inertia, surviving long after its purpose ended, because canceling a recurring meeting feels like a decision and letting it run feels like the default. The trap is that the cost is invisible and distributed. Schedule recurring meetings with an explicit review date and kill them if they no longer earn their time.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the communication architecture been designed from the project's actual needs rather than copied from a template?
- [ ] Is each communication need assigned to the cheapest effective channel, with meetings reserved for what truly requires synchronous interaction?
- [ ] Does each cadence's frequency match the rate of change of the information it exchanges?
- [ ] Does every meeting in the architecture own a distinct purpose that no other meeting covers?
- [ ] Are cadences sequenced so that one meeting's output feeds the next meeting's input?
- [ ] Are operational, governance, and improvement loops kept separate rather than mashed into single meetings?
- [ ] Has the total meeting load been budgeted against the team's available focus time as a hard constraint?
- [ ] Is there a visible map of the whole meeting set that is periodically reviewed and pruned?
- [ ] Does each recurring meeting have an explicit review date and a clear criterion for being killed?
- [ ] Can the team name, for each meeting, the distinct outcome it produces that no other meeting covers?
