# Budget Variance And Forecasting

Variance is the gap between plan and reality, and it is the project's earliest signal that something has changed. But variance is only useful if it is explained, not merely reported. A variance without a cause is a number; a variance with a diagnosed cause is a decision input. Forecasting extends this forward, predicting where the project will land if conditions continue or change. The project manager must analyze variance rigorously, attribute it correctly, and produce forecasts that are credible, assumption-based, and responsive to what the variance is actually saying.

Use this skill before explaining budget variance, forecasting completion cost, preparing a financial outlook for sponsors, or revising a cost baseline. The goal is to prevent the agent from reporting variance as isolated numbers and from producing forecasts that ignore the conditions driving them.

## Core Rules

### Analyze Variance By Cause, Not Just By Amount

A variance figure tells you there is a gap. It does not tell you why. Before acting, attribute the variance to its cause.

Common causes include scope change, estimate error, rate change, timing difference, rework, vendor price change, resource substitution, and risk realization. Each cause implies a different response and a different forecast. Scope change may require a baseline update; estimate error may require recalibration; timing difference may reverse on its own.

Group variances by cause so patterns emerge. Many small variances from the same cause are more important than one large variance from a one-time event.

### Separate Controllable From Uncontrollable Drivers

Some variance drivers are within the project's control, such as productivity, rework, and resource choices. Others are not, such as rate changes, currency movement, or vendor price escalation imposed externally.

Separating them clarifies what can be managed. A project cannot recover uncontrollable cost through effort alone; it needs scope, schedule, or budget decisions. Reporting all variance as if it were a performance issue misdirects the response.

### Forecast With Explicit Assumptions

A forecast predicts the future, and the future depends on assumptions. State them. Will current cost performance continue? Will remaining work go as originally planned? Are pending changes included? Are known risks assumed not to occur?

Different assumptions produce different forecasts. The estimate at completion assuming current performance continues is usually higher than the one assuming remaining work goes to plan. Present the range and the assumption behind each, so decision makers understand what they are being told.

### Use Trend, Not Just Point-In-Time

A single month's variance is noisy. The trend across months reveals whether performance is stable, improving, or deteriorating. A project that is on plan this month but whose performance index has been declining for three months is heading for trouble.

Track variance and performance indices over time. Establish whether a variance is a spike, likely to reverse, or a trend, likely to persist. Trend analysis prevents overreacting to noise and underreacting to slow deterioration.

### Distinguish Forecast From Commitment

A forecast is a prediction of where the project will land. A commitment is a promise, usually carrying reserve or scope flexibility. They are different, and confusing them creates false promises or false alarms.

Communicate which is which. A forecast that the project will overrun is information for decision; a commitment is a bound the project intends to hold. Presenting a forecast as a commitment invites failure; presenting a commitment as a forecast hides the buffer that protects it.

### Incorporate Pending Changes And Known Risks

A forecast that includes only approved work and assumes no risk realization is optimistic by construction. Incorporate pending change requests, even unapproved ones, as contingent exposure. Include the expected cost of known risks weighted by probability where material.

The result is a forecast that reflects the real possible outcomes, not just the approved plan. Sponsors can then see the gap between the committed baseline and the realistic outlook.

### Re-Forecast At A Regular Cadence

Forecasts degrade as conditions change. Re-forecast at a regular cadence, and whenever a material event occurs, such as a major change, a risk realization, or a significant variance.

Each re-forecast should compare the new forecast to the previous one and explain the movement. A forecast that jumps without explanation undermines confidence; a forecast that moves with clear causes builds it.

### Reconcile Forecast With Reserve

A forecast above the baseline should be covered by contingency or management reserve, or the gap is an uncovered exposure. Reconcile the forecast to available reserve and report the remaining buffer.

When forecast plus reserve exceeds the total funding, the project has a financial problem that requires a decision, not just a report. Surface it as a decision with options.

## Common Traps

### Variance Without Cause

Reporting a number without diagnosing why misdirects the response.

### Treating All Variance As Performance

Blaming the team for uncontrollable rate or price changes misallocates effort.

### Single-Point Forecasts

One forecast number with no assumption hides the range of possible outcomes.

### Overreacting To Noise

Treating a one-month spike as a trend produces unstable plans.

### Underreacting To Trend

Treating a slow decline as noise allows deterioration to become irreversible.

### Forecast Confused With Commitment

Presenting a prediction as a promise sets up failure or false alarms.

### Optimistic Exclusion

Forecasting only approved work with no risk or pending change understates real exposure.

### Unexplained Forecast Movement

A forecast that jumps without explanation erodes sponsor confidence.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Variances are attributed to cause, not merely reported by amount, and grouped to reveal patterns.
- [ ] Controllable and uncontrollable variance drivers are separated to direct the response correctly.
- [ ] Forecasts state their assumptions, including whether current performance continues or remaining work goes to plan.
- [ ] Variance and performance indices are tracked over time to distinguish spikes from trends.
- [ ] Forecasts are distinguished from commitments in communication.
- [ ] Pending changes and known risks are incorporated as contingent exposure, not excluded for optimism.
- [ ] Forecasts are re-produced at a regular cadence and after material events, with movement explained.
- [ ] The forecast is reconciled to available contingency and management reserve, with uncovered exposure flagged.
- [ ] A forecast above baseline with insufficient reserve is surfaced as a decision with options.
- [ ] The trend, not only the latest point, drives the interpretation of cost health.
