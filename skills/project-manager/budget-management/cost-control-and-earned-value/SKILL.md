# Cost Control And Earned Value

A budget that is never measured against actuals is a wish. Cost control is the discipline of comparing planned spend to actual spend, understanding why they diverge, forecasting where the project will land, and acting before the overrun is irreversible. Earned value management extends this by measuring scope, schedule, and cost together, exposing whether the project is really progressing or merely consuming money. The project manager must track cost honestly, interpret variance correctly, and avoid the false comfort of reports that show spending on plan while value lags behind.

Use this skill before tracking cost performance, applying earned value, forecasting completion cost, or diagnosing why a project on budget is still in trouble. The goal is to prevent the agent from equating money spent with progress made and from reporting cost health without measuring value earned.

## Core Rules

### Track Planned Value, Earned Value, And Actual Cost Together

Three measures anchor cost control. Planned value is the budgeted cost of work scheduled. Earned value is the budgeted cost of work actually completed. Actual cost is what was spent to complete that work.

Comparing the three reveals the real picture. Earned value against planned value shows schedule performance. Earned value against actual cost shows cost performance. Tracking only actual cost against planned spend hides whether the money bought the intended progress.

### Use Variance And Performance Indices To Diagnose

Variance and index measures turn raw numbers into signals. Cost variance is earned value minus actual cost; negative means over budget. Schedule variance is earned value minus planned value; negative means behind schedule. The cost performance index and schedule performance index express the same as ratios.

An index below one signals trouble. The magnitude shows how much, and the trend shows whether it is worsening. A project with indices persistently below one is heading for overrun or delay even if current spend looks controlled.

### Forecast Estimate At Completion Honestly

Forecasting predicts where the project will land. Estimate at completion can be forecast several ways: assuming the remaining work goes as planned, assuming the current cost performance rate continues, or combining schedule and cost performance.

Choose the method that fits the situation. If current performance reflects a permanent condition, use the performance rate. If it reflects a one-time problem, plan-based may be better. State the assumption behind the forecast, because a forecast without an assumption is a number without meaning.

### Distinguish Cost Overrun From Timing Differences

Not every variance is an overrun. A cost variance may reflect work performed earlier or later than planned, an accrual timing difference, or a reclassification. Investigate before concluding.

A variance that is timing will reverse; a variance that is real performance will persist. Reporting a timing difference as an overrun, or a real overrun as timing, both mislead decisions.

### Act On Negative Trends Early

By the time an overrun is large, the options to recover are few. Small negative variances, watched and investigated early, are far cheaper to correct.

Establish thresholds that trigger investigation and action. A variance within tolerance may need only monitoring; a variance beyond tolerance needs a cause analysis and a response. Waiting for the variance to become undeniable surrenders the chance to recover.

### Connect Cost Control To Scope, Schedule, And Quality

Cost does not move in isolation. A cost underrun may reflect delayed work, not efficiency. A cost overrun may reflect scope growth, rework, or crashing. Quality problems show up later as rework cost.

Interpret cost performance alongside schedule and scope performance. A project under budget because it is behind schedule is not healthy; it is deferred trouble.

### Manage Contingency And Reserve Deliberately

Contingency is consumed as identified risks materialize. Management reserve is consumed as unidentified work emerges. Both should be tracked, governed, and reported.

Reserve that is consumed faster than planned signals that risk is materializing faster than expected. Reserve that is exhausted while work remains signals that the project is exposed to any further surprise. Report reserve consumption as a leading indicator, not only as a budget line.

### Report Cost Health With Confidence And Risk

Cost reporting should not only state where the project is. It should state the forecast, the confidence, and the risk to that forecast. A project on budget today may have low confidence in its completion cost because of late-arriving invoices, pending change requests, or risk not yet realized.

Pair the numbers with the assumptions and risks that could move them. A cost report that presents a single confident number hides the uncertainty that decision makers need.

## Common Traps

### Equating Spend With Progress

Money spent is not value earned. A project can be on budget and badly behind.

### Ignoring Schedule-Cost Interaction

A cost underrun caused by delay is deferred trouble, not efficiency.

### Forecasts Without Stated Assumptions

A completion forecast is meaningless without the performance assumption behind it.

### Waiting For Large Variances

Acting only when overruns are undeniable surrenders the chance to recover.

### Misreading Timing As Performance

Accrual or timing differences can look like variances that are not real.

### Silent Reserve Consumption

Reserve consumed without tracking or reporting hides emerging risk.

### Single Confident Number

Reporting one figure without confidence or risk hides the uncertainty decision makers need.

### Cost Control Disconnected From Scope And Quality

Cost performance interpreted without scope and quality hides rework and growth.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Planned value, earned value, and actual cost are tracked together, not only actual against planned spend.
- [ ] Cost and schedule variance and performance indices are used to diagnose performance and trend.
- [ ] Estimate at completion is forecast with the underlying assumption stated.
- [ ] Variances are investigated to distinguish real performance from timing differences before action.
- [ ] Negative trends trigger investigation and response at defined thresholds, before they become large.
- [ ] Cost performance is interpreted alongside schedule, scope, and quality, not in isolation.
- [ ] Contingency and management reserve consumption is tracked, governed, and reported as a leading indicator.
- [ ] Cost reports include forecast, confidence, and the risks that could move the forecast.
- [ ] Spend is not confused with progress, and earned value is the measure of value delivered.
- [ ] Reserve exhaustion while work remains is flagged as exposure to further surprise.
