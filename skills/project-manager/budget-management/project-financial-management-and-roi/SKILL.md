# Project Financial Management And ROI

Project budget management tracks whether spending matches the plan; project financial management tracks whether the project remains a sound investment. The two are related but distinct. A project can be on budget and still destroy value if the business case assumptions that justified it no longer hold, if the expected benefits have eroded, or if funding constraints create cash flow problems. The judgment problem is that financial viability is often established in the business case and then forgotten during execution, even as conditions change. The skill is tracking the project's financial health holistically, monitoring the business case against reality, managing funding and cash flow, and raising the question of continued viability when the numbers shift.

Use this skill when managing project finances beyond budget tracking, when business case assumptions may have changed, when funding or cash flow is constrained, when managing vendor financials, or when evaluating whether a project should continue. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating the business case as settled, from ignoring cash flow constraints, from letting vendor costs drift unmanaged, or from continuing a project that has ceased to be financially viable.

## Core Rules

### [ ] Track The Business Case Throughout Execution

The business case that justified the project contains assumptions about benefits, costs, and timing. These assumptions can erode during execution: benefits shrink, costs rise, timelines slip. Track the business case against actual conditions and reassess viability when assumptions diverge significantly.

- [ ] Document the business case assumptions at project approval.
- [ ] Track actual benefits, costs, and timing against the business case.
- [ ] Reassess viability when assumptions diverge significantly.
- [ ] Raise the viability question rather than continuing by inertia.

### [ ] Monitor ROI And Benefit Realization

ROI is not confirmed at project approval; it is realized after delivery. Monitor whether the expected benefits are materializing, and track benefit realization against the business case. A project that delivers on time and on budget but whose benefits never materialize has failed financially.

- [ ] Define the benefits and how they will be measured.
- [ ] Track benefit realization against the business case after delivery.
- [ ] Identify benefits that are not materializing and investigate why.
- [ ] Feed benefit realization data back into portfolio and investment decisions.

### [ ] Manage Funding And Cash Flow

A project may be approved with a total budget but constrained by funding availability over time. Cash flow management ensures that funds are available when commitments must be paid. A project can be within total budget but unable to pay current invoices due to cash flow timing.

- [ ] Understand the funding model: how and when funds are released.
- [ ] Manage cash flow to ensure funds are available for commitments.
- [ ] Align spending pace with funding availability.
- [ ] Anticipate and address cash flow gaps before they cause payment problems.

### [ ] Manage Vendor And Contract Financials

Vendor and contract costs are a significant portion of many project budgets, and they can drift through change orders, scope creep, and rate escalations. Manage vendor financials actively: track committed versus actual costs, control change orders, and verify invoices against contract terms.

- [ ] Track committed versus actual vendor costs.
- [ ] Control change orders through formal approval.
- [ ] Verify invoices against contract terms and delivered work.
- [ ] Watch for scope creep and rate escalation in vendor engagements.

### [ ] Reassess Viability At Major Decision Points

At major milestones or phase gates, reassess whether the project remains financially viable given current costs, expected benefits, and remaining investment. A project that made sense at approval may no longer make sense at phase two if conditions have changed. Be willing to recommend cancellation.

- [ ] Reassess financial viability at phase gates and major milestones.
- [ ] Compare remaining investment to expected benefits under current conditions.
- [ ] Be willing to recommend cancellation when viability has eroded.
- [ ] Avoid continuing by inertia when the financial case no longer holds.

### [ ] Distinguish Capital Versus Operating Expenditures

How costs are classified, capital versus operating, affects financial reporting, tax treatment, and funding sources. Misclassification creates accounting and compliance problems. Understand the classification rules and ensure costs are categorized correctly.

- [ ] Understand the organization's capitalization rules and thresholds.
- [ ] Classify costs correctly as capital or operating.
- [ ] Ensure capitalizable costs are captured and capitalized.
- [ ] Avoid misclassification that creates accounting problems.

### [ ] Manage Financial Risks Proactively

Financial risks, budget cuts, currency fluctuations, vendor failures, cost escalations, can threaten project viability. Identify financial risks in the risk register and manage them with the same discipline as schedule and technical risks.

- [ ] Identify financial risks in the risk register.
- [ ] Develop responses for budget cuts, cost escalations, and vendor failures.
- [ ] Monitor currency, rate, and market risks for relevant projects.
- [ ] Manage financial risks with the same discipline as other risks.

### [ ] Ensure Financial Transparency And Reporting

Stakeholders and funders need transparent financial reporting to maintain confidence and make decisions. Report not just spending against budget but the full financial picture: commitments, forecasts, reserve status, and viability indicators. Opacity breeds distrust; transparency builds confidence.

- [ ] Report the full financial picture, not just spending against budget.
- [ ] Include commitments, forecasts, reserve status, and viability indicators.
- [ ] Disclose financial risks and their potential impact.
- [ ] Provide the information stakeholders need to make funding decisions.

### [ ] Align Project Financials With Portfolio And Organizational Finance

A project's finances do not exist in isolation; they are part of a portfolio and an organizational financial system. Changes in organizational priorities, budget allocations, or financial health affect the project. Stay aligned with portfolio and organizational financial management.

- [ ] Understand the project's place in the organizational portfolio.
- [ ] Respond to changes in organizational priorities and budget allocations.
- [ ] Align project financial reporting with organizational standards.
- [ ] Participate in portfolio financial reviews and rebalancing.

### [ ] Plan For Benefit Measurement After Delivery

Benefit realization extends beyond project closure. Plan how benefits will be measured and reported after the project delivers, and assign ownership for post-delivery benefit tracking. Without post-delivery measurement, the project's financial success is never confirmed.

- [ ] Define post-delivery benefit measurement during the project.
- [ ] Assign ownership for benefit tracking after closure.
- [ ] Establish measurement mechanisms that survive project closure.
- [ ] Report benefit realization to inform future investment decisions.

## Common Traps

### [ ] Business Case Settled At Approval

Treating the business case as fixed and never reassessing viability during execution.

### [ ] No Benefit Tracking

Delivering on time and on budget without confirming that benefits materialized.

### [ ] Ignoring Cash Flow

Being within total budget but unable to meet current payment commitments.

### [ ] Unmanaged Vendor Costs

Letting vendor costs drift through uncontrolled change orders and scope creep.

### [ ] Continuing By Inertia

Persisting with a project whose financial viability has eroded.

### [ ] Cost Misclassification

Misclassifying capital and operating costs, creating accounting problems.

### [ ] Financial Risks Unmanaged

Failing to identify and manage budget, currency, and vendor financial risks.

### [ ] Opacity In Reporting

Reporting spending without the full financial picture, eroding stakeholder confidence.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the business case tracked throughout execution, with viability reassessed when assumptions diverge?
- [ ] Are benefits defined, measured, and tracked for realization after delivery?
- [ ] Is cash flow managed to ensure funds are available for commitments?
- [ ] Are vendor and contract financials actively managed against commitments and terms?
- [ ] Is financial viability reassessed at phase gates, with willingness to recommend cancellation?
- [ ] Are costs correctly classified as capital or operating per organizational rules?
- [ ] Are financial risks identified in the risk register and managed proactively?
- [ ] Is financial reporting transparent, covering commitments, forecasts, reserves, and viability?
- [ ] Are project financials aligned with portfolio and organizational financial management?
- [ ] Is post-delivery benefit measurement planned and owned beyond project closure?
