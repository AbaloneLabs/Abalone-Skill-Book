# Solicitation and Source Selection

Source selection is the point at which a project commits to a supplier who will shape its outcome for months or years. The quality of the solicitation determines the quality of the proposals, and the rigor of the evaluation determines whether the selection is defensible and correct. Most source-selection failures are not corruption; they are process failures: vague requirements that produce uncomparable proposals, criteria applied inconsistently, or a selection that cannot be reconstructed from the record.

The judgment problem is how to write a solicitation that elicits comparable, complete proposals, how to evaluate them on a level playing field without bias, and how to make a selection that is both correct and defensible. Agents tend to under-invest in the solicitation, evaluate on price when they said they would evaluate on value, and fail to document the reasoning that would protect the decision from challenge.

## Core Rules

### Write the Solicitation to Produce Comparable Proposals

A good solicitation forces proposers to answer the same questions in the same structure, so that proposals can be compared apples to apples. Define the required proposal sections, the format, the evaluation criteria, and the weights. A solicitation that lets each bidder structure their own response produces proposals that cannot be meaningfully compared and invites selection by impression rather than evidence.

### Separate Mandatory Compliance From Competitive Evaluation

First determine whether each proposal meets mandatory requirements: eligibility, completeness, required forms, minimum technical thresholds. Proposals that fail compliance are removed before competitive scoring. Mixing compliance with scoring muddies both and creates grounds for challenge. Apply compliance checks uniformly and document the result for every proposal.

### Evaluate Against Pre-Defined, Weighted Criteria

The evaluation criteria and their weights must be defined before proposals are received and applied consistently to all. Criteria typically include technical approach, capability and experience, past performance, key personnel, price, and risk. Evaluators score each proposal against each criterion with written justification. Criteria applied after the fact, or weighted differently for different bidders, invalidate the process.

### Use Multiple Independent Evaluators and Reconcile Differences

A single evaluator's bias, fatigue, or preference can skew the result. Use at least two independent evaluators per proposal, require each to document scores and rationale, and reconcile significant disagreements through discussion. Persistent, unexplained divergence is a signal to revisit the criteria or the evaluators. Documented reconciliation makes the evaluation defensible.

### Clarify, Do Not Negotiate, During Evaluation

Bidders may have ambiguities or errors in their proposals. A structured clarification process allows written questions and answers shared with all bidders if the issue is common. Clarification must not introduce new requirements or allow a bidder to materially revise their proposal, which would unfair the process. The line between clarification and re-proposal must be held firmly.

### Document the Selection Rationale to Survive a Challenge

The selection decision must be reconstructable from the record: the solicitation, the proposals, the evaluation scores and rationale, the reconciliation, the clarifications, and the final selection memorandum. A selection that cannot be explained from the documents is vulnerable to protest and may indicate the decision was not actually based on the stated criteria. Write the selection memo as if it will be read by a skeptic.

### Conduct Best-and-Final or Negotiation Only When the Process Allows It

Some processes permit a best-and-final offer round or structured negotiation with the highest-ranked proposer. These must be conducted within the rules of the solicitation and the governing procurement framework. Introducing negotiation where the process promised award on submitted proposals is a process violation. Confirm what the solicitation permitted before initiating any post-submission interaction.

### Debrief Unsuccessful Bidders to Improve Future Solicitations

A structured debrief to unsuccessful bidders, focused on the strengths and weaknesses of their proposal relative to the criteria, serves two purposes: it reduces the likelihood of uninformed protests, and it improves the quality of future proposals. Debriefs must not disclose other bidders' confidential information or the internal scoring deliberations beyond the outcome.

## Common Traps

### Vague Solicitation Producing Uncomparable Proposals

The RFP is loosely written, so each bidder responds in their own structure with their own assumptions. Comparison becomes subjective and the selection is indefensible. The trap is treating the solicitation as a formality. Invest in structure and clarity up front.

### Saying Value, Scoring Price

The criteria say technical and past performance matter, but the selection goes to the lowest price. The trap is that price is easy to rank and quality is hard. If price will dominate, say so in the weights; do not pretend otherwise.

### Single-Evaluator Bias

One person scores all proposals, and their preferences, good or bad, determine the outcome. The trap is efficiency at the cost of defensibility. Use independent evaluators and reconcile.

### Clarification That Becomes Re-Proposal

A bidder is allowed to "clarify" their proposal by substantially changing it, gaining an unfair advantage. The trap is the desire to get the best proposal rather than the fairest process. Hold the line on clarification scope.

### Undocumented Selection Rationale

The memo says "Bidder X offered the best value" with no supporting analysis. The trap is assuming the reasoning is obvious. A skeptic, or an auditor, will not infer it; write it out.

### Negotiation Introduced Where Not Permitted

The process promised award on submitted proposals, but the team opens negotiation with the favorite. The trap is pursuing a better deal at the cost of process integrity. Confirm what was permitted before any post-award interaction.

### No Debrief, Inviting Protest

Unsuccessful bidders receive no explanation and assume favoritism, prompting protests. The trap is treating debriefs as optional courtesy. They are a process safeguard.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the solicitation prescribe proposal structure, sections, and format so proposals are comparable?
- [ ] Are mandatory compliance checks applied uniformly and documented for every proposal before competitive scoring?
- [ ] Were evaluation criteria and weights defined before proposal receipt and applied consistently to all bidders?
- [ ] Are at least two independent evaluators used per proposal, with documented reconciliation of significant disagreements?
- [ ] Is clarification held to its proper scope, with no bidder allowed to materially revise a proposal through clarification?
- [ ] Can the selection decision be fully reconstructed from the solicitation, proposals, evaluations, reconciliations, clarifications, and selection memo?
- [ ] Is any best-and-final or negotiation round conducted strictly within what the solicitation and governing framework permitted?
- [ ] Are unsuccessful bidders offered a structured debrief focused on their proposal's strengths and weaknesses against the criteria?
- [ ] Does the selection memo explain, with evidence, why the winner scored highest against each criterion?
- [ ] Would an independent reviewer, reading only the record, reach the same selection?
