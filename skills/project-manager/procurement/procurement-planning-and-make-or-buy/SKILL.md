# Procurement Planning and Make-or-Buy

Procurement planning is the decision that determines a large fraction of a project's eventual cost, risk, and schedule exposure, yet it is often treated as a paperwork step that happens after the "real" planning. The decisions made here, what to buy, from whom, under what contract type, and with what requirements, lock in constraints that are extremely expensive to change later. A poorly scoped statement of work or a mismatched contract type will haunt the project through every status report and every dispute.

The judgment problem is not "how do I write a purchase order." It is how to decide what should be done internally versus externally, how to define requirements precisely enough to be enforceable but not so tightly that they foreclose good solutions, and how to align contract structure with the project's risk and incentive profile. Agents tend to default to whatever procurement path is familiar, under-specify the work, and pick contract types that misalign risk between buyer and seller.

## Core Rules

### Decide Make-or-Buy on Capability, Capacity, Cost, and Risk

The make-or-buy decision should weigh four factors, not just unit cost: whether the organization has the capability to do the work well, whether it has the capacity without displacing higher-value work, whether the total cost of ownership (including management overhead) favors buying, and whether the risk of external dependency is acceptable. A decision based only on headline cost ignores the integration, coordination, and dependency risks that external sourcing introduces. Document the reasoning so the decision can be revisited if conditions change.

### Define Requirements as Outcomes, Not Prescriptions, Where Possible

A statement of work should describe what the deliverable must do and the acceptance criteria, leaving room for the seller to propose how. Over-prescribing the method forecloses innovation and shifts solution risk to the buyer, who then owns any method failure. Under-specifying outcomes, however, leaves the seller free to deliver the letter of the contract while missing the intent. Find the level of specificity that defines success clearly without dictating approach.

### Match Contract Type to Risk Allocation and Certainty

Contract type is a risk-allocation tool. Fixed-price contracts place cost risk on the seller and suit well-defined work; cost-reimbursable contracts place cost risk on the buyer and suit uncertain or evolving work; time-and-materials suits work whose scope is hard to predict. Choosing fixed-price for ill-defined work to "transfer risk" usually produces disputes, change orders, and quality corners. Match the contract type to the actual certainty of the scope and the party best able to manage each risk.

### Plan Procurement on the Critical Path, Not the Calendar

Procurement lead times, especially for specialized goods or services, often determine the project's earliest possible completion. Procurement planning must be integrated with schedule planning, not run in parallel and merged later. Identify long-lead items early and sequence their acquisition before the schedule is frozen. A schedule that assumes instant materialization of procured items is fiction.

### Build the Evaluation Criteria Before Issuing, Not After Receiving

The criteria by which proposals will be evaluated, and their relative weights, must be defined before solicitation. Defining criteria after seeing proposals invites unconscious bias toward a favored bidder and undermines defensibility. Criteria should cover technical approach, capability, past performance, price, and risk, with weights that reflect what actually matters to project success, not just lowest cost.

### Include Total Cost of Ownership, Not Just Acquisition Price

The purchase price is a fraction of the lifetime cost. Integration, training, maintenance, support, licensing, decommissioning, and the cost of managing the vendor relationship all contribute. A low-bid selection that ignores these often costs more over the project life than a higher-priced, better-fitting option. Require total cost analysis for significant procurements.

### Plan for Contract Administration From the Start

Procurement does not end at award. Someone must administer the contract: monitor performance, approve deliverables, manage changes, process payments, and enforce terms. Under-resourcing contract administration is a common failure that lets vendor problems fester until they become project problems. Identify the contract administrator and the effort required during planning, not after award.

### Align Procurement With Regulatory and Policy Constraints

Public-sector, regulated-industry, and enterprise-governance environments impose procurement rules: competitive thresholds, approved-vendor lists, diversity requirements, and documentation standards. These are not optional. Planning that ignores them produces disallowed procurements, audit findings, and forced rework. Confirm the applicable constraints before defining the acquisition strategy.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to Familiar Vendors Without Competition

Routing procurements to known vendors feels faster and safer, but it forgoes price discipline, misses better options, and can violate competitive requirements. The trap is mistaking convenience for good procurement. Even when sole-source is justified, document the justification.

### Fixed-Price for Ill-Defined Work to "Transfer Risk"

Buyers choose fixed-price hoping the seller absorbs scope uncertainty. Sellers respond by padding the price, cutting corners, or litigating scope. The trap is that risk cannot be transferred away from undefined scope; it just surfaces as disputes. Use the right contract type or define the scope first.

### Over-Prescriptive SOW That Owns the Method

The SOW dictates exactly how the work must be done, so when the method fails, the buyer owns the failure because they specified it. The trap is confusing detail with control. Specify outcomes and acceptance criteria; let the seller own the method.

### Ignoring Long-Lead Procurement Items

The schedule is built assuming parts arrive on time, but a critical long-lead item has a six-month lead time that was never checked. The trap is treating procurement as a downstream activity. Identify and sequence long-lead items during schedule planning.

### Lowest-Price Selection Ignoring Total Cost and Risk

The lowest bid wins, then integration overruns, support costs, and quality failures make it the most expensive option. The trap is that price is easy to compare and quality is hard. Use weighted criteria that include past performance and total cost.

### No Contract Administration Plan

The contract is awarded and then neglected, because no one was assigned to administer it. Vendor slippage goes unnoticed until it affects the critical path. The trap is assuming the contract manages itself. Assign administration explicitly.

### Misaligned Incentives in the Contract Structure

The contract pays for activity rather than outcome, or penalizes the wrong behavior, so the vendor optimizes for what is measured rather than what is wanted. The trap is that incentives drive behavior more than goodwill. Align payment and performance terms with desired outcomes.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Was the make-or-buy decision based on capability, capacity, total cost, and risk, with reasoning documented?
- [ ] Does the statement of work define outcomes and acceptance criteria without over-prescribing the method?
- [ ] Is the contract type matched to the certainty of the scope and the party best able to manage each risk?
- [ ] Are long-lead procurement items identified and sequenced before the schedule is frozen?
- [ ] Were evaluation criteria and weights defined before solicitation, not after proposals arrived?
- [ ] Does the selection consider total cost of ownership, not just acquisition price?
- [ ] Is a contract administrator identified and the administration effort planned from the start?
- [ ] Are applicable regulatory, competitive, and governance constraints confirmed and reflected in the strategy?
- [ ] Are contract incentives and payment terms aligned with desired outcomes rather than activity?
- [ ] Can the procurement decision be defended against a challenge that a better or cheaper option was overlooked?
