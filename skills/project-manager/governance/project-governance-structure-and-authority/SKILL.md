# Project Governance Structure and Authority

Governance is the framework of authority, accountability, and oversight that directs a project. It is not bureaucracy added on top of management; it is the structure that determines who can decide what, who is accountable for outcomes, and how the project connects to the organization that funds and depends on it. Weak governance produces projects with unclear authority, decisions that no one owns, and oversight that is either absent or suffocating. The most common governance failure is not too little governance but misaligned governance: structures that concentrate authority in the wrong place, create approval bottlenecks, or sever the link between authority and accountability.

The judgment problem is how to design a governance structure that provides real oversight without paralyzing execution, how to align decision authority with accountability, and how to connect the project to the organization's strategic direction. Agents tend to default to whatever governance template is handy, create committees without clear authority, and confuse governance with status reporting.

## Core Rules

### Align Decision Authority With Accountability for Outcomes

Authority and accountability must travel together. A steering committee that can override project decisions but is not accountable for the outcome produces decisions without consequence. A project manager who is accountable but lacks authority to make decisions is set up to fail. Map each significant decision type to the role that has both the authority to decide and the accountability for the result. Where these are separated, governance fails: either decisions are unaccountable, or accountable people cannot decide.

### Design Governance Weight to Project Risk and Scale

Governance must match the project's risk, scale, and strategic importance. A small internal project needs light governance; a major strategic program needs robust oversight. Over-governance on a small project creates cost and delay without value; under-governance on a major program allows problems to compound unobserved. Scale the governance structure, meeting cadence, and reporting depth to the consequence of being wrong. Define the governance weight explicitly and revisit it as the project evolves.

### Define Decision Rights Explicitly by Decision Type

Ambiguity about who can decide what produces either paralysis, where no one decides, or conflict, where multiple parties claim the decision. Define decision rights by type: scope changes, budget changes, schedule changes, risk acceptance, resource reallocation, and quality trade-offs. For each, state who decides, who must be consulted, and who must be informed. Decision rights documented in advance prevent the case-by-case negotiation that slows projects and erodes authority.

### Make the Steering Committee a Decision Body, Not a Report Recipient

A steering committee that only receives reports adds no value; its members' time is consumed listening to status they could read. A steering committee earns its existence by making decisions the project manager cannot make alone: resolving cross-project conflicts, committing resources, accepting strategic risks, and authorizing major changes. Design the agenda around decisions, not around status. If the committee has no decisions to make, either the governance is misaligned or the project manager is not escalating what they should.

### Establish Clear Escalation Paths and Criteria

Escalation should not be an act of desperation; it should be a defined path with criteria. Define what issues, risks, or decisions escalate, to whom, and within what timeframe. Escalation criteria based on thresholds (cost variance, schedule slip, risk classification) remove the ambiguity and stigma that prevent timely escalation. A project with no escalation path accumulates problems until they become crises; one with a clear path resolves them while they are manageable.

### Separate Governance From Management

Governance is the oversight and direction of the project; management is the execution. The steering committee governs; the project manager manages. Confusing the two produces either a committee that micromanages execution, undermining the project manager, or a project manager who makes governance decisions, undermining oversight. Define the boundary clearly: governance decides direction and constraints; management executes within them. When the boundary blurs, both functions degrade.

### Connect the Project to Strategic Direction Through Governance

Governance is how the project stays connected to the organization's strategy. The steering committee should include representation from the strategic ownership of the project's outcome, so that strategic shifts are reflected in project direction and so that the project's progress informs strategic decisions. A project whose governance is disconnected from strategy delivers something that may no longer be strategically relevant. Build the strategic connection into the governance structure.

### Ensure Governance Is Visible and Understood by the Team

Governance that is invisible to the team produces confusion about authority and decisions that are challenged or circumvented. The governance structure, decision rights, and escalation paths should be documented and communicated to the team. People need to know who decides what, how to escalate, and what governance expects of them. Invisible governance is governance that does not function.

## Common Traps

### Authority Without Accountability

A committee can override decisions but is not accountable for the outcome, producing unaccountable decisions. The trap is that authority feels like oversight. Align authority with accountability.

### One-Size Governance

The same governance template is applied regardless of project scale, over-governing small projects and under-governing large ones. The trap is that uniformity feels fair. Scale to risk and size.

### Ambiguous Decision Rights

No one is sure who can decide what, producing paralysis or conflict. The trap is that roles feel clear enough. Define decision rights by type.

### Steering Committee as Status Audience

The committee meets to hear reports, makes no decisions, and adds no value. The trap is that meeting feels like governing. Design for decisions.

### No Clear Escalation Path

Problems accumulate because no one knows how or when to escalate, until they become crises. The trap is that escalation feels like failure. Define paths and criteria.

### Governance Micromanaging Execution

The committee delves into execution detail, undermining the project manager. The trap is that detail feels like engagement. Separate governance from management.

### Disconnected From Strategy

The project is governed without connection to strategic direction, and delivers something no longer relevant. The trap is that the project feels self-contained. Connect through governance.

### Invisible Governance

The team does not understand the governance structure and circumvents or challenges it. The trap is that governance feels like a committee concern. Make it visible.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is decision authority aligned with accountability for outcomes for each significant decision type?
- [ ] Is the governance weight, structure, and cadence scaled to the project's risk, scale, and strategic importance?
- [ ] Are decision rights defined explicitly by type (scope, budget, schedule, risk, resources, quality), with decide, consult, and inform roles?
- [ ] Is the steering committee designed as a decision body with decision-oriented agendas, not a status report audience?
- [ ] Are escalation paths and criteria defined, threshold-based, and free of stigma?
- [ ] Is governance (oversight and direction) clearly separated from management (execution)?
- [ ] Does the governance structure connect the project to the strategic ownership of its outcome?
- [ ] Is the governance structure, decision rights, and escalation paths documented and understood by the team?
- [ ] Would the team know who has authority to decide a given issue and how to escalate if needed?
- [ ] Does the governance produce decisions and direction, or merely meetings and reports?
