# Resource Planning And Allocation

Resources, whether people, equipment, facilities, or budget, are finite and shared. A plan that assigns the same specialist to three concurrent critical tasks, or that assumes equipment is infinitely available, will collapse on contact with reality. Resource planning is the discipline of matching demand to capacity honestly, sequencing work to fit real availability, and surfacing the tradeoffs when demand exceeds supply. The project manager must plan resources against capacity, not against wishful schedules, and must protect the scarce resources that determine whether the project can move.

Use this skill before allocating resources, building a resource plan or calendar, balancing competing demands, or diagnosing why work keeps stalling despite apparent staffing. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a resource plan that looks staffed but is actually overallocated and unexecutable.

## Core Rules

### Plan Against Real Capacity, Not Theoretical Availability

Capacity is not the same as existence. A person exists on the team, but their capacity to do project work is reduced by other projects, support duties, meetings, leave, holidays, and administrative load.

Build the resource plan against realistic capacity. Apply utilization factors that reflect actual availability rather than assuming every person is fully dedicated. Calibrate against historical actuals where possible, because optimism about availability is a recurring source of schedule failure.

### Identify And Protect Bottleneck Resources

Most projects have one or more bottleneck resources: a scarce specialist, a shared piece of equipment, a licensed tool, or a facility with limited slots. The throughput of the project is constrained by these bottlenecks, and scheduling around them determines the real pace.

Identify the bottleneck resources early. Schedule their work first, protect their time from low-priority demands, and avoid letting non-critical work consume their capacity. Improving throughput anywhere except the bottleneck does not improve the project.

### Build A Resource Calendar

A resource calendar captures when each resource is available, including working days, holidays, leave, maintenance windows, and shared-use constraints. It is the foundation for honest allocation.

Without a resource calendar, allocations assume continuous availability and break when reality intervenes. Maintain the calendar as conditions change, because leave, illness, and maintenance are not exceptions but expected variation.

### Use Resource Leveling And Smoothing Deliberately

Resource leveling resolves overallocation by delaying work until resources are free, which may extend the schedule and change the critical path. Resource smoothing adjusts work within available float to reduce peaks without changing the end date.

Choose the technique that fits the constraint. If the binding constraint is the end date, smooth within float and accept some peak load. If the binding constraint is resource capacity, level honestly and accept the schedule impact. Pretending overallocation does not exist produces a plan that cannot be executed.

### Sequence To Fit Availability

When a scarce resource is available only in a window, sequence the work that depends on it into that window. When a specialist supports multiple projects, coordinate their allocation across projects rather than each project manager independently assuming full availability.

Cross-project resource coordination is often the difference between a plan that works and one that collapses. Engage the resource managers and other project managers who share the resource.

### Surface Demand-Supply Gaps As Decisions

When resource demand exceeds supply, the gap is not a problem to absorb silently. It is a decision about scope, schedule, cost, or risk. Surface the gap with options: add resources, extend the schedule, reduce scope, accept quality risk, or reallocate from lower-priority work.

Presenting the gap as a decision forces the tradeoff into the open rather than letting it manifest as burnout, slippage, and quality defects.

### Track Utilization And Reallocations

Resource plans drift. Track actual utilization against the plan, watch for emerging overallocation and underallocation, and reallocate as priorities and capacity shift.

Underallocation is not always good news. It can indicate blocked work, missing dependencies, or scope that has not been decomposed. Investigate idle capacity as well as overloaded capacity.

### Account For Onboarding And Ramp-Up

New resources do not contribute at full capacity immediately. They need onboarding, context, and time to become productive. Plans that assume instant productivity from a newly added person overstate near-term capacity.

Build ramp-up into the plan. Adding resources to recover a schedule often produces a short-term dip before any gain, because onboarding consumes the time of existing team members.

## Common Traps

### Theoretical Availability Over Real Capacity

Assuming full dedication ignores the load that reduces real capacity.

### Ignoring Bottlenecks

Scheduling as if every resource is equally available hides the constraint that actually determines pace.

### No Resource Calendar

Allocations without a calendar break on the first holiday or leave.

### Hidden Overallocation

Plans that look staffed but assign one person to more hours than exist cannot be executed.

### Silent Absorption Of Demand-Supply Gaps

Absorbing overload through burnout produces slippage, defects, and attrition rather than resolution.

### Cross-Project Double Booking

Independently assuming a shared resource produces conflicts that surface as delays.

### Instant Productivity Assumption

Treating new resources as fully productive immediately overstates near-term capacity.

### Ignoring Underallocation

Idle capacity may signal blocked work or missing decomposition, not surplus.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The resource plan is built against realistic capacity with utilization factors, not theoretical full availability.
- [ ] Bottleneck resources are identified, scheduled first, and protected from non-critical demands.
- [ ] A resource calendar captures availability, holidays, leave, maintenance, and shared-use constraints.
- [ ] Resource leveling or smoothing is applied deliberately based on whether the binding constraint is capacity or end date.
- [ ] Work is sequenced to fit the availability windows of scarce resources.
- [ ] Shared resources are coordinated across projects rather than independently double-booked.
- [ ] Demand-supply gaps are surfaced as decisions with options rather than absorbed silently.
- [ ] Actual utilization is tracked against the plan and reallocations are made as conditions change.
- [ ] Onboarding and ramp-up time are built into plans for new resources.
- [ ] Underallocation is investigated as a possible signal of blocked work, not only treated as surplus.
