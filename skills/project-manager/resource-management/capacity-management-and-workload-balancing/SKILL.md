# Capacity Management And Workload Balancing

Capacity is the throughput a team can sustain over time, not the burst it can produce for a week. When workload is unbalanced, some people are overloaded toward burnout while others are underused, and the team's actual throughput falls below its potential because the overloaded become bottlenecks and the underused cannot help. Managing capacity means understanding real sustainable throughput, distributing work to match it, and protecting the team from the peaks that produce defects, attrition, and collapse.

Use this skill before balancing workload, assessing capacity, smoothing peaks, or diagnosing why a team that looks fully staffed is underperforming. The goal is to prevent the agent from confusing busyness with throughput and from allowing overload to degrade quality and retention.

## Core Rules

### Understand Sustainable Throughput, Not Peak Output

Sustainable throughput is the work a team can deliver week after week without degrading. Peak output is the burst possible under pressure for a short period. Plans built on peak output fail because peaks cannot be sustained.

Establish sustainable throughput from historical data where possible: how much work has the team reliably completed over time? Use this as the planning baseline, and treat bursts as a reserve for genuine emergencies, not as the normal operating rate.

### Balance Workload To Match Capacity And Capability

Workload balancing distributes work so that no individual is overallocated and so that work goes to people with the right capability. Imbalance creates overloaded bottlenecks and idle capacity simultaneously.

Assess each person's load against their real capacity and the work's skill requirements. Reassign work from overloaded to available people where capability allows. Where it does not, the skill gap is a capacity issue to address through training, pairing, or hiring, not a reason to keep overloading the specialist.

### Identify And Relieve Bottlenecks

In any team, the slowest constrained resource determines throughput. If a specialist, a reviewer, or an environment is the bottleneck, adding capacity elsewhere does not help.

Identify the bottleneck, then relieve it: offload non-critical work from the bottleneck resource, add capacity to the bottleneck through training or hiring, automate or simplify the bottleneck step, or sequence work to keep the bottleneck continuously fed. Throughput improves only when the bottleneck improves.

### Smooth Peaks To Protect Quality And People

Peaks, periods where demand spikes above sustainable capacity, produce defects, fatigue, and attrition. Smoothing spreads the peak across time or across people to keep load closer to sustainable.

Smoothing techniques include pulling work forward where dependencies allow, deferring non-critical work, parallelizing across more people where the work permits, and using float in the schedule. Smoothing that changes the end date is leveling; smoothing within float preserves it.

### Protect Focus Time And Reduce Context Switching

Context switching, juggling many tasks or projects at once, dramatically reduces effective throughput. A person split across five tasks produces less than the sum of their capacity because of switching overhead.

Protect blocks of focus time, limit work in progress per person, and group similar work to reduce switching. Throughput often improves more from reducing switching than from adding hours.

### Watch For Burnout Signals

Burnout is the endpoint of sustained overload, and it arrives after the warning signs have been visible for some time. Signals include declining quality, increased cynicism, withdrawal, rising sick time, and attrition risk.

Treat these signals as capacity warnings, not attitude problems. Investigate the load and the environment causing them, and act before burnout becomes departure. A person lost to burnout takes their knowledge and their replacement cost with them.

### Use Historical Data To Forecast Capacity

Capacity forecasts based on hope repeat past mistakes. Use historical velocity, completion rates, and utilization data to forecast what the team can realistically deliver.

When conditions change, such as team size, skill mix, or parallel demands, adjust the forecast and explain the basis. A capacity forecast that cannot be traced to evidence is a guess presented as a plan.

### Distinguish Utilization From Effectiveness

High utilization, keeping everyone busy, is not the same as high effectiveness, delivering valuable outcomes. A team can be fully utilized on low-value or blocked work and still deliver little.

Balance utilization against effectiveness. Some slack is healthy because it creates capacity for improvement, problem-solving, and absorbing variation. A team scheduled to 100 percent utilization has no resilience.

## Common Traps

### Planning On Peak Output

Basing plans on burst capacity guarantees eventual collapse.

### Tolerating Overload As Dedication

Treating overload as commitment hides the burnout and quality risk it creates.

### Ignoring The Bottleneck

Adding capacity anywhere except the bottleneck does not improve throughput.

### High Utilization Mistaken For Effectiveness

A fully busy team may be busy on low-value or blocked work.

### Excessive Context Switching

Splitting people across many tasks reduces effective throughput through switching overhead.

### No Slack

Scheduling to 100 percent removes resilience and the capacity to absorb variation.

### Hope-Based Capacity Forecasts

Forecasts without historical basis repeat the same over-optimistic errors.

### Missing Burnout Signals

Dismissing early warning signs as attitude problems leads to attrition.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Plans are based on sustainable throughput from historical data, not peak burst output.
- [ ] Workload is balanced so no individual is overallocated and work matches capability.
- [ ] The team bottleneck is identified and relieved rather than ignored.
- [ ] Peaks are smoothed through pulling work forward, deferring non-critical work, or parallelizing.
- [ ] Focus time is protected and work in progress is limited to reduce context switching.
- [ ] Burnout signals are treated as capacity warnings and acted upon before attrition.
- [ ] Capacity forecasts are grounded in historical data and adjusted when conditions change.
- [ ] Utilization is balanced against effectiveness, with healthy slack preserved.
- [ ] Skill gaps causing overload are addressed through training, pairing, or hiring.
- [ ] Idle capacity is investigated for blocked work, not assumed to be surplus.
