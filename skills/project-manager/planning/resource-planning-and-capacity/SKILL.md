# Resource Planning And Capacity

Resource planning is the discipline of matching the work that needs to be done to the people, equipment, and budget available to do it, and it is where many projects fail before they begin. The most common failure mode is optimistic resource assumption: planning as if the assigned people will be fully dedicated, fully productive, and uninterrupted, when in reality they are shared across projects, subject to meetings and overhead, subject to leave and turnover, and limited by the bottleneck resource that constrains the whole project. A plan built on unrealistic resource assumptions is a plan that will miss, and the miss will be blamed on execution rather than on the resource fiction it was built on. The judgment problem is planning resources realistically, identifying and managing constraints, negotiating for what the project actually needs, and balancing workloads so that the team can execute sustainably rather than burning out or sitting idle.

Use this skill when planning project resources, allocating people across work, resolving constraints and bottlenecks, negotiating for resources, managing workloads, or diagnosing resource-driven performance issues. The goal is to prevent the agent from planning with optimistic resource assumptions, from ignoring the bottleneck resource, from over-allocating people to burnout, and from treating resource planning as a paperwork exercise rather than the constraint analysis it actually is.

## Core Rules

### Plan Resources Based On Realistic Availability, Not Headcount

Headcount is not capacity. Ten people assigned to a project do not provide ten full-time equivalents of work, because people are shared across projects, spend time in meetings and overhead, take leave, and have productivity variation. Planning based on headcount rather than realistic availability produces plans that assume more capacity than exists.

Plan realistically by:

- accounting for allocation percentages, a person assigned half-time provides half capacity;
- deducting overhead, meetings, email, administrative time, from productive capacity;
- accounting for time off, holidays, and predictable absences;
- recognizing learning curves for new team members or new domains;
- distinguishing nominal allocation from effective capacity.

A team of ten at varying allocations and overhead may provide six effective full-time equivalents, and the plan must reflect that.

### Identify And Plan Around The Bottleneck Resource

Every project has a bottleneck resource, the person, skill, equipment, or asset whose capacity limits the whole project's throughput. The bottleneck may be a specialist whose expertise is required across components, a piece of shared equipment, or an approval authority. Work expands to fill and wait on the bottleneck, and optimizing non-bottleneck resources while the bottleneck is constrained produces no improvement.

Identify and manage by:

- identifying which resource, by skill or asset, constrains the project's throughput;
- scheduling the bottleneck resource first and protecting its time;
- avoiding loading non-bottleneck resources in ways that create more work for the bottleneck;
- increasing bottleneck capacity, through additional people, training, or equipment, where possible;
- recognizing that improvements anywhere but the bottleneck do not speed the project.

A project's duration is set by its bottleneck. Planning that ignores the bottleneck optimizes the wrong things.

### Negotiate For Resources Based On Project Needs, Not Wishful Thinking

Project managers rarely control their resources directly; they negotiate with functional managers, resource owners, and stakeholders for the people and assets the project needs. Negotiating effectively requires clarity about what the project actually needs, the consequences of not getting it, and the tradeoffs available. Wishful thinking, hoping the resources will materialize, is not a strategy.

Negotiate by:

- being specific about what resources, with what skills and allocation, the project needs and when;
- explaining the consequences of resource gaps, delayed milestones, reduced scope, increased risk;
- offering tradeoffs, scope, schedule, or budget flexibility in exchange for resource commitments;
- documenting resource agreements so they are enforceable;
- escalating unresolved resource gaps rather than absorbing them into an unachievable plan.

A project plan that depends on resources not actually committed is a plan waiting to fail.

### Balance Workload To Avoid Burnout And Idle Time

Workload imbalance harms the project either way. Over-allocated people burn out, producing errors, turnover, and diminished productivity that costs more than the apparent extra hours gained. Under-allocated people idle, wasting capacity that could advance the project. Balancing workload across the team and over time sustains productivity.

Balance by:

- tracking individual allocations across all projects, not just this one, to detect over-allocation;
- avoiding sustained overtime, which produces diminishing returns and burnout;
- smoothing workload over the project timeline rather than concentrating it in crunches;
- cross-training to reduce dependency on single individuals and enable load balancing;
- recognizing that burned-out people leave, and turnover costs dwarf any short-term gain.

A plan that depends on sustained over-allocation is borrowing productivity from the future at high interest.

### Account For Shared Resources And Multi-Project Constraints

Resources are rarely dedicated to a single project. People, equipment, and facilities are shared across projects, and shared resources introduce dependencies and contention that single-project planning misses. A resource available to your project may be claimed by another, and the conflict may not surface until it delays your work.

Account for shared resources by:

- identifying resources that are shared and the other projects competing for them;
- coordinating with other project managers and resource owners to manage contention;
- building in contingency for shared resource delays;
- recognizing that a shared resource's commitment to your project competes with others' commitments.

A resource shared across projects has capacity allocated across all of them, not available fully to any one.

### Plan For Skill Mix, Not Just Headcount

Having enough people is not the same as having the right people. A project needs a specific skill mix, and a headcount that lacks a critical skill creates a bottleneck at that skill regardless of total capacity. Planning for headcount without skill mix produces a team that is numerically sufficient and skill-constrained.

Plan skill mix by:

- identifying the specific skills the project requires and when;
- ensuring the team has those skills, through assignment, training, or contracting;
- recognizing single points of failure where only one person has a critical skill;
- planning for skill development where the team must grow into the work;
- avoiding the assumption that any developer, analyst, or designer can do any task.

A team of ten generalists cannot complete work that requires a specialist no one on the team is.

### Manage Resource Risks Proactively

Resources carry risks: key people may leave, become ill, or be reassigned; equipment may fail; budgets may be cut. These risks, unmanaged, derail projects that were otherwise well-planned. Resource risk management identifies and mitigates these risks before they become problems.

Manage risks by:

- identifying single points of failure, key people or resources whose loss would block the project;
- cross-training and documentation to reduce dependency on individuals;
- having backup resource plans for critical roles and assets;
- monitoring indicators of resource risk, such as burnout signals or budget pressure;
- not assuming key resources will remain available throughout the project.

A project that depends on an irreplaceable person is one resignation away from crisis.

### Replan Resources As Conditions Change

Resource plans are not static. People leave, scopes change, priorities shift, and new constraints emerge. A resource plan created at project start and never revised becomes increasingly disconnected from reality. Regular replanning keeps the resource plan aligned with actual conditions.

Replan by:

- reviewing resource allocation regularly against actual availability and progress;
- adjusting the plan when resources change, through loss, reallocation, or new constraints;
- communicating resource changes and their impacts to stakeholders;
- treating the resource plan as a living document, not a one-time artifact.

A resource plan that does not change over a long project is a resource plan that has stopped reflecting reality.

## Common Traps

### Headcount Equals Capacity Assumption

Headcount overstates capacity. Plan based on realistic availability with allocations and overhead deducted.

### Ignoring The Bottleneck Resource

The bottleneck sets project duration. Identify and manage it; optimizing elsewhere does not help.

### Wishful Resource Assumptions

Hoping resources materialize is not planning. Negotiate, document, and escalate for commitments.

### Sustained Over-Allocation And Burnout

Crunch-based plans borrow future productivity at high cost. Balance workload sustainably.

### Single-Project Planning Ignoring Shared Resources

Shared resources introduce contention across projects. Coordinate and plan for shared resource delays.

### Right Headcount, Wrong Skill Mix

Numerical sufficiency without required skills creates skill bottlenecks. Plan for skill mix.

### No Backup For Critical Resources

Single points of failure create fragility. Cross-train, document, and plan backups.

### Static Resource Plans

Conditions change. Replan resources regularly to stay aligned with reality.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Resource plans are based on realistic availability, with allocations, overhead, and absences deducted from headcount.
- [ ] The bottleneck resource has been identified and is scheduled and protected.
- [ ] Resources have been negotiated based on project needs with documented commitments, not wishful assumptions.
- [ ] Workload is balanced to avoid burnout and idle time, without sustained over-allocation.
- [ ] Shared resources and multi-project contention are accounted for in the plan.
- [ ] The skill mix, not just headcount, matches the project's requirements.
- [ ] Resource risks, including single points of failure, are identified and mitigated.
- [ ] The resource plan is revisited and revised as conditions change.
- [ ] No plan depends on resources that have not been actually committed.
- [ ] Tradeoffs among scope, schedule, and resources are explicit rather than absorbed into unrealistic plans.
