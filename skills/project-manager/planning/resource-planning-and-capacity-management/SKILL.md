# Resource Planning And Capacity Management

A project plan is only as realistic as the resources available to execute it. A schedule built without regard to who is available, what skills they have, and how much they can do produces optimistic plans that collapse on contact with reality. Resource planning matches the work to the people, and capacity management ensures the plan does not demand more than the team can deliver. The judgment problem is that resources are shared, finite, and often managed by people outside the project, and ignoring these constraints produces plans that look feasible and fail in execution. The most common failure is planning work without checking whether the people to do it exist, are available, and have the capacity, then discovering overload when it is too late to recover. The skill is planning resources and capacity honestly so that the schedule is executable rather than aspirational.

Use this skill before planning resource allocation, before committing to a schedule, before balancing workloads, or when a project is resource-constrained or over-allocated. The goal is to prevent the agent from planning in a resource vacuum, from over-allocating individuals, or from treating shared and scarce resources as infinitely available.

## Core Rules

### [ ] Plan Resources Before Committing The Schedule

The schedule must be built on resource reality, not the reverse. Committing dates before confirming who is available and how much capacity they have produces plans that cannot be met. Resource planning is a precondition for credible scheduling, not an afterthought.

- [ ] Identify the roles and skills the work requires before setting dates.
- [ ] Confirm availability of the people who will do the work.
- [ ] Build the schedule around confirmed capacity, not wishful assignments.
- [ ] Revise the schedule when resource reality conflicts with it.

### [ ] Identify And Secure Critical And Scarce Resources

Most projects depend on a few critical resources, specialists, shared equipment, decision-makers, whose scarcity constrains the whole. Identifying these early and securing their availability prevents the bottleneck that delays everything. Critical resources must be managed proactively, not discovered late.

- [ ] Identify the resources that are critical, scarce, or shared.
- [ ] Secure their commitment and availability early.
- [ ] Plan work around their constraints, not against them.
- [ ] Have contingency for the loss or unavailability of critical resources.

### [ ] Balance Workloads To Avoid Over-And Under-Allocation

Over-allocation burns people out and guarantees slippage; under-allocation wastes capacity and signals poor planning. Balancing workloads so that each person is fully but not over-utilized sustains throughput and morale. Balance must account for the reality that people are not 100% productive on project work.

- [ ] Track each person's allocation across all work, not just this project.
- [ ] Avoid sustained over-allocation that guarantees burnout and slippage.
- [ ] Use realistic productivity assumptions, not 100% utilization.
- [ ] Rebalance work when allocation becomes uneven.

### [ ] Account For Shared And Matrix Resources

In matrix organizations, resources are shared across projects and reporting lines, and their availability is governed by managers outside the project. Assuming full access to shared resources that are actually contested produces plans that fail. Shared resources must be negotiated and their availability confirmed.

- [ ] Identify resources shared across projects or reporting lines.
- [ ] Negotiate and confirm their availability with their managers.
- [ ] Account for contention with other demands on their time.
- [ ] Plan around the reality of shared access, not assumed exclusivity.

### [ ] Match Skills To Work Requirements

Having enough people is not enough; the people must have the skills the work requires. Assigning work to people who lack the skills produces delays, rework, and quality problems. Skill matching ensures the right capabilities are applied to the right work.

- [ ] Identify the skills each work package requires.
- [ ] Match available people's skills to the work.
- [ ] Identify and address skill gaps through hiring, training, or reassignment.
- [ ] Avoid assigning critical work to under-skilled resources without support.

### [ ] Plan For Onboarding And Ramp-Up

New resources do not contribute at full capacity immediately; they need onboarding, context, and ramp-up time. Planning as if a newly assigned person produces at full speed from day one overstates near-term capacity. Ramp-up must be built into the plan.

- [ ] Account for onboarding and ramp-up time for new resources.
- [ ] Plan for the productivity curve as people get up to speed.
- [ ] Provide onboarding and context to accelerate ramp-up.
- [ ] Avoid relying on full productivity from day one.

### [ ] Manage Resource Dependencies And Sequencing

Resources create dependencies: if one person or piece of equipment is needed for multiple tasks, those tasks must be sequenced around its availability. Ignoring resource dependencies produces schedule conflicts where the same resource is needed simultaneously. Resource leveling resolves these conflicts.

- [ ] Identify where the same resource is needed across tasks.
- [ ] Sequence tasks to resolve resource conflicts, even if it extends duration.
- [ ] Use resource leveling to produce a feasible schedule.
- [ ] Watch for resource-driven critical paths that differ from task-driven ones.

### [ ] Monitor Allocation And Adjust As Reality Changes

Resource plans are hypotheses that must be tested against reality. People leave, get reassigned, or underperform; scope changes shift the work. Monitoring actual allocation against planned and adjusting prevents resource surprises from becoming project failures.

- [ ] Track actual resource usage against the plan.
- [ ] Detect over-allocation, under-allocation, and skill mismatches early.
- [ ] Adjust allocation and schedule when reality diverges from plan.
- [ ] Escalate resource constraints before they become crises.

### [ ] Plan Contingency For Resource Loss And Variability

Resources are variable: people get sick, leave, or are pulled into emergencies. A plan with no contingency for resource variability is fragile. Building in buffer and cross-training reduces the impact of inevitable resource disruptions.

- [ ] Build schedule buffer for resource variability and absence.
- [ ] Cross-train to reduce dependence on single individuals.
- [ ] Document critical knowledge so it is not lost with a person.
- [ ] Have backup resources identified for critical roles.

### [ ] Align Resource Decisions With Organizational Priorities

Resources are allocated within a portfolio of competing projects, and the project manager must align resource decisions with organizational priorities. Competing for resources without regard to portfolio priorities produces conflict and sub-optimization. Resource negotiation must be grounded in strategic priority.

- [ ] Understand how the project ranks in the organizational portfolio.
- [ ] Negotiate resources based on strategic priority, not just project need.
- [ ] Escalate resource conflicts to portfolio-level decision-making.
- [ ] Accept reallocations that serve higher organizational priorities.

## Common Traps

### [ ] Planning In A Resource Vacuum

Building schedules without confirming who is available and capable.

### [ ] Infinite Resource Assumption

Treating scarce and shared resources as if they are infinitely available.

### [ ] Sustained Over-Allocation

Assigning more than people can sustainably deliver, guaranteeing burnout and slippage.

### [ ] Skill Mismatch

Assigning work to people who lack the skills, producing delays and rework.

### [ ] Ignoring Ramp-Up

Planning full productivity from newly assigned resources on day one.

### [ ] Resource Conflict Ignorance

Sequencing tasks without resolving when the same resource is needed simultaneously.

### [ ] No Resource Contingency

A plan so tight that any absence or loss derails it.

### [ ] Portfolio Blindness

Competing for resources without regard to organizational priorities.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are resources planned and confirmed before the schedule is committed?
- [ ] Are critical and scarce resources identified, secured, and managed proactively?
- [ ] Are workloads balanced to avoid over- and under-allocation with realistic productivity?
- [ ] Are shared and matrix resources negotiated and their availability confirmed?
- [ ] Are skills matched to work requirements, with gaps addressed?
- [ ] Is onboarding and ramp-up time built into resource plans?
- [ ] Are resource dependencies and sequencing resolved through leveling?
- [ ] Is actual allocation monitored and adjusted as reality changes?
- [ ] Is there contingency for resource loss, absence, and variability?
- [ ] Are resource decisions aligned with organizational portfolio priorities?
