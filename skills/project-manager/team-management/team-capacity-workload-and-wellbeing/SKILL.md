# Team Capacity, Workload, And Wellbeing

A project plan that assigns more work than the team can deliver is not a plan; it is a setup for failure. Capacity and workload management ensures that commitments match what the team can realistically sustain, and that the pace is sustainable rather than a sprint to burnout. The judgment problem is that capacity is often estimated optimistically, assuming 100% productive time and no interruptions, and workload is balanced by availability rather than by skill and sustainability. The result is chronic overallocation, multitasking that destroys throughput, quality decline, and burnout that silently erodes the team's ability to deliver. The skill is estimating capacity realistically, balancing workload by the right criteria, and protecting sustainable pace as a project management responsibility.

Use this skill before committing to a schedule or sprint, when assessing whether the team can meet commitments, when workload imbalance appears, or when signs of overload or burnout emerge. The goal is to prevent the agent from overcommitting the team, from assuming ideal productivity, from ignoring skill-based workload allocation, or from treating wellbeing as separate from project performance.

## Core Rules

### [ ] Estimate Capacity Realistically, Not Optimistically

Productive capacity is not the same as available hours. Meetings, communication, administrative work, context switching, and fatigue consume a significant portion of the workday. A common heuristic is that productive capacity is 60-80% of nominal hours. Estimating at 100% guarantees overcommitment.

- [ ] Estimate productive capacity, not nominal available hours.
- [ ] Account for meetings, communication, overhead, and context switching.
- [ ] Use a realistic productivity factor (e.g., 60-80% of nominal hours).
- [ ] Adjust for known absences, holidays, and part-time availability.

### [ ] Balance Workload By Skill And Capability, Not Just Availability

Assigning work to whoever is available, regardless of skill, produces bottlenecks and quality problems. Workload must be balanced by the skills required for each task and each member's capabilities. A team member may be available but unqualified for critical-path work.

- [ ] Match task assignments to members' skills and capabilities.
- [ ] Identify and manage bottlenecks around scarce skills.
- [ ] Avoid assigning work based solely on availability.
- [ ] Develop cross-training to reduce single-person bottlenecks.

### [ ] Limit Multitasking To Preserve Throughput

Multitasking, working on multiple tasks in parallel, destroys throughput due to context switching and rework. A team member juggling four tasks completes all of them later than if they worked sequentially. Limit work-in-progress to maintain flow and throughput.

- [ ] Limit work-in-progress per person to reduce context switching.
- [ ] Sequence work to enable focus rather than parallel juggling.
- [ ] Recognize that multitasking reduces, not increases, throughput.
- [ ] Use WIP limits to expose and manage overallocation.

### [ ] Plan For Sustainable Pace

A pace that cannot be sustained leads to burnout, quality decline, and attrition, all of which reduce long-term throughput. Sustainable pace is a project management responsibility, not a personal preference. Plan workloads that the team can maintain over the project duration.

- [ ] Plan workloads that are sustainable over the project's duration.
- [ ] Avoid sustained overtime, which reduces quality and causes burnout.
- [ ] Build in recovery time after intense periods.
- [ ] Treat sustainable pace as a delivery commitment, not a perk.

### [ ] Monitor For Overload And Burnout Signs

Overload and burnout manifest as declining quality, missed commitments, cynicism, withdrawal, and increased conflict. These signs often appear before explicit complaints. Monitor for them and intervene early; waiting for burnout to be declared is waiting too long.

- [ ] Watch for quality decline, missed commitments, and disengagement as overload signs.
- [ ] Monitor for cynicism, withdrawal, and increased conflict.
- [ ] Intervene at early signs, not after burnout is declared.
- [ ] Check in regularly on workload and wellbeing, not only at crises.

### [ ] Use Velocity And Throughput Data To Inform Capacity

Historical data on what the team has actually delivered, velocity in agile, throughput in flow-based systems, provides the most reliable basis for capacity planning. Plans based on aspiration rather than history are systematically optimistic. Use data to calibrate commitments.

- [ ] Base capacity planning on historical velocity or throughput data.
- [ ] Adjust for changes in team size, skill mix, or context.
- [ ] Avoid planning above sustainable historical throughput without justification.
- [ ] Update capacity estimates as new data arrives.

### [ ] Manage Resource Constraints Proactively

Resource constraints, scarce skills, shared resources, or competing demands, limit capacity and create bottlenecks. Identify constraints early and manage them through cross-training, prioritization, or negotiation, rather than discovering them when they cause delays.

- [ ] Identify scarce skills and shared resources as constraints early.
- [ ] Cross-train to reduce single-person bottlenecks.
- [ ] Negotiate shared resource availability in advance.
- [ ] Prioritize work to manage around constraints.

### [ ] Protect The Team From Uncommitted Work

Teams are often overloaded not by the project plan but by uncommitted work: support requests, meetings, ad hoc tasks that consume capacity without being planned. Protect the team by making all demand visible and routed through prioritization.

- [ ] Make all work demand visible, including support and ad hoc requests.
- [ ] Route uncommitted work through prioritization, not direct interruption.
- [ ] Protect focused work time from interruptions.
- [ ] Reserve capacity for planned work before accepting ad hoc demands.

### [ ] Address Workload Imbalance Openly

Workload imbalance, some members overloaded while others are underutilized, breeds resentment and reduces throughput. Address imbalance openly by redistributing work, cross-training, or adjusting assignments. Ignoring imbalance lets it fester.

- [ ] Monitor workload distribution across the team for imbalance.
- [ ] Redistribute work to balance load by skill and capacity.
- [ ] Use cross-training to enable redistribution to underutilized members.
- [ ] Address imbalance proactively rather than waiting for complaints.

### [ ] Treat Wellbeing As A Project Performance Factor

Wellbeing is not a personal matter separate from the project; burnout and disengagement directly reduce capacity, quality, and retention. Treat wellbeing as a project performance factor that the project manager influences through workload, pace, and culture.

- [ ] Recognize that wellbeing directly affects capacity, quality, and retention.
- [ ] Manage workload and pace to protect sustainable performance.
- [ ] Address workload-driven stress as a project issue, not a personal one.
- [ ] Track attrition and engagement as indicators of capacity risk.

## Common Traps

### [ ] Optimistic Capacity Estimation

Assuming 100% productive time and overcommitting the team.

### [ ] Availability-Based Assignment

Assigning work to whoever is free rather than to the right skills.

### [ ] Multitasking As Throughput

Believing parallel work increases output when it reduces it.

### [ ] Unsustainable Pace

Planning sustained overtime that leads to burnout and quality decline.

### [ ] Ignoring Overload Signs

Missing early signals and intervening only after burnout.

### [ ] Aspiration-Based Planning

Planning above historical throughput without data justification.

### [ ] Invisible Uncommitted Work

Letting ad hoc demands consume capacity without being planned.

### [ ] Wellbeing As Personal Matter

Treating burnout as unrelated to project performance.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is capacity estimated realistically, accounting for overhead and a productivity factor?
- [ ] Is workload balanced by skill and capability, not just availability?
- [ ] Is multitasking limited to preserve throughput through reduced context switching?
- [ ] Is the planned pace sustainable over the project duration?
- [ ] Are overload and burnout signs monitored and addressed early?
- [ ] Is capacity planning based on historical velocity or throughput data?
- [ ] Are resource constraints identified early and managed proactively?
- [ ] Is the team protected from uncommitted work through visible demand management?
- [ ] Is workload imbalance addressed openly through redistribution and cross-training?
- [ ] Is wellbeing treated as a project performance factor, not a personal matter?
