# Stakeholder Communication Planning

Communication does not happen by default. Without a plan, information reaches stakeholders inconsistently, through the wrong channels, at the wrong times, and with the wrong emphasis. A communication plan is the deliberate design of what each stakeholder needs to know, how they will receive it, how often, from whom, and what action it should prompt. The project manager must treat communication as a designed system, not as an overflow of activity reports.

Use this skill before creating a communication plan, setting up project messaging, coordinating communication across audiences, or diagnosing why stakeholders feel uninformed or misinformed. The goal is to prevent the agent from substituting volume for design and from sending the same undifferentiated update to every audience.

## Core Rules

### Start From Stakeholder Needs, Not From What Is Easy To Send

A communication plan begins with what each stakeholder needs to stay aligned, make decisions, and provide input, not with what is convenient to broadcast. Map communication back to the stakeholder analysis.

For each stakeholder or group, define:

- what decisions or actions they are responsible for;
- what information those decisions require;
- what concerns or interests they hold;
- what format and depth they can absorb;
- how often they need updates;
- who they trust as the source.

Designing from needs prevents both information overload and information starvation.

### Choose Channels Appropriate To Purpose And Audience

Different channels serve different purposes. Choose the channel that fits the message and the recipient.

- Executive briefings and steering committees for decisions and tradeoffs.
- Written status reports for reference and record.
- Working sessions and workshops for collaboration and input.
- Direct calls or messages for urgent or sensitive items.
- Broad announcements for awareness across many people.
- Formal submissions for regulatory or contractual communication.

Urgent or sensitive information deserves a direct, high-bandwidth channel, not a buried line in a mass email. Routine information deserves an asynchronous, referenceable channel, not a meeting.

### Define Frequency Proportional To Need And Volatility

Frequency should match how quickly the stakeholder's situation changes and how much they care. Critical decision makers may need weekly or biweekly updates. Broad audiences may need monthly summaries. Regulators may need communication at defined milestones.

Avoid two failure modes. Too infrequent, and stakeholders feel uninformed and start seeking information informally, which breeds rumor. Too frequent, and stakeholders stop reading, so important messages are lost in noise.

### Assign Communication Ownership

Every communication channel and audience needs an owner responsible for ensuring the communication happens, is accurate, and is consistent. Diffuse ownership produces inconsistent messaging and gaps.

For sensitive or cross-functional communication, define who speaks for the project. Mixed messages from different team members undermine credibility. A single voice, or a coordinated set of voices, maintains alignment.

### Plan For Two-Way Communication

Communication is not only broadcasting. Stakeholders need channels to raise concerns, ask questions, and provide input. Plan how feedback and questions will be received, routed, and responded to.

Without two-way channels, stakeholders raise concerns through back channels, escalate prematurely, or disengage. A feedback path turns communication into engagement.

### Coordinate Messaging Across Audiences

When multiple audiences receive communication about the same project, the messages must be consistent in substance even if tailored in form. Conflicting messages to different audiences create confusion and erode trust when stakeholders compare notes.

Coordinate timing so that audiences who need to act first receive information before those who only need to be aware. For example, the delivery team should understand a change before it is announced to customers.

### Build Communication Around Decision Points And Milestones

Routine cadence keeps stakeholders informed, but the highest-value communication clusters around decisions and milestones. Plan specific communication for upcoming decision points, gate reviews, launches, and major changes.

For each decision point, ensure the decision maker has the context, options, recommendation, and deadline in time to act. Communication that arrives after the decision window is useless.

### Include Crisis And Bad-News Communication In The Plan

Do not wait for a crisis to decide how to communicate one. Define in advance who must be informed, in what order, through what channel, and with what approval when something goes wrong.

Bad news communicated promptly and honestly preserves trust. Bad news delayed or softened destroys it. Planning the path in advance removes the hesitation that makes delays worse.

### Review And Adapt The Plan

Communication needs change as the project progresses. Early phases need more alignment and direction-setting communication. Execution needs more status and coordination. Closure needs more transition and lessons communication.

Review the communication plan at major milestones and adjust channels, frequency, and content to match the current phase and the current stakeholder concerns.

## Common Traps

### Broadcasting The Same Update To Everyone

Undifferentiated communication under-serves decision makers and overwhelms peripheral audiences.

### Volume Mistaken For Effectiveness

More updates do not mean better-informed stakeholders. Relevance and timing matter more than frequency.

### Wrong Channel For The Message

Sensitive news buried in a mass email, or routine status delivered in a meeting, both fail their purpose.

### No Feedback Path

One-way communication leaves concerns to fester and escalate through back channels.

### Inconsistent Messaging Across Audiences

Conflicting messages destroy trust when stakeholders compare notes.

### Reactive Crisis Communication

Without a planned path, bad news is delayed and softened until trust is damaged.

### Static Plan

A communication plan built at kickoff and never adapted diverges from stakeholder needs as the project evolves.

### No Owner For Key Communication

Communication without an owner happens inconsistently or not at all.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The communication plan is designed from stakeholder needs, not from what is easy to send.
- [ ] Channels are chosen to match the purpose, urgency, and audience of each message.
- [ ] Frequency is proportional to how quickly each stakeholder's situation changes.
- [ ] Each communication channel and audience has a named owner.
- [ ] Two-way feedback channels exist so stakeholders can raise concerns and questions.
- [ ] Messaging is coordinated across audiences to remain consistent in substance.
- [ ] Communication is clustered around decision points and milestones, not only routine cadence.
- [ ] Crisis and bad-news communication paths are defined in advance.
- [ ] The plan is reviewed and adapted at major milestones and phase transitions.
- [ ] Communication effectiveness is judged by stakeholder understanding and action, not by message volume.
