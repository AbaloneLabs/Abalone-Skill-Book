# Project Health Assessment And Recovery

Project health assessment is the integrative judgment that combines scope, schedule, budget, quality, risk, and team signals into an overall picture of whether the project is healthy, at risk, or in trouble. Recovery is the deliberate process of bringing a failing project back toward viability. The judgment problem is that health is often assessed by a single dimension, usually schedule, while other dimensions quietly deteriorate. A project can be on schedule but over budget, or on budget but delivering poor quality, or meeting all three but with a demoralized team about to quit. Integrative assessment catches what single-dimension monitoring misses. And when a project is in trouble, recovery requires honest diagnosis and decisive action, not incremental adjustments to a failing plan.

Use this skill when assessing overall project health, when a project shows signs of trouble, when planning recovery, or when evaluating whether to continue or cancel a failing project. The goal is to prevent the agent from assessing health by one dimension only, from missing deteriorating signals, from applying incremental adjustments to projects that need fundamental recovery, or from continuing unrecoverable projects by inertia.

## Core Rules

### [ ] Assess Health Across All Dimensions

Project health is multi-dimensional: scope, schedule, budget, quality, risk, and team. A project that is green on schedule may be red on quality or team morale. Assess all dimensions and integrate them into an overall health picture. Single-dimension assessment misses deterioration in other areas.

- [ ] Assess scope, schedule, budget, quality, risk, and team dimensions.
- [ ] Integrate the dimensions into an overall health rating, not separate siloed views.
- [ ] Recognize that weakness in one dimension can undermine strength in others.
- [ ] Avoid declaring health based on a single favorable dimension.

### [ ] Use Leading Indicators, Not Only Lagging Ones

Lagging indicators (variance, defects delivered) report what has already happened. Leading indicators (risk trends, team morale, requirement volatility, test coverage) predict what will happen. Leading indicators provide early warning before lagging ones confirm trouble.

- [ ] Track leading indicators: risk trends, morale, volatility, coverage, dependency status.
- [ ] Use leading indicators for early warning, not just lagging metrics for confirmation.
- [ ] Investigate deteriorating leading indicators before they become lagging failures.
- [ ] Balance leading and lagging indicators in health reporting.

### [ ] Diagnose Root Causes Of Trouble

A troubled project's symptoms, schedule slip, budget overrun, quality defects, usually have root causes: unclear requirements, unrealistic estimates, scope creep, resource constraints, or poor governance. Effective recovery addresses root causes; symptom-level fixes produce temporary improvement that reverses.

- [ ] Diagnose the root causes of trouble, not just the symptoms.
- [ ] Distinguish causes within the project's control from external ones.
- [ ] Address root causes through fundamental changes, not symptom patches.
- [ ] Verify that recovery actions target the diagnosed causes.

### [ ] Differentiate Recovery From Incremental Adjustment

A project that is slightly off track may need incremental adjustment: re-prioritization, minor re-planning, added resources. A project that is fundamentally failing needs recovery: re-baselining, scope reduction, leadership change, or restructuring. Applying incremental adjustment to a project needing recovery delays the inevitable.

- [ ] Assess whether the project needs incremental adjustment or fundamental recovery.
- [ ] Apply recovery, not incremental adjustment, to fundamentally troubled projects.
- [ ] Recognize the threshold beyond which adjustment is insufficient.
- [ ] Avoid prolonging failure through inadequate incremental responses.

### [ ] Re-Plan Around Reality, Not The Original Plan

Recovery planning starts from current reality, not from the original plan. What is the remaining scope, the remaining budget, the current team capacity, and the current risks? Recovery plans built on these realities are achievable; recovery plans that try to get back to the original baseline often are not.

- [ ] Base recovery plans on current reality, not the original baseline.
- [ ] Assess remaining scope, budget, capacity, and risks honestly.
- [ ] Define a recovery baseline that is achievable from the current state.
- [ ] Avoid recovery plans that assume a return to an unachievable original plan.

### [ ] Reduce Scope As A Primary Recovery Lever

When a project cannot meet its objectives within constraints, scope reduction is often the most effective recovery lever. Delivering less, but delivering it well and on time, is better than delivering everything late and poorly. Recovery should explicitly evaluate what scope can be cut.

- [ ] Evaluate scope reduction as a primary recovery lever.
- [ ] Prioritize scope to identify what is essential versus deferrable.
- [ ] Negotiate scope reductions with stakeholders as part of recovery.
- [ ] Prefer delivering less well over delivering everything poorly.

### [ ] Stabilize Before Recovering

A project in chaos must be stabilized before recovery can begin. Stabilization means stopping scope churn, establishing clear priorities, restoring basic control processes, and calming the team. Recovery planning on top of chaos fails because the foundation is unstable.

- [ ] Stabilize the project before planning recovery: stop scope churn, restore control.
- [ ] Establish clear priorities and stop non-essential work.
- [ ] Restore basic monitoring and control processes.
- [ ] Calm and re-engage the team before driving recovery.

### [ ] Engage Stakeholders Honestly About Recovery

Recovery requires stakeholder support, which requires honesty about the project's true state and the recovery options. Presenting optimistic recovery plans that stakeholders later discover were unrealistic destroys trust. Present honest assessments, realistic options, and tradeoffs.

- [ ] Present stakeholders with an honest assessment of the project's state.
- [ ] Offer realistic recovery options with their tradeoffs.
- [ ] Avoid optimistic recovery plans not grounded in reality.
- [ ] Obtain stakeholder commitment to the chosen recovery path.

### [ ] Evaluate Continue Versus Cancel Honestly

Some projects cannot be recovered, or the cost of recovery exceeds the value of completion. Honest health assessment includes evaluating whether the project should continue or be cancelled. Continuing an unrecoverable project by inertia wastes resources that could be deployed elsewhere.

- [ ] Evaluate whether the project is recoverable or should be cancelled.
- [ ] Compare the cost of recovery to the value of completion.
- [ ] Be willing to recommend cancellation when recovery is not viable.
- [ ] Avoid continuing unrecoverable projects by inertia or sunk-cost fallacy.

### [ ] Monitor Recovery Progress Against Recovery Milestones

Recovery plans need their own milestones and metrics to confirm that recovery is working. Monitor progress against recovery-specific targets, not the original plan. If recovery is not progressing, escalate or reconsider the approach.

- [ ] Define recovery-specific milestones and metrics.
- [ ] Monitor recovery progress against these targets, not the original plan.
- [ ] Escalate if recovery is not progressing as planned.
- [ ] Reassess the recovery approach if milestones are missed.

## Common Traps

### [ ] Single-Dimension Health Assessment

Declaring health based on schedule while budget, quality, or team deteriorate.

### [ ] Lagging Indicators Only

Waiting for variance to confirm trouble that leading indicators already signaled.

### [ ] Symptom-Level Recovery

Patching symptoms while root causes persist, producing temporary improvement.

### [ ] Incremental Adjustment For Fundamental Failure

Applying minor fixes to projects that need fundamental recovery.

### [ ] Recovery To The Original Plan

Building recovery around an unachievable return to the original baseline.

### [ ] Reluctance To Cut Scope

Trying to deliver everything rather than reducing scope to achieve viability.

### [ ] Recovery On Chaos

Planning recovery before stabilizing the project's foundation.

### [ ] Sunk-Cost Continuation

Continuing unrecoverable projects because of investment already made.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is project health assessed across all dimensions, not just schedule?
- [ ] Are leading indicators used for early warning alongside lagging metrics?
- [ ] Are root causes of trouble diagnosed, not just symptoms addressed?
- [ ] Is recovery distinguished from incremental adjustment and applied appropriately?
- [ ] Are recovery plans based on current reality rather than the original plan?
- [ ] Is scope reduction evaluated as a primary recovery lever?
- [ ] Is the project stabilized before recovery planning begins?
- [ ] Are stakeholders engaged honestly about the project's state and recovery options?
- [ ] Is continue-versus-cancel evaluated honestly, with willingness to recommend cancellation?
- [ ] Is recovery progress monitored against recovery-specific milestones and metrics?
