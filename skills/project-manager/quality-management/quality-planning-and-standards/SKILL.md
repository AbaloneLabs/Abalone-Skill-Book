# Quality Planning And Standards

Quality is not inspected in at the end; it is designed in from the start. When quality is undefined, every stakeholder applies a different standard, and the project either over-engineers to be safe or under-delivers and is rejected. Quality planning is the discipline of deciding, before execution, what qualities the deliverable must have, how they will be measured, what standards apply, and how conformance will be verified. The project manager must make quality explicit, measurable, and agreed, because quality left implicit becomes a source of late conflict and rework.

Use this skill before defining quality requirements, setting acceptance criteria, building a quality plan, or diagnosing why a deliverable keeps being rejected. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating quality as a final review rather than a planned set of standards and checks built into the work.

## Core Rules

### Define Quality In Measurable Terms

Quality must be observable, not subjective. Vague quality goals such as robust, user-friendly, or high-performance cannot be verified and invite disagreement at acceptance.

Translate quality into measurable criteria: response time under stated load, defect density below a threshold, accessibility conformance to a named standard, uptime percentage, accuracy rate, or review approval from a named authority. Each quality attribute should have a target and a measurement method.

If a quality attribute cannot be measured, define the acceptance evidence that will stand in for measurement, such as expert review signoff or user testing results.

### Distinguish Quality Of Product From Quality Of Process

Product quality is whether the deliverable meets its requirements and is fit for purpose. Process quality is whether the work was performed according to defined standards and practices. Both matter, and they are managed differently.

Product quality is verified through testing, inspection, and acceptance. Process quality is assured through adherence to standards, reviews, and audits. A project can have good process quality and poor product quality if the standards were wrong, and good product quality and poor process quality if the result was right but unreproducible.

### Set Acceptance Criteria Early And Agree Them

Acceptance criteria define what must be true for the deliverable to be accepted. Set them early, before the work to satisfy them is done, and agree them with the accepting stakeholder.

Late or vague acceptance criteria produce rework, because the team builds to one standard and is judged against another. Criteria should cover functional correctness, non-functional qualities, completeness, documentation, and any regulatory or contractual requirements.

### Apply Relevant Standards Deliberately

Standards, whether industry, regulatory, organizational, or domain-specific, encode accumulated quality knowledge. Identify which standards apply and incorporate them rather than improvising.

Relevant standards may cover security, accessibility, data protection, safety, interoperability, coding conventions, or documentation. Applying a standard is not bureaucratic overhead when the standard addresses real risk; ignoring it creates exposure. Confirm which standards are mandatory versus recommended.

### Build Quality Activities Into The Plan

Quality does not happen by hope. Build the activities that produce and verify quality into the schedule and budget: reviews, inspections, testing, audits, validation, and rework cycles.

If these activities are not in the plan, they will be skipped under pressure, and quality will be discovered missing at the worst time. Plan for rework realistically, because first attempts are rarely perfect, and quality processes surface defects that must then be fixed.

### Distinguish Quality Assurance From Quality Control

Quality assurance is process-focused and preventive: ensuring the right processes are defined and followed so that quality is built in. Quality control is product-focused and detective: inspecting and testing the output to find defects.

Both are needed. Assurance prevents defects; control catches the ones that escape. A project with only control finds defects late and expensively; a project with only assurance assumes prevention always works and misses escapes.

### Define Quality Roles And Authority

Quality decisions need clear authority. Who decides whether a defect must be fixed before release? Who can accept a quality deviation? Who owns the quality plan and the standards?

Name the quality owner and define the decision rights around defects, deviations, and release. Quality decisions made by the wrong person, or by no one, produce either release of poor work or endless delay chasing perfection.

### Plan For Continuous Quality Improvement

Quality planning is not only about meeting a threshold. It includes learning from defects, improving processes, and raising quality over time. Build in retrospectives on quality, root cause analysis of significant defects, and process changes that prevent recurrence.

Improvement requires that defects and quality issues are surfaced honestly rather than hidden, which depends on a culture that treats quality problems as information rather than blame.

## Common Traps

### Subjective Quality Goals

Unmeasurable quality attributes cannot be verified and become late disputes.

### Quality As Final Inspection

Treating quality as something checked at the end finds defects when they are most expensive to fix.

### Late Or Vague Acceptance Criteria

Building without agreed criteria guarantees rework at acceptance.

### Ignoring Applicable Standards

Improvising where standards exist creates risk and rework.

### Quality Activities Not In The Plan

Unplanned quality work is skipped under pressure and discovered missing late.

### Control Without Assurance

Detecting defects without preventing them is expensive and reactive.

### Undefined Quality Authority

Quality decisions without a clear owner produce either poor releases or endless delay.

### Blame Culture Hiding Defects

When quality problems are punished, they are concealed rather than learned from.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Quality attributes are defined in measurable terms with targets and measurement methods.
- [ ] Product quality and process quality are distinguished and both managed.
- [ ] Acceptance criteria are set early, agreed with the accepting stakeholder, and cover functional and non-functional requirements.
- [ ] Relevant standards, mandatory and recommended, are identified and incorporated.
- [ ] Quality activities such as reviews, testing, audits, and rework cycles are built into the schedule and budget.
- [ ] Quality assurance and quality control are both present, preventive and detective.
- [ ] Quality roles and decision authority for defects, deviations, and release are defined.
- [ ] Continuous improvement uses defect analysis and process change to raise quality over time.
- [ ] The culture surfaces quality problems as information rather than concealing them as blame.
- [ ] Quality is designed in from the start, not inspected in at the end.
