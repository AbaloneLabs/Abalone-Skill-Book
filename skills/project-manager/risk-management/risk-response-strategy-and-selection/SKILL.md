# Risk Response Strategy And Selection

Identifying risks is necessary but insufficient; risks must be responded to, and the choice of response strategy is where risk management delivers or fails to deliver value. Each risk can be addressed through a small set of strategies: avoid, by eliminating the source; mitigate, by reducing probability or impact; transfer, by shifting the risk to another party; accept, by choosing to bear it; and for opportunities, exploit, enhance, or share. Selecting among these strategies requires analyzing the risk, the available responses, and the cost-effectiveness of each response. The characteristic failures are responding to all risks identically, usually with vague mitigation plans; accepting risks by default through inaction; and investing in responses whose cost exceeds the risk they address. The judgment problem is selecting response strategies deliberately based on risk analysis, designing responses that are specific and actionable, sizing contingency appropriately, and recognizing when response cost exceeds risk value.

Use this skill when selecting risk response strategies, designing contingency plans, setting thresholds and triggers, allocating reserves, or evaluating response cost-effectiveness. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating all risks the same, from accepting risks through inaction, from producing vague mitigation plans, and from over-investing in responses to low-value risks.

## Core Rules

### Select Strategy Based On Risk Analysis, Not Default

Different risks call for different responses, and selecting a strategy requires analyzing the risk's probability, impact, controllability, and the available responses. Applying a default strategy, usually mitigation, to all risks produces responses that fit some and waste effort on others. Selection should follow analysis.

Select based on analysis by:

- analyzing each risk's probability, impact, and timing before selecting a response;
- considering which strategy, avoid, mitigate, transfer, accept, fits the risk's characteristics;
- recognizing that some risks are best avoided by changing the plan, not mitigated within it;
- recognizing that some risks are best accepted because response cost exceeds expected loss;
- documenting the strategy choice and its rationale.

A risk response selected without analysis is a guess, and guesses produce both under-response to serious risks and over-response to trivial ones.

### Match Strategy To Risk Type: Threat Versus Opportunity

Risks include both threats, negative events, and opportunities, positive events. The response strategies differ: threats are avoided, mitigated, transferred, or accepted; opportunities are exploited, enhanced, shared, or accepted. Treating all risks as threats misses the upside that opportunity management captures.

Match by:

- distinguishing threats from opportunities in the risk register;
- applying threat strategies to threats and opportunity strategies to opportunities;
- recognizing that opportunity management is risk management, not a separate activity;
- ensuring the response strategy fits whether the risk is a downside or an upside.

A risk register that lists only threats ignores the positive uncertainties that projects should pursue.

### Avoid When The Source Can Be Eliminated

Avoidance eliminates the risk by changing the plan: choosing a different approach, dropping a feature, or selecting a proven technology over an experimental one. Avoidance is the most complete response because it removes the risk entirely, but it is available only where the source can be changed without losing essential value.

Avoid by:

- identifying risks whose source can be eliminated through plan changes;
- choosing proven approaches over risky ones where the risk is not worth the potential benefit;
- recognizing that avoidance often has its own cost, reduced capability or increased effort;
- preferring avoidance for high-impact risks where mitigation cannot reduce impact enough.

Avoidance is not always possible or desirable, but it is underused because it requires changing the plan rather than adding controls.

### Mitigate By Reducing Probability Impact Or Both

Mitigation reduces the risk by lowering its probability, its impact, or both. Effective mitigation is specific and actionable, not a vague intention to "manage" the risk. A mitigation plan states what action will reduce what dimension of the risk, by how much, and at what cost.

Mitigate by:

- identifying specific actions that reduce probability, such as additional testing or training;
- identifying specific actions that reduce impact, such as fallback designs or recovery procedures;
- estimating the residual risk after mitigation, not assuming mitigation eliminates the risk;
- ensuring mitigation actions are assigned, scheduled, and tracked, not just listed.

A mitigation plan that says "monitor and manage" is not a mitigation plan; it is an acknowledgment that no plan exists.

### Transfer When Another Party Can Bear It Better

Transfer shifts the risk to another party better able to bear or manage it, typically through insurance, warranties, fixed-price contracts, or outsourcing. Transfer does not eliminate the risk; it moves it, and it often introduces new risks, such as dependency on the transferee or contractual disputes.

Transfer by:

- identifying risks that another party can manage more effectively or bear more cheaply;
- using contracts, insurance, or partnerships to transfer specific risks;
- recognizing that transfer introduces new risks that must themselves be managed;
- ensuring the transfer is real, with enforceable terms, not just a label;
- accounting for the cost of transfer, which is the premium paid to the transferee.

A risk transferred to a vendor who cannot bear it returns to the project when the vendor fails, now compounded.

### Accept Deliberately, Not By Default

Acceptance is a legitimate strategy when the risk's expected loss is less than the response cost, or when no effective response exists. But acceptance must be deliberate and documented, not the default result of inaction. Default acceptance, failing to respond and thereby accepting, is the most common risk management failure.

Accept deliberately by:

- choosing acceptance explicitly when response cost exceeds expected loss;
- distinguishing active acceptance, with contingency plans, from passive acceptance, without;
- documenting the acceptance decision and its rationale;
- setting triggers for revisiting accepted risks if conditions change;
- avoiding the pattern of accepting all risks through failure to select other strategies.

A risk accepted through inaction is not managed; it is ignored, and the difference matters when the risk materializes.

### Design Contingency Plans With Triggers And Reserves

For risks that are accepted or whose mitigation does not eliminate them, contingency plans specify what will be done if the risk materializes. Effective contingency includes triggers, the conditions that activate the response, and reserves, the resources set aside to execute it. Contingency without triggers and reserves is an intention, not a plan.

Design contingency by:

- defining specific trigger conditions that indicate the risk is materializing;
- specifying the response that will be executed when triggers fire;
- allocating contingency reserve, schedule or budget, to fund the response;
- assigning ownership for monitoring triggers and executing the response;
- reviewing contingency plans as conditions change.

A contingency plan that has no trigger will fire too late, and one with no reserve cannot execute.

### Evaluate Response Cost Against Risk Expected Value

Risk responses cost resources, and a response whose cost exceeds the risk's expected value, probability times impact, destroys value. Evaluating response cost-effectiveness prevents over-investment in low-value risks and focuses resources where they reduce the most expected loss.

Evaluate by:

- estimating the risk's expected value, probability times impact;
- comparing response cost to the expected loss reduction the response achieves;
- preferring responses whose cost is justified by loss reduction;
- accepting risks where no cost-justified response exists;
- recognizing that some high-impact low-probability risks justify response despite low expected value, due to severity.

A response that costs more than the risk it addresses is not risk management; it is risk avoidance theater.

## Common Traps

### Default Mitigation For All Risks

Different risks need different strategies. Select based on analysis, not default.

### Vague Mitigation Plans

"Monitor and manage" is not mitigation. Specify actions, residual risk, and ownership.

### Acceptance Through Inaction

Failing to respond accepts risks by default. Accept deliberately with documented rationale.

### Transfer That Introduces Unmanaged New Risks

Transfer moves risk, it does not eliminate it. Manage the risks the transfer introduces.

### Contingency Without Triggers Or Reserves

Contingency plans need triggers and reserves to be actionable. Specify both.

### Over-Investment In Low-Value Risks

Response cost must be justified by expected loss reduction. Do not over-respond to trivial risks.

### Ignoring Opportunities

Risks include positive uncertainties. Apply opportunity strategies, not only threat strategies.

### Residual Risk Assumed Eliminated

Mitigation reduces risk, it rarely eliminates it. Estimate and manage residual risk.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Response strategy is selected based on risk analysis, not applied as a default.
- [ ] Threats and opportunities are distinguished, with matched strategies for each.
- [ ] Avoidance is considered for risks whose source can be eliminated through plan changes.
- [ ] Mitigation plans are specific, actionable, with estimated residual risk and assigned ownership.
- [ ] Transfer is used where another party can bear the risk better, with new risks managed.
- [ ] Acceptance is deliberate and documented, not the result of inaction.
- [ ] Contingency plans have defined triggers, allocated reserves, and assigned ownership.
- [ ] Response cost is evaluated against expected loss reduction to ensure cost-effectiveness.
- [ ] High-impact low-probability risks are evaluated for severity, not just expected value.
- [ ] No risk is accepted through failure to select a strategy.
