# Risk Identification Techniques

You cannot manage a risk you have not identified. Risk identification is the foundation of risk management, and its quality determines whether the entire risk process has something real to work on. Yet identification is often treated as a one-time brainstorm at kickoff, driven by the project manager alone, producing a list that reflects one perspective and never gets refreshed. The result is a risk register full of generic threats while the risks that actually derail the project, the ones rooted in specific assumptions, technical unknowns, vendor dependencies, or stakeholder dynamics, are never surfaced. The judgment problem is that good identification is broad, structured, and continuous, drawing on multiple techniques and perspectives rather than a single brainstorm.

Use this skill before running a risk identification session, before building a risk checklist, before analyzing assumptions for hidden risks, or when diagnosing why the risk register failed to catch a materialized risk. The goal is to prevent the agent from relying on a single technique or perspective, from treating identification as a one-time event, or from producing generic risks that do not enable action.

## Core Rules

### [ ] Use Multiple Identification Techniques

No single technique catches all risks. Brainstorming captures creative and unexpected threats; checklists capture common and recurring ones; assumption analysis exposes risks hidden in what the project takes for granted; interviews capture perspectives that group sessions suppress. Combining techniques produces a richer, more complete risk picture.

- [ ] Combine brainstorming, checklists, assumption analysis, and interviews.
- [ ] Use different techniques to surface different categories of risk.
- [ ] Avoid relying on a single method for all identification.
- [ ] Match techniques to the project type and phase.

### [ ] Engage Multiple Perspectives

The project manager sees some risks; developers see others; stakeholders, vendors, and end users see still others. Identification driven by one perspective misses the risks visible only from other vantage points. Broaden participation to capture the full risk picture.

- [ ] Include team members, stakeholders, subject matter experts, and vendors.
- [ ] Solicit risks from people closest to the work and the uncertainty.
- [ ] Use individual input before group sessions to avoid groupthink.
- [ ] Value dissenting and pessimistic voices; they surface real risks.

### [ ] Analyze Assumptions And Constraints

Many risks hide inside assumptions the project treats as facts. "The vendor will deliver on time," "the API will be stable," "the team will stay staffed." Explicitly listing and challenging assumptions exposes the uncertainties that underlie the plan and that, if wrong, become risks.

- [ ] List the project's key assumptions and constraints.
- [ ] Challenge each assumption: what if it is wrong?
- [ ] Convert fragile assumptions into tracked risks.
- [ ] Re-examine assumptions as the project evolves.

### [ ] Treat Identification As Continuous, Not One-Time

Risks emerge throughout the project, not just at kickoff. New dependencies, changing requirements, team turnover, and external events create risks that did not exist at the start. Identification must be ongoing, woven into regular reviews and triggered by project changes.

- [ ] Identify risks continuously, not only at project kickoff.
- [ ] Capture new risks in regular reviews and at milestones.
- [ ] Re-identify risks after major changes or scope shifts.
- [ ] Encourage the team to raise risks whenever they spot them.

### [ ] Use Historical Data And Lessons Learned

Past projects are a rich source of identified risks. Lessons learned, post-mortems, and historical risk registers reveal risks that recur across projects of a given type. Leveraging this data avoids re-discovering known risks the hard way.

- [ ] Review lessons learned from similar past projects.
- [ ] Use historical risk registers as a starting checklist.
- [ ] Capture risks that recur across project types.
- [ ] Feed current risks back into organizational memory.

### [ ] Use Checklists Tailored To The Project Type

Generic risk checklists are a starting point, but the most useful checklists are tailored to the project's domain, technology, and organization. A software project, a construction project, and an organizational change project face different risk categories. Tailored checklists ensure domain-specific risks are not missed.

- [ ] Start with a standard risk checklist, then tailor it to the project.
- [ ] Include domain-specific categories relevant to the work.
- [ ] Add categories for technology, vendor, regulatory, and organizational risks.
- [ ] Update the checklist as new categories emerge.

### [ ] Structure Brainstorming To Maximize Breadth

Unstructured brainstorming often converges quickly on obvious risks and suppresses unconventional ones. Structured techniques, such as round-robin input, nominal group method, or category-prompted brainstorming, broaden the output and ensure all participants contribute.

- [ ] Use round-robin or nominal group techniques to ensure all voices contribute.
- [ ] Prompt by risk category to ensure breadth.
- [ ] Separate idea generation from evaluation.
- [ ] Capture all ideas before filtering or prioritizing.

### [ ] Document Risks With Enough Specificity To Act

A risk identified as "schedule risk" is not actionable. Identification must capture enough detail, cause, event, and consequence, to enable ownership and response. Vague identification produces a register that cannot be managed.

- [ ] Document each risk with cause, event, and consequence.
- [ ] Make the description specific enough to assign an owner.
- [ ] Avoid generic risks that apply to any project.
- [ ] Separate distinct risks rather than combining them.

### [ ] Distinguish Root Causes From Symptoms

A symptom-level risk, "testing is delayed," points to a consequence but not the underlying cause. Root-cause-level identification, "the test environment is shared and often unavailable," surfaces what can actually be addressed. Identifying root causes enables effective response.

- [ ] Push past symptom-level risks to underlying causes.
- [ ] Ask why repeatedly to reach the root.
- [ ] Identify risks at the level where they can be acted on.
- [ ] Avoid treating symptoms as risks.

### [ ] Create A Culture That Values Raising Risks

If raising risks is treated as negativity or complaining, people stop raising them. A culture that values early risk identification, that rewards rather than punishes surfacing bad news, produces more complete and timely identification. Culture determines whether techniques succeed.

- [ ] Encourage and reward early risk identification.
- [ ] Avoid punishing messengers who raise risks.
- [ ] Respond visibly to raised risks to reinforce the behavior.
- [ ] Model risk openness as the project manager.

## Common Traps

### [ ] One-Time Brainstorm

Treating identification as a kickoff event and never refreshing it.

### [ ] Single Perspective

Identifying risks only from the project manager's viewpoint.

### [ ] Generic Risks

Producing vague risks that could apply to any project and enable no action.

### [ ] Unchallenged Assumptions

Letting fragile assumptions pass as facts, hiding the risks within them.

### [ ] Symptom Over Root Cause

Identifying symptoms rather than the causes that can be addressed.

### [ ] Ignoring History

Failing to leverage lessons learned and recurring risk patterns.

### [ ] Groupthink In Sessions

Converging on obvious risks and suppressing unconventional ones.

### [ ] Culture That Suppresses Risks

Punishing or ignoring raised risks so people stop raising them.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are multiple identification techniques used, not just brainstorming?
- [ ] Are risks identified from multiple perspectives, not just the project manager?
- [ ] Are assumptions and constraints explicitly analyzed for hidden risks?
- [ ] Is identification continuous throughout the project, not just at kickoff?
- [ ] Is historical data and lessons learned used to inform identification?
- [ ] Are checklists tailored to the project's domain and context?
- [ ] Is brainstorming structured to maximize breadth and participation?
- [ ] Are risks documented with enough specificity to assign ownership and response?
- [ ] Are root causes identified rather than symptoms?
- [ ] Does the project culture value and reward raising risks?
