# Project Charter And Scope

A project starts failing before execution when people agree to a title but not to the same outcome. A project manager must turn a vague initiative into an aligned agreement about purpose, scope, decision rights, constraints, success, and the first planning assumptions. The charter does not need to be bureaucratic, but the work needs a shared definition before schedules and tasks become meaningful.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what should be considered before starting a project", "how should a kickoff be prepared", "how should project scope be defined", or "what should be clarified before execution begins". The goal is to prevent the agent from building a plan around hidden disagreement.

## Core Rules

### Define The Outcome Before The Work

Start with the outcome, not the task list. A project may involve documents, software, process changes, events, vendor work, migration, training, or launch activities, but those are means. The outcome explains why the work exists.

Ask:

- What problem, obligation, or opportunity caused this project?
- What will be different when it is complete?
- Who receives the benefit?
- What business, operational, customer, compliance, or learning result matters?
- What would make the project not worth doing?

Avoid charter language that only restates activity, such as "implement the new system" or "run the campaign". Explain what successful implementation or campaign delivery changes.

### Identify Sponsor, Owner, And Decision Rights

Projects stall when authority is unclear. Name who sponsors the project, who owns day-to-day delivery, who approves scope changes, who resolves conflicts, and who must be consulted.

Clarify:

- sponsor or accountable executive;
- project manager or delivery owner;
- product, technical, operations, legal, finance, or customer owners;
- decision maker for tradeoffs;
- approvers for budget, launch, risk acceptance, and scope changes;
- stakeholders who are informed but not decision makers.

Do not confuse being interested with having approval authority. Too many unclassified stakeholders create late reversals.

### Set Scope Boundaries Explicitly

Scope is both what the project includes and what it deliberately excludes. A clear boundary allows teams to say no without renegotiating every adjacent idea.

Define:

- deliverables;
- affected systems, teams, customers, locations, or processes;
- required capabilities;
- excluded capabilities;
- phases or future work;
- assumptions about existing tools, staffing, data, vendors, or policy;
- handoff and support boundaries after delivery.

Out-of-scope items should be specific. "Everything else" is not useful. If something is not included but is likely to be requested, name it.

### Establish Success Criteria And Acceptance

Success criteria should be observable. They may include delivery outputs, business outcomes, operational readiness, quality thresholds, training completion, compliance signoff, migration accuracy, customer communication, or support readiness.

Define:

- what must be delivered;
- how quality will be judged;
- who accepts completion;
- what evidence proves completion;
- what must be true for launch or handoff;
- what metrics or outcomes will be reviewed after delivery.

Avoid treating "tasks closed" as sufficient evidence. A project can close tasks and still fail the intended outcome.

### Surface Constraints Early

Every project has constraints. Hidden constraints become surprise blockers.

Check:

- deadline drivers;
- budget limits;
- staffing and availability;
- vendor lead times;
- legal or regulatory requirements;
- procurement needs;
- security and privacy review;
- system freeze windows;
- dependencies on other projects;
- customer commitments;
- operational capacity.

If a deadline is fixed, scope or quality tradeoffs must be explicit. If scope is fixed, schedule and resources may need flexibility.

### Record Assumptions And Open Questions

Early project planning contains guesses. Good project managers name them. Assumptions should be testable, assigned, and reviewed.

Examples:

- vendor API will support the required data;
- legal review will take one week;
- migration volume is under a certain threshold;
- support can handle launch week volume;
- design assets are already approved;
- customer list is accurate.

Open questions should have owners and due dates. Otherwise they remain hidden risk.

### Make Kickoff Useful

A kickoff is not a ceremonial meeting. It should align people on purpose, scope, responsibilities, decision process, risks, communication rhythm, and immediate next steps.

Before kickoff, prepare:

- project outcome;
- scope and non-scope;
- roles and decision rights;
- initial milestone view;
- known risks and assumptions;
- dependency list;
- communication plan;
- first actions and owners.

The meeting should expose disagreement early, not simply announce that work has begun.

## Common Traps

### Starting With A Schedule

A schedule built before outcome, scope, and authority are clear is usually false precision. It may look like progress while hiding disagreement.

### Letting The Project Name Define The Project

"CRM migration", "website redesign", or "compliance launch" can mean different things to different stakeholders. The title is not the charter.

### Assuming Stakeholders Share The Same Definition Of Done

Engineering, sales, legal, support, customers, and executives may each define completion differently. Acceptance criteria must reconcile those expectations.

### Treating Out-Of-Scope As Negative

Out-of-scope clarity protects delivery. It does not mean the excluded work is unimportant; it means it is not part of this project.

### Ignoring Post-Launch Ownership

Projects often deliver something that someone must operate, maintain, support, or measure. Handoff is part of scope.

### Leaving Risks For Later

Early risks are cheaper to discuss before commitments harden. Waiting until execution makes risk handling look like failure rather than planning.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The project outcome is defined in terms of the change or value expected, not only the activities to perform.
- [ ] Sponsor, delivery owner, decision makers, approvers, consulted stakeholders, and informed stakeholders are clear.
- [ ] In-scope and out-of-scope boundaries are explicit enough to prevent adjacent work from silently entering the project.
- [ ] Success criteria and acceptance evidence are observable.
- [ ] Constraints such as deadline, budget, staffing, legal, vendor, security, and operational capacity were surfaced.
- [ ] Assumptions and open questions have owners and review dates.
- [ ] Kickoff materials include outcome, scope, roles, milestones, risks, dependencies, communication rhythm, and next actions.
- [ ] Handoff and post-launch ownership are included where relevant.
- [ ] The plan does not rely on the project title as a shared definition.
- [ ] Major stakeholder disagreements are exposed before execution commitments harden.
