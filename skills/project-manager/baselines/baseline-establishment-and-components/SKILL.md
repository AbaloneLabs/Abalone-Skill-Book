# Baseline Establishment And Components

A baseline is the reference against which everything the project does is judged. Without one, there is no way to say whether the project is ahead or behind, over or under budget, or delivering what was promised, because there is nothing stable to compare against. Yet baselines are often established carelessly: a schedule is frozen before its dependencies are understood, a budget is set before estimates are decomposed, a scope statement is approved while still ambiguous. A weak baseline does not fail immediately; it fails slowly, by making every later measurement unreliable and every later decision unmoored.

The judgment problem is deciding what must be true before a baseline can be approved, which components together constitute a complete performance measurement baseline, and how to set completeness criteria that are strict enough to be useful without paralyzing the project. Agents tend to treat baselining as a scheduling milestone rather than a measurement foundation, freezing whatever exists at the deadline. The discipline is to insist that the baseline be complete, internally consistent, and approved against defined criteria before it is treated as the reference.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Baseline As A Measurement Foundation, Not A Schedule Milestone

The baseline's purpose is to enable measurement: variance, earned value, forecast accuracy, and change tracking all depend on a stable reference. If you treat it as merely the date the plan was frozen, you will baseline whatever is handy and inherit a reference that cannot support honest measurement. Establish the baseline with measurement in mind: it must be detailed enough to compare against, structured enough to roll up, and stable enough that deviations are meaningful. A baseline that cannot support measurement is just a snapshot.

### Establish All Three Core Components Together

A complete performance measurement baseline has three interlocking components: the scope baseline, the schedule baseline, and the cost baseline. Each must be consistent with the others: the schedule must deliver the scope, the cost must fund the schedule's work, and the scope must be decomposed into the work packages the schedule and cost are built on. Baselining one without the others, or with inconsistencies between them, produces a reference where scope, time, and cost disagree from day one. Establish them as an integrated set, and cross-check for consistency before approval.

### Decompose Scope Into Measurable Work Packages

The scope baseline must be broken down into work packages small enough to be estimated, assigned, tracked, and verified. A scope statement at the deliverable level is not a baseline; it is an aspiration. The work breakdown structure is what turns scope into something measurable, because each package has defined boundaries, acceptance criteria, and a cost and schedule home. Without this decomposition, variance cannot be localized and earned value cannot be computed meaningfully. Insist on adequate decomposition as a baseline completeness criterion.

### Define Schedule Completeness Before Freezing It

A schedule is baseline-ready only when its critical dependencies are mapped, its critical path is identified, its milestones have firm dates tied to the work, and resource assignments are feasible. A schedule frozen with unresolved dependencies, unspecified milestones, or unvalidated resource assumptions is a baseline that will mislead. Define explicit readiness criteria for the schedule and verify them before treating it as the reference. A baseline built on unresolved assumptions inherits those assumptions as hidden risk.

### Tie Cost To The Work Breakdown Structure

The cost baseline must be derived from the same work packages as the schedule, so that cost, schedule, and scope share a common structure. When cost is estimated at a different granularity than the work, or assigned top-down without decomposition, variance cannot be traced to its source and earned value becomes unreliable. The cost baseline includes the time-phased budget against which spending is compared, not just a total. Ensure the cost baseline is structured to support measurement, not merely to sum to a target.

### Set And Enforce Baseline Completeness Criteria

Before approval, the baseline must meet defined completeness criteria: scope decomposed to measurable packages, schedule with mapped dependencies and identified critical path, cost tied to the WBS and time-phased, acceptance criteria defined, assumptions documented, and risks reflected. Make these criteria explicit and check each one. A baseline that passes a checklist but fails a criterion is not ready, and approving it anyway stores up measurement problems for the entire project. The criteria are the defense against baselining under pressure.

### Secure Formal Approval Against The Criteria

A baseline is not a baseline until an accountable authority approves it against the completeness criteria. Approval establishes the reference as a commitment, not a draft. Without approval, the baseline is a suggestion that drifts with every conversation. Route the baseline, with its criteria verification, to the sponsor or governance body for formal sign-off, and record the approval. This is what converts a plan into a measurement standard.

### Document Assumptions And Baseline Basis

A baseline without its assumptions is uninterpretable later, because no one can reconstruct why it looks the way it does. Record the estimating basis, the key assumptions, the excluded items, and the known risks at the time of baselining. When variance appears, this basis is what lets you tell whether reality diverged from the plan or the plan was built on a flawed premise. The basis is part of the baseline, not an optional appendix.

## Common Traps

### Freezing Whatever Exists At The Deadline

Under schedule pressure, the team baselines the current draft rather than a complete, consistent plan. The trap is that the reference is flawed from the start, and every later measurement is measured against a broken standard. Hold the completeness criteria.

### Baselining One Component In isolation

The schedule is frozen while the budget is still top-down, or the scope is approved while the WBS is incomplete. The trap is that the components disagree, and variance becomes untraceable. Establish all three together, cross-checked.

### Scope Too Coarse To Measure

The scope baseline lists deliverables but not decomposed work packages, so variance cannot be localized and earned value is meaningless. The trap is a baseline that looks complete but cannot support measurement. Decompose to work packages.

### Schedule Frozen With Unresolved Dependencies

Dependencies are guessed, the critical path is unidentified, and the baseline hides structural risk. The trap is that the reference is unstable and slippage appears to come from nowhere. Verify schedule readiness first.

### Cost Not Tied To The WBS

The budget is allocated top-down at a different granularity than the work, so cost variance cannot be traced. The trap is an unmeasurable cost baseline. Tie cost to the same packages as the schedule.

### Approving Without Criteria Verification

The baseline is approved on trust or under pressure, without checking completeness criteria. The trap is that gaps surface during execution as unexplained variance. Check each criterion explicitly before approval.

### No Documented Basis Or Assumptions

The baseline is approved without recording its estimating basis, assumptions, or exclusions. The trap is that later variance is uninterpretable. Document the basis as part of the baseline.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the baseline established as a measurement foundation, with enough detail, structure, and stability to support variance and earned value?
- [ ] Are the scope, schedule, and cost baselines established together and cross-checked for internal consistency?
- [ ] Is the scope decomposed into measurable work packages with defined acceptance criteria, rather than left at the deliverable level?
- [ ] Does the schedule meet explicit readiness criteria, including mapped dependencies, identified critical path, firm milestones, and feasible resource assignments, before freezing?
- [ ] Is the cost baseline derived from and tied to the same work breakdown structure as the schedule, and time-phased rather than a single total?
- [ ] Are baseline completeness criteria defined and verified for each component before approval?
- [ ] Has the baseline received formal approval from an accountable authority against the criteria, with the approval recorded?
- [ ] Are the estimating basis, key assumptions, excluded items, and known risks documented as part of the baseline?
- [ ] Can the baseline support meaningful measurement of scope, schedule, and cost variance from the start of execution?
- [ ] Is the baseline treated as a commitment and reference, not as a draft subject to informal drift?
