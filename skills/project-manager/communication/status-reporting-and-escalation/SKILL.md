# Status Reporting And Escalation

Project communication is not a transcript of activity. It is a decision support tool. Good status reporting tells stakeholders what changed, whether the project is healthy, what decisions are needed, what risks threaten delivery, and what will happen next. Poor status reporting lists busy work while hiding the information that would allow people to help.

Use this skill before preparing weekly updates, executive summaries, stakeholder emails, steering committee notes, escalation messages, launch readiness summaries, or project health reports. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing vague progress language that does not support action.

## Core Rules

### Lead With Health And Change

Start by stating overall health and what changed since the last update. Stakeholders need orientation before details.

Cover:

- overall status: on track, at risk, blocked, or changed;
- major changes since last update;
- milestone movement;
- new risks or issues;
- decisions needed;
- next critical work.

Avoid opening with a long list of completed tasks. Activity matters only insofar as it changes delivery confidence, risk, or decisions.

### Use Consistent Status Definitions

Color or status labels are useful only if they mean something. Define what green, yellow, red, blocked, or at risk means.

Example:

- Green: project is expected to meet agreed scope, date, and quality with normal management.
- Yellow: project can still meet goals but requires active mitigation or decision.
- Red: project will miss a major commitment unless scope, date, resources, or risk acceptance changes.
- Blocked: work cannot proceed without a specific external action or decision.

Do not use yellow as a polite green or green as a political shield.

### Report Milestones, Not Just Tasks

Executives and cross-functional stakeholders usually care about milestone health and decision points more than every task.

Include:

- last milestone achieved;
- next milestone;
- target date;
- confidence;
- changes from previous forecast;
- blockers;
- owner;
- acceptance criteria.

If a milestone date moves, explain why and what tradeoff is proposed.

### Make Decisions Explicit

Many projects are delayed by unnamed decisions. A status update should separate information from decision requests.

For each decision needed, state:

- decision question;
- decision owner;
- options;
- recommendation;
- deadline;
- impact of delay;
- affected scope, schedule, cost, quality, or risk.

Do not bury decisions inside paragraphs.

### Escalate With Enough Context To Act

Escalation should be concise but complete. The receiver should know why the issue matters and what action is requested.

An escalation should include:

- problem statement;
- current facts;
- impact;
- attempted mitigation;
- options;
- recommendation;
- decision or help needed;
- deadline;
- owner for follow-up.

Avoid escalating only emotion: "we are blocked and need help". Say what help.

### Tailor Detail To Audience

Different audiences need different levels of detail.

Executives need impact, risk, decisions, and tradeoffs. Delivery teams need blockers, owners, dependencies, and next actions. Customers may need expectation, timing, impact, and support path. Vendors need commitments and dependencies. Legal or compliance needs decision evidence and risk acceptance.

Do not send the same update to everyone if it hides what each audience needs.

### Keep A Written Decision Trail

Status communication should create a record of decisions, changes, and accepted risks. This protects alignment when memories differ later.

Record:

- scope changes;
- date changes;
- risk acceptance;
- major tradeoffs;
- dependency commitments;
- approval decisions;
- action owners and due dates.

Meeting notes should not be a transcript. They should capture decisions and actions.

### Show Forecast Confidence

A status update should not only say where the project is today. It should say how confident the project manager is about the next commitment. A project can have no current blocker and still have low confidence because an external review is late, testing has not started, or a critical owner is unavailable.

For important milestones, state whether the date is committed, forecast, tentative, or at risk. Explain the main confidence drivers:

- completed prerequisites;
- remaining critical path work;
- unresolved decisions;
- external dependencies;
- review or approval risk;
- quality signals;
- capacity constraints.

This helps stakeholders distinguish normal uncertainty from unmanaged risk. It also prevents the false comfort of a green report that only describes the past.

## Common Traps

### Activity Theater

"Held meetings, continued work, aligned with teams" may be true but not useful. Report outcomes, decisions, risks, and next steps.

### Surprise Red Status

If a project suddenly turns red, warning signs were probably missed or underreported. Use yellow early enough for action.

### Vague Blockers

"Blocked by legal" or "waiting on engineering" is not actionable. Name the exact decision, document, reviewer, or dependency.

### No Ask In An Escalation

Stakeholders cannot help if they do not know what decision or intervention is requested.

### Overloading Updates With Raw Detail

Too much task-level detail can hide the message. Use appendices or links for those who need depth.

### Failing To Close The Loop

When a stakeholder makes a decision or provides help, confirm the outcome and next action. Otherwise alignment decays.

### Hiding Confidence Behind Exact Dates

Exact dates look authoritative, but they can mislead when confidence is low. A status report should make uncertainty visible rather than presenting every forecast as a promise.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The update starts with overall health and what changed since the last update.
- [ ] Status labels have clear definitions and are applied honestly.
- [ ] Milestone health, forecast, confidence, and movement are visible.
- [ ] Committed dates, forecast dates, tentative dates, and at-risk dates are distinguished where relevant.
- [ ] Decisions needed are separated from general information and include owner, options, recommendation, deadline, and impact of delay.
- [ ] Escalations include facts, impact, attempted mitigation, options, recommendation, specific ask, and deadline.
- [ ] The message is tailored to audience needs rather than copied unchanged to everyone.
- [ ] Risks, issues, blockers, and dependencies are specific enough to act on.
- [ ] Scope, date, risk, and decision changes are recorded for future reference.
- [ ] Completed work is framed by its effect on delivery confidence, not as activity theater; [ ] Every action item has an owner and due date
