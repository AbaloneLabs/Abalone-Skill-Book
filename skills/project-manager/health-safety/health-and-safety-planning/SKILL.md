# Health and Safety Planning

Health and safety is the one project dimension where failure causes harm to people, not merely cost or delay. This gives it a priority that is not negotiable: no schedule, budget, or scope objective justifies a preventable injury. Yet safety is frequently treated as a compliance overlay, a set of procedures and paperwork added to satisfy regulation, rather than as a planning discipline that designs harm out of the work. The result is projects with safety documents that do not reflect the actual hazards, controls that exist on paper but not in practice, and a culture where production pressure quietly erodes safety until an incident reveals the gap.

The judgment problem is how to plan safety so that it identifies the real hazards, designs effective controls, and survives the production pressure that will test it during execution. Agents tend to treat safety planning as paperwork, under-identify hazards, and assume that procedures alone will protect people without the cultural and operational commitment that makes them work.

## Core Rules

### Treat Safety as a Planning Discipline, Not a Paperwork Exercise

Safety planning is the process of identifying what could harm people, designing the work to eliminate or control those hazards, and verifying the controls are in place and effective. It is not the production of a safety document to satisfy a regulator. A safety plan that is written to satisfy process but does not reflect the actual work and hazards provides no protection. Plan safety as you plan the work: integrated, specific, and operationally real.

### Identify Hazards Systematically Before Designing Controls

Effective safety planning starts with systematic hazard identification: what are the energies, materials, conditions, and activities that could cause harm, and who could be exposed. Use structured methods, job hazard analysis, preliminary hazard analysis, and domain-specific checklists, and involve the people who will do the work, who know the hazards that paperwork misses. Designing controls before hazards are fully identified produces gaps. Identify comprehensively, then control.

### Apply the Hierarchy of Controls, Not Just Procedures

The hierarchy of controls, elimination, substitution, engineering controls, administrative controls, and personal protective equipment, ranks controls by reliability. Eliminating a hazard is more reliable than procedure or PPE, which depend on human behavior under pressure. Safety planning that defaults to procedure and PPE, because elimination and engineering are harder or more expensive, produces controls that fail when people are tired, rushed, or pressured. Push up the hierarchy: prefer controls that do not depend on perfect human behavior.

### Make Safety Roles and Responsibilities Explicit

Safety requires clear roles: who is responsible for what, who has authority to stop work, who verifies controls, who investigates incidents. Ambiguity in safety roles produces gaps where no one acts. Define the roles explicitly, ensure the people in them are competent and empowered, and make sure the authority to stop unsafe work is real and protected. A stop-work authority that people are afraid to use provides no protection.

### Integrate Safety Into the Schedule and Method, Not Alongside It

Safety must be integrated into how the work is sequenced and performed, not added as a separate consideration. The schedule must allow time for safety activities: hazard assessment before tasks, controls installation, permits, and safety holds. The method must reflect the controls: if a task requires isolation, the plan includes the isolation. A schedule that assumes work proceeds without safety time produces pressure to skip safety steps. Integrate safety into the plan's logic.

### Plan for the High-Risk and Non-Routine, Not Just the Routine

Most incidents occur during non-routine work, startups, shutdowns, maintenance, abnormal conditions, and high-risk activities, because the controls developed for routine operation do not fit. Identify the non-routine and high-risk activities and plan them specifically: permits, additional controls, supervision, and contingency. A safety plan that covers only routine operation leaves the highest-risk moments unprotected. Plan deliberately for the unusual.

### Involve the Workforce in Safety Planning

The people who do the work know the hazards that planners miss: the awkward access, the unreliable equipment, the pressure to rush. Involving the workforce in hazard identification and control design produces more effective safety planning and builds the ownership that makes controls work. Safety planned without workforce input is safety that does not match reality. Involve the people who will be exposed to the hazards.

### Plan for Incident Response and Emergency

Despite planning, incidents can occur, and the response in the first minutes determines the severity of harm. Plan for emergency response: evacuation, medical response, notification, and scene preservation. Ensure the people on site know the response, the equipment is available, and the plan is practiced. A project with safety controls but no emergency response plan leaves the consequences of any failure uncontrolled. Plan for the response as deliberately as for prevention.

## Common Traps

### Safety as Paperwork

The safety plan is written to satisfy process but does not reflect the actual work and hazards. The trap is that the document feels like the deliverable. Plan operationally.

### Incomplete Hazard Identification

Hazards are missed because identification was not systematic or did not involve the workforce, leaving gaps in controls. The trap is that the obvious hazards feel sufficient. Identify systematically.

### Defaulting to Procedure and PPE

Controls rely on human behavior under pressure rather than elimination or engineering, and fail when pressure mounts. The trap is that procedure feels cheaper. Push up the hierarchy.

### Ambiguous Safety Roles

No one is clearly responsible or empowered, producing gaps where no one acts. The trap is that roles feel understood. Define them explicitly.

### Safety Time Not in the Schedule

The schedule assumes work without safety time, creating pressure to skip steps. The trap is that safety feels like it should fit in. Integrate into the schedule.

### Non-Routine Work Unplanned

The highest-risk activities are left to routine controls that do not fit, and incidents occur. The trap is that routine feels comprehensive. Plan for the non-routine.

### Workforce Not Involved

Hazards known to the workforce are missed, and controls lack the ownership that makes them work. The trap is that planning feels like a specialist job. Involve the workforce.

### No Emergency Response Plan

An incident occurs and the unprepared response increases the harm. The trap is that prevention feels sufficient. Plan the response.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is safety planning treated as an operational discipline integrated with work planning, not as paperwork?
- [ ] Have hazards been identified systematically using structured methods and workforce involvement, not just obvious hazards?
- [ ] Do controls push up the hierarchy (elimination, substitution, engineering) rather than defaulting to procedure and PPE?
- [ ] Are safety roles and responsibilities explicit, with competent people and real, protected stop-work authority?
- [ ] Is safety integrated into the schedule and method, with time for assessment, controls, permits, and holds?
- [ ] Are high-risk and non-routine activities (startup, shutdown, maintenance, abnormal conditions) planned specifically?
- [ ] Has the workforce been involved in hazard identification and control design?
- [ ] Is there an emergency response plan that people know, with available equipment and practice?
- [ ] Would the safety plan withstand scrutiny against the actual work, or only against a checklist?
- [ ] Is there any production pressure under which the safety controls would be bypassed, and what prevents that?
