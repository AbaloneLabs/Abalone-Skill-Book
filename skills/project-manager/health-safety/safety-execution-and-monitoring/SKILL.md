# Safety Execution and Monitoring

A safety plan is only a promise until it is executed and verified. During execution, the controls designed in planning meet the reality of production pressure, changing conditions, human behavior, and the drift that erodes controls over time. The gap between planned safety and actual safety is where harm occurs, and closing that gap requires active monitoring, not passive assumption. Most incidents are preceded by warning signs, near-misses, deteriorating conditions, and control failures, that a monitoring system would have caught. The failure is rarely the absence of a plan; it is the absence of vigilance that lets the plan erode.

The judgment problem is how to execute safety controls under real conditions, how to monitor leading indicators that warn before harm occurs, and how to maintain safety vigilance when production pressure and familiarity push toward complacency. Agents tend to treat monitoring as compliance checking, react to lagging indicators after harm, and allow production pressure to quietly override safety controls.

## Core Rules

### Verify Controls Are in Place Before Work Proceeds

Before any task with safety controls begins, verify that the controls are actually in place and effective: the isolation is locked, the permit is issued, the PPE is worn, the competent person is present. Assuming controls are in place because they were planned is a common precursor to incidents. Use pre-task verification, permits-to-work, and checklists to confirm. A control that exists in the plan but not on the ground provides no protection. Verify, do not assume.

### Monitor Leading Indicators, Not Just Lagging

Lagging indicators, injury rates and incident counts, confirm harm has occurred; they are accurate but too late to prevent it. Leading indicators, near-miss reports, control observations, safety interactions, permit compliance, and training completion, signal whether the conditions that produce harm are present before harm occurs. A monitoring system of only lagging indicators reports history. Build leading indicators into monitoring, and act on their signals before they become lagging ones.

### Build a Near-Miss Reporting Culture

Near-misses are free lessons: warnings that a control failed or a hazard nearly caused harm, without the harm. A culture that reports near-misses provides early warning; one that reports only incidents loses the warning and waits for the harm. Build a reporting culture: make reporting easy and blame-free, act visibly on reports so people see they matter, and share the lessons. The number of near-miss reports going up is a sign of a healthy reporting culture, not of deteriorating safety.

### Resist the Drift Toward Normalized Deviance

Over time, under familiarity and production pressure, small deviations from safe practice become accepted as normal: a step skipped, a control relaxed, a procedure abbreviated. Each deviation seems harmless because nothing went wrong, so the next deviation is easier, until the accumulated drift has eroded the safety margin and an incident occurs. Monitor for drift: observe actual practice against the standard, investigate deviations, and re-set the standard when it slips. Complacency is not the absence of risk; it is the blindness to accumulated risk.

### Conduct Safety Observations and Audits on the Work, Not the Paperwork

Safety audits that review documents and procedures confirm the paperwork is in order but may miss that actual practice differs. Audit the work: observe tasks being performed, talk to the workforce, verify controls on the ground. The gap between document and practice is where harm hides. Combine document review with work observation, and treat gaps between the two as findings requiring action.

### Empower and Protect Stop-Work Authority

Anyone who identifies an unsafe condition must have the authority to stop the work, and must be protected from consequences for using that authority. Stop-work authority that exists in policy but is culturally unusable, because using it is treated as obstruction or weakness, provides no protection. Make stop-work a recognized, supported action, and respond to stops by addressing the hazard, not by pressuring the person who stopped. The test of stop-work authority is whether people use it.

### Respond to Every Signal, Not Just Incidents

Every near-miss, control failure, observation, and audit finding is a signal that deserves a response. A monitoring system that collects signals but does not act on them teaches people that reporting is pointless and allows conditions to deteriorate to incident. Triage signals, assign owners, and close out actions. The response to signals is what makes monitoring effective; collection without response is bureaucratic theater.

### Monitor Cumulative Risk and Fatigue

Individual tasks may be safe, but cumulative risk, fatigue, long hours, consecutive high-risk activities, and overlapping hazards, can create conditions where a single error causes harm. Monitor workload, hours, and the density of high-risk work, and intervene when cumulative risk rises. A workforce that is fatigued or overloaded is more error-prone, and the safety controls that depend on human performance degrade. Treat fatigue and overload as safety hazards, not just productivity concerns.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Controls Are in Place

Work proceeds on the assumption that planned controls are active, when they are not, and an incident occurs. The trap is that planning feels like assurance. Verify before work proceeds.

### Monitoring Only Lagging Indicators

The monitoring system reports only incidents, so action is always after harm. The trap is that lagging indicators feel concrete. Add leading indicators.

### Near-Misses Not Reported

Warnings go unreported because of blame or futility, and the next warning becomes an incident. The trap is that low reporting feels like high safety. Build a reporting culture.

### Normalized Deviance

Small deviations accumulate into eroded safety margins, accepted because nothing went wrong, until something does. The trap is that each deviation feels harmless. Monitor and reset the standard.

### Auditing Paperwork Instead of Work

Audits confirm documents are in order but miss that practice differs. The trap is that document review feels thorough. Observe the work.

### Stop-Work Authority Unusable in Practice

The authority exists in policy but people are afraid to use it, so it provides no protection. The trap is that the policy feels sufficient. Make it culturally real.

### Signals Collected but Not Acted On

Reports and findings accumulate without response, teaching people that monitoring is pointless. The trap is that collection feels like action. Respond to every signal.

### Cumulative Risk and Fatigue Ignored

Individually safe tasks combine with fatigue to create error-prone conditions. The trap is that individual task safety feels sufficient. Monitor cumulative risk.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are safety controls verified to be in place and effective before tasks proceed, not assumed from the plan?
- [ ] Does the monitoring system include leading indicators (near-misses, observations, control compliance), not just lagging incident counts?
- [ ] Is there a blame-free near-miss reporting culture, with visible action on reports?
- [ ] Is normalized deviance monitored, with actual practice observed against standards and deviations re-set?
- [ ] Do safety audits observe the work and the gap between document and practice, not just review paperwork?
- [ ] Is stop-work authority culturally usable and protected, with the test being whether people actually use it?
- [ ] Does every near-miss, observation, and finding receive a response with an owner and closure?
- [ ] Are cumulative risk, fatigue, and workload monitored and intervened upon when they rise?
- [ ] Would the workforce say safety monitoring is genuine and responsive, or bureaucratic and ignored?
- [ ] Are warning signs acted upon before they become incidents, or only after?
