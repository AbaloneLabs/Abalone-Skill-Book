# Continuity Exercise And Plan Maintenance

Continuity plans decay unless they are exercised and maintained. Names change, vendors change, tools change, manual workarounds break, and people forget what the plan says. Agents often treat a plan document as proof of readiness, but continuity readiness is proven by execution under realistic conditions. This skill helps the agent design exercises and maintenance routines that reveal gaps before a real disruption.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Exercise Objective

Define what the exercise is testing: decision rights, critical process fallback, communication, manual records, vendor escalation, remote work, site loss, data recovery, staffing shortage, or executive escalation. A broad exercise with no objective creates vague lessons.

Focus on the highest-risk assumptions in the continuity plan. If an assumption is untested and critical, test it first.

### Use Realistic Scenarios

Build scenarios that stress actual dependencies: system outage during peak, vendor failure before cutoff, site closure, simultaneous staff absence, lost access, network outage, power loss, severe weather, or data restore from backup. Include time pressure and incomplete information.

Avoid exercises that everyone can pass because the scenario is too clean.

### Test Roles And Decision Rights

Exercises should verify that people know who leads, who approves service degradation, who contacts vendors, who communicates externally, who accepts risk, and who owns recovery. If the named person is unavailable, test the backup.

Role confusion during an exercise is useful evidence. Fix the plan rather than explaining it away.

Include people who would actually execute the plan, not only managers. Frontline staff, shift leads, vendor contacts, and support functions often reveal gaps that planners miss.

### Validate Contact Paths And Access

Check phone numbers, distribution lists, vendor contacts, emergency channels, system access, shared folders, offline copies, and alternate communication tools. Contact lists often fail quietly until a real event.

Access tests should include permissions needed for manual fallback, emergency approvals, and recovery work.

### Exercise Manual Fallback And Reconciliation

Do not only discuss manual fallback. Walk through records, required fields, approval, privacy handling, later system entry, duplicate prevention, and reconciliation. If possible, process a sample case through the fallback.

Manual procedures that work in theory can fail because forms are missing, fields are unclear, or staff do not know where to store evidence.

Measure how long the fallback takes and what capacity it can support. A fallback that works for one sample may still be unusable for expected incident volume.

### Capture Gaps As Actions

Record gaps with owner, due date, risk, dependency, and verification method. Distinguish documentation updates from capability gaps such as missing backup access or vendor escalation failure.

An exercise that produces lessons but no tracked actions does not improve readiness.

### Retest Material Gaps

High-risk gaps should be retested after remediation. Updating the plan is not enough if the gap involved access, vendor response, manual processing, communication, or decision rights.

Retesting shows whether the corrective action works under the scenario.

Track who did not participate and how they will be brought current. Continuity readiness cannot depend only on the people who happened to attend the exercise.

### Maintain Plans After Change

Trigger plan review after process changes, system changes, automation, vendor changes, staffing changes, site changes, product launches, regulatory changes, major incidents, and organizational redesign. Do not wait for the annual review if a critical dependency changed yesterday.

Assign plan owners and review cadence. A plan without an owner decays by default.

### Report Readiness Honestly

Communicate what was tested, what was not tested, what failed, what risk remains, and what actions are open. Avoid declaring readiness based on a narrow tabletop that did not test critical execution.

Leaders need a clear view of residual continuity risk, not just a green exercise status.

Use readiness language carefully. "Tabletop completed" is not the same as "manual fallback proven" or "alternate site ready." The report should state the level of evidence behind each readiness claim.

## Common Traps

- Treating a signed plan as proof of continuity readiness.
- Running exercises with no specific objective or risk assumption.
- Choosing scenarios that are too easy, clean, or familiar.
- Testing the primary owner but not backups or unavailable-person scenarios.
- Reviewing contact lists visually without actually validating channels.
- Discussing manual fallback without testing records, approvals, storage, and reconciliation.
- Logging lessons learned without owners, dates, and verification; updating documentation but not retesting high-risk gaps
- Waiting for annual review despite major process, vendor, or system changes; reporting readiness without saying what was not tested

## Self-Check

- Does the exercise have a clear objective tied to a critical continuity assumption?
- Is the scenario realistic enough to stress dependencies and decision making?
- Are roles, backups, decision rights, and risk acceptance tested?
- Are contact paths, emergency channels, vendor contacts, and access verified?
- Is manual fallback tested through records, approvals, privacy, entry, and reconciliation?
- Are gaps captured as actions with owner, due date, risk, and verification?
- Are material gaps retested after remediation?
- Are plan reviews triggered by operating changes, not only annual cadence?
- Is a plan owner accountable for maintenance and readiness evidence?
- Does readiness reporting distinguish tested capability, untested scope, failures, and residual risk?
