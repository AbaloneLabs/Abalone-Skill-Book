# Performance Metric Integrity And Gaming Prevention

Metrics shape behavior. In operations, a poorly designed measure can make people close work too early, avoid hard cases, hide backlog, skip quality, discourage escalations, or serve the dashboard instead of the customer. This skill helps the agent review whether performance metrics preserve operating truth and encourage the right behavior under pressure.

## Core Rules

### Identify the behavior the metric rewards

For each metric or target, ask what behavior it rewards and what behavior it discourages. Throughput may reward speed; handle time may discourage careful resolution; SLA attainment may encourage reclassification; utilization may discourage training or recovery; quality pass rate may discourage honest sampling.

The stated purpose of a metric is less important than the behavior it predictably creates.

### Pair speed with quality and outcome

Speed measures need balancing signals: defect rate, rework, repeat contact, reopen rate, customer effort, quality sampling, control exceptions, employee fatigue, and downstream backlog. A fast process that creates repeated work is not high-performing.

Do not let one metric define success. Operations health is multi-dimensional: service, quality, cost, control, customer impact, staff sustainability, and learning.

### Check for gaming patterns

Look for suspicious shifts: cases closed then reopened, status changes before deadlines, work moved out of measured queues, exception labels increasing, quality samples avoided, difficult cases parked, customer contacts split or merged, manual edits near reporting cutoffs, or overtime hidden from capacity reports.

Gaming may be intentional misconduct, but it may also be a response to impossible targets or metric design. Diagnose both behavior and system pressure.

### Review targets before enforcing consequences

Before using a metric for discipline, bonus, staffing judgment, or public ranking, confirm that the target is attainable under current demand, staffing, tool reliability, case mix, and policy constraints. A target that was reasonable last quarter may become unethical pressure after volume, product, or data changes.

If consequences attach to a metric, the evidence standard should be higher. Staff must know the definition, how disputes are handled, and what context is considered.

### Protect raw data and audit trails

Metric integrity depends on traceable data. Define who can edit statuses, close cases, override timestamps, change categories, exclude records, or modify reports. Sensitive metric changes should be logged and reviewed.

Avoid manual adjustments without reason, owner, and version history. If corrections are needed, preserve both the original record and the corrected interpretation.

### Align incentives with truthful escalation

If managers are penalized for reporting risks, they may delay escalation. If staff lose rewards for complex cases, they may avoid them. Incentives should encourage accurate reporting, early risk surfacing, and sustainable performance, not only green dashboards.

Include leading indicators and learning measures where appropriate. Near misses, defect reporting, coaching completion, and root-cause closure can support a healthier culture when used carefully.

### Communicate metric limits

Every metric has blind spots. Explain what the metric does not show, which segments it hides, whether it is lagging, whether it is sample-based, and where data quality is weak. Leaders should not use a metric for decisions it cannot support.

When a target is missed, ask what changed in demand, capacity, complexity, tools, policy, or data before judging performance.

### Review metrics after process changes

Automation, policy updates, queue redesign, staffing changes, new customer segments, or tool migrations can change metric meaning. Revalidate definitions and targets after operational change. Historical comparisons may become invalid.

If the metric no longer reflects the intended outcome, revise it rather than coaching people to fit an obsolete measure.

### Include independent review for high-stakes metrics

Metrics used for pay, promotion, discipline, vendor penalties, regulatory reporting, or customer commitments should receive independent review. Check data lineage, sampling, exclusions, overrides, and appeal path. High-stakes metrics should not depend on unreviewed local spreadsheets or one manager's interpretation.

## Common Traps

- Believing a green dashboard proves ethical, healthy operations.
- Rewarding speed without quality, customer, control, and sustainability measures.
- Treating metric gaming only as individual misconduct while ignoring impossible targets.
- Allowing manual metric adjustments without audit trail.
- Penalizing early escalation and then wondering why risks appear late.
- Enforcing consequences from targets that were not revalidated against current operating conditions.
- Using a metric beyond its design purpose.
- Using high-stakes metrics without independent review or appeal path; keeping targets unchanged after the work mix or data definition changes

## Self-Check

- Has the expected behavior created by each metric or target been identified?
- Are speed and productivity balanced by quality, rework, customer, control, and staff sustainability signals?
- Have gaming patterns been checked in status changes, exclusions, reopens, queues, sampling, and cutoff behavior?
- Are targets revalidated before being used for discipline, bonuses, staffing judgment, or public ranking?
- Are status edits, closures, overrides, report changes, and manual adjustments auditable?
- Do incentives support truthful reporting and early escalation rather than only green results?
- Are metric limits, blind spots, segment gaps, lag, sampling, and data quality caveats visible?
- Are missed targets interpreted against demand, capacity, complexity, tools, policy, and data changes?
- Are metrics revalidated after process, automation, staffing, customer, or system changes?
- Do high-stakes metrics have independent review, data lineage, exclusion review, and appeal path?
