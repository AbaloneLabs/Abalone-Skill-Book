# Metric Gaming And Balanced Scorecard

Metrics become incentives even when leaders say they are only for visibility. If one number receives attention, teams may optimize for that number in ways that harm customers, quality, fairness, compliance, or future capacity. Agents often recommend targets without considering how people will respond. This skill helps the agent anticipate gaming and design balanced scorecards that make harmful tradeoffs visible.

## Core Rules

### Identify Who Is Judged By The Metric

Start by naming who is evaluated, rewarded, penalized, or scrutinized based on the metric: frontline workers, team leads, vendors, managers, sites, regions, or functions. The stronger the consequence, the stronger the gaming pressure.

Even informal attention matters. A number that leaders praise publicly may shape behavior as much as a bonus plan.

### Map The Likely Gaming Paths

For each target, ask how someone could improve the number without improving the outcome. Throughput can be gamed by cherry-picking easy work. SLA can be gamed by pausing clocks or closing prematurely. Quality can be gamed by sampling low-risk work. Utilization can be gamed by staying busy on low-value tasks. Customer satisfaction can be gamed by avoiding hard cases or asking selectively for feedback.

Do this before rollout. After gaming behavior becomes normal, it is harder to unwind.

### Pair Metrics With Guardrails

Use counter-metrics that reveal tradeoffs. Pair speed with quality and rework. Pair cost with service level and risk events. Pair utilization with backlog age and employee load. Pair customer satisfaction with complaint rate and repeat contact. Pair vendor productivity with audit findings and escalation quality.

Guardrails should be few enough to use. A balanced scorecard is not a wall of numbers; it is a small set of measures that prevent one-dimensional optimization.

### Segment Incentives Fairly

People should not be compared on metrics without accounting for work mix, complexity, shift, tenure, region, tool access, customer segment, and case assignment. If one team receives harder work, raw productivity comparisons can punish the team doing the most valuable work.

Fair segmentation does not mean explaining away every difference. It means comparisons should be meaningful enough to guide action without creating perverse pressure.

### Audit Metric Integrity

Review status changes, paused clocks, reopened work, sampling choices, manual adjustments, assignment patterns, exception use, and sudden changes in mix. Look for improvements that are too sudden, too localized, or disconnected from customer and quality outcomes.

Audit should be routine, not accusatory. The goal is to protect metric trust and identify system incentives that make gaming attractive.

### Avoid Single-Number Performance Management

Do not use one KPI as the primary basis for coaching, ranking, vendor payment, or leadership judgment. Operational performance includes speed, quality, judgment, teamwork, customer impact, adherence to controls, improvement, and resilience.

When individual metrics are necessary, combine them with manager review, quality evidence, workload context, and behavior expectations. Avoid reducing complex work to a scoreboard.

### Watch For Hidden Work And Harm

Gaming often moves harm out of view. Staff may leave difficult items for others, under-document, avoid escalation, transfer work prematurely, defer low-tier customers, or spend time making dashboards look clean. Customers may experience repeated contacts even while closure metrics improve.

Ask what work disappeared from the metric and where it may have gone.

### Align Vendor And Internal Scorecards

Vendor scorecards are especially prone to narrow optimization because contract terms may attach money to metrics. Ensure vendor targets include quality, timeliness, security, rework, escalation, customer impact, and cooperation, not only volume or cost.

Internal teams that receive vendor output should have a voice in scorecard design. Otherwise the vendor may meet its metric while downstream teams absorb cleanup.

### Revise Metrics When Behavior Changes

If people are optimizing in harmful ways, do not only tell them to stop. Revisit targets, definitions, incentives, workload assignment, training, controls, and leadership messaging. Behavior follows the system.

Retire metrics that have become untrustworthy or counterproductive. Keeping them for historical continuity can do more harm than good.

## Common Traps

- Assuming a metric will be used neutrally even though people know leaders care about it.
- Setting throughput or productivity targets without checking cherry-picking and quality harm.
- Measuring SLA without auditing pause rules, closure timing, and reopened work.
- Comparing individuals or teams without accounting for work mix and complexity.
- Creating a balanced scorecard so large that no one knows which tradeoff matters.
- Treating audit as blame rather than metric integrity protection.
- Paying vendors for volume while downstream teams carry rework and escalations; using one metric for performance management in complex work
- Ignoring hidden work, deferred customers, and under-documentation because headline metrics improved; responding to gaming only with reminders instead of changing incentives and definitions

## Self-Check

- Has the agent identified who is judged, rewarded, penalized, or scrutinized by the metric?
- Are likely gaming paths named for each important metric?
- Are speed, quality, cost, service, risk, workload, and customer impact balanced by guardrail metrics?
- Are comparisons segmented for work mix, complexity, shift, tenure, region, and assignment differences?
- Are metric integrity audits defined for status changes, pauses, reopenings, sampling, manual edits, and exceptions?
- Is performance management protected from overreliance on a single number?
- Has the review looked for hidden work or harm moved outside the metric?
- Do vendor scorecards include downstream quality, rework, escalation, and cooperation?
- Are harmful behaviors treated as system feedback, not only individual misconduct?
- Is there a path to revise or retire metrics that create distorted behavior?
