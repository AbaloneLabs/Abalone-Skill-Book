# Outsourcing Governance And Performance Review

Outsourced work still requires active operational governance. A contract and dashboard do not ensure that service, quality, controls, and customer experience remain healthy. Agents often review provider performance through a few SLA numbers and miss rework, knowledge drift, escalation load, customer effort, retained-team burden, and future capacity risk. This skill helps the agent govern outsourced operations as a shared operating system.

## Core Rules

### Keep a retained owner accountable

The organization should name a retained owner for outsourced service health. This owner monitors performance, clarifies policy, manages change, reviews quality, handles escalations, coordinates internal dependencies, and decides when scope or service levels should change.

Do not let procurement or contract management be the only owner if the work affects customers, controls, or operations. Commercial management and operational ownership are related but not the same.

### Use a balanced performance view

Review SLA attainment, backlog, aging, throughput, quality, rework, repeat contact, escalation rate, customer or employee satisfaction, complaints, staffing, attrition, training, knowledge updates, control findings, and provider improvement actions. A green SLA can hide poor experience or excessive internal cleanup.

Segment performance by queue, case type, region, language, shift, complexity, and customer group. Averages hide weak pockets.

### Establish an operating cadence

Set daily, weekly, monthly, and quarterly reviews based on risk and volume. Daily reviews may focus on backlog and escalations; weekly reviews on quality and defects; monthly reviews on trends and improvement; quarterly reviews on strategy, scope, capacity, and commercial issues.

Each cadence should produce decisions or actions. Meetings that only repeat dashboard data waste both organizations' time.

### Govern escalations and disputes

Define how provider operational issues, policy questions, customer complaints, quality disputes, commercial disputes, security incidents, and service failures escalate. Include owner, evidence, response time, decision authority, and communication path.

Escalations should not depend on personal relationships. If the provider must chase informal contacts to resolve work, governance is weak.

### Review retained-team burden

Outsourcing can shift hidden work back to internal teams through approvals, exception handling, rework, provider questions, reporting cleanup, quality review, and customer recovery. Track this retained effort. If internal workload remains high, the outsourcing model may be under-scoped, under-trained, or poorly controlled.

The cost and capacity model should include retained burden, not only provider output.

### Manage change deliberately

Policies, systems, products, scripts, volumes, customer promises, and controls change over time. Provider governance should include change intake, impact assessment, training update, acknowledgement, effective date, testing, and post-change monitoring.

Do not push urgent changes through side-channel messages and expect consistent adoption.

### Hold providers accountable without hiding shared causes

When performance misses, distinguish provider execution failure from unclear scope, delayed internal approvals, bad data, insufficient training, unrealistic volume assumptions, tool outages, or changed requirements. Accountability should be evidence-based.

Shared causes still need owners. Avoid both extremes: blaming the provider for everything or excusing every miss as a dependency.

### Maintain improvement and innovation channels

Providers may see repeat defects, customer confusion, process waste, automation opportunities, and policy gaps. Create a channel for provider improvement ideas and require evidence. Decide which ideas to adopt, reject, pilot, or defer.

Do not limit governance to penalty avoidance. Strong outsourcing relationships improve the operation over time.

### Watch incentive and penalty effects

Commercial terms shape provider behavior. Per-ticket pricing can encourage unnecessary contacts, strict SLA penalties can encourage superficial closure, and aggressive productivity targets can suppress quality. Review whether incentives reward the outcome the organization actually wants.

When incentives conflict with service quality or control, change the measure or add guardrails rather than relying on goodwill.

## Common Traps

- Assuming outsourced work is governed because a contract exists.
- Letting procurement own commercial terms while no operations owner owns service health.
- Reviewing only headline SLA and cost.
- Ignoring rework, repeat contact, complaints, quality, control findings, and internal cleanup.
- Using averages that hide poor performance in a region, shift, language, queue, or case type.
- Holding meetings that do not produce decisions or actions; resolving escalations through personal favors rather than governed paths
- Forgetting retained-team burden when evaluating outsourcing success; sending changes informally without testing, training, acknowledgement, or adoption checks
- Blaming provider misses without checking internal dependency failures and unclear scope; using incentives or penalties that make the provider optimize volume, closure, or avoidance instead of real outcomes

## Self-Check

- Is there a retained operational owner for service health, policy, change, quality, escalation, and internal dependencies?
- Are commercial ownership and operational ownership both clear?
- Does performance review include SLA, backlog, aging, throughput, quality, rework, repeat contact, escalation, satisfaction, complaints, staffing, attrition, training, knowledge, controls, and improvements?
- Is performance segmented by queue, case type, region, language, shift, complexity, and customer group?
- Does the review cadence match risk and produce decisions or actions?
- Are escalation paths defined for operations, policy, customer, quality, commercial, security, and service-failure issues?
- Is retained-team burden tracked for approvals, exceptions, rework, questions, reporting, quality, and recovery?
- Are changes governed through intake, impact assessment, training, acknowledgement, effective date, testing, and monitoring?
- Are performance misses analyzed for provider execution, internal dependency, data, training, tooling, volume, scope, and requirement causes?
- Is there a channel for provider improvement ideas with evidence, decision, pilot, adoption, rejection, or deferral?; are commercial incentives and penalties checked for unintended effects on quality, closure behavior, volume, control, and customer outcome?
