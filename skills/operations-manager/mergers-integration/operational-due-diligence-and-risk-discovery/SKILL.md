# Operational Due Diligence And Risk Discovery

Operational due diligence asks whether the business can keep serving customers, employees, and regulators after a transaction closes. It is not only a document request list. Agents often review visible process descriptions and miss hidden manual work, fragile staffing, aging backlog, informal vendor dependency, weak controls, and service promises that will not survive integration. This skill helps the agent discover operational risk before integration decisions are locked.

## Core Rules

### Translate deal assumptions into operating questions

Identify what the transaction is supposed to achieve: growth, cost synergy, market entry, capability acquisition, customer retention, consolidation, carve-out, or platform migration. Then ask what operations must do for that thesis to hold.

If the deal assumes cross-selling, shared service consolidation, site closure, system migration, vendor rationalization, or headcount reduction, test the operational prerequisites. A synergy target without process, system, staffing, and service evidence is only a financial hope.

### Look for hidden workload and backlog

Request volume, backlog age, rework, escalations, defect queues, manual adjustments, service credits, complaint themes, aging exceptions, unpaid claims, unresolved tickets, and late vendor deliverables. Backlog may be hidden in spreadsheets, email inboxes, personal trackers, local site queues, or relationship-based favors.

Ask how work actually moves, not only how the SOP says it moves. Observe where people wait, chase approvals, copy data between systems, or rely on a key person.

### Assess process maturity and variation

Compare process design across sites, teams, countries, products, and customer segments. Differences are not automatically bad; they may reflect legitimate local needs. But undocumented variation creates integration risk when teams try to standardize too quickly.

Check ownership, standard work, exception handling, metrics, quality review, escalation, and controls. A process that works because a small team knows every exception may not scale into a combined organization.

### Identify key-person and knowledge dependency

Find roles where one person holds customer context, vendor relationship, system workaround, approval knowledge, reporting logic, compliance evidence, or operational history. Key-person dependency is especially risky when transaction uncertainty increases attrition.

Ask what would happen if the person left before close, during day one, or during system migration. The mitigation may be documentation, retention, cross-training, shadowing, or redesign.

### Test vendor and third-party exposure

Review vendor contracts, service levels, concentration, change-of-control clauses, termination rights, data access, subcontractors, renewal timing, support coverage, and informal relationships. Integration can break vendor service even when the contract remains valid.

Check whether vendors support both companies' systems, whether pricing changes after transaction, and whether operational continuity depends on non-contractual favors.

### Review controls and compliance in operating reality

Due diligence should test whether controls are actually performed, evidenced, reviewed, and remediated. Look at access controls, approvals, segregation of duties, safety processes, privacy handling, financial adjustments, inventory controls, complaint handling, and audit findings.

Integration can weaken controls when teams merge queues, grant broad access, or use temporary workarounds. Identify where the target's control environment cannot be absorbed without redesign.

### Evaluate system and data constraints

List systems of record, manual bridges, reporting definitions, data owners, duplicate fields, integration limits, access dependencies, data quality issues, and migration risks. A process may be operationally strong only because local data definitions are understood informally.

Pay attention to customer IDs, product codes, employee IDs, inventory items, vendor masters, contracts, SLA definitions, and status codes. These details drive operational continuity.

### Record risks in integration terms

For each risk, record impact, likelihood, evidence, uncertainty, owner, control, early warning signal, mitigation, decision needed, and post-close timing. Distinguish close blockers, day-one risks, 30-60-90 day risks, and long-term integration risks.

Do not overstate certainty. If information is unavailable because of deal confidentiality or access limits, label the gap and recommend conservative integration planning.

## Common Traps

- Treating operational due diligence as a checklist of policies and org charts.
- Accepting SOPs as proof of how work is actually done.
- Missing backlog hidden in email, spreadsheets, personal trackers, and local queues.
- Assuming process variation is waste without checking local customer, regulatory, site, or product needs.
- Ignoring key-person dependency until transaction uncertainty causes attrition.
- Treating vendor contracts as continuity proof while missing informal support and change-of-control risk.
- Reviewing controls on paper but not evidence, remediation, and operating discipline; ignoring data definitions, identifiers, and status codes that determine integration feasibility
- Expressing deal synergies without operational prerequisites and risk owners; failing to label unknowns caused by confidentiality limits, missing access, or weak data

## Self-Check

- Are deal assumptions translated into operational prerequisites and evidence needs?
- Are hidden backlog, rework, exceptions, complaints, manual adjustments, service credits, and local trackers reviewed?
- Has actual work movement been observed or tested beyond SOP descriptions?
- Are legitimate local variations separated from undocumented process drift?
- Are key-person dependencies mapped for customer context, vendor relationships, system workarounds, reporting, controls, and approvals?
- Are vendor contracts, change-of-control clauses, informal dependencies, support coverage, and concentration risks assessed?
- Are controls tested through evidence, review, remediation, and integration impact?
- Are system and data constraints reviewed for records of truth, identifiers, definitions, access, and migration risk?
- Are risks categorized as close blockers, day-one risks, 30-60-90 day risks, and long-term integration risks?
- Are uncertainty, evidence quality, owner, mitigation, early warning, and decision needed recorded for each material risk?
