# Post Merger Process Integration

Post-merger process integration is not choosing which company's SOP survives. It is redesigning the combined workflow so customers, employees, vendors, controls, data, and service levels continue to work in a larger operating system. Agents often recommend standardization too quickly and miss local constraints, system dependencies, hidden workarounds, and control gaps. This skill helps the agent integrate processes deliberately instead of creating a fragile hybrid.

## Core Rules

### Start from the end-to-end service path

Map the combined process from trigger to outcome across both organizations. Include intake, decision, fulfillment, support, exception handling, escalation, billing or financial treatment, data update, reporting, and closure. The merged process should be judged by the full service path, not by individual team convenience.

Identify where each legacy process differs and why. Differences may reflect customer segment, regulation, product complexity, system capability, geography, staffing model, or historical workaround.

### Decide what to standardize, preserve, or redesign

Not every difference should be eliminated. Standardize where variation creates risk, cost, confusion, poor data, control weakness, or customer inconsistency. Preserve variation where it is required by law, customer contract, site reality, language, product, or channel. Redesign where both legacy processes are weak.

Avoid the false choice between acquirer process and target process. The best combined process may need a new design.

### Test system and data dependencies

Process integration often fails because systems cannot support the desired workflow. Check systems of record, statuses, identifiers, data fields, routing, reporting, permissions, integrations, automation, and audit trails. A process step that is easy in one company may be manual or impossible in the other.

Do not define the future process before knowing whether data migration, tool configuration, and reporting definitions can support it.

### Protect controls during process change

Merging queues, approvals, roles, and systems can weaken segregation of duties, access control, financial approval, privacy handling, safety review, complaint handling, and audit evidence. Define which controls must remain, which change, and how evidence will be captured during transition.

Temporary control gaps should have explicit risk acceptance, compensating controls, owner, and expiry. Do not let "integration speed" become a reason to normalize weak controls.

### Include frontline usability

The combined process must be executable by people doing the work. Test whether staff can identify the right path, find the required data, use the tool, obtain approval, communicate with customers, and recover from exceptions. If the process depends on remembering legacy rules, it is not integrated.

Training and job aids should be developed with the process, not after go-live. Frontline feedback should influence design before standardization is locked.

### Sequence migration by operating risk

Some process changes can happen quickly; others require data cleanup, customer notice, vendor change, retraining, system release, contract review, or compliance approval. Sequence by service risk and dependency, not only by synergy target.

Use pilots, phased rollout, parallel runs, or controlled dual processes when full migration would create excessive risk. But dual operation needs clear ownership and exit criteria.

### Reconcile service levels and metrics

Merged teams may have different SLA definitions, backlog calculations, quality standards, cost allocation, escalation thresholds, and customer commitments. Harmonize definitions before comparing performance. Otherwise one team may appear better because it measures differently.

Define the metrics that will govern the combined process and how legacy data will be interpreted during transition.

### Close legacy paths deliberately

When a process is retired, close old forms, queue categories, macros, vendor instructions, dashboards, shared inboxes, training material, and informal channels. If old paths remain open, work will fragment and metrics will become unreliable.

Communicate closure and redirect users to the new source of truth. Keep archival evidence where audit or customer history requires it.

## Common Traps

- Treating process integration as copying the acquirer's SOP onto the acquired team.
- Standardizing before understanding why legacy variation exists.
- Designing the future process without checking system, data, reporting, and permission constraints.
- Weakening approvals, segregation of duties, privacy, or audit evidence during queue and role consolidation.
- Assuming frontline teams can infer new rules from process diagrams.
- Sequencing changes by synergy deadline rather than operating risk.
- Running dual processes without ownership, metrics, and exit criteria; comparing performance across teams before SLA and metric definitions are harmonized
- Leaving retired forms, inboxes, macros, dashboards, and training material active; preserving local workarounds without deciding whether they are legitimate variants or control risks

## Self-Check

- Is the end-to-end combined service path mapped from trigger to closure?
- Are legacy process differences explained by customer, regulation, product, geography, system, staffing, or workaround causes?
- Has each difference been classified as standardize, preserve, redesign, or defer?
- Are system records, identifiers, fields, statuses, routing, permissions, automation, reporting, and audit trails able to support the process?
- Are controls for approvals, access, privacy, safety, complaints, finance, and audit evidence protected during transition?
- Are temporary control gaps explicitly accepted with compensating controls, owner, and expiry?
- Has frontline usability been tested with real scenarios, exceptions, tools, data, and approvals?
- Is migration sequenced by operating risk and dependency, with pilots or dual operation where justified?
- Are service levels, backlog, quality, cost, escalation, and customer-commitment definitions harmonized?
- Are legacy paths closed or redirected with archival evidence preserved where needed?
