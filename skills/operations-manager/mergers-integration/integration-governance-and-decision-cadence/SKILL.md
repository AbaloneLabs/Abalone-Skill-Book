# Integration Governance And Decision Cadence

Merger integration creates many decisions that cut across teams: whose process wins, which system remains, which service promise changes, which vendor is retained, and which risk is accepted. Without governance, integration becomes a collection of local projects that optimize their own deadlines while creating downstream instability. This skill helps the agent structure decision rights, cadence, dependencies, and escalation so the combined operation can move deliberately.

## Core Rules

### Define integration outcomes and non-negotiables

Name the integration outcomes: customer continuity, synergy capture, operating model alignment, regulatory compliance, service consolidation, system migration, site integration, vendor rationalization, or capability preservation. Then define non-negotiables such as safety, legal compliance, payroll continuity, customer commitments, data privacy, financial close, and business-critical service levels.

Governance should make tradeoffs visible. If cost synergy conflicts with customer continuity or control integrity, the forum must have authority to decide.

### Assign decision rights by decision type

Different decisions need different owners: operating model, staffing, process standardization, technology migration, vendor termination, customer communication, controls, data migration, policy harmonization, site changes, and synergy timing. Define who recommends, who approves, who must be consulted, and who is informed.

Avoid consensus by default. Consensus can hide accountability and delay decisions until teams create workarounds.

### Structure workstreams around operating dependencies

Common workstreams include customer operations, people, process, technology, finance, legal/compliance, vendors, facilities, data, communications, and synergy. But the boundaries should match the actual dependency map, not a generic template.

Each workstream needs owner, scope, milestones, risks, dependencies, decisions needed, metrics, and handoff points. Cross-workstream dependencies should be tracked centrally because they are where integration failures often appear.

### Keep a decision and risk log

Integration decisions should be recorded with context, options, tradeoffs, owner, date, affected teams, effective date, conditions, and follow-up. Risks should include impact, likelihood, owner, mitigation, trigger, residual risk acceptance, and review cadence.

Do not rely on meeting memory. Integration spans months and many people; decisions made in one forum can surprise teams not present.

### Use cadence for decisions, not ceremony

Set operating cadence by risk and phase: daily cutover standups, weekly workstream reviews, dependency reviews, executive steering, risk reviews, and post-close stabilization. Each meeting should have a purpose, decision threshold, required inputs, and output.

Cancel or redesign meetings that only read status. Keep cadence tight during high-risk phases and reduce it when steady-state ownership takes over.

### Manage integration scope creep

Integration can become a container for every desired improvement. Distinguish must-do integration work from opportunistic improvement. A change may be valuable but unsafe during day-one stabilization or system migration.

Classify items as day-one required, 30-60-90 day stabilization, synergy work, future optimization, or defer. This protects focus and prevents the team from overloading operations.

### Preserve local risk visibility

Standardization pressure can erase local constraints such as regulatory rules, customer commitments, language needs, site safety requirements, union terms, vendor relationships, or product-specific exceptions. Governance should require local risk disclosure before making enterprise decisions.

The goal is not to preserve every local variation. The goal is to avoid breaking legitimate operating requirements through abstract standardization.

### Define transition to steady state

Integration governance should not continue forever. Define when workstreams close, when decisions move to normal operating forums, what risks remain, what metrics are monitored, and who owns unfinished work. Temporary integration authority should either expire or be converted into formal governance.

If the integration team leaves before ownership transfers, the combined operation inherits unresolved ambiguity.

### Escalate stalled tradeoffs explicitly

Some integration issues stall because each workstream is waiting for another team to absorb cost, risk, or workload. Define escalation criteria for missed decisions, unresolved dependencies, customer-impact risk, control gaps, repeated date movement, and conflicts between synergy and continuity.

Escalation should bring options and tradeoffs, not merely ask leaders to "align." A useful escalation states what will happen if no decision is made.

## Common Traps

- Treating integration governance as project status reporting rather than decision control.
- Failing to name non-negotiables before cost or speed pressure appears.
- Using consensus for decisions that need accountable ownership.
- Creating workstreams from a template without mapping real operational dependencies.
- Losing decisions in meeting notes instead of maintaining a usable decision log.
- Holding frequent meetings that surface problems but do not resolve tradeoffs; letting integration scope expand into unrelated improvement work
- Standardizing away local regulatory, customer, safety, language, union, or site constraints; allowing temporary integration authority to persist without formal ownership
- Closing workstreams without transferring risks, metrics, documentation, and open decisions to steady-state owners; letting unresolved dependency conflicts sit in workstreams until teams create incompatible local solutions

## Self-Check

- Are integration outcomes and non-negotiables explicit?
- Are decision rights defined for operating model, staffing, process, technology, vendors, customer communication, controls, data, policy, site, and synergy decisions?
- Is each workstream tied to real operating dependencies with owner, scope, milestone, risk, decision, metric, and handoff?
- Are cross-workstream dependencies tracked centrally?
- Does the decision log capture context, options, tradeoffs, owner, date, affected teams, effective date, and follow-up?
- Does the risk log capture impact, likelihood, owner, mitigation, trigger, residual acceptance, and review cadence?
- Does each meeting cadence have a decision purpose, input, threshold, and output?
- Are work items classified as day-one, stabilization, synergy, future optimization, or defer?
- Are local constraints surfaced before standardization decisions?
- Is transition to steady-state governance defined with remaining risks, metrics, owners, documentation, and open decisions?; are stalled tradeoffs escalated with options, consequences, decision owner, and timing?
