# Operations Knowledge Base Governance

An operations knowledge base is part of the operating system, not a document archive. It shapes how work is done, how exceptions are handled, how new staff learn, and how teams respond under pressure. Agents often improve a single article while missing the governance that keeps the whole knowledge base trustworthy. This skill helps the agent define ownership, authority, structure, review, and retirement so operational knowledge remains usable and safe.

## Core Rules

### Define the knowledge base's operating purpose

Start by naming what the knowledge base is meant to support: frontline execution, internal service answers, incident response, onboarding, vendor coordination, quality control, compliance evidence, manager decision-making, or customer-facing consistency. A knowledge base designed for training may need more explanation; one designed for live operations needs fast retrieval and unambiguous action.

Do not let one repository serve every audience without structure. Different users need different depth, authority, and risk warnings.

### Assign article ownership and decision authority

Every important article needs an accountable owner, subject matter contributors, approver, review cadence, and escalation path. Ownership should sit with someone who can change the process or policy, not merely the person who typed the article.

Define who can create, edit, approve, publish, archive, and override content. If sensitive or regulated guidance is involved, approval may need HR, legal, finance, compliance, safety, security, IT, procurement, or customer-account owners. Without clear authority, articles become informal opinions that staff may over-trust.

### Govern taxonomy and article boundaries

Knowledge becomes hard to use when similar content appears under many names. Define taxonomy by service, process, user intent, system, location, role, or operating event. Article boundaries should help agents choose the right page quickly: one article should answer one meaningful work intent, while related articles should link rather than duplicate.

Use naming conventions, tags, synonyms, and redirects for terms that frontline users actually search. Avoid internal jargon as the only path to critical guidance.

### Control duplicates and conflicting guidance

Duplicate articles are not harmless. They create different answers, split feedback, and make staff unsure which source is authoritative. Define how duplicate or conflicting pages are detected, merged, redirected, or retired. When two sources disagree, name the authority that resolves the conflict.

If local teams need variations, structure them explicitly by region, site, customer, product, system, shift, or policy version. Hidden local exceptions are worse than visible variants.

### Set review cadence by risk

High-risk articles need more frequent review than low-risk reference pages. Review cadence should reflect safety, compliance, customer impact, financial exposure, system change frequency, process volatility, vendor dependency, and incident history. A payroll workaround, access-control process, safety procedure, or customer-impacting escalation path should not wait for an annual cleanup.

Review should check accuracy, owner, linked process, screenshots, forms, approval paths, metrics, exception rules, and related articles. A date stamp alone does not prove content is current.

### Manage access and sensitive content

Some operational knowledge should be broadly visible; other guidance may include security procedures, personal data, customer-sensitive details, pricing, legal positions, disciplinary process, incident vulnerabilities, or vendor contract terms. Define who can view and edit sensitive categories.

Avoid hiding ordinary process knowledge behind unnecessary permissions. Excessive restriction forces staff into chat threads and personal memory, which weakens consistency.

### Make retirement a controlled act

Obsolete articles should be archived, redirected, or retired with a reason and effective date. If an article is removed because a process changed, update links, templates, runbooks, training material, macros, forms, and service catalog entries. Retiring one page while leaving related artifacts unchanged preserves the old behavior.

For high-risk guidance, keep an audit trail of what changed and when. Staff may need to reconstruct what instruction was valid during a past incident or audit period.

### Use governance metrics carefully

Track stale articles, ownerless articles, conflicting articles, search failures, low-rated content, escalations caused by unclear guidance, incident references, and high-use pages without recent review. Do not optimize only for article count or page views. A smaller knowledge base with trusted, findable content is often stronger than a large archive.

Metrics should drive owner action. Reporting stale content without authority to fix it only documents decay.

## Common Traps

- Treating the knowledge base as a place to store documents rather than a control surface for operations.
- Assigning article ownership to writers who cannot change the underlying process.
- Creating many small duplicate pages because each team wants its own copy.
- Letting local exceptions live as hidden wording differences instead of explicit variants.
- Equating a recent review date with actual accuracy.
- Over-restricting ordinary knowledge and pushing staff back to informal channels.
- Publishing sensitive procedures without access, edit, or distribution controls; retiring articles without updating links, training, templates, macros, forms, and runbooks
- Measuring knowledge health by page count instead of usefulness, conflict, freshness, and operational outcomes; letting stale or ownerless articles remain visible because no one wants to break links

## Self-Check

- Is the knowledge base purpose clear for the actual operating audience and use case?
- Does each important article have owner, contributors, approver, review cadence, and escalation path?
- Are create, edit, approve, publish, archive, and override rights defined?
- Does taxonomy match how frontline users search and how operations are organized?
- Are duplicates, conflicts, redirects, and explicit local variants governed?
- Is review cadence based on risk, change frequency, impact, compliance, and incident history?
- Does review check process accuracy, links, screenshots, forms, approvals, exception rules, and related artifacts?
- Are sensitive content visibility and edit controls balanced against the need for usable access?
- Are retired articles archived or redirected with reason, effective date, and related artifact updates?
- Do governance metrics lead to accountable owner action rather than passive reports?
