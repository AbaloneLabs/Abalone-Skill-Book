# Time Zone Coverage And Follow The Sun Handoffs

Follow-the-sun operations can improve coverage, but only if ownership, handoff evidence, escalation, and customer promises are designed carefully. Otherwise work pauses between regions, incidents lose context, and one location becomes the hidden backstop. This skill helps the agent design time-zone coverage that preserves continuity without unfair burden or handoff loss.

## Core Rules

### Define the coverage promise

State what coverage means: monitoring only, response, resolution, customer communication, incident command, fulfillment, approvals, or full service. A 24-hour promise is meaningless unless the authority and capability available in each window are clear.

Define which services, priorities, customers, regions, languages, and issue types are covered after hours or by another region.

### Map working windows and overlap

Document local hours, holidays, weekends, shift boundaries, overlap windows, and coverage gaps. Overlap time should be used for handoff, decision transfer, complex escalations, and relationship building, not just status meetings.

If no overlap exists, strengthen written handoffs and define emergency contact rules.

### Assign ownership during transfer

Every active item should have an owner at all times. Define when ownership transfers, how acceptance is confirmed, and what happens if the receiving team rejects or cannot accept the work. The sending team should not disappear before critical items are acknowledged.

For incidents, decide whether command transfers or only execution support transfers.

### Prioritize work across regions consistently

Time-zone handoffs fail when each region interprets priority differently. Define severity, customer impact, SLA, aging, compliance, and escalation criteria consistently. If local rules differ, document them.

The receiving team should know whether to continue, pause, escalate, or monitor each item.

### Protect customer communication continuity

Customers and internal stakeholders should not receive contradictory updates from different regions. Define who communicates, what template or status source is used, and how next update times survive handoff.

If a customer expects a response during a region's night, ensure another team has authority and context to respond.

### Balance fairness and fatigue

Follow-the-sun can hide after-hours burden on one region, language group, or senior expert. Track inconvenient coverage, emergency calls, overtime, missed breaks, and repeated handoff failures. Rotate load or adjust staffing where possible.

Coverage design should not rely on informal heroics.

### Handle holidays and regional disruptions

Global operations need calendars for local holidays, severe weather, political disruptions, connectivity issues, and site outages. Define backup coverage before the gap arrives.

Do not assume another region can absorb work without language, system, policy, or capacity readiness.

### Review handoff performance

Track missed handoffs, duplicate work, reopened items, SLA misses, customer follow-ups, incident delays, and receiving-team questions. Use the evidence to adjust overlap, templates, priority rules, staffing, and authority.

Follow-the-sun maturity is proven by smooth continuity, not by the number of regions involved.

### Match regional capability, not only hours and control next-update commitments

Coverage requires the receiving region to have the skill, system access, language, customer authority, vendor contacts, and policy knowledge needed for the work. A region may be awake but unable to resolve the issue. Define which work each region can own, support, monitor, or only escalate.

Capability gaps should be visible in the handoff plan. Otherwise customers receive a coverage promise that the receiving team cannot fulfill.

Follow-the-sun teams often miss customer expectations because one region promises an update during another region's shift without confirming ownership. Next-update commitments should include time zone, owner, message source, and contingency if the receiving team cannot act.

Customer trust depends on continuity of commitments, not only continuity of staffing.

## Common Traps

- Promising 24-hour coverage when only monitoring is available overnight.
- Treating overlap time as optional.
- Letting ownership become unclear between sending and receiving regions.
- Transferring incidents without deciding whether command or only task execution moved.
- Allowing each region to use different priority and severity definitions; sending customers inconsistent updates from different regions
- Depending on one senior person for every cross-time-zone exception; forgetting holidays, regional outages, and language constraints
- Assuming another region can absorb work without capacity and authority; measuring follow-the-sun success by schedule coverage rather than handoff outcomes
- Treating an awake region as capable when it lacks access, authority, language, or policy knowledge; making next-update promises across regions without confirming ownership

## Self-Check

- Is the coverage promise clear for monitoring, response, resolution, communication, command, fulfillment, approvals, and full service?
- Are covered services, priorities, customers, regions, languages, and issue types defined?
- Are local hours, weekends, holidays, shift boundaries, overlap, and gaps mapped?
- Is overlap used for handoff, decision transfer, escalation, and relationship context?
- Does every active item have owner, transfer point, acceptance rule, and rejection path?
- For incidents, is command transfer distinguished from execution support?
- Are severity, SLA, customer impact, compliance, aging, and escalation criteria consistent across regions?
- Are customer updates, status sources, templates, and next update times preserved through handoff?
- Are fairness, emergency calls, overtime, missed breaks, and senior-expert dependency monitored?; are missed handoffs, duplicate work, reopened items, SLA misses, follow-ups, delays, and questions used to improve coverage?
- Is regional capability mapped for skill, access, language, customer authority, vendor contacts, and policy knowledge?; do next-update commitments include time zone, owner, message source, and contingency?
