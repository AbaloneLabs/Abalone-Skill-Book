# Ergonomic Fatigue And Human Factors

Ergonomic and fatigue risks often appear as productivity, quality, or attitude problems before they appear as injuries. Poor workstation design, repetitive motion, manual handling, long shifts, cognitive overload, and confusing tools create errors and harm. This skill helps the agent recognize human factors as operating design issues, while escalating medical or technical safety questions to qualified owners.

## Core Rules

### Look at the work system, not only the person

Review task design, posture, reach, force, repetition, pace, break pattern, tools, screen layout, lighting, noise, temperature, workspace, queue pressure, and decision complexity. Ask whether the system makes safe, accurate work easy.

Avoid assuming that injuries, discomfort, or mistakes are caused by individual weakness or carelessness.

### Treat fatigue as an operating risk

Fatigue affects attention, reaction time, judgment, memory, and emotional control. Review shift length, overtime, night work, split shifts, commute burden, on-call load, peak periods, consecutive workdays, and recovery time.

High fatigue can create safety incidents, quality defects, customer escalation, and poor handoffs. It should be managed like capacity and risk, not only personal wellness.

### Identify repetitive and forceful work

Tasks involving lifting, pushing, pulling, twisting, static posture, awkward reach, repetitive keystrokes, scanning, packing, inspection, or tool use may need redesign. Consider workstation height, tool selection, mechanical aids, rotation, job pacing, and recovery breaks.

Do not wait for injury reports. Discomfort, high error rates, slowdowns, and near misses can indicate ergonomic risk.

### Reduce cognitive load and error traps

Human factors include mental workload. Confusing screens, similar labels, hidden fields, frequent interruptions, multitasking, unclear priorities, and memory-dependent processes increase errors. Design work so important cues are visible and the correct action is easy.

If people repeatedly make the same error, inspect the workflow before retraining again.

### Use controls beyond reminders

Training and reminders are weak controls when the work design remains poor. Consider layout changes, automation, mechanical aids, checklists, standard work, visual controls, workload limits, shift redesign, staffing changes, and tool improvements.

The best control reduces reliance on perfect attention under pressure.

### Include remote and office ergonomics

Remote or office-based operations also carry ergonomic risk: poor seating, laptop-only work, small screens, glare, headset issues, long video meetings, lack of movement, and uninterrupted screen time. Provide practical setup guidance and escalation path for accommodations.

Do not assume low physical exertion means low safety risk.

### Monitor leading indicators

Track discomfort reports, fatigue reports, error spikes, rework, absenteeism, overtime, near misses, quality drift, handle time changes, turnover, and repeated requests for equipment or schedule changes. Segment by team, shift, task, site, and peak period.

Leading indicators are more useful than waiting for recordable injuries.

### Escalate medical and specialist questions

Operations managers should not diagnose conditions or prescribe medical accommodations. Route medical, disability, ergonomic assessment, industrial hygiene, or legal questions to qualified HR, safety, medical, legal, or ergonomics specialists.

Keep personal health information private and need-to-know.

### Design rotation carefully

Task rotation can reduce exposure, but only if rotated tasks use different muscles, postures, cognitive demands, and risk patterns. Rotating people between two equally stressful tasks only spreads harm and makes ownership harder to see.

Use rotation with training, pace adjustment, recovery time, and monitoring. Do not treat rotation as a substitute for fixing a bad workstation or unsafe workload.

## Common Traps

- Treating discomfort and fatigue as personal resilience issues.
- Retaining a poor workstation or process while repeatedly retraining people.
- Ignoring cognitive load, interruptions, and confusing interfaces.
- Measuring productivity without checking fatigue, overtime, and quality impact.
- Assuming office or remote work has no ergonomic safety risk.
- Waiting for injury reports instead of using discomfort, errors, and near misses as signals; using reminders instead of redesigning tools, layout, staffing, or workload
- Overlooking night shifts, consecutive workdays, on-call load, and recovery time; discussing personal health information too broadly
- Giving medical or accommodation advice beyond role and qualification; using task rotation as a cosmetic fix while all rotated tasks carry similar ergonomic or fatigue risk

## Self-Check

- Has the task been reviewed for posture, reach, force, repetition, pace, breaks, tools, screens, lighting, noise, temperature, workspace, pressure, and decisions?
- Are injuries, discomfort, errors, and slowdowns examined as system signals rather than individual weakness?
- Are fatigue risks reviewed for shift length, overtime, night work, split shifts, commute, on-call, peaks, consecutive days, and recovery?
- Are lifting, pushing, pulling, twisting, static posture, awkward reach, keystrokes, scanning, packing, inspection, and tool risks addressed?
- Are cognitive-load risks such as confusing screens, similar labels, interruptions, hidden fields, memory steps, and priority ambiguity reduced?
- Are controls stronger than reminders, including layout, tools, automation, mechanical aids, rotation, checklists, limits, staffing, and scheduling?
- Are remote and office ergonomics considered for seating, screens, glare, headsets, meetings, movement, and equipment?
- Are leading indicators monitored by task, shift, site, team, and peak period?
- Are medical, disability, industrial hygiene, legal, and specialist ergonomic issues escalated appropriately?
- Is personal health information handled on a need-to-know basis?; does task rotation genuinely reduce exposure through different demands, training, recovery time, and monitoring?
