# Workplace Safety and Hazard Control

Workplace safety is an operating design problem, not only a compliance checklist. Hazards appear when work changes, staffing changes, space changes, equipment ages, shortcuts become normal, or controls are not verified. Agents tend to treat safety as paperwork to complete rather than a system to design, which produces procedures that look thorough on paper while the actual work remains dangerous.

This skill helps the agent identify and control hazards in daily operations while staying conservative about technical, legal, and medical judgments that require qualified review. The goal is to make the safe path the easy path, not to add forms on top of unsafe work.

## Core Rules

### Identify hazards in the actual work

Review the real task, location, equipment, materials, people, pace, environment, and handoffs. Ask what could harm someone during normal work, peak work, rushed work, maintenance, cleaning, setup, teardown, emergency response, or abnormal conditions.

Do not rely only on written procedures. Observe or ask about how work is actually performed, including shortcuts and informal fixes. The gap between the documented procedure and the real practice is often where the hazard lives.

### Use the hierarchy of controls

Prefer eliminating the hazard, then substituting safer materials or methods, then engineering controls, then isolation, then administrative controls, and only then personal protective equipment. PPE can be necessary, but it is a last line of defense and depends on correct, consistent use by every worker on every shift.

If the only proposed control is "be careful" or "remember to," the hazard is not controlled. Move up the hierarchy until the control does not depend solely on human vigilance.

### Assign ownership for every control

Each control needs an owner, implementation date, verification method, maintenance requirement, and escalation path. Safety controls fail when everyone agrees they are important but no one owns inspection, repair, training, replacement, or enforcement.

Ownership should include contractors and vendors when they create or share the hazard. A named person with a date beats a team-wide reminder every time.

### Match control strength to severity and exposure

Assess severity, likelihood, frequency of exposure, number of people affected, detectability, and ability to recover. A rare hazard with catastrophic consequence may need strong controls even if it has not yet caused an incident.

Do not wait for injury history to prove risk. Near misses, discomfort, property damage, and worker concerns are all evidence that the hazard is real and active.

### Consider workflow pressure and incentives

Safety shortcuts often emerge when targets, staffing, queue pressure, layout, tool access, or supervisor behavior reward speed over control. Review whether the process makes the safe way harder, slower, or more awkward than the unsafe way.

If people routinely bypass a control, investigate why before assuming carelessness. The control may be poorly designed, unavailable, or incompatible with the real pace of work.

### Control temporary and changed conditions

Moves, maintenance, construction, new equipment, seasonal peaks, staffing changes, weather, outages, and special events can create temporary hazards. Define temporary controls, communication, access restrictions, signage, and review dates for each.

Temporary conditions often become dangerous precisely because they fall outside normal inspection routines and have no standing owner.

### Verify controls after implementation

Closing a corrective action requires evidence that the control exists, works, is understood, and remains in place. Use inspection, observation, photos, training records, maintenance logs, test results, or worker feedback as appropriate.

Do not close a safety item because an email was sent or a sign was posted. Verification is a separate step from action.

### Keep controls under review

Hazards evolve with new equipment, new chemicals, new workflows, new workers, and new shift patterns. A control that worked at launch may degrade, drift, or become obsolete. Schedule periodic reviews of the hazard inventory and the control set, and review again after every incident, near miss, and significant change.

When reviewing, ask three questions of each control: Is it still needed? Is it still effective? Is it still being used as designed? A control that fails any of these questions needs maintenance, redesign, or replacement.

### Connect safety to operations, not separate it and escalate high-risk uncertainty

The strongest safety systems are embedded in operational decisions: layout and flow, equipment selection, staffing levels, scheduling, procurement specifications, and maintenance priorities. Safety treated as a separate compliance function tends to produce paperwork that does not match what workers actually do. Safety treated as an operational design goal produces environments where the safe path is also the easy path.

Involve workers who perform the tasks in identifying hazards and designing controls. They know where the shortcuts are, where the documentation diverges from reality, and which controls actually hold up under production pressure.

When hazards involve legal requirements, medical exposure, hazardous materials, machinery guarding, electrical safety, fire safety, structural risk, violence threats, or regulatory reporting, route to qualified safety, legal, facilities, security, or medical owners. The agent should not improvise technical safety determinations.

When in doubt, choose the conservative path and preserve evidence. Delaying a non-urgent decision is far cheaper than acting wrongly on a safety matter.

## Common Traps

- Treating a written procedure as proof that work is safe.
- Using PPE or reminders as the first and only control.
- Closing corrective actions without verifying that the control works in practice.
- Waiting for an injury before treating a high-severity hazard as real.
- Blaming workers for shortcuts without examining targets, layout, staffing, tools, and supervision.
- Ignoring temporary hazards created by moves, repairs, peaks, weather, and outages; leaving contractor-created hazards outside normal ownership and inspection
- Using vague owners such as "the team" or "operations" for safety controls; treating near misses, discomfort, and worker concerns as weak or anecdotal evidence
- Giving technical safety advice beyond qualified expertise; reviewing controls only on an annual calendar instead of after incidents and changes

## Self-Check

- Has the real work been reviewed across normal, peak, rushed, maintenance, cleaning, emergency, and abnormal conditions?
- Are hazards identified by task, location, equipment, materials, people, pace, environment, and handoff?
- Are controls selected using elimination, substitution, engineering, isolation, administrative controls, and PPE in that order?
- Does every control have a named owner, date, verification method, maintenance plan, and escalation path?
- Are severity, likelihood, exposure frequency, affected people, detectability, and recoverability all considered?
- Are workflow pressure, incentives, staffing, layout, tools, and supervision checked for unsafe shortcuts?
- Are temporary conditions controlled with signage, access limits, communication, owner, and review date?
- Is closure supported by inspection, observation, photos, training records, maintenance logs, tests, or worker feedback?
- Are contractors and vendors included where they create or share hazards?
- Are controls scheduled for periodic review and re-reviewed after incidents, near misses, and significant changes?; are high-risk technical, legal, medical, security, fire, machinery, electrical, structural, or regulatory issues escalated to qualified owners rather than improvised?
