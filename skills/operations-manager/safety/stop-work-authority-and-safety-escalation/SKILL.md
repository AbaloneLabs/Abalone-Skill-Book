# Stop Work Authority And Safety Escalation

Safety controls are weak if people do not have practical authority to stop unsafe work. Stop-work authority must be understood before the emergency, supported by managers, and linked to clear restart criteria. This skill helps the agent design escalation and pause rules that protect people without turning every concern into confusion or retaliation.

## Core Rules

### Define who can stop work

Stop-work authority should be available to employees, supervisors, contractors, and sometimes visitors when they observe uncontrolled serious risk. Define who can stop a task, area, equipment, vehicle, supplier activity, or customer-facing process.

Authority should not depend only on seniority. The person closest to the hazard may be the first to see it.

### Clarify stop-work triggers

Triggers may include imminent injury risk, missing guard, unsafe equipment, hazardous material exposure, fire or electrical risk, violence threat, severe fatigue, missing critical PPE, untrained worker, unsafe weather, structural concern, or failed permit condition. The list should include examples but not be so narrow that people hesitate.

When uncertainty exists and potential harm is serious, the conservative action is to pause and escalate.

### Protect against retaliation and stigma

People will not use stop-work authority if they expect blame, lost pay, manager anger, or peer pressure. Communicate that good-faith safety stops are protected, even when later review shows the risk was lower than feared.

Managers should be evaluated on how they respond to safety stops, not only on production recovery.

### Define escalation and decision rights

A stop-work event needs a path: who is notified, who secures the area, who assesses the hazard, who communicates to affected teams, who approves restart, and who records the decision. For technical hazards, involve qualified safety, facilities, engineering, security, legal, or medical owners.

Do not let the same person who is under production pressure be the sole restart authority for a high-risk hazard.

### Maintain service and customer communication

Stopping work may affect customers, schedules, vendors, and downstream teams. Define how operations communicates delay, alternate service, queue impact, or contingency without minimizing the safety issue or overpromising restart.

Safety stops should trigger operational continuity planning, not pressure to restart prematurely.

### Establish restart criteria

Work should resume only when the hazard is assessed, controls are implemented, affected people are briefed, equipment or area is verified, required approvals are complete, and residual risk is accepted by the right owner. Restart criteria should be documented.

Avoid vague restart reasons such as "looks fine now" or "we need to catch up."

### Learn from stop-work events

A stop-work event is a leading indicator. Review cause, control failure, reporting path, response time, communication, worker experience, and whether the event reveals a broader process issue. Share learning without exposing personal details unnecessarily.

Repeated stops for the same condition should trigger redesign, not repeated reminders.

### Include contractors and shared sites

Contractors and shared-site partners need stop-work rules before work begins. Define whose safety rules apply, who can stop contractor work, how vendors escalate, and how restart is authorized. Contract terms should support safety authority.

Shared work areas create ambiguity unless authority is explicit.

### Manage recovery pressure after a stop

After work restarts, backlog, customer promises, overtime, and supervisor pressure can recreate the same hazard. Recovery plans should include safe pacing, staffing, communication, and revised deadlines where needed.

A stop-work event is not resolved if the restart plan pushes people into unsafe catch-up behavior.

## Common Traps

- Saying people can stop work but punishing them informally when they do.
- Limiting stop-work authority to managers far from the hazard.
- Writing trigger lists so narrow that workers hesitate during serious uncertainty.
- Restarting work because production pressure is high before controls are verified.
- Letting the pressured operating owner be the only restart authority for serious hazards.
- Treating stop-work events as disruptions rather than learning signals; failing to communicate customer or downstream impact responsibly
- Excluding contractors, vendors, visitors, or shared-site partners from stop-work rules; recording the stop but not restart criteria, approvals, and residual risk
- Repeating the same stop-work event without redesigning the system; restarting safely but then using catch-up pressure that recreates the hazard

## Self-Check

- Is stop-work authority available to the people closest to the hazard, including contractors where appropriate?
- Are triggers defined for injury, equipment, hazardous material, fire, electrical, violence, fatigue, PPE, training, weather, structure, and permit risks?
- Does guidance allow conservative pause when serious harm is uncertain?
- Are good-faith safety stops protected from retaliation, stigma, and production pressure?
- Are notification, area control, hazard assessment, communication, restart approval, and recordkeeping paths clear?
- Are qualified safety, facilities, engineering, security, legal, or medical owners involved for technical hazards?
- Are customer, vendor, schedule, queue, and continuity communications defined without pressuring premature restart?
- Are restart criteria based on assessment, controls, briefing, verification, approvals, and residual risk acceptance?
- Are stop-work events reviewed for cause, control failure, response, worker experience, and process redesign?
- Are contractor and shared-site stop-work rights, escalation, and restart authority explicit?; does the recovery plan control backlog, overtime, customer communication, staffing, pacing, and deadline pressure after restart?
