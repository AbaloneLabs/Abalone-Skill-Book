# Recurring Problem Elimination

Recurring problems are evidence that prior fixes did not change the operating system enough. They often survive because teams normalize workarounds, treat each event as isolated, or solve the symptom closest to them. Agents often recommend another reminder, training, or escalation without checking why previous actions failed. This skill helps the agent identify patterns, compare recurrence, and design durable prevention.

## Core Rules

### Prove The Problem Is Recurring

Define the recurrence pattern: same failure mode, same process step, same customer segment, same vendor, same system, same shift, same handoff, same control, or same impact. Use frequency, trend, interval, severity, and examples.

Do not group unrelated issues just because they feel similar. Bad grouping produces broad fixes that solve none of the actual problems.

### Review Prior Fixes And Why They Failed

Collect prior action items, training, communications, process changes, vendor notices, control updates, and temporary workarounds. Ask whether they were completed, verified, sustained, or bypassed.

A recurring problem may mean the action was never done, was too weak, addressed the wrong cause, was not adopted, lacked ownership, or was overwhelmed by changed conditions.

### Identify The System Pattern

Look for common conditions: unclear ownership, weak intake, overloaded specialist, bad data, tooling friction, fragile vendor dependency, incentive conflict, untrained backups, exception overload, missing control, poor detection, or service promise mismatch.

Recurring problems often sit between teams. If each team sees only its step, the system pattern remains invisible.

### Stop Reinforcing Workarounds As Normal Work

Workarounds can be necessary during containment, but recurring workaround use hides the need for redesign. Identify manual patches, side spreadsheets, informal approvals, repeated escalations, copied templates, or senior expert rescue behavior that keep the problem livable.

Decide whether to formalize, automate, eliminate, or stop the workaround. Do not let it remain invisible.

### Choose Prevention Over Repeated Detection

Better monitoring can reduce harm, but it may not prevent recurrence. Decide whether the right action is elimination, prevention, earlier detection, impact reduction, or faster recovery. Be clear when prevention is not feasible and why.

When prevention is possible, prioritize actions that remove error opportunity, clarify ownership, simplify workflow, improve inputs, or strengthen controls.

### Redesign Ownership And Incentives

If recurrence spans teams, assign an end-to-end owner or governance path. Local owners may optimize their step while the total process fails. Incentives may reward speed, volume, or local cost reduction while creating downstream rework.

Durable elimination often requires changing accountability, not just instructions.

Set a recurring review cadence while the pattern remains active. The review should track recurrence count, leading indicators, action progress, blockers, and whether the current owner still has enough authority to remove the constraint.

### Use Control And Adoption Checks

After implementing a fix, verify adoption and effectiveness. Check samples, dashboards, audits, queue age, recurrence count, staff behavior, customer complaints, and vendor performance. If a control is bypassed, find why.

Set a monitoring window long enough to see recurrence risk. One quiet week may not prove elimination for a monthly or seasonal problem.

### Decide When To Escalate Investment

Recurring problems may require investment: automation, staffing, system change, vendor replacement, policy redesign, training program, or service promise change. If repeated low-cost fixes fail, present the cost of recurrence against the investment required.

Include customer harm, staff time, rework, risk exposure, and lost trust, not only direct cost.

### Close The Pattern Deliberately

Close the recurring problem only when recurrence has reduced to an agreed level, controls are adopted, owners accept residual risk, and monitoring is assigned. If recurrence remains but is accepted, record the rationale and review date.

Do not close because a meeting happened or a document was updated.

Define a sunset rule for extra monitoring or temporary controls. If controls remain necessary indefinitely, decide whether they should become standard work with proper ownership, training, and audit coverage.

## Common Traps

- Treating similar-looking but distinct failures as one recurring problem.
- Ignoring prior corrective actions and repeating the same weak fix.
- Assuming prior actions worked because they were marked complete.
- Focusing on the team where the symptom appears instead of the end-to-end system.
- Allowing manual workarounds to become the hidden operating model.
- Improving detection while calling it prevention.
- Leaving recurrence ownership split across teams with no end-to-end accountable owner; measuring success too soon for a seasonal or low-frequency problem
- Avoiding investment decisions by repeatedly asking staff to try harder; closing the problem after a policy update without checking adoption and recurrence

## Self-Check

- Is the recurrence pattern defined by failure mode, segment, process step, system, owner, or impact?
- Are unrelated issues kept separate rather than forced into one broad problem?
- Have prior fixes been reviewed for completion, verification, adoption, and failure mode?
- Is the common system pattern identified across teams and handoffs?
- Are repeated workarounds named and either formalized, automated, eliminated, or stopped?
- Is the recommendation clear about prevention, detection, impact reduction, or recovery improvement?
- Is end-to-end ownership defined where recurrence crosses teams?
- Are adoption and effectiveness checks assigned with a sufficient monitoring window?
- Has the cost of recurrence been compared with needed investment where low-cost fixes have failed?
- Is closure based on reduced recurrence, adopted controls, residual risk ownership, and monitoring?
