# Sales To Operations Handoff Readiness

Sales-to-operations handoff is where promises become operational obligations. The risk is not that sales and operations have different goals; it is that the deal context, customer expectations, exceptions, and delivery constraints are not translated into executable work. This skill helps the agent check whether operations can accept, deliver, and govern what has been sold without relying on guesswork or after-the-fact negotiation.

## Core Rules

### Capture the promise exactly

Start by identifying what was promised to the customer or partner: service scope, timeline, volume, customization, onboarding support, response time, pricing terms, reporting, integrations, compliance obligations, support channels, renewal terms, and exclusions. Separate signed commitments from sales intent, verbal expectations, and assumptions.

Operations should not inherit ambiguous promises. If a commitment is unclear, route it for clarification before delivery begins. If the promise exceeds standard capability, identify who approved the exception and what tradeoff was accepted.

### Confirm operational fit before acceptance

Assess whether the customer, deal, or renewal fits current operating model, staffing, tools, geography, vendor capacity, legal terms, support hours, data requirements, and service levels. A profitable deal can still be operationally unsafe if it requires unsupported exceptions or hidden manual work.

If readiness is partial, state what must be true before operations accepts full responsibility: data complete, access granted, contract terms clarified, implementation plan approved, customer contacts named, integration tested, billing rules confirmed, or exception owner assigned.

### Define required handoff packet

The handoff should include customer profile, contract or order reference, scope, start date, success criteria, known risks, special terms, exclusions, implementation tasks, stakeholder contacts, support model, priority, billing or pricing notes, compliance requirements, data needs, and open questions. Include the source of truth for each item.

Avoid asking operations to mine CRM notes, emails, and meeting recordings to reconstruct the deal. The handoff packet should be complete enough for intake, triage, and execution.

### Surface exceptions and nonstandard work

Sales exceptions are not inherently bad, but they must be visible. Custom timelines, nonstandard reporting, manual billing, special support coverage, unusual data handling, regional requirements, bespoke integrations, or waived prerequisites should have approval, owner, duration, and operational impact.

Do not let exception work hide in individual relationships. If a customer receives special handling, operations needs a controlled way to deliver it consistently and know when it ends.

### Align customer communication

Sales, implementation, support, and operations should communicate consistent expectations. If operations needs a different timeline, prerequisite, or support boundary than what the customer heard, resolve it before the first operational interaction. Misalignment early in the relationship damages trust and increases escalations.

Clarify who communicates changes to the customer and what language is approved. Operations should not be forced to walk back promises without sales or account ownership alignment.

### Set ownership after handoff

Name the accountable operational owner, sales or account owner, implementation owner if separate, escalation path, and decision authority for scope disputes. Define who handles customer questions, internal blockers, contract interpretation, billing issues, and changes after go-live.

The handoff should not end sales accountability for promises that require commercial clarification. Some questions remain joint until the customer is stable.

### Monitor early delivery against promise

Track early signals after handoff: missed prerequisites, customer confusion, onboarding delay, support contact volume, exception count, billing disputes, delivery defects, and escalations. Early delivery should verify whether the handoff was accurate and whether the promise is sustainable.

Feed recurring issues back into sales enablement, deal desk, pricing, contract templates, qualification criteria, and operational readiness checks.

### Protect capacity from unmanaged deal complexity

Nonstandard deals often consume support, supervisor, billing, implementation, or reporting capacity that was not in the sales forecast. Estimate the operating load of custom commitments and decide whether it needs pricing, staffing, phased onboarding, or explicit risk acceptance. If every special commitment is absorbed informally, operations will miss standard commitments while the true cost of the deal remains invisible.

## Common Traps

- Treating closed-won as operationally ready. Commercial closure does not prove delivery readiness.
- Confusing verbal expectations with signed commitments. Both matter, but they require different handling.
- Hiding nonstandard promises in notes. Operations needs explicit exception approval and owner.
- Accepting incomplete customer data to preserve momentum, then absorbing rework later.
- Letting sales exit too early. Commercial context may be needed through onboarding and first delivery.
- Allowing inconsistent customer messages across sales, onboarding, support, and operations.
- Failing to use early delivery issues to improve qualification and handoff design.
- Treating custom deal complexity as relationship management only, without capacity, pricing, or service impact review.

## Self-Check

- Are signed commitments, verbal expectations, assumptions, exclusions, and nonstandard promises separated?
- Has operational fit been assessed against staffing, tools, geography, support hours, vendors, legal terms, data, and service levels?
- Is the handoff packet complete enough for intake, triage, execution, and escalation without CRM archaeology?
- Are exceptions approved, owned, time-bounded, and tied to operational impact?
- Are customer messages aligned across sales, account, implementation, support, and operations?
- Is ownership after handoff clear for operational delivery, commercial clarification, billing, disputes, and scope changes?
- Are prerequisites and acceptance criteria explicit before operations takes full responsibility?
- Are early delivery issues tracked and fed back into sales qualification, deal review, and handoff standards?
- Has nonstandard deal complexity been translated into capacity, cost, staffing, service, or risk implications?
