# Cross-Functional Handoff Contract And Ownership

Cross-functional handoffs fail when teams believe they transferred work but the receiving team cannot act on it. The gap is usually not effort; it is missing acceptance criteria, unclear ownership, weak evidence, conflicting priorities, or no feedback loop. This skill helps the agent design handoffs as operational contracts that define what is transferred, when it is ready, who owns it, and how problems are resolved.

## Core Rules

### Define the handoff object

Start by naming what is being handed off: a customer, case, order, incident, launch, vendor issue, approval, report, contract obligation, compliance question, data file, or operational risk. State what completion means for the sending team and what readiness means for the receiving team. These are often different.

Avoid vague handoffs such as "operations will take it from here." The receiving team needs enough context, authority, data, and timing to perform the next action without rediscovery.

### Set acceptance criteria

Define the minimum information, evidence, approvals, system status, documentation, and quality level required before the receiving team accepts work. Acceptance criteria may include complete customer records, validated data, contract terms, priority, due date, exception notes, risk flags, owner, promised service level, and unresolved questions.

If the item is incomplete, define whether it is rejected, returned for correction, accepted with exception, or accepted into a triage queue. This prevents silent rework and hidden backlog.

### Clarify ownership before and after transfer

Every handoff needs a clear owner before transfer, after acceptance, and during exceptions. Name who answers questions, who communicates to stakeholders, who updates systems, who escalates delays, and who accepts residual risk. If multiple teams contribute, define the accountable owner rather than listing everyone.

The transition moment matters. Ownership should not disappear during "waiting for handoff" states. Work in limbo is a common source of missed commitments.

### Include service expectations and timing

State when handoff should occur, how quickly the receiving team must acknowledge, when work should start, and what service expectation applies. If priority changes after handoff, define who can change it and how stakeholders are notified.

Timing rules should account for cutoff times, time zones, shift coverage, holidays, vendor hours, approval windows, and peak volume. A handoff that arrives after the receiving team's cutoff may not be late by the sender's clock but can still miss the operational window.

### Preserve context and evidence

A strong handoff packet includes why the work exists, current state, decisions made, evidence, risks, open questions, customer or stakeholder commitments, constraints, and next action requested. It should also identify what not to assume.

Do not force the receiving team to reconstruct history from chat threads, sales notes, emails, or meeting memory. The source of truth should be clear and durable.

### Define exception and dispute paths

Some handoffs will be incomplete, urgent, high-risk, or disputed. Define exception criteria, approval authority, escalation route, temporary workaround, and expiration. Also define how repeated disputes are reviewed. If the same missing field or unclear promise recurs, the handoff design needs correction.

Avoid resolving every exception through personal relationships. Informal favors create inconsistent service and hide systemic failure.

### Measure handoff quality

Track rejection rate, missing information, rework, time to acceptance, time in limbo, downstream defects, stakeholder complaints, SLA misses, and repeated exception categories. Handoff quality should be visible to both sending and receiving teams.

Metrics should not be used only to blame the sending team. They should identify where criteria, training, tools, incentives, or source systems need improvement.

### Design for return paths without blame

Returned work should be controlled, not emotional. Define how the receiving team explains what is missing, how the sender corrects it, whether the original service clock pauses, and when repeated returns trigger joint review. A return path protects quality only if it is fast, specific, and fair. Otherwise teams either reject too much or accept bad handoffs silently.

## Common Traps

- Treating handoff as sending a message. Transfer is not complete until the receiving team can act or has explicitly rejected it.
- Defining completion from the sender's view only. Receiver readiness is the real operating test.
- Leaving ownership unclear during transition. Limbo states create missed deadlines and customer confusion.
- Accepting incomplete work silently. Hidden rework makes capacity and quality problems invisible.
- Using chat history as the source of truth. Critical context can be lost, edited, or inaccessible.
- Letting exceptions become relationship-based favors instead of controlled paths.
- Measuring only volume handed off, not quality, rework, or downstream impact.
- Treating returned work as interpersonal conflict instead of a controlled correction path.

## Self-Check

- Is the handoff object defined clearly enough that both teams know what is being transferred?
- Are sender completion and receiver readiness distinguished?
- Are acceptance criteria explicit for data, evidence, approvals, status, priority, risk, and open questions?
- Is the path clear for reject, return, accept with exception, or triage incomplete work?
- Is ownership defined before transfer, after acceptance, during limbo, and during exceptions?
- Are timing, acknowledgement, cutoff, service expectation, and priority rules clear?
- Does the handoff packet preserve durable context and source of truth without relying on memory or chat trails?
- Are handoff quality, rework, limbo time, downstream defects, and repeated exceptions measured and reviewed?
- Is returned work handled through a specific correction path with ownership, timing, and review of repeated patterns?
