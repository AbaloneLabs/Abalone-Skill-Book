# Recovery Governance And Turnaround Cadence

Turnaround work needs governance because recovery requires repeated tradeoffs: speed versus quality, customer promises versus capacity, root-cause fixes versus immediate throughput, and morale versus overtime. Without cadence and decision rights, recovery becomes a collection of urgent tasks. This skill helps the agent create a management system for restoring a failing operation.

## Core Rules

### Define recovery objectives and guardrails

State what recovery must achieve: service restoration, backlog reduction, quality stabilization, customer trust, control repair, safety protection, vendor recovery, staffing stability, or cost containment. Define guardrails that cannot be violated during recovery.

Clear objectives prevent teams from optimizing one metric while damaging another.

### Create workstreams with accountable owners

Common workstreams include backlog, quality, staffing, customer communication, process fixes, tools, vendor issues, controls, training, and metrics. Each needs owner, scope, goals, blockers, decisions needed, and escalation path.

Do not let one turnaround lead personally manage every detail. Recovery requires distributed ownership.

### Set cadence by phase

Stabilization may need daily or twice-daily reviews. Recovery may need daily execution and weekly decision reviews. Sustainment may move to normal governance. Cadence should change as risk changes.

Each meeting should produce decisions, blocker removal, owner confirmation, or communication updates. If it only repeats status, redesign it.

### Use a recovery scorecard

Track a balanced set of metrics: backlog, aging, throughput, quality, rework, SLA, customer complaints, escalations, staffing, overtime, attrition, control defects, incident risk, and recovery action completion. Segment by work type where needed.

The scorecard should show trend and action, not just current value.

### Maintain a decision and risk log

Record material decisions, tradeoffs, rejected options, accepted risks, owners, due dates, and review dates. Turnaround decisions are often made under pressure and later questioned. A log preserves context.

Risk acceptance should be explicit, especially for temporary controls, service degradation, overtime, and customer commitments.

### Separate recovery from transformation

Some improvements are necessary for recovery; others are desirable but distracting. Classify work as stabilize now, recover next, harden soon, or transform later. This protects focus and prevents overloaded teams from being asked to fix everything at once.

Turnaround is not a license to start every improvement project.

### Keep frontline feedback in the cadence

Frontline teams know whether recovery actions are working. Include feedback loops through supervisors, sampled cases, customer language, quality findings, and blocker reports. Do not rely only on leadership dashboards.

If recovery rules create new friction, adjust quickly.

### Plan exit to normal governance and make tradeoffs explicit to sponsors

Recovery cadence should have exit criteria: stable backlog, quality, staffing, controls, customer communication, owner transfer, and normal operating review. Temporary workstreams, dashboards, meetings, and roles should be retired or formalized.

Recovery is complete when normal governance can hold the gains.

Turnaround sponsors need options, not just status. Present what each path costs: slower recovery, more overtime, reduced scope, customer concessions, vendor spend, delayed transformation, or accepted risk. Ask for decisions when the team lacks authority.

If leaders do not own tradeoffs, frontline teams will absorb them informally.

### Track recovery promises and retire emergency permissions

Recovery governance should track commitments made to customers, employees, regulators, vendors, and leaders. Each promise needs owner, date, evidence, and status. A turnaround can fail because the operation fixes the core issue but misses promises made during recovery.

Promise tracking prevents trust damage after the technical problem improves.

Recovery often grants temporary access, spending authority, manual override, or expedited approval. Track these permissions and remove or formalize them when recovery ends.

## Common Traps

- Running turnaround through urgency without objectives and guardrails.
- Giving workstreams names but no accountable owners or decision rights.
- Keeping crisis meeting cadence after risk has changed.
- Tracking only backlog or throughput while quality and morale degrade; making tradeoff decisions without recording risk acceptance
- Starting broad transformation while the operation is still unstable; ignoring frontline evidence because dashboards look better
- Letting recovery workstreams continue indefinitely; exiting recovery before normal governance can sustain gains
- Treating temporary roles and dashboards as permanent without design; reporting status without asking sponsors to own real recovery tradeoffs
- Losing track of promises made during the recovery effort; forgetting to remove emergency permissions after recovery stabilizes

## Self-Check

- Are recovery objectives and non-negotiable guardrails explicit?
- Are backlog, quality, staffing, customer communication, process, tools, vendors, controls, training, and metrics workstreams assigned where needed?
- Does each workstream have owner, scope, goal, blockers, decisions, and escalation path?
- Does cadence match stabilization, recovery, and sustainment phases?
- Does each meeting produce decisions, blocker removal, owner confirmation, or communication updates?
- Does the scorecard include backlog, age, throughput, quality, rework, SLA, complaints, escalations, staffing, overtime, attrition, controls, incidents, and actions?
- Are material decisions, tradeoffs, rejected options, accepted risks, owners, and review dates logged?
- Is work classified as stabilize now, recover next, harden soon, or transform later?; is frontline feedback included through supervisors, cases, customer language, quality, and blockers?
- Are exit criteria and transition to normal governance defined?; are sponsor tradeoffs presented with cost, risk, authority needed, and decision deadline?
- Are recovery promises tracked with owner, date, evidence, and status?; are temporary access, spending, override, and approval permissions retired or formalized?
