# Backlog Burn Down And Recovery Plan

Backlog recovery is not just processing more work faster. A backlog contains different risks: urgent customer harm, aged commitments, duplicates, low-value work, compliance deadlines, and items that no longer need action. This skill helps the agent reduce backlog while protecting quality, preventing new backlog from forming, and restoring normal flow.

## Core Rules

### Clean and classify the backlog first

Before burn-down, remove duplicates, closed items, invalid requests, misrouted work, items waiting on others, and work that no longer needs action. Classify remaining work by age, risk, customer impact, revenue, compliance, effort, dependency, and priority.

Processing an unclean backlog wastes capacity and can create customer confusion.

### Protect high-risk work

Prioritize safety, legal, compliance, customer-impacting, revenue-critical, irreversible, and high-escalation items. A simple first-in-first-out approach may be fair for routine work but unsafe for mixed-risk backlog.

Define priority rules and communicate them so stakeholders understand why some old work waits while high-risk work moves first.

### Stop or reduce new inflow

Backlog burn-down fails if new work keeps entering faster than the team can process. Consider intake limits, clearer forms, deflection, self-service, temporary scope narrowing, scheduling windows, demand shaping, or pausing low-value categories.

If inflow cannot be reduced, the recovery plan needs more capacity or changed service promises.

### Estimate true burn capacity

Calculate capacity after accounting for normal incoming work, meetings, training, quality review, escalations, rework, breaks, and fatigue. Temporary overtime may help briefly but cannot be the core plan for a long recovery.

Use actual throughput and quality data, not optimistic targets.

### Choose the right burn-down method

Options include risk triage, batch processing, specialist pods, oldest-first within priority, customer segment focus, automation, vendor support, weekend blitz, or temporary reassignment. Match method to work type and risk.

Avoid mass closure or superficial processing that creates rework, complaints, or compliance issues.

### Manage customer and stakeholder expectations

Communicate backlog state, priority rules, recovery actions, expected timing, what customers or requesters should do, and next update cadence. Do not promise exact completion dates without capacity evidence.

If service levels are temporarily degraded, say so clearly and define review date.

### Monitor quality during recovery

Burn-down pressure can increase errors, poor documentation, missed controls, and premature closure. Use sampling, QA review, reopen rate, complaint rate, and downstream defect tracking. Slow down if quality guardrails are breached.

Recovery that creates rework is not recovery.

### Prevent recurrence

After the backlog is stable, identify why it formed: demand spike, staffing gap, unclear intake, poor prioritization, tool failure, vendor delay, process defect, policy change, or management inattention. Assign corrective actions.

Backlog recovery is incomplete if the same conditions remain.

### Protect current flow while burning down old work and reconcile commitments and duplicates

Recovery plans must decide how much capacity goes to new incoming work versus old backlog. If all capacity burns old work, new urgent work becomes tomorrow's backlog. If all capacity protects new work, old commitments decay further.

Set allocation rules, review them frequently, and adjust when risk shifts.

Backlogs often contain multiple promises to the same customer, duplicate requests, or outdated commitments. Before contacting customers or closing items, reconcile related records so the recovery team does not send conflicting messages.

One bad recovery contact can undo several good ones.

## Common Traps

- Starting burn-down before cleaning duplicates, invalid work, and misroutes.
- Treating oldest work as highest priority regardless of risk.
- Ignoring new inflow while celebrating completed old items.
- Planning capacity from ideal output instead of actual throughput and quality.
- Using overtime as the main recovery plan for too long; mass-closing items without verifying customer or compliance impact
- Hiding temporary service degradation from stakeholders; measuring only count reduced while reopen, complaint, and defect rates rise
- Failing to remove the causes that created backlog; keeping recovery staffing after the backlog is gone without redesigning normal capacity
- Burning old backlog while allowing new work to become the next backlog; contacting customers from unreconciled duplicate records and creating conflicting promises

## Self-Check

- Has the backlog been cleaned for duplicates, closed items, invalid requests, misroutes, waiting states, and obsolete work?
- Is remaining work classified by age, risk, customer, revenue, compliance, effort, dependency, and priority?
- Are safety, legal, compliance, customer-impacting, revenue-critical, irreversible, and escalation-heavy items protected?
- Are priority rules communicated to stakeholders?
- Is new inflow reduced through intake limits, forms, deflection, self-service, scope narrowing, scheduling, demand shaping, or pauses?
- Is burn capacity based on normal incoming work, meetings, training, QA, escalations, rework, breaks, and fatigue?
- Is the burn-down method matched to work type and risk?
- Are customer expectations, degraded service, timing, requester actions, and update cadence communicated honestly?
- Are quality guardrails monitored through sampling, QA, reopens, complaints, and downstream defects?; are root causes of the backlog assigned corrective actions to prevent recurrence?
- Are capacity allocation rules set between new work, high-risk work, and old backlog?; are duplicate records and customer commitments reconciled before outreach or closure?
