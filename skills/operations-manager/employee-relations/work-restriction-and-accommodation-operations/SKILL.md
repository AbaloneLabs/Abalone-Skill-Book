# Work Restriction And Accommodation Operations

Work restrictions and accommodations turn individual needs into operating constraints. They can affect staffing, schedules, task assignment, safety, productivity, privacy, fairness, and customer commitments. Agents often jump to whether the request is convenient and miss confidentiality, essential duties, interactive-process ownership, temporary versus ongoing limits, coworker burden, and safety controls. This skill helps the agent support operations while routing protected decisions correctly.

## Core Rules

### Route entitlement questions to the proper owner

Operations managers should not independently decide legal eligibility for disability, pregnancy, religious, medical, injury, or protected leave accommodations. Route entitlement, documentation, and approval questions to HR, benefits, legal, leave administration, occupational health, or the designated accommodation owner.

The operational role is to describe the work, constraints, staffing impact, safety requirements, and feasible adjustments. Do not ask for diagnoses, private medical details, or proof beyond the approved process.

### Focus on functional restrictions and essential work

Translate the situation into operational terms: lifting limit, standing duration, repetitive motion restriction, schedule limit, travel restriction, PPE requirement, light exposure, customer-facing limitation, break need, remote-work requirement, or task exclusion. Compare restrictions against essential duties, not against assumptions about the person.

Be precise about which tasks are essential, which are occasional, which can be reassigned, and which require certification or safety qualification. Vague statements such as "they cannot do the job" are not useful and may be unfair.

### Explore operational adjustments before rejecting feasibility

Potential adjustments can include modified task mix, temporary light duty, assistive equipment, ergonomic changes, schedule changes, break structure, shift swap, remote or administrative work, reduced travel, job aids, additional training, altered workstation, or temporary coverage support.

Do not reject an accommodation because it disrupts the normal routine. But also do not pretend a restriction has no impact. Provide factual operational constraints to the owner so the decision can balance feasibility, safety, business need, and rights.

### Protect safety without using safety as a vague excuse

Some restrictions directly affect safety: equipment operation, driving, fatigue, medication effects, lifting, exposure, emergency response, confined spaces, or lone work. Identify specific hazards and controls. If a task cannot be performed safely under the restriction, explain why with evidence.

Avoid using "safety concerns" as a blanket refusal. Safety analysis should name the hazard, likelihood, severity, possible mitigations, and residual risk.

### Manage coverage and fairness carefully

Accommodations may shift work to others, change schedules, reduce capacity, or require cross-training. Plan coverage openly at the operational level without revealing private reasons. Track whether coworker burden becomes sustained or unsafe.

Fairness does not mean everyone gets the same assignment. It means approved restrictions are respected while workload, overtime, and unpopular tasks are managed transparently and sustainably.

### Maintain confidentiality in daily operations

Supervisors may need to know task restrictions or schedule changes, not medical diagnoses. Coworkers usually need only operational instructions: who handles which task, what process changes, and where to escalate coverage issues.

Do not explain that a worker has a medical condition, disability, pregnancy issue, religious need, or injury unless the approved process requires disclosure. Gossip can turn a legitimate accommodation into stigma or retaliation.

### Revisit temporary arrangements before they drift

Temporary restrictions can become indefinite by neglect. Set review points, owner, expected duration if known, and triggers for reassessment. Confirm whether productivity targets, quality expectations, and attendance rules need temporary adjustment.

If the arrangement no longer works operationally, escalate through the accommodation owner rather than unilaterally withdrawing it.

### Prevent retaliation and informal penalty

Workers using restrictions or accommodations may be excluded from overtime, training, promotion, preferred assignments, or social trust. Some differences may be required by restrictions; others may be retaliation or bias. Review decisions carefully.

Do not penalize someone for following approved restrictions. If restrictions create role-fit concerns, handle them through the formal process.

## Common Traps

- Asking for a diagnosis or private medical detail instead of functional work restrictions.
- Deciding accommodation eligibility without HR, legal, leave, benefits, or occupational health.
- Describing a role as impossible without separating essential duties from flexible tasks.
- Rejecting an adjustment because it is inconvenient, without considering feasible alternatives.
- Using vague safety concerns instead of specific hazard and mitigation analysis.
- Telling coworkers private reasons for task or schedule changes.
- Letting temporary light duty become permanent without review, ownership, or staffing plan; shifting the burden to coworkers until resentment or safety problems emerge
- Excluding the worker from opportunities unrelated to the restriction; removing or changing an accommodation informally because business conditions changed

## Self-Check

- Are entitlement and documentation questions routed to the approved HR, legal, leave, benefits, or occupational health owner?
- Has the agent avoided asking for diagnoses or unnecessary private medical details?
- Are restrictions described functionally in relation to tasks, schedule, environment, travel, equipment, and safety?
- Are essential duties separated from flexible, occasional, or reassignable tasks?
- Have feasible operational adjustments and coverage options been considered?
- Are safety concerns specific, evidence-based, and tied to hazards and mitigations?
- Is confidentiality protected in supervisor and coworker communication?
- Are workload, overtime, task burden, and morale impacts managed without exposing private information?
- Is there a review point, owner, and escalation path for temporary or changing restrictions?
- Has retaliation risk been checked in assignments, training, overtime, evaluation, and promotion access?
