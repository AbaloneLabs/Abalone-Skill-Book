# Multi Site Rollout And Localization

Multi-site scaling is not copying a playbook into new locations. Sites differ by demand, regulation, labor market, language, facilities, vendors, customer expectations, and local management maturity. This skill helps the agent decide what to replicate, what to localize, and how to sequence rollout without losing control or service consistency.

## Core Rules

### Define the core model and local variables

Separate the operating model into core elements and local variables. Core elements may include safety rules, service promise, data definitions, quality standards, escalation, controls, and brand-critical customer handling. Local variables may include staffing pattern, language, vendors, facility layout, local calendar, transport, or regulatory steps.

If everything is core, rollout becomes inflexible. If everything is local, scale becomes ungovernable.

### Assess site readiness before rollout

Each site needs readiness across leadership, staffing, facilities, equipment, systems, access, vendors, training, inventory, safety, support, reporting, and local demand. A site should not launch because the central timeline says so if readiness evidence is weak.

Use readiness gates that can delay, narrow, or phase the rollout.

### Sequence rollout by risk and learning

Choose pilot and rollout sequence based on operational learning, risk, representativeness, capacity, and dependency. Do not start only with the easiest site if it will not reveal the risks of larger or more complex sites.

Use early sites to test assumptions, update playbooks, and improve training before broader rollout.

### Localize with documented rationale

When a site adapts the model, document reason, owner, rule difference, customer or legal basis, data impact, control impact, and review date. Local changes should be visible to central operations and other sites.

Hidden localization becomes drift. Documented localization becomes controlled learning.

### Preserve common data and reporting

Sites may need local practices, but core metrics and definitions should remain comparable. Define shared fields, status meanings, SLA calculations, quality criteria, incident categories, and reporting cadence.

If local reporting differs, leaders cannot tell whether performance differences are real or definitional.

### Prepare local leadership and escalation

Site leaders need authority, playbooks, escalation paths, staffing support, training, and decision rules. Central teams should not become permanent remote managers for every local issue.

Define when local leaders can decide, when they must escalate, and how central support is requested.

### Manage rollout load on central teams

Expansion creates demand on trainers, IT, facilities, procurement, HR, finance, support, analytics, and leadership. Central bottlenecks can delay or weaken site launch. Include central capacity in the rollout plan.

Do not treat each site as isolated if the same experts are supporting all launches.

### Retire launch support deliberately

After a site launches, define stabilization metrics and transition into normal governance. Keep temporary support only with owner and expiry. Capture lessons into the rollout kit before the next site.

Rushing from one site to the next without learning repeats defects at scale.

### Maintain a versioned rollout kit and compare sites without shaming them

The rollout kit should include readiness checklist, training, runbooks, role descriptions, vendor setup, system access, metrics, communication templates, risk log, local-variant rules, and lesson history. Version it so each site knows which guidance was used.

Unversioned rollout material causes sites to launch with different instructions while leaders assume the model is consistent.

Use site comparison to find learning, constraints, and support needs, not to rank teams without context. Differences may reflect demand mix, local labor, facility limits, regulatory rules, maturity, or launch sequence.

Fair comparison makes good practices portable and weak spots visible before they become permanent.

## Common Traps

- Copying a playbook without checking local regulation, labor, language, vendor, facility, and customer differences.
- Calling every central preference a non-negotiable standard.
- Letting each site customize without documenting rationale and controls.
- Choosing only easy pilots that do not test real rollout risk.
- Launching sites on schedule despite weak readiness evidence; keeping central teams as permanent support because local leadership was not enabled
- Comparing site metrics before definitions are aligned; ignoring central team capacity during rapid rollout
- Failing to capture lessons before the next site launch; leaving temporary launch support active with no exit criteria
- Using unversioned rollout materials so sites quietly follow different instructions; comparing sites without context and turning learning signals into blame

## Self-Check

- Are core model elements separated from local variables?
- Are safety, service promise, data, quality, escalation, controls, and brand-critical handling protected as needed?
- Is site readiness assessed for leadership, staffing, facilities, equipment, systems, access, vendors, training, inventory, safety, support, reporting, and demand?
- Can readiness gates delay, narrow, or phase rollout?
- Is rollout sequence based on learning, risk, representativeness, capacity, and dependency?
- Are local adaptations documented with reason, owner, rule difference, legal or customer basis, data impact, control impact, and review date?
- Are shared metrics, status meanings, SLA rules, quality criteria, incident categories, and reporting cadence preserved?
- Do local leaders have authority, escalation paths, staffing support, playbooks, training, and decision rules?
- Is central capacity for trainers, IT, facilities, procurement, HR, finance, support, analytics, and leadership included?; are stabilization, temporary support exit, and lesson capture completed before expanding further?
- Is the rollout kit versioned with readiness, training, runbooks, roles, vendors, access, metrics, communication, risks, variants, and lessons?; are site comparisons adjusted for demand, labor, facility, regulation, maturity, and launch sequence before conclusions are drawn?
