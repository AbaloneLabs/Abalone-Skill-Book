# Transportation Carrier And Delivery Performance

Transportation performance is where internal operational promises meet external networks. A shipment may leave the warehouse correctly and still fail because of carrier capacity, cutoff misses, routing errors, weather, address quality, customs, appointment scheduling, damage, or last-mile access. Agents often look only at on-time delivery percentage and miss cost leakage, exception handling, claims recovery, and customer communication. This skill helps the agent manage carriers as operational partners with measurable service, clear exceptions, and accountable recovery.

Use this skill when reviewing carrier performance, delivery delays, freight cost, parcel or LTL operations, last-mile delivery, route planning, service levels, claims, expedites, carrier onboarding, or customer delivery commitments. The agent should distinguish what the operation controls from what the carrier controls, while still managing the customer impact.

## Core Rules

### Define the delivery promise and measurement point

Delivery performance depends on where the clock starts and stops. Clarify whether the promise is ship-by, pickup-by, delivery-by, appointment window, first delivery attempt, customer receipt, or installation completion. A carrier can meet transit time while the warehouse missed cutoff, or the warehouse can ship on time while last-mile appointment failed.

Use metrics that match the customer promise. Internal on-time ship rate is not enough if customers care about delivery date. Carrier on-time delivery is not enough if pickup reliability or damage is poor.

### Segment carrier performance

Carrier performance varies by lane, service level, region, product type, weight, size, season, residential versus commercial delivery, temperature requirements, customs, and last-mile complexity. A single carrier score can hide weak lanes or expensive exceptions.

Review performance by segment before changing routing. A carrier may be best for small parcels but poor for bulky freight. Another may handle urban last mile well but fail rural delivery windows. Segmenting prevents overgeneralized decisions.

### Manage cost beyond base rate

Transportation cost includes fuel surcharge, residential surcharge, dimensional weight, delivery area surcharge, liftgate, inside delivery, detention, reattempt, storage, address correction, customs, claims denial, expedites, and manual rework. A cheap base rate may be expensive in practice.

Inspect accessorial charges and exception costs. If accessorials are rising, root causes may include poor address capture, packaging dimensions, missed appointments, bad customer instructions, carrier mismatch, or warehouse cutoff behavior.

### Control carrier handoff and pickup reliability

Carrier performance begins before transit. Check manifest accuracy, label quality, trailer or pickup readiness, dock schedule, scan close, pallet count, documentation, hazardous or regulated handling, customs paperwork, and pickup confirmation. Missed pickup or bad paperwork can appear later as carrier delay.

Do not mark shipment as safely handed off until evidence supports it. For high-value or regulated shipments, chain-of-custody and scan evidence may be required.

### Design exception playbooks

Common transportation exceptions include lost package, late scan, weather delay, damaged freight, failed delivery attempt, wrong address, customs hold, refused delivery, appointment miss, carrier capacity failure, and tracking mismatch. Each exception should have an owner, evidence requirement, customer communication rule, escalation path, and claim or recovery process.

Avoid treating each carrier exception as a new mystery. Standard playbooks reduce response time and improve claim quality.

### Communicate delivery risk before the customer discovers it

If tracking shows delay, missed scan, failed attempt, or exception likely to affect the customer, decide whether proactive communication is needed. The message should explain status, impact, what is being done, what the customer should do if anything, and when the next update will come.

Do not overpromise carrier outcomes. Say what is confirmed, what is being checked, and what alternatives exist. Customers value realistic recovery more than unsupported reassurance.

### Use claims and disputes as evidence loops

Damage, loss, late delivery, and billing disputes require timely evidence: photos, packaging details, weights, dimensions, scan history, invoice, bill of lading, proof of delivery, customer statement, and internal handling records. Weak evidence leads to denied claims and hidden cost.

Track claims by root cause and carrier. Repeated denials may indicate missing documentation, packaging weakness, wrong service selection, or carrier quality issue.

### Manage carrier dependency and resilience

Relying on one carrier can simplify operations but create capacity, strike, weather, cyber, pricing, or service disruption risk. Define backup carriers, routing rules, volume commitments, emergency escalation contacts, and criteria for switching service.

Carrier diversification should be deliberate. Too many carriers can also increase complexity, training burden, label errors, and weak volume leverage. The right balance depends on service risk and operational capacity.

## Common Traps

- Measuring only ship-on-time while customers experience delivery failures.
- Using one carrier average to make decisions across very different lanes and service levels.
- Choosing the lowest base rate while ignoring accessorials, claims, damage, and reattempt cost.
- Blaming carriers for delays caused by warehouse cutoff, paperwork, labeling, or pickup readiness.
- Waiting for the customer to report a delivery exception that tracking already showed.
- Filing claims without the evidence required for recovery.
- Switching carriers after a visible failure without checking segment-level performance and operational fit.
- Depending on one carrier without a contingency plan for capacity or service disruption.

## Self-Check

- Is the delivery promise defined from the customer's point of view?
- Are ship-by, pickup, transit, delivery attempt, final receipt, and appointment metrics separated?
- Is carrier performance segmented by lane, service, region, product, size, season, and delivery type where relevant?
- Are accessorials, claims, damage, reattempts, and manual rework included in cost review?
- Is carrier handoff supported by manifest, scan, documentation, pickup, or chain-of-custody evidence?
- Are exception playbooks defined with owner, evidence, customer communication, and escalation path?
- Are customers proactively updated when tracking shows likely impact?
- Are claims filed with complete evidence and tracked for root-cause patterns?
- Is carrier dependency balanced with backup capacity and operational simplicity?
- Does carrier scorecard data lead to routing, packaging, process, or contract decisions?
