# Contract Renewal And Spend Risk Review

Contract renewal is a chance to correct service, cost, and risk before the organization is locked into another term. Operations teams often let renewals pass because the supplier is familiar, while procurement may negotiate price without seeing service pain or changed demand. This skill helps the agent provide operational evidence for renewal decisions so contracts reflect current service needs and risks.

## Core Rules

### Start renewal review early

Identify renewal dates, notice periods, auto-renewal clauses, termination windows, price escalation dates, volume commitments, and transition lead time. Some suppliers cannot be replaced quickly because implementation, qualification, data migration, inventory, training, or customer notice takes months.

Late review reduces options and creates forced renewals. The operational calendar should feed procurement's renewal pipeline.

### Compare contract scope to actual use

Review what the contract says against how operations actually uses the supplier. Check services, locations, volumes, support hours, deliverables, response times, reporting, materials, subcontractors, and exceptions. Actual use may have expanded beyond contract scope or drifted away from it.

Uncontracted work creates pricing, liability, quality, and control risk. Unused contracted services may show waste or changed demand.

### Bring service evidence to procurement

Provide evidence on delivery, uptime, quality, defects, response time, missed SLAs, escalation behavior, rework, complaints, safety issues, invoice disputes, and operational effort required to manage the supplier. Include examples and trend data.

Procurement needs more than "good" or "bad." Operational evidence improves negotiation leverage and prevents renewal based only on price.

### Reassess demand and volume commitments

Volume may have changed because of growth, decline, seasonality, new sites, process changes, automation, customer commitments, or inventory policy. Review whether minimums, tiers, forecasts, and capacity commitments still fit.

Do not renew old volume assumptions automatically. Overcommitment wastes money; undercommitment can create service and price risk.

### Review risk and continuity dependency

Assess how dependent operations is on the supplier, how difficult replacement would be, whether there are qualified alternatives, and what continuity plan exists. Check concentration, single-source risk, lead time, data ownership, specialized knowledge, location coverage, and financial health.

If the supplier is critical, renewal should include continuity, escalation, recovery, and termination-assistance terms.

### Identify contract gaps and operating friction

Look for gaps in service levels, reporting, data access, change control, support hours, acceptance criteria, warranty, credits, security, safety, insurance, subcontractor approval, audit rights, and exit obligations. Also note operational friction such as unclear contacts, slow quotes, invoice errors, and poor documentation.

These gaps should become negotiation items or internal process fixes.

### Decide renewal posture deliberately

Options include renew as-is, renew with amendments, renegotiate, competitive bid, extend short term, dual-source, reduce scope, terminate, or transition. Each option has cost, service, risk, and timing implications.

Do not treat renewal as default. Also do not switch suppliers without accounting for transition cost, knowledge loss, and service disruption.

### Close the loop after renewal decision

Once the decision is made, update operating teams on terms, scope, contacts, service levels, ordering rules, escalation path, price changes, transition timeline, and any temporary constraints. Update requisition rules, catalogs, runbooks, vendor records, and dashboards.

If the supplier is terminated or replaced, manage open orders, inventory, data, access, and outstanding issues.

### Check commercial incentives against behavior

Renewal should review whether pricing tiers, minimums, rebates, penalties, credits, and exclusivity terms drive the supplier behavior operations needs. A cheaper structure can encourage batching, under-service, or unnecessary volume.

## Common Traps

- Discovering auto-renewal or notice deadlines too late to change supplier.
- Renewing based on familiarity instead of service evidence and current demand.
- Letting procurement negotiate price without operational performance data.
- Ignoring actual scope creep outside the contract.
- Keeping volume commitments that no longer match growth, decline, or seasonality.
- Underestimating dependency on a single supplier or specialized knowledge; treating poor service as anecdotal because defects and escalations were not tracked
- Missing contract gaps in reporting, data, change control, support, audit, exit, and continuity; switching suppliers without transition, training, inventory, data, and customer-impact planning
- Failing to update operational artifacts after renewal terms change; renewing commercial incentives that reward supplier behavior operations is trying to reduce

## Self-Check

- Are renewal, notice, auto-renewal, termination, price escalation, volume, and transition dates known early enough?
- Does operational review compare contract scope to actual services, locations, volumes, support, deliverables, reporting, and exceptions?
- Is service evidence provided for delivery, quality, defects, response, SLA, escalation, rework, complaints, safety, invoices, and management effort?
- Are demand, volume tiers, minimums, forecasts, and capacity commitments still valid?
- Is supplier dependency assessed through alternatives, lead time, data ownership, specialized knowledge, location coverage, concentration, and financial health?
- Are continuity, escalation, recovery, and termination-assistance terms considered for critical suppliers?
- Are gaps in SLAs, reporting, access, change control, warranty, credits, security, safety, insurance, subcontracting, audit, and exit identified?
- Is renewal posture chosen deliberately among as-is, amendment, renegotiation, bid, extension, dual-source, scope reduction, termination, or transition?
- Are transition cost, knowledge loss, inventory, data, open orders, and service disruption considered before switching?
- Are teams, requisition rules, catalogs, runbooks, records, dashboards, contacts, and escalation paths updated after the decision?; are pricing tiers, minimums, rebates, penalties, credits, and exclusivity terms checked against desired supplier behavior?
