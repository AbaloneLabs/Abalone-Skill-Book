# Purchase Request And Approval Flow

Purchase request flow is where operational urgency meets financial and procurement control. If the flow is too loose, teams create unmanaged spend, poor supplier choices, and control gaps. If it is too slow or unclear, operations bypasses the process and still creates risk. This skill helps the agent design purchase requests and approvals so operating needs are met with evidence, authority, and traceability.

## Core Rules

### Define what a complete request contains

A purchase request should include requester, business need, operating impact, required date, quantity, location, budget or cost center, specification, preferred or required supplier rationale, alternatives considered, risk if delayed, and acceptance criteria. High-risk purchases may need security, safety, legal, IT, compliance, or facilities input.

Incomplete requests should be returned quickly with specific missing information. Letting vague requests sit in a queue creates false delay and frustration.

### Match approval to risk and spend

Approval rules should reflect spend amount, budget availability, contract status, supplier status, data access, safety impact, regulatory risk, customer impact, recurring commitment, and exception level. A small purchase can be high risk if it touches personal data, customer commitments, safety, or regulated work.

Avoid using only dollar thresholds. Operational risk and financial risk are different dimensions.

### Make urgency rules explicit

Urgent purchasing should require evidence of service, safety, compliance, revenue, customer, or continuity impact. Define who can approve expedited buying, what documentation can follow later, what spending limits apply, and what post-purchase review is required.

Do not let poor planning automatically qualify as urgency. Repeated emergency requests should trigger demand planning, inventory, or process review.

### Separate requester, approver, and receiver roles

Good control often requires separation between the person requesting, the person approving, the person receiving, and the person validating invoice or service completion. The exact segregation depends on risk and staffing reality.

Where small teams cannot fully separate duties, use compensating controls such as manager review, sample audit, evidence attachment, or post-purchase reconciliation.

### Keep status and ownership visible

Requesters need to know whether the request is awaiting information, budget approval, sourcing, supplier onboarding, contract review, PO creation, delivery, receipt, or closure. Define owner and next action for each status.

Ambiguous status creates side-channel chasing and duplicate requests. A visible workflow reduces pressure on procurement and operations.

### Control off-system and after-the-fact purchases

Purchases made outside the process, on personal cards, through verbal commitments, or before PO approval can create legal, financial, tax, security, and supplier risk. Define when exceptions are allowed, who approves them, and how they are reconciled.

After-the-fact approval should not become normal operations. Track patterns by team, supplier, item, and reason.

### Align budget approval with operational reality

Budget approval should confirm funding and priority, but it should not substitute for specification, supplier, compliance, or service-fit review. Conversely, procurement review should not override operating priority without escalation.

When budget is insufficient, define options: defer, reduce scope, use substitute, reallocate funds, seek exception approval, or change service commitment.

### Close the loop after purchase

The request is not complete when the PO is issued. Confirm delivery, receipt, service start, user acceptance, invoice match, asset or inventory update, and any operational issue. Feed delivery or supplier problems back to procurement and vendor management.

Without closure evidence, the organization cannot learn whether the purchase solved the operating need.

### Watch duplicate and split requests

Requesters may split purchases across multiple requests, teams, cards, or suppliers to avoid thresholds or speed review. Track related requests by supplier, item, project, site, and requester so approval logic reflects the real commitment.

## Common Traps

- Accepting vague purchase requests without operating impact, specification, date, budget, and acceptance criteria.
- Using dollar thresholds as the only approval logic.
- Letting urgency mean "the requester wants it now."
- Allowing repeated emergency buys without reviewing planning or inventory failures.
- Combining requester, approver, receiver, and invoice validator without compensating controls.
- Hiding request status so teams chase through chat, email, and personal relationships; normalizing off-system purchases and after-the-fact approvals
- Treating budget approval as proof that supplier, compliance, security, and service risks are acceptable; issuing a PO but never confirming delivery, receipt, acceptance, and operational outcome
- Failing to feed supplier or delivery issues back into procurement decisions; missing split or duplicate requests that avoid approval thresholds and obscure total commitment

## Self-Check

- Does a complete request require business need, impact, date, quantity, location, budget, specification, supplier rationale, alternatives, delay risk, and acceptance criteria?
- Are high-risk reviews such as safety, security, legal, IT, compliance, or facilities triggered when needed?
- Do approval rules account for spend, budget, contract, supplier, data, safety, regulatory, customer, recurring, and exception risk?
- Are urgency criteria based on service, safety, compliance, revenue, customer, or continuity impact?
- Are expedited approvals, documentation timing, spending limits, and post-purchase review defined?
- Are requester, approver, receiver, and invoice validator roles separated or compensated by review controls?
- Is request status visible with owner and next action at each stage?
- Are off-system, personal-card, verbal, and after-the-fact purchases governed and tracked?
- Are budget shortfalls tied to options such as defer, scope, substitute, reallocate, exception, or service change?
- Does closure confirm delivery, receipt, service start, acceptance, invoice match, records, and supplier feedback?; are related requests checked for duplicate or split purchasing by supplier, item, project, site, and requester?
