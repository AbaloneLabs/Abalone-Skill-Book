# Operational Requirements And Sourcing Brief

Procurement can only source well when operations explains what the business truly needs. A weak sourcing brief turns an operating requirement into a price comparison and leaves service fit, implementation constraints, quality, continuity, and support needs ambiguous. This skill helps the agent translate operating reality into procurement-ready requirements without over-specifying a preferred supplier or hiding critical constraints.

## Core Rules

### Start with the operating problem

Describe the service, process, customer, site, team, or risk the purchase must support. Name the current pain, volume, failure mode, deadline, operational impact, and what good performance will enable. Procurement needs to know why the requirement matters, not only what item or service is requested.

Avoid starting with a brand, SKU, or vendor unless there is a justified compatibility, safety, regulatory, or continuity reason.

### Separate must-haves from preferences

Classify requirements as mandatory, important, optional, and disqualifying. Must-haves should be tied to safety, compliance, compatibility, capacity, service level, continuity, customer commitment, data security, or operational feasibility. Preferences should not be disguised as requirements.

This distinction helps procurement negotiate and compare alternatives without breaking the operation.

### Include usage and demand assumptions

Provide expected volume, seasonality, peak demand, locations, users, shift coverage, service hours, language needs, growth assumptions, delivery frequency, support needs, and implementation timing. A supplier can meet a specification but fail under real demand.

If demand is uncertain, give a range and explain the uncertainty. Procurement can then seek pricing, capacity, and contract flexibility that match the operating risk.

### Define performance and service expectations

State measurable expectations such as delivery lead time, uptime, response time, defect rate, fill rate, quality acceptance, support hours, escalation path, reporting, training, replacement, warranty, and recovery. These should reflect operational needs, not generic vendor promises.

Include what happens when performance misses: notification, corrective action, service credit, replacement, contingency, or escalation. Procurement needs these to negotiate terms.

### Surface implementation and change constraints

Sourcing decisions should consider installation, onboarding, integration, data migration, access setup, training, site readiness, safety review, inventory transition, decommissioning, and change communication. A low-cost option may be expensive if implementation disrupts operations.

Name internal dependencies and who owns them. Procurement cannot solve a delayed access review, training gap, or facility constraint without operational ownership.

### Protect evaluation from narrow price logic

Evaluation criteria should include total cost, quality, service reliability, risk, supplier capacity, implementation effort, support, compliance, continuity, references, and lifecycle cost. Price matters, but price without fit creates downstream rework.

Weight criteria explicitly where possible. If service continuity is critical, it should carry more weight than marginal savings.

### Identify constraints and conflicts of interest

Disclose preferred suppliers, prior relationships, incumbent issues, urgent deadlines, sole-source rationale, compatibility restrictions, and potential conflicts. Operations should provide evidence, not steer procurement through hidden bias.

If only one supplier seems viable, document why alternatives would fail and what risk remains.

### Define acceptance and handoff criteria

Before procurement starts, clarify how operations will accept the goods or service: test, inspection, pilot, reference check, site trial, user acceptance, documentation, training completion, or service validation. Define who signs off and what evidence is required.

Without acceptance criteria, procurement may consider the purchase complete while operations still cannot use it.

### Name post-award operating owners

Sourcing briefs should say who will manage supplier onboarding, first delivery, issue escalation, performance feedback, and renewal input after award. Procurement can source the supplier, but operations must own whether the purchase works in daily service.

## Common Traps

- Sending procurement a vendor name without explaining the operating requirement.
- Treating preferences as mandatory requirements and reducing supplier options unnecessarily.
- Omitting peak volume, locations, service hours, support needs, or implementation timing.
- Accepting generic supplier SLAs that do not match actual service risk.
- Ignoring onboarding, integration, training, access, site readiness, and change-management effort.
- Comparing suppliers mainly on price while excluding lifecycle cost, reliability, support, and risk; hiding incumbent dissatisfaction or preferred-supplier bias instead of documenting evidence
- Using sole-source justification casually because it is faster; failing to define how operations will test and accept the purchase
- Leaving procurement to infer customer or frontline impact from a short requisition note; forgetting post-award operating ownership after sourcing is complete

## Self-Check

- Is the operating problem, current failure mode, impact, deadline, and desired outcome clear?
- Are brand, SKU, and vendor preferences justified only when compatibility, safety, continuity, or regulation requires them?
- Are mandatory, important, optional, and disqualifying requirements separated?
- Are volume, seasonality, peaks, locations, users, shifts, service hours, language, growth, delivery, support, and timing assumptions included?
- Are uncertain demand assumptions expressed as ranges?
- Are delivery, uptime, response, defect, fill-rate, quality, support, escalation, reporting, training, replacement, warranty, and recovery expectations defined where relevant?
- Are implementation dependencies such as onboarding, integration, data, access, training, site readiness, safety, inventory, and communication surfaced?
- Do evaluation criteria include total cost, quality, reliability, risk, capacity, implementation, support, compliance, continuity, references, and lifecycle cost?
- Are preferred suppliers, incumbents, conflicts, urgency, compatibility, and sole-source claims disclosed with evidence?
- Are acceptance, test, pilot, inspection, signoff, and handoff criteria defined before procurement proceeds?; are post-award owners named for onboarding, first delivery, escalation, performance feedback, and renewal input?
