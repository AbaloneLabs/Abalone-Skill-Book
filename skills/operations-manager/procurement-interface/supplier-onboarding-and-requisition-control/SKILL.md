# Supplier Onboarding And Requisition Control

Supplier onboarding is not only adding a vendor to a system. It determines whether operations can buy from the supplier legally, safely, accurately, and without future payment or service disruption. Agents often focus on speed of setup and miss supplier master data, compliance checks, operational readiness, ordering controls, and downstream receiving or invoice problems. This skill helps the agent coordinate supplier onboarding as an operating control.

## Core Rules

### Confirm why the supplier is needed

Before onboarding, identify the operating need, current gap, expected volume, urgency, location, service or item type, contract status, and whether an existing supplier can meet the need. Onboarding a new supplier adds administrative, control, and management burden.

If the request is driven by dissatisfaction with an incumbent, document the service evidence and corrective action history. Do not create duplicate suppliers without understanding the root problem.

### Complete compliance and risk checks

Supplier setup may require tax information, banking validation, insurance, licenses, safety records, data protection review, sanctions screening, conflict-of-interest review, diversity classification, financial health, cybersecurity review, or legal terms. The required checks should match supplier risk and work type.

Do not bypass checks because the operational need is urgent. Use an approved emergency path with temporary limits and follow-up.

### Set up accurate supplier master data

Supplier name, legal entity, tax ID, bank details, address, contacts, remittance information, currency, payment terms, purchase categories, sites, and contract references must be accurate. Bad master data creates invoice holds, duplicate payments, fraud exposure, and delivery confusion.

Define who can create or change supplier master data and how changes are validated. Bank changes and contact changes are especially sensitive.

### Define ordering and requisition rules

Clarify what can be bought, who can request, who approves, which catalog or item codes apply, whether quotes are required, whether a PO is required before work starts, and what spend limits apply. If the supplier is approved only for a narrow scope, make that visible.

Without requisition controls, an onboarded supplier may become a route around sourcing, budget, or policy rules.

### Align operational and payment readiness

Operations may need supplier contacts, delivery instructions, lead times, site access, safety briefing, work permits, insurance certificates, support hours, and escalation paths. Finance may need payment terms, tax forms, invoice submission method, and matching rules. Both sides need to be ready before the first order.

If the supplier can deliver but cannot invoice correctly, the relationship will still fail.

### Control urgent or provisional onboarding

Sometimes operations needs a supplier before full onboarding completes. Define provisional approval, allowed scope, spending cap, expiry, required missing checks, owner, and escalation. Provisional status should be visible to requesters and procurement.

Repeated provisional onboarding indicates planning failure, supplier strategy gap, or a requisition process that is too slow for legitimate operating needs.

### Prevent duplicate and fragmented supplier records

Check for existing related entities, parent companies, duplicate names, local branches, alternate spellings, and legacy records. Duplicate supplier records distort spend, weaken controls, and increase fraud or payment risk.

If multiple records are necessary for legal or operational reasons, document why and how spend will be consolidated for review.

### Validate first transaction and close onboarding

Onboarding should include a first-order or first-service validation where appropriate: PO issued correctly, delivery received, service accepted, invoice matched, payment path works, and operational contacts function. Close onboarding only after the supplier can actually support the operating need.

Capture any setup defects and update the onboarding process.

## Common Traps

- Onboarding a new supplier because it is easier than fixing an incumbent issue.
- Treating supplier setup as administrative data entry rather than a control point.
- Skipping compliance, insurance, safety, security, or legal checks for urgent needs without a controlled provisional path.
- Entering supplier master data with unverified bank, tax, legal, or contact details.
- Letting an approved supplier be used for categories or locations outside its approved scope.
- Failing to align site access, permits, delivery instructions, support contacts, invoice method, and payment terms.
- Leaving provisional suppliers active with no expiry or follow-up; creating duplicate supplier records that hide total spend and payment risk
- Considering onboarding complete before the first order, delivery, invoice, and payment path work; not feeding setup defects back into requisition and onboarding design

## Self-Check

- Is the operating need, gap, volume, urgency, location, work type, contract status, and existing-supplier alternative clear?
- If replacing or adding to an incumbent, is service evidence and corrective history documented?
- Are risk-based checks defined for tax, banking, insurance, license, safety, data, sanctions, conflicts, diversity, financial health, cybersecurity, and legal terms?
- Are emergency onboarding paths limited by scope, spend cap, expiry, owner, and missing-check follow-up?
- Is supplier master data complete and validated for legal entity, tax, bank, address, contacts, currency, terms, categories, sites, and contracts?
- Are supplier master data changes restricted and validated, especially bank and contact changes?
- Are ordering rules clear for allowed purchases, requester, approver, catalog, quote, PO timing, scope, and spend limits?
- Are operational readiness and payment readiness both confirmed?
- Are duplicate, parent, branch, alternate spelling, and legacy supplier records checked?
- Is onboarding closed only after first transaction, delivery or service acceptance, invoice match, payment path, and operational contact validation where appropriate?
