# Internal Request Intake And Service Catalog

Internal service work fails when employees do not know where to ask, what information to provide, what the service team owns, or what timeline to expect. The result is side-channel requests, incomplete tickets, duplicate work, and escalations based on relationships rather than service design. This skill helps the agent design internal request intake and service catalogs that make work visible, actionable, and governable.

## Core Rules

### Define the service catalog by user intent

Start by listing the employee or internal customer intents the service team supports: access request, equipment issue, facility request, data correction, vendor help, policy clarification, onboarding support, billing correction, report request, approval, or operational support. Catalog entries should use the requester's language while mapping to owner and workflow.

Do not build the catalog only around internal departments. Users need to recognize where their need belongs.

### State what is included and excluded

Each service entry should define scope, prerequisites, required information, owner, service expectation, escalation path, and exclusions. If a request belongs to HR, IT, finance, legal, facilities, security, or another team, say where it goes.

Clear exclusions reduce misroutes and prevent the service team from becoming the default owner of ambiguous work.

### Design forms for actionability

Request forms should collect enough information to act: requester, affected person or asset, urgency, deadline, location, business impact, evidence, approvals, sensitive data flags, and requested outcome. Avoid unnecessary fields that slow urgent requests or push users to side channels.

Use conditional fields where possible. A facilities issue, access request, and data correction need different evidence.

### Define triage and ownership

Intake should specify how requests are categorized, prioritized, accepted, returned, reassigned, or escalated. Every accepted request should have an accountable owner, not just a team queue. Returned requests should explain what is missing and how to resubmit.

Work should not sit in "new" or "unassigned" status without aging rules and review.

### Design for request bundles and dependencies

Many internal needs are not single requests. New hire setup, site move, team onboarding, audit response, or role change may require access, equipment, facilities, training, approvals, and data updates. The catalog should show whether these are bundled services, linked child requests, or separate entries.

Define which team coordinates the bundle and which teams own individual tasks. Otherwise employees must become project managers for internal services.

### Protect sensitive and high-risk requests

Some internal requests involve employee data, customer data, safety, legal matters, payroll, access, financial controls, or confidential investigations. Tag these requests early and route them through approved channels with appropriate access restrictions.

Do not let sensitive information sit in broad shared inboxes or free-text fields where unnecessary people can see it.

### Keep the catalog current

Service catalogs decay as teams, policies, systems, vendors, and responsibilities change. Assign an owner for each service entry and a review cadence. Retire obsolete entries and update descriptions when ownership or requirements change.

If employees repeatedly choose the wrong category, the catalog may be unclear, not the users.

### Measure intake health

Track misroutes, missing information, duplicate requests, time to acknowledgement, time in triage, returned requests, side-channel volume, and requester satisfaction. Use these signals to improve forms, instructions, service ownership, and staffing.

Intake health matters because poor intake creates downstream delay and invisible workload.

### Keep requester guidance practical

Catalog language should tell employees what to expect and what they must do. Include examples of common requests, required lead time, approvals, and what not to submit. For high-volume services, use plain-language decision help so requesters choose the right path without knowing the internal organization.

## Common Traps

- Organizing the catalog by internal department rather than requester intent.
- Accepting vague requests and fixing missing information manually, hiding intake defects.
- Asking for too many fields and driving users to chat or email side channels.
- Leaving accepted work in unassigned queues.
- Splitting bundled internal needs into separate requests without a coordinator or dependency view.
- Allowing sensitive requests to enter broad shared channels.
- Failing to update catalog entries after ownership or policy changes.
- Treating repeated wrong-category selection as user error instead of design feedback; writing catalog language around internal jargon that requesters cannot map to their need

## Self-Check

- Are service catalog entries written around internal customer intent and mapped to owner and workflow?
- Does each service entry state scope, prerequisites, required information, owner, service expectation, escalation, and exclusions?
- Do forms collect enough information to act without unnecessary friction?
- Are triage, acceptance, return, reassignment, escalation, and ownership rules clear?
- Are unassigned or new requests governed by aging rules and review?
- Are bundled services and dependent requests coordinated so employees do not have to manage internal handoffs?
- Are sensitive and high-risk requests tagged and routed through restricted approved channels?
- Is each catalog entry owned and reviewed for currency?
- Are misroutes, missing information, duplicates, triage time, side channels, and requester feedback measured?
- Does requester guidance include examples, lead times, approvals, and practical category-selection help?
