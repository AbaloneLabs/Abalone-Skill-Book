# Role Responsibility And Decision Rights

Operating models fail when people are busy but accountability is unclear. A process can have named participants and still lack decision rights: who approves exceptions, who owns blocked work, who can change priority, who tells the customer, who accepts risk, and who is accountable when work crosses teams. Agents often produce role lists or RACI tables that look complete but do not answer what happens when a real decision is needed.

Use this skill before defining responsibilities, redesigning an operating model, clarifying ownership, setting approval thresholds, creating escalation paths, or resolving cross-functional accountability gaps. The goal is to make operational authority explicit enough that work can move without confusion or unsafe improvisation.

## Core Rules

### Separate responsibility from authority

A team may be responsible for performing work without authority to approve exceptions, spend money, change policy, communicate externally, override a control, or accept risk. Define both what each role does and what it is allowed to decide.

Do not assign accountability to a role that lacks the authority or resources to act. That creates frustration and hidden escalation. If authority must remain elsewhere, define the escalation path and expected response time.

### Define ownership by process state

Ownership changes as work moves through intake, validation, execution, review, approval, blocked state, customer response, vendor wait, completion, and remediation. Each state should have an accountable owner. Waiting is not ownerless.

For blocked work, define who monitors aging, who chases the blocker, who communicates delay, and who decides whether to escalate or reprioritize. Without blocked-state ownership, work disappears while everyone believes someone else has it.

### Set decision thresholds

Approval thresholds should be practical and risk-based. Define what frontline staff can decide independently, what supervisors approve, what requires finance, legal, compliance, security, HR, product, or executive review, and what must never be handled locally.

Thresholds may depend on financial value, customer tier, legal exposure, safety, privacy, reputational risk, reversibility, service impact, or precedent. Avoid thresholds so low that every routine case escalates or so high that risky decisions happen without review.

### Clarify exception authority

Exceptions are where decision-right ambiguity causes the most damage. Define who can approve policy exceptions, service-level exceptions, refunds, manual overrides, access changes, vendor deviations, customer promises, expedited work, and temporary workarounds.

Exception decisions should include reason, evidence, approver, expiration where relevant, and precedent risk. If the same exception repeats, it should trigger process or policy review rather than endless case-by-case approval.

### Make handoffs and shared work accountable

Shared work needs one current owner even when multiple teams contribute. Use contributor roles for input, review, or execution, but do not let shared ownership become no ownership. For cross-functional work, define the lead owner, decision forum, tie-breaker, and escalation route.

When teams disagree, the operating model should say who decides. Otherwise the decision will be made by delay, political influence, or customer escalation.

### Align roles with capacity and capability

Responsibility design must match actual capacity, skill, access, and availability. Do not assign review, coaching, escalation, or improvement work to managers or specialists who already lack capacity. Decision bottlenecks are often created by well-intended approval design.

Check whether each role has training, system access, backup coverage, metrics, and time to perform its responsibilities. A role description without operating support is only a wish.

### Protect controls and segregation of duties

Some responsibilities should not sit with the same person: request and approval, execution and audit, refund and reconciliation, access grant and access review, vendor invoice approval and vendor performance scoring. Where segregation is required, define the split and fallback.

Do not relax control boundaries casually for speed. If temporary authority is needed, record it, limit it, and review it.

### Make accountability visible in metrics and records

Systems should show owner, decision status, approver, blocked reason, next action, and escalation age. Metrics should reveal where decisions wait and where ownership changes create delay or rework.

If work is tracked only by team queue, the operation may not see who owns the next decision. Visibility should support action, not blame.

### Review decision rights as work changes

Roles and authority need review when volume grows, risk changes, products launch, automation changes the work mix, vendors are added, regulations shift, or customer promises change. A decision model that worked at small scale may become a bottleneck or risk at larger scale.

Review recurring escalations, approval delays, override patterns, customer complaints, audit findings, and staff confusion. These are signals that decision rights need adjustment.

## Common Traps

- Creating a RACI chart that does not define actual approval authority or escalation timing.
- Assigning accountability without giving the role enough authority, tools, access, or capacity.
- Treating blocked, waiting, or vendor-owned work as ownerless.
- Making every exception require senior approval until the approval path becomes the bottleneck.
- Letting frontline staff make risky exceptions because the formal approval path is too slow.
- Using shared ownership as a polite way to avoid naming one accountable owner.
- Ignoring segregation-of-duties requirements when consolidating roles; measuring team queues without showing who owns the next decision
- Forgetting backup decision rights for absence, after-hours, surge, or incident conditions; leaving decision rights unchanged after scale, automation, vendor changes, or new risk categories

## Self-Check

- Are responsibility and decision authority defined separately for each role?
- Does every process state, including waiting and blocked states, have a current accountable owner?
- Are approval thresholds tied to value, risk, reversibility, service impact, and precedent?
- Is exception authority defined for policy, service, refund, access, vendor, priority, and workaround decisions?
- Is shared work assigned to a lead owner with contributor roles and a tie-breaker?
- Do roles have the capacity, skill, training, system access, and backup coverage needed?
- Are segregation-of-duties and control boundaries preserved?
- Do systems show owner, approver, decision status, blocked reason, next action, and escalation age?
- Are recurring approval delays, overrides, escalations, and audit findings used to revise decision rights?
- Can staff tell who decides when normal process and exception pressure conflict?
