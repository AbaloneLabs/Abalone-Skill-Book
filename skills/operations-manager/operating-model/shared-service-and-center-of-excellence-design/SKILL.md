# Shared Service And Center Of Excellence Design

Shared services and centers of excellence can improve consistency, expertise, tooling, and cost efficiency. They can also become bottlenecks, internal ticket factories, detached policy groups, or advisory teams with no authority to change outcomes. Agents often recommend a shared service or CoE without defining whether it owns execution, standards, advisory support, governance, tools, or improvement.

Use this skill before creating or redesigning a shared operations team, internal service center, specialist review group, operational excellence team, process governance group, or center of excellence. The goal is to make the model useful to the teams it serves and accountable for outcomes.

## Core Rules

### Define the mission and service boundary

State whether the shared group performs work, sets standards, provides expert review, runs tools, trains teams, manages vendors, audits quality, improves processes, or advises local teams. These are different missions and require different staffing, authority, metrics, and service promises.

Avoid vague missions such as "drive excellence" or "support the business." Define services, customers, intake paths, exclusions, and decision rights.

### Build a service catalog

A shared service should publish what it does, who can request it, required inputs, expected turnaround, priority rules, escalation path, cost or chargeback model if any, and completion criteria. The service catalog prevents informal requests from overwhelming the team.

If the group is a CoE rather than a transaction center, define advisory offerings such as playbooks, reviews, templates, training, standards, and complex-case consultation.

### Align authority with accountability

A CoE that is accountable for quality but cannot enforce standards will struggle. A shared service that is expected to meet SLAs but cannot control intake quality will miss targets. Match authority to accountability.

Define what the shared group can mandate, what it can recommend, what requires leadership approval, and how disputes with business units are resolved.

### Design intake and prioritization

Shared teams often receive more demand than they can serve. Define intake requirements, triage criteria, priority tiers, strategic work versus routine work, emergency handling, and what will be declined or deferred. Include requester responsibilities.

Without intake discipline, the shared service becomes a queue for unclear work and the loudest stakeholder wins.

### Keep local context connected

Shared services risk losing local customer, regional, product, or frontline context. Build feedback loops with business units, local operators, customers, and downstream teams. Use liaison roles, office hours, embedded rotations, case reviews, or local champions where needed.

Standards should not ignore legitimate local variation. The model should distinguish harmful variation from necessary adaptation.

### Measure outcomes, not only activity

Shared service metrics should include service levels, quality, rework, requester satisfaction, cycle time, adoption of standards, defect reduction, cost-to-serve, reuse of tools, training effectiveness, and business impact. CoEs should be measured by whether standards and expertise improve outcomes, not only by how many templates or meetings they produce.

Activity metrics can make a shared service look busy while business units still experience delay or poor support.

### Protect capacity for improvement

Shared teams can be consumed by urgent requests and lose time for standardization, automation, training, root-cause work, and tooling. Decide what capacity is reserved for improvement and governance versus daily service.

If the shared service only processes work, it may never deliver the scale benefits that justified centralization.

### Avoid becoming a bottleneck

Expert review and centralized execution can improve quality, but they can also slow decisions. Define which work truly needs shared-service involvement, which can be self-served, which local teams can handle after training, and which exceptions require escalation.

Use enablement to reduce dependency. A strong CoE helps others make better decisions, not just forces every decision through itself.

### Plan funding and stakeholder governance

Shared services need a funding model and governance forum. Decide whether cost is central, allocated, chargeback, subscription, or project-funded. Governance should review demand, performance, priorities, disputes, investment, and service catalog changes.

If funding is unclear, the team may be expected to serve everyone with no mechanism to prioritize or expand.

## Common Traps

- Creating a CoE with a broad mission but no service catalog, authority, or measurable outcome.
- Making the shared group accountable for standards it cannot enforce.
- Allowing informal requests to bypass intake and priority rules.
- Measuring tickets completed or meetings held instead of business outcomes and quality improvement.
- Centralizing expertise so much that local teams become dependent and slower.
- Ignoring local context and calling all variation noncompliance.
- Letting urgent work consume all capacity for standardization and improvement; using shared service as a cost-cutting label while underfunding service expectations
- Failing to define dispute resolution between shared teams and business units; keeping funding and governance ambiguous until demand exceeds capacity

## Self-Check

- Is the shared group mission clear: execution, standards, expert review, tooling, training, audit, vendor management, or improvement?
- Is there a service catalog with requesters, inputs, turnaround, priority, escalation, exclusions, and completion criteria?
- Are authority and accountability aligned for standards, SLAs, intake quality, and decision rights?
- Are intake, triage, emergency handling, requester responsibilities, and deferral rules defined?
- Are local context and legitimate variation captured through feedback loops or local roles?
- Do metrics show quality, cycle time, adoption, rework, requester satisfaction, cost, and business impact?
- Is capacity reserved for improvement, automation, training, and governance?
- Does the model avoid forcing every decision through the shared team when enablement or self-service is better?
- Is funding clear enough to support expected demand and service levels?
- Is there governance for priorities, disputes, service catalog changes, investment, and performance review?
