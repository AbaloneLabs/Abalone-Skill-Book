# Operational Investment And Capex Business Case

Operations investments compete for capital and leadership attention. A weak business case overstates savings, ignores implementation load, or treats benefits as guaranteed. A strong case shows the operating problem, options, benefits, risks, cash timing, readiness, and how value will be measured. This skill helps the agent evaluate investments such as equipment, facilities, tools, automation, staffing infrastructure, and vendor implementation.

## Core Rules

### Start with the operating problem

Define the problem the investment solves: capacity constraint, downtime, quality defect, compliance gap, safety risk, customer delay, labor inefficiency, asset obsolescence, vendor failure, space constraint, or inability to scale. Tie the problem to evidence and business consequence.

Do not begin with the preferred purchase. A tool, equipment, or facility upgrade should be one option, not the assumption.

### Compare alternatives, including no action

Evaluate options such as repair, lease, buy, outsource, automate, redesign process, change staffing, renegotiate vendor, relocate, or do nothing. Include consequences of no action: rising maintenance, lost capacity, incident risk, customer impact, compliance exposure, or missed growth.

A business case is stronger when it explains why other reasonable options are weaker.

### Build benefits from operating drivers

Benefits should connect to measurable drivers: labor hours saved, defects reduced, downtime avoided, throughput increased, space created, safety risk reduced, vendor cost avoided, revenue enabled, compliance evidence improved, or customer delays reduced. State assumptions and confidence.

Avoid counting theoretical savings that cannot be captured. If labor time is saved but headcount or overtime does not change, the benefit may be capacity release, not expense reduction.

### Match funding to lifecycle cost

Investment decisions should consider total lifecycle cost: purchase, implementation, maintenance, support, license renewal, parts, calibration, facilities work, insurance, upgrades, disposal, vendor dependency, and eventual replacement. A low purchase price can create high operating cost.

If the investment replaces an existing asset or process, include decommissioning, contract exit, data migration, asset disposal, and transition support.

### Include full implementation cost

Costs include purchase, implementation, integration, training, downtime, migration, support, maintenance, licensing, facilities work, security review, disposal, change management, and internal project time. Include operating disruption during transition.

For capital requests, coordinate with finance on capex versus opex treatment, depreciation, approval thresholds, procurement, and timing.

### Assess operational readiness and risk

Check whether the organization can implement and use the investment: owners, skills, vendor support, data quality, process readiness, staffing, maintenance, safety, controls, adoption, and support model. A good purchase can fail if operations is not ready to absorb it.

Identify risks: benefit uncertainty, dependency, implementation delay, downtime, user adoption, vendor lock-in, integration failure, regulatory review, or asset disposal.

### Use stage gates for uncertain benefits

When benefits are uncertain or implementation risk is high, structure the request with stage gates: discovery, pilot, limited rollout, full deployment, and value review. Tie each funding stage to evidence and decision criteria. This reduces the risk of committing full spend before operational assumptions are proven.

Stage gates are especially useful for automation, new tools, facility changes, and vendor transitions.

### Define value realization and governance

Before approval, define success metrics, baseline, owner, review cadence, benefit tracking, and corrective action if benefits miss. Include nonfinancial benefits and risk reduction where relevant.

Do not let the business case end at purchase approval. Someone must own value realization after go-live.

### Preserve integrity in ROI claims

Be conservative with ROI. Separate hard savings, soft savings, avoided cost, capacity creation, risk reduction, and strategic enablement. Do not add benefits that overlap or double count. State timing and probability.

If the case depends on assumptions that are not yet proven, recommend pilot, staged approval, or conditional funding.

## Common Traps

- Starting with a desired purchase rather than the operating problem.
- Ignoring no-action cost and reasonable alternatives.
- Counting capacity release as direct expense savings without a capture plan.
- Omitting training, integration, support, downtime, and internal project effort.
- Approving purchase cost while ignoring lifecycle, renewal, disposal, and decommissioning cost.
- Assuming adoption because the tool or asset is better.
- Treating approval as success instead of tracking realized value.
- Double-counting ROI or mixing hard savings with soft benefits without labels; funding high-uncertainty investments all at once instead of using stage gates and evidence

## Self-Check

- Is the operating problem evidenced and tied to capacity, quality, safety, compliance, customer, cost, scale, or asset risk?
- Are reasonable alternatives and no-action consequences compared?
- Are benefits tied to operating drivers with assumptions, confidence, and capture method?
- Are full purchase, implementation, integration, training, downtime, support, maintenance, security, disposal, and internal effort costs included?
- Are lifecycle costs, renewals, parts, upgrades, contract exit, decommissioning, and replacement considered?
- Has finance reviewed capex, opex, depreciation, approval, procurement, and timing treatment where relevant?
- Is operational readiness assessed for owners, skills, vendor support, data, process, staffing, maintenance, controls, and adoption?
- Are success metrics, baseline, owner, cadence, and corrective action defined for value realization?
- For uncertain investments, are stage gates tied to pilot evidence, decision criteria, and value review?
- Are ROI claims separated into hard savings, soft savings, avoided cost, capacity, risk reduction, and strategic enablement without double counting?
