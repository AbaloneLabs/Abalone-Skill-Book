# Decision Log And Action Accountability

Operations often fail after the meeting, not during it. A decision may be made verbally, buried in chat, assigned to no one, or revisited because the rationale was never recorded. Agents often summarize discussion but fail to capture decision, owner, due date, evidence, and verification. This skill helps the agent make governance follow-through durable.

## Core Rules

### Record The Decision, Not Just Discussion

A decision log should state what was decided, who decided, when, based on what evidence, and what options were rejected. Meeting notes that only summarize conversation do not create accountability.

If no decision was made, record that explicitly with the blocker and next decision point.

### Capture Decision Rationale

Include the reason for the decision, key tradeoffs, assumptions, risk accepted, and expected outcome. Rationale prevents future teams from undoing decisions without understanding the context.

Do not write long narratives. Capture enough context for a future owner to understand why the choice was made.

Record known constraints and dissent when material. If finance, compliance, frontline operations, or a vendor raised a serious concern, the log should show how it was handled.

### Assign Action Ownership

Every action should have one accountable owner, due date, expected output, dependency, and verification method. Contributors can be listed, but one owner must be responsible for progress and escalation.

Avoid assigning actions to committees, teams, or functions without a named accountable person.

### Define Completion Evidence

Completion should be observable: SOP updated, access removed, backlog under threshold, vendor response received, control tested, customer notice sent, training completed and verified, or dashboard corrected. "Follow up" is not a completion criterion.

If evidence is unavailable, the action is not ready to close.

Define who verifies completion. The action owner should not always be the only closer, especially for controls, customer commitments, compliance fixes, or vendor remediation.

### Track Aging And Blockers

Action trackers should show overdue items, blocked items, decisions needed, dependency owners, and escalation path. Aging actions often indicate authority gaps, unclear scope, or low priority.

Do not let old actions remain open indefinitely without decision to escalate, re-scope, defer, or cancel.

Use aging thresholds. A minor documentation task and a high-risk control action should not wait for the same number of days before escalation.

### Manage Decision Changes

Decisions can change when facts change. Record what changed, who approved the change, and whether prior commitments, customer promises, vendor instructions, or controls need update.

Do not quietly reverse decisions in side conversations. Hidden reversals damage trust and create conflicting execution.

Notify affected owners when a decision changes. A log update is not enough if teams are already executing from the previous decision.

### Separate Decisions From Tasks

A decision is a choice; an action is the work to implement it. Track both. A decision without implementation is only intent. A task without decision context can drift or optimize the wrong thing.

This separation helps teams see whether they are blocked by authority or execution.

### Make Logs Accessible And Controlled

Decision logs should be stored where relevant owners can find them, with appropriate sensitivity controls. Sensitive legal, HR, privacy, security, or customer information should be protected.

If decisions live only in chat, they will be lost, misquoted, or inaccessible to new owners.

Use stable identifiers for decisions and actions. This makes it easier to connect meeting notes, tickets, risks, audit evidence, and follow-up reviews without relying on memory.

### Review Follow-Through In Governance

Operating cadence should review action status, overdue decisions, completed evidence, and whether actions produced expected outcome. Closing actions without outcome review can hide ineffective follow-through.

Recurring missed actions should trigger governance review, not just reminders.

When actions are closed, check whether related SOPs, dashboards, templates, training, access, and stakeholder communication also changed. Many governance actions fail because only the visible task was completed.

## Common Traps

- Writing meeting summaries that do not state decisions.
- Omitting rationale, assumptions, and tradeoffs.
- Assigning actions to teams instead of accountable owners.
- Closing actions based on effort rather than evidence.
- Letting blocked actions age without escalation.
- Reversing decisions in chat without updating the log.
- Mixing decisions and tasks until nobody knows whether authority or execution is blocked; storing decision records where future owners cannot find them
- Oversharing sensitive decision context in broad logs; reviewing new issues while old decisions and actions remain unresolved

## Self-Check

- Does the log state what was decided, by whom, when, and based on what evidence?
- Is rationale captured, including tradeoffs, assumptions, and risk accepted?
- Does each action have one accountable owner, due date, dependency, and expected output?
- Is completion evidence defined and verifiable?
- Are aging, blocked, deferred, and canceled actions visible?
- Are changed decisions recorded with approval and downstream updates?
- Are decisions separated from implementation tasks?
- Is the log accessible to relevant owners and protected for sensitive content?
- Does governance review action follow-through and outcome, not only status?
- Are repeated missed actions escalated as governance problems?
