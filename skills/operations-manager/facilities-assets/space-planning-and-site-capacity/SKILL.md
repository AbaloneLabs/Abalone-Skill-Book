# Space Planning And Site Capacity

Space planning is an operating capacity decision, not just a floor-plan exercise. Poor space allocation creates queue delays, crowding, safety risk, confidentiality breaches, wasted rent, and inefficient handoffs. Agents often count seats and miss the physical flows, support spaces, storage, accessibility, and peak conditions that determine whether a site works. This skill helps the agent evaluate site capacity in relation to real operating demand.

## Core Rules

### Start from demand and work patterns

Define expected headcount, shifts, attendance pattern, customer traffic, equipment needs, storage, collaboration, private work, training, and peak days. Include future growth, seasonal volume, hybrid attendance variation, and temporary project teams.

Capacity is not only assigned seats. It includes meeting rooms, quiet rooms, lockers, storage, break areas, customer waiting areas, loading zones, secure rooms, and support spaces.

### Map physical flow

Review how people, customers, materials, documents, assets, and waste move through the site. Look for bottlenecks at entrances, elevators, reception, security, shared equipment, supply areas, restrooms, loading docks, and handoff points.

Physical layout can create operational delay even when enough square footage exists. A long walk to shared equipment or secure storage may reduce productivity and increase errors.

### Protect safety, accessibility, and confidentiality

Space plans must preserve emergency egress, occupancy limits, accessibility, ergonomic needs, customer privacy, secure storage, confidential conversations, and separation of incompatible activities. Do not solve space pressure by using areas that create safety or privacy risk.

If work involves regulated records, money, health information, customer identity, or sensitive employee matters, plan for secure handling and conversation space.

### Match space to roles and work modes

Different work requires different space: focused case handling, customer conversations, dispatch, training, coaching, quality review, inventory handling, vendor work, team huddles, and incident command. Avoid one-size-fits-all seating if the work requires different levels of quiet, privacy, tools, or supervision.

Hybrid models need rules for booking, priority, equipment, visitor days, onboarding, and peak overlap. Without rules, space feels arbitrary and capacity data becomes unreliable.

### Include transition and move risk

Space plans often fail during the move, not in the final layout. Plan sequencing, temporary seating, equipment moves, network readiness, labeling, storage, cleaning, access changes, and staff communication. Decide what work must pause, what work can continue, and who handles issues on move day.

For customer-facing or regulated operations, protect records, confidential materials, and chain of custody during relocation. A move can create data, privacy, and service risks if treated as furniture logistics only.

### Evaluate utilization carefully

Low average utilization may hide peak crowding, and high utilization may hide poor experience or safety risk. Track utilization by day, shift, room type, role, and critical area. Include no-shows, ghost bookings, unofficial use, and rooms used for storage.

Do not remove space solely because average usage looks low. Some space exists for resilience, privacy, training, or peak events.

### Coordinate changes with operations

Space changes affect seating, access, equipment, network, phones, signage, cleaning, safety procedures, storage, and employee routines. Plan moves or reconfigurations with communication, downtime windows, temporary capacity, and issue support.

If space changes reduce operating capacity during transition, reflect that in staffing, service levels, and customer commitments.

### Review after occupancy changes

After a move, growth, hybrid change, or layout update, check whether queues, handoffs, safety observations, room conflicts, employee complaints, missed huddles, storage issues, and customer flow improved or worsened. Adjust based on evidence, not only design intent.

Space planning should be iterative because work patterns change.

### Keep space governance current

Define who can approve seat changes, room repurposing, storage use, customer-area changes, and occupancy exceptions. Without governance, local teams may solve immediate pressure by creating safety, privacy, or utilization problems elsewhere. Space ownership should include periodic review and a path to resolve competing needs.

## Common Traps

- Counting seats as capacity while ignoring support spaces, storage, and physical flow.
- Planning for average occupancy and missing peak overlap.
- Solving crowding by compromising egress, accessibility, privacy, or secure storage.
- Treating all roles as having the same workspace needs.
- Using utilization data without checking peak, room type, ghost bookings, and unofficial use.
- Moving teams without planning IT, access, equipment, cleaning, and communication.
- Treating relocation as furniture movement while ignoring records, privacy, service, and chain-of-custody risk.
- Allowing local space changes without governance, creating hidden safety or privacy issues; declaring success after a floor plan change without reviewing operational impact

## Self-Check

- Is space demand based on headcount, shifts, customer traffic, tools, storage, training, hybrid patterns, and peak conditions?
- Are support spaces such as meeting rooms, quiet rooms, lockers, break areas, waiting areas, loading zones, and secure storage included?
- Has physical flow been checked for people, customers, materials, documents, assets, waste, and bottlenecks?
- Are safety, egress, accessibility, ergonomics, privacy, secure handling, and confidential conversation needs protected?
- Are spaces matched to role, work mode, supervision, tools, quiet, and privacy needs?
- Is move sequencing planned for temporary seating, equipment, network, storage, records, communication, and issue support?
- Is utilization segmented by day, shift, room type, role, critical area, and peak rather than average only?
- Are space changes coordinated with IT, access, equipment, cleaning, signage, communication, and temporary capacity?
- Is post-change impact reviewed through queues, handoffs, safety, room conflicts, storage, and customer flow?
- Is governance clear for seat changes, room repurposing, storage use, customer areas, and occupancy exceptions?
