# Launch Support Training And Hypercare

Launch support is the bridge between readiness and normal operations. A launch can be technically successful and still fail operationally if frontline teams are confused, queues spike, managers lack escalation paths, or hypercare never exits. Agents often treat training as a completion item and support as extra staffing, but launch support needs role-specific capability, live feedback, and clear transition criteria. This skill helps the agent design support that stabilizes the launch without becoming permanent emergency mode.

## Core Rules

### Define who needs to be ready for what

List every role affected by the launch: frontline support, operations processors, team leads, managers, vendor contacts, field teams, fulfillment, finance, compliance, sales, customer success, IT, and escalation owners. For each role, define what they must know, decide, do, and escalate.

Do not use one generic training deck for roles with different decisions. A support agent needs issue diagnosis and messaging; a fulfillment team needs process and exception handling; a manager needs thresholds and authority.

### Train on scenarios, not only features

Launch training should include common cases, edge cases, failure modes, eligibility questions, customer confusion, internal handoffs, system errors, manual workaround, and stop conditions. People need to practice the decisions they will face, not merely watch a walkthrough.

Use realistic examples from pilot data, known defects, customer questions, and prior launches. Include what to say when the answer is unknown.

### Prepare job aids and point-of-use guidance

Training fades quickly during launch pressure. Provide concise job aids, macros, runbooks, escalation maps, decision trees, queue category definitions, dashboard notes, and quick references where work happens. Link deeper policy for uncommon or high-risk cases.

Point-of-use guidance should be version-controlled and easy to update during hypercare. If teams rely on screenshots in chat, the knowledge base is not ready.

### Plan surge coverage by demand signal

Support planning should use expected launch volume, contact rate, defect rate, manual work rate, peak windows, channels, language needs, regions, and vendor response times. Define backup coverage, manager availability, overtime rules, queue routing, and deflection options.

Surge coverage should have triggers. If contact rate, backlog, SLA breach, or severity count crosses a threshold, the response should already be defined.

### Define hypercare scope and exit criteria

Hypercare should have a purpose, start, planned end, coverage model, issue types, escalation path, reporting cadence, and exit criteria. Exit criteria may include issue volume, severity trend, backlog level, support contact rate, defect burn-down, training completion, handoff completion, and stable dashboards.

Without exit criteria, hypercare becomes indefinite special handling. That hides the true cost of launch and delays process hardening.

### Keep feedback loops short

During launch, frontline questions and errors are evidence. Capture confusing guidance, repeated customer questions, misroutes, macro defects, form issues, missing categories, unclear ownership, and workaround use. Review daily or more frequently during high-risk launches.

Assign owners for updates to knowledge, training, tooling, messaging, and process. Feedback that is collected but not acted on increases frustration.

### Protect quality while moving fast

Launch support often pressures staff to close cases quickly. Define quality checks for customer messaging, manual adjustments, refunds, access changes, data fixes, exceptions, and compliance-sensitive work. Speed should not create downstream correction work or control failures.

Use sampling, peer review, supervisor approval, or system checks for risky actions. Make it clear which shortcuts are prohibited even during launch.

### Transfer cleanly to steady-state operations

At exit, confirm issue ownership, updated runbooks, training gaps, queue categories, unresolved defects, open customer commitments, reporting cadence, vendor actions, and remaining risks. Normal teams should not inherit a pile of ambiguous launch leftovers.

Document which temporary roles, channels, and workarounds are retired. If some must continue, give them an owner, expiry, and improvement path.

## Common Traps

- Treating training attendance as proof of readiness.
- Giving every role the same launch briefing regardless of their decisions and authority.
- Training only the happy path and feature overview.
- Relying on chat screenshots instead of maintained point-of-use guidance.
- Planning surge coverage without thresholds that trigger action.
- Running hypercare with no scope, end date, or exit criteria.
- Collecting frontline feedback without changing knowledge, tooling, messaging, or process; letting speed pressure weaken quality checks for manual, financial, access, or compliance actions
- Handing launch leftovers to steady-state teams without owners and context; allowing temporary launch channels and workarounds to become permanent by neglect

## Self-Check

- Are all affected roles identified with what they must know, decide, do, and escalate?
- Is training role-specific rather than one generic deck?
- Do training scenarios include common cases, edge cases, known defects, customer confusion, system errors, workarounds, and stop conditions?
- Are job aids, macros, runbooks, escalation maps, decision trees, queue definitions, and dashboard notes available at the point of work?
- Are support volume, contact rate, defect rate, manual work, peak windows, language, region, and vendor assumptions translated into coverage?
- Are surge triggers tied to backlog, SLA, contact rate, severity, or other signals?
- Does hypercare have scope, owner, cadence, planned end, and exit criteria?
- Are frontline feedback loops short enough to update knowledge, tooling, messaging, training, and process during launch?
- Are quality checks defined for risky manual, financial, access, data, customer, and compliance actions?
- Is steady-state transition clear for unresolved issues, updated artifacts, retired workarounds, remaining risks, and owners?
