# Launch Retrospective And Process Hardening

A launch retrospective should convert launch stress into stronger operations. It is not a ceremonial meeting to list what went well and what went poorly. Agents often summarize launch issues without linking them to root causes, ownership, permanent process changes, or retirement of temporary workarounds. This skill helps the agent harden operations after launch by separating launch noise from structural weakness and turning lessons into durable changes.

## Core Rules

### Anchor the review in evidence

Collect launch timeline, issue log, severity history, customer impact, support volume, backlog, SLA performance, manual work, defects, escalations, refunds or credits, vendor performance, staffing data, quality findings, communications, and decision records. Do not rely only on memory or the loudest stories.

Compare actual results with readiness assumptions. The most useful questions are where the forecast, training, tooling, process, vendor, communication, or control assumptions were wrong.

### Separate symptoms from operating causes

A launch issue may appear as customer complaints, long queues, bad data, missed SLAs, or confusing communication. Investigate the operating cause: unclear ownership, weak handoff, bad eligibility logic, insufficient staffing, missing runbook, poor vendor readiness, wrong forecast, tooling gap, approval delay, or unsupported promise.

Avoid stopping at "the team worked hard" or "volume was higher than expected." Those observations do not harden the process.

### Review temporary workarounds and special handling

List launch-only channels, manual spreadsheets, extra approvals, special queues, exception paths, leadership check-ins, vendor escalations, and customer accommodations. Decide which should be retired, formalized, automated, controlled, or kept temporarily with owner and expiry.

Temporary practices are often useful during launch but dangerous if they become hidden permanent operations. They can distort metrics, bypass controls, and hide true cost.

### Evaluate customer and employee experience

Review not only internal execution but the experience of customers, users, frontline staff, support teams, vendors, and managers. Look at confusion, repeated questions, effort, inconsistent answers, escalation burden, training gaps, and trust impact.

A launch can meet internal milestones while creating avoidable customer effort or frontline overload. Those are operational defects.

### Convert findings into owned changes

Each material finding should produce a concrete action: process change, runbook update, training revision, dashboard change, staffing model update, vendor SLA review, service-level adjustment, tooling fix, control change, communication template, inventory policy update, or product feedback. Assign owner, due date, priority, evidence of completion, and expected outcome.

Do not create a large lesson-learned list with no governance. Fewer owned actions are better than many vague recommendations.

### Update readiness gates for the next launch

If a failure would have been caught by a better readiness gate, add or revise the gate. Examples include support contact forecast, manual work simulation, vendor coverage confirmation, data reconciliation test, training scenario completion, rollback test, customer messaging review, or queue routing validation.

The retrospective should improve future launch criteria, not only the launched operation.

### Preserve what worked

Retrospectives often focus on failures and lose useful practices. Identify decisions, roles, dashboards, communication patterns, triage rules, training formats, vendor contacts, or phased rollout choices that reduced risk. Decide whether they should become standard launch practice.

Positive findings should be specific. "Good teamwork" is less useful than "hourly issue review with decision owner reduced duplicate escalations."

### Confirm stabilization before closing

Before declaring launch closed, check issue volume trend, unresolved high-severity items, backlog, customer commitments, support contact rate, manual work, temporary controls, vendor actions, documentation updates, and owner handoff. Hypercare exit should be evidence-based.

If stabilization is incomplete, define remaining risks and continue a lighter operating review rather than ending support abruptly.

## Common Traps

- Running a retrospective from anecdotes instead of launch evidence.
- Treating high effort as success while ignoring process weakness.
- Stopping at symptoms such as high volume without finding operating causes.
- Keeping launch-only spreadsheets, channels, approvals, and workarounds after hypercare.
- Reviewing internal milestones while ignoring customer effort and frontline overload.
- Creating lessons learned with no owner, due date, or completion evidence.
- Failing to update readiness gates after discovering what was missing; forgetting to standardize practices that worked well
- Closing hypercare because the calendar says so rather than because stabilization criteria are met; allowing unresolved launch defects to disappear into normal backlog without priority and context

## Self-Check

- Is the retrospective grounded in timeline, issue log, severity, customer impact, support volume, backlog, SLA, manual work, defects, escalations, vendor, staffing, quality, communication, and decision evidence?
- Are actual launch results compared with readiness assumptions?
- Are symptoms traced to operating causes such as ownership, handoff, forecast, tooling, training, vendor, approval, control, or promise gaps?
- Are temporary launch channels, spreadsheets, queues, approvals, workarounds, and accommodations retired, formalized, controlled, or time-bounded?
- Are customer, user, frontline, vendor, and manager experiences reviewed, not only internal milestones?
- Does each material finding have owner, due date, priority, completion evidence, and expected outcome?
- Are runbooks, training, dashboards, staffing models, vendor agreements, service levels, controls, templates, and product feedback updated where needed?
- Are future readiness gates improved based on launch misses?
- Are effective launch practices captured as reusable standards?
- Is hypercare exit supported by stable volume, backlog, severity, customer commitments, manual work, vendor actions, documentation, and owner handoff?
