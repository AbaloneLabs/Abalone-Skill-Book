# Improvement Sustainment And Standard Work

An improvement is not complete when the change is launched. It is complete only when the new way of working is stable, owned, measured, and protected from regression. Agents often stop after implementation and miss the work that makes the improvement durable: standard work updates, coaching, control plans, metric follow-up, old artifact removal, and ownership. This skill helps the agent ensure that continuous improvement produces lasting operating change.

## Core Rules

### Define the new standard work

State exactly what the standard process is after the improvement. Include who performs the work, trigger, inputs, steps, handoffs, evidence, tools, quality checks, escalation path, exceptions, and completion criteria. If the improvement changes only one local condition, say so; if it changes the standard process, update the official source of truth.

Standard work should be usable by someone who was not in the improvement project. Avoid relying on meeting memory, chat threads, or one expert's explanation.

### Update the operating system around the change

SOPs alone do not sustain behavior. Update job aids, forms, templates, scripts, queue labels, system fields, automation rules, quality checklists, training, onboarding, dashboards, performance measures, escalation guides, and vendor instructions where relevant. Remove or archive old materials that would lead people back to the prior method.

Check incentives. If the old metric rewards speed while the improvement requires better documentation, people may revert. Sustainment requires alignment between process, measures, coaching, and leadership attention.

### Assign ongoing ownership

Name the owner responsible for maintaining the improved process after the project team moves on. Ownership should cover updates, training refresh, metric review, exception decisions, control evidence, and periodic checks. If ownership is shared, define who decides when there is disagreement.

Do not let "the team" own sustainment vaguely. Shared accountability without decision rights leads to drift.

### Verify benefit realization

Compare actual results with the expected benefit. Use baseline, target, and post-change evidence: cycle time, defect rate, backlog, rework, customer contacts, staff effort, quality, control exceptions, cost, or satisfaction. Include unintended consequences, not only the intended measure.

Benefits may take time to appear. Define review points and avoid declaring victory before the process has operated through normal variation, peak volume, and exception cases.

### Create a control plan for regression

Identify the few signals that show whether the improvement is holding: old process artifacts, repeated exceptions, quality defects, manual workarounds, backlog growth, staff questions, missing fields, skipped checks, or customer complaints. Define who reviews these signals and what action follows.

Regression is not always people ignoring instructions. It can occur because volume changes, upstream data changes, a system release breaks a field, new hires miss training, or leaders stop reinforcing the behavior. The control plan should detect both behavior drift and system drift.

### Close old paths and workarounds

Old forms, macros, spreadsheets, reports, queue labels, saved links, and local instructions can keep the prior process alive. Find and retire them. If some old path must remain temporarily, label it, restrict scope, assign owner, and set an end date.

Leaving old artifacts in circulation creates inconsistent execution and makes performance data unreliable. Sustainment requires cleanup, not only announcement.

### Hand over learning, not just documentation

Explain why the improvement exists, what was tried, what evidence supported the decision, what tradeoffs were accepted, and what issues remain. This helps future operators avoid reversing the improvement for understandable but incomplete reasons.

If the improvement revealed a broader operating model issue, capture it for the backlog. Sustainment is not only preserving one fix; it is learning how the system should improve next.

### Review adoption after support fades

Initial adoption can look strong while project support is active. Check again after hypercare, after a staffing change, after a volume peak, and after new hires enter the process. Look for shadow work, inconsistent answers, reopened defects, and coaching demand.

If adoption fades, diagnose before blaming. The issue may be unclear standard work, poor tooling, insufficient training, conflicting metrics, weak ownership, or a change that did not fit live conditions.

## Common Traps

- Treating launch as completion. Sustained behavior and measured benefit still need verification.
- Updating the SOP but not the tools, templates, metrics, and coaching that shape behavior.
- Leaving old artifacts active. People may follow outdated instructions because they are easier to find.
- Assigning vague ownership. Someone must own maintenance and decisions after implementation.
- Measuring only the intended benefit. A change can improve speed while harming quality or controls.
- Checking adoption only during hypercare. Regression often appears after extra support ends.
- Blaming users for drift without checking incentives, training, tooling, and volume changes.

## Self-Check

- Is the new standard work explicit enough for someone outside the project to follow?
- Have SOPs, job aids, forms, scripts, system fields, automation rules, quality checks, training, dashboards, and vendor instructions been updated where needed?
- Are old artifacts, shortcuts, reports, and workarounds retired or given temporary owners and end dates?
- Is ongoing ownership clear for maintenance, training, metric review, exceptions, and control evidence?
- Are expected benefits compared with baseline and post-change evidence, including unintended consequences?
- Does a control plan define regression signals, review owner, review cadence, and response actions?
- Is learning from the improvement handed over, including tradeoffs, unresolved risks, and future backlog items?
- Has adoption been checked after support fades, volume changes, staffing changes, or new hires enter the process?
