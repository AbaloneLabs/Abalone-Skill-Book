# Evidence Retention And Audit Readiness

Audit readiness is not scrambling for screenshots after the fact. It is designing operations so evidence is created, retained, protected, and retrievable as work happens. Agents often say "keep records" without defining which records, where they live, who owns them, how long they are retained, and how sensitive data is protected. This skill helps the agent make evidence reliable enough for audit, compliance, customer commitments, and internal review.

## Core Rules

### Define Evidence Purpose

Identify what the evidence must prove: approval, identity verification, customer notice, control operation, exception review, training completion, access removal, reconciliation, vendor action, complaint handling, or regulatory deadline. Evidence design depends on the claim it must support.

Do not retain random artifacts because they look useful. Keep the evidence that proves required actions.

### Specify Required Records

For each process, define required record type, fields, timestamp, owner, system of record, retention period, and sensitivity level. Include logs, approvals, case notes, attachments, audit trails, reports, communications, quality samples, and exception decisions where needed.

Evidence should be understandable to someone who was not present when the work happened.

Include negative evidence where needed. Some controls require proof that no exception existed, no unauthorized access occurred, no unresolved reconciliation remained, or no required notice was missed.

### Protect Sensitive Data

Evidence may contain personal data, financial information, health details, security data, employee records, contracts, or confidential customer information. Limit access, avoid unnecessary duplication, and follow retention and deletion rules.

Audit readiness does not justify over-collecting or broadly sharing sensitive information.

### Store Evidence In The Right System

Evidence should live in a controlled, searchable, backed-up system with ownership and access controls. Chat threads, personal drives, local downloads, and email attachments are fragile evidence sources.

If temporary storage is unavoidable during disruption, define transfer, retention, and deletion steps.

Name the system of record when duplicates exist. If evidence appears in CRM, ticketing, storage folders, and reports, teams need to know which record is authoritative during audit.

### Maintain Evidence Quality

Evidence should be complete, accurate, timely, and tied to the correct item. Check for missing timestamps, unclear approver, inconsistent naming, broken links, inaccessible attachments, duplicate records, and notes that do not support the decision.

Poor evidence can fail audit even when the underlying work was done correctly.

Check whether evidence can prove sequence. For many controls, it matters that verification happened before approval, notice before closure, or access removal before role change took effect.

### Prepare Audit Trails For Changes

Auditors may need to see who changed a record, when, why, and what the prior value was. For high-risk records, ensure status changes, approvals, overrides, access changes, and deletions are logged.

Manual corrections should include reason and approval where required.

### Assign Evidence Owners

Define who creates, reviews, retains, retrieves, and certifies evidence. The process owner may not be the system owner or retention owner. Make handoffs clear.

If an audit request arrives, the team should know who coordinates response and who approves sharing.

### Test Retrieval Before Audit

Periodically sample evidence and retrieve it as an auditor would. Confirm that records are complete, accessible, understandable, and tied to the control or obligation. Fix gaps before a formal audit.

Testing retrieval often reveals broken links, access issues, inconsistent naming, and missing context.

Test retrieval across time periods and owners. Evidence that is easy to retrieve for last week may be missing after retention moves, team changes, vendor transitions, or system migrations.

### Respond To Audit Requests Carefully

For audit requests, provide the requested evidence, scope, time period, and explanation without oversharing unrelated sensitive data. Track what was provided, when, by whom, and under what approval.

If evidence is missing or flawed, be transparent with the appropriate owner and define remediation. Do not fabricate or recreate evidence without disclosure.

Keep a record of audit questions and responses so future reviews use consistent explanations. Repeated clarification requests can reveal weak evidence design or unclear control ownership.

## Common Traps

- Keeping records without knowing what claim they must prove.
- Retaining too much sensitive data because it may be useful someday.
- Storing key evidence in chat, personal drives, or local files.
- Assuming a completed workflow automatically creates audit-ready evidence.
- Missing timestamps, approvers, item links, or decision rationale.
- Allowing manual edits without reason or audit trail.
- Leaving evidence ownership split between process and system teams; waiting for an audit request before testing retrieval
- Oversharing sensitive records outside the audit scope; recreating missing evidence in a way that misrepresents when it was created

## Self-Check

- Is the purpose of each evidence type clear?
- Are required records, fields, owner, system, retention, and sensitivity defined?
- Is sensitive data minimized, protected, and retained only as allowed?
- Is evidence stored in controlled systems rather than fragile channels?
- Are completeness, timestamps, approvals, item links, and rationale checked?
- Are record changes, overrides, and deletions auditable where needed?
- Are evidence creation, retention, retrieval, and sharing owners assigned?
- Has retrieval been tested before audit?
- Are audit responses scoped, approved, tracked, and free of unnecessary oversharing?
- Are missing or flawed evidence issues escalated honestly with remediation?
