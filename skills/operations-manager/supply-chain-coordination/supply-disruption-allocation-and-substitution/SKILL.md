# Supply Disruption Allocation And Substitution

Supply disruptions force operations to make explicit choices that are often hidden during normal flow. When material, labor, supplier capacity, transportation, or inventory is constrained, not every customer, site, product, or order can be served normally. Agents often jump to expediting or generic stakeholder updates without defining allocation rules, substitution safety, customer impact, and recovery governance. This skill helps the agent manage disruption decisions with fairness, traceability, and operational control.

Use this skill during shortages, supplier outages, inbound failures, constrained inventory, disrupted transportation, quality quarantine, recall, allocation, rationing, or substitution review. The agent should protect safety, customer trust, and service continuity while making tradeoffs visible.

## Core Rules

### Define the disruption and its horizon

Name what is constrained: item, supplier, lane, facility, labor, system, carrier, quality release, packaging, or regulatory approval. Estimate the affected volume, sites, customers, products, open orders, future demand, and expected duration. Separate confirmed facts from assumptions.

A one-day receiving delay, a six-week supplier outage, and an unknown quality hold require different responses. Do not use the same playbook for every shortage.

### Stabilize the current state before optimizing

First prevent bad decisions: freeze uncontrolled promises, stop automatic allocation if needed, protect inventory records, confirm actual stock, identify in-transit supply, check quality holds, and assign disruption owner. If teams continue making commitments based on stale availability, the shortage will worsen.

Stabilization does not mean stopping all work. It means ensuring decisions use the same current facts.

### Establish allocation principles

Constrained supply must be allocated by a visible rule. Consider safety-critical need, regulatory obligation, contractual commitment, customer tier, backorder age, margin, launch dependency, service level, geographic fairness, public-sector obligations, vulnerable customers, or operational continuity. The rule may combine factors, but it should be explicit.

Avoid allocation through informal escalation, personal relationships, or whoever notices first. Unclear allocation damages trust internally and externally.

### Validate substitutions before offering them

Substitutions can be product, material, supplier, configuration, packaging, shipping mode, process, or service-level changes. Check compatibility, safety, quality, regulatory approval, customer consent, warranty, documentation, pricing, inventory system setup, and downstream process impact.

Do not offer a substitute simply because it appears available. A technically similar item may fail in the customer's environment, violate a contract, create support burden, or require different handling.

### Coordinate customer and stakeholder communication

Shortage communication should be consistent across sales, support, operations, customer success, procurement, and leadership. Explain what is affected, what is confirmed, expected timing, available alternatives, customer action if any, and next update. Avoid overpromising recovery dates.

If different customer groups receive different treatment, the rationale should be documented and approved. Inconsistent messages create escalation and reputational risk.

### Decide when to expedite, reserve, or cancel

Recovery actions include expediting inbound supply, reserving limited stock, reallocating from lower-priority demand, placing emergency buys, changing production or service schedules, splitting orders, offering substitutes, delaying lower-impact work, or cancelling orders. Each action has cost and fairness implications.

Choose based on impact, feasibility, recoverability, cost, and risk. Do not use premium freight or emergency buys reflexively if they only move the problem by a day while creating major cost or chaos.

### Track residual and downstream risk

Shortage recovery can create hidden debt: depleted safety stock, customer dissatisfaction, rushed quality checks, supplier quality risk, unbalanced inventory, exhausted staff, inflated costs, and future backlog. Record what was consumed to recover and what must be rebuilt.

If a recovery action weakens resilience, assign an owner to restore buffers, update plans, or review policy.

### Conduct a post-disruption review

After the immediate event, review trigger, detection time, decision speed, supplier performance, inventory accuracy, allocation fairness, communication quality, customer impact, cost, and recurrence prevention. Identify whether earlier risk indicators were missed.

Do not let a successful scramble hide the system weakness that made the scramble necessary.

## Common Traps

- Treating every disruption as an expedite problem instead of defining scope, horizon, and decision rules.
- Continuing normal order promises while availability data is stale.
- Allocating scarce supply through escalation pressure or personal influence.
- Offering substitutions before safety, quality, contract, pricing, and customer consent are checked.
- Sending different recovery messages through sales, support, and operations.
- Using premium recovery actions without comparing cost, benefit, and downstream disruption.
- Failing to rebuild safety stock, staff capacity, or supplier confidence after recovery.
- Skipping post-disruption review because the immediate crisis was resolved.

## Self-Check

- Is the constrained item, process, supplier, site, or lane clearly defined?
- Are affected volume, customers, sites, open orders, future demand, and duration estimated with confidence level?
- Are stale promises paused or controlled while the current state is stabilized?
- Are actual inventory, in-transit supply, quality holds, and supplier commitments reconciled?
- Are allocation principles explicit, approved, and fair enough to explain?
- Are substitutions validated for compatibility, safety, quality, regulatory, commercial, and customer consent needs?
- Are customer and internal communications consistent and evidence-based?
- Are expedite, reservation, cancellation, and reallocation decisions justified by impact, feasibility, cost, and risk?
- Are residual risks such as depleted buffers, backlog, cost, and staff strain owned?
- Is a post-disruption review planned to prevent recurrence?
