# Supplier Capacity And Lead Time Risk

Supplier performance is not only whether purchase orders are placed or invoices are paid. Operations depend on whether suppliers can produce, allocate, ship, and recover within the time and quality needed. Agents often treat lead time as a static field and supplier commitments as reliable until they fail. This skill helps the agent inspect supplier capacity and lead-time risk before it becomes a customer service or production crisis.

Use this skill when reviewing supplier readiness, constrained materials, lead-time assumptions, supplier commitments, expedite requests, capacity reservations, dual sourcing, allocation, or supply risk. The agent should turn vague supplier confidence into operational evidence and decision points.

## Core Rules

### Treat lead time as a distribution, not a fixed number

Lead time varies by order size, season, supplier backlog, raw material availability, production slot, transportation mode, customs, quality inspection, and disruption. Ask for historical actuals, current queue, confirmed production dates, shipping dates, and known risks. A catalog lead time or ERP default may be outdated.

For critical items, plan with variability: best case, typical, delayed, and severe delay. Averages hide tail risk. If the operation cannot tolerate late delivery, the plan needs protection.

### Verify supplier capacity against the required mix

A supplier may have total capacity but not capacity for the specific SKU, configuration, grade, packaging, certification, region, or delivery schedule needed. Confirm capacity by item and timing, not just supplier-level assurance.

Check whether the supplier is constrained by raw materials, labor, tooling, machine time, quality testing, regulatory release, subcontractors, or transportation. The bottleneck may be outside the supplier's direct factory.

### Distinguish promise, allocation, and reservation

A supplier saying they "should be able to support" is different from confirmed allocation, production reservation, shipped inventory, or contractual commitment. Name the level of commitment and what evidence supports it.

If a customer promise depends on supplier output, avoid treating soft supplier language as firm supply. Escalate when the commitment level is too weak for the downstream promise.

### Monitor early warning signals

Signals include late acknowledgments, partial shipments, quality escapes, frequent date changes, reduced responsiveness, unusual minimums, price pressure, raw material notices, capacity rationing, high expedite requests, financial distress, labor issues, regional disruption, or carrier failures. Track patterns, not isolated excuses.

If a supplier starts missing small commitments, do not wait for a major miss. Review mitigation before the constraint reaches customer-facing operations.

### Use expediting as an exception, not a planning method

Expediting can recover a critical need, but repeated expedites reveal planning, supplier, or policy failure. Before expediting, confirm feasibility, cost, impact on other orders, transportation mode, internal receiving readiness, and whether the supplier is displacing another customer's or another order's capacity.

Do not promise expedited availability until supplier commitment, carrier plan, customs or receiving constraints, and internal handling are verified.

### Build contingency options before crisis

Contingency may include alternate suppliers, approved substitutes, safety stock, different transport modes, early order release, tooling redundancy, supplier development, contract clauses, local stock, or customer allocation rules. Some options require qualification and cannot be created during an outage.

For high-risk materials, know the qualification time, documentation, quality approval, and cost of switching. A "backup supplier" that has never been qualified is only an idea.

### Align supplier risk with customer and operational promises

If supplier uncertainty is high, adjust customer commitments, launch dates, inventory targets, substitution policy, service levels, or sales guidance. Do not isolate supplier risk in procurement while operations continues making confident promises.

Communicate risk in practical terms: what supply is confirmed, what is at risk, when certainty improves, what decisions are needed, and what customer impact could occur.

### Review supplier reliability as a planning input

Track supplier on-time in-full, lead-time variance, quality defects, responsiveness, expedite frequency, allocation accuracy, recovery performance, and forecast adherence. Use this evidence to adjust planning parameters, safety stock, approval thresholds, and supplier development priorities.

Do not give every supplier the same planning assumption. Reliability should influence how much risk the operation can take.

## Common Traps

- Using static lead times without checking recent actual performance and current queue.
- Accepting supplier optimism as confirmed capacity.
- Checking supplier capacity at total volume while missing SKU, configuration, certification, or timing constraints.
- Treating repeated expedites as normal operations.
- Creating customer commitments from unreserved supplier supply.
- Calling a supplier "backup" before qualification, documentation, and commercial setup are ready.
- Keeping supplier risk inside procurement instead of updating operations, sales, and customer promises.
- Measuring supplier performance only after failures rather than using reliability to plan.

## Self-Check

- Is lead time represented with variability and current evidence, not only a static master-data value?
- Is supplier capacity confirmed for the exact item, configuration, quality, timing, and delivery need?
- Are raw material, tooling, labor, testing, subcontractor, and transport bottlenecks considered?
- Is the supplier commitment level clearly labeled as soft promise, allocation, reservation, shipped, or contractual?
- Are early warning signals tracked and escalated before major failure?
- Are expedites justified with cost, feasibility, impact, and internal readiness?
- Are contingency options qualified and usable before crisis?
- Are customer promises adjusted when supplier risk is high?
- Are supplier reliability metrics feeding planning assumptions?
- Would downstream teams understand what supply is confirmed, at risk, and decision-dependent?
