# Team Performance Review And Coaching

Operational coaching should improve capability and system performance, not simply pressure people with metrics. A good review separates facts from interpretation, distinguishes individual behavior from process constraints, and turns feedback into specific support and follow-up. Agents often write vague feedback or jump from dashboard variance to blame. This skill helps the agent make performance review fair, evidence-based, and useful for frontline operations.

## Core Rules

### Start With Evidence And Context

Use specific examples, metric trends, quality samples, customer or requester impact, audit findings, queue data, and observed behaviors. Include context such as work mix, staffing, tool issues, training stage, shift conditions, and policy changes.

Do not coach from one isolated data point unless the risk is high. A single severe control failure may need immediate action, but routine coaching should use enough evidence to be fair.

### Separate System Issues From Behavior

Before giving performance feedback, ask what the person or team controlled. Poor results may come from unclear SOPs, bad routing, broken tools, unrealistic targets, missing access, weak training, demand spikes, or conflicting priorities. These require management action, not only coaching.

When behavior is the issue, describe the observable behavior and its impact. Avoid labels such as careless, lazy, negative, or not a team player unless supported by specific facts and handled through proper management process.

### Coach To One Or Two Focus Areas

Too many coaching points create confusion. Choose the few behaviors or skills that will most improve service, quality, flow, or risk. State what good looks like, what support will be provided, and how progress will be reviewed.

Good coaching converts abstract expectations into practice: how to document a case, when to escalate, how to check evidence, how to prioritize a queue, or how to communicate delays.

### Balance Accountability With Support

Coaching should include expectations and help. Support may include training, shadowing, job aids, clearer priority rules, system access, feedback frequency, reduced complexity during ramp, or paired review. Accountability includes commitments, timeline, and consequences where appropriate.

Do not present support as optional if the person lacks training or tools. Do not avoid accountability when expectations and support are already clear.

### Use Metrics Without Reducing People To Metrics

Metrics can focus discussion, but they should be paired with examples and quality evidence. Explain what the metric means, what it may hide, and what behavior would improve it. Avoid vague pressure such as "get your numbers up."

For teams, review distribution and root causes rather than only averages. Averages hide people who need support and people carrying hidden load.

### Protect Psychological Safety And Candor

Teams need to be able to surface blockers, errors, near misses, and unclear instructions without fear of automatic blame. Coaching that punishes disclosure will reduce visibility into operational risk.

This does not mean ignoring repeated issues. It means distinguishing honest error, risky shortcut, willful noncompliance, training gap, and system failure so the response is proportionate.

### Document Decisions And Follow-Up

Record the feedback topic, evidence, agreed action, support, owner, timeline, and review date. Documentation should be factual and appropriate for the forum. Sensitive individual performance records should follow company policy and HR guidance.

Follow-up is essential. If coaching is not revisited, staff learn that feedback is performative or arbitrary.

### Avoid Public Shaming

Team reviews can discuss process performance, shared metrics, and learning. Individual performance concerns should usually be handled privately through the right management path. Public comparison can damage trust and encourage gaming.

Use team-level examples when the issue is process learning. Use individual examples only when appropriate, consented, anonymized, or required by the situation.

### Turn Coaching Patterns Into System Improvement

If several people need the same coaching, the system likely needs better training, SOPs, tools, quality checks, or priority rules. Do not repeat the same individual coaching indefinitely when the pattern is shared.

Use coaching data to improve onboarding, knowledge management, process design, and metrics.

## Common Traps

- Coaching from a dashboard number without examples, context, or quality evidence.
- Blaming individuals for results driven by routing, tools, staffing, unclear SOPs, or unrealistic targets.
- Giving broad feedback with no specific behavior, practice standard, support, or follow-up date.
- Trying to correct every issue in one conversation.
- Using metrics as pressure rather than as evidence for specific behavior change.
- Avoiding accountability under the name of support, or denying support under the name of accountability.
- Publicly comparing individual performance in ways that create shame, fear, or gaming; punishing people for surfacing mistakes and then wondering why risk is hidden
- Documenting feedback in vague judgmental language instead of factual examples and commitments; repeating the same coaching topic across many people without fixing training, SOP, or process design

## Self-Check

- Is feedback grounded in specific evidence, examples, and relevant operating context?
- Has the agent separated system constraints from controllable behavior?
- Are coaching focus areas limited enough to be acted on?
- Does the coaching define what good looks like in observable operational practice?
- Are support, accountability, owner, timeline, and follow-up clear?
- Are metrics paired with examples and quality evidence rather than used as generic pressure?
- Does the approach protect candor around blockers, errors, and near misses?
- Is documentation factual, appropriate, and aligned with HR or company policy where needed?
- Are individual concerns handled privately unless there is a valid reason otherwise?
- Do repeated coaching patterns feed into training, SOP, tooling, or process improvement?
