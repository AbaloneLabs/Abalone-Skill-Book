# Underperformance Remediation And Support

Underperformance remediation is high-stakes because it affects service, team fairness, and employment outcomes. It must be evidence-based and humane while still protecting the operation. Agents often rush to a performance plan from low metrics or, conversely, avoid the issue by suggesting more coaching without decisions. This skill helps the agent distinguish root causes, provide support, document expectations, and involve the right owners without pretending to give legal or HR advice.

## Core Rules

### Verify The Performance Gap

Define the expected standard and the observed gap using specific evidence. Include quality, productivity, attendance where relevant, documentation, escalation judgment, customer impact, control compliance, teamwork expectations, and prior feedback. Compare against peers only if work mix and context are reasonably comparable.

Do not rely on vague impressions. A fair remediation process needs observable examples and agreed expectations.

### Check System And Context First

Before treating the issue as individual underperformance, review training, onboarding, SOP clarity, tooling, workload, scheduling, assignment complexity, access, manager feedback, health and safety factors, accommodation or protected-leave considerations, and recent process changes. Involve HR or appropriate specialists for sensitive matters.

If the cause is systemic, remediation should address the system. If both system and individual factors exist, separate the actions.

### Clarify The Type Of Gap

Different gaps require different support. A skill gap may need training and practice. A knowledge gap may need clearer SOPs. A judgment gap may need supervised review and decision rules. A motivation or conduct issue may need management action. A workload or health-related issue may require policy-sensitive support.

Do not apply the same generic improvement plan to every gap. The support should match the cause.

### Define A Support Plan With Evidence-Based Milestones

A support plan should state expected behavior or output, quality requirements, support provided, timeline, check-in cadence, evidence to review, and consequences or next steps as appropriate. Keep goals measurable but not narrowly numeric if quality and judgment matter.

Milestones should be realistic. If the employee is expected to improve while tools are broken or training is missing, the plan is not credible.

### Protect Fairness To The Team

Underperformance can burden coworkers through rework, extra coverage, escalations, missed handoffs, or lower trust. A remediation plan should reduce that burden while still providing reasonable support. This may require temporary assignment changes, closer review, or clearer escalation paths.

Do not let support for one person silently become unfair load for others. Track impact.

### Communicate Directly And Respectfully

Feedback should be specific, factual, and focused on work impact. Explain the gap, why it matters, what support is available, what improvement looks like, and what follow-up will occur. Avoid personal labels and unsupported motives.

For sensitive employment matters, coordinate with HR and follow company process. The agent should not invent disciplinary language or legal conclusions.

### Document Appropriately

Record facts, examples, expectations, support, dates, check-ins, employee responses where appropriate, and outcomes. Documentation should be clear enough for continuity and fair enough to withstand review. Avoid emotional, speculative, or medical details unless handled through the proper process.

Documentation is not only for escalation. It helps managers follow through consistently.

### Review Progress And Adjust

At each check-in, compare evidence against milestones. If progress is improving, continue or taper support. If the plan is not working, decide whether the cause was misdiagnosed, support is insufficient, expectations are unclear, or further HR-led steps are needed.

Do not keep extending vague plans with no decision. That is unfair to the employee, manager, and team.

### Convert Patterns Into Operating Fixes

If multiple people struggle with the same task, quality issue, or productivity standard, treat it as a signal about training, process design, assignment, tooling, or metric quality. Individual remediation should not become the default answer to a systemic problem.

Use underperformance reviews to strengthen onboarding, SOPs, coaching, and workload design.

## Common Traps

- Starting remediation from a low metric without checking quality evidence, work mix, and expected standard.
- Treating a training, tool, assignment, or process problem as individual failure.
- Writing a generic improvement plan that does not match the actual gap.
- Setting numeric targets that encourage shortcuts or ignore judgment and quality.
- Providing support without a timeline, evidence, and decision points.
- Avoiding direct feedback until the issue becomes severe.
- Using speculative or personal language instead of observable behavior and impact; letting one person's remediation burden coworkers indefinitely
- Extending plans repeatedly without deciding whether progress is sufficient; failing to involve HR or proper policy owners for sensitive employment, accommodation, legal, or conduct issues

## Self-Check

- Is the performance gap defined against a clear standard using specific evidence?
- Has context been checked for training, tooling, workload, access, schedule, assignment, manager feedback, and policy-sensitive factors?
- Is the gap type identified as skill, knowledge, judgment, conduct, workload, health or safety, or system design?
- Does the support plan match the cause of the gap?
- Are milestones, support, timeline, check-in cadence, and evidence clear?
- Does the plan protect coworkers from unmanaged rework or extra burden?
- Is communication factual, respectful, and focused on work impact?
- Is documentation appropriate, specific, and free of unsupported personal claims?
- Are HR or other policy owners involved where employment-sensitive issues may apply?
- Do repeated similar gaps feed into training, SOP, tool, assignment, or process improvement?
