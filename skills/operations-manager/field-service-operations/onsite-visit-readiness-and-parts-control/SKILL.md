# Onsite Visit Readiness And Parts Control

An onsite visit can fail before the technician leaves. Missing parts, unclear scope, wrong tools, unconfirmed access, incomplete customer prerequisites, or bad asset data can turn a scheduled visit into an expensive non-productive trip. Agents often focus on booking the appointment and miss whether the visit is actually ready to succeed. This skill helps the agent inspect onsite readiness as a precondition for field work quality.

Use this skill when preparing field service visits, dispatch packets, work orders, parts staging, van stock, customer access, technician briefing, or pre-visit confirmation. The agent should reduce wasted visits without creating excessive administrative burden.

## Core Rules

### Define the job outcome and scope

Before confirming readiness, state what the visit is meant to accomplish: inspect, diagnose, repair, install, replace, maintain, calibrate, train, remove, verify, or collect evidence. Scope should include asset, location, symptom, required result, exclusions, and decision authority.

If the work order says only "fix issue" or "check unit," the technician may arrive unprepared. Clear scope determines parts, tools, skill, time, and customer expectations.

### Verify asset and site information

Field work depends on accurate site address, building access, contact, asset ID, model, serial number, configuration, warranty or entitlement, prior service history, photos, error codes, and environmental conditions. Wrong asset data leads to wrong parts and diagnosis.

If the customer cannot confirm asset details, decide whether a diagnostic visit is appropriate or whether remote evidence is needed first. Do not pretend uncertainty is readiness.

### Control parts before the visit

Parts readiness includes correct part, quantity, revision, compatibility, condition, location, reservation, kitting, lot or serial control, warranty handling, and return path for removed parts. A part showing in stock is not enough if it is not reserved, staged, or accessible to the technician.

For high-failure or repeat jobs, review whether van stock or pre-positioning should change. But avoid overloading field inventory with rarely used parts that create loss, obsolescence, or reconciliation problems.

### Confirm tools, equipment, and permissions

Some jobs require specialty tools, test equipment, personal protective equipment, lifting equipment, software access, calibration devices, keys, badges, permits, work authorization, or customer escorts. These prerequisites should be checked before dispatch.

If a technician needs system access or temporary permission, confirm it is approved and active. Field time should not be spent waiting for preventable access setup.

### Check customer prerequisites

Customers may need to provide site access, adult presence, shutdown window, cleared workspace, power, network access, parking, safety briefing, data backup, equipment availability, or approval for downtime. Confirm prerequisites in plain language before the visit.

Do not assume the customer understands technical prerequisites. Tell them what must be ready and what happens if it is not.

### Prepare the technician briefing

A good briefing includes issue summary, customer context, asset details, prior steps, parts and tools, safety notes, access instructions, success criteria, escalation contact, documentation requirement, and customer communication guidance. It should be concise enough to use in the field.

Avoid making technicians parse long ticket histories while traveling or standing onsite. If the work is complex, provide a structured job packet.

### Plan for removed parts and evidence

Repairs and replacements often require return material authorization, warranty part return, photos, measurements, customer signoff, disposal, chain-of-custody, or failure analysis. Plan this before the visit so evidence is captured correctly.

If removed parts are lost or undocumented, warranty recovery, quality learning, and customer disputes become harder.

### Use failed readiness as process feedback

Track reasons for no-access visits, missing parts, wrong tools, wrong technician, incomplete scope, customer not ready, or bad asset data. Repeated readiness failures reveal intake, scheduling, inventory, knowledge, or customer communication gaps.

Do not treat failed visits as isolated technician problems when the pre-visit process set them up to fail.

## Common Traps

- Scheduling a visit before the job outcome and scope are clear.
- Trusting inventory availability without reservation, staging, compatibility, or technician access.
- Discovering customer prerequisites only after the technician arrives.
- Sending technicians without site access, permits, software permissions, or specialty tools.
- Making the field team read a messy ticket history instead of providing a usable briefing.
- Forgetting removed-part return, warranty evidence, photos, or customer signoff requirements.
- Treating no-access or missing-part visits as normal field variance.
- Expanding van stock without considering reconciliation, loss, obsolescence, and carrying cost.

## Self-Check

- Is the onsite visit outcome and scope specific enough to determine readiness?
- Are site address, contact, access instructions, asset ID, model, serial, and history verified?
- Are required parts compatible, reserved, staged, and accessible?
- Are specialty tools, PPE, equipment, software access, keys, permits, and permissions confirmed?
- Are customer prerequisites communicated in plain language and acknowledged?
- Does the technician briefing include issue, context, assets, parts, tools, safety, success criteria, and escalation path?
- Are removed-part, warranty, disposal, evidence, and signoff requirements planned?
- Are readiness failures tracked by root cause?
- Would the visit still be dispatched if the readiness checklist were audited after a wasted trip?
- Is the process lean enough for routine visits but strict enough for high-risk work?
