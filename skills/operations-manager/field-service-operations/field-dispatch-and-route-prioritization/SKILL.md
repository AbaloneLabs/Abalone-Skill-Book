# Field Dispatch And Route Prioritization

Field dispatch turns work orders into real-world movement. It is not just assigning the closest technician or filling the schedule. Dispatch decisions affect safety, customer appointments, travel cost, first-time fix, overtime, SLA risk, technician fatigue, parts availability, and emergency response. Agents often optimize for utilization or distance while missing skill fit, access constraints, service priority, and the operational consequences of reshuffling a route. This skill helps the agent design and inspect dispatch decisions as a service reliability system.

Use this skill when planning technician routes, scheduling field visits, prioritizing field queues, handling emergency dispatch, balancing appointment windows, or reviewing route performance. The agent should make dispatch tradeoffs explicit and avoid hiding risk in a tidy-looking schedule.

## Core Rules

### Start with service promise and risk level

Identify the promise or constraint attached to each work order: emergency response, regulatory deadline, customer appointment window, asset downtime, safety risk, contractual SLA, repeat visit, vulnerable customer, parts availability, or revenue impact. Dispatch priority should reflect more than queue age.

Do not treat every job as interchangeable. A short nearby job may be tempting to fit into the route, but a high-risk outage or safety issue may need priority even if it disrupts efficiency.

### Match technician capability to the job

Route assignment must account for skill, certification, equipment, product knowledge, customer site rules, language needs, security clearance, union or labor constraints, and escalation authority. A nearby technician who cannot complete the job creates repeat visits and customer frustration.

Skill matching should be visible in the dispatch decision. If a lower-fit technician is assigned due to urgency, document the risk and support plan.

### Validate job readiness before dispatch

Before sending someone, confirm customer availability, site access, safety requirements, parts, tools, permits, documentation, asset information, work scope, and prerequisites. A field visit that cannot start is wasted capacity and customer time.

Readiness checks should happen early enough to recover. Same-day discovery that parts are missing or access is unavailable creates route chaos.

### Route for reality, not map optimism

Travel time depends on traffic, parking, building access, rural distance, security check-in, elevator wait, weather, loading needs, and technician breaks. Route plans that assume perfect conditions create late arrivals and overtime.

Use buffers deliberately. Too much buffer reduces capacity; too little buffer creates cascading misses. The right buffer depends on area, work type, appointment strictness, and historical variance.

### Manage emergency insertions transparently

Emergency work can require rerouting, but every insertion has consequences. Identify which appointments move, which customers need updates, which SLAs are at risk, and whether overtime or backup resources are needed. Do not quietly reshuffle without communication.

Emergency priority criteria should be defined in advance: safety, critical asset, contractual severity, public impact, regulatory issue, or customer tier. Otherwise, escalation pressure will distort the route.

### Balance utilization with service quality

High technician utilization can look efficient while increasing late arrivals, rushed work, repeat visits, safety shortcuts, and burnout. Pair utilization with first-time fix, on-time arrival, repeat visit rate, customer satisfaction, overtime, travel ratio, and safety incidents.

If routes are always fully packed, the operation has no recovery capacity. Field work needs some slack for variability.

### Coordinate customer communication

Dispatch decisions affect customer expectations. If a technician is delayed, appointment is moved, or emergency changes the route, communicate early with accurate windows and options. Do not let the customer discover delay only when no one arrives.

Customer updates should reflect confirmed route reality, not hopeful dispatch estimates. If the schedule is uncertain, say what is known and when the next update will come.

### Learn from route exceptions

Track missed appointments, access failures, no-parts visits, incorrect skill assignment, late arrivals, overtime, safety stops, and emergency reroutes. Review by dispatcher, technician, geography, job type, customer segment, and time of day.

Repeated route failures usually indicate planning assumptions, readiness checks, territory design, staffing, parts strategy, or customer communication need redesign.

## Common Traps

- Assigning the closest technician without checking skill, certification, tools, or site requirements.
- Filling routes to maximum capacity and leaving no buffer for real-world variance.
- Treating emergency insertions as free changes with no customer or SLA consequences.
- Dispatching before parts, access, scope, and customer availability are confirmed.
- Measuring field productivity only through utilization or jobs per day.
- Letting vocal escalations override defined priority criteria.
- Giving customers optimistic arrival windows unsupported by route reality.
- Reviewing missed visits as individual failures instead of route design and readiness problems.

## Self-Check

- Are service promises, risk levels, and appointment constraints visible before prioritizing?
- Is technician skill, certification, equipment, clearance, and site fit checked?
- Are customer availability, access, parts, tools, permits, and scope ready before dispatch?
- Does route planning account for realistic travel, site access, breaks, weather, and variance?
- Are emergency insertions governed by defined criteria and customer communication?
- Are utilization metrics balanced with on-time arrival, first-time fix, repeat visits, overtime, safety, and customer experience?
- Is there enough recovery capacity for predictable field variability?
- Are schedule changes communicated early and accurately?
- Are route exceptions analyzed by pattern, not only individual case?
- Would the dispatch decision still make sense if reviewed after a missed appointment or safety event?
