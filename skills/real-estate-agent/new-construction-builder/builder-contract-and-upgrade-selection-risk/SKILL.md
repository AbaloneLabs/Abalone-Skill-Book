# Builder Contract And Upgrade Selection Risk

New construction feels simpler because the home is new, but the contract is often more builder-controlled than a resale contract. Buyers may rely on model-home finishes, verbal sales promises, upgrade allowances, delivery estimates, and warranty language without understanding what is actually binding. Agents representing buyers in builder transactions need to protect expectations, documentation, and advisory boundaries. This skill helps the agent notice where new construction risk hides before the buyer commits deposits and design choices.

## Core Rules

### Treat Builder Forms As Negotiation And Risk Documents

Builder contracts may limit remedies, change deadlines, allow substitutions, restrict inspections, define completion broadly, control dispute resolution, and handle delays differently from standard resale forms. The agent should not interpret legal terms, but should prompt the buyer to review contract rights with qualified counsel where needed.

Do not assume familiar resale protections exist in a builder contract.

### Document What Is Included

Model homes, renderings, brochures, sales-center displays, and online images may include upgrades, staging, optional elevations, landscaping, appliances, or lot features not included in the base price. The buyer should know exactly what is standard, optional, credited, or excluded.

Verbal promises should be converted into written contract documents or builder-approved addenda.

### Track Deposits And Nonrefundable Commitments

New construction may require earnest money, design deposits, lot premiums, upgrade deposits, and change-order payments. Some may become nonrefundable at specific milestones. Buyers should understand when money becomes at risk.

Deposit decisions should be matched to financing, inspection, appraisal, and personal timeline confidence.

### Evaluate Upgrade Value And Financing Effects

Design-center upgrades can improve enjoyment but may not appraise dollar-for-dollar. They can also change cash requirements, loan amount, taxes, insurance, and future resale. Some upgrades are easier to complete after closing; others are difficult or warranty-sensitive.

Encourage buyers to separate lifestyle value from resale or appraisal value.

### Manage Change Orders Strictly

Change orders should be written, priced, dated, and tied to completion expectations. Late changes can affect build schedule, cost, financing, and warranty. Casual design changes can become expensive disputes.

Do not rely on informal messages or sales-center assurances for material changes.

### Understand Lot And Site Premiums

Lot premiums may reflect view, size, cul-de-sac, orientation, slope, privacy, drainage, future development, road exposure, or open space. Some premiums are subjective and may not be fully supported by appraisal or resale.

Buyers should understand why the lot costs more and what risks remain around future surroundings.

### Review Builder Warranty Limits

Builder warranties may cover some defects for limited periods and exclude maintenance, cosmetic issues, appliances, manufacturer items, settlement, landscaping, or owner modifications. Warranty claim procedures and deadlines matter.

A warranty is not a substitute for inspection, walkthrough discipline, or documentation.

### Coordinate Lender And Appraisal Timing

Base price, upgrades, incentives, completion date, rate lock, appraised value, and final certificate of occupancy can affect financing. If the buyer uses the builder's lender, terms and incentives should still be compared carefully.

The contract and financing path should fit the construction timeline, not only the sales goal.

### Preserve Buyer Representation

Builder sales representatives work for the builder. The buyer's agent should clarify representation, register the buyer as required, attend key meetings where allowed, and keep client questions focused on written commitments.

Client advocacy matters even when the builder process appears standardized.

### Treat Builder Paper As A Different Contract Universe

Builder contracts may allocate risk differently from local resale forms. They can include unilateral change rights, construction tolerances, escalation clauses, arbitration, limited remedies, extension rights, material substitutions, HOA creation, design-center deadlines, and warranty limitations. The agent should encourage legal review where appropriate and should not assume familiar resale protections apply.

Upgrade decisions should be tied to appraisal, financing, resale, timeline, and change-order risk. A buyer may love options that increase cash due, fail to appraise, delay completion, or have little resale value. The agent's role is to help the buyer ask the right questions before selection deadlines, not to act as designer or legal advisor.

## Common Traps

- Assuming builder contracts preserve the same protections as resale forms.
- Discussing upgrades only as taste choices rather than financing, appraisal, delay, and resale risks.

- Assuming builder contracts include normal resale protections.
- Letting model-home features become buyer expectations without written inclusion.
- Missing when deposits or upgrade payments become nonrefundable.
- Treating upgrades as guaranteed appraisal value.
- Relying on verbal sales promises or text messages for material terms.
- Ignoring lot premium assumptions and future development risk.
- Treating builder warranty as proof that inspection is unnecessary.
- Missing financing effects of upgrades, incentives, and construction delays.
- Letting the builder's sales representative define the buyer's representation.
- Failing to document change orders and selection deadlines.
- Underestimating how limited buyer remedies may be under builder forms.

## Self-Check

- Have builder-specific contract rights, remedies, substitutions, delays, and warranty limits been flagged for review?
- Are upgrades evaluated for appraisal, financing, timeline, cash, and resale impact?

- Has the buyer been warned that builder forms may differ from resale protections?
- Are included features, upgrades, exclusions, allowances, and lot premiums written clearly?
- Are deposits and nonrefundable milestones understood?
- Are upgrade choices evaluated for cash, loan, appraisal, tax, insurance, and resale effects?
- Are change orders written and priced before reliance?
- Are lot assumptions and future surroundings reviewed?
- Are warranty limits and claim procedures understood?
- Are lender, appraisal, rate-lock, and completion timing aligned?
- Is buyer representation clear despite builder-controlled process?
- Would the buyer know what is actually promised versus merely displayed or discussed?
