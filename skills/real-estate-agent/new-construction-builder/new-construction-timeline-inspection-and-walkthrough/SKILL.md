# New Construction Timeline Inspection And Walkthrough

New construction timing is uncertain. Weather, permits, labor, materials, inspections, utilities, lender conditions, and municipal approvals can shift completion dates. Buyers may also assume a new home does not need inspection or that a blue-tape walkthrough catches everything. Agents should help clients plan for timeline uncertainty, inspection opportunities, punch-list documentation, and handoff risk. This skill supports disciplined new construction due diligence from contract to post-closing warranty.

## Core Rules

### Treat Completion Dates As Risk Estimates

Builder timelines may be approximate unless the contract says otherwise. Delays can affect rate locks, leases, sale contingencies, moving plans, school starts, storage, temporary housing, and financing. The buyer should understand what remedies exist for delay.

Do not promise a move-in date based on sales-office optimism.

### Track Construction Milestones

Milestones may include permit issuance, foundation, framing, rough mechanicals, drywall, interior finishes, utility connections, municipal inspections, certificate of occupancy, appraisal, orientation, and closing. Knowing the stage helps assess whether the claimed timeline is realistic.

Ask the builder for milestone updates rather than vague progress language.

### Encourage Independent Inspections Where Allowed

New does not mean defect-free. Buyers may consider pre-drywall, final, and warranty-period inspections. Builder contracts and site safety rules may control access and timing.

Agents should not inspect as experts, but should help clients schedule qualified inspections within allowed windows.

### Protect Pre-Drywall Opportunities

Pre-drywall inspection can reveal framing, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, insulation, moisture, or structural concerns before walls conceal them. Missing this window may limit later visibility.

If the buyer wants this inspection, schedule proactively because construction can move quickly.

### Manage Blue-Tape And Punch Lists

Cosmetic walkthroughs are useful but not the same as technical inspection. Punch-list items should be written, photographed, assigned, and tracked with completion expectations. Some items may be completed after closing; others should be resolved before closing.

Distinguish cosmetic touch-ups from functional, safety, or completion issues.

### Confirm Certificate And Utility Readiness

Closing may depend on certificate of occupancy, final permits, utility meters, water, sewer, gas, electricity, driveway, landscaping, mailbox, and municipal approvals. A home can look nearly complete but lack approvals or services.

Ask whether approvals and utilities are complete before relying on closing date.

### Coordinate Appraisal And Final Loan Conditions

The lender may need completion evidence, final inspection, certificate of occupancy, updated appraisal, insurance, and final cost figures. Construction delays can affect rate locks and closing disclosures.

Financing status should be tracked alongside build status.

### Prepare For Post-Closing Warranty Workflow

Many builders have 30-day, 60-day, 11-month, or one-year warranty processes. Buyers should know how to submit claims, document defects, track responses, and distinguish warranty items from maintenance.

Post-closing follow-up can protect client satisfaction and reduce missed deadlines.

### Document Access And Builder Responses

Site access, inspection requests, punch-list items, completion promises, and warranty instructions should be documented. If the builder denies access or delays repairs, the client may need broker or legal guidance.

Written records are essential because construction details change quickly.

### Build Milestone Verification Into The Calendar

New construction timelines depend on permits, weather, labor, materials, utility hookups, inspections, certificate of occupancy, lender draw or closing conditions, and builder scheduling. The agent should help the buyer track milestones and decision points rather than relying on a projected completion month.

Inspection opportunities may be limited or need advance scheduling: pre-drywall, foundation, framing, final walkthrough, blue tape, and warranty walk. Buyers should understand what each stage can reveal and what may be inaccessible later. A missed inspection stage can remove leverage and make defects harder to document.

## Common Traps

- Treating the builder's estimated completion date as a reliable closing plan without milestone checks.
- Waiting until final walkthrough to inspect issues that should have been seen before drywall or completion.

- Treating builder delivery dates as guaranteed move-in dates.
- Planning lease termination or sale closing with no delay cushion.
- Assuming new homes do not need independent inspection.
- Missing the pre-drywall inspection window.
- Confusing blue-tape walkthrough with full technical due diligence.
- Letting punch-list items remain vague or undocumented.
- Ignoring certificate of occupancy, utility, or municipal approval status.
- Forgetting rate-lock and final loan-condition effects of construction delays.
- Closing with unresolved functional defects without understanding remedies.
- Missing warranty claim deadlines after move-in.
- Relying on verbal builder updates without written records.

## Self-Check

- Are construction, utility, permit, occupancy, lender, and inspection milestones tracked with owners and dates?
- Has the buyer been told which inspection opportunities may disappear if missed?

- Are completion dates treated as estimates unless contractually guaranteed?
- Are build milestones tracked in enough detail to assess timing risk?
- Has the buyer considered independent inspections within allowed windows?
- Is the pre-drywall inspection opportunity protected if desired?
- Are punch-list items written, photographed, prioritized, and tracked?
- Are certificate of occupancy, permits, utilities, and municipal approvals confirmed?
- Are appraisal, rate-lock, disclosure, and final loan conditions aligned with completion?
- Does the buyer understand post-closing warranty procedures and deadlines?
- Are builder access decisions and repair responses documented?
- Would the buyer have a realistic plan if completion slips by weeks or months?
