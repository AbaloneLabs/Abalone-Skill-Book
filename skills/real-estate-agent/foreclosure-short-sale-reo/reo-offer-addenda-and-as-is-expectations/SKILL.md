# REO Offer Addenda And As Is Expectations

REO transactions often use institutional seller addenda that override ordinary expectations. The seller may have limited property knowledge, strict timelines, per-diem penalties, as-is terms, repair refusal, asset-manager approvals, and special title or closing processes. Agents often treat REO as a normal resale with a bank seller, but the process is more rigid and less personal. This skill helps the agent prepare clients for bank-owned property risk and institutional procedure.

## Core Rules

### Read Seller Addenda Carefully

REO addenda may change inspection rights, financing timelines, closing fees, title choice, possession, personal property, default remedies, per-diem charges, assignment limits, and dispute terms. They may override parts of the standard contract.

The agent should not interpret legal effects, but must prompt client and counsel review before deadlines.

### Set As-Is Expectations

REO sellers often make few or no repairs and may provide limited disclosures. As-is does not mean the buyer should skip due diligence; it means the buyer must price and inspect with limited seller cooperation.

Clients should understand they may inherit unknown condition risk.

### Confirm Utility And Inspection Access

Utilities may be off, winterized, damaged, or unavailable. Some sellers restrict activation, water testing, invasive inspection, or access times. Inspection quality may be limited.

Buyer decision timelines should reflect what can and cannot be tested.

### Account For Institutional Timing

Asset managers, banks, attorneys, and servicers may respond slowly or only through portals. Verbal flexibility is limited. Deadlines may be rigid once addenda are issued.

Prepare clients for process friction and slower communication.

### Watch Title And Deed Issues

Foreclosure deeds, redemption periods, eviction history, HOA liens, municipal liens, tax issues, and title curative work can affect closing. REO title may be clean enough to insure, but delays can occur.

Do not assume bank ownership means title is simple.

### Clarify Personal Property And Debris

REO properties may contain trash, appliances, fixtures, vehicles, or abandoned belongings. The seller may not remove items unless specifically agreed. Buyers should understand what stays and what cleanup costs may follow.

Personal property assumptions should be written.

### Evaluate Financing And Appraisal Fit

Condition may prevent certain loan types. Appraisers may require repairs the REO seller refuses to perform. Renovation loans or cash may be more realistic for some properties.

Offer strategy should match property condition and seller repair posture.

### Monitor Per-Diem And Extension Terms

Some REO addenda impose charges if closing is delayed, even when delay relates to buyer financing or missed documents. Extensions may require written asset-manager approval.

Clients should understand who bears delay cost and what triggers it.

### Keep Communication Formal And Documented

Institutional sellers may not honor informal statements outside the portal or written addenda. Track uploads, approvals, counteroffers, addenda, deadlines, and receipts.

Written process discipline protects the client in a rigid transaction.

### Review Institutional Addenda As Deal Terms, Not Boilerplate

REO sellers often use addenda that change inspection rights, seller disclosures, title choices, repair obligations, per diem penalties, closing extensions, utilities, occupancy, personal property, and cancellation remedies. The agent should make sure the client reads these terms and seeks legal advice where needed before assuming the standard contract controls.

"As-is" does not eliminate every obligation or risk allocation question. It may still matter whether utilities can be activated, whether lender-required repairs are allowed, whether credits are permitted, whether personal property remains, and what happens if title or occupancy cannot be delivered on time. The agent should translate addendum effects into practical buyer decisions.

## Common Traps

- Treating REO addenda as harmless boilerplate rather than terms that may override the standard contract.
- Assuming "as-is" answers questions about utilities, lender repairs, title, occupancy, and extensions.

- Assuming standard contract terms control over REO addenda.
- Treating as-is as a reason to skip inspection.
- Forgetting utilities may not be available for testing.
- Expecting personal negotiation flexibility from an asset manager.
- Assuming bank-owned title is automatically clean and fast.
- Ignoring debris, personal property, and cleanup obligations.
- Writing financed offers on properties that cannot meet loan conditions.
- Missing per-diem charges or strict extension terms.
- Relying on verbal statements outside the seller's written process.
- Failing to route legal addendum questions to counsel or broker.
- Underestimating buyer frustration with slow institutional communication.

## Self-Check

- Have REO addenda been reviewed for changes to inspection, title, repairs, extensions, penalties, and cancellation rights?
- Are as-is expectations connected to financing, utilities, occupancy, and repair feasibility?

- Are REO addenda reviewed for changes to standard contract expectations?
- Does the buyer understand as-is condition and limited disclosure?
- Are utility and inspection-access limits known before contingency decisions?
- Are institutional response times and portal procedures built into expectations?
- Are title, redemption, lien, and deed issues monitored?
- Are personal property and debris terms written clearly?
- Does buyer financing fit the property's condition and seller repair policy?
- Are per-diem and extension consequences understood?
- Is communication documented through required channels?
- Would the buyer understand how REO process differs from ordinary resale?
