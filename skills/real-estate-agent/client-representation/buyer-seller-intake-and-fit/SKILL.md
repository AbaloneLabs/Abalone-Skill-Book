# Buyer Seller Intake And Fit

Real estate intake is not just collecting bedrooms, price range, or desired closing date. It determines whether the client is ready, whether the agent can competently represent them, what risks are already visible, and what expectations must be reset before marketing, showings, pricing, offers, or contracts begin. Agents often rush into property search or listing advice before identifying decision authority, financing reality, agency relationship, legal constraints, and deal-breakers. This skill helps the agent convert a loose buyer or seller request into a clear representation brief.

## Core Rules

### Clarify The Transaction Type And Role

Start by identifying whether the client is buying, selling, leasing, investing, relocating, exchanging, downsizing, divorcing, handling an estate, representing a business, or acting for another party. The same address can involve different duties depending on whether the agent represents a buyer, seller, landlord, tenant, investor, trustee, executor, entity, or unrepresented customer.

Do not assume the person speaking has authority to act. Ask who owns the property, who will sign, who must approve price or terms, who will occupy, and whether there are co-owners, spouses, lenders, attorneys, guardians, corporate officers, or estate representatives involved. A pleasant intake call can become a stalled transaction if decision rights were never clarified.

### Separate Preferences From Constraints

Preferences are negotiable; constraints are not. A buyer may prefer a large yard but must stay within a school district, accessibility need, commute limit, pet policy, loan product, or move date. A seller may prefer a high price but must resolve a lien, coordinate replacement housing, satisfy court approval, or close before a tax or relocation deadline.

Ask which requirements are must-haves, strong preferences, and tradeable features. Then test the list against market reality. If the budget and requirements do not match available inventory, surface that early and frame the tradeoff: location, condition, size, timing, financing, concessions, or price.

### Establish Financial Readiness Without Overstepping

For buyers, confirm price range, available cash, financing status, preapproval, loan type, down payment, closing cost capacity, gift funds, sale contingency, and comfort with monthly payment. For sellers, discuss mortgage payoff, liens, expected net proceeds, repairs, carrying costs, staging, taxes, commission, and replacement housing. The agent should not provide tax, legal, or lending advice beyond competence, but must know when those issues affect transaction feasibility.

Financial readiness is not only whether the client can afford the property. It includes whether funds can be documented, whether the offer will be credible, whether appraisal risk can be handled, and whether the client understands the cash required at each point in the transaction.

### Identify Motivation And Failure Conditions

Ask why the client is moving, what success looks like, and what would make the transaction fail. A buyer may care more about certainty, privacy, renovation potential, investment yield, or speed than about the lowest price. A seller may need discretion, minimal disruption, leaseback, quick close, or maximum proceeds. Motivation affects marketing, negotiation, showing strategy, and contingency tolerance.

Be careful with sensitive reasons such as divorce, job loss, medical needs, financial distress, safety concerns, immigration status, disability, family conflict, or protected-class identity. Capture the operational implication without exposing private information unnecessarily.

### Check Representation Fit And Competence

Before accepting the work, decide whether the request fits the agent's license, market knowledge, brokerage policy, availability, language ability, property type competence, conflict position, and risk tolerance. A residential agent should be cautious about complex commercial leases, development land, farms, distressed sales, short sales, probate, or property management if they lack support.

Fit also includes client conduct. Watch for requests to discriminate, hide material facts, misstate income, evade disclosure, pressure inspectors, bypass required paperwork, or communicate only verbally on important terms. Set boundaries before the relationship becomes dependent.

### Convert Intake Into A Written Brief

After intake, produce a concise working brief: client role, agency status, goals, decision makers, budget, property criteria, location, timeline, financing or sale status, known risks, required advisors, next steps, and assumptions. This brief prevents the agent from relying on memory and gives the client a chance to correct misunderstandings.

The brief should be specific enough to guide action but not filled with private details that do not need to be widely shared. Treat it as a control document for search, listing preparation, market analysis, and communication.

### Set Expectations About Process And Responsibility

Explain what the agent will do, what the client must do, what outside professionals may need to handle, how quickly decisions are needed, how offers and disclosures work, and how availability or pricing can change. Real estate clients often expect the agent to solve lending, tax, legal, repair, appraisal, insurance, or title issues. The agent should coordinate and flag issues, not pretend to be every professional.

Expectation-setting at intake reduces later conflict. If a client wants an impossible combination of high price, no prep, no showings, no disclosure risk, and immediate closing, say so before investing work.

## Common Traps

- Treating intake as a preference checklist instead of a readiness and risk assessment.
- Assuming the person requesting help has authority to buy, sell, lease, or sign.
- Accepting a transaction outside the agent's competence because the lead looks valuable.
- Letting clients describe discriminatory preferences without redirecting to lawful property criteria.
- Hiding bad market fit instead of explaining price, location, condition, and timeline tradeoffs.
- Collecting sensitive personal facts that are not needed for representation or that should not be shared.
- Starting showings or listing work before agency status, compensation, and representation expectations are clear; confusing buyer affordability with offer strength; cash-to-close, appraisal gap, and contingency capacity matter
- Failing to document assumptions, which makes later changes look like agent error; ignoring early warning signs that the client wants to conceal facts, pressure vendors, or avoid written records

## Self-Check

- Is the client role, transaction type, and representation status clear?
- Are decision makers, signers, owners, or approval parties identified?
- Are must-haves separated from preferences and tested against market reality?
- Is financial readiness understood without giving unauthorized lending, tax, or legal advice?
- Are motivation, urgency, privacy concerns, and failure conditions captured at an operational level?
- Has the agent checked competence, license, brokerage policy, conflicts, and risk fit before proceeding?
- Are discriminatory, misleading, or improper client requests redirected or declined?
- Is there a written working brief with goals, constraints, assumptions, risks, and next steps?
- Does the client understand what the agent will handle and what outside professionals must handle?
- Would another agent on the team be able to continue from the intake record without guessing?
