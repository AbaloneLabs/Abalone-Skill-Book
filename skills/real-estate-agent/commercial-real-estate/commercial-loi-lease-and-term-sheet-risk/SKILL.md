# Commercial LOI Lease And Term Sheet Risk

Commercial letters of intent and term sheets can feel nonbinding, but they frame the economics and leverage of the deal. A vague or poorly reviewed LOI may lock parties into expectations around rent, term, improvements, guarantees, expenses, exclusivity, or delivery condition before counsel is engaged. Agents often focus on rent and term while missing operating expense pass-throughs, buildout obligations, and exit restrictions. This skill helps the agent surface commercial term risk before documents harden.

## Core Rules

### Treat The LOI As A Serious Negotiation Document

Even when nonbinding, an LOI sets expectations and may include binding confidentiality, exclusivity, deposits, or expense provisions. It also shapes attorney drafting. Review the proposed terms with the client and recommend legal counsel before submission or signature where appropriate.

Do not tell clients an LOI "doesn't matter." Business people often rely on it as the deal blueprint.

### Define The Economic Structure Fully

Commercial economics may include base rent, escalations, free rent, percentage rent, NNN charges, CAM, taxes, insurance, utilities, janitorial, maintenance, management fees, parking, signage fees, security deposit, improvement allowance, rent commencement, and restoration obligations. Purchase term sheets may include price, deposits, due diligence, financing, environmental, closing costs, and prorations.

A low base rent can become expensive when pass-throughs and buildout costs are included. Compare total economics over the term.

### Clarify Premises And Delivery Condition

Term sheets should specify suite, square footage source, measurement standard, parking, storage, common areas, signage rights, loading, utilities, condition at delivery, code compliance, existing improvements, landlord work, tenant work, and who obtains permits. Ambiguity creates disputes before opening.

If the client needs a specific use, delivery condition should support that use or identify approvals still required.

### Examine Tenant Improvement And Buildout Risk

Tenant improvement allowances, landlord work, tenant work, permits, contractors, construction timelines, change orders, cost overruns, lien waivers, rent commencement, and opening deadlines are central commercial risks. A generous allowance may not cover actual buildout. A delayed permit can consume free rent.

Encourage contractor, architect, and legal review before relying on buildout assumptions. Do not estimate buildout as fact.

### Watch Guarantees And Security

Personal guarantees, good-guy guarantees, letters of credit, deposits, burn-down provisions, parent guarantees, and financial covenants can create major exposure. Clients may focus on rent and miss the personal risk of default.

The agent should flag guarantee terms and route legal or financial implications to counsel and advisors. Do not minimize guarantee exposure to keep the deal moving.

### Evaluate Flexibility And Exit Rights

Assignment, sublease, expansion, contraction, renewal, termination, relocation, exclusivity, co-tenancy, use clauses, operating hours, signage, radius restrictions, and transfer rights affect business flexibility. The client may need room to grow, sell the business, franchise, bring partners, or exit.

A space that works on day one can become a trap if exit and change rights are too narrow.

### Identify Operating Expense And Maintenance Exposure

NNN, CAM, controllable expenses, capital expenditures, HVAC maintenance, roof, structure, parking lot, common areas, management fees, taxes, insurance, audit rights, caps, and exclusions can materially affect cost. Ask what historical expenses are, what can increase, and what the tenant can audit.

Do not let "standard NNN" substitute for understanding who pays for what.

### Preserve Attorney Review And Final Document Discipline

The agent can help negotiate business terms but should not draft legal lease language beyond forms and role. Ensure the LOI states attorney review where appropriate and that final lease terms match the agreed business points. Track changes between LOI and lease.

Commercial leases are long-term legal instruments. Specialist review is usually worth the delay.

### Mark Open Issues Explicitly

If a term is not resolved, label it as open instead of letting silence imply agreement. Open issues may include HVAC replacement, exclusivity radius, signage size, delivery condition, landlord work, after-hours HVAC, audit rights, restoration, assignment approval, or guaranty release.

An LOI with hidden open issues creates false momentum. The client should know which business points are settled and which still require negotiation or counsel review.

## Common Traps

- Saying a nonbinding LOI is harmless.
- Comparing deals by base rent only.
- Leaving premises, measurement, parking, signage, or delivery condition vague.
- Underestimating buildout cost, permit time, and rent commencement risk.
- Ignoring personal guarantee and security exposure.
- Missing assignment, sublease, renewal, and exit restrictions; treating CAM, NNN, maintenance, and capital expenses as boilerplate
- Assuming landlord work will satisfy the client's use without verification; letting final lease language drift from the LOI unnoticed
- Providing legal interpretation instead of recommending counsel; treating silence on an important term as agreement

## Self-Check

- Is the LOI treated as the deal blueprint even if nonbinding?
- Are total economics clear over the full term?
- Are premises, square footage, parking, signage, utilities, and delivery condition defined?
- Are tenant improvement, buildout, permits, and rent commencement risks reviewed?
- Are guarantees, deposits, and security instruments flagged for advisor review?
- Are assignment, sublease, renewal, expansion, termination, and exclusivity issues considered?
- Are NNN, CAM, taxes, insurance, maintenance, and audit rights understood?
- Is attorney review preserved for legal terms?
- Does the final lease match the negotiated business terms?
- Would the client understand both the business opportunity and the long-term obligations?; are unresolved business terms clearly marked as open issues?
