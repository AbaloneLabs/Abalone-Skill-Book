# Commercial Client Needs And Property Search

Commercial real estate search is not just finding square footage. The property must support a business model, customer flow, employees, equipment, zoning, parking, loading, signage, utilities, budget, lease obligations, and future growth. Agents who treat commercial search like residential preference matching can miss operational constraints that make a space unusable. This skill helps the agent convert business needs into property criteria and search discipline.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Business Use First

Ask what the client will actually do in the space: retail, office, medical, restaurant, warehouse, light industrial, flex, showroom, childcare, fitness, lab, manufacturing, storage, professional service, or mixed use. The use determines zoning, layout, access, utilities, ventilation, parking, customer traffic, loading, signage, licensing, and improvement needs.

Do not begin with square footage alone. Two businesses needing 3,000 square feet may have completely different property requirements.

### Define Operational Requirements

Capture ceiling height, column spacing, loading doors, power, plumbing, grease trap, HVAC, ventilation, internet, security, floor load, ADA access, restrooms, exam rooms, kitchen, storage, parking ratio, truck access, customer visibility, signage, delivery routes, employee commute, and proximity to suppliers or customers. For office clients, consider hybrid work, meeting rooms, privacy, reception, and growth.

Separate must-have operations from preferences. A beautiful space that cannot support equipment, permits, or customer flow is a bad fit.

### Check Use Permissions Early

Commercial property use can be limited by zoning, conditional use permits, certificate of occupancy, building code, fire code, health department rules, liquor license rules, HOA or condo restrictions, exclusive-use clauses, and landlord restrictions. Agents should not promise approval.

Identify use risks early and route to municipal, legal, architect, contractor, or licensing review. A lease signed before use verification can be costly.

### Build A Full Occupancy Cost View

Commercial cost may include base rent, NNN or CAM charges, taxes, insurance, utilities, janitorial, maintenance, management fees, percentage rent, security deposit, tenant improvements, permits, furniture, signage, moving, technology, parking, and restoration obligations. Purchase costs may include financing, due diligence, environmental review, reserves, and operating expenses.

Clients often compare base rent only. The agent should frame total occupancy cost and cash timing.

### Align Term With Business Risk

Lease or purchase commitments should match business stage, capital, growth expectations, location dependency, buildout investment, and exit options. A startup may need flexibility; a restaurant investing heavily in buildout may need enough term and renewal rights to recover investment.

Discuss expansion, contraction, assignment, sublease, renewal, termination, relocation rights, and personal guaranty exposure. Route legal implications to counsel.

### Evaluate Location By Customer And Workforce Reality

Commercial location depends on customer demographics, foot traffic, visibility, access, parking, transit, delivery, competition, co-tenancy, anchors, neighborhood development, employee commute, and brand fit. Avoid unsupported claims about demographics or future growth. Use data sources and client business judgment.

A cheap space in the wrong location can be more expensive than a higher-rent space that supports revenue.

### Plan Tours Around Decision Evidence

Commercial tours should gather operational evidence: measurements, loading, utilities, condition, restroom count, signage, parking, neighboring uses, access, noise, HVAC, prior buildout, code concerns, and landlord responsiveness. Bring contractors, architects, or operations leads when needed.

Do not rely on memory after tours. Commercial differences are complex and need structured comparison.

### Know When The Deal Exceeds Competence

Complex leases, investment sales, development sites, environmental risk, franchise requirements, medical or restaurant buildouts, industrial uses, or multi-tenant properties may require specialists. If the agent lacks commercial competence, bring in a qualified commercial broker or refer the client.

Commercial mistakes can damage a client's business, not just housing plan.

### Identify The Business Decision Team

Commercial clients often have several decision makers: owner, CFO, operations lead, franchise representative, attorney, lender, architect, contractor, facilities manager, or investor partner. A search can stall if the touring contact likes a space but the operations or finance owner later rejects it.

Ask who must approve site, economics, buildout, lease terms, and timing. Bring the right people into tours and term discussions early.

## Common Traps

- Searching by square footage and rent before understanding business use.
- Ignoring zoning, licensing, building code, or certificate-of-occupancy requirements.
- Comparing base rent without CAM, NNN, taxes, utilities, and buildout costs.
- Letting aesthetics override loading, power, parking, ventilation, or customer access.
- Promising a use is allowed without official verification.
- Underestimating tenant improvement cost and timeline; ignoring personal guaranty, assignment, sublease, renewal, and exit issues
- Touring without the client's operations decision maker; applying residential negotiation habits to commercial terms
- Handling a specialized commercial deal outside competence; advancing a space before finance, operations, legal, or buildout decision makers have reviewed it

## Self-Check

- Is the client's actual business use understood?
- Are operational requirements separated from preferences?
- Are zoning, use, license, and occupancy approvals flagged for verification?
- Is total occupancy cost considered beyond base rent or purchase price?
- Does term length match business risk, buildout investment, and exit needs?
- Is location evaluated by customer, workforce, logistics, and brand realities?
- Are tours structured to gather operational evidence?
- Are contractors, architects, attorneys, or specialists involved where needed?
- Has the agent avoided promising use approval or legal consequences?
- Would the property support the business after the excitement of the tour fades?; are business, finance, operations, legal, and buildout decision makers identified?
