# Commercial Due Diligence Zoning And Operating Costs

Commercial due diligence determines whether the property can legally and economically support the client's plan. A building can look suitable but fail because zoning blocks the use, environmental risk is unresolved, operating expenses are understated, tenants have rights that reduce flexibility, or building systems require major capital. Agents often let attorneys, accountants, or inspectors handle due diligence without tracking whether the client's business assumptions still hold. This skill helps the agent coordinate the risk picture.

## Core Rules

### Tie Due Diligence To Intended Use Or Investment Thesis

Start from the reason the client wants the property: owner occupancy, retail lease, restaurant, warehouse, redevelopment, investment yield, medical practice, franchise, or mixed-use income. Then identify what must be verified for that thesis to work.

Due diligence is not a generic document dump. It should test the client's actual use, income, cost, timing, and exit assumptions.

### Verify Zoning, Permits, And Occupancy

Commercial use may depend on zoning classification, conditional use, certificate of occupancy, parking ratios, signage permits, fire code, health department, liquor license, environmental permits, ADA requirements, and local business licensing. Prior use does not guarantee the new use is allowed.

Route verification to municipal officials, land-use counsel, architects, engineers, or permit expediters as appropriate. Do not promise approval based on listing claims.

### Review Operating Costs And Pass-Throughs

For leased space, review CAM, NNN, taxes, insurance, utilities, maintenance, management fees, reconciliations, caps, exclusions, and historical increases. For investment purchases, review operating statements, rent roll, leases, reimbursements, vacancy, reserves, capital expenses, and normalized expenses.

Clients can overpay when they rely on advertised rent or net operating income without validating assumptions.

### Inspect Building Systems And Capital Exposure

Commercial roofs, HVAC, elevators, fire systems, plumbing, electrical, loading areas, parking lots, facade, structure, environmental systems, and code compliance can create large capital needs. A general walkthrough rarely identifies full exposure.

Recommend qualified inspections and specialist reports. Tie findings to lease obligations, purchase price, reserves, lender requirements, insurance, and operating plan.

### Address Environmental And Hazard Risk

Industrial, automotive, dry cleaner, gas station, agricultural, medical, lab, older building, or redevelopment sites may require environmental review. Phase I or Phase II assessments, contamination, asbestos, lead, underground tanks, vapor intrusion, or hazardous materials can affect financing and liability.

The agent should not interpret environmental reports as legal conclusions. Escalate to environmental consultants and counsel.

### Review Tenant And Lease Evidence

For occupied commercial properties, leases, amendments, rent rolls, deposits, arrears, options, renewal rights, exclusives, estoppels, subleases, guarantees, and tenant disputes matter. Income quality depends on enforceable lease terms and tenant performance.

Do not assume the rent roll is accurate without supporting documents. Tenant rights can limit future owner use or redevelopment.

### Track Due Diligence Deadlines And Deliverables

Commercial contracts often have due diligence periods, financing dates, title deadlines, survey review, environmental deadlines, lease review, estoppel delivery, and deposit hardening dates. Missing a deadline can put large deposits at risk.

Maintain a deliverable tracker and alert the client before rights expire. Commercial deposits and option payments can be substantial.

### Reconcile Findings With Price And Terms

Due diligence should lead to decisions: proceed, renegotiate price, request credits, require repairs, extend diligence, modify lease terms, cancel, or seek specialist advice. Do not collect reports without updating the economic model or client recommendation.

If the thesis no longer works, say so. Sunk pursuit cost should not drive the decision.

### Confirm Financing And Insurance Requirements

Commercial lenders and insurers may require environmental reports, surveys, leases, estoppels, tenant financials, property condition reports, flood review, ADA or code items, reserves, or repairs before closing. These requirements can differ from what the buyer personally wants to review.

Ask lender and insurance contacts what conditions must be satisfied before deposits harden or contingencies expire. Financing assumptions are part of due diligence.

## Common Traps

- Treating prior use as proof that the client's intended use is allowed.
- Ignoring CAM, NNN, taxes, insurance, and capital expense exposure.
- Relying on seller operating statements without lease and expense backup.
- Skipping environmental review for risky historical uses.
- Missing tenant options, exclusives, arrears, or estoppel discrepancies.
- Letting due diligence deadlines expire while waiting for reports; underestimating roof, HVAC, parking lot, elevator, or fire-system costs
- Treating zoning, environmental, and lease interpretation as agent expertise; failing to update price or terms after material findings
- Letting emotional attachment to a site override the business thesis; forgetting lender or insurer diligence requirements until after deposit risk increases

## Self-Check

- Is due diligence tied to the client's actual use or investment thesis?
- Are zoning, permits, occupancy, parking, signage, and licensing routed for verification?
- Are operating costs, pass-throughs, rent roll, and expense history reviewed?
- Are building systems and capital needs inspected by qualified professionals?
- Are environmental risks identified and escalated?
- Are leases, options, estoppels, guarantees, and tenant rights reviewed?
- Are due diligence deadlines and deposit-risk dates tracked?
- Are findings reconciled with price, rent, reserves, and terms?
- Has the agent avoided legal, environmental, engineering, or accounting conclusions?
- Would the client still want the deal after seeing the verified operating reality?; are lender and insurer due diligence conditions tracked before rights expire?
