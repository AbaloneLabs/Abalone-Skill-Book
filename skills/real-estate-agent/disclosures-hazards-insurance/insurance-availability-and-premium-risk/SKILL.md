# Insurance Availability And Premium Risk

Insurance is no longer a routine closing item in many markets. Wildfire, wind, flood, roof age, claims history, carrier withdrawals, building materials, older systems, vacant properties, and short-term rental use can make coverage expensive or unavailable. Agents do not sell insurance, but they must treat insurability as a material transaction risk. This skill helps the agent encourage early insurance verification and connect coverage issues to financing, affordability, negotiation, and client expectations.

## Core Rules

### Raise Insurance Early

Insurance should be checked during due diligence, not the week before closing. Premiums, deductibles, coverage exclusions, and carrier availability can affect buyer affordability and lender approval.

Encourage buyers to obtain quotes early, especially for high-risk locations, older homes, condos, rural properties, vacant homes, investment properties, and homes with unusual features.

### Identify Property Features That Affect Coverage

Roof age, electrical systems, plumbing, foundation, prior claims, pools, trampolines, wood stoves, oil tanks, knob-and-tube wiring, aluminum wiring, wildfire exposure, flood zones, distance to hydrants, short-term rental use, and vacant status may affect insurance.

Do not assume a standard policy is available until an insurance professional confirms it.

### Connect Insurance To Loan Approval

Lenders may require acceptable hazard, flood, wind, condo master, replacement-cost, or landlord coverage before funding. If insurance is unavailable or too expensive, financing can fail even after loan approval appears strong.

Insurance problems should be communicated to lender and client promptly.

### Discuss Premium Shock And Monthly Payment

Premiums and deductibles can change the true monthly cost of ownership. A buyer who qualifies based on an estimate may be strained by final insurance numbers. High deductibles also affect post-closing cash risk.

Encourage the buyer to compare payment estimates after real insurance quotes, not generic assumptions.

### Watch Claims History And Seller Disclosures

Prior claims, repeated water losses, fire damage, roof claims, or remediation history can affect future coverage. Claim history may not be fully visible to the buyer without seller disclosure or insurer review.

If claims or losses appear in disclosures, route insurance questions to the buyer's insurance professional.

### Understand Master Policy Complexity

Condo, co-op, townhome, and HOA properties may involve master policies, walls-in coverage, deductibles, special assessments, fidelity coverage, flood policies, and lender review. Gaps between master coverage and individual policy can surprise buyers.

Do not treat condo insurance as a simple version of single-family coverage.

### Consider Use And Occupancy

Owner occupancy, landlord use, vacancy, renovation, short-term rental, mixed-use, home business, or extended travel may change coverage. A buyer's intended use must align with insurance and lending assumptions.

If use is unusual, insurance review should happen before removing contingencies.

### Handle Seller Insurance Questions Carefully

Sellers may need to maintain coverage until closing and sometimes during post-closing possession. Vacant or tenant-occupied properties may require policy changes. A lapse before closing can create serious exposure.

Advise sellers to confirm coverage with their insurer rather than assuming existing policy remains adequate.

### Document Insurance Verification Advice

Because agents are not insurance advisors, document when clients are advised to obtain quotes, review exclusions, confirm lender requirements, and ask about deductibles and use. If a client delays, the risk should be clear.

Insurance surprises are easier to manage when early advice is documented.

### Verify Insurance Before Removing Protection

Insurance availability can change the real affordability and financeability of a property. The agent should encourage buyers to obtain quotes early, especially for wildfire, flood, coastal, older roof, knob-and-tube, prior claims, short-term rental, vacant, rural, or high-value properties. A premium surprise after contingency removal can trap the client between unaffordable ownership and contract default.

Insurance review should include coverage scope, exclusions, deductibles, replacement-cost assumptions, lender requirements, binding conditions, and whether coverage can remain in force after closing. Sellers also need awareness that insurance obstacles can shrink the buyer pool or create appraisal and loan timing problems.

## Common Traps

- Assuming insurance is available and affordable because the seller currently has coverage.
- Removing contingencies before quotes, deductibles, exclusions, and binding conditions are understood.

- Treating insurance as a routine closing checklist item.
- Waiting until late underwriting to discover coverage is unavailable or expensive.
- Ignoring roof age, claims history, wiring, plumbing, vacancy, or short-term rental use.
- Assuming flood, wind, wildfire, or condo coverage is included in a basic policy.
- Forgetting that insurance premiums affect loan approval and monthly payment.
- Letting buyers compare properties without considering insurance cost differences.
- Missing master policy deductibles and coverage gaps in HOA properties.
- Giving coverage opinions outside the agent's role.
- Allowing sellers to cancel insurance too early.
- Failing to document advice to obtain insurance quotes and review exclusions.
- Assuming a prior owner had coverage that a new buyer can obtain on the same terms.

## Self-Check

- Has the client been prompted to verify availability, premium, deductible, exclusions, and binding conditions early?
- Are insurance findings tied to financing, affordability, contingency, and negotiation strategy?

- Has the buyer been urged to obtain insurance quotes early?
- Are property features and uses that affect coverage identified?
- Are lender insurance requirements and financing effects considered?
- Are premium, deductible, and monthly-payment effects reviewed with the lender or insurer?
- Are prior claims and disclosure issues routed to insurance professionals?
- Are condo or HOA master policy gaps considered?
- Are occupancy, rental, vacancy, and renovation uses disclosed to the insurer?
- Are seller insurance obligations through closing considered?
- Is the agent avoiding coverage advice beyond their role?
- Would the client understand insurance as both a closing condition and ownership cost?
