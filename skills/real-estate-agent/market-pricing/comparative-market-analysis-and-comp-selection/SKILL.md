# Comparative Market Analysis And Comp Selection

A comparative market analysis is a judgment exercise, not a spreadsheet of nearby sales. The agent must decide which properties are actually comparable, which differences matter, and how market timing, condition, financing, concessions, and buyer behavior affect value. Agents often choose comps that support the desired price instead of the most relevant evidence. This skill helps the agent produce pricing guidance that is honest, defensible, and clear about uncertainty.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Property's True Competitive Set

Comparable selection begins with the subject property's likely buyer pool. Identify property type, location, size, condition, age, layout, lot, parking, views, school assignment where lawful and data-sourced, HOA, amenities, zoning, financing eligibility, and functional quirks. A buyer comparing a condo, rural home, luxury estate, duplex, townhouse, or fixer will not evaluate it like a generic single-family home.

Use proximity as one factor, not the only factor. A nearby sale may be less comparable than a farther property if it differs in property type, condition, school boundary, noise exposure, view, lot utility, or buyer segment.

### Prefer Recent Closed Sales But Understand Their Limits

Closed sales show what buyers actually paid, but they may reflect market conditions from weeks or months ago. In a shifting market, older comps may mislead. Pending sales, active listings, price reductions, days on market, and failed listings can help interpret current conditions, but they have different reliability.

Make the hierarchy explicit: closed sales anchor value, pending sales suggest current demand if details are known, active listings show competition, and expired or withdrawn listings show what the market rejected. Do not treat list prices as value.

### Adjust For Meaningful Differences

Differences may include size, lot, condition, upgrades, floor plan, bedroom function, garage, parking, outdoor space, views, noise, location, HOA dues, amenities, pool, basement, ADU, rental restrictions, energy systems, and known concessions. Adjustments do not need false precision, but the analysis should explain why a comp is superior, inferior, or similar.

Avoid automatic dollar adjustments copied from another market. If a difference would not change buyer behavior in the local segment, it may not justify a large adjustment. If a difference strongly affects financing or buyer pool, it may matter more than square footage.

### Account For Condition And Presentation Honestly

Condition is often the hardest pricing variable. A renovated home is not comparable to a worn home only because size matches. Deferred maintenance, roof age, systems, water intrusion, outdated finishes, layout obsolescence, odors, landscaping, staging, cleanliness, and access all affect buyer response.

Do not let seller pride or buyer optimism erase condition differences. If condition is uncertain, state that price guidance may change after inspections, contractor bids, or buyer feedback.

### Consider Concessions And Financing Context

Sale price may hide seller concessions, rate buydowns, repair credits, personal property, rent-backs, closing cost assistance, or unusual financing. A high sale with large concessions may not support the same net value as it appears. Loan type and appraisal norms can also affect buyer pool.

Where available, review agent remarks, public records, MLS concessions fields, and transaction context. If concession data is unavailable, acknowledge uncertainty instead of assuming all closed prices are equal.

### Present A Range And Strategy, Not A Magic Number

Pricing guidance should usually be a range with recommended strategy. For sellers, discuss where to list to maximize exposure, generate offers, test demand, or protect net. For buyers, discuss likely market value, competition, appraisal risk, and offer terms. The right price may differ depending on speed, certainty, competition, and negotiation plan.

Do not pretend the CMA is an appraisal or guarantee. It is professional market guidance based on available evidence.

### Explain Excluded Comps

Often the strongest part of a CMA is explaining what was not used. Exclude properties that differ materially by location, condition, size, property type, financing, distress status, lot utility, view, school boundary, HOA, or sale circumstances. This protects the analysis from cherry-picking accusations.

If the client insists on irrelevant comps, address them directly. Ignoring them may leave the client believing the agent missed evidence.

### Update The CMA When The Market Moves

A CMA can become stale quickly when rates change, new listings appear, price reductions occur, similar properties go pending, or buyer feedback accumulates. For listings, revisit pricing after launch signals. For buyers, refresh comps before writing or revising an offer.

Pricing discipline is iterative. The first analysis is a starting point, not a permanent truth.

## Common Traps

- Choosing comps to justify the client's desired price.
- Treating distance as more important than buyer pool and property type.
- Ignoring active competition, failed listings, and current market direction.
- Adjusting with false precision without explaining buyer relevance.
- Comparing renovated and unrenovated properties as if size alone controls value.
- Ignoring concessions, credits, rent-backs, or unusual sale terms.
- Presenting a single value without uncertainty or strategy; calling a CMA an appraisal or implying guaranteed market value
- Failing to explain why attractive but irrelevant comps were excluded; letting the CMA become stale after new market evidence appears

## Self-Check

- Is the subject property's true competitive set defined?
- Are closed, pending, active, expired, and withdrawn listings used according to their reliability?
- Are meaningful differences explained without false precision?
- Is condition, presentation, and deferred maintenance treated honestly?
- Are concessions, financing, and unusual sale terms considered where available?
- Is the result a defensible range and strategy rather than a magic number?
- Are excluded comps addressed, especially those the client may raise?
- Are appraisal limitations and uncertainty clear?
- Is the CMA refreshed when market conditions or property facts change?
- Could another competent agent understand why these comps were selected?
