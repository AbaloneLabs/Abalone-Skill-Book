# Title Commitment And Exception Review

Title documents can look procedural until an exception limits use, blocks financing, delays closing, or surprises the buyer after purchase. Real estate agents are not title attorneys, but they should recognize when title exceptions, easements, covenants, liens, or survey matters require client attention and specialist review. Agents often treat title as a closing-office task and miss contract deadlines or practical property-use implications. This skill helps the agent turn title review into a disciplined risk screen.

## Core Rules

### Stay Clear On Professional Boundaries

Do not give legal opinions on title validity, insurability, priority, or enforceability. The agent should help the client obtain, read, and route title documents to the title company, escrow officer, attorney, surveyor, lender, or broker as appropriate.

The agent's value is noticing issues and timing, not replacing legal title review.

### Track Title Delivery And Objection Deadlines

Contracts often include deadlines for title commitment delivery, document review, objections, cure, and cancellation. Missing a title objection deadline can waive important rights.

When the title package arrives, calendar review periods and confirm the client knows who will review the documents.

### Distinguish Standard From Property-Specific Exceptions

Some exceptions are routine; others are property-specific and may affect use, access, financing, future resale, or improvements. Easements, covenants, restrictions, mineral rights, leases, encroachments, assessments, and unrecorded interests require attention.

Do not dismiss an exception as boilerplate until someone qualified confirms it is routine.

### Connect Exceptions To Client Plans

A title exception matters differently depending on what the client wants to do. A utility easement may be minor for one buyer and a major problem for a pool, addition, fence, driveway, accessory unit, or farm use.

Ask what the client plans to do with the property and compare that plan against known restrictions.

### Review Easement And Access Issues Carefully

Access, ingress and egress, shared driveways, private roads, maintenance agreements, utility easements, drainage easements, and beach or water access can affect daily use and financing. Rural and land transactions are especially sensitive.

If access is not clear and legally insured, closing risk is high.

### Watch Liens And Payoff Dependencies

Mortgages, tax liens, mechanic's liens, judgment liens, HOA liens, municipal liens, code enforcement fines, and unpaid utilities can delay closing or reduce seller proceeds. Some liens are expected; others require cure or payoff verification.

Track whether the settlement side has payoff demands and release plans.

### Ask About Title Insurance Coverage

Clients may not understand owner policy, lender policy, endorsements, exclusions, exceptions, or survey coverage. Agents should not advise on coverage adequacy, but should prompt the client to ask title professionals what is and is not insured.

This is especially important where exceptions remain after closing.

### Coordinate Survey And Title Review

Survey matters may reveal encroachments, boundary conflicts, fences, easements, setbacks, access, or improvements outside legal boundaries. A title commitment without survey context may not reveal practical use problems.

Where property boundaries matter, encourage timely survey review and specialist advice.

### Document Client Decisions On Exceptions

If a client accepts an exception, closes without a survey, waives objection, or relies on title advice, document the decision and the professional source of advice. This prevents later confusion about what the agent did or did not interpret.

Title acceptance should be intentional, not accidental.

### Revisit Title After Amendments Or New Facts

Title risk can change when the deal changes. A new closing date, seller credit, added personal property, post-closing possession, repair escrow, parcel change, survey result, HOA discovery, or new lien can require updated title or settlement review.

Do not treat title as a one-time document packet. Keep title questions alive until recording and possession are complete.

## Common Traps

- Treating title documents as closing paperwork rather than due diligence.
- Missing title objection or cure deadlines.
- Calling exceptions routine without qualified review.
- Ignoring how easements or covenants affect the client's intended use.
- Overlooking private road, shared driveway, or access maintenance issues.
- Assuming liens will clear without payoff and release confirmation.
- Letting clients confuse lender title policy with owner protection.
- Skipping survey review where boundaries, fences, or improvements matter.
- Interpreting legal title documents beyond the agent's competence.
- Failing to document when the client accepts an exception or declines review.
- Discovering restrictions only after inspection, appraisal, or closing.
- Treating title review as finished even after amendments, survey findings, or new lien information change the transaction.

## Self-Check

- Are title documents routed to qualified reviewers without the agent giving legal opinions?
- Are delivery, objection, cure, and cancellation deadlines tracked?
- Are property-specific exceptions separated from routine items?
- Are exceptions connected to the client's intended use and improvement plans?
- Are access, easements, private roads, and maintenance duties checked?
- Are liens and payoff dependencies being resolved by the settlement side?
- Has the client been prompted to ask what title insurance covers and excludes?
- Is survey review considered where boundaries or improvements matter?
- Are client decisions to accept, object, or waive documented?
- Would the client understand which title matters could affect use, financing, or resale?
- Has title review been revisited after amendments, survey results, or newly discovered facts?
