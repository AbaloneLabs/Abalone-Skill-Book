# Escrow Instructions And Funds Flow Control

Money movement is one of the highest-risk parts of a real estate transaction. Earnest money, closing funds, seller proceeds, payoffs, commissions, repair escrows, and wire instructions all depend on accurate instructions and trusted channels. Agents often focus on deal terms and assume escrow will handle funds safely, but wire fraud, missed deposits, ambiguous holdbacks, or misunderstood disbursements can create serious client harm. This skill helps the agent coordinate funds flow without stepping outside escrow or legal boundaries.

## Core Rules

### Treat Wire Fraud As A Constant Risk

Clients should verify wire instructions through known, trusted channels before sending funds. Email changes, urgent messages, spoofed domains, and last-minute instruction changes are red flags. Agents should avoid forwarding wiring instructions unless local practice and brokerage policy permit it.

Use brokerage-approved fraud warnings early and repeatedly, not only on closing day.

### Confirm Earnest Money Duties

Earnest money amount, delivery method, deadline, holder, receipt, default consequences, release rules, and dispute procedures should be clear. A missed or late deposit can create breach risk or weaken the buyer's position.

Track the deadline and confirm receipt through the authorized escrow or broker channel.

### Keep Escrow Instructions Consistent With Contract Terms

Escrow instructions should match purchase price, deposits, credits, prorations, commissions, possession terms, repairs, personal property, holdbacks, and closing date. If amendments change terms, settlement instructions must update too.

Do not assume the closing file reflects every contract change; verify material updates are transmitted.

### Handle Holdbacks Carefully

Repair escrows, possession holdbacks, utility escrows, permit holdbacks, rent prorations, or post-closing work agreements need clear amount, holder, release condition, deadline, dispute process, and lender approval where required.

Vague holdbacks create post-closing conflict and may violate lender or escrow rules.

### Respect Settlement Professional Roles

Escrow officers, attorneys, settlement agents, and title companies control funds under legal and contractual duties. The agent should coordinate and ask questions, not direct unauthorized disbursement or interpret settlement law.

When clients dispute funds, route them to the proper settlement and legal channels.

### Track Payoffs And Disbursement Dependencies

Seller mortgages, liens, HOA fees, taxes, judgments, commissions, referral fees, utilities, and contractor invoices may need payoff or disbursement. Missing payoff information can delay recording or reduce proceeds unexpectedly.

Ask early whether all payoff and lien information is complete.

### Prepare Clients For Cash-To-Close Timing

Buyers may need wired funds, cashier's checks, verified deposits, or lender-approved sources before signing or recording. Large last-minute transfers can create lender questions or delays.

Coordinate timing with the lender and escrow so funds arrive safely and are sourced properly.

### Review Seller Proceeds Expectations

Sellers may expect immediate funds after signing, but proceeds timing depends on recording, funding, payoff processing, wire cutoffs, and local practice. Net proceeds can change because of prorations, payoffs, credits, repairs, or fees.

Prepare sellers for realistic disbursement timing and final-statement review.

### Document Funds-Related Communications

Funds instructions, deposit confirmations, holdback terms, wire warnings, amendment transmittals, and client questions should be documented. This is not bureaucracy; it protects clients in high-risk money movement.

When money instructions change, confirm through trusted channels and record the verification path.

### Resolve Commission And Referral Fee Issues Early

Commission splits, buyer-broker compensation, referral fees, transaction fees, concessions, and brokerage payment instructions can affect settlement statements and client questions. These items should be consistent with brokerage agreements, MLS rules where applicable, contract terms, disclosures, and local law.

Do not let compensation confusion surface at the signing table. Resolve brokerage and settlement questions early enough that clients can review final figures calmly.

### Check Cross-Border And Entity Payment Complexity

Trusts, estates, LLCs, foreign sellers, remote signers, relocation companies, and multiple heirs can complicate disbursement authority and tax withholding. The agent should not advise on tax withholding or entity authority, but should notice when settlement needs additional documentation.

Early identification prevents funding delays after everyone assumes the deal is ready.

## Common Traps

- Sending wire instructions casually by email.
- Waiting until closing day to warn clients about wire fraud.
- Missing earnest money delivery or receipt confirmation.
- Assuming escrow instructions automatically update after amendments.
- Creating vague repair or possession holdbacks.
- Directing disbursements outside the agent's authority.
- Ignoring payoffs, liens, and fees until final settlement statement.
- Letting buyers move large funds without lender guidance.
- Promising sellers immediate proceeds without knowing local funding and recording practice.
- Failing to document funds-related advice and confirmations.
- Treating funds flow as someone else's issue until a problem appears.
- Allowing commission, referral, or brokerage payment questions to surprise clients at settlement.
- Missing entity, estate, foreign-seller, or remote-signer disbursement complexity until closing.

## Self-Check

- Have clients received clear wire-fraud warnings and verification instructions?
- Are earnest money amount, holder, method, deadline, and receipt confirmed?
- Do escrow instructions match the current contract and amendments?
- Are holdbacks specific, authorized, lender-approved where needed, and enforceable?
- Is the agent coordinating without directing unauthorized disbursement?
- Are payoff, lien, commission, and fee dependencies being tracked?
- Are buyer cash-to-close timing and source-of-funds issues coordinated with lender and escrow?
- Are seller proceeds and disbursement timing explained realistically?
- Are funds-related changes verified through trusted channels?
- Would the file show a clear record of funds-flow advice and confirmations?
- Are brokerage compensation and referral-fee items consistent with agreements and settlement instructions?
- Are entity, estate, foreign-party, or remote-signing payment complexities identified early?
