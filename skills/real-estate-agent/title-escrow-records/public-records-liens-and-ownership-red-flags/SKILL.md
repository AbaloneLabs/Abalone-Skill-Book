# Public Records Liens And Ownership Red Flags

Public records can reveal problems that are not visible during a showing: uncertain ownership, unpaid taxes, recorded liens, estate issues, entity authority, code violations, permit gaps, or inconsistent property characteristics. Real estate agents should not perform a legal title examination, but they should notice public-record red flags early enough to route them to title, escrow, attorneys, or the broker. Agents often wait for title work and lose time that could have prevented a failed transaction.

## Core Rules

### Use Public Records As A Risk Screen

Tax records, recorder data, assessor information, permit databases, court records, HOA records, and listing history can help identify questions. They are not always complete or current, and they do not replace title or legal review.

Treat public records as prompts for verification, not final answers.

### Verify Seller Authority Early

Check whether the apparent seller matches recorded ownership and whether ownership involves trusts, estates, LLCs, corporations, divorces, guardianships, powers of attorney, or multiple owners. Authority issues can delay signing or invalidate assumptions about who can accept terms.

If authority is not straightforward, involve title, escrow, broker, or legal counsel before deadlines become urgent.

### Watch Lien And Judgment Signals

Recorded mortgages, tax liens, mechanics liens, HOA liens, judgments, municipal liens, and code enforcement fines can affect payoff and closing. Some are normal; others may exceed equity or require negotiation.

Do not assume a seller has enough proceeds to clear liens without settlement confirmation.

### Compare Property Characteristics

Public records may differ from listing claims on square footage, bedrooms, baths, lot size, zoning, year built, additions, basements, accessory units, or property type. Differences can affect value, appraisal, insurance, permitting, and disclosure.

Ask whether discrepancies are explainable and documented.

### Review Permit And Code Indicators

Permit databases can reveal additions, remodels, roof work, electrical upgrades, septic work, code violations, expired permits, or lack of permits for visible improvements. Records may be incomplete, but absence or inconsistency can trigger due diligence.

Do not tell clients an improvement is legal simply because no violation appears.

### Note Tax And Assessment Issues

Delinquent taxes, special assessments, exemptions, reassessment rules, supplemental bills, tax abatements, and transfer taxes can affect closing and ownership cost. Tax data also helps identify ownership or address discrepancies.

Refer exact tax consequences to the appropriate professional, but do not ignore visible tax risk.

### Identify Boundary And Legal Description Questions

Odd lot shapes, parcel splits, merged parcels, easements, private roads, metes-and-bounds descriptions, and assessor map inconsistencies can affect use and transfer. Buyers may need a survey or title clarification.

Property marketing should not overstate lot or boundary certainty.

### Be Alert To Fraud And Distress Patterns

Vacant land, absentee owners, distressed sellers, recent deed changes, urgent cash demands, suspicious powers of attorney, identity mismatch, and unusual communication patterns can signal fraud. Public records can help spot inconsistencies.

Follow brokerage identity and fraud-prevention procedures when records and behavior do not align.

### Document Referrals And Open Questions

When records raise questions, record what was found, who was asked, and what professional response was received. Do not let an unanswered public-record concern disappear because the transaction becomes busy.

Open questions should be resolved, disclosed where required, or consciously accepted by the client with advice.

### Reconcile Public Records With Client Statements

Clients may describe additions, ownership, access, rentals, repairs, permits, or liens differently from the public record. The mismatch may have an innocent explanation, but it should not be ignored. Ask for documents and route uncertainty to the right professional.

This is especially important before marketing claims, offer deadlines, inspection negotiations, or disclosure responses rely on the disputed fact.

## Common Traps

- Treating public records as either irrelevant or absolutely authoritative.
- Waiting for title work before checking obvious ownership mismatch.
- Assuming all recorded liens can be cleared from proceeds.
- Marketing square footage or improvements without reconciling record discrepancies.
- Calling unpermitted work legal because the city has not cited it.
- Ignoring delinquent taxes, exemptions, or special assessments.
- Overlooking trust, estate, entity, divorce, or power-of-attorney authority issues.
- Missing fraud signs in vacant land or absentee-owner transactions.
- Failing to route public-record questions to qualified professionals.
- Letting open questions remain undocumented.
- Confusing assessor data with insured title or legal boundary certainty.
- Accepting client statements that conflict with public records without further verification or disclosure analysis.

## Self-Check

- Are public records used as a verification prompt rather than a legal conclusion?
- Does recorded ownership match the party claiming authority to sell?
- Are trust, estate, entity, marital, guardianship, or power-of-attorney issues identified?
- Are liens, judgments, taxes, code issues, and payoffs routed to settlement professionals?
- Are property-characteristic discrepancies investigated before pricing or marketing reliance?
- Are permits and code records checked where improvements or condition raise questions?
- Are tax, assessment, and exemption issues considered?
- Are parcel, boundary, easement, or legal-description concerns escalated?
- Are fraud and identity red flags handled under brokerage procedures?
- Are open questions tracked until resolved or consciously accepted?
- Are client statements reconciled with public records before they are used in marketing, pricing, negotiation, or disclosure?
