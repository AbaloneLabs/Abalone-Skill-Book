# Condo Financing Warrantability And Special Assessment Risk

A condo buyer can be financially qualified and still unable to close if the project fails lender review. Warrantability, litigation, reserves, insurance, owner-occupancy, commercial space, delinquency, short-term rentals, structural issues, and special assessments can affect financing and resale. Agents often treat the unit as the main asset and miss project-level risks. This skill helps the agent identify condo financing and assessment issues early enough to protect offer strategy and deadlines.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Unit Approval From Project Approval

The lender must evaluate both the borrower and the condominium project. A buyer's preapproval does not guarantee the project will meet loan-program requirements. Project review can involve budgets, insurance, litigation, reserves, ownership concentration, delinquency, occupancy, and questionnaire responses.

Ask early whether the lender has reviewed the specific project or needs documents.

### Know Warrantability Is Program-Specific

Conventional, FHA, VA, jumbo, portfolio, and local programs may treat condo risk differently. A project rejected by one lender or program may be acceptable to another, but switching programs can affect rate, down payment, and timeline.

Do not describe a condo as financeable without lender-specific confirmation.

### Obtain Association Documents Promptly

Condo questionnaires, budgets, insurance certificates, reserve studies, litigation disclosures, meeting minutes, master deed, bylaws, rules, and assessment history may be needed by both buyer and lender. Delayed documents can threaten closing.

The offer timeline should reflect association response speed.

### Investigate Special Assessments

Special assessments can be pending, approved, rumored, paid by seller, assumed by buyer, financed, or tied to a future project. The timing and responsibility should be clear in contract terms and settlement statements.

Ask whether assessment risk affects both affordability and lender review.

### Watch Deferred Maintenance And Structural Issues

Roof, facade, balcony, elevator, plumbing, fire-safety, foundation, and structural issues can create large assessments, insurance problems, financing difficulty, and safety concerns. Meeting minutes and reserve studies may reveal issues before final numbers exist.

Do not wait for a formal assessment to discuss visible project risk.

### Check Insurance Adequacy

Lenders may review master insurance, fidelity coverage, liability, flood, wind, walls-in responsibility, and deductibles. High deductibles can create owner exposure or assessment risk after a loss.

Insurance review should happen early with lender and insurance professionals.

### Consider Investor And Rental Concentration

High investor concentration, short-term rentals, hotel-like operations, single-entity ownership, or low owner occupancy can affect financing and project culture. Rental restrictions may also affect the buyer's future plans.

Project composition matters for both underwriting and ownership experience.

### Plan For Financing Contingency Protection

Because condo project approval can fail late, contingency timing should protect the buyer until project review is complete where possible. If the buyer waives financing or appraisal protections, project-level risk must be explicit.

Do not let a strong borrower file hide a weak project file.

### Document Lender And Association Answers

Record when documents were requested, what the lender said about project approval, what the association disclosed, and what remains unresolved. Condo failures often involve unclear responsibility for missing information.

Clear records help determine whether delay came from buyer, seller, association, lender, or management company.

### Check Project Risk Before Buyer Emotion Hardens

Condo financing risk is often a project issue, not a buyer issue. Owner-occupancy ratios, litigation, insurance coverage, reserves, commercial space, investor concentration, deferred maintenance, structural reports, budget deficits, and special assessments can affect warrantability or lender appetite even when the buyer is financially strong. The agent should prompt early lender and HOA document review before the buyer becomes anchored to the unit.

Different loan types and lenders may treat the same project differently. A conventional loan, FHA, VA, portfolio lender, jumbo lender, or non-warrantable condo program can have different tolerances, costs, and timelines. The agent should not promise financing viability; instead, coordinate early review and help the client understand how project risk can alter payment, cash needed, contingencies, and closing certainty.

## Common Traps

- Treating condo loan approval as only the buyer's qualification rather than a project-level risk.
- Waiting until late underwriting to examine litigation, reserves, insurance, or special assessments.

- Assuming buyer preapproval means the condo project is acceptable.
- Treating warrantability as a universal yes/no rather than program-specific.
- Waiting too long to order condo questionnaires and association documents.
- Ignoring pending or rumored assessments because they are not yet billed.
- Overlooking deferred maintenance and structural risk in minutes or reserve studies.
- Missing master insurance gaps and high deductibles.
- Ignoring investor concentration, short-term rental activity, or single-entity ownership.
- Releasing financing protections before project approval is complete.
- Assuming another unit's past sale proves current financing approval.
- Failing to document lender and association responses.
- Treating special assessments only as seller-buyer negotiation, not ownership risk.

## Self-Check

- Has project-level warrantability risk been reviewed before the buyer is deeply committed?
- Are loan-type differences and non-warrantable alternatives framed without guaranteeing approval?

- Has the lender reviewed the specific condo or co-op project?
- Is warrantability assessed by loan program and lender, not assumed generally?
- Are association documents and questionnaires ordered early?
- Are special assessments clarified for amount, timing, responsibility, and financing effect?
- Are deferred maintenance, structural, reserve, and insurance issues reviewed?
- Are investor concentration, rentals, and occupancy patterns considered?
- Does the financing contingency timeline protect project-review risk?
- Are lender, association, seller, and buyer communications documented?
- Are unresolved project issues escalated before contingency release?
- Would the buyer understand project-level risk separate from the unit itself?
