# Fiduciary Duty Confidentiality And Loyalty

Real estate agency duties shape nearly every client interaction. The agent must advocate for the client while staying honest, lawful, and within the scope of representation. Agents can fail by sharing confidential motivation, obeying unlawful instructions, concealing material facts, favoring convenience over the client, or confusing friendliness with loyalty. This skill helps the agent reason through fiduciary and ethical duties before communication, negotiation, and disclosure decisions.

## Core Rules

### Know Which Duties Apply To This Relationship

Duties vary by jurisdiction and agency model, but may include loyalty, confidentiality, disclosure, obedience to lawful instructions, reasonable care, diligence, accounting, and honesty. The agent should know whether they represent a buyer, seller, landlord, tenant, both sides under limited agency, or no party as a customer-facing facilitator.

Do not assume every conversation carries the same duty. Agency status controls what advice can be given, what information is protected, and what must be disclosed.

### Protect Client Confidentiality

Confidential information can include motivation, urgency, financial limits, willingness to concede, family circumstances, health issues, business plans, negotiation strategy, replacement housing pressure, and internal disagreements. Share only when authorized, required, or necessary within the representation team under proper controls.

Confidentiality also applies after the transaction or representation ends, subject to law and brokerage policy. Casual comments to other agents can damage the client.

### Obey Only Lawful And Ethical Instructions

Clients may instruct the agent to hide defects, mislead about competing offers, discriminate, pressure inspectors, conceal compensation, backdate documents, bypass signatures, or avoid written records. The duty of obedience does not require following unlawful or unethical instructions.

Respond by explaining the boundary, offering a lawful alternative, and escalating to broker or counsel if the client persists. If necessary, withdraw according to policy.

### Distinguish Advocacy From Misrepresentation

Strong advocacy highlights the client's position, uses evidence, negotiates terms, and protects confidentiality. Misrepresentation lies, omits required material facts, exaggerates demand, fabricates deadlines, or presents unsupported claims as fact. The agent should advocate firmly without sacrificing honesty.

When unsure whether a fact must be disclosed or can remain confidential, escalate before communicating. The tension between loyalty and disclosure is a common risk point.

### Account For Money And Property Carefully

Deposits, earnest money, keys, access devices, personal property, seller proceeds, rent, security deposits, referral fees, and client documents require care. Follow brokerage and escrow procedures. Do not hold funds, keys, or documents casually outside authorized systems.

Accounting duties are not only financial. They include accurate handling of property and records entrusted to the agent.

### Avoid Self-Interest Conflicts

An agent's commission, referral fee, affiliated business relationship, desire to close, team production goals, or relationship with another agent can conflict with client loyalty. The agent should disclose required interests and avoid letting compensation influence advice on price, offer strength, cancellation, repair negotiation, or vendor selection.

If the best client advice may kill the deal, still give the advice. Loyalty is tested when commission is at risk.

### Maintain Reasonable Care And Competence

Fiduciary duty includes knowing when the agent is outside competence. Complex tax, legal, title, zoning, construction, environmental, investment, or commercial questions may require qualified experts. Reasonable care means spotting issues and routing them, not pretending to be the expert.

Do not use "not my field" as an excuse to ignore visible risk. Flag and refer.

### Document Material Advice And Client Decisions

Record significant advice, risk explanations, client instructions, consent, disclosure decisions, conflicts, referrals, and escalation. Documentation should be factual and timely. It should not be used to hide poor advice after the fact.

A well-kept file shows that the agent acted loyally, honestly, and with care.

### Reassess Duties When The Relationship Changes

Agency duties can change when an unrepresented lead signs a buyer agreement, a listing agent begins limited dual agency, a team member represents the other side, a client terminates representation, or a past client returns with a related matter. Do not keep using the same communication habits after the legal relationship changes.

When status changes, update disclosures, confidentiality handling, compensation expectations, and broker supervision. Transition moments are where accidental conflicts often appear.

## Common Traps

- Sharing seller urgency or buyer maximum price to move negotiation along.
- Treating unlawful client instructions as aggressive advocacy.
- Concealing known material facts under confidentiality.
- Letting commission pressure influence advice about cancellation or concessions.
- Recommending vendors because of relationships without proper disclosure.
- Giving legal, tax, construction, or investment conclusions outside competence; mishandling keys, deposits, documents, or client funds
- Assuming friendliness to both sides eliminates agency conflicts; failing to document major client instructions or risk discussions
- Forgetting that duties can continue after closing or termination; continuing old communication patterns after agency status, dual agency, or termination changes the duty landscape

## Self-Check

- Is the agency relationship and duty set clear?
- Is confidential client information protected from unnecessary disclosure?
- Are client instructions lawful and ethical before being followed?
- Is advocacy firm without misrepresentation or concealment?
- Are money, keys, documents, and property handled through proper channels?
- Are self-interest, referral, and compensation conflicts disclosed and controlled?
- Are issues outside competence routed to qualified professionals?
- Are material facts handled according to law, contract, and broker guidance?
- Are significant advice, consent, instructions, and escalations documented?
- Would the agent's conduct still look loyal and honest if the commission were removed from the analysis?; are duties, disclosures, and confidentiality practices updated when agency status changes?
