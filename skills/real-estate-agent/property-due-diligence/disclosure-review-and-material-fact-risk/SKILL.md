# Disclosure Review And Material Fact Risk

Disclosure work is where property facts, client honesty, legal duties, and transaction trust meet. Buyers rely on disclosures to decide whether to offer, inspect, renegotiate, or cancel. Sellers may not understand what matters, may minimize old problems, or may fear that disclosure will reduce price. Agents can create serious risk by completing disclosures for clients, ignoring known facts, or treating disclosure as paperwork. This skill helps the agent handle disclosure review with care and escalation.

## Core Rules

### Keep Disclosure Responsibility With The Proper Party

Sellers usually complete seller disclosures, and buyers review them. The agent can explain process, deadlines, and the importance of accuracy, but should not answer factual disclosure questions for the seller unless the agent has direct knowledge and the form requires agent input. If the seller is unsure, encourage records review, contractor input, broker guidance, or legal advice.

Do not let convenience turn into authorship. If the agent fills in facts from memory or suggestion, later disputes may become the agent's problem.

### Distinguish Known Facts From Suspicion And Opinion

Material facts may include known defects, water intrusion, roof leaks, structural issues, pest damage, fire damage, insurance claims, unpermitted work, boundary disputes, HOA violations, environmental hazards, deaths or stigmatizing events where legally relevant, tenant rights, assessments, easements, or neighborhood conditions. Requirements vary by jurisdiction.

The agent should be careful with language. "Seller knows of prior leak repaired in 2022" is different from "no water issues." If the client is unsure, say what is known and recommend proper review. Do not convert uncertainty into a false negative.

### Review Disclosures Against Other Evidence

Compare disclosures with listing history, inspection reports, repair receipts, permits, MLS remarks, seller statements, visible condition, HOA documents, title information, insurance claims if available, and prior transaction records. Inconsistencies should be resolved before they become buyer accusations.

Agents should not conduct an investigation beyond role and competence, but they should not ignore contradictions they actually notice. If the listing says "new roof" and disclosures show recurring leaks, the inconsistency matters.

### Update Disclosures When New Information Appears

Disclosures may need updating after pre-listing inspections, buyer inspections, repair bids, contractor findings, HOA notices, title issues, appraisal conditions, seller conversations, or new damage. A disclosure that was accurate at listing can become stale.

Document updates and delivery. Buyers may have rights tied to disclosure timing, and the agent should follow local forms, law, and broker direction rather than improvising.

### Avoid Concealment Through Marketing Or Staging

Marketing choices should not hide material issues. Furniture, rugs, paint, photo angles, or wording can become problematic if used to obscure defects. Attractive presentation is allowed; concealment is not.

If a known issue is visible or likely material, coordinate disclosure and marketing language carefully. When uncertain, escalate to broker or legal counsel.

### Handle Buyer Questions With Discipline

When buyers or buyer agents ask about condition, permits, repairs, neighborhood issues, or seller knowledge, answer based on verified information, disclosures, or seller-authorized statements. If unknown, say it is unknown and route the question appropriately. Do not guess to keep momentum.

For buyer-side work, help the buyer identify gaps and follow-up questions. Do not imply that a blank or "unknown" disclosure response proves absence of a problem.

### Protect Confidentiality Without Hiding Required Facts

Some seller facts are confidential strategy, while some property facts must be disclosed. A seller's motivation may be private; a known material defect may not be. The agent must not use confidentiality as an excuse to conceal facts that law, contract, or ethics require to be disclosed.

If the seller instructs the agent not to disclose something potentially material, stop and escalate. Continuing as if it is a normal negotiation issue is dangerous.

### Document Disclosure Conversations

Record when disclosures were requested, received, reviewed, updated, delivered, questioned, and corrected. Keep notes of seller instructions, broker guidance, and referrals to specialists. If a buyer chooses to proceed despite known or unresolved issues, document that decision.

Good records are not a substitute for proper disclosure, but they help show that the agent treated the process seriously.

## Common Traps

- Filling out seller disclosure answers to save time.
- Treating "unknown" as proof that no problem exists.
- Ignoring contradictions between disclosures, inspection reports, photos, and seller statements.
- Forgetting to update disclosures after inspections or new damage.
- Using staging, paint, or photography to obscure known issues.
- Guessing answers to buyer questions instead of verifying.
- Assuming disclosure rules are the same in every jurisdiction; hiding material facts under the label of seller confidentiality
- Letting the client minimize old problems that still matter; failing to document delivery, updates, and buyer acknowledgment

## Self-Check

- Is the proper party completing factual disclosures?
- Are known facts, uncertainty, and opinion kept distinct?
- Are disclosures compared against visible condition and available records for inconsistencies?
- Are new facts added through proper update and delivery process?
- Does marketing avoid concealing or contradicting disclosed issues?
- Are buyer questions answered from verified information or routed appropriately?
- Are confidentiality and required disclosure boundaries handled correctly?
- Are broker or legal escalation points used for uncertain material facts?
- Are disclosure delivery, updates, and client decisions documented?
- Would the disclosure record look honest if reviewed after a dispute?
