# Title Permit HOA And Boundary Issue Spotting

Some of the most important property risks are not visible during a showing. Title defects, easements, liens, unpermitted work, HOA restrictions, zoning limits, boundary disputes, assessments, and use restrictions can change value, financing, insurability, and whether the client can use the property as intended. Agents often treat these issues as title-company or attorney problems until late in the transaction. This skill helps the agent spot issues early and route them to the right professionals.

## Core Rules

### Ask What The Client Intends To Do With The Property

Due diligence depends on intended use. A buyer planning an addition, rental, home business, ADU, animals, parking, short-term rental, pool, fence, subdivision, commercial use, or multigenerational occupancy needs different verification than a buyer simply moving in. A seller with past renovations, boundary agreements, easement disputes, or HOA violations may need records before listing.

Do not assume ordinary residential use covers the client's plan. The agent should ask enough to know which restrictions could matter.

### Treat Title And Ownership Issues As Early Risks

Liens, judgments, unpaid taxes, probate, trusts, divorce, entity authority, unreleased mortgages, easements, encroachments, access rights, mineral rights, lease rights, restrictive covenants, and title exceptions can delay or prevent closing. Sellers may not know these issues exist until title search.

Encourage early title, escrow, attorney, or broker review when warning signs appear. A title issue discovered days before closing can collapse a transaction that could have been managed earlier.

### Verify Permit And Renovation Claims

Listing claims about additions, finished basements, converted garages, decks, electrical upgrades, plumbing, HVAC, roofs, windows, pools, septic systems, or ADUs may require permit verification. Unpermitted work can affect safety, financing, insurance, appraisal, taxes, resale, and required correction.

The agent should not guarantee permit status from seller statements alone. Ask for records, direct clients to municipal resources, and clarify uncertainty. If a seller says "it was done before we bought," that is not the same as verified approval.

### Review HOA And Condo Documents For Use Restrictions

HOA and condo rules can control rentals, pets, parking, vehicles, exterior changes, flooring, leasing caps, short-term rental, age restrictions where lawful, business use, amenities, fees, reserves, special assessments, insurance, litigation, and approval requirements. Buyers may focus on the unit and miss community obligations.

Agents should encourage timely review of governing documents, budgets, reserves, meeting minutes where available, resale certificates, and assessment notices. For sellers, unresolved violations or unpaid dues should be addressed before they surprise buyers.

### Watch Boundary, Survey, And Access Clues

Fences, driveways, sheds, walls, landscaping, shared roads, private lanes, docks, waterfront access, parking spaces, rural acreage, and steep lots can raise boundary or access questions. Public maps may not resolve them. A survey, attorney, title company, or municipality may be needed.

Do not tell clients that a fence is the property line unless verified. Boundary assumptions can become expensive disputes.

### Consider Zoning And Use Restrictions

Zoning, occupancy rules, short-term rental laws, building setbacks, historic districts, flood zones, fire zones, coastal rules, agricultural restrictions, conservation easements, septic capacity, well rules, and parking requirements can affect a client's plans. These rules change by jurisdiction and may require current verification.

The agent can identify that a use needs verification; the agent should not guarantee zoning compliance. Route clients to municipal officials, land-use counsel, surveyors, architects, or other specialists when plans depend on approval.

### Integrate Findings Into Contract And Timeline

Title, permit, HOA, and boundary issues often require contingency deadlines, document delivery, review periods, extensions, repair or cure obligations, credits, price adjustments, or cancellation rights. The agent should track deadlines and ensure clients understand whether issues can be resolved before closing.

Do not let a client waive contingencies before critical documents are reviewed. If documents are delayed, consider whether an extension or written reservation of rights is needed under local forms and legal guidance.

### Document Referrals And Unresolved Issues

When recommending title, legal, survey, municipal, HOA, or permit review, document the issue and referral. If the client declines review, document that decision. The agent should also update marketing or disclosure if new information affects material facts.

Documentation shows that the agent did not ignore non-obvious property constraints.

## Common Traps

- Assuming title, permit, HOA, or zoning issues are someone else's problem until closing.
- Relying on seller statements for permitted work without records.
- Telling buyers a fence, driveway, or parking space belongs to the property without verification.
- Ignoring HOA rental caps, pet rules, reserves, litigation, or special assessments.
- Missing use restrictions that conflict with the buyer's plans.
- Letting review deadlines expire before documents arrive.
- Treating public maps as a survey; giving legal conclusions about easements, zoning, or title exceptions
- Forgetting to update disclosures after discovering an issue; failing to document specialist referrals or client refusal to investigate

## Self-Check

- Has the client's intended use been identified?
- Are title, ownership, lien, easement, and authority issues considered early?
- Are permit and renovation claims verified or marked uncertain?
- Are HOA or condo documents requested and reviewed within deadline?
- Are boundary, survey, access, and parking assumptions checked before reliance?
- Are zoning and use restrictions routed for current verification when material?
- Are contract deadlines and contingencies aligned with document review?
- Has the agent avoided legal, survey, title, or municipal conclusions outside competence?
- Are referrals, unresolved issues, and client decisions documented?
- Do disclosures and marketing reflect any newly discovered material issues?
