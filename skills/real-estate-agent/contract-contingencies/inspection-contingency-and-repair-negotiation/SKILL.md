# Inspection Contingency And Repair Negotiation

The inspection contingency is where buyers convert uncertainty into decisions and sellers decide how much risk or cost to absorb. It can protect clients, but it can also become chaotic when every item is treated equally, deadlines are missed, repair language is vague, or emotions overtake strategy. This skill helps the agent manage inspection findings and repair negotiation as a disciplined risk decision.

## Core Rules

### Start With Contract Rights And Deadlines

Before interpreting findings, confirm the inspection period, notice requirements, repair request deadline, seller response deadline, cancellation rights, extension process, and any limits on what can be requested. Local contracts differ, and some deadlines are strict.

Do not assume the client can keep negotiating indefinitely. If more information is needed, consider whether an extension, cancellation, or written reservation of rights is required under broker or legal guidance.

### Sort Findings By Materiality

Inspection reports can be long and alarming. Sort items into categories: safety, structural, water, roof, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, pest, environmental, code or permit concern, financing or insurance issue, near-term major cost, maintenance, cosmetic, and informational. This helps clients avoid overreacting to minor notes while missing major risk.

The agent should not decide technical severity alone. Use inspector explanation, specialist review, contractor bids, and client priorities.

### Link Requests To Client Goals

Buyer requests should reflect what the buyer needs to proceed: repair before closing, credit, price reduction, seller disclosure update, specialist review, warranty, documentation, or cancellation. Sellers should evaluate whether responding preserves net, certainty, timeline, and deal quality.

Avoid asking for every minor issue by default. Overbroad requests can damage negotiation credibility. But do not minimize material findings just to protect the deal.

### Choose Repair, Credit, Or Price Change Deliberately

Repairs before closing can resolve safety or lender issues but create quality, timing, access, permit, and proof problems. Credits can let buyers control work after closing but may be limited by lender rules. Price reductions affect loan-to-value and may not help cash-strapped buyers. Escrows or holdbacks may need lender and escrow approval.

Explain the tradeoff and verify financing constraints before proposing a remedy.

### Use Clear Repair Language

If repairs are negotiated, specify the item, standard, responsible party, licensed contractor if required, permits, receipts, completion deadline, access, reinspection rights, and what happens if work is incomplete. Vague terms such as "fix roof leak" can create closing disputes.

Agents should use approved forms and broker guidance. Do not draft technical work scopes beyond competence.

### Watch Disclosure And Future Marketing Effects

For sellers, inspection findings may reveal facts that need disclosure to the current buyer or future buyers if the deal fails. A seller cannot treat a buyer inspection as irrelevant simply because the buyer cancelled. Requirements vary, so escalate unclear duties.

For buyers, inspection information should be used according to contract and confidentiality obligations. Avoid misusing reports or spreading unsupported claims.

### Coordinate Specialists Without Losing Time

Major findings may require roofers, electricians, plumbers, structural engineers, pest inspectors, sewer scopes, HVAC technicians, environmental testers, or foundation experts. Specialist availability can collide with contingency deadlines. Schedule quickly and decide what information is essential.

If the client cannot obtain enough review in time, discuss extension, narrower request, risk acceptance, or cancellation.

### Keep Emotions And Leverage In Check

Buyers may feel betrayed by defects; sellers may feel attacked by inspection requests. The agent should translate findings into terms and options, not blame. A calm, evidence-based response is more likely to preserve cooperation.

Negotiation leverage depends on market condition, backup offers, property condition, seller net, buyer commitment, and contract rights. Do not bluff cancellation if the client is unwilling to cancel.

### Tie Repairs To Final Walk-Through

If repairs are accepted, plan how completion will be verified before closing. Receipts, permits, photos, paid invoices, reinspection, utility access, and final walk-through timing should be considered before the buyer releases leverage. If work is incomplete near closing, options may include extension, credit, escrow if allowed, cancellation if rights remain, or closing with documented acceptance.

Do not let repair agreements become a surprise on closing day.

## Common Traps

- Missing inspection notice or response deadlines.
- Treating every inspection item as equally important.
- Requesting cosmetic or minor items in a way that distracts from material issues.
- Accepting vague repair promises without standard, receipts, or reinspection.
- Forgetting lender limits on credits or repair escrows.
- Minimizing safety, structural, water, pest, or insurance issues to preserve the deal; failing to update seller disclosures after new material findings
- Waiting too long to schedule specialists; letting emotional language damage negotiation
- Blustering cancellation without client authority or willingness; discovering incomplete agreed repairs during final walk-through with no time or contract leverage left

## Self-Check

- Are inspection rights, deadlines, notices, and cancellation options clear?
- Are findings sorted by materiality and client impact?
- Are requests tied to the client's ability and willingness to proceed?
- Are repair, credit, price change, escrow, and cancellation tradeoffs explained?
- Has the lender confirmed credits or repair arrangements where needed?
- Is repair language specific enough to avoid closing disputes?
- Are disclosure implications considered if material findings emerge?
- Are specialists scheduled early enough for the contingency timeline?
- Is negotiation tone evidence-based and professional?
- Are client decisions and authorizations documented before sending requests or responses?; is repair completion verification planned before final walk-through and closing?
