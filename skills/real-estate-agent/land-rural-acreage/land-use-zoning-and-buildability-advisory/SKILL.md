# Land Use Zoning And Buildability Advisory

Land transactions can look simple because there is no house to inspect, but buildability risk can be deeper than in improved property. Zoning, setbacks, access, utilities, soils, wetlands, slopes, easements, subdivision rules, conservation restrictions, and permitting capacity determine whether the buyer can use the land as intended. Agents should not give legal, engineering, surveying, or planning opinions, but must help clients identify which questions need official answers before closing.

## Core Rules

### Start With Intended Use

Buildability depends on what the buyer wants: single-family home, accessory dwelling, farming, animals, business use, subdivision, short-term rental, manufactured home, camping, storage, solar, or long-term investment. A parcel that fits one purpose may fail another.

Do not evaluate land in the abstract; evaluate it against the client's use case.

### Verify Zoning And Permitted Uses

Zoning, overlays, comprehensive plans, agricultural districts, historic rules, environmental zones, and local ordinances affect use. Online zoning maps may be outdated or hard to interpret.

Encourage buyers to confirm intended use with the planning or zoning authority, not just listing remarks.

### Check Setbacks And Building Envelope

Lot size alone does not determine buildability. Setbacks, easements, slopes, wetlands, septic area, wells, utility corridors, floodplain, buffers, and access can reduce usable area. A legal lot may have little practical building envelope.

Buyers may need survey, site plan, engineer, soil scientist, or planner input.

### Consider Subdivision And Density Limits

Buyers may assume acreage can be split or developed later. Subdivision rules, minimum lot sizes, road frontage, utility capacity, conservation limits, and approval politics may block that plan.

Future development potential should be verified before it is priced into the offer.

### Identify Environmental And Conservation Constraints

Wetlands, streams, endangered species, steep slopes, protected trees, wildfire zones, floodplain, conservation easements, farmland preservation, and habitat rules can limit clearing, grading, building, or access.

Environmental constraints can be invisible during a casual site visit.

### Review Road And Access Requirements

Legal access, physical access, road maintenance, driveway permits, emergency vehicle access, private road agreements, gates, bridges, and seasonal conditions affect buildability and lender interest.

Land without reliable access can be difficult to finance, insure, or develop.

### Ask About Permitting Timeline And Moratoria

Even where use is allowed, permits may take months or years. Utility moratoria, water rights, sewer capacity, school impact fees, road improvements, or neighbor appeals can delay projects.

Timeline risk should be part of the buyer's due diligence and offer deadlines.

### Separate Listing Claims From Official Determinations

"Buildable," "subdividable," "horse property," or "ADU possible" are not substitutes for official confirmation. Marketing language should be cautious unless supported by documents.

Agents should avoid guaranteeing use potential.

### Structure Contingencies Around Land Due Diligence

Land buyers may need longer feasibility periods for zoning, survey, soil, septic, well, environmental, access, financing, and builder review. A standard residential inspection period may be inadequate.

Offer terms should match the complexity of the land question.

### Separate Possible Use From Permitted Use

Raw land can invite imagination, but buildability depends on zoning, subdivision rules, setbacks, easements, wetlands, floodplain, slope, access, utilities, soil, septic approval, water rights, fire access, agricultural restrictions, conservation overlays, and local permitting capacity. The agent should help the buyer identify which authorities and specialists must confirm the intended use.

Marketing language such as "great homesite," "development potential," or "farm-ready" should be treated carefully unless supported by evidence. A parcel may be physically appealing but legally or economically impractical for the client's plan. The agent should connect enthusiasm to verification steps before offer deadlines remove leverage.

## Common Traps

- Confusing physically plausible use with legally permitted and economically feasible use.
- Repeating buildability claims from marketing materials without verification sources.

- Evaluating land without first defining the buyer's intended use.
- Relying on listing remarks or old maps for zoning conclusions.
- Assuming legal parcel equals practical building site.
- Ignoring setbacks, easements, wetlands, slopes, or septic area.
- Pricing subdivision potential without official feasibility.
- Missing conservation easements or agricultural restrictions.
- Overlooking legal and physical access problems.
- Underestimating permit timelines, fees, moratoria, or appeals.
- Saying land is buildable without qualified confirmation.
- Using ordinary home-inspection timelines for complex land due diligence.
- Failing to document referrals to planners, surveyors, engineers, or attorneys.

## Self-Check

- Has the client's intended use been checked against zoning, access, utilities, environmental, and permitting constraints?
- Are buildability claims supported by appropriate authorities or specialist review?

- Is the buyer's intended use clearly defined?
- Has zoning and permitted use been verified with official sources?
- Are setbacks, easements, environmental constraints, and building envelope considered?
- Is subdivision or density potential confirmed rather than assumed?
- Are wetlands, floodplain, conservation, habitat, and slope risks checked?
- Are legal access, physical access, and road maintenance reviewed?
- Are permitting timeline, fees, capacity, and moratoria considered?
- Are marketing claims separated from official determinations?
- Are contingencies long and specific enough for land due diligence?
- Would the buyer know which professionals must confirm buildability before closing?
