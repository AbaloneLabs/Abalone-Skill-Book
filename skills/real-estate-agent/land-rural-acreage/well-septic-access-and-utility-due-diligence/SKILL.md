# Well Septic Access And Utility Due Diligence

Rural and acreage properties often depend on private systems that urban buyers take for granted: wells, septic, propane, private roads, culverts, electric extensions, internet, drainage, and sometimes water rights. These systems affect habitability, financing, insurance, maintenance, and resale. Agents should not evaluate water quality, septic function, or road rights as experts, but they must help clients ask the right questions before closing. This skill supports utility and infrastructure due diligence for properties outside ordinary municipal service.

## Core Rules

### Identify All Essential Services

List water, wastewater, electricity, gas or propane, internet, phone, trash, snow removal, road maintenance, drainage, fire access, and emergency services. Determine whether each is public, private, shared, seasonal, off-grid, or unavailable.

Do not assume services exist because neighboring properties have them.

### Inspect And Test Wells Properly

Well due diligence may include location, depth, flow rate, recovery rate, pump condition, water quality, treatment systems, permits, shared-well agreements, and maintenance history. Water quality and quantity can vary by season.

Use qualified well inspectors or water professionals; agents should not interpret technical results as final.

### Evaluate Septic Function And Capacity

Septic systems may require inspection, pumping records, permits, drain field location, reserve area, bedroom capacity, repairs, or replacement feasibility. A system may function for current use but not support additions, more bedrooms, or higher occupancy.

Clients should understand both current condition and future capacity limits.

### Confirm Legal Access And Road Maintenance

Private roads, shared driveways, easements, gates, bridges, snow removal, grading, and maintenance agreements affect daily use and financing. Physical access is not the same as legal access.

Ask who maintains the road, who pays, and what happens if neighbors disagree.

### Check Utility Extension Cost

If electricity, water, sewer, gas, or internet must be extended, costs can be substantial and approval uncertain. Distance to the road or nearest line is not enough; easements, permits, trenching, poles, transformers, and provider capacity matter.

Buyers should obtain utility provider estimates before relying on future service.

### Consider Propane, Fuel, And Off-Grid Systems

Propane tanks, generators, solar, batteries, wood stoves, oil tanks, and alternative systems have ownership, lease, maintenance, safety, and delivery issues. Fuel contracts and tank ownership should be clarified.

These systems can affect insurance and inspection scope.

### Verify Internet And Cell Service

Remote work buyers may assume broadband is available. Advertised service maps may not reflect real speed, installation cost, data caps, or reliability. Cell reception can vary by carrier and season.

Internet feasibility should be tested or confirmed before contingency release if important.

### Link Utilities To Financing And Insurance

Some lenders require functioning utilities, potable water, acceptable septic, year-round access, and insurable systems. Insurance carriers may care about fire distance, road access, roof, heating type, and protection class.

Infrastructure issues can become closing issues, not just lifestyle preferences.

### Document Professional Findings And Open Items

Well, septic, road, utility, and internet findings should be tracked with reports, permits, provider responses, agreements, and client decisions. Rural due diligence often involves many small documents that together determine feasibility.

Unanswered infrastructure questions should remain visible until resolved.

### Confirm Serviceability, Not Just Existence

A well, septic system, driveway, easement, power line, or propane setup may exist but still be inadequate for the client's intended use. The agent should prompt review of flow, water quality, septic capacity and location, reserve drain field, maintenance history, access rights, utility easements, road maintenance obligations, internet availability, and seasonal conditions.

Rural utility defects can become expensive after closing because alternatives are limited. A buyer planning bedrooms, livestock, vacation rental use, a home business, or new construction may need different serviceability than the current owner. The agent should frame due diligence around future use, not only current occupancy.

## Common Traps

- Treating existing well, septic, access, or utilities as proof they are adequate for the buyer's plan.
- Ignoring seasonal access, road maintenance, internet, and reserve septic area issues.

- Assuming rural property has the same service reliability as municipal property.
- Skipping well flow, quality, or permit review.
- Treating septic inspection as optional when future use depends on capacity.
- Confusing physical driveway use with legal access rights.
- Underestimating utility extension cost and approval time.
- Ignoring road maintenance agreements and neighbor cost sharing.
- Forgetting propane, fuel tank, generator, or off-grid system obligations.
- Relying on internet coverage maps without confirmation.
- Missing lender or insurance requirements tied to utilities and access.
- Failing to gather permits, agreements, and inspection reports before deadlines.
- Treating seasonal access or weather limitations as minor inconvenience.

## Self-Check

- Are well, septic, access, utilities, and internet evaluated for serviceability under the client's intended use?
- Have maintenance obligations, seasonal conditions, and reserve capacity been checked?

- Are water, wastewater, power, fuel, internet, road, drainage, and emergency access identified?
- Are well quantity, quality, permits, and equipment reviewed by qualified professionals?
- Is septic condition, capacity, permit status, and replacement feasibility addressed?
- Are legal access and road maintenance obligations clear?
- Are utility extension costs and approvals verified by providers?
- Are propane, fuel, generator, solar, or off-grid systems understood?
- Is internet and cell service confirmed if important to the client?
- Are lender and insurance requirements tied to rural infrastructure considered?
- Are reports, permits, agreements, and open questions tracked?
- Would the buyer understand the true operating obligations of the property?
