# Investor Tax Advisory Boundaries And Referral

Real estate investment decisions are tax-sensitive, but agents are not tax advisors. Casual statements about write-offs, depreciation, capital gains, entity ownership, rent deductions, or exchange benefits can mislead clients and create liability. At the same time, agents should recognize when tax issues are material enough to affect offer strategy, timing, and professional referrals. This skill helps the agent support investor decision-making without stepping into tax practice.

## Core Rules

### State The Boundary Clearly

Tell clients that tax consequences depend on their personal situation and must be confirmed by a CPA, enrolled agent, tax attorney, or qualified advisor. The agent can identify topics to ask about, not provide conclusions.

This boundary should be explicit before clients rely on projected after-tax returns.

### Identify Tax-Sensitive Deal Features

Capital gains, depreciation, depreciation recapture, passive activity rules, mortgage interest, property taxes, repairs versus improvements, cost segregation, short-term rental taxes, entity ownership, transfer taxes, and local fees may affect net outcome.

The agent should flag the issue when it is transaction-relevant.

### Avoid Marketing Tax Benefits As Guaranteed

Statements like "you can write this off" or "this qualifies for 1031" are risky. Eligibility and treatment depend on law, use, holding period, taxpayer status, documentation, and advisor interpretation.

Use cautious language: "ask your tax advisor whether this may apply."

### Coordinate Timing With Advisors

Tax planning may affect closing date, exchange deadlines, sale timing, installment sale terms, entity formation, purchase allocation, or repair timing. Last-minute referrals may be too late.

Encourage tax review before contract terms are locked.

### Separate Pre-Tax And After-Tax Analysis

Agents may discuss rent, expenses, debt service, cap rate, cash flow, and market assumptions, but after-tax return should come from client advisors. If the investor compares properties after tax, the assumptions should be clearly sourced.

Do not let generic investor math imply tax advice.

### Watch Local And Use-Based Taxes

Short-term rental occupancy taxes, business licenses, transfer taxes, mansion taxes, vacancy taxes, rent-registration fees, and local investor restrictions can materially affect returns. These may vary by city or county.

Local rules should be checked before a strategy is marketed as profitable.

### Consider Entity And Liability Questions

Investors may ask whether to buy through an LLC, trust, partnership, corporation, or personal name. This affects tax, lending, insurance, liability, privacy, and title. The agent should coordinate with professionals, not recommend structures.

Ownership form is not a simple transaction preference.

### Document Referrals And Assumption Sources

When investor analysis includes tax-sensitive assumptions, document that figures are estimates, identify data sources, and record referrals. If the client declines tax review, note the decision.

Clear documentation distinguishes market advice from tax advice.

### Revisit Tax Questions When Use Changes

A property may shift from personal use to rental, long-term rental to short-term rental, primary residence to investment, or hold to flip. Use changes can alter tax, financing, insurance, and local compliance.

Ask clients to revisit advisors when their intended use changes.

### Give Numbers Without Becoming The Tax Advisor

Investor clients need estimates of rent, expenses, cap rate, cash flow, appreciation scenarios, depreciation questions, local taxes, transfer taxes, and exit consequences. The agent can help organize assumptions and market inputs, but should clearly separate real estate analysis from tax advice. "Ask your CPA" is not enough if the agent has already made tax claims in the spreadsheet.

Referral discipline matters. When tax treatment could drive the decision, the agent should encourage review before contingency removal or closing, not after. The client should know which assumptions are agent-provided market estimates and which require CPA, attorney, exchange accommodator, or financial advisor confirmation.

## Common Traps

- Putting tax conclusions into investor analysis and then disclaiming responsibility afterward.
- Waiting until after contingency removal to involve CPA, counsel, or exchange professionals.

- Saying a client can deduct, depreciate, or exchange without advisor confirmation.
- Advertising tax benefits as part of property value.
- Producing after-tax return projections without tax advisor input.
- Waiting until after contract signing to raise tax timing questions.
- Ignoring local occupancy, transfer, vacancy, or investor taxes.
- Recommending LLC or entity ownership without legal and tax advice.
- Treating repairs and improvements as the same for tax purposes.
- Assuming short-term rental tax treatment is the same as long-term rental.
- Failing to document tax referrals.
- Letting client sophistication justify casual tax conclusions.
- Forgetting to revisit tax questions when intended use changes.

## Self-Check

- Are market assumptions separated from tax conclusions in investor materials and conversations?
- Are tax professionals involved before deadlines or contingency decisions make advice too late?

- Has the agent clearly stated they are not providing tax advice?
- Are tax-sensitive deal features identified and referred?
- Are tax benefits described as questions for advisors, not guarantees?
- Are tax timing issues raised before contract terms are locked?
- Are pre-tax and after-tax analyses separated?
- Are local and use-based taxes considered?
- Are entity ownership questions referred to legal and tax professionals?
- Are assumptions and referrals documented?
- Are tax questions revisited if property use changes?
- Would the client know which tax conclusions require professional confirmation?
