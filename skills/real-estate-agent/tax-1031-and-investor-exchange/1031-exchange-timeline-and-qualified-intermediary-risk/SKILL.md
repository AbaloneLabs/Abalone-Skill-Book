# 1031 Exchange Timeline And Qualified Intermediary Risk

A 1031 exchange can preserve tax deferral, but it is deadline-driven and unforgiving. Agents should never give tax advice, yet they often influence search timing, contract deadlines, identification strategy, and closing coordination. A missed qualified intermediary step or identification deadline can destroy the exchange even if the real estate deal closes. This skill helps the agent support exchange logistics while keeping tax and legal advice with qualified professionals.

## Core Rules

### Establish Advisory Boundaries Immediately

The agent should not determine eligibility, tax basis, boot, related-party rules, holding period, depreciation recapture, or exchange structure. The client needs a tax advisor, attorney, and qualified intermediary before relying on an exchange strategy.

The agent's role is transaction coordination and risk spotting.

### Involve The Qualified Intermediary Before Closing

Exchange funds generally must not be received or controlled by the taxpayer. The qualified intermediary must be in place before the relinquished property closes. Late setup can destroy the exchange.

Ask early whether the exchange team is engaged and whether escrow instructions reflect the exchange.

### Track The 45-Day And 180-Day Deadlines

Identification and exchange completion deadlines are strict and typically run from sale of the relinquished property. Weekends, holidays, market scarcity, lender delays, and inspection problems do not casually extend them.

Build search and offer strategy around deadline reality.

### Clarify Identification Strategy

Investors may identify one or more replacement properties under technical rules. The agent should not advise which rule applies, but should help the client understand that backup properties and documentation timing matter.

A single fragile target can put the exchange at risk if inspection, appraisal, title, or seller issues arise.

### Coordinate Sale And Purchase Contracts

Exchange language, assignment to intermediary, closing dates, extensions, financing, contingencies, and seller cooperation should be coordinated with the exchange team. Some counterparties may not understand exchange paperwork.

Contract logistics should support the tax structure, not surprise it.

### Watch Financing And Debt Replacement

Debt levels, cash reinvestment, loan approval, appraisal, and lender timing can affect exchange outcomes and purchasing power. Exact tax effects belong to advisors, but transaction feasibility belongs in the agent's planning.

Encourage lender and tax coordination before the client identifies replacement properties.

### Consider Property Fit Beyond Deadline Pressure

Exchange urgency can push investors into poor assets, weak leases, hidden repairs, or bad locations just to meet deadlines. Tax deferral is not a substitute for investment quality.

Help clients preserve due diligence discipline even under exchange pressure.

### Plan For Failed Exchange Scenarios

The client should understand what happens if no replacement closes, a seller defaults, financing fails, or identified property becomes unacceptable. Tax and legal advisors should explain consequences, but the agent can plan real estate alternatives.

Contingency planning reduces panic decisions.

### Document Referrals And Timing Alerts

Record when the client was advised to consult tax counsel, engage a qualified intermediary, and track deadlines. Keep timeline reminders visible without representing that the agent controls tax compliance.

Exchange files need clear records because deadline mistakes are costly.

### Protect Deadlines Without Interpreting Tax Eligibility

In a 1031 exchange, the agent should respect the 45-day identification and 180-day exchange timeline, but should not decide tax eligibility, related-party consequences, debt replacement, boot, entity structure, or constructive receipt issues. Those belong with tax counsel, CPA, and the qualified intermediary.

The agent can still add value by aligning property search, offer timelines, contingency dates, closing coordination, identification backup options, and document routing with the exchange calendar. Missing a deadline or mishandling funds can destroy the client's tax plan even when the property choice is sound.

## Common Traps

- Treating 1031 eligibility as an agent judgment rather than a tax and QI determination.
- Letting offer, closing, or identification timelines drift without respect to exchange deadlines.

- Letting the client mention 1031 late without an intermediary in place.
- Giving tax opinions about eligibility, boot, or basis.
- Treating 45-day and 180-day deadlines as flexible.
- Relying on one replacement property with no backup strategy.
- Forgetting exchange assignment language and escrow coordination.
- Ignoring lender timing and debt replacement issues.
- Letting tax deferral pressure override property due diligence.
- Assuming every counterparty understands exchange documents.
- Failing to plan for failed identification or failed closing.
- Missing deadline documentation and referral records.
- Treating exchange coordination as ordinary investor search.

## Self-Check

- Are exchange deadlines integrated into search, offer, contingency, and closing coordination?
- Are tax eligibility, boot, debt, entity, and related-party questions routed to CPA, counsel, or QI?

- Are tax and legal questions referred immediately?
- Is the qualified intermediary engaged before relinquished-property closing?
- Are 45-day and 180-day deadlines tracked visibly?
- Is identification strategy coordinated with qualified advisors?
- Do sale and purchase contracts support exchange logistics?
- Are financing and debt replacement feasibility considered with lender and tax advisors?
- Is replacement property quality still being evaluated despite deadline pressure?
- Are failed-exchange scenarios discussed with qualified advisors?
- Are referrals and timing alerts documented?
- Would the investor understand which exchange tasks the agent can and cannot handle?
