# Repair Request Prioritization And Credit Strategy

Inspection negotiation can derail a transaction when clients treat every defect as a demand or dismiss serious issues as normal. The agent's job is to help clients prioritize risk, cost, safety, financing, insurance, and leverage while leaving technical conclusions to qualified professionals. Repair strategy should be connected to the contract, market, lender rules, and client goals. This skill helps the agent turn inspection findings into a focused negotiation rather than a chaotic list.

## Core Rules

### Sort Findings By Consequence

Separate safety hazards, active leaks, structural concerns, major systems, pest or environmental issues, lender-required repairs, insurance barriers, permit questions, maintenance items, cosmetic issues, and buyer preferences. Consequence matters more than report length.

Requests should focus on issues that affect value, safety, insurability, habitability, or the client's stated priorities.

### Understand Contract And Local Practice

Inspection contingency rights, repair request forms, cancellation rights, notice deadlines, seller response time, credits, repair caps, and as-is language vary. Some contracts allow broad objection; others limit requests.

Do not assume the client can demand any repair without understanding contract framework and legal boundaries.

### Compare Repairs, Credits, And Price Changes

Repairs before closing may solve lender or safety issues but create quality, timing, and reinspection risk. Credits may let the buyer control work but may be limited by lender rules and cash-to-close structure. Price reductions may help long-term cost but not immediate cash for repairs.

Choose the concession type based on financing, timing, control, and practical use of funds.

### Get Qualified Cost Input

Inspection reports often do not provide reliable repair estimates. Contractor, roofer, plumber, electrician, engineer, pest, mold, sewer, HVAC, or foundation input may be needed before making a demand.

Avoid negotiating large concessions based only on guesswork unless the client knowingly accepts uncertainty.

### Consider Seller Leverage And Market Context

In a strong seller market, broad repair requests may risk deal collapse. In a slower market, inspection findings may justify significant concessions. Seller motivation, backup offers, property history, disclosure exposure, and comparable condition matter.

Repair strategy should be realistic, not merely ideal.

### Avoid Turning Inspection Into A Renegotiation Of Known Condition

If condition was obvious or already priced in, the seller may resist repair demands. That does not prevent negotiation, but the client should understand that inspection is not always a blank check to reprice the deal.

Focus on surprises, material defects, and risks not apparent during offer.

### Check Financing And Insurance Limits

Some credits cannot be used or may affect loan approval. Some repairs must be completed before closing. Insurance may require roof, electrical, plumbing, or hazard fixes. Escrow holdbacks may need lender approval.

Coordinate with lender and insurer before finalizing repair terms.

### Specify Work Standards

If seller repairs are agreed, define scope, permits, licensed contractor requirements, receipts, warranties, completion deadline, reinspection rights, and what happens if work is incomplete. Vague repair clauses create final-walkthrough conflict.

The more important the repair, the more precise the agreement should be.

### Keep Client Emotion From Driving Bad Terms

Inspection findings can make buyers feel betrayed and sellers feel attacked. Help clients return to practical choices: accept, negotiate, investigate, credit, repair, reduce price, extend, or cancel.

Good repair negotiation preserves client interests without turning every issue into a personal conflict.

### Prioritize By Closing Risk And Ownership Impact

Repair negotiations should distinguish safety, habitability, structural, financing-required, insurance-required, code, cosmetic, maintenance, and preference items. The agent should help the client focus on issues that affect value, use, insurability, loan approval, or near-term ownership cost rather than sending an unfocused list that weakens credibility.

Credits, price reductions, seller repairs, escrow holdbacks, warranties, or accepting as-is each carry different risks. Seller repairs can be rushed or low quality; credits may be limited by lender rules; price reductions may not solve cash needed after closing. The strategy should fit the client's cash position, lender constraints, timeline, and tolerance for post-closing work.

## Common Traps

- Treating every inspection finding as equal instead of prioritizing by safety, financing, insurance, value, and cash impact.
- Asking for credits or repairs without checking lender limits, timeline, and post-closing cash needs.

- Sending the entire inspection report as a repair demand without prioritization.
- Treating cosmetic or visible issues the same as safety or system defects.
- Asking for credits that lender rules will not allow.
- Demanding seller repairs without specifying quality, permits, receipts, or reinspection.
- Negotiating large amounts without contractor input.
- Ignoring market leverage and backup-offer risk.
- Using inspection to renegotiate conditions that were already obvious and priced.
- Forgetting insurance or financing consequences of defects.
- Letting client anger create unrealistic demands.
- Failing to document why the client accepted, waived, or declined repairs.
- Assuming a seller-completed repair will be satisfactory without verification.

## Self-Check

- Are repair requests prioritized by closing risk and ownership impact rather than volume?
- Does the chosen remedy fit lender rules, cash needs, timing, and repair-quality risk?

- Are inspection findings sorted by consequence and client priorities?
- Are contract rights, deadlines, and local repair practice understood?
- Are repair, credit, and price-reduction options compared for practical effect?
- Is qualified cost input obtained where stakes are material?
- Does the request account for seller leverage and market context?
- Are known versus surprise conditions distinguished?
- Are lender and insurance limits checked before final terms?
- Are seller repair obligations specific enough to verify?
- Is client emotion managed toward practical decision-making?
- Are decisions, waivers, and professional inputs documented?
