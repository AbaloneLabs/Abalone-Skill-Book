# Rental Income Expense And Cash Flow Review

Investment property analysis can look precise because it uses numbers, but many inputs are estimates, seller claims, or optimistic assumptions. Rent, vacancy, repairs, taxes, insurance, HOA, utilities, management, financing, and capital reserves can change the outcome dramatically. Agents often repeat cap rates or pro formas without testing whether they reflect real operating conditions. This skill helps the agent discuss cash flow responsibly and route financial conclusions to qualified advisors.

## Core Rules

### Separate Actuals, Seller Claims, And Pro Forma

Identify which numbers come from leases, rent roll, bank records, tax returns, property management statements, utility bills, insurance quotes, tax records, repair invoices, or seller estimates. Then separate projected market rent, future expense reductions, renovation upside, and occupancy assumptions.

Do not blend actual and projected figures without labeling them. A pro forma is a scenario, not evidence of current performance.

### Review Gross Rent Quality

Rent quality depends on signed leases, payment history, arrears, concessions, deposits, lease expiration dates, rent control, subsidy programs, tenant rights, and market comparability. A high listed rent may not be collectible or renewable. Month-to-month tenants, under-market rents, or nonpaying tenants can change value.

Ask for rent roll, leases, ledgers, estoppels where appropriate, and local rent rules. Do not assume current rent can immediately rise to market.

### Include Real Operating Expenses

Operating expenses may include property taxes, insurance, HOA, utilities, repairs, maintenance, landscaping, pest, property management, leasing fees, vacancy, legal, accounting, licenses, permits, local taxes, trash, security, capital reserves, and replacements. Some expenses are owner-paid; others may be tenant-paid depending on lease.

Seller expense statements may omit self-managed labor, deferred maintenance, capital expenses, or future tax reassessment. Normalize expenses carefully and recommend accountant review.

### Account For Financing And Cash Timing

Cash flow depends on down payment, interest rate, loan fees, amortization, reserves, closing costs, renovation costs, insurance, taxes, and timing of rent collection. A property can have positive net operating income but negative cash flow after debt service.

Do not quote returns without financing assumptions. Cash-on-cash return, cap rate, and monthly cash flow answer different questions.

### Build Vacancy And Reserve Assumptions

Vacancy is not only empty months. It includes turnover, leasing time, concessions, cleaning, repainting, repairs, advertising, and bad debt. Reserves should account for roof, HVAC, appliances, plumbing, flooring, exterior, code updates, and unexpected issues.

Optimistic analysis often fails because it treats every month as fully rented and every repair as minor.

### Consider Management Reality

Self-management can improve cash flow on paper but requires time, legal knowledge, tenant communication, emergency response, bookkeeping, leasing, and maintenance coordination. Professional management reduces time burden but costs money and still requires oversight.

Ask whether the client wants an investment or a part-time operating job. Management fit affects property suitability.

### Avoid Tax And Investment Advice Overreach

Clients may ask about depreciation, passive losses, 1031 exchange, entity structure, deductions, capital gains, or return targets. The agent can flag that these affect investment outcome, but should route to CPA, attorney, lender, or financial advisor.

Do not present tax benefits or appreciation as guaranteed. Real estate investing carries loss risk.

### Present Sensitivity Scenarios

Show how outcomes change if rent is lower, vacancy is higher, repairs are larger, taxes reassess, insurance rises, interest rates change, or management costs more. Sensitivity analysis is more honest than one optimistic return figure.

If the property only works under best-case assumptions, say so.

### Verify Insurance And Tax Reassessment Exposure

Investment properties can face different insurance underwriting, landlord policy costs, short-term rental exclusions, flood or fire premiums, and post-sale tax reassessment. These costs may be materially higher than the seller's current expenses.

Encourage current insurance quotes and tax reassessment review before the client relies on cash flow. A property that looks profitable under seller costs may fail under buyer costs.

## Common Traps

- Repeating seller pro forma as if it were actual performance.
- Ignoring rent arrears, concessions, lease expirations, or rent restrictions.
- Omitting vacancy, repairs, management, capital reserves, and turnover costs.
- Comparing cap rates without checking expense assumptions.
- Treating self-management as free.
- Forgetting tax reassessment and insurance increases after purchase; quoting cash flow without financing assumptions
- Promising appreciation, rent growth, or tax benefits; ignoring tenant rights that limit rent changes or possession
- Failing to recommend CPA, attorney, lender, or property manager review; using seller tax and insurance costs without checking buyer-specific post-closing costs

## Self-Check

- Are actuals, seller claims, and pro forma projections separated?
- Are rents supported by leases, rent roll, ledgers, and market evidence?
- Are operating expenses complete and normalized?
- Are financing, closing costs, renovation costs, and debt service included?
- Are vacancy, turnover, bad debt, and capital reserves modeled?
- Is management cost and effort realistic?
- Are tax, legal, and investment conclusions routed to qualified advisors?
- Are sensitivity scenarios shown for rent, vacancy, repairs, taxes, insurance, and rates?
- Are tenant rights and rent restrictions considered?
- Would the client understand the downside, not only the advertised return?; are insurance quotes and tax reassessment exposure checked for the buyer's use?
