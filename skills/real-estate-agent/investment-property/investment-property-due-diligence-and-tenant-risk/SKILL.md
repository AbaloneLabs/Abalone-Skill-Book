# Investment Property Due Diligence And Tenant Risk

Tenant-occupied property carries two assets: the real estate and the income stream. The income stream may be weaker than advertised if leases are incomplete, tenants are delinquent, deposits are mishandled, rent increases are restricted, or maintenance is deferred. Agents often focus on cap rate and purchase price while missing tenant rights and operational handoff. This skill helps the agent structure investment property due diligence around leases, occupants, and income reliability.

## Core Rules

### Review Leases And Occupancy Evidence

Ask for signed leases, amendments, renewals, rent roll, ledgers, deposits, move-in condition records, notices, side agreements, pet agreements, parking, storage, utility allocations, and tenant contact information where appropriate. Compare documents against seller statements and advertised income.

Do not rely only on a rent roll summary. Lease terms control income, possession, and obligations.

### Confirm Rent Collection And Delinquency

Actual rent collection matters more than scheduled rent. Review payment history, arrears, concessions, partial payments, payment plans, pending disputes, security deposit application, and any nonpaying occupants. A tenant paying below market but consistently may be lower risk than a tenant listed at high rent but delinquent.

If records are incomplete, label income uncertainty. Do not assume full collection.

### Understand Tenant Rights And Restrictions

Rent control, just-cause eviction, notice requirements, relocation payments, source-of-income protections, lease renewal rights, subsidy programs, local registration, habitability claims, and moratorium or emergency rules may affect value and possession. These rules vary and change.

Agents should flag legal review. Do not promise that a buyer can raise rent, remove tenants, convert use, or occupy immediately without verification.

### Use Estoppels Or Tenant Confirmations Where Appropriate

Tenant estoppels or confirmations can verify rent, deposits, lease terms, disputes, repairs, concessions, and side agreements. They are especially useful for multi-unit or commercial properties, but local practice varies.

If estoppels are unavailable, understand the risk. Seller records and tenant reality may differ.

### Review Maintenance And Habitability History

Deferred maintenance, repeated complaints, code violations, pest issues, water intrusion, safety problems, appliance failures, and open repair requests affect both property condition and tenant relations. A buyer inheriting unresolved habitability issues may inherit immediate cost and conflict.

Tie maintenance review to inspections, reserves, rent assumptions, and legal counsel where needed.

### Verify Deposits And Prorations

Security deposits, pet deposits where lawful, prepaid rent, last month's rent, interest, transfer obligations, and proration at closing must be accurate. Mishandled deposits can create liability for the new owner.

Coordinate with escrow, property manager, attorney, and seller to ensure deposits and ledgers transfer correctly.

### Plan Property Management Handoff

At closing, the buyer may need leases, keys, deposits, ledgers, tenant notices, maintenance records, vendor contracts, warranties, utility accounts, rent payment instructions, emergency contacts, and property management agreements. Tenants need to know where to pay and how to request repairs.

Income can be disrupted if handoff is sloppy. The agent should help coordinate without becoming the property manager unless engaged.

### Reconcile Tenant Risk With Price And Terms

Tenant issues may justify price adjustment, seller cure, escrow, credit, extension, cancellation, or different financing assumptions. Do not let the buyer proceed using clean-income assumptions after discovering tenant risk.

The investment thesis should be updated when lease or tenant facts change.

### Check Transfer And Licensing Requirements and confirm Tenant Notice Requirements

Some rental properties require landlord registration, rental licenses, business licenses, inspections, habitability certificates, short-term rental permits, subsidy approvals, or property management transitions. These may not transfer automatically to the buyer.

Ask what must be reissued, inspected, notified, or approved after closing. Income may be interrupted if the buyer cannot legally continue the same rental operation immediately.

Ownership changes may require notices about new owner, rent payment address, deposit holder, property manager, emergency contact, or insurance-related access. Missing tenant notices can disrupt rent collection and maintenance response immediately after closing.

## Common Traps

- Accepting rent roll as complete proof of income.
- Ignoring arrears, concessions, side agreements, or oral promises.
- Assuming tenants can be removed or rents raised immediately.
- Forgetting rent control, registration, notice, or subsidy rules.
- Skipping tenant estoppels where they are customary or useful; underestimating habitability complaints and code violations
- Failing to transfer deposits and prepaid rent accurately; closing without management handoff plan
- Treating tenant disputes as seller drama rather than investment risk; failing to update valuation after tenant facts change
- Assuming rental licenses, registrations, subsidy approvals, or short-term rental permissions transfer automatically; forgetting tenant notices needed after ownership or management changes

## Self-Check

- Are signed leases, amendments, rent roll, ledgers, and deposits reviewed?
- Is actual rent collection separated from scheduled rent?
- Are tenant rights, rent restrictions, notice rules, and possession limits flagged for legal review?
- Are estoppels or tenant confirmations considered where useful?
- Are maintenance, code, and habitability issues included in risk assessment?
- Are deposits, prepaid rent, and prorations verified for transfer?
- Is property management handoff planned?
- Are tenant facts reconciled with valuation, financing, and offer terms?
- Has the agent avoided promising rent increases, vacancy, or immediate possession?; would the buyer understand the income stream as it actually exists?
- Are rental licenses, registrations, permits, and management transitions checked for transfer risk?; are tenant notices for ownership, payment, deposits, and management transition planned?
