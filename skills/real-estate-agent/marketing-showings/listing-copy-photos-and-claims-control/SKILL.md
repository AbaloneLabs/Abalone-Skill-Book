# Listing Copy Photos And Claims Control

Real estate marketing must attract attention without creating false expectations or compliance risk. Listing copy, photos, video, floor plans, maps, and social posts become evidence of what the agent and seller represented. Agents often polish language until it overstates facts, hides defects, or implies unlawful preferences. This skill helps the agent market property persuasively while keeping claims accurate, sourced, and fair.

## Core Rules

### Describe Verifiable Property Features

Use specific, supportable facts: layout, finishes, appliances, parking, outdoor space, storage, lot characteristics, view direction, transit proximity, HOA amenities, building services, recent updates, and included items. When a fact comes from a source, know the source: tax record, survey, builder plan, permit, HOA document, seller disclosure, appraisal, prior MLS, or measurement service.

Avoid presenting uncertain facts as verified. If square footage, lot size, bedroom count, school assignment, permit status, rental eligibility, or renovation date is uncertain, use proper source attribution or recommend verification.

### Avoid Exaggerated Or Absolute Claims

Words such as "new," "fully renovated," "waterproof," "legal unit," "guaranteed income," "best schools," "safe neighborhood," "unobstructed forever views," or "turnkey" can be risky if not strictly true. Even common marketing phrases may imply warranties or facts the seller cannot support.

Use balanced language. A property can be appealing without promising permanence, legality, safety, income, or future value.

### Align Photos With Reality

Photos should present the property well but not materially mislead. Avoid deceptive editing, lens distortion that changes room perception, hiding obvious defects, mislabeling rooms, using outdated photos after condition changes, or showing amenities not actually available. Virtual staging should be disclosed where required or appropriate.

Image order matters. If the property has a major condition issue, unusual access, or small room, do not create a marketing path that guarantees buyer disappointment.

### Control Fair Housing And Audience Language

Marketing should describe the property, not preferred people. Avoid copy that implies preference or exclusion by family status, age, disability, religion, national origin, race, sex, or locally protected categories. "Perfect for young professionals," "ideal for families," or "walk to church" can create risk depending on context and law.

Use feature-based language: number of rooms, office nook, nearby transit, accessible entry, playground proximity, or flexible floor plan. Verify current local advertising rules when in doubt.

### Treat AI And Automation As Drafting Aids, Not Authority

AI-generated copy, auto-filled MLS fields, photo captions, and platform descriptions can hallucinate features or introduce discriminatory wording. Review every generated claim against source material. Do not let automation create square footage, school, amenity, or renovation claims.

The agent owns the final marketing output even if a tool drafted it.

### Check MLS And Platform Rules

MLS fields, public remarks, private remarks, coming-soon status, photo rules, compensation fields, showing instructions, branded content, floor plans, virtual tours, and off-market promotion may have specific rules. Portals and social platforms may have advertising and targeting constraints.

Compliant marketing is not only truthful language. It must also follow distribution rules.

### Update Marketing When Facts Change

If repairs occur, damage appears, a price changes, a feature is removed, tenant access changes, HOA information updates, school assignment is corrected, or a disclosure issue emerges, update marketing promptly. Stale claims can mislead later buyers even if they were accurate at launch.

Do not leave old social posts, flyers, or syndication text uncorrected when a material claim changes and the platform is still active.

### Preserve Review Evidence

Keep records of approved listing copy, source documents, seller approvals, photo licenses, virtual staging disclosures, measurement sources, and change history. If a buyer later claims they relied on a statement, the file should show why the agent believed it was accurate.

Marketing review is a compliance control, not just brand polish.

## Common Traps

- Copying prior MLS remarks without checking whether facts are still true.
- Calling renovations, additions, bedrooms, rental units, or square footage legal without records.
- Using photo edits, wide lenses, or virtual staging in ways that mislead.
- Advertising school, safety, demographics, or neighborhood identity carelessly.
- Letting AI generate unsupported claims.
- Forgetting MLS, portal, or social ad rules.
- Leaving stale marketing active after facts change; overstating views, income, zoning, accessibility, or future development protection
- Using seller-provided claims without verification or source attribution; treating private remarks as a place to hide improper statements

## Self-Check

- Are listing claims specific, verifiable, and source-aware?
- Are uncertain facts attributed or marked for buyer verification?
- Does copy avoid guarantees, absolutes, and unsupported legal or income claims?
- Do photos and media present the property attractively without materially misleading?
- Is fair housing risk removed from copy, images, targeting, and audience language?
- Are AI-generated or auto-filled claims manually verified?
- Are MLS, portal, social, and brokerage marketing rules followed?
- Is marketing updated when property facts, price, access, or disclosures change?
- Are seller approvals, source documents, media rights, and revisions preserved?
- Would the marketing still look truthful in a dispute after closing?
