# Roadmap To Plan Translation

A roadmap describes what the product intends to achieve and why; a team plan describes the specific work, sequenced and resourced, that will pursue those outcomes. The translation between them is where strategy meets execution, and it is where many strategies quietly fail. The roadmap may be sound, but if the team plan does not faithfully pursue its outcomes, the strategy is irrelevant. Done well, translation produces team plans that accumulate toward roadmap goals, with every initiative traceable to a strategic intent. Done poorly, the team executes diligently on work that does not move the roadmap, or the roadmap becomes a document the team never references. Agents often produce team plans that list work without connecting it to roadmap themes, severing the link between strategy and execution.

The harm this skill prevents is the disconnect where strategy and execution live in separate worlds. The roadmap is presented to leadership; the team plan is what actually gets built; and the two bear little relationship to each other. The result is a product that ships steadily without moving toward anything strategic.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how do we turn the roadmap into a plan", "how do we break roadmap themes into work", "does our plan support our strategy", or "how do we keep execution aligned with roadmap". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a team plan that has no traceable connection to roadmap outcomes.

## Core Rules

### Decompose Roadmap Themes Into Outcome-Linked Initiatives

A roadmap theme is usually a strategic outcome stated at a high level. Translation begins by decomposing each theme into specific initiatives that, if delivered, would advance that outcome. Each initiative should have a clear hypothesis about how it contributes to the theme's outcome, not just a description of work. The decomposition forces the team to think about what specific efforts would actually move the strategic needle, rather than defaulting to whatever work was already planned.

For each initiative, state the roadmap theme it serves and the contribution it is expected to make. If an initiative cannot be connected to a theme, either the roadmap is missing something or the initiative is not strategic and should be questioned. This traceability is the core discipline of translation; without it, the plan and the roadmap drift apart immediately.

### Sequence Initiatives To Build Cumulative Value

The order in which initiatives are executed matters as much as which initiatives are chosen. Some initiatives create foundations that others depend on; some produce early value that validates the direction; some are only worth doing if earlier ones succeed. Sequencing is the art of ordering initiatives so that each builds on the last and the cumulative effect moves toward the roadmap outcome, rather than producing a set of disconnected deliverables.

Consider dependencies, validation value, and risk in sequencing. Deliver initiatives that reduce uncertainty early, so that later work is built on validated assumptions rather than guesses. Deliver foundational initiatives before those that depend on them. Avoid sequencing that front-loads low-value work or back-loads all the value, because both erode the team's ability to learn and adapt.

### Preserve The Outcome Focus, Do Not Collapse Into Feature Lists

The most common translation failure is collapsing roadmap outcomes into a list of features to build, losing the connection to the outcome the features were meant to achieve. Once the plan becomes a feature list, the team optimizes for shipping features rather than for moving the outcome, and the roadmap intent is lost. Preserve the outcome focus by keeping the hypothesis and the target metric visible in every initiative, so that the team is always aware of what success means beyond delivery.

Reframe feature requests as hypotheses about how to move the outcome. A feature is a means; the outcome is the end. When the team holds both in view, it can adapt the means when the end is not being served, rather than dutifully shipping a feature that does not move the metric.

### Build Validation Points Into The Translation

Roadmap themes rest on assumptions that may prove wrong during execution. Build validation points into the plan: moments where the team checks whether the initiative is producing the expected movement toward the outcome, before committing to the full build. These checkpoints let the team adapt based on evidence rather than executing a plan that was based on assumptions that no longer hold.

A plan without validation points commits the team to full delivery of every initiative regardless of results. A plan with validation points lets the team double down on what works, adjust what is partially working, and stop what is not, redirecting capacity toward higher-value work. The validation points are what keep execution honest relative to the roadmap's outcomes.

### Account For Capacity And Dependencies In The Translation

A faithful translation must be executable within real capacity and respect real dependencies. A plan that pursues every roadmap theme simultaneously will overload the team and deliver nothing well. A plan that ignores dependencies will stall when an initiative cannot proceed without unfinished foundational work. The translation must allocate initiatives across available capacity, sequence around dependencies, and make explicit tradeoffs when capacity is insufficient for all themes.

This often requires prioritization feedback to the roadmap: if the themes cannot all be pursued within capacity, the roadmap itself may need to be sharpened. The translation is not a one-way flow from roadmap to plan; it is a dialogue, where the realities of execution inform the roadmap's priorities.

### Maintain A Feedback Loop From Execution Back To Roadmap

The translation is not complete when the plan is written; it continues as the team executes and learns. Results from execution must flow back to the roadmap, updating it when initiatives succeed, fail, or reveal that a theme needs revision. Without this feedback loop, the roadmap becomes static while execution evolves, and the two diverge. The product manager's job is to keep the connection alive in both directions: roadmap shapes plan, and plan results reshape roadmap.

Build a regular review where execution results are assessed against roadmap outcomes, and where the roadmap is updated based on what was learned. The roadmap that never changes has become disconnected from reality; the roadmap that changes constantly provides no direction. The right cadence keeps it honest and stable.

## Common Traps

### Plan Disconnected From Roadmap Themes

Team work that cannot be traced to any strategic outcome. The trap is diligent execution that does not move the product toward anything.

### Collapse Into Feature Lists

Losing the outcome focus once work is decomposed. The trap is shipping features without moving the metric the roadmap targeted.

### Sequencing That Front-Loads Low Value

Ordering initiatives so that value arrives only at the end. The trap is a team that cannot validate direction and cannot adapt.

### No Validation Points

Committing to full delivery regardless of results. The trap is completing initiatives that do not move the outcome because adaptation was never possible.

### Ignoring Capacity And Dependencies

Planning all themes simultaneously without sequencing. The trap is overload and stall, with nothing delivered well.

### One-Way Translation With No Feedback

Roadmap shapes plan but plan results never reshape roadmap. The trap is a roadmap that drifts from reality while execution drifts from strategy.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Every initiative in the plan is traceable to a roadmap theme with a stated contribution to its outcome.
- [ ] Initiatives are sequenced to build cumulative value, with dependencies and validation considered.
- [ ] The outcome focus is preserved; initiatives are framed as hypotheses about moving outcomes, not just features to ship.
- [ ] Validation points are built into the plan, allowing adaptation based on execution results.
- [ ] The plan fits within real capacity and respects dependencies, with prioritization feedback to the roadmap when it cannot.
- [ ] A feedback loop carries execution results back to the roadmap, keeping both honest and connected.
- [ ] The roadmap is updated on a regular cadence based on what execution revealed.
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
