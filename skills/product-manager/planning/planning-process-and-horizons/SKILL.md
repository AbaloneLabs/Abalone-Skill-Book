# Planning Process And Horizons

Planning is the process of turning strategy into sequenced, resourced work across multiple time horizons. It is not a single event but a system of overlapping horizons, each with a different purpose, certainty, and commitment level. Done well, planning gives the team a clear near-term commitment, a credible mid-term direction, and a long-term vision that informs both, while staying honest about how certainty falls with distance. Done poorly, planning produces a single detailed annual plan that is obsolete within weeks, or a perpetual re-planning that never commits to anything. Agents often design planning as one horizon treated at one level of detail, ignoring that commitment must decay with time.

The harm this skill prevents is planning theater or planning paralysis. A detailed one-year plan that no one believes creates false confidence and breaks the moment reality arrives. Continuous re-planning with no commitment creates a team that cannot coordinate or make tradeoffs, because nothing is stable long enough to build on. Both failures come from misunderstanding what planning at each horizon is for.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how should we structure our planning", "what planning horizons should we use", "how far ahead should we plan", or "how do we balance commitment and flexibility". The goal is to prevent the agent from designing a planning process that confuses commitment with forecasting.

## Core Rules

### Treat Planning As Multiple Horizons With Different Purposes

A healthy planning system operates across at least three horizons, each serving a different function. The near horizon, typically the sprint or month, is a commitment: the team has high certainty and is expected to deliver what it plans. The mid horizon, typically the quarter, is a directional forecast: the team has reasonable confidence in the outcomes and the major initiatives, but specifics will shift as learning accumulates. The far horizon, the half-year or year and beyond, is a strategic vision: the team knows the direction but not the specific work, and the plan exists to inform near-term decisions, not to be executed literally.

Each horizon should be planned at a level of detail and certainty appropriate to its distance. Detailing the far horizon as if it were a commitment wastes effort and creates false precision. Treating the near horizon as a loose forecast destroys coordination. Match the planning style to the horizon's purpose.

### Decay Commitment With Time And Say So Explicitly

The most common planning error is presenting every horizon with the same implied certainty, so that a one-year plan looks as committed as a two-week sprint. This false precision misleads stakeholders, who treat distant plans as promises, and creates a credibility crisis when the distant plan inevitably changes. Be explicit about the certainty of each horizon: near-term is a commitment, mid-term is a forecast, far-term is a vision. Label them as such, and manage stakeholder expectations accordingly.

This explicit labeling also protects the team. When a far-term plan changes, as it should when learning occurs, the change is not a broken promise but an expected evolution. The team that has been honest about certainty can adapt without losing credibility; the team that presented everything as committed cannot.

### Connect Horizons So Each Informs The Others

The horizons are not independent; they form a system where each informs the others. The far horizon sets the direction that the mid horizon plans toward, and the mid horizon shapes what the near horizon commits to. Conversely, learning from the near horizon updates the mid horizon, and accumulated mid-horizon learning can revise the far horizon. This flow of information up and down the horizons is what keeps planning honest and adaptive.

Build the connections deliberately. Near-horizon commitments should be traceable to mid-horizon initiatives. Mid-horizon initiatives should serve the far-horizon direction. When the connections break, the team ends up executing near-term work that does not accumulate toward anything, or chasing a far vision with no credible path.

### Separate Planning From Prioritization, But Connect Them

Planning is the sequencing and resourcing of prioritized work; prioritization is the decision about what is worth doing. Conflating them produces planning meetings that relitigate priorities instead of sequencing agreed work, which is slow and demoralizing. Keep prioritization as a distinct activity that feeds the planning process, so that planning can focus on how to deliver the prioritized work effectively.

At the same time, planning often reveals prioritization problems: a prioritized list that cannot be sequenced given dependencies and capacity is not actually a plan. The feedback from planning back to prioritization is essential. When planning surfaces that a prioritized initiative is infeasible or blocked, that information must flow back and trigger a prioritization revision, not be suppressed to preserve the plan.

### Plan Around Capacity And Dependencies, Not Wish Lists

A plan that ignores how much capacity exists and how work depends on other work is a wish list, not a plan. Effective planning accounts for the realistic capacity of the team, the dependencies between initiatives, the shared resources and bottlenecks, and the inevitable disruptions from support, incidents, and unplanned work. A plan that assumes uninterrupted focus on planned work will be wrong every time, because reality always includes the unplanned.

Build in slack for unplanned work based on historical patterns, and sequence around known dependencies and bottlenecks. A plan that fits within real capacity and respects dependencies is credible and executable; one that does not is aspirational and will be abandoned under pressure.

### Replan On A Cadence Tied To Learning, Not To Calendar Alone

The replanning cadence should be tied to when meaningful learning accumulates, not only to calendar boundaries. Quarterly replanning is a common default, but if a team learns faster, it may need to adjust mid-quarter, and if it learns slower, a quarter may be too frequent. The trigger for replanning is that the assumptions underlying the plan have changed enough that the plan no longer reflects the best path. Build in both scheduled replanning and a mechanism for triggered replanning when learning warrants it.

Avoid the extremes of never replanning, which produces a stale plan that everyone ignores, and constant replanning, which produces churn that prevents the team from building momentum. The right cadence lets the team execute with stability while staying responsive to what it learns.

## Common Traps

### Single-Horizon Planning At One Level Of Detail

Treating a one-year plan like a sprint commitment. The trap is false precision that breaks on contact with reality.

### False Certainty Across All Horizons

Presenting distant plans as promises. The trap is a credibility crisis every time the distant plan evolves, which it must.

### Disconnected Horizons

Near-term work that does not accumulate toward mid- or far-term direction. The trap is execution without progress.

### Planning Meetings That Relitigate Priorities

Mixing planning with prioritization. The trap is slow, demoralizing meetings that never reach sequencing decisions.

### Ignoring Capacity And Dependencies

Planning a wish list that exceeds what the team can deliver. The trap is a plan abandoned within weeks.

### Calendar-Only Replanning

Replanning on schedule regardless of learning. The trap is either stale plans or unnecessary churn, depending on whether learning outpaced the calendar.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Planning operates across multiple horizons, each with a distinct purpose, detail level, and certainty.
- [ ] The certainty of each horizon is stated explicitly, with near-term as commitment, mid-term as forecast, and far-term as vision.
- [ ] Horizons are connected so near-term work traces to mid-term initiatives and the far-term direction.
- [ ] Prioritization is separate from planning but connected by feedback when planning reveals infeasibility.
- [ ] The plan accounts for realistic capacity, dependencies, bottlenecks, and historical unplanned work.
- [ ] Replanning happens on a cadence tied to learning, with both scheduled and triggered replanning.
- [ ] The plan is credible and executable, not a wish list that will be abandoned under pressure.
