# Launch Communication And Announcement

A launch is not complete when the feature ships; it is complete when the right people know about it, understand it, and can act on it. The communication around a launch is where adoption is won or lost, and it is also where the most preventable launch failures occur: marketing announces a feature that support was not trained on, sales pitches capability that does not exist yet, customers read a vague announcement and miss the value, or the internal team learns about the launch from the public blog. The judgment problem is that launch communication looks like writing copy but is actually a coordinated multi-audience operation: each audience, customers, prospects, sales, support, partners, press, internal teams, needs different information at different times, and a single announcement cannot serve them all. The skill is sequencing and tailoring communication so that every audience is prepared rather than surprised.

Use this skill before planning launch communications, before writing announcement messaging, before coordinating across marketing, sales, and support, or before deciding what to announce and to whom. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a single announcement that under-serves every audience, or from letting external communication outrun internal readiness.

## Core Rules

### Map Every Audience And What Each Needs

A launch has many audiences, and each needs different information, depth, and timing. Treating the launch as one announcement to one audience leaves the others unprepared.

Map audiences and their needs:

- existing customers: what changed, what it means for them, how to use it, any action required;
- prospects: why it matters, the value and differentiation, proof points;
- sales: how to position, qualify, and handle objections, pricing and packaging;
- support: how it works, known issues, how to troubleshoot, what to escalate;
- partners and integrators: compatibility, integration changes, co-marketing;
- press and analysts: the story, the proof, the market significance;
- internal teams: what is launching, why, and their role in it.

Each audience gets tailored content, not a recycled version of one announcement.

### Sequence Internal Before External

The most common launch communication failure is external announcement preceding internal readiness. When customers hear about a feature before support, sales, or success teams, those teams are caught flat-footed and credibility collapses. Internal readiness must lead external announcement.

Sequence by:

- briefing support and success before the announcement;
- enabling sales with materials and training before launch;
- informing internal teams through a clear internal announcement;
- staging partner communication to precede or coincide with public launch;
- releasing external communication only after internal teams are prepared.

### Lead With Customer Value, Not Feature Description

Customers do not buy features; they buy outcomes. Announcements that lead with what the feature does, "we added a new dashboard", underperform announcements that lead with what the customer gains, "you can now see and fix churn risk before it happens".

Frame by:

- stating the customer problem or job addressed;
- articulating the outcome and benefit in the customer's language;
- showing proof, a result, a quote, a before-and-after;
- placing the feature in context of the customer's workflow;
- reserving technical detail for the audiences who need it.

### Match Messaging To The Launch Tier

Not every launch deserves the same communication investment. A major release, a minor enhancement, and a silent fix require different levels of announcement, and over- or under-communicating both waste attention and credibility.

Tier the launch:

- tier one, major: full campaign, press, customer email, launch event, sales enablement;
- tier two, moderate: announcement, in-app message, sales and support briefing;
- tier three, minor: release notes, changelog, internal note;
- silent: no announcement, operational or compliance change.

Match the communication investment to the actual significance.

### Coordinate Cross-Functional Timing Precisely

Launch communication involves marketing, product, sales, support, success, legal, and PR, and each has dependencies on the others. Misaligned timing produces gaps: marketing announces before the feature is live, sales enables before pricing is approved, support learns from a customer ticket.

Coordinate by:

- building a single launch timeline with all teams' milestones;
- defining the go-live moment and back-timing all enablement before it;
- assigning owners for each communication artifact;
- running a readiness review before any external communication;
- having a single source of truth for messaging and timing.

### Prepare For Questions, Objections, And Known Issues

A launch generates questions, and unprepared answers damage credibility. Anticipate what each audience will ask and prepare responses in advance.

Prepare:

- a FAQ for customers and prospects;
- objection handling for sales;
- known issues and workarounds for support;
- analyst and press Q&A for PR;
- internal Q&A for teams fielding questions.

A launch where the front-line teams have prepared answers projects competence; one where they improvise projects confusion.

### Build A Feedback Loop Into The Announcement

The announcement is the start of the launch, not the end. The communication should create channels to capture how the launch lands and feed that back into iteration.

Build loops by:

- monitoring adoption, sentiment, and questions after announcement;
- capturing support ticket themes and sales objections;
- measuring announcement engagement and conversion;
- iterating messaging based on what resonates and confuses;
- feeding learnings into the next launch.

### Manage Expectations About Availability And Rollout

Announcements that imply universal availability when the feature is rolling out gradually create frustration. Be explicit about who gets what and when.

Clarify:

- whether the feature is available to all or rolling out in phases;
- any eligibility, plan, region, or account requirements;
- pricing and packaging implications;
- known limitations or dependencies;
- how to request or access it.

## Common Traps

### External Before Internal

Announcing to customers before support, sales, and success are prepared, leaving front-line teams exposed.

### Feature Over Value

Leading with what the feature does rather than the outcome the customer gains.

### One Announcement For All Audiences

Recycling one message across customers, sales, support, and press, under-serving each.

### Over- Or Under-Tiering

Treating a minor enhancement as a major launch, or burying a significant release in release notes.

### Uncoordinated Timing

Marketing, sales, support, and PR operating on independent timelines that produce gaps.

### Unprepared For Questions

Front-line teams improvising answers to predictable questions, projecting confusion.

### Vague Availability

Implying universal availability during a phased rollout, creating frustration and support load.

### Set-And-Forget Announcement

Treating the announcement as the end rather than building feedback loops to iterate.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Every audience, customers, prospects, sales, support, partners, press, internal, is mapped with tailored content and timing.
- [ ] Internal enablement, support, sales, success, precedes external announcement.
- [ ] Messaging leads with customer value and outcome, with technical detail reserved for audiences who need it.
- [ ] The communication investment matches the launch tier, major, moderate, minor, or silent.
- [ ] A single coordinated timeline aligns marketing, product, sales, support, legal, and PR milestones.
- [ ] FAQs, objection handling, known issues, and press Q&A are prepared before announcement.
- [ ] Availability, rollout phasing, eligibility, pricing, and limitations are stated explicitly.
- [ ] Feedback loops capture adoption, sentiment, questions, and objections to iterate messaging.
- [ ] A readiness review confirms all teams are prepared before external communication.
- [ ] The announcement is treated as the start of the launch, with iteration built in.
