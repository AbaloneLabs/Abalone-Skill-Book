# Market Sizing And Segment Selection

Strategy begins with choosing whom to serve, and that choice is harder than it looks because the obvious answer, serve everyone who might buy, is usually the wrong one. A product that tries to be acceptable to every segment ends up excellent for none, and a market sizing that aggregates everyone with a pulse produces a large number that conceals the fact that most of those people will never choose you. The judgment problem is that market sizing is not arithmetic, it is a series of assumptions about who has the problem, who will pay, who you can reach, and who you can defend, and segment selection is not about finding the biggest group but about finding the group whose needs you can serve better than anyone who already serves them.

Use this skill before sizing a market opportunity, before selecting or revising a target segment, before deciding between broad horizontal and focused vertical strategy, or before defending a focus decision to leadership. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a TAM number that justifies anything and therefore justifies nothing, or from selecting a segment because it is large rather than because it is winnable.

## Core Rules

### Size The Market In Layers, Not One Number

A single TAM figure is the least useful output of market sizing because it hides the assumptions that actually matter. Decompose the market into layers that reflect real reachability.

Decompose into:

- total addressable market, everyone with the problem and budget, theoretical ceiling;
- serviceable addressable market, those you can reach with your model and geography;
- serviceable obtainable market, the realistic share you could capture in a period;
- qualified demand, those actively looking to buy now;
- winnable demand, those you can realistically convert given competition.

The ratio between these layers reveals whether the opportunity is real or inflated. A huge TAM with a tiny SOM is a trap.

### Question Every Assumption In The Funnel

Each layer of sizing rests on assumptions, and errors compound down the funnel. A 20% error in market size, penetration rate, and win rate compounds into a 50% error in obtainable demand.

Make assumptions explicit:

- the source and recency of each input;
- whether the figure counts users, accounts, or revenue;
- whether growth is assumed or evidenced;
- whether the segment definition matches your actual product;
- the confidence interval, not a single point estimate.

A sizing with no visible assumptions is a sales pitch, not analysis.

### Evaluate Segment Attractiveness On Multiple Dimensions

Size alone does not make a segment attractive. A large segment that is expensive to reach, price-sensitive, and defended by incumbents may be worse than a smaller segment that is reachable, high-value, and underserved.

Evaluate segments on:

- size and growth trajectory;
- pain intensity and willingness to pay;
- reachability through your channels and motion;
- competitive intensity and incumbent strength;
- fit with your existing capabilities and assets;
- strategic value, references, network effects, expansion potential;
- cost to serve and expected margin.

Weight these dimensions deliberately rather than defaulting to size.

### Assess Winnability, Not Just Attractiveness

A segment can be attractive and still unwinnable. The critical question is whether you can serve this segment better than the alternatives it already has, including doing nothing.

Assess winnability by:

- the incumbent's strength and willingness to defend;
- your differentiation against existing solutions;
- switching costs and inertia in the segment;
- whether the segment's needs match your product's strengths;
- whether you can reach and convert them economically;
- whether you can defend the position once won.

Focus follows winnability. The segment you can win is more valuable than the segment that is merely large.

### Choose Focus Deliberately: Broad Versus Vertical

The broad-versus-vertical choice is a strategic bet with real tradeoffs, and it should be made consciously rather than by drift.

Consider:

- horizontal breadth: larger TAM, network effects, but harder differentiation and stronger incumbents;
- vertical depth: smaller TAM, but stronger fit, word-of-mouth, defensibility, and premium pricing;
- the wedge: start narrow to win a beachhead, then expand, which requires the narrow segment to enable expansion;
- the risk of being stuck in the middle, too broad to differentiate, too narrow to scale.

A focused strategy that is consciously chosen and revisited beats a vague strategy that tries to be all things.

### Align Segment Choice With Business Model And Motion

The segment you choose must be compatible with how you sell, price, and serve. A segment mismatched to your motion will be reachable in theory and unconvertable in practice.

Check alignment:

- does the segment buy through your sales motion, self-serve or enterprise;
- does the price point match the segment's budget and value perception;
- does the cost to acquire fit the lifetime value for this segment;
- does the segment's support and success needs match your cost to serve;
- does the segment generate the references and expansion you need.

### Validate Sizing With Bottom-Up And Top-Down Cross-Checks

A single sizing method is fragile. Triangulate between approaches to catch errors.

Cross-check:

- top-down: start from industry reports and narrow down by share assumptions;
- bottom-up: count reachable accounts or users and multiply by realistic conversion and value;
- behavioral: estimate from observed demand, search volume, inquiries, pilots;
- comparative: benchmark against analogous products or segments.

When methods disagree, investigate the gap rather than picking the larger number.

### Revisit Segment Choice As The Market And Product Evolve

Segment selection is not permanent. The product matures, the market shifts, competitors enter, and the right focus changes. A segment chosen at founding may be wrong at scale.

Revisit when:

- the beachhead segment saturates;
- a new adjacent segment becomes winnable;
- a competitor pivots into your segment;
- the product's strengths shift;
- unit economics in the segment deteriorate.

## Common Traps

### TAM Theater

Quoting a huge total addressable market to excite without showing the realistic obtainable share.

### Size As The Only Criterion

Choosing the largest segment regardless of winnability, reachability, or fit.

### Hidden Assumptions

Presenting a sizing with no visible assumptions, making it impossible to challenge or trust.

### Ignoring Incumbent Defense

Assuming you can take share from a strong incumbent who will fight to keep it.

### Motion Mismatch

Selecting a segment whose buying motion does not fit your sales, pricing, or support model.

### Stuck In The Middle

A focus too broad to differentiate and too narrow to scale, chosen by drift rather than decision.

### One-Time Sizing

Treating the initial segment choice as permanent as the market and product change.

### Optimistic Compounding

Letting small optimistic errors compound through the funnel into a wildly inflated opportunity.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The market is sized in layers, TAM, SAM, SOM, qualified and winnable demand, not as a single number.
- [ ] Every assumption in the sizing funnel is explicit, sourced, and confidence-bounded.
- [ ] Segment attractiveness is evaluated on size, pain, reachability, competition, fit, strategic value, and cost to serve.
- [ ] Winnability is assessed against incumbents, differentiation, switching costs, and economic conversion.
- [ ] The broad-versus-vertical focus is a conscious strategic choice, not drift, with tradeoffs understood.
- [ ] Segment choice is aligned with sales motion, pricing, CAC-to-LTV, and cost to serve.
- [ ] Sizing is triangulated across top-down, bottom-up, behavioral, and comparative methods.
- [ ] The segment choice has a defined review trigger and is revisited as market and product evolve.
- [ ] The wedge strategy, if used, has a credible path from beachhead to expansion.
- [ ] No TAM figure is presented without the realistic obtainable share and the assumptions behind it.
