# Decision Rights And RACI

Most alignment failures are not failures of persuasion; they are failures of ownership. When no one is clearly accountable for a decision, the decision is either never made or made by whoever is loudest, and then relitigated by everyone who assumed they had a say. A RACI or decision-rights matrix is not bureaucratic decoration. It is the mechanism that converts a vague "we'll figure it out together" into a named owner who is empowered to call the shot and a clear set of people whose input must be heard first.

Use this skill before kicking off a cross-functional initiative, before a decision that spans teams, and whenever two stakeholders both believe they own the call. The goal is to prevent the agent from leaving decision rights implicit to avoid an awkward conversation, and from confusing consultation with authority. The trap is that ambiguity feels diplomatic in the moment and produces collisions later, when the cost of resolving ownership is far higher.

Decision rights are also a fairness and speed issue. Over-assigning "responsible" roles creates bottlenecked approval chains that paralyze delivery, while under-assigning consultation leaves experts bypassed and resentful. The judgment is matching the rigor of the matrix to the stakes of the decision.

## Core Rules

### Name A Single Accountable Owner Per Decision

Every decision needs exactly one person who is accountable for the outcome and empowered to make the call. "Accountable" in RACI means the buck stops here: this person cannot delegate the responsibility for the decision, only the work of informing it. A decision with two accountable owners is unowned, because when they disagree there is no rule to break the tie. If two stakeholders both believe they decide, resolve that ambiguity first, because it will otherwise surface as a mutual veto at the worst moment.

### Distinguish Responsible, Consulted, And Informed Precisely

These roles collapse into noise if they are not defined sharply. Responsible does the work and is answerable for execution; consulted must be asked before the decision is made and their input weighed; informed is told after the decision is made but has no veto. The most common error is promoting a consulted party into a de facto approver, which turns advice into a veto and stalls every decision. Be explicit: consulted means their input is sought, not that their consent is required, unless you have deliberately made them accountable.

### Match Decision Rights To The Stakes

Not every decision needs a formal RACI. Low-stakes, easily reversible calls can be made by the product manager alone with a light note to those affected. High-stakes, hard-to-reverse decisions that commit budget, headcount, customer promises, or strategic direction need an explicit owner, named consultants, and documented approval. Applying heavy ceremony to trivial decisions wastes time, while applying no structure to consequential ones invites collisions. Calibrate the formality to the cost of being wrong and the cost of delay.

### Assign Rights Per Decision, Not Per Person

A person's authority varies by topic. The engineering lead may be the accountable owner for a technical migration and merely consulted on a pricing decision. Assigning someone a blanket "decider" role across all decisions produces both overreach and gaps. Build the matrix per decision, naming who is accountable, responsible, consulted, and informed for that specific call, and revisit it when the scope changes. A static org-chart-style ownership map goes stale quickly.

### Resolve Overlapping Ownership Before The Work Starts

When two teams both claim a decision, the conflict will not resolve itself during execution; it will escalate at the moment of highest pressure. Surface the overlap early, identify the shared higher-level objective both serve, and either pick a single owner or define a decision rule that breaks ties. Escalate to a common leader only when the parties cannot converge. Leaving the overlap unresolved to avoid conflict guarantees a collision later.

### Make Consultation Binding On Process, Not Outcome

A consulted stakeholder must be heard before the decision is made, and the owner must show that their input was considered. They are not, however, guaranteed to win. Document that the consultation happened, what the input was, and how it was weighed, so the consulted party knows they were taken seriously even when overruled. This is what separates consultation from a rubber stamp and prevents the resentment that comes from feeling ignored.

### Document The Decision Rights So They Survive Turnover

Implicit ownership understood only by the current team evaporates when someone leaves or reorganizes. Record the decision owner, the consulted parties, and the decision itself in a short written artifact. This prevents the decision from being quietly relitigated by a new stakeholder who was not part of the original conversation, and gives the next person in the role a clear inheritance rather than a puzzle.

## Common Traps

### Leaving Ownership Implicit To Avoid Conflict

Naming an owner feels like picking a side, so teams often leave it unsaid. The result is that the decision is made by inertia, by the loudest voice, or not at all, and the bypassed party raises the conflict at higher stakes. Naming the owner early is kinder than a collision during execution.

### Promoting Consulted Into A De Facto Approver

When every consulted party must agree, the decision requires unanimous consent that no one intended. This turns advice into veto power and stalls delivery. Be clear that consultation means input is sought and weighed, not that consent is required, unless you have explicitly made them accountable.

### Applying Heavy RACI Ceremony To Trivial Decisions

Forcing a formal matrix onto low-stakes, reversible calls wastes time and trains people to ignore the process. Reserve explicit decision-rights documentation for consequential, hard-to-reverse decisions where the cost of a collision justifies the ceremony.

### Assigning Blanket Ownership By Role

Treating "the engineering lead decides all technical things" as a rule ignores that authority is topic-specific and that the same person may be accountable on one decision and merely informed on another. Build the matrix per decision, not per job title.

### Two Accountable Owners

When two people are both accountable, neither is. Disagreement between them has no resolution rule, and each assumes the other will act. A decision with co-owners becomes a decision with no owner, which is the worst of both worlds.

### Confusing Informed With Consulted

Telling someone after the fact and asking them before the fact are different obligations. Marking a stakeholder as informed when they expected to be consulted produces the feeling of being bypassed, even if the decision itself was sound. Match the role to the stakeholder's legitimate need for input.

### Never Revisiting The Matrix As Scope Changes

A RACI built at kickoff can be wrong by mid-project as scope, leadership, and dependencies shift. A newly relevant stakeholder who is not on the matrix discovers their interest late and blocks the work. Re-check decision rights at each major phase change.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each decision has exactly one accountable owner, not two and not zero.
- [ ] Responsible, consulted, and informed roles are defined sharply, with consultation not treated as a veto.
- [ ] The formality of the decision-rights matrix matches the stakes and reversibility of the decision.
- [ ] Decision rights are assigned per decision, not as blanket authority by role or org chart.
- [ ] Overlapping ownership claims were surfaced and resolved before execution began.
- [ ] Consulted stakeholders were heard before the decision, and the consideration of their input is documented.
- [ ] The decision owner, consulted parties, and decision itself are recorded in writing to survive turnover.
- [ ] No consulted party was silently promoted into a de facto approver requiring unanimous consent.
- [ ] The matrix was revisited at major phase changes rather than assumed static from kickoff.
- [ ] Informed stakeholders were genuinely informed after the decision, not confused with consulted ones.
