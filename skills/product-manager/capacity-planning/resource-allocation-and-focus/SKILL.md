# Resource Allocation And Focus

Resource allocation is the decision about how to distribute finite team capacity across the initiatives competing for it. The central tension is between focus and responsiveness: concentrating capacity on few initiatives delivers them faster and better, but leaves the team unable to respond to incoming needs; spreading capacity across many initiatives maintains responsiveness but dilutes attention, increases context switching, and delivers nothing quickly. Done well, allocation concentrates enough capacity on the most important work to make real progress while preserving enough flexibility to respond to what matters. Done poorly, it either over-focuses and becomes brittle or, more commonly, spreads so thin that everything is in progress and nothing finishes. Agents often default to saying yes to every request, allocating a little capacity to each, which produces the appearance of progress on everything and delivery of nothing.

The harm this skill prevents is the team that is always busy and never ships. When capacity is spread across too many parallel initiatives, context switching consumes the marginal capacity, each initiative advances slowly, and the team loses the satisfaction and momentum of completing work. The cost is not only slow delivery but demoralization, because the team works hard without the reward of finished, valuable output.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how should we allocate our team", "how many things should we work on at once", "how do we balance focus and responsiveness", or "why is everything always in progress". The goal is to prevent the agent from allocating capacity in a way that maximizes busyness while minimizing delivery.

## Core Rules

### Limit Work In Progress To Preserve Focus And Throughput

The single most important allocation decision is how many initiatives the team works on simultaneously. Every additional item in progress increases context switching, coordination overhead, and cognitive load, while reducing the capacity available for each item. Beyond a small number, adding work in progress reduces total throughput, because the switching cost exceeds the value of parallelism. Limit work in progress to a small number, often two to four initiatives for a typical team, to preserve focus and finish things faster.

The counterintuitive truth is that working on fewer things at once delivers more things over time, because finished work creates value while in-progress work does not. A team that finishes two initiatives per month delivers more than a team that has eight in progress and finishes one per month. Limiting work in progress is the lever that converts busyness into delivery.

### Allocate Capacity In Concentrated Blocks, Not Thin Slices

When an initiative receives capacity, give it enough to make real progress, not a thin slice that barely moves it. An initiative staffed at ten percent of a team's capacity advances negligibly, because the overhead of context, coordination, and ramp-up consumes most of the allocated effort. Concentrate capacity so that each active initiative has enough to advance meaningfully, rather than spreading capacity so thin that every initiative crawls.

This often means saying no to requests that would require thin allocations. A stakeholder request that would consume five percent of capacity is better declined than accepted, because the five percent produces almost no progress on the request while consuming focus and coordination that could have advanced a concentrated initiative. Concentration requires the discipline to decline small demands.

### Reserve Capacity For Responsiveness Based On Historical Demand

Pure focus is brittle: a team concentrated entirely on planned initiatives cannot respond to the incoming work that every operating environment generates. Reserve a portion of capacity for responsiveness, sized to the historical rate of incoming urgent and unplanned work. A team that historically receives thirty percent of its work as unplanned should reserve roughly that much capacity, so that incoming work displaces reserved capacity rather than planned commitments.

The reservation must be honest. If the team reserves capacity for responsiveness but then fills it with planned commitments, the reservation is fictional, and incoming work will displace the planned commitments anyway. Treat the reservation as genuinely unavailable for planned work, and let the historical unplanned-work rate determine its size.

### Sequence To Reduce Context Switching Across The Team

Context switching is not only an individual cost but a team-level cost. When team members are spread across different initiatives, coordination meetings multiply, handoffs increase, and the cognitive overhead of tracking multiple contexts grows. Sequencing initiatives so that the team works on fewer things at the same time, even if the total work is the same, reduces switching cost and effectively increases capacity.

This sometimes means deferring an initiative that could start now so that the team can finish something already in progress, even if the deferred initiative is higher priority. The deferral costs a little time; the reduction in switching may save more. Balance priority against the switching cost of adding parallel work.

### Make Allocation Tradeoffs Visible And Deliberate

Every allocation decision is a tradeoff: capacity given to one initiative is capacity not available for another. Make these tradeoffs visible rather than letting them happen implicitly through the accumulation of accepted requests. When a new initiative is added, name what it displaces or delays. When a stakeholder requests capacity, present the tradeoff rather than silently absorbing the cost into the team's overloaded plan. Visible tradeoffs produce deliberate allocation; invisible tradeoffs produce accidental overload.

This visibility also protects the team. When every accepted request has a visible cost in displaced work, stakeholders make more informed demands and the team is not left to absorb an impossible load silently. The alternative, saying yes to everything and hoping capacity materializes, guarantees failure and burnout.

### Re-Allocate When Priorities Or Learning Shift

Allocation is not set once; it evolves as priorities shift and as the team learns. An initiative that seemed important may prove less so after early results; an incoming urgent need may require reallocation; a finished initiative frees capacity for the next priority. Build in regular re-allocation decisions, tied to the planning cadence and triggered by significant learning or priority changes. Static allocation under changing conditions leaves capacity stuck in initiatives that no longer deserve it.

The re-allocation should be deliberate and communicated, not a quiet drift. When capacity moves from one initiative to another, the affected stakeholders should understand why, so that the change is interpreted as a considered decision rather than randomness.

## Common Traps

### Too Much Work In Progress

Saying yes to every request and running many initiatives in parallel. The trap is busyness without delivery, as switching cost consumes capacity.

### Thin Allocations That Barely Move Initiatives

Giving every request a small slice of capacity. The trap is negligible progress on many things and finishing none.

### Fictional Responsiveness Reservation

Reserving capacity for unplanned work but filling it with commitments. The trap is incoming work displacing planned commitments anyway.

### Invisible Allocation Tradeoffs

Absorbing every request without naming what it displaces. The trap is accidental overload that no one chose and everyone suffers.

### Static Allocation Under Changing Conditions

Refusing to re-allocate when priorities or learning shift. The trap is capacity stuck in initiatives that no longer deserve it.

### Prioritizing Initiatives That Add Switching Cost

Starting a higher-priority initiative immediately rather than finishing in-progress work. The trap is more total switching cost that reduces effective capacity.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Work in progress is limited to a small number of initiatives to preserve focus and throughput.
- [ ] Capacity is allocated in concentrated blocks sufficient for meaningful progress, not thin slices.
- [ ] Capacity is reserved for responsiveness based on historical unplanned-work rate, and the reservation is genuine.
- [ ] Initiatives are sequenced to reduce context switching across the team.
- [ ] Allocation tradeoffs are made visible, with every accepted request naming what it displaces.
- [ ] Re-allocation happens on a cadence and is triggered by priority or learning shifts, with changes communicated.
- [ ] The team finishes work regularly, not merely advances many things in parallel.
