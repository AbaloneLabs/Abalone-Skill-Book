# Distribution And Acquisition Strategy

Distribution strategy is the decision about how a product reaches the people who buy and use it. It is not a marketing tactic or a single channel choice. It is the structural answer to who carries the product to the customer, what motion creates demand, and how the economics of acquisition compare across options. A product can be excellent and still fail if its distribution does not match how its audience actually buys.

Agents miss this because distribution is often treated as something marketing handles after the product is built. They default to a channel because it is familiar, add channels to hedge, or chase a growth tactic without checking whether it fits the product, the audience, or the unit economics. The harm is a product that cannot find its users affordably, a team spread thin across channels none of which work, or a motion that fights the product's natural buying behavior.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do we acquire users", "should we be product-led or sales-led", "what channels should we invest in", "how do we grow distribution", or "why is acquisition so expensive". The goal is to prevent the agent from choosing distribution by default or fashion and to force the fit between product, audience, channel, and economics.

## Core Rules

### Match The Motion To The Buying Behavior

The first decision is which motion carries the product: product-led growth, sales-led, marketing-led, or a hybrid. The right motion is determined by how the target customer actually buys, not by which motion is fashionable.

Consider:

- product-led growth fits low-friction, self-serve products where users can experience value without a sales conversation, often with viral or expansion mechanics;
- sales-led fits higher-price, higher-complexity products where the buyer needs trust, customization, and a procurement process;
- marketing-led fits products where demand generation and brand carry awareness, often paired with either motion;
- hybrid models use self-serve to land and sales to expand, or sales to land and product to retain.

Forcing a self-serve motion onto a product that requires executive buy-in, or a sales motion onto a five-dollar-a-month tool, fights the market and burns money.

### Evaluate Channel Fit Between Product, Audience, And Channel

A channel is only valuable if the product, the audience, and the channel align. The same channel can be exceptional for one product and useless for another.

For each candidate channel, ask:

- does the target audience actually spend attention here?
- does the channel's format fit how this product is understood and evaluated?
- can the product's value be demonstrated within the channel's constraints?
- is the channel compatible with the motion, or does it fight it?
- what is the competitive density and cost of attention in this channel?

Channel fit is empirical. Assumptions about where an audience lives should be tested, not asserted, and a channel that worked for a prior product may not transfer.

### Model CAC, Payback, And Channel Economics

Distribution is an economic decision before it is a tactical one. A channel that grows signups but never pays back is a leak, not a win.

Model per channel:

- customer acquisition cost, including fully loaded spend and effort;
- payback period relative to gross margin and retention;
- contribution to lifetime value, not just to top-of-funnel volume;
- how volume scales with spend, and where diminishing returns begin;
- the quality of customers acquired, since cheap signups that churn destroy economics.

A channel is worth investing in when unit economics work at scale, not when a campaign looked good in a sprint. Distinguish channels that grow efficiently from channels that grow only with disproportionate spend.

### Use Viral And Network Mechanics Deliberately

Viral and network effects can make distribution compounding rather than linear, but they are properties of the product design, not marketing tricks bolted on afterward.

Ask:

- does the product become more valuable as more people use it, network effects?
- does use naturally invite others, viral loops?
- is virality authentic to the product, or does it feel forced and damage trust?
- what is the viral coefficient, and is it sustainable or campaign-driven?

Virality that degrades the experience to chase shares is self-defeating. Build network mechanics into the product where they fit the value, and do not assume virality where the product is fundamentally single-player.

### Treat Partnerships And Integrations As Distribution

Partnerships, integrations, and marketplaces are distribution channels, not just technical features. They place the product in front of an audience already gathered by another platform.

Evaluate:

- which platforms does the target audience already use and trust?
- does an integration create genuine value for the partner's users, or is it one-sided?
- what are the dependency and platform risks of building on someone else's distribution?
- what is the economics and governance of the partnership or marketplace?

Integrations can be the most efficient acquisition channel when the fit is real, and the most wasteful when they are checkbox features nobody discovers.

### Avoid Spreading Thin Across Channels

A common failure is attempting many channels at a low level of investment so that none reaches the depth needed to work. Shallow presence across ten channels usually loses to deep presence in one or two.

Discipline matters:

- start with the one or two channels with the strongest fit and economics;
- invest enough to reach a meaningful signal before judging the channel;
- add channels only when the first are working and resourced, not to hedge doubt;
- kill channels that do not pay back rather than keeping them on life support.

Focus is a distribution strategy. Spreading thin is often a way to avoid committing to the hard work of making one channel succeed.

### Let Distribution Shape Product Requirements

Distribution is not only downstream of the product; it shapes what the product must be. A product-led motion requires onboarding, activation, and self-serve workflows as core features. A sales-led motion requires demo-ability, security reviews, and admin controls. A marketplace integration requires the surfaces and performance the partner demands.

Build distribution requirements into the product, not after it:

- what must the product do for this motion to work?
- what onboarding, virality, or enterprise readiness does the channel require?
- what instrumentation is needed to measure channel performance?

A product built without its distribution motion in mind will be missing the very features its channel needs to convert.

## Common Traps

### Choosing A Motion By Fashion

The team adopts product-led growth or an outbound sales motion because it is trendy, not because it fits how the customer buys, and the motion fights the market.

### Spreading Thin To Hedge

Investing shallowly across many channels so that none reaches the depth needed to work, producing activity without results.

### Ignoring Unit Economics

The team celebrates signup growth while CAC exceeds payback, so growth destroys value rather than creating it.

### Assuming A Channel Transfers From A Prior Product

A channel that worked before is assumed to work again without checking audience and fit, and it produces nothing for the new product.

### Bolting Virality Onto A Single-Player Product

The team forces sharing mechanics onto a product with no natural network effect, damaging the experience for a viral coefficient that never materializes.

### Treating Integrations As Checkbox Features

Integrations are built but never distributed or discovered, so they consume engineering effort without generating acquisition.

### Building The Product Before Its Distribution

The product is built to its feature spec, then distribution is handed to marketing, only for the team to discover the product lacks the onboarding or enterprise features its motion requires.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The primary motion, product-led, sales-led, marketing-led, or hybrid, is chosen to match how the target customer actually buys.
- [ ] Each candidate channel is evaluated for fit between product, audience, format, and competitive density, not assumed.
- [ ] CAC, payback period, and contribution to lifetime value are modeled per channel, and growth is judged on unit economics, not signup volume.
- [ ] Viral and network mechanics are built into the product where they fit the value, not forced where they do not.
- [ ] Partnerships and integrations are treated as distribution with genuine partner value, platform risk, and economics assessed.
- [ ] The strategy commits depth to one or two channels rather than shallow presence across many.
- [ ] Channels that do not pay back are killed rather than kept on life support.
- [ ] Distribution requirements, such as onboarding, virality, or enterprise readiness, are built into the product, not added afterward.
- [ ] Instrumentation exists to measure channel performance and attribute acquisition to its source.
- [ ] Channel assumptions are tested empirically rather than asserted from prior experience or fashion.
