# Packaging And Plans

Packaging is the decision about which capabilities live in which plan, what is free versus paid versus enterprise, and how a customer moves between editions as they grow. It is distinct from the pricing model, which defines what you charge for, and from price-setting, which defines the numbers. Packaging is the map of the product's commercial surface, and it determines who can adopt, who upgrades, and who churns at the boundary between tiers.

Agents miss this because packaging looks like arranging features into columns. They sort capabilities into plans by gut, gate the impressive-sounding features behind the top tier, and ship the matrix. The harm is packaging that confuses buyers, gates the wrong things, blocks natural expansion, and creates support and billing complexity that costs more than it earns. Packaging is a product surface in its own right, and like any product surface it needs deliberate design and testing.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what goes in each plan", "how should we structure our tiers", "what should be free versus paid", "where do we put the paywall", or "how many plans should we have". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating packaging as a sorting exercise and to make the logic of each gate, upgrade, and tier explicit.

## Core Rules

### Define The Logic Of Free, Paid, And Enterprise

Each edition should exist for a reason, not by convention. Free, paid, and enterprise serve different purposes in the business, and the features in each should reflect that purpose.

Reasoned roles for each:

- free serves distribution, adoption, and future conversion; it should deliver real value and a natural reason to upgrade, not a crippled demo;
- paid serves the core customer who receives the product's primary value and pays for it;
- enterprise serves buyers who need scale, control, security, support, or customization worth a premium.

If a feature is in a tier, it should be there because it serves that tier's buyer and business role. Packaging without a logic per tier becomes a feature dump that serves no one well.

### Gate By Value And Segment, Not Arbitrarily

Feature gating is the core act of packaging, and it should follow principle, not convenience. The question for every feature is which buyer it belongs to and what value it unlocks.

Principles for gating:

- gate by who values the feature and which segment it serves;
- gate by usage scale, so capabilities that matter at scale sit in higher tiers;
- avoid gating table-stakes features, which feels punitive and damages trust;
- avoid gating so little that there is no reason to upgrade;
- make each tier's value coherent, so a buyer can recognize the tier meant for them.

Arbitrary gating, where features land in tiers by habit or politics, produces a matrix no one can explain and sales cannot defend.

### Design Natural Upgrade Triggers And Expansion Paths

Good packaging makes upgrade feel like growth, not a penalty. As a customer gets more value, the structure should lead them naturally to the next tier.

Design for:

- upgrade triggers tied to value, such as more seats, more usage, or more advanced capability, that emerge as the customer succeeds;
- expansion paths that are visible before the customer hits a wall, so the upgrade is anticipated, not resented;
- paywalls that explain what is beyond them and why it is worth it, rather than blocking silently;
- downgrade paths that feel fair, so the relationship survives a change in need.

A customer who upgrades because they are succeeding is the goal. A customer who upgrades only because they were blocked is a transaction that breeds resentment.

### Use Good-Better-Best With Plan-Count Discipline

The good-better-best structure works because it lets customers self-select and uses the middle tier as a relational anchor. But plan count is a discipline: every additional tier adds sales, support, billing, and decision complexity.

Guidance:

- three tiers is often the sweet spot for self-serve and SMB motion, with enterprise as a fourth, negotiated edition;
- the middle tier should be designed as the most attractive option, with the decoy effect making it the obvious choice;
- each tier must be distinct enough that a buyer can choose quickly;
- resist adding tiers to capture edge cases, since complexity costs more than it earns.

More plans is rarely more revenue. It is usually more confusion.

### Decide Between Add-Ons And Bundles Deliberately

Some capabilities belong inside a tier; others are better as add-ons that a customer can layer on. The choice affects how customers buy and how the company captures value.

Consider:

- bundles simplify buying and drive up average deal size, but can force customers to pay for what they do not want;
- add-ons let customers pay for exactly what they value, but increase configuration and billing complexity;
- the decision should follow how customers actually combine capabilities, not how the company is organized internally.

A coherent bundle that matches a real use case is powerful. A bundle assembled to justify a price point is friction.

### Map Packaging To The ICP And Segment

Packaging should reflect the ideal customer profile and the segments the business serves. A matrix built without the customer in mind will misprice, misgate, and mislead.

Ask:

- does each tier correspond to a real segment with distinct needs and budget?
- does the free or entry tier attract the customers who can eventually convert?
- does enterprise capture the buyers who need and will pay for scale and control?
- are the plan boundaries where real customers actually feel a difference in need?

Packaging that does not map to how real customers buy and grow creates friction at exactly the moments where conversion and expansion should be easy.

### Treat Packaging As A Product Surface That Needs Design And Testing

Packaging is not a spreadsheet; it is a surface customers interact with. Like any surface, it should be designed, prototyped, tested, and iterated.

Treat packaging as a product by:

- testing plan names, feature lists, and paywall language with real buyers;
- measuring conversion, upgrade, and downgrade at each boundary;
- reviewing support and billing friction caused by the matrix;
- iterating the structure as the product, segments, and competitive context evolve.

A packaging page that confuses buyers costs revenue invisibly. Treat it with the same rigor as the product's core flows.

## Common Traps

### Sorting Features Into Plans By Gut

Capabilities are distributed across tiers by feel, producing a matrix that no one can explain and that gates the wrong things for the wrong buyers.

### Crippling The Free Tier

The free edition is so restricted that it delivers no real value, so it neither drives adoption nor creates a credible path to conversion.

### Gating Table-Stakes Features

Basic capabilities are locked behind paid tiers, which feels punitive and trains even willing buyers to resent the product.

### Too Many Plans Chasing Edge Cases

Tiers multiply to capture every segment and exception, and the resulting complexity raises support costs and decision friction more than it raises revenue.

### Blocking Instead Of Guiding At The Paywall

The paywall stops the user without explaining what is beyond it or why it is worth paying, so the moment of potential conversion becomes a moment of friction.

### Forcing Bundles That Customers Do Not Want

Capabilities are bundled to justify a price point, forcing customers to pay for what they do not value and increasing friction at the point of sale.

### Packaging Built Without The Customer

The matrix is designed around internal logic or competitor mimicry rather than how real customers buy and grow, so the boundaries fall in the wrong places.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each edition, free, paid, and enterprise, has an explicit purpose and contains features that serve that purpose.
- [ ] Feature gating follows principle, based on value and segment, rather than arbitrary sorting.
- [ ] Table-stakes features are not gated in ways that feel punitive, and there is enough gated value to motivate upgrade.
- [ ] Upgrade triggers are tied to value and growth, with expansion paths visible before the customer hits a wall.
- [ ] Paywalls explain what is beyond them and why it is worth paying, rather than blocking silently.
- [ ] Plan count is disciplined, with each tier distinct and the middle tier designed as the attractive anchor.
- [ ] Add-ons versus bundles are chosen based on how customers actually combine capabilities, not internal convenience.
- [ ] Packaging maps to the ideal customer profile and real segment boundaries in need and budget.
- [ ] Packaging is treated as a product surface that is tested with buyers and iterated against conversion, upgrade, and friction data.
- [ ] Support and billing complexity created by the matrix has been reviewed and is justified by the value it captures.
