# Launch Positioning And Narrative

Positioning is the strategic choice of what category the product belongs to, who it is for, and what frame of reference customers should use to understand it. It is decided once, mostly, and every message that follows inherits it. Narrative is the arc that turns that positioning into something a person can repeat, remember, and act on. Both are foundational launch decisions, and both are easy to skip in favor of jumping straight to headlines and taglines.

Agents miss this because positioning feels abstract and messaging feels concrete. They draft launch copy, pick a tagline, and call the strategic work done, never realizing that the copy is built on an unstated and often contradictory positioning. The harm is that the market cannot tell what the product is, sales tells a different story than marketing, analysts place the product in the wrong category, and the launch generates attention without comprehension. The opposite failure is over-engineering a narrative so layered and abstract that no customer can repeat it, leaving the team with a positioning document no one uses.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how should we position this launch", "what is our core narrative", "what category are we in", "how do we frame this against competitors", or "what is the story for this release". The goal is to prevent the agent from writing launch messaging before the positioning it depends on has been decided.

## Core Rules

### Decide The Category And Frame Of Reference First

Customers understand new things by placing them next to things they already know. The category you choose is the frame of reference for every comparison, and choosing it is the most consequential positioning decision. A product positioned as a better spreadsheet is judged against spreadsheets; the same product positioned as a new kind of database is judged against databases and understood differently.

State the category explicitly, and consider the alternatives deliberately. Sometimes the right move is to fit an existing category where the product can win; sometimes it is to define a new category, which is harder and slower but avoids direct comparison to entrenched leaders. A new category requires education budget and patience; an existing category requires differentiation. Choose knowing the tradeoff.

### Name The Target And The Alternative, Not Just The Benefit

Positioning that names only the benefit is incomplete. Strong positioning states who the product is for, what they do today instead, and why this is better for them specifically. Without the alternative, the benefit has no contrast and no urgency.

Identify the specific audience whose problem this solves, the status quo or competitor they currently use, and the reason this product wins for them. A positioning that tries to serve everyone usually serves no one distinctly, because the alternatives and the winning reasons differ by segment.

### Anchor The Narrative In A Shift Or Tension

A flat list of benefits is forgettable. A narrative that the audience can repeat needs tension: a change in the world that makes the old way inadequate, or a problem that has gotten worse until the current tools no longer suffice. The product enters as the response to that shift.

Build the arc around what changed and why the status quo no longer works, then introduce the product as the answer. The shift creates urgency and relevance; without it, the launch reads as "we made a thing" rather than "the world moved, and here is how to move with it."

### Make The Core Claim Specific And Defensible

The central positioning claim should be specific enough to be falsifiable and defensible enough to survive scrutiny. "We are the fastest" or "we are the easiest" are claims competitors also make and customers do not believe. A claim tied to a specific audience, mechanism, or proof point is credible and hard to copy.

Test each claim by asking whether a competitor could say the same thing and whether a skeptical customer would believe it. If both answers are yes, the claim is too generic. Sharpen it until it is true for you and awkward for the alternative.

### Align Positioning With Product Reality And Roadmap

Positioning that outruns the product creates a credibility gap that destroys launch momentum. If the narrative promises a category the product does not yet fill, or a capability that is on the roadmap but not shipped, customers and reviewers will discover the gap on day one.

Confirm that every claim in the positioning is true of the product today, and that near-term roadmap supports the trajectory the narrative implies. Positioning can point toward where the product is going, but it must not assert arrival at a destination the product has not reached.

### Keep The Narrative Repeatable And Compressible

A positioning document that no customer service rep, salesperson, or partner can repeat from memory is not a narrative, it is a document. The core story should compress to a sentence, expand to a paragraph, and extend to a page, all consistent.

Write the one-sentence version first, then the paragraph, then the long form. If the long form contradicts the sentence, the narrative is not coherent. Launches spread through people who retell the story in their own words, so the story must survive compression.

### Pressure-Test Against Competitors And Analysts

Positioning does not exist in a vacuum. Competitors will counter-position, analysts will categorize, and customers will compare. The narrative should be robust to the obvious objections, not blindsided by them.

Stress-test the positioning by asking how each major competitor would respond, how an analyst would categorize the product, and what a skeptical reviewer would lead with. Adjust the frame so the most likely attacks land on prepared ground rather than exposing an unconsidered weakness.

## Common Traps

### Skipping Positioning To Write Copy

Jumping to taglines and headlines before deciding category, audience, and frame produces messaging that looks finished but rests on contradictory or unstated assumptions.

### Claiming A New Category Without Education Budget

Defining a new category is slow and expensive. Teams claim one for distinction but fund it like an existing category, then wonder why the market keeps misclassifying the product.

### Positioning For Everyone

A frame that tries to cover every segment ends up distinct for none. The alternatives and winning reasons differ by audience, so a universal positioning usually has no contrast.

### Generic Benefit Claims

"We are faster, easier, and more powerful" are claims every competitor makes. Without specificity and proof, customers dismiss them, and the launch generates noise without differentiation.

### Narrative Outrunning The Product

Positioning the product as the leader of a category it does not yet fill creates a day-one credibility gap. Reviewers and customers expose it immediately.

### An Over-Engineered Story No One Repeats

A narrative so layered and abstract that only its authors understand it dies on contact with sales, support, and partners who cannot retell it.

### Ignoring How Competitors Will Counter-Position

A positioning that looks strong in isolation can collapse when a competitor reframes the comparison. Not pressure-testing against likely attacks leaves the launch exposed.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The category and frame of reference are decided explicitly, with the existing-category versus new-category tradeoff considered.
- [ ] The positioning names the specific target audience and the alternative they use today, not just the benefit.
- [ ] The narrative is anchored in a shift or tension that makes the status quo inadequate, not a flat list of benefits.
- [ ] The core claim is specific and defensible, not something any competitor could say.
- [ ] Every positioning claim is true of the shipped product, and the roadmap supports the implied trajectory.
- [ ] The narrative compresses to a sentence, a paragraph, and a page without contradiction.
- [ ] The positioning has been pressure-tested against how competitors, analysts, and skeptical reviewers would respond.
- [ ] The frame creates contrast with the alternative rather than describing the product in isolation.
- [ ] Sales, support, and partners can repeat the core story in their own words.
- [ ] The positioning was decided before messaging and copy were written, not reverse-engineered from them.
