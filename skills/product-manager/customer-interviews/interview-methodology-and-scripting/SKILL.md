# Interview Methodology And Scripting

A customer interview is a research instrument, not a chat. The way questions are ordered, worded, and sequenced determines whether the answers describe reality or flatter the interviewer's existing beliefs. Done well, an interview surfaces what customers actually do, why, and where they struggle. Done poorly, it manufactures agreement, rewards polite lies, and returns the team to the same assumptions they started with. Agents often draft scripts that lead the respondent, ask about hypothetical futures, and treat enthusiastic answers as evidence.

The harm this skill prevents is confident decisions built on contaminated data. When interview methodology is weak, the team believes it has validated a direction when it has only collected social permission. The product then ships to customers whose real behavior was never understood.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "what should we ask in customer interviews", "how should we structure the interview", "how many interviews do we need", or "how do we write a discussion guide". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a script that feels thorough but systematically produces biased answers.

## Core Rules

### Decide The Research Question Before The Script

Every interview should serve a specific learning goal stated as a question the team needs answered. Vague goals like "understand our users" produce vague scripts and un synthesizable answers. Define what decision the interview will inform, what is currently unknown, and what evidence would change the team's mind. If you cannot name the decision, the interview is premature.

The research question determines everything downstream: whom to recruit, how many to interview, what to ask, and how to synthesize. A study designed to understand current behavior differs from one designed to test a concept, which differs again from one exploring willingness to pay. Conflating these goals in a single interview dilutes all of them.

### Recruit For The Right Respondents, Not The Convenient Ones

The validity of findings depends on whether respondents represent the population whose behavior you need to understand. Recruiting existing happy customers is easy but tells you little about non-customers or churned users. Recruiting from your network introduces social desirability bias. Define the target respondent by the criteria that matter for the research question, and recruit to those criteria even when it is slower.

Be explicit about whom the sample represents and whom it does not. A finding from ten enterprise power users does not generalize to self-serve small teams, and claiming it does is a research error, not a stretch.

### Write Questions That Describe The Past, Not The Future

People are reliable narrators of past behavior and unreliable predictors of future behavior. Ask about specific, recent experiences: the last time they faced the problem, what they did, what tools they used, what went wrong. Avoid asking what they would do, what they would buy, or whether they would use a hypothetical feature. Answers to hypothetical questions are wishes, not evidence.

When you must explore the future, anchor it in the present. Instead of asking whether they would pay for a feature, ask what they currently spend to solve the problem, including time and workarounds. Observed past behavior is the strongest available proxy for future behavior.

### Sequence From Broad And Open To Specific

Begin with broad, open questions that let the respondent define the topic in their own terms. This avoids imposing your frame on their experience and surfaces context you did not anticipate. Move to more specific questions only after the respondent has established their own framing. Leading with your terminology or your assumed problem teaches them what you want to hear.

Resist filling silence. After asking an open question, pause. Respondents often add the most valuable detail after an initial, polished answer, when the silence signals you want more. A script that rushes to the next question loses the richest material.

### Avoid Leading Questions And Loaded Language

Any question that signals the answer you want contaminates the response. Asking "how frustrating is it when..." assumes frustration. Asking "would you find this useful?" invites a yes that costs the respondent nothing. Asking "do you agree that..." hands them the conclusion. Rewrite every question to be neutral about the answer and to avoid the team's preferred vocabulary.

Audit the script for words that presuppose a problem, a feeling, or a frequency. Replace "how often do you struggle with X" with "tell me about the last time you dealt with X". The neutral version lets the respondent reveal whether struggle even occurred.

### Mix Probes And Standardized Questions By Purpose

Some questions must be identical across respondents to allow comparison; others must adapt to follow where each respondent leads. Decide which is which. Standardized questions support pattern-finding across the sample. Adaptive probes support depth and surprise. A script that is entirely standardized misses unexpected insight; one that is entirely freeform cannot be synthesized.

For each section, note whether the goal is comparability or exploration, and design accordingly. Pilot the script with a few respondents and revise, because the first version always contains questions that do not work as intended.

## Common Traps

### Pitching Instead Of Listening

Turning the interview into a sales pitch or a concept test where you describe your idea and ask for reactions. The trap is that respondents are polite and will validate almost anything, leaving you with false confidence.

### Asking About Hypothetical Future Behavior

Asking what respondents would do, buy, or use. The trap is that hypothetical answers are unreliable and feel like evidence, leading the team to build for stated intent rather than observed behavior.

### Leading Questions That Confirm Assumptions

Questions that embed the desired answer. The trap is that the script returns the team's own assumptions dressed up as customer voice, which is worse than no research because it feels validated.

### Convenient Sampling

Recruiting friendly existing customers or personal contacts because they are easy to reach. The trap is findings that describe only the people you already understand, missing the segments you most need to learn about.

### Overloading A Single Interview

Trying to explore behavior, test a concept, and assess pricing in one session. The trap is shallow answers to all three, because each goal requires different framing and time that a single interview cannot provide.

### Treating Enthusiasm As Evidence

Counting nods, compliments, and excited reactions as validation. The trap is that enthusiasm in an interview rarely predicts adoption, because the social context rewards agreement.

## Self-Check

- [ ] A specific research question and the decision it informs are stated before the script is written.
- [ ] Respondents are recruited to criteria that match the population whose behavior the team needs to understand.
- [ ] Questions focus on specific past behavior and observed experience, not hypothetical future intent.
- [ ] The script sequences from broad and open to specific, and avoids imposing the team's framing early.
- [ ] Every question has been audited for leading language and loaded assumptions.
- [ ] Standardized and adaptive questions are deliberately mixed based on whether the goal is comparison or exploration.
- [ ] The script was piloted and revised before full rollout.
- [ ] Enthusiasm and politeness are not treated as evidence of future adoption.
