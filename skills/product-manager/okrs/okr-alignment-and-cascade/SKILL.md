# OKR Alignment And Cascade

OKR alignment is the discipline of connecting team goals to company strategy so that decentralized effort compounds toward shared outcomes rather than canceling out. The central tension is between alignment and autonomy: goals must connect upward so teams pull in the same direction, but rigid cascading, where every team's key result is an arithmetic slice of the parent, destroys local problem-solving and creates a contract that blocks adaptation. Done well, alignment gives every team a clear line of sight to strategy while preserving its freedom to find the best local path. Done poorly, it produces either disconnected goals that pull sideways or a rigid cascade that turns OKRs into a compliance exercise. Agents often default to strict cascading because it looks clean, without recognizing the rigidity and gaming it creates.

The harm this skill prevents is organizational effort that does not compound. When team goals are disconnected from strategy, teams succeed locally while the company fails globally. When the cascade is too rigid, teams stop thinking, sandbag to hit their slice, and cannot adapt to what they learn. Both failures waste the focusing power that OKRs are meant to provide.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how do we cascade OKRs", "how do team goals connect to company strategy", "should every team's OKR roll up to the parent", or "how do we align without losing autonomy". The goal is to prevent the agent from choosing cascade mechanics on aesthetics rather than on their effect on alignment and autonomy.

## Core Rules

### Align On Intent Rather Than Decomposing Arithmetic

The strongest alignment connects each team's objectives to a strategic goal through shared intent, not through arithmetic decomposition. Rather than slicing a parent key result into equal portions for each team, each team identifies how its work contributes to the strategic objective and sets its own key results for that contribution. The team owns the how; the alignment ensures the what points the right way. This preserves autonomy while maintaining line of sight to strategy.

Check alignment by tracing each team objective upward to a strategic goal and asking whether the contribution is plausible and material. If a team's goals could fully succeed while the strategy fails, the alignment is broken. If the team has no room to choose its own key results based on what it learns, the cascade is too rigid. The balance is intent alignment with local ownership.

### Verify The Line Of Sight From Team Work To Strategy

Alignment is only real if every team can explain how its objectives serve a strategic goal. This line of sight must be explicit and testable, not assumed. When a team cannot connect its work to strategy, either the strategy is unclear, the team's goals are misdirected, or the connection is genuinely weak and the work should be questioned. Build the connection deliberately and revisit it when strategy shifts.

The line of sight also runs downward: leadership should be able to look at the set of team OKRs and see how they collectively cover the strategy. Gaps, where no team's goals address a strategic priority, are as revealing as misalignments, where team goals point the wrong way. Both indicate that the alignment needs repair.

### Avoid Rigid Cascade That Kills Adaptation

Strict top-down cascading, where each team's key result is a predetermined slice of the parent, assumes that the contribution of each team is known in advance and fixed. In reality, teams learn during the cycle, and the best contribution often differs from what was planned at the start. A rigid cascade prevents teams from adapting their key results to what they discover, because changing a slice breaks the arithmetic. The result is that teams either chase stale targets or quietly ignore the cascade.

If the cascade is so tight that teams cannot change a key result when they learn something new, the system is too rigid. Build in a low-friction process for teams to revise their key results when the rationale changes, with the revision visible and the reason documented. Alignment to intent survives these revisions; arithmetic cascade does not.

### Detect And Prevent Sandbagging And Gaming

When team goals are judged as performance and cascade tightly, teams learn to set safe targets they can easily exceed, because the cost of missing is high and the reward for ambition is low. This sandbagging systematically under-aims the organization. The counter is to separate committed goals, which should be realistic, from aspirational goals, which should stretch, and to ensure that OKR scores are not the sole input to performance evaluation. When missing an aspirational goal carries no penalty, teams set more honest and more ambitious targets.

Gaming also appears in how teams choose key results that are easy to hit rather than meaningful. A team that selects key results it knows it can already achieve is optimizing for the score rather than for the outcome. Review proposed key results for whether they represent real stretch or merely safe bets, and challenge those that look pre-achieved.

### Resolve Conflicts Between Team Goals Explicitly

When teams align to the same strategy, their goals can still conflict with each other. One team's optimization may harm another team's metric; two teams may depend on each other in ways that create sequencing conflicts; resources may be insufficient for all teams to hit committed goals simultaneously. These conflicts are inevitable in a complex organization and must be resolved explicitly rather than allowed to fester. A forum for cross-team OKR review, before the cycle begins, surfaces and resolves these conflicts.

The resolution may involve reassigning resources, adjusting targets, sequencing goals, or establishing shared metrics with clear ownership. Ignoring conflicts guarantees that they will surface mid-cycle as blockers, when they are harder to resolve. Pre-cycle alignment is cheaper than mid-cycle firefighting.

### Keep The Cascade Shallow To Preserve Coherence

Deep cascades, where goals decompose through many organizational layers, accumulate distortion at each level and become impossible to keep coherent. By the time a goal reaches an individual contributor through four layers of decomposition, its connection to strategy is often unrecognizable. Keep the cascade as shallow as the organization allows: company strategy to team objectives, with individuals connecting their work to team objectives without a formal individual OKR layer in most cases.

Individual OKRs, where used, should be sparse and focused on outcomes the individual genuinely owns, not a task list. The deeper the cascade, the more the system resembles performance management rather than focus and alignment, and the more it generates the gaming and bureaucracy that destroy OKR value.

## Common Traps

### Arithmetic Cascade Treating Teams As Interchangeable

Slicing a parent key result equally across teams. The trap is lost autonomy and inability to adapt, because the slice assumes identical contribution.

### Disconnected Team Goals Pulling Sideways

Team OKRs that succeed while strategy fails. The trap is local success that does not compound into organizational outcomes.

### Rigid Cascade That Blocks Mid-Cycle Adaptation

Goals that cannot change when teams learn. The trap is chasing stale targets or quietly abandoning the cascade.

### Sandbagging Under Performance Pressure

Safe targets set to guarantee hitting one hundred percent. The trap is an organization that systematically under-aims because ambition is punished.

### Unresolved Cross-Team Conflicts

Goals that conflict surfacing as mid-cycle blockers. The trap is firefighting that could have been prevented by pre-cycle alignment.

### Deep Cascade Accumulating Distortion

Goals decomposing through many layers until connection to strategy is lost. The trap is a system that resembles performance management more than focus.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Team objectives align to strategic goals through shared intent, not arithmetic decomposition into identical slices.
- [ ] Every team can trace a clear, material line of sight from its objectives to company strategy.
- [ ] The cascade allows teams to revise key results when they learn, with changes visible and documented.
- [ ] Committed and aspirational goals are separated, and OKR scores are not the sole performance input, to prevent sandbagging.
- [ ] Proposed key results are reviewed for real stretch rather than pre-achieved safe bets.
- [ ] Cross-team goal conflicts are surfaced and resolved before the cycle begins.
- [ ] The cascade is kept shallow enough to preserve coherence and avoid performance-management bureaucracy.
