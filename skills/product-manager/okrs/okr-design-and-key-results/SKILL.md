# OKR Design And Key Results

OKR design is the craft of turning strategic intent into measurable commitment. An objective names the qualitative direction; key results prove whether the direction was reached. The quality of the key results determines whether the OKR focuses the team or generates bureaucracy. Done well, key results are sharp, measurable, and connected to real outcomes, so that hitting them genuinely means success. Done poorly, they count activity, overlap redundantly, or set targets that are either safely easy or demoralizingly impossible, producing a quarterly ritual that no decision references. Agents often draft key results that restate the objective in vaguer terms or that measure work produced rather than value created.

The harm this skill prevents is goal theater, the tidy OKR spreadsheet that everyone completes and no one believes. When key results measure outputs, teams ship volume without impact. When they are vague, no one can tell whether they were achieved. When they overlap, they measure one thing three ways and miss the rest of the objective.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how do we write good key results", "are these OKRs any good", "should this be an outcome or output measure", or "how many key results per objective". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing OKRs that are syntactically correct but behaviorally empty.

## Core Rules

### Make Objectives Qualitative And Directional, Key Results Numeric And Verifiable

An objective is a statement of what kind of outcome the team seeks and why it matters. It should be memorable, directional, and free of numbers, because its job is to inspire and orient. A key result is a measurable proof that the objective was achieved; it must contain a number, a timeframe, and a definition of done so that completion is binary rather than a matter of opinion. Mixing the two produces objectives that are neither inspiring nor verifiable and key results that are neither numeric nor clear.

A strong objective answers where the team is trying to go. Strong key results answer how we will know we got there. If a key result cannot be scored as met or not met without subjective judgment, it is not yet a key result; rewrite it until completion is unambiguous.

### Write Key Results That Measure Distinct Facets Of The Objective

Each key result should illuminate a different dimension of the objective, so that hitting all of them gives genuine evidence of success rather than measuring the same thing multiple times. If all key results move together, they are probably one metric in disguise, and the objective is under-measured. A well-designed set of key results covers the objective from complementary angles: breadth, depth, quality, and timing, so that no single dimension can be gamed to claim success while others fail.

Test the set by asking whether a team could hit all key results while failing the objective. If so, the key results are measuring the wrong things or missing a critical facet. Add or revise until hitting all key results genuinely constitutes achieving the objective.

### Prefer Outcome Measures Over Output Measures

Outcome key results measure a change in the world: retention improved, activation rose, latency fell, churn dropped. Output key results measure work produced: features shipped, experiments run, tickets closed. Outputs are easy to count and easy to game, which is why teams default to them. But shipping a feature does not prove it helped anyone. The strongest key results measure the user or business change the work is meant to cause.

Use output key results only when the output is itself the commitment, such as a compliance deadline or a launch that must happen. Otherwise, ask what change the output is supposed to produce, and measure that. When an outcome cannot be measured within the cycle, choose a proxy that still reflects value and label it honestly as a proxy.

### Calibrate Ambition Against Commitment Level

Stretch goals can motivate, but impossible goals demoralize and invite gaming. Decide explicitly whether each OKR is committed, meaning the team is expected to hit it and resources are planned accordingly, or aspirational, meaning partial achievement is acceptable and the goal is meant to pull ambition. The distinction changes how targets are set and how scores are interpreted. Mixing the two without labeling them confuses everyone, because the same score means different things for each type.

For committed OKRs, set targets the team genuinely believes it can reach, because missing committed goals should be rare and diagnostic. For aspirational OKRs, set targets that scare the team a little, and communicate that hitting seventy percent is a strong result. Never relabel a missed committed goal as aspirational after the fact.

### Limit The Number Of OKRs Before Focus Dilutes

Every additional objective competes for the same finite attention. A team with five objectives effectively has none, because no single goal can be decisive. A practical ceiling is three objectives per team, each with three to four key results. Beyond that, focus dilutes and teams context-switch instead of making real progress on anything. When stakeholders demand more goals, the right response is prioritization, not addition.

Reserve OKR slots for the outcomes that matter most this cycle. Capture lower-priority work as a backlog or as committed operational work that does not consume an objective. Conflating everything the team does with OKRs turns the system into a task list and destroys its focusing power.

### Ensure Key Results Are Controllable And Attributable

A key result the team cannot influence through its work is frustrating and demotivating, because effort will not move it. Choose key results that sit close enough to the team's work that the team has real leverage, while still measuring outcomes rather than activity. If the only outcome metrics are far downstream and influenced by many factors outside the team's control, choose a leading indicator the team can move that predicts the outcome, and be clear about the causal assumption.

Attributability also matters for shared outcomes. When multiple teams contribute to the same outcome, either assign each team a key result reflecting its specific contribution, or define a shared key result with clear ownership of the levers each team owns. Ambiguous shared key results lead to diffuse accountability where no one feels ownership.

## Common Traps

### Output Key Results Disguised As Outcomes

Counting features shipped or experiments run. The trap is volume that feels productive but proves nothing about impact.

### Redundant Key Results Measuring One Thing

Three key results that all move together. The trap is the appearance of comprehensive measurement that actually covers one dimension.

### Vague Key Results Without Verifiable Completion

Key results that require subjective scoring. The trap is end-of-quarter arguments about whether the goal was met, eroding trust in the system.

### Mixing Committed And Aspirational Without Labels

Setting stretch targets and judging them as committed. The trap is a culture where teams feel they failed despite strong results, or where sandbagging becomes rational.

### Too Many OKRs Diluting Focus

Adding an objective for every stakeholder request. The trap is inclusion mistaken for ambition, with nothing receiving decisive attention.

### Key Results The Team Cannot Influence

Downstream outcomes driven by many factors. The trap is demoralization and gaming, because effort does not visibly move the number.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each objective is qualitative and directional; each key result is numeric, time-bound, and independently verifiable.
- [ ] Key results measure distinct facets of the objective, not the same dimension three ways.
- [ ] Key results measure outcomes, not outputs, unless the output is itself the commitment.
- [ ] Each OKR is explicitly labeled committed or aspirational, with targets calibrated accordingly.
- [ ] The number of objectives is capped, typically three per team, to preserve focus.
- [ ] Key results are controllable by the team and attributable, with clear ownership for shared outcomes.
- [ ] Hitting all key results genuinely constitutes achieving the objective, with no obvious way to game the set.
