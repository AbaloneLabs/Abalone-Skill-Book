# OKR Cadence And Review Cycle

An OKR set without a cadence is a document, not a management tool. The value of OKRs comes from the decisions they trigger during the cycle, not from the score assigned at the end. Cadence is the rhythm of check-ins, reviews, and adjustments that turns goals into a living system. Done well, the cadence surfaces blockers early, updates confidence as evidence accumulates, and creates moments where the team decides to adjust, double down, or abandon. Done poorly, the cadence becomes status theater, weekly reports of green that hide problems until the quarter ends and the goals are missed. Agents often treat OKR cadence as a reporting schedule rather than as a decision rhythm.

The harm this skill prevents is the OKR cycle that produces a final score and no intermediate decisions. When check-ins only record status, problems fester, confidence never updates, and the team arrives at quarter-end surprised by a miss it could have corrected months earlier. The cadence exists to make course correction possible, not to generate paperwork.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how often should we review OKRs", "how do we run OKR check-ins", "how do we score OKRs", or "what should happen at mid-quarter". The goal is to prevent the agent from designing a cadence that reports rather than decides.

## Core Rules

### Choose A Cadence That Allows Real Adjustment

The check-in rhythm must be frequent enough that the team can act on what it learns, but not so frequent that it becomes noise. For quarterly OKRs, a monthly check-in is often too sparse and a weekly one too granular; a biweekly or monthly cadence with a dedicated mid-cycle review usually works. The test is whether the cadence leaves enough time to change course when a goal is off track. If the next check-in after a problem appears is the end-of-quarter review, the cadence is too slow to be useful.

Match cadence to the velocity of the product. Faster-moving products with shorter feedback loops may benefit from shorter OKR cycles or more frequent check-ins. Slower-moving enterprise products may need longer cycles. The cadence should fit the reality of how quickly the team learns and can act, not a generic quarterly default.

### Make Check-Ins Decision-Moments, Not Status Reports

A check-in that only records a confidence score and a progress percentage wastes the team's time. The purpose of the check-in is to surface what has changed, identify blockers, and decide whether to adjust the plan. Structure the check-in around questions that trigger decisions: what did we learn since last time, what is blocking progress, is our confidence rising or falling, and should we change anything about the approach or the goal itself. If a check-in never results in a decision, it has become theater.

Encourage teams to flag risks and falling confidence early, and treat those flags as useful rather than as failure. A check-in culture where confidence only ever rises is hiding problems. The most valuable check-ins are the ones where confidence drops, because that is when the system is doing its job of surfacing reality.

### Update Confidence Based On Evidence, Not Hope

Confidence scores are meant to reflect the team's honest assessment of whether the goal will be reached, given current evidence. They should move as evidence accumulates: up when results exceed expectations, down when they fall short. The common failure is sticky confidence that never updates, either because the team does not want to signal trouble or because the score is set once and forgotten. A confidence score that does not move during the cycle is not being used.

Base confidence on leading indicators and early results, not on optimism about the future. If the leading indicators that predict the key result are tracking below plan, confidence should fall even if there is time to recover. Honest confidence gives leadership early warning; optimistic confidence guarantees late surprises.

### Run A Genuine Mid-Cycle Review

The mid-cycle review is the most important checkpoint, because it is the last realistic opportunity to adjust before the cycle ends. Treat it as a decision point, not a formality. At mid-cycle, ask whether each goal is still relevant, whether the approach is working, whether resources are correctly allocated, and whether any goal should be killed, changed, or re-prioritized. Goals that have become irrelevant should be retired rather than chased; goals that are off track need a credible recovery plan or a deliberate decision to accept the miss.

The mid-cycle review is also where aspirational and committed goals are treated differently. A committed goal off track at mid-cycle requires intervention: more resources, scope reduction elsewhere, or escalation. An aspirational goal off track at mid-cycle may be perfectly healthy, on track for a strong partial result. The review must interpret status through the lens of commitment level.

### Score Honestly At End Of Cycle And Extract Learning

End-of-cycle scoring should be honest, not generous. Inflated scores erode trust in the system and prevent learning, because they disguise what actually happened. Score each key result against its target, interpret the score through the committed-versus-aspirational lens, and use the scoring to extract learning: what worked, what did not, what assumptions were wrong, and what should change next cycle. The scoring is valuable only if it feeds the next cycle's design.

Resist the temptation to score generously to protect morale or to make the cycle look successful. Honest scoring, paired with a culture that treats misses as learning rather than failure, produces better goals over time. Generous scoring produces a system that everyone knows is fictional and that therefore influences nothing.

### Build A Feedback Loop From Scoring Back To OKR Design

The way OKRs scored this cycle reveals flaws in how they were designed. Key results that were impossible to measure, objectives that proved irrelevant mid-cycle, targets that were wildly mis-calibrated: all of these are signals about how to design better OKRs next time. After scoring, conduct a brief retrospective on the OKR design itself, not only on the results, and carry the learning into the next cycle's design. Without this loop, the same design mistakes recur quarter after quarter.

This is also where metric quality improves. A key result that was hard to measure or that did not predict the outcome should be replaced with a better measure next cycle. OKR design is a skill that improves through deliberate reflection on what the previous cycle revealed.

## Common Traps

### Check-Ins As Status Theater

Weekly reports of green that never surface problems. The trap is a cadence that produces paperwork and no decisions.

### Sticky Confidence That Never Updates

Confidence set once and never revised. The trap is late surprises, because early warning was suppressed.

### No Real Mid-Cycle Decision Point

Treating mid-cycle as a status update rather than a decision. The trap is missed goals that could have been corrected with timely adjustment.

### Generous End-Of-Cycle Scoring

Inflating scores to protect morale or appearance. The trap is a fictional system that everyone knows is fictional and that therefore changes nothing.

### No Learning Loop Into Next Cycle's Design

Scoring without reflecting on OKR design quality. The trap is recurring design mistakes, quarter after quarter.

### One-Size Cadence For All Teams

Imposing a single cadence regardless of product velocity. The trap is cadence that fits some teams and actively harms others.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The check-in cadence is frequent enough to allow course correction and fits the product's velocity.
- [ ] Check-ins are structured around decisions, not status reporting, and regularly result in adjustments.
- [ ] Confidence scores update based on evidence and leading indicators, and falling confidence is welcomed as signal.
- [ ] A genuine mid-cycle review evaluates relevance, approach, and resource allocation, and retires stale goals.
- [ ] Committed and aspirational goals are interpreted differently at every checkpoint.
- [ ] End-of-cycle scoring is honest, and misses are treated as learning rather than failure.
- [ ] A retrospective on OKR design quality feeds into the next cycle's design, improving metrics and calibration over time.
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
