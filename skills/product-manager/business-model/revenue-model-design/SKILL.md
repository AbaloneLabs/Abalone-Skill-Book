# Revenue Model Design

A revenue model is the architecture of how a product captures value, not just what it charges. The choice of model shapes who buys, how they buy, what behavior the product rewards, and what the company optimizes. Done well, the model aligns value capture with value creation, so that the more the product helps customers, the more the company earns. Done poorly, the model fights the product, rewarding behavior that harms customers, or capturing value in a way that customers resent or that competitors undercut. Agents often pick a revenue model by copying the category default without examining whether it fits how value is actually created and perceived.

The harm this skill prevents is a model that looks standard and sustainable but structurally misaligns incentives. A product priced per seat rewards low adoption; a product priced per transaction rewards friction; a freemium model with no conversion path burns capital indefinitely. The model is a strategic choice, not a pricing detail.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "what revenue model should we use", "should we be subscription or usage-based", "how does our model align with customer value", or "why is our model not converting". The goal is to prevent the agent from selecting a model on convention rather than on fit.

## Core Rules

### Align Value Capture With How And When Customers Perceive Value

The strongest revenue models charge in the same rhythm and on the same dimension in which the customer experiences value. If value accrues continuously over time, a subscription aligns well. If value comes in discrete transactions, transaction-based pricing aligns. If value scales with usage, usage-based pricing aligns. Mismatch between value perception and value capture creates friction: customers feel they are paying for something they have not yet received, or they receive value they are never asked to pay for.

Map when and how the target customer perceives value, then test whether the model charges in alignment with that. A model that charges before value is realized, or in a unit disconnected from value, creates resentment and suppresses conversion. The closer charge tracks value, the less sales friction and the more sustainable the growth.

### Understand What Behavior Each Model Rewards And Punishes

Every revenue model creates incentives, for the company and for the customer. A per-seat model rewards the company when customers add seats and punishes adoption that could reduce seat count through efficiency. A usage model rewards friction and complexity that drives more usage. A flat subscription rewards delivering enough value to prevent churn but not so much that the price looks low relative to value. Examine the incentives the model creates, because customers and internal teams will optimize for them, intentionally or not.

Choose a model whose incentives push toward the behavior you want. If the strategy depends on broad adoption and viral spread, a per-seat model that penalizes adding users works against you. If the strategy depends on deepening usage, a model that rewards usage reinforces it. The model is an engine of incentives, not just a billing mechanism.

### Match The Model To The Customer's Buying Motion

Different customers buy in different ways, and the revenue model must fit the buying motion of the target segment. Enterprise customers often prefer annual contracts with predictable budgets and procurement processes; self-serve small teams prefer monthly subscriptions they can start and stop without negotiation; developers often prefer usage-based models that require no upfront commitment. A model that fits one buying motion will fail in another, regardless of how well it captures value in theory.

Consider the entire buying journey, including who approves spend, how budget is allocated, and what procurement friction exists. A model that creates procurement friction for the target segment will suppress conversion even when the value is clear. Sometimes the right model is determined less by value alignment than by buying-motion fit.

### Evaluate The Unit Economics The Model Produces

A revenue model is only viable if it produces healthy unit economics: the cost to acquire and serve a customer must be recoverable within an acceptable period given the revenue the model generates. A model can align perfectly with value and still fail economically if the revenue per customer is too low relative to acquisition and serving costs, or if churn is too high to recover the acquisition cost. Test the model against projected unit economics, not just against revenue.

Pay attention to how the model affects gross margin, expansion revenue, and churn. Subscription models build compounding revenue but require low churn. Transaction models have lower committed revenue but can scale with usage. Each model has a different economic shape, and the choice should fit the cost structure and growth expectations of the business.

### Consider Competitive And Market Norms Without Being Bound By Them

Customers have expectations shaped by what competitors and adjacent markets charge, and a model that violates those expectations faces adoption friction even if it is theoretically superior. Understand the prevailing model in the market and the reasons for it, because deviating requires educating the customer and overcoming inertia. However, prevailing norms are not destiny; the most valuable model innovations often come from rejecting the category default when it misaligns with value.

Distinguish between deviation that creates advantage and deviation that creates friction. Deviating to align value capture with value creation is advantage; deviating to capture more value than customers perceive is friction that invites competition.

### Design The Model To Be Simple Enough To Understand And Trust

Complexity in a revenue model erodes trust. If customers cannot predict what they will pay, they hesitate to commit, and procurement becomes an obstacle. If the model has many tiers, surcharges, minimums, and exceptions, the cost of sale rises and customers suspect they are being overcharged. Simplicity is a feature of a revenue model, because predictability and transparency reduce buying friction.

Resist the temptation to add complexity to capture incremental value from edge cases. The value of precision in pricing is often outweighed by the friction complexity creates. A simpler model that slightly under-monetizes some segments may produce more total revenue than a complex model that captures every dollar but slows every deal.

## Common Traps

### Copying The Category Default Without Examining Fit

Adopting the prevailing model because everyone uses it. The trap is inheriting a misalignment between value and capture that the category has tolerated but that a better model could exploit.

### Model Incentives That Fight The Strategy

Choosing a model whose incentives push against the desired behavior. The trap is the company optimizing against its own strategy because the model rewards the wrong things.

### Charging Before Value Is Realized

Billing in advance of the customer experiencing value. The trap is conversion friction and churn, because customers pay for promise rather than result.

### Ignoring Buying-Motion Fit

Selecting a model that captures value well but requires a buying motion the target segment will not perform. The trap is a theoretically optimal model that fails in practice.

### Complexity That Erodes Trust

Adding tiers and exceptions to capture edge-case value. The trap is slower deals, procurement friction, and customer suspicion that erodes more value than it captures.

### Model That Produces Weak Unit Economics

A model that aligns with value but generates revenue too low or churn too high to recover costs. The trap is growth that destroys value with every customer added.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The model charges in alignment with when and how the target customer perceives value.
- [ ] The incentives the model creates, for company and customer, push toward the desired behavior.
- [ ] The model fits the buying motion of the target segment, including procurement and budget patterns.
- [ ] Projected unit economics, acquisition cost, serving cost, churn, and expansion, are healthy under the model.
- [ ] Market norms are understood, and any deviation is justified by value alignment rather than by greed.
- [ ] The model is simple enough that customers can predict and trust what they will pay.
- [ ] The model was chosen on fit, not merely on category convention.
