# Value Capture And Pricing Power

Value capture is the question of how much of the value a product creates can be claimed as revenue, and at what cost to adoption and growth. A product can create enormous value and capture very little of it, leaving money on the table or starving the business of resources. Or it can capture too aggressively, suppressing adoption, inviting competition, and slowing the very growth that creates long-term value. Pricing power is the ability to raise prices without losing disproportionate customers, and it is a function of the value delivered relative to alternatives, not of the company's wishes. Done well, value capture sustains the business while preserving the adoption that creates future value. Done poorly, it either underfunds the product or throttles its growth. Agents often treat pricing as a number to set rather than as a strategic balance between capture and growth.

The harm this skill prevents is the product that creates value but cannot sustain itself, or the product that captures value but stops growing. Both failures come from treating pricing as a static decision rather than as a continuous balance shaped by value, competition, and the product's stage.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how much should we charge", "do we have pricing power", "should we raise prices", or "are we capturing enough value". The goal is to prevent the agent from setting prices on cost-plus or competitor-matching logic without examining value capture dynamics.

## Core Rules

### Ground Pricing In Value Delivered, Not Cost Or Competitors

The strongest basis for price is the value the product creates for the customer relative to the next best alternative. Cost-plus pricing ignores value entirely and systematically under-prices high-value products while over-pricing low-value ones. Competitor-matching pricing surrenders the question of value to rivals and prevents the product from capturing the value its differentiation creates. Both approaches abdicate the strategic question of what the product is worth to the customer.

Estimate value through the lens of the customer's economics: what does the product save them, earn them, or enable, compared to the alternative? The gap between the product's value and the alternative's value defines the ceiling for capture. Price somewhere within that gap, leaving enough customer surplus to motivate adoption while capturing enough to sustain and grow the business.

### Assess Pricing Power Honestly Before Raising Prices

Pricing power is not a feeling; it is the observed willingness of customers to pay more without churning disproportionately. Before raising prices, assess the sources of pricing power: how differentiated the product is, how high switching costs are, how price-sensitive the target segment is, how strong the alternatives are, and how essential the product has become to the customer's operations. Where pricing power is weak, a price increase will lose more revenue through churn than it gains through higher rates.

Test pricing power through small, measured changes, through willingness-to-pay research anchored in behavior, and through analysis of which customers churned at previous price points. Honest assessment prevents the common error of raising prices on the assumption of power that the market does not actually grant.

### Balance Value Capture Against Adoption And Growth

Aggressive value capture suppresses adoption, and suppressed adoption slows the growth that creates long-term value, especially when network effects, data advantages, or switching costs compound over time. In some markets, the strategically correct choice is to capture less value now to accelerate adoption, build the moat, and capture more later from a stronger position. In others, under-capturing starves the business and never converts to advantage.

Make the tradeoff explicit. If the strategy depends on network effects or market share, lean toward lower capture to drive adoption. If the strategy depends on serving a price-insensitive segment deeply, lean toward higher capture. The right balance depends on the source of long-term advantage, not on a universal rule about pricing.

### Segment Capture By Willingness To Pay

Not all customers derive the same value or have the same willingness to pay. A single price either under-captures from high-value customers or over-prices for price-sensitive ones. Segmented capture, through tiers, packaging, or negotiated pricing, allows the product to capture more total value by aligning price with the value each segment receives. Done well, segmentation expands the market by serving price-sensitive segments while capturing full value from those who receive more.

Segmentation must be based on signals of value or willingness to pay, not on arbitrary distinctions. And it must remain simple enough that customers understand and trust it. Over-segmentation creates complexity that erodes the very trust and adoption it aims to expand.

### Recognize That Price Is A Signal Of Value And Positioning

Price communicates value. A low price can signal a premium product as cheap or unproven, suppressing adoption among customers who equate price with quality. A high price can signal confidence and exclusivity that attracts a segment that would ignore a cheaper product. The price is read by customers as information about what the product is and who it is for, separate from the actual economics.

Consider what the price signals about positioning, not only what it captures. Sometimes a higher price accelerates adoption among the target segment by signaling that the product belongs in a premium category, even though it suppresses adoption among price-sensitive segments the product does not want anyway.

### Revisit Capture As The Product And Market Mature

Value capture is not set once. As the product creates more value, as switching costs build, as network effects compound, and as competitors enter or exit, the optimal capture changes. A product that captured little at launch to drive adoption may have the power to capture more at maturity. A product facing new competition may need to capture less to defend share. Treat capture as a variable that is revisited as conditions change, not as a permanent setting.

Build the organizational habit of reviewing pricing and capture on a regular cadence, with the evidence of value delivered, competitive position, and observed willingness to pay. Inertia in pricing leaves value uncaptured or share undefended.

## Common Traps

### Cost-Plus Or Competitor-Matching Pricing

Setting price based on cost or rivals rather than on value delivered. The trap is systematic under-capture of high-value products and surrender of the pricing question to competitors.

### Assuming Pricing Power Without Evidence

Raising prices on the belief that customers will stay. The trap is churn that costs more revenue than the increase gains, because pricing power was assumed rather than tested.

### Maximizing Capture At The Expense Of Adoption

Pricing aggressively in a market where adoption compounds advantage. The trap is short-term revenue gains that sacrifice the long-term moat.

### Single Price For All Segments

Charging one price regardless of willingness to pay. The trap is under-capture from high-value segments and lost adoption among price-sensitive ones.

### Ignoring The Signal Price Sends

Treating price only as capture, not as positioning. The trap is a price that mispositions the product and suppresses adoption among the very segment it targets.

### Set-And-Forget Pricing

Failing to revisit capture as the product matures and the market shifts. The trap is leaving value uncaptured or share undefended long after conditions changed.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Pricing is grounded in value delivered relative to alternatives, not in cost-plus or competitor matching.
- [ ] Pricing power has been assessed honestly from differentiation, switching costs, segment sensitivity, and alternative strength before any increase.
- [ ] The capture-versus-adoption tradeoff is explicit and aligned with the source of long-term advantage.
- [ ] Capture is segmented by willingness to pay, using signals of value, while remaining simple and trustworthy.
- [ ] The signal the price sends about positioning has been considered alongside the value it captures.
- [ ] Pricing and capture are revisited on a regular cadence as the product and market mature.
