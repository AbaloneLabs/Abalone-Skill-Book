# Unit Economics And Economics Modeling

Unit economics are the financial mechanics of a single customer relationship: what it costs to acquire and serve that customer, what revenue they generate over their lifetime, and whether the relationship creates or destroys value. These mechanics determine whether growth creates or consumes value, whether the business can scale, and whether the product model is sustainable. Done well, unit economics modeling reveals which customers, segments, and channels are profitable and which quietly destroy value. Done poorly, it produces aggregate numbers that hide the customers bleeding cash, or it uses assumptions so optimistic that the model justifies unsustainable growth. Agents often compute lifetime value with heroic assumptions about retention and expansion, producing a number that flatters the model rather than describing reality.

The harm this skill prevents is growth that destroys value. A business can add customers rapidly while losing money on every one, and aggregate revenue growth can mask deteriorating economics until the model collapses. Honest unit economics are the early warning system that distinguishes valuable growth from value-destroying growth.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "are our unit economics healthy", "what is our LTV to CAC ratio", "is this segment profitable", or "can we scale this model". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing optimistic aggregate economics that hide unsustainable per-customer dynamics.

## Core Rules

### Model At The Level Of The Individual Customer, Not The Aggregate

Aggregate metrics hide the distribution that determines whether the business works. An aggregate lifetime value that looks healthy can rest on a small minority of long-tenured customers while the majority churn before paying back acquisition cost. Model economics at the level of cohorts and segments, so that the variation is visible. Only by seeing the distribution can you tell which customers create value and which destroy it.

Segment the model by the dimensions that matter: acquisition channel, segment, plan, geography, and tenure. The segments that pay back quickly and expand are the engine; the segments that churn before payback are the leak. Aggregate economics that average across them conceal the leaks and prevent corrective action.

### Use Conservative Assumptions For Retention And Expansion

Lifetime value is extraordinarily sensitive to assumptions about how long customers stay and how much they expand over time. Optimistic assumptions, projecting low churn and steady expansion indefinitely, produce lifetime value figures that justify almost any acquisition spend. These figures are almost always wrong, because churn tends to be higher and expansion less reliable than the model assumes. Use conservative assumptions grounded in observed cohort behavior, and show how lifetime value changes under worse-than-expected retention.

Prefer payback period as the primary metric over lifetime value ratio. Payback period, the time to recover acquisition cost from contribution margin, depends on fewer and more reliable assumptions. A business that recovers acquisition cost within a reasonable period can tolerate uncertainty about long lifetime; a business relying on distant lifetime to justify current spend is fragile.

### Separate Contribution Margin From Revenue

Revenue overstates the economics of a customer because it ignores the cost of serving them. The relevant figure is contribution margin: revenue minus the variable costs directly attributable to serving that customer, including infrastructure, support, payment processing, and any usage-based costs. A customer who generates high revenue but also high serving cost may contribute less margin than a smaller customer who is cheap to serve. Modeling on revenue rather than contribution margin systematically overstates profitability.

Be rigorous about which costs are variable versus fixed. Costs that scale with customers, even if labeled fixed for accounting purposes, are economically variable and must be subtracted to understand true unit economics. The distinction determines whether adding customers creates or destroys value.

### Account For The Full Cost Of Acquisition

Customer acquisition cost is often understated by counting only direct marketing spend. The full cost includes sales salaries and commissions, marketing team costs, onboarding and implementation effort, content and brand investment, and the discounting or free period used to win the customer. Undercounting acquisition cost inflates the apparent health of unit economics and justifies unsustainable spending. Count the fully loaded cost of acquiring a customer, allocated sensibly across channels and segments.

Equally important is to attribute acquisition cost to the right channel and segment. Blended acquisition cost hides the channels that acquire customers cheaply from those that acquire them expensively. Channel-level and segment-level attribution reveals where acquisition spend creates value and where it is wasted.

### Recognize That Unit Economics Change Over Time And With Scale

Unit economics are not fixed. Acquisition cost tends to rise as the easiest customers are won and channels saturate. Gross margin can improve with scale as fixed costs spread, or deteriorate as complexity grows. Retention can improve as the product matures, or worsen as the customer base broadens. Expansion can accelerate or stall. A model that assumes current economics persist indefinitely will mislead, because the dynamics shift as the business scales.

Model how unit economics are likely to evolve: will acquisition get harder, will margin improve or erode, will retention hold? The trajectory matters as much as the current snapshot. A business with modest current economics that improve with scale may be far more valuable than one with strong current economics that deteriorate.

### Distinguish Profitable Growth From Value-Destroying Growth

The decisive question is whether each additional customer creates or destroys value. Growth that adds customers with healthy unit economics compounds value. Growth that adds customers who never pay back acquisition cost destroys value with every addition, even as aggregate revenue rises. The aggregate can look like success while the per-customer reality is failure. Judge growth by its unit economics, not by its top-line rate.

This distinction is especially important when growth is funded by raising acquisition spend. If the marginal customers acquired by higher spend have worse economics than the average, raising spend destroys value even as it accelerates growth. Always model the marginal, not only the average.

## Common Traps

### Optimistic Lifetime Value Assumptions

Projecting low churn and steady expansion indefinitely. The trap is lifetime value figures that justify any acquisition spend, when the real economics are far weaker.

### Aggregate Metrics That Hide The Distribution

Reporting blended LTV and CAC that average across segments. The trap is profitable segments cross-subsidizing value-destroying ones without anyone noticing.

### Revenue Instead Of Contribution Margin

Modeling on revenue and ignoring serving cost. The trap is overstated profitability for customers who are expensive to serve.

### Undercounting Acquisition Cost

Counting only direct marketing spend. The trap is understated CAC that makes destructive acquisition spending look healthy.

### Assuming Static Economics

Treating current unit economics as permanent. The trap is a model that ignores how acquisition, margin, and retention shift with scale.

### Funding Growth With Deteriorating Marginal Economics

Raising acquisition spend to grow without checking the economics of marginal customers. The trap is growth that destroys more value with every customer added.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Economics are modeled at the cohort and segment level, making the distribution visible, not only in aggregate.
- [ ] Retention and expansion assumptions are conservative and grounded in observed cohort behavior.
- [ ] Payback period is used as a primary metric, reducing dependence on long-lifetime assumptions.
- [ ] Contribution margin, not revenue, is the basis for per-customer profitability.
- [ ] Acquisition cost is fully loaded and attributed to channel and segment.
- [ ] The model accounts for how unit economics evolve with scale, not only the current snapshot.
- [ ] Growth is judged by marginal unit economics, distinguishing value-creating from value-destroying customer acquisition.
