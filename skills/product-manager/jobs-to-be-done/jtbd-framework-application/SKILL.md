# JTBD Framework Application

Jobs-To-Be-Done reframes product work around the progress a customer is trying to make, rather than the features a product contains. The central insight is that customers do not buy products; they hire them to get a job done, and they will fire a product that does the job poorly and hire one that does it better. This reframing is powerful because it anchors everything in the customer's underlying motivation, which is more stable than any feature preference. Done well, JTBD reveals why customers switch, what they are really evaluating, and where unmet jobs create opportunity. Done poorly, it becomes a labeling exercise where every feature gets a "job" tag without changing any decision.

The harm this skill prevents is building better versions of features customers do not actually care about. When the team thinks in features, it optimizes the wrong thing and loses to a competitor who understood the job better. Agents often produce JTBD statements that are either so abstract they apply to everything or so specific they describe a single feature.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "what job is our product hired for", "how do we apply JTBD", "why do customers switch to or from us", or "what is the real problem we solve". The goal is to prevent the agent from using JTBD vocabulary without adopting the framework's actual analytical discipline.

## Core Rules

### Define The Job At The Right Level Of Abstraction

A job statement must be abstract enough to be stable and specific enough to be actionable. Too abstract, and every product on earth serves it; too specific, and it is just a feature description. The useful middle describes the functional outcome the customer is trying to achieve, independent of any solution. A test is whether the same job could be satisfied by very different solutions, including non-product ones.

State jobs as verbs in the customer's language, focused on what they are trying to accomplish, not how. "Manage my team's expenses so I can approve spending without chasing receipts" is a job; "expense report software" is a category. The job should remain true even if the technology to do it changes entirely, because that stability is what makes the framework useful for long-term strategy.

### Separate Functional, Emotional, And Social Jobs

Customers hire products for more than functional outcomes. They also seek emotional outcomes, how the product makes them feel, and social outcomes, how they believe they are perceived by others. A complete JTBD analysis names all three, because competing only on the functional job misses dimensions that drive choice and loyalty. A product that wins functionally can still lose to one that makes the customer feel competent or look good to peers.

For each target customer, ask what functional progress they seek, what emotional state they want to reach or avoid, and how they want to be seen by others. These layered jobs explain why customers choose products that are not the most functionally capable: the emotional and social jobs may matter more.

### Identify The Forces That Block Progress

A job that is important and unsatisfied is an opportunity, but understanding why it remains unsatisfied is what makes the insight actionable. Customers fail to make progress because of forces that push them away from change and forces that pull them toward it. The pushes are dissatisfaction with the current way and the pull of something better. The anxieties and habits are what hold them back: worry about the new solution and attachment to the familiar current way.

Map these forces for the switching moment. A new product succeeds when the push and pull together exceed the anxiety and habit holding the customer to the status quo. If the anxieties are high, no amount of feature improvement overcomes them; the product must reduce anxiety and ease transition. This is why objectively better products often fail to displace incumbents.

### Use JTBD To Find Competition Outside Your Category

Because jobs are solution-independent, the real competitors are all the ways customers currently get the job done, including doing nothing, manual workarounds, and products in other categories. JTBD expands the competitive set in a way that feature analysis cannot. A product whose job is "help me feel in control of my finances" competes with spreadsheets, advisors, apps, and the decision to simply not think about it.

This reframing reveals where share is actually being lost and won. A product can dominate its category and still lose customers to a solution in another category that does the same job better or more cheaply.

### Anchor Roadmap And Positioning To The Job

JTBD only creates value if it changes decisions. Once the job is defined, every roadmap item should be traceable to helping the customer make that progress, and positioning should describe the progress the product enables rather than the features it contains. If the job analysis changes nothing about what is built or how it is sold, it was an academic exercise.

The job also defines what to measure. Metrics should reflect whether customers are making the progress they hired the product to enable, not only whether they are using features. A product can have high feature usage and still fail the job, which shows up as churn despite engagement.

### Validate Jobs From Behavior, Not Stated Preference

Customers rarely articulate their jobs cleanly, because the job is often latent. They describe solutions, features, and frustrations. Discovering the job requires inference from switching behavior, from the workarounds they build, and from the moments of struggle. A job inferred from observed behavior is stronger than one taken from a direct answer to "what are you trying to accomplish".

Look especially at the forces around switching: why customers fired a previous solution and hired yours, and why they fired yours and hired another. These switching moments reveal the job more clearly than any interview about preferences.

## Common Traps

### Job Statements That Are Just Feature Descriptions

Writing the job as "I want to use a dashboard" rather than the progress sought. The trap is that the "job" collapses back into features and provides no strategic reframing.

### Jobs So Abstract They Explain Everything

Defining the job so broadly that every product serves it. The trap is a statement that sounds profound but cannot distinguish competitors or guide decisions.

### Ignoring Emotional And Social Jobs

Capturing only the functional outcome. The trap is missing why customers choose products that are not the most functionally capable, because the emotional and social jobs were never named.

### Assuming A Better Product Wins

Believing that superior features overcome switching barriers. The trap is building objectively better products that still fail to displace incumbents, because anxiety and habit were never addressed.

### Taking Stated Jobs At Face Value

Asking customers their job and recording the answer. The trap is that jobs are often latent and must be inferred from behavior, not collected as self-report.

### JTBD As Vocabulary Without Discipline

Tagging things with "job" language without changing roadmap, metrics, or positioning. The trap is the appearance of a framework without its analytical benefit.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Job statements are at a level of abstraction that is stable across solutions and specific enough to guide decisions.
- [ ] Functional, emotional, and social jobs are all identified for the target customer.
- [ ] The forces blocking progress, push, pull, anxiety, and habit, are mapped for the switching moment.
- [ ] The competitive set includes solutions outside the product category that get the same job done.
- [ ] Roadmap items and positioning trace back to helping the customer make the defined progress.
- [ ] Metrics reflect whether customers are making progress on the job, not only feature usage.
- [ ] Jobs are inferred from switching behavior and workarounds, not only from stated preference.
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
