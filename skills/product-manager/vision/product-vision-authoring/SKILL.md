# Product Vision Authoring

A product vision is a statement of the future the product is meant to create and why it matters. It is not a feature list, a tagline, or a forecast. Its job is to align a diverse group of people around a shared direction so that thousands of decentralized decisions point the same way. Done well, a vision makes tradeoffs easier, energizes the team, and survives changes in tactics. Done poorly, it is either so vague it could describe any product or so specific it becomes obsolete the moment the market shifts. Agents often produce visions that sound inspiring but contain no actual direction, or visions that are really roadmaps dressed up as aspiration.

The harm this skill prevents is strategic drift. Without a clear vision, every team optimizes locally, priorities shift with the loudest stakeholder, and the product accumulates features without moving toward anything. A weak vision provides the illusion of alignment while leaving every real decision unresolved.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "what is our product vision", "how do we write a vision statement", "is our vision any good", or "how ambitious should our vision be". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing polished vision language that cannot guide a single decision.

## Core Rules

### Make The Vision Describe A Future State For A Specific Customer

A vision must paint a world that does not yet exist, in which a specific customer is dramatically better off because of the product. The specificity of the customer and the nature of the better-off state are what make the vision directional. A vision that names no customer describes no one's future, and a vision that names no transformation describes no change worth pursuing.

State whose world changes, what becomes possible that was not before, and why that matters. The future state should be materially different from today, not an incremental extension of current features. If the vision describes a world that is five percent better, it is not pulling the team toward anything; it is summarizing the current trajectory.

### Separate Vision From Strategy And Roadmap

Vision is the destination, the future state the product aims to create. Strategy is the path, the choices about how to get there. Roadmap is the plan, the sequenced work for the near term. Conflating these produces visions that are really plans, which become obsolete and lose their guiding power. The vision should be stable enough to outlast multiple strategies and many roadmaps.

Test the separation by asking whether the vision would still hold if the current strategy failed and had to be replaced. If the vision depends on a specific approach, it is strategy, not vision. A durable vision names the destination; the route to it can and should change as the team learns.

### Anchor Ambition To A Real Problem, Not To Aspiration Alone

A strong vision is ambitious, but ambition without a real problem is fantasy. The vision must connect to a genuine, important, enduring problem that the target customer faces, and the future state must represent a meaningful resolution of that problem. Ambition that is not tied to a problem reads as grandiosity and fails to motivate or guide.

The problem anchor also keeps the vision honest. If the problem is small or transient, the vision cannot be large no matter how the language is scaled. The scale of the vision is earned by the scale of the problem it resolves, not by the adjectives used to describe it.

### Ensure The Vision Constrains Decisions, Not Just Inspires

The practical test of a vision is whether it rules anything out. A vision that is compatible with every possible decision provides no direction. A useful vision creates tension with some opportunities, because those opportunities, however attractive, do not move toward the envisioned future. If a team can pursue any feature and claim it serves the vision, the vision is too broad.

Apply the constraint test: name two plausible initiatives that the vision would cause the team to reject. If you cannot, the vision is not yet specific enough to guide tradeoffs. The rejections are where the vision does its real work, because they protect focus.

### Make The Vision Communicable And Memorable

A vision that cannot be repeated from memory by people outside the leadership team cannot guide decentralized decisions. Brevity and clarity matter more than completeness. A vision should be short enough to say in one breath and vivid enough to remember. The supporting narrative can be longer, but the core vision must be portable.

Avoid jargon, internal acronyms, and abstract nouns that mean different things to different people. The vision will be interpreted by engineers, designers, salespeople, partners, and customers, and it must mean the same thing to all of them or it will fragment rather than align.

### Revisit The Vision On Strategy Cadence, Not Feature Cadence

Visions should be stable, but not immortal. Markets shift, problems evolve, and sometimes the envisioned future arrives or becomes irrelevant. Review the vision on a slow cadence tied to strategy reviews, not in every planning cycle. Frequent revision destroys the vision's anchoring power, because no one invests in a direction that changes quarterly. But refusing ever to revisit a vision that no longer fits the world is dogma.

The signal to revisit is when the vision consistently fails to constrain or guide decisions, when the market has structurally changed, or when the team can no longer connect their work to it meaningfully.

## Common Traps

### Vision As Feature Wishlist

Describing the vision as a set of capabilities the product will have. The trap is a vision that becomes a roadmap and loses its guiding, stable quality.

### Vague Aspiration That Could Describe Anyone

Language so abstract that any product could claim it. The trap is a vision that provides the comfort of alignment without any actual direction.

### Ambition Unmoored From Problem

Grand language about changing the world without naming a real problem. The trap is a vision that feels inspiring but cannot guide tradeoffs or motivate sustained effort.

### Vision That Rules Nothing Out

A vision compatible with every initiative. The trap is the appearance of direction while every decision remains unguided.

### Conflating Vision With Strategy

Embedding the chosen path into the vision. The trap is a vision that must be rewritten whenever the strategy changes, destroying its stability.

### Never Revisiting A Stale Vision

Treating the vision as sacred long after the market moved. The trap is a vision that no longer connects to the work and becomes decorative.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The vision describes a specific future state in which a named customer is materially better off.
- [ ] The vision is distinct from strategy and roadmap and would survive a change of approach.
- [ ] Ambition is anchored to a real, important, enduring problem, not to aspiration alone.
- [ ] The vision constrains decisions; at least two plausible initiatives would be rejected by it.
- [ ] The vision is short, clear, and free of jargon, so it can be repeated from memory across functions.
- [ ] The vision is reviewed on a slow strategy cadence, not rewritten every planning cycle.
- [ ] The team can connect their daily work to the vision, or the vision has been flagged as stale.
