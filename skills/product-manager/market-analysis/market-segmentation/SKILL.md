# Market Segmentation

Segmentation is the act of dividing a broad market into groups that differ in ways that matter for product decisions. It is not labeling people by demographic convenience. A useful segment is a group of customers who share the same important problem, the same buying behavior, the same value perception, and the same path to reach. When segmentation is done well, it tells the product manager who to build for, who to ignore, how to position, and how to prioritize. When it is done poorly, it produces persona posters that no roadmap decision ever references.

The harm this skill prevents is diffuse focus. A product built for "everyone" serves no one well, and a product aimed at segments defined by surface traits serves the wrong people. Agents often produce segments by age, company size, or job title because those are easy to count, even when the actual differences that matter are in behavior, motivation, or unmet need.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "who are our segments", "which segment should we target", "how should we group our customers", or "is this segment worth pursuing". The goal is to prevent the agent from inventing segments that look tidy but do not change any decision.

## Core Rules

### Segment On Variables That Change Product Decisions

A segment is only useful if grouping customers that way changes what you build, how you price, how you message, or how you sell. The test of a segmentation variable is whether two segments would receive a different product, different messaging, different pricing, or different channels. If the answer is no for every variable, the segmentation is decorative.

Behavioral and needs-based variables usually pass this test: the problem being solved, the frequency of use, the sophistication of the user, the buying trigger, the alternatives considered, the willingness to pay, the integration environment. Demographic variables often fail it, because two people with the same age or title may have entirely different needs. Prefer segmentation that reflects how customers differ in what they are trying to accomplish.

### Make Each Segment Homogeneous Within And Distinct Between

A good segment is internally similar and externally different. Customers within a segment should share the key need, behavior, and value perception, so that one solution fits them. Customers in different segments should differ enough that a single solution would underserve at least one group. If two segments would receive the identical product and messaging, they are probably one segment.

Test homogeneity by asking whether a feature decision would be the same for every member of the segment. Test distinctness by asking whether a feature that delights one segment would be irrelevant or annoying to another. Segments that fail both tests are too coarse or too fine.

### Evaluate Segment Attractiveness Before Targeting

Not every segment is worth pursuing. Evaluate each segment against criteria that determine whether serving it is a good use of limited capacity. Common criteria include size and growth, willingness to pay, competitive intensity, cost to serve, reachability through existing channels, strategic fit with the company's strengths, and alignment with the broader product vision.

Score segments against these criteria rather than picking the one that feels largest or loudest. A large segment that is price-sensitive, expensive to serve, and crowded with competitors may be less attractive than a smaller segment with strong willingness to pay and weak competition. The decision to target is a portfolio decision, not a size decision.

### Confirm Segments Are Identifiable And Reachable

A segment you cannot identify in the wild or reach with your channels is theoretical. Identifiability means you can tell, from observable signals, whether a given prospect belongs to the segment. Reachability means you can deliver a message, product, or sales motion to that segment through channels you have or can build. Segmentation that fails either test cannot drive go-to-market decisions.

This is where many segmentations break. A segment defined by an internal attitude or motivation may be real, but if you cannot detect it before the customer is already using the product, you cannot target acquisition toward it. Prefer segments defined by signals available at the point of first contact.

### Avoid Over-Segmenting Into Unactionable Slivers

More segments are not better. Each segment adds complexity in positioning, pricing, roadmap tradeoffs, and support. Beyond a small number, the team cannot serve any segment distinctly, and the segmentation collapses into a list no one uses. A practical ceiling is rarely more than four or five primary segments for a single product, with secondary segments noted but not actively designed for.

If a proposed segment is too small to justify distinct product or go-to-market investment, fold it into a broader segment rather than creating a sliver. Segmentation should reduce decision complexity, not multiply it.

### Revisit Segmentation When The Market Or Product Shifts

Segments are not permanent. As the product matures, new behaviors emerge, competitors reshape expectations, and the boundary between segments can blur or split. A segmentation that drove good decisions two years ago may no longer reflect how customers actually differ. Treat segmentation as a model that is tested against reality and revised, not as a fixed truth.

Signals that segmentation is stale include features that perform inconsistently across supposedly similar customers, support patterns that cluster in unexpected ways, and sales conversations that no longer map cleanly to the defined segments.

## Common Traps

### Demographic Default

Reaching for age, gender, company size, or job title because they are easy to measure, even when they do not predict need or behavior. The trap is tidy segments that explain nothing about what customers want.

### Segmenting By Product Usage Instead Of Need

Grouping customers by which features they use describes the current product, not the customer. The trap is that the segmentation changes every time the product changes, and it cannot guide what to build next.

### One Segment Disguised As Several

Splitting a homogeneous group into multiple segments to satisfy stakeholders or to look thorough. The trap is multiplied positioning and roadmap debate with no underlying difference in customer need.

### Targeting The Largest Segment By Default

Choosing the biggest segment without weighing willingness to pay, cost to serve, competition, and strategic fit. The trap is committing capacity to a segment where the economics or competitive position are weak.

### Treating Segments As Fixed Personas

Enshrining segments in persona documents and never revisiting them. The trap is designing for a model of the customer that no longer matches reality, while the actual customer base has shifted.

### Ignoring Segments You Cannot Reach

Defining attractive segments that existing channels and sales motion cannot access. The trap is a strategy that is correct on paper but unexecutable, because the customers cannot be found or sold to.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Segmentation variables are those that change product, pricing, messaging, or channel decisions, not merely easy-to-count demographics.
- [ ] Each segment is homogeneous within and distinct from other segments on the variables that matter.
- [ ] Segment attractiveness was evaluated on size, growth, willingness to pay, competition, cost to serve, reachability, and strategic fit, not only size.
- [ ] Segments are identifiable from observable signals and reachable through existing or buildable channels.
- [ ] The number of primary segments is small enough that each receives distinct treatment rather than diluting focus.
- [ ] Segmentation is treated as a revisable model, with triggers for when it should be re-examined.
- [ ] Targeting decisions reflect a portfolio view, not a default toward the largest or loudest segment.
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding conclusion?
