# Competitive Landscape And Mapping

Competitive analysis is not a feature checklist. Listing what rivals do tells you what exists, not what matters. A useful competitive map explains where the real contest is, who is winning which customer, why they are winning, and where the openings are. Done well, it sharpens positioning, exposes blind spots, and prevents the team from building a weaker copy of an existing product. Done poorly, it produces a comparison matrix that flatters the home team and ignores the competitors customers actually choose.

The harm this skill prevents is strategic myopia. Teams define competitors too narrowly, compare on dimensions that customers do not care about, or copy features without understanding the underlying advantage that makes a rival hard to beat. Agents often generate a feature comparison table and declare victory, when the real competitive question is about distribution, trust, switching cost, or business model.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "who are our competitors", "how do we compare", "how should we differentiate", or "what is our competitive position". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a shallow feature matrix in place of genuine competitive judgment.

## Core Rules

### Define Competitors From The Customer's Perspective

A competitor is anyone the customer could choose to solve the same problem, including substitutes you do not think of as rivals. Customers compare you against direct competitors, adjacent products, manual workarounds, spreadsheets, doing nothing, and internal builds. The set of alternatives is defined by the job the customer is hiring a product to do, not by your category label.

Cast the net wide enough to include indirect and substitute competitors, because the most dangerous threats often come from outside your category. A product that solves the same job with a different business model or distribution channel can erode your share without ever appearing on a feature comparison.

### Compare On Dimensions Customers Actually Use To Choose

Feature lists compare on what is easy to enumerate, not on what drives choice. Customers decide on dimensions like ease of getting started, trust and reliability, total cost including switching, integration with their existing stack, support quality, speed, and the confidence that the product will still exist and improve. Identify which dimensions are decisive for your target segment, and compare on those.

Weight the comparison by what matters to the customer you are targeting, not by what is easy to score. A product can win on twenty features and lose the deal because it lacks the one integration the customer cannot live without.

### Understand The Source Of Each Competitor's Advantage

Features are usually a symptom of advantage, not the advantage itself. The durable advantages are distribution, brand and trust, network effects, switching costs and data lock-in, economies of scale, proprietary data, regulatory position, and integration breadth. When a rival consistently wins, ask what makes them hard to displace, not what features they have.

This reframes differentiation. If a competitor wins because of distribution, adding their features to your product does not change the outcome. The competitive response must address the actual source of advantage, or find a segment and dimension where that advantage does not apply.

### Map Positioning Along Two Meaningful Axes

A positioning map plots competitors on two axes that represent how customers perceive tradeoffs. The value of the map is choosing axes that are decisive for the target segment and that reveal open space. Common meaningful axes include ease of use versus power, price versus premium value, breadth versus focus, self-serve versus high-touch, and speed versus reliability.

Avoid axes that flatter your product by construction. A map that places you in the desirable corner on every dimension is a sign the axes were chosen to make you look good, not to reveal where the contest actually is. The most useful maps show where no competitor currently serves a combination of needs well.

### Track Share, Momentum, And Dynamics, Not Just Features

A static snapshot misses movement. A competitor may have modest share today but strong momentum, or large share but declining relevance. Track direction as well as position: which competitors are gaining customers in your target segment, which are losing them, and what is driving the movement. Momentum often predicts future position more reliably than current share.

Look for structural shifts that reshape the landscape: a new business model, a platform change, regulation, consolidation, or a new entrant with different economics. These change who is competitive, sometimes faster than feature development does.

### Identify Where You Can Win And Where You Cannot

Competitive analysis should end with a clear point of view on where the product can establish a durable position and where it will struggle. This requires honesty about your own sources of advantage and disadvantage relative to each meaningful competitor. Naming where you will not win is as important as naming where you will, because it focuses investment and prevents wasted effort attacking fortified positions.

The output is not a ranking of who is best overall. It is a map of which segments and dimensions favor which competitor, and where the defensible opening for your product lies.

## Common Traps

### Feature Comparison Theater

Building a matrix that lists every feature and checks boxes, then declaring superiority based on count. The trap is that customers do not buy feature counts, and the matrix usually weights features the home team happens to have.

### Ignoring Indirect And Substitute Competitors

Defining competitors only as products in the same category. The trap is missing the spreadsheet, the manual process, or the adjacent product that actually captures the customer, because it never appears on the radar.

### Copying Features Without Understanding Advantage

Replicating a rival's feature to close a comparison gap. The trap is that the feature works for them because of an underlying advantage you lack, so the copy underperforms and you remain behind.

### Choosing Axes That Flatter The Home Team

Designing the positioning map so your product sits in the best quadrant. The trap is a comfortable picture that hides the real contest and leads to complacent strategy.

### Treating The Landscape As Static

Snapshotting competitors once and treating the map as fixed. The trap is missing momentum and structural shifts until a competitor has already taken the position you wanted.

### Declaring A Winner By Overall Score

Summing up a comparison to name the best product overall. The trap is that competitiveness is segment- and dimension-specific; a product can be best for one customer and worst for another.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The competitor set includes indirect and substitute alternatives defined by the customer's job, not only same-category products.
- [ ] Comparison dimensions are those customers actually use to choose, weighted by what is decisive for the target segment.
- [ ] The analysis identifies the durable source of each competitor's advantage, not only their features.
- [ ] A positioning map uses two meaningful axes that reveal open space rather than axes chosen to flatter the home product.
- [ ] Share, momentum, and structural dynamics are tracked, not only a static feature snapshot.
- [ ] The analysis concludes with a clear view of where the product can win durably and where it cannot.
- [ ] Competitive responses address the actual source of rival advantage rather than copying surface features.
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
