# Market Sizing And TAM

Market sizing is not arithmetic theater. A large number on a slide does not prove an opportunity is worth pursuing. Sizing is an estimate of how much value a market could represent, under stated assumptions, with a confidence level the product manager must name honestly. Done well, it forces the team to define who the buyer is, what unit of value is being counted, and how much of the market is actually reachable. Done poorly, it produces a defensible-looking figure that hides weak assumptions, double-counts revenue, or counts people who would never buy.

The harm this skill prevents is investment in products aimed at markets that look enormous on paper but are structurally unreachable, already saturated, or defined so broadly that the number is meaningless. Agents often produce TAM figures by multiplying a population by a price without questioning whether the population is the buyer, whether the price reflects what customers actually pay, or whether the addressable slice is realistic.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how big is this market", "is this opportunity worth investing in", "what is our TAM", or "how do we size a new segment". The goal is to prevent the agent from generating a confident market number that no one can interrogate.

## Core Rules

### Define The Unit Of Value Before Counting

A market size is only meaningful once you decide what is being counted. Revenue, units, users, licenses, and transactions are different numbers and cannot be mixed. State the unit explicitly and keep it consistent across every layer of the sizing. If leadership expects revenue and you present users, the comparison is broken.

Decide whether the figure is annual recurring revenue, one-time revenue, transaction volume, gross merchandise value, or something else. Each choice changes what the number means and how it should be compared to company financials. A market measured in transaction volume can dwarf one measured in revenue, and conflating the two inflates apparent opportunity.

### Choose Top-Down Versus Bottom-Up Deliberately

Top-down sizing starts from an analyst total and takes a slice. It is fast and impressive on a slide but rests on someone else's definitions and assumptions, which are often opaque. Bottom-up sizing counts the number of buyers, multiplied by what they would pay, constrained by reachability. It is slower and more exposed to challenge, but it forces the team to defend each assumption.

Prefer bottom-up for decisions that matter, and use top-down as a sanity check. When the two disagree by an order of magnitude, do not pick the larger one. Investigate the disagreement, because it usually reveals a wrong assumption about who buys, how often, or at what price.

### Separate TAM, SAM, And SOM By Reachability

Total addressable market is everyone who could theoretically use the product. Serviceable addressable market is the slice your business model, geography, channels, and positioning can actually reach. Serviceable obtainable market is what you could realistically capture in a defined period given competition and constraints. The three differ by reachability, not by enthusiasm.

The most common error is presenting TAM as if it were obtainable. A market of billions is irrelevant if your channels, pricing, or regulatory position limit you to a fraction of a percent. Always show the funnel down to SOM, and state the assumptions that shrink each layer: geography, language, segment, channel, sales capacity, competitive share, and time horizon.

### Make Assumptions Explicit And Interrogatable

Every market number is a stack of assumptions. List them. A buyer count assumption, a penetration assumption, a price assumption, a frequency assumption, and a churn assumption multiply into the final figure, and each carries uncertainty. When assumptions are hidden, the number feels solid and cannot be challenged. When they are visible, the team can argue about the right things.

For each assumption, note the source: analyst report, government statistic, first-party sales data, customer interview, or guess. Flag which assumptions are load-bearing, meaning a small change dramatically moves the result. A market size that swings by a factor of five when penetration moves from one to two percent is not a stable number, and the decision should not depend on it.

### Distinguish Demand From Willingness To Pay

Many people may want a product; far fewer will pay enough to sustain a business. Sizing by "people who have this problem" ignores whether they value a solution enough to pay, switch, or change behavior. Separate the count of potential users from the economics of serving them. A large market with low willingness to pay and high serving cost can be a worse opportunity than a smaller market with strong willingness to pay.

Where possible, anchor price assumptions to actual spending behavior, not to what customers say they would pay. Stated willingness to pay is unreliable. Observed spending on adjacent or substitute products is a stronger signal.

### Account For Market Growth, Saturation, And Timing

A static snapshot misleads. Markets grow, saturate, commoditize, and decline. State the time horizon of the estimate and whether it assumes growth. A market that is large today but contracting is a different decision from one that is small but expanding fast. Avoid extrapolating recent growth rates indefinitely; growth curves flatten, and early rates rarely persist.

Consider whether you are early, on time, or late. Entering a mature, saturated market requires differentiation or a cost advantage. Entering a nascent market requires patience and capital. The size alone does not tell you which situation you are in.

### Sanity-Check Against Comparable Products And Revenue

Compare the estimate to real, observable businesses. If your SOM implies capturing more share than an established competitor with stronger distribution, the assumption is probably wrong. If the TAM implies average revenue per user that no comparable product achieves, the price assumption is too high. Comparable companies are the most powerful sanity check available, because they are real where analyst reports are abstract.

## Common Traps

### Multiplying Population By Price And Calling It TAM

Counting everyone in a category and multiplying by a price assumes everyone buys, buys at that price, and buys from a reachable channel. This inflates TAM by orders of magnitude and is the most common sizing mistake.

### Treating Analyst Reports As Ground Truth

Analyst definitions of a market rarely match your product's actual buyer or unit of value. They use convenient categories that may include products you do not compete with and exclude ones you do. Read the methodology before quoting the number.

### Confusing Users With Paying Customers

A market of a billion users is not a billion-customer market if most are free users. Sizing by registered users, downloads, or visits counts attention, not economics. Separate the attention market from the revenue market.

### Picking The Larger Number To Make The Case

When two methods disagree, teams gravitate to the larger figure because it makes the investment case easier. This is decision-driven sizing, not analysis. The honest move is to reconcile the gap.

### Ignoring Cannibalization And Substitution

A new product may capture share by taking it from your own existing products or from substitutes outside the defined market. Net opportunity can be far smaller than gross opportunity once substitution is counted.

### Sizing Once And Never Revising

Markets change. A sizing done at investment time may be wrong a year later as competitors enter, prices shift, or demand moves. Treat sizing as a living estimate that is revisited at major decisions.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The unit of value (revenue, users, transactions, units) is stated explicitly and used consistently across TAM, SAM, and SOM.
- [ ] A bottom-up sizing exists and is not merely an analyst slice, and disagreements between methods are investigated rather than ignored.
- [ ] TAM, SAM, and SOM are separated by reachability assumptions, and SOM reflects realistic capture given competition, channels, and time.
- [ ] Every load-bearing assumption is listed with a source, and the most sensitive assumptions are flagged.
- [ ] Willingness to pay is anchored to observed spending behavior, not only stated intent.
- [ ] Growth, saturation, and timing assumptions are stated, and growth rates are not extrapolated indefinitely.
- [ ] The estimate is sanity-checked against comparable companies' actual revenue and share.
- [ ] Cannibalization and substitution effects are considered in the net opportunity.
- [ ] The sizing is treated as revisable, with a trigger for when it should be re-estimated.
