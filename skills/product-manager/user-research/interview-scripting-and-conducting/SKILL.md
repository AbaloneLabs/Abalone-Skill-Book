# Interview Scripting And Conducting

Discovery interviews fail when the interviewer treats the session as a chance to validate a product idea instead of a chance to understand a person's reality. A weak script, a friendly-but-leading question, or a participant chosen for convenience can all produce confident-sounding answers that have nothing to do with how users actually behave. The harm is not only wasted time; it is false confidence. Teams build features on top of quotes that were never evidence.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how should we run customer interviews", "what should a discussion guide include", "how do we avoid leading questions", "who should we recruit for discovery", or "how do we synthesize interview findings". The goal is to prevent the agent from scripting a pitch disguised as research, from over-weighting one memorable story, and from treating social-desirability answers as behavioral truth.

## Core Rules

### Script To Explore Problems, Not To Pitch Solutions

A discovery interview is not a sales call or a concept test. The discussion guide should be organized around the participant's world, not the product you are considering. Open with their context, their goals, and their current process before any feature discussion. If you must show a concept, do it late, after you have established their unfiltered reality.

Structure the guide in three movements: establish context and rapport, explore the target problem space deeply, then (only if relevant) test specific assumptions or reactions. Within each, write the actual questions you will ask, but treat them as a flexible path, not a rigid survey. The value of qualitative interviews comes from following up on surprises, not from reading every question in order.

A common failure is front-loading the product. "Would you use a feature that does X?" tells you almost nothing useful, because people are poor at predicting their own future behavior and polite to strangers. Instead, ask how they solve the problem today, when it hurts, and what they have tried.

### Recruit For Segment Fit, Not Convenience

The right participant matters more than a polished script. Decide the target segment before recruiting: primary user role, experience level, context of use, and any constraints that change the problem. Then screen for fit. A sample of friendly colleagues, existing power users, or whoever replied fastest will produce findings that do not generalize.

Screening questions should be neutral and should not reveal the answer you want. If you need people who reconcile financial data monthly, ask how often they perform reconciliation rather than "do you reconcile data monthly?". Incentives should be fair and proportional so participants value the time without feeling coerced into pleasing you.

Balance homogeneity and breadth. A tight segment lets you reach saturation faster and compare patterns; a deliberately varied sample can surface edge cases and segment differences. Decide which you need, and document the sampling logic so readers of the findings can judge who they apply to.

### Ask Open Questions And Watch The Ladder Of Inference

Leading questions quietly manufacture the answer you already wanted. "How frustrating is it when the tool is slow?" assumes frustration and slowness in one move. "Do you think this feature would be helpful?" invites a yes that costs the participant nothing. Rewrite these as open, neutral questions: "Walk me through the last time you did this," or "What was that experience like?"

Be aware of the ladder of inference: the participant observes something, assigns meaning, draws a conclusion, and states it; you then hear the conclusion and may treat it as fact. Probe back down the ladder. When a participant says "the dashboard is confusing," ask what specifically felt confusing, what they were trying to do at that moment, and what happened next. You want observed detail, not the participant's theory of their own experience.

Avoid question forms that bake in an assumption: double-barreled questions ("was it fast and accurate?"), judgmental framing ("why didn't you just..."), and absolutist words ("always", "never", "everyone"). Neutral language is a discipline, not a nicety.

### Probe For Specific Past Behavior Over Hypothetical Claims

People are reliable narrators of recent, specific events and unreliable predictors of their own future actions. Prioritize questions about the last time, the most recent occurrence, a concrete instance. "Tell me about the last time this happened" produces detail you can analyze. "Would you use this if it existed?" produces a guess that feels like data.

When a participant offers a hypothetical ("I would definitely pay for that"), treat it as an opinion, not a commitment. Probe for the closest past analog: have they paid for something similar, built a workaround, or switched tools for this reason before? Past behavior is a far stronger signal than stated intent.

This rule also protects against the invention of neat narratives. Asking "how do you usually do this?" invites a tidy idealized process. Asking "walk me through what you did yesterday" surfaces the messy, real, workaround-laden workflow that actually matters.

### Use Active Listening, Silence, And Neutral Probes

The interviewer's job is to create space for the participant to think and reveal, not to fill every pause. Silence is a tool. After a participant answers, a brief pause often surfaces a second, more honest layer. Resist the urge to jump in with the next question or to offer reassurance.

Use neutral probes that invite expansion without steering: "tell me more about that", "what happened next", "can you give me an example", "what did you mean by...". Reflecting a participant's own words back ("you mentioned it felt chaotic") encourages depth without imposing interpretation.

Manage your own reactions carefully. Nodding, "mm-hmm", and visible enthusiasm can reward certain answers and discourage others. Note your tone when a participant criticizes your product; defensiveness or visible disappointment will shut down the honest critique you most need.

### Recognize Saturation And Avoid Overfitting

Qualitative sample size is governed by saturation, not statistical power. Saturation is the point at which new interviews stop revealing substantially new themes and begin confirming existing ones. For a focused segment this is often reached within roughly five to twelve interviews, but it is a judgment based on diminishing new insight, not a fixed number.

Do not declare saturation prematurely after two or three sessions because the stories feel consistent. Conversely, do not keep interviewing indefinitely chasing every edge case; decide in advance what themes you are trying to understand and stop when those themes are saturated and the remaining interviews add detail, not understanding.

When synthesizing, resist overfitting to one vivid participant. A single memorable quote can dominate a debrief because it is easy to retell, not because it is representative. Triangulate: note how many participants independently raised each theme, flag which findings came from one person, and distinguish recurring patterns from compelling anecdotes.

### Handle Recording, Consent, And Notes With Care

Decide recording, consent, and note-taking logistics before the session, not during. Get explicit consent for recording and for how the data will be used. Be honest about purpose; covert research and hidden recording are both unethical and legally risky in many jurisdictions.

Recording enables accurate quotes and lets the interviewer focus on listening, but transcripts are not analysis. If you cannot record, assign roles: one person leads, another takes detailed notes capturing verbatim phrases, observed emotion, and the question that prompted each answer. Notes that capture only conclusions ("user likes the feature") are far less useful than notes that capture evidence ("user said 'I'd probably use it daily', but could not name a recent time they needed it").

Protect participant data. Minimize what you collect, store it securely, separate identifying details from findings where possible, and avoid putting real names in shared decks unless you have clear permission.

## Common Traps

### The Compliment Trap And Social Desirability Bias

Participants tend to agree, praise, and offer encouraging feedback because it is socially easier than criticizing a stranger's work, especially when an incentive or ongoing relationship is involved. This is the compliment trap. "I love that idea" is often politeness, not demand. The trap is treating politeness as validation and then building on it. Counter it by asking for past behavior and tradeoffs rather than approval.

### Pitching Instead Of Listening

When the interviewer is excited about a solution, questions drift toward selling it. Participants pick up on enthusiasm and mirror it. The trap is that the interview feels great, the participant seems engaged, and the resulting findings are worthless because the interviewer supplied the conclusion. Keep the concept out of the conversation until context is established.

### Over-Weighting The Most Articulate Participant

Articulate, confident participants shape the debrief disproportionately because their quotes are clean and their stories are memorable. But eloquence is not representativeness. The trap is letting the best storyteller define the problem frame. Weight findings by how many participants independently raised them, not by how well one person expressed them.

### Treating Stated Preference As Revealed Behavior

"What would you do?" answers are predictions, and humans systematically overestimate their own future virtue, spending, and effort. The trap is quoting "80% said they would use it" as if it were evidence of adoption. Always pull stated claims back toward observed past behavior before treating them as signal.

### Asking Hypotheticals About Non-Existent Futures

Asking people to imagine a product that does not exist and predict their reaction produces confident, detailed, and mostly fictional answers. The trap is that the detail feels authoritative. Use hypotheticals sparingly and always anchor them to a real past situation the participant actually lived through.

### Confusing Interview Count With Research Quality

More interviews do not automatically mean better research. The trap is running many shallow, leading sessions and then claiming rigor because the number is large. Quality comes from good recruitment, neutral questions, behavioral probing, and honest synthesis, not from volume. Conversely, two excellent interviews beat ten biased ones.

### Synthesizing Toward The Conclusion You Already Held

Confirmation bias shows up most in synthesis. The trap is cherry-picking the quotes that support the team's preferred direction and quietly dropping the ones that complicate it. Force yourself to write down the strongest counter-evidence and the disconfirming quotes before drawing conclusions.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The discussion guide explores the participant's problem space before any concept or solution discussion.
- [ ] Recruitment targets a defined segment with documented screening criteria, not whoever was easiest to reach.
- [ ] Questions are open and neutral, with no leading assumptions, double-barreled phrasing, or baked-in conclusions.
- [ ] The script prioritizes specific recent past behavior over hypothetical future claims.
- [ ] Neutral probes and intentional silence are planned, and reactive body language is consciously managed.
- [ ] Sample size is justified by saturation logic, not an arbitrary number, and the saturation decision is documented.
- [ ] Synthesis distinguishes recurring patterns across participants from single compelling anecdotes.
- [ ] Recording, consent, and data-handling decisions are made in advance and respect participant privacy.
- [ ] Stated preferences are clearly separated from observed past behavior in the findings.
- [ ] The strongest disconfirming evidence was actively sought and recorded, not omitted.
