# Unreinforced Masonry And Veneer Design

Unreinforced masonry and veneer design is the work of designing or assessing masonry that carries load without embedded steel reinforcement, where the masonry itself must resist all tension, compression, and shear, and where the low tensile strength and brittle behavior create narrow margins and severe limitations that reinforced masonry does not face. The difficulty is not computing a compressive stress; it is the judgment that unreinforced masonry cannot reliably resist tension, that empirical design height and load limits exist because the method has no analysis behind it, that veneer is a non-structural cladding whose anchors and flashing govern its performance, and that existing unreinforced masonry buildings are seismic hazards whose retrofit requires a different framework than new design. A wall that satisfies compressive stress can still crack and overturn under wind or seismic tension, a veneer that looks stable can peel off in a storm if its anchors are undersized or corroded, and an empirical design that exceeds the height or load limits is invalid regardless of the stress calculation. TMS 402 and the IBC govern, and the engineer cannot treat unreinforced masonry as reinforced masonry without steel. This skill covers the judgment exercised while designing and assessing unreinforced masonry and veneer, with the understanding that final design, sealing of drawings, and code interpretations require a licensed professional engineer, and that seismic retrofit of existing URM buildings may require a structural specialist.

## Core Rules

### Respect The Limits Of Empirical Design And Allowable Stress For URM

Begin every unreinforced masonry design by recognizing that the empirical design method and the allowable stress design method both have strict limits that must be respected. Empirical design, which sizes walls based on rules of thumb for height, thickness, and load without structural analysis, is permitted only within the height, load, and occupancy limits specified in TMS 402, and a wall that exceeds those limits must be designed by allowable stress or strength methods, not by empirical rules extended beyond their validity. Allowable stress design for unreinforced masonry checks the masonry against allowable compressive, shear, and flexural stresses, with the masonry permitted to take limited tension only in specific cases such as out-of-plane flexure of running bond, and the engineer must verify whether tension is permitted for the condition and the bond pattern. Do not use empirical rules for buildings outside the empirical scope, and do not credit tension to masonry where the code does not permit it, because unreinforced masonry in tension is unreliable and cracking is the real failure mode. Recognize that unreinforced masonry is governed by its tensile and shear limits far more often than by its compressive capacity, and that a wall that passes compression can still fail in flexural tension or shear.

### Design Anchored Masonry Veneer For The Cladding Loads And Anchorage

Masonry veneer is a non-structural cladding that must be anchored to a backing of wood studs, steel studs, concrete, or masonry, and the design must address the anchors, the backing, the air space, and the flashing as a system. Design the veneer anchors for the wind and seismic lateral loads on the veneer weight, using the anchor capacity from TMS 402 based on the anchor type, the backing material, and the embedment, because the anchors are the only thing holding the veneer to the building and an undersized or corroded anchor allows the veneer to detach. Space anchors at the maximum spacing required by the code, with closer spacing at edges, corners, and openings where loads concentrate, because uniformly spaced anchors at the maximum spacing leave the edges and openings vulnerable. Detail the air space or cavity behind the veneer to allow drainage, and provide through-wall flashing at the base, at openings, and at interruptions to direct water out, because veneer is not waterproof and water that enters the cavity must exit or it will damage the backing and the anchors. Verify that the backing, whether wood or steel framing, is designed for the veneer weight and the anchor forces, because the backing is structural and a backing that cannot carry the veneer load will deflect and crack the veneer.

### Assess Existing URM For Seismic Vulnerability And Retrofit Needs

Existing unreinforced masonry buildings are among the most seismically vulnerable structures, and their assessment requires a framework that recognizes their brittle behavior, their lack of tension capacity, and their parapet and wall anchorage deficiencies. Evaluate the building against the seismic evaluation provisions, such as ASCE 41 or the IEBC, which use performance-based criteria rather than new-building strength, because applying new-building code strength to an existing URM building will condemn virtually all of them and is not the correct basis. Check the parapets and the wall-to-floor and wall-to-roof anchorage, because these are the most common failure points and the most dangerous, as parapets fall and walls separate from diaphragms in earthquakes. Assess the mortar condition and the masonry compressive strength through in-situ testing where the design depends on it, because the actual strength of existing masonry is often far below assumed values, and the wall capacity depends on the actual, not the assumed, strength. Design retrofit measures such as wall anchors, buttresses, or reinforced cores based on the evaluation, recognizing that the goal is usually life safety, not full new-building performance, and that the retrofit must be compatible with the existing construction.

### Provide Flashing, Drainage, And Movement Joints For Durability

Unreinforced masonry and veneer fail as often through water and movement as through structural overload, and the design must address flashing, drainage, and movement joints. Provide through-wall flashing at the base of walls, at window and door sills, at shelf angles, and wherever the cavity is interrupted, because water that enters the masonry must be directed out, and un-flashed water saturates the wall, corrodes the ties, and damages the interior. Provide expansion joints in clay brick veneer and control joints in concrete masonry to accommodate the differential movement of the materials, because brick expands and concrete masonry shrinks, and without joints the walls crack unpredictably. Detail the movement joints at locations of stress concentration such as changes in wall height, at openings, and at spacing appropriate to the material, because a movement joint in the wrong location does nothing and a wall without adequate joints cracks. Treat flashing, drainage, and movement joints as part of the structural and durability design, not as architectural afterthoughts, because their failure leads to water damage, corrosion, and masonry distress that can become a structural issue.

## Common Traps

### Extending Empirical Design Beyond Its Permitted Limits

The engineer uses the empirical design rules for a wall that exceeds the height, load, or occupancy limits of the empirical method. The mechanism is that empirical design is calibrated only within its limits, and beyond those limits the rules have no basis and the wall may be inadequate. The false signal is that the empirical thickness satisfies the rule of thumb. The harm is a wall whose capacity is unknown and likely below the demand, because the empirical method was applied outside its validity. Walls outside the empirical limits must be designed by allowable stress or strength methods, and empirical rules must not be extended.

### Crediting Tension To Unreinforced Masonry Where Not Permitted

The engineer checks the wall and allows the masonry to resist flexural tension based on an assumed tensile strength, where the code does not permit tension for the condition. The mechanism is that unreinforced masonry tension is unreliable and bond-dependent, and the code permits it only in specific cases such as running bond out-of-plane flexure. The false signal is that the wall passes with the tension included. The harm is cracking and potential instability under wind or seismic load, because the tension capacity credited did not exist. Tension must be credited to unreinforced masonry only where the code explicitly permits it, and otherwise the wall must be redesigned or reinforced.

### Undersizing Or Corroding Veneer Anchors

The engineer specifies veneer anchors at the maximum spacing without checking the load, or details anchors that will corrode in the cavity. The mechanism is that the anchors hold the veneer to the building, and an undersized or corroded anchor allows the veneer to detach under wind or seismic load. The false signal is that the anchors are at the code maximum spacing. The harm is veneer detachment and fall, a life-safety hazard, because the anchors were inadequate or corroded. Anchors must be designed for the veneer load with closer spacing at edges and openings, and must be corrosion-resistant in the cavity environment.

### Omitting Through-Wall Flashing And Movement Joints

The engineer details the masonry without through-wall flashing at the base and openings, or without expansion or control joints. The mechanism is that water enters the masonry and without flashing saturates the wall, and differential movement without joints cracks the masonry. The false signal is that the wall is structurally adequate. The harm is water damage, corrosion of ties, and cracking that becomes a structural and durability problem. Flashing and movement joints must be detailed as part of the design, because their omission causes failures that structural strength does not prevent.

## Self-Check

- If empirical design is used, does the wall fall within the height, load, and occupancy limits of the empirical method, and if not, has allowable stress or strength design been used?
- Is flexural tension credited to unreinforced masonry only where the code explicitly permits it, and is the wall otherwise designed or reinforced to avoid relying on tension?
- Are veneer anchors designed for the wind and seismic veneer loads, with spacing at or below the code maximum and closer spacing at edges, corners, and openings?
- Is the backing, wood or steel framing or concrete, designed for the veneer weight and anchor forces, and is the air space and drainage detailed?
- For existing URM, is the assessment based on ASCE 41 or the IEBC performance criteria, with parapets and wall-to-diaphragm anchorage evaluated and retrofit designed to life-safety goals?
- Are through-wall flashing at the base, sills, and shelf angles, and expansion or control joints at stress concentrations, detailed as part of the design?
- Is the design sealed and reviewed by a licensed professional engineer, with specialist involvement for URM seismic retrofit?
