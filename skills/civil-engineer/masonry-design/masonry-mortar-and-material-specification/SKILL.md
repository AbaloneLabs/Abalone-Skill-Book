# Masonry Mortar And Material Specification

Masonry mortar and material specification is the work of selecting and specifying the mortar, grout, units, and their compressive strength so that the as-built masonry achieves the strength and durability assumed in the structural design, and it is where the gap between designed and built capacity is most often created. The difficulty is not choosing a mortar type from a table; it is the judgment that mortar strength and masonry compressive strength are different things, that high-strength mortar does not produce high-strength masonry, that grout must be fluid enough to fill the cells without honeycombing, and that the unit type, the mortar type, and the construction conditions interact to determine the real wall strength. A wall designed for a specified compressive strength of masonry that the materials and construction cannot achieve has a capacity below the design, a mortar specified too strong for the units creates a brittle wall that cracks rather than flexes, and grout that is too stiff or placed in too high a lift leaves voids that leave bars unbonded. ASTM C270 for mortar, ASTM C476 for grout, ASTM unit standards, and TMS 602 govern the specification and construction, and the engineer cannot treat material specification as a paperwork exercise disconnected from the structural capacity. This skill covers the judgment exercised while specifying masonry materials, with the understanding that final specification, sealing of drawings, and code interpretations require a licensed professional engineer.

## Core Rules

### Select Mortar Type By Application, Not By Compressive Strength Alone

Begin every mortar specification by selecting the mortar type, M, S, N, or O, based on the application, the exposure, and the required strength and durability, not by the assumption that a higher type is always better. Type M has the highest compressive strength and is used for below-grade, high-load, and severe exposure applications, but its high cement content makes it less workable and more prone to shrinkage cracking. Type S has high flexural bond strength and is used for reinforced masonry and where moderate flexural resistance is needed, making it the common choice for structural walls. Type N is a medium-strength mortar used for general above-grade exterior walls and veneer, balancing strength and workability. Type O is a low-strength mortar used for interior non-load-bearing walls and historic restoration where high strength would damage existing units. Do not specify the highest mortar type by default, because mortar stronger than the units creates a brittle wall that cracks through the units rather than at the joints, and the wall is less forgiving of differential movement. Match the mortar type to the application, the unit, and the exposure, and recognize that the wall strength depends on the combination, not the mortar alone.

### Specify Grout For Fluidity, Strength, And Cell Fill

Grout in reinforced masonry bonds the steel to the masonry and fills the cells to create the composite section, and its specification must address fluidity, strength, and the pour height. Specify grout compressive strength equal to or greater than the specified masonry compressive strength, because grout weaker than the masonry reduces the wall capacity, and the grout is part of the compressive section. Specify a high grout slump, typically in the eight-to-eleven-inch range, because grout must be fluid enough to flow into the cells and around the bars without vibration, and a stiff grout leaves voids that leave bars unbonded. Specify the maximum pour height and the lift sequence based on the cell size and the grout type, fine or coarse, because a pour that is too high for the cell bridging and absorption leaves voids, and the code limits the pour height to ensure fill. Require grout sample testing per ASTM C1019 to verify the strength, because the grout strength is the basis of the wall capacity and must be confirmed, not assumed. Do not treat grout as concrete with aggregate, because grout is formulated for fluidity and bond in narrow cells and behaves differently from structural concrete.

### Define The Specified Compressive Strength Of Masonry And Verify By Prism

The specified compressive strength of masonry, f-prime-m, is the basis of all masonry structural capacity, and it is a property of the masonry assemblage, not of the mortar or the unit alone. Define f-prime-m based on the unit strength and the mortar type using the TMS 602 unit strength method, or verify it by prism testing per ASTM C1314, because the assemblage strength depends on the interaction of the unit, the mortar, and the construction, and the unit or mortar strength alone does not determine it. Where the design assumes a high f-prime-m, verify it by prism testing rather than relying on the unit strength tables, because the tables have upper limits and the actual assemblage strength may be below the assumed value for high-strength units or unusual mortars. Require prism or unit strength verification during construction, because the design capacity exists only if the as-built masonry achieves the specified f-prime-m, and unverified masonry has an unknown capacity. Recognize that f-prime-m develops over time and that the 28-day strength is the standard reference, and do not assume the design strength is present at early ages or under accelerated construction schedules.

### Address Durability, Efflorescence, And Weather Construction

Masonry durability is determined by the materials and the construction conditions, and the specification must address efflorescence, freezing, and hot weather to prevent distress. Specify units and mortars with low soluble salts and adequate drainage to minimize efflorescence, the white deposit formed when soluble salts in the mortar or units are carried to the surface by water and crystallize, because efflorescence indicates water movement and can be a sign of saturation and freeze-thaw distress. Require cold weather construction practices per TMS 602, including protecting units from freezing, heating materials, and protecting the completed work from freezing, because masonry constructed with frozen materials or frozen before the mortar sets never develops its strength and is permanently damaged. Require hot weather construction practices, including shading units, using cool mixing water, and fog spraying the completed work, because hot dry conditions pull water from the mortar before it hydrates, weakening the bond and the wall. Treat weather construction provisions as part of the specification, not as optional field decisions, because masonry built under adverse weather without the provisions has a capacity and durability below the design.

## Common Traps

### Specifying The Highest Mortar Type By Default

The engineer specifies Type M mortar for all masonry on the assumption that higher strength is better. The mechanism is that mortar stronger than the units creates a brittle wall that cracks through the units rather than at the joints, and the wall is less able to accommodate movement. The false signal is that the high-strength mortar gives a strong wall. The harm is cracking and distress under differential movement that a lower-type mortar would have tolerated. The mortar type must be matched to the application, the unit, and the exposure, and the highest type is not always the best choice.

### Specifying Stiff Grout That Leaves Voids

The engineer specifies grout at a concrete-like slump, on the theory that stiffer grout is stronger and easier to place. The mechanism is that grout must be fluid to flow into narrow cells and around bars, and stiff grout bridges and leaves voids that leave bars unbonded and the section unfilled. The false signal is that the grout meets a strength requirement. The harm is a reinforced wall with voids and unbonded bars whose capacity is below the design, because the composite action was never achieved. Grout must be specified at the high slump required for cell fill, and the pour height must be limited to ensure fill.

### Assuming F-prime-M Without Prism Or Unit Strength Verification

The engineer designs for a specified f-prime-m and does not require prism or unit strength verification during construction, assuming the masonry will achieve it. The mechanism is that f-prime-m is a property of the assemblage that depends on the unit, mortar, and construction, and without verification the actual strength is unknown. The false signal is that the design shows the required f-prime-m. The harm is a wall with capacity below the design, because the as-built strength was never confirmed. F-prime-m must be verified by the unit strength method or prism testing during construction, because the design capacity exists only if the as-built masonry achieves it.

### Omitting Cold Or Hot Weather Construction Provisions

The engineer does not specify cold or hot weather construction provisions, leaving them to the contractor. The mechanism is that masonry built with frozen materials or in hot dry conditions without protection is permanently damaged, with low bond and low strength. The false signal is that the wall is built per the drawings. The harm is masonry with capacity and durability far below the design, because the weather provisions were not applied. Cold and hot weather construction provisions per TMS 602 must be specified, because masonry built under adverse weather without them does not achieve the design.

## Self-Check

- Is the mortar type, M, S, N, or O, selected for the application, unit, and exposure, rather than defaulting to the highest strength?
- Is grout specified with compressive strength at or above f-prime-m, at the high slump required for cell fill, and with pour height limited to ensure fill?
- Is the specified compressive strength of masonry, f-prime-m, defined by the unit strength method or prism testing, and is verification required during construction?
- Are prism samples per ASTM C1314 or unit strength verification required to confirm the as-built f-prime-m matches the design value?
- Are efflorescence control through unit and mortar selection and drainage, and freeze-thaw durability, addressed in the specification?
- Are cold and hot weather construction provisions per TMS 602 specified, including material protection, heating, and work protection?
- Is the specification sealed and reviewed by a licensed professional engineer, with special inspection required for structural masonry?
