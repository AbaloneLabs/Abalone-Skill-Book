# Schedule Delay and Time Extension Analysis

Schedule delay and time extension analysis is the forensic and contractual evaluation of whether a project is late, why it is late, who is responsible, and what time and money is owed as a result. It is governed by the contract's general conditions (AIA A201, EJCDC C-700, FIDIC Red or Yellow Book), by the scheduling specification, and by the body of delay-analysis practice recognized by AACE International, the Society of Construction Law (SCL) Delay and Disruption Protocol, and the Associated General Contractors. The harm this skill prevents is a one-sided or methodologically unsound delay conclusion: an owner that denies an excusable extension and imposes liquidated damages on a contractor actually delayed by the owner's own design changes; or a contractor that recovers both time and money for a delay it caused, by abusing float, mischaracterizing concurrent delay, or running an analysis method chosen to produce a favorable result. Because delay findings drive extensions of time, liquidated damages, extended overhead, and sometimes termination for default, the analysis must be transparent, contemporaneously grounded, and methodologically defensible, and it must distinguish clearly between entitlement to time and entitlement to money. Agents must treat delay analysis as decision support for a scheduler, claims consultant, or counsel of record, and must never assert final entitlement without the contract clauses, the notices, and the contemporaneous schedule data.

## Core Rules

### Establish Entitlement from the Contract Clauses and Notice Requirements First

Before any schedule analysis, establish what the contract actually allows, because entitlement is a contractual question, not a scheduling one. Identify the relevant clauses: the time extension clause (what delays are excusable), the no-damage-for-delay or damage-for-delay clause (whether time-only or time-and-money relief is available), the differing site conditions clause, the force majeure or Acts of God clause, and the notice provisions that condition entitlement. Verify whether the notice was given in the form, within the time limit, and with the content the clause requires, because many otherwise-valid delay claims are barred solely by late or deficient notice, and an owner's waiver of notice must be documented rather than assumed. Classify each delay event as excusable or non-excusable (was the cause beyond the contractor's control and not its fault) and as compensable or non-compensable (does the contract allow money as well as time), because these classifications are independent and drive entirely different remedies.

### Apply the Correct Delay Analysis Method to the Facts and the Data Available

Delay analysis methods are not interchangeable; each answers a different question and requires different data, and choosing a method to favor a party rather than to fit the facts is a fundamental error. The principal methods are the time impact analysis (TIA, also called the prospective or insert method, which inserts a delay event or fragnet into an update schedule to measure the projected impact), the as-planned versus as-built (APAB, which compares the baseline to the actual to measure total delay), the windows or contemporaneous-period analysis (which slices the project into periods and measures delay period by period using the schedule updates that existed at the time), the collapsed as-built or but-for (which removes the owner-caused delays from the as-built to see what the finish date would have been), and the modified total cost approach. Prefer contemporaneous methods (windows analysis using actual updates) when the data exists, because they reflect what the parties knew and decided at the time and are the most defensible; reserve global methods (collapsed as-built, total cost) for cases where contemporaneous schedules are absent, and acknowledge their lower evidentiary weight. Document the method, the schedules used, the logic and assumptions, and the software runs, so that the analysis can be replicated and cross-examined.

### Resolve Concurrent Delay and Float Ownership Explicitly

Concurrent delay, where two independent delays affect the project completion in the same period, is the most contested issue in delay analysis, and it must be resolved by an explicit rule rather than by avoidance. Apply the governing rule, which varies by contract and jurisdiction: under the but-for rule, concurrent owner-caused and contractor-caused delays on the critical path may bar compensation but not necessarily time; under the dominant cause rule, the court or board identifies the predominant delay; and under the SCL and much modern US practice, true concurrent critical-path delay generally entitles the contractor to time (extension) but not to money (extended overhead). Resolve float ownership with equal clarity, because if the contract is silent, the prevailing view is that float is a project resource available to whoever uses it first, so an owner-caused delay that consumes project float is not compensable until float is exhausted, and a contractor that built in hidden float cannot claim it as its own. Never assume a single rule; state the rule applied, the authority for it, and how it changes the result, because the concurrent-delay and float rules often determine the entire outcome.

### Distinguish Critical from Non-Critical Delay and Use the Contemporaneous Schedule

A delay only affects project completion if it is on the critical path or consumes the float of a near-critical path that becomes critical; a delay to a float-bearing activity that does not push the project finish is non-compensable as time impact and must be reported as such. Use the contemporaneous schedule updates, not a reconstructed baseline, to identify the critical path at the time of each delay event, because the critical path shifts over the life of the project and an event that was critical in one period may be irrelevant in another. Track the migration of the critical path across updates, identify the near-critical paths (within a few days of float) that could become critical, and measure each delay against the schedule that actually governed at the time. A delay analysis that applies all events to the original baseline ignores the reality that the project changed, and it produces impact numbers that have no relationship to what actually happened on the job.

### Tie the Time Extension to the Money and Preserve the Record

A time extension is not only relief from liquidated damages; it carries financial consequences through extended field overhead, home office overhead (under the Eichleay or equivalent formula), escalation, and extended insurance and bonds, and the delay analysis must connect the excusable, compensable delay to these costs with the same rigor as the schedule impact. Quantify extended overhead only for the period and to the extent of compensable delay, not for the whole project overrun, and apply the correct formula with documented input data. Preserve the contemporaneous record throughout: daily reports, schedule updates, correspondence, meeting minutes, weather records, change orders, and notices, because delay entitlement is won or lost on contemporaneous documentation, and a retrospective reconstruction without it is weak. Ensure every delay event is traced from cause (the contract clause and the triggering event) through schedule impact (the analysis method and the days) to consequence (the extension and the money), so that the claim is internally consistent and auditable.

## Common Traps

### Choosing the Analysis Method to Favor a Party

The contractor selects collapsed as-built or total cost because it maximizes recovery, or the owner selects a narrow windows slice because it minimizes impact, regardless of which method fits the data. The mechanism is that each method answers a different question, so the false signal of a rigorous-looking analysis hides a method chosen for its result; the harm is a conclusion that fails in negotiation, mediation, or litigation and undermines the analyst's credibility.

### Treating Concurrent Delay as a Single Event

Owner-caused and contractor-caused delays in the same period are lumped together, so the contractor either recovers full time and money or gets nothing, depending on who is telling the story. The mechanism is that concurrency is a rule question, not a fact question, so the false signal of overlapping delays hides the governing legal rule; the harm is an over- or under-stated recovery that the contract or the jurisdiction does not support.

### Measuring Delay Against the Original Baseline Only

All delay events are inserted into the as-planned baseline, ignoring the schedule updates that show how the project actually progressed and how the critical path migrated. The mechanism is that the baseline is a static prediction, so the false signal of a complete analysis hides the real contemporaneous critical path; the harm is impact days that bear no relationship to the actual project finish and that collapse under cross-examination.

### Claiming Extended Overhead for the Entire Overrun

Extended field and home office overhead is claimed for the full project duration increase, including periods of non-excusable or concurrent contractor-caused delay. The mechanism is that overhead is time-related, so the false signal of a documented overrun hides the compensable-only portion; the harm is an inflated money claim that is partially barred and that discredits the entire submission.

## Self-Check

- Has entitlement been established from the specific contract clauses (time extension, no-damage-for-delay, differing site conditions, force majeure) and have the notice requirements been verified for form, timing, and content?
- Is each delay event classified as excusable or non-excusable and as compensable or non-compensable, with the contractual basis for each classification stated?
- Is the delay analysis method (TIA, as-planned versus as-built, windows, collapsed as-built, modified total cost) chosen to fit the available data and the question asked, with the reason documented rather than chosen for a favorable result?
- Where delay events overlap, is the concurrent delay rule (but-for, dominant cause, or time-but-not-money) explicitly identified and applied, and is float ownership resolved under the contract or prevailing rule?
- Is each delay measured as critical or non-critical against the contemporaneous schedule update that governed at the time, with critical path migration tracked across updates?
- Are extended field and home office overhead, escalation, and time-related costs quantified only for the excusable and compensable period, with the correct formula and documented inputs?
- Is the contemporaneous record (daily reports, updates, correspondence, notices, weather) preserved and cited to support each cause-and-impact link in the claim?
