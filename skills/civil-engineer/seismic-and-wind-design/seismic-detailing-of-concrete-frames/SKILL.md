# Seismic Detailing Of Concrete Frames

Seismic detailing of concrete frames is the discipline of arranging the reinforcement so that the frame yields in a ductile, energy-dissipating mode, flexural hinging in the beams, rather than a brittle mode, shear failure or joint failure, and the detailing, not the analysis, is what earns the response modification coefficient that allows a reduced design force. Agents who size the frame members for the seismic force and stop miss that the force is reduced precisely because the detailing guarantees ductile behavior, and that without the special detailing the frame cannot develop the ductility the coefficient assumes, and the building designed with a ductile coefficient but without ductile detailing will fail in the brittle mode the coefficient assumed away. This skill covers the judgment exercised while detailing concrete seismic frames, with the goal that the beam hinges, the column remains elastic, the joint transmits the force, and the confinement preserves the core through the reversed cyclic loading.

## Core Rules

### Achieve Strong-Column Weak-Beam Through Capacity Design

The seismic frame's intended mechanism is beam hinging, where the beams yield at the column faces and dissipate energy, while the columns remain elastic to carry the gravity and prevent a soft story, and the mechanism is enforced through the strong-column weak-beam provision. Check that the column's flexural strength exceeds the beam's expected flexural strength at every joint, using the code's ratio and the beam's overstrength, because a column weaker than the beam hinges instead, and a column hinge creates a soft story mechanism that concentrates the drift and the damage at one floor and can collapse the building. Apply the provision at every joint, accounting for the columns above and below and the beams on both sides, because the mechanism can form at any joint and the provision must hold everywhere. Strong-column weak-beam is the capacity design principle applied to the frame, and it is what forces the ductile beam mechanism over the brittle column mechanism.

### Design The Beam For Ductile Flexural Hinging

The beam must hinge in a ductile mode, yielding its tension steel and stretching before the concrete crushes, and the ductility is achieved through the reinforcement ratios and the detailing. Limit the tension reinforcement ratio to below the code maximum, and provide at least the minimum, so that the section is under-reinforced and yields before crushing, because an over-reinforced beam crushes without yielding and provides no ductility. Provide compression steel at the support, at least half the tension steel or the code minimum, because the compression steel increases the section's ductility and its cyclic performance, and it prevents the section from becoming over-reinforced under moment reversal. Detail the beam's longitudinal bars with the proper development into the joint, because a beam whose bars are not developed cannot develop its moment and cannot hinge. The beam's ductility is in its detailing, and the detailing must support the hinging the mechanism assumes.

### Design Shear For The Capacity, Not The Analysis Demand

In a seismic frame, the beam's shear must be designed for the shear produced by the beam's hinging, not for the shear from the analysis, because once the beam hinges at both ends its shear is determined by its flexural capacity, and designing for the analysis demand under-designs the shear if the capacity exceeds the demand. Compute the capacity-based shear from the beam's expected moment capacities at both ends and the gravity shear, using the code's method, because this is the maximum shear the beam can experience, and designing below it allows a shear failure that pre-empts the ductile hinging. Provide the shear reinforcement, the stirrups, at the spacing and extent required, with the closer spacing in the plastic hinge regions near the joints, because the hinge regions experience the most damage and need the tightest confinement. Capacity-based shear design is what ensures the beam hinges before it shears, and it is a separate and governing computation from the analysis shear.

### Detail The Beam-Column Joint For Force Transfer And Confinement

The beam-column joint is the most highly stressed region of a seismic frame, transmitting the beam's moment into the column through bond and shear, and it must be detailed to transfer the force and to confine the concrete through the reversed cyclic loading. Design the joint for the horizontal shear from the beam moments, providing the joint's shear capacity through the concrete and the transverse reinforcement, because a joint that fails in shear loses its moment transfer and the frame's mechanism cannot develop. Confine the joint with transverse reinforcement, the hoops and ties, at the code-required spacing, because the confinement preserves the joint's concrete and the bond of the beam bars through the cyclic loading, and an unconfined joint degrades and fails. Limit the beam bar sizes through the joint, because large bars cannot develop their bond within the joint's limited width and pull out, and the code limits the bar diameter relative to the column depth. The joint is the connection, and its detailing is what makes the frame a frame.

### Provide Confinement In The Plastic Hinge Regions

The plastic hinge regions, the ends of the beams and the columns where the hinging occurs, must be confined with closely spaced transverse reinforcement, because the confinement preserves the concrete core and prevents the bars from buckling after the cover spalls. Provide the hoops or spirals at the code-required spacing within the hinge length, with the spacing a fraction of the member depth, because the close spacing is what confines the core and restrains the bars. Detail the hoops with the 135-degree hooks and the required extensions, because a hoop that is not anchored opens under the cyclic load and provides no confinement, and the seismic hook detail is mandatory. Extend the confinement beyond the hinge length, transitioning to the less stringent spacing, because the hinge region's extent must be fully confined. Confinement is what allows the hinge to sustain the cyclic rotation without losing capacity, and it is the most critical detailing in a seismic frame.

### Avoid And Address Seismic Irregularities

Seismic irregularities, in plan or in elevation, are geometric or stiffness discontinuities that the code penalizes because they concentrate the demand and complicate the response, and the detailing must address the penalties or the layout must avoid the irregularities. Identify the irregularities, the torsional, the re-entrant corner, the discontinuous wall, the soft story, the weight or the stiffness discontinuity, by comparing the frame's geometry and stiffness to the code's definitions, because an irregularity triggers design penalties, additional analysis, or in some cases prohibition. Avoid the irregularities in the layout where possible, by regularizing the plan and the elevation, because a regular frame performs better and is simpler to detail. Where an irregularity is unavoidable, apply the penalties, the amplified torsion, the dynamic analysis, the special detailing, and document the basis, because the penalty is the cost of the irregularity and it must be paid in the design. Irregularities are not forbidden, but they are penalized, and the penalties must be applied or the irregularity removed.

### Specify Inspection And Construction Practices For Seismic Frames

Seismic frames are built, and the construction must preserve the detailing, because the dense confinement, the seismic hooks, and the joint reinforcement are difficult to place and easy to get wrong, and a frame built with the wrong detailing does not perform. Require special inspection of the reinforcement placement, especially in the joints and the hinge regions, because the congestion is high and the inspector must verify the hoops, the hooks, and the bar placement. Require the concrete to be properly consolidated in the congested joints, with the aggregate size and the vibration matched to the congestion, because honeycombed concrete in a joint destroys its capacity. Require the development lengths and the lap splices to be executed as detailed, with the staggered splices and the seismic hooks, because a deviation undermines the ductility. The construction specification is what delivers the seismic frame, and it must require the practices that preserve the detailing.

## Common Traps

### The Strong-Beam Weak-Column That Creates A Soft Story

The engineer sizes the columns for the axial load and the beams for the moment, without checking the strong-column weak-beam ratio, and the columns are weaker than the beams at the joints. The mechanism is that the seismic frame's intended mechanism is beam hinging, and if the column is weaker, the column hinges instead, and a column hinge at every joint of a floor creates a soft story mechanism where the entire drift concentrates at that floor, and the columns cannot sustain the rotation and collapse. The false signal is that the members "meet their design forces." The harm is that the building collapses in a soft story in the earthquake, because the column hinging that the strong-column weak-beam provision was meant to prevent was allowed by the unchecked ratio. The strong-column weak-beam ratio must be checked at every joint, with the column strength exceeding the beam's expected strength, because the mechanism's formation depends on it.

### Shear Designed For The Analysis Demand, Not The Capacity

The engineer designs the beam's shear reinforcement for the shear from the seismic analysis, without computing the capacity-based shear from the beam's hinging. The mechanism is that once the beam hinges at both ends, its shear is determined by its flexural capacity, which exceeds the analysis demand due to overstrength, and designing for the analysis demand under-designs the shear, allowing a shear failure that pre-empts the ductile hinging. The false signal is that the shear "meets the code demand." The harm is that the beam shears before it hinges, in a sudden, brittle mode, because the shear capacity was below the capacity-based demand, and the ductile mechanism the seismic coefficient assumed never develops. The shear must be designed for the capacity, computed from the expected moment strengths and the gravity, because the capacity-based shear is the maximum the beam can experience, and designing below it allows the brittle failure.

### The Unconfined Joint That Loses Its Capacity

The engineer designs the joint for shear but provides minimal transverse reinforcement, treating the joint as a connection rather than a confined region, and the joint degrades under the reversed cyclic loading. The mechanism is that the beam-column joint experiences high shear and bond stress from the beam moments, and without confinement the concrete degrades, the beam bars lose their bond, and the joint loses its moment transfer capacity, and the frame's mechanism cannot develop. The false signal is that the joint "is designed for shear." The harm is that the joint fails in the earthquake, the frame's connection is lost, and the building's lateral system performs far below its intended capacity, because the confinement that preserves the joint through the cyclic loading was not provided. The joint must be confined with the hoops at the required spacing and with the seismic hooks, because the confinement is what preserves the joint's capacity through the reversed loading.

### Beam Bars Too Large To Develop In The Joint

The engineer specifies large-diameter beam bars to satisfy the flexural capacity, without checking the bar diameter relative to the column depth, and the bars cannot develop their bond within the joint. The mechanism is that the beam bars pass through the joint and transfer their force through bond to the surrounding concrete, and the bond length is limited by the column depth, and large bars in a narrow column exceed the bond capacity and pull out, preventing the beam from developing its moment. The false signal is that the bars "have the required area." The harm is that the beam cannot develop its design moment, the hinge cannot form, and the frame's mechanism fails, because the bars that were meant to provide the flexural capacity pull out of the joint instead. The bar diameter must be limited relative to the column depth, per the code's bond provision, or the column widened, because a bar that cannot bond cannot develop its strength.

### Confinement Hoops With Wrong Hooks Or Spacing

The engineer specifies the confinement hoops but with the wrong hooks, 90-degree instead of 135-degree, or at a spacing wider than the code requires, and the hoops do not confine. The mechanism is that the confinement hoop's effectiveness depends on its anchorage and its spacing, and a 90-degree hook opens under the cyclic load and pulls out, and a wide spacing does not restrain the bars or confine the core, and either error negates the confinement. The false signal is that hoops "are provided." The harm is that the hinge region's core crushes or the bars buckle in the earthquake, because the confinement that would have preserved them was ineffective, and the hinge loses its capacity and its ductility. The hoops must use the 135-degree seismic hooks with the required extensions and the code-required spacing, because the confinement's effectiveness is in the detail, and a wrong hook or a wide spacing is no confinement.

### The Irregularity Identified But Not Penalized

The engineer identifies a seismic irregularity, a soft story or a discontinuous wall, but does not apply the code's penalties, treating the identification as documentation rather than a design trigger. The mechanism is that an irregularity concentrates the seismic demand and complicates the response, and the code imposes penalties, amplified forces, dynamic analysis, or special detailing, to address the concentration, and omitting the penalties under-designs the affected elements. The false signal is that the irregularity "is noted." The harm is that the irregular element, the soft story column or the discontinuous wall, is under-designed for the concentrated demand, and it fails in the earthquake, because the penalty that addressed the concentration was never applied. The irregularity's penalties must be applied, with the amplified forces, the dynamic analysis, and the special detailing, because the penalty is the design's response to the irregularity, and noting it without acting on it is no design.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the strong-column weak-beam ratio checked at every joint, with the column's strength exceeding the beam's expected strength, to enforce the beam-hinging mechanism?
- [ ] Are the beams detailed for ductile flexural hinging, with tension steel below the maximum, compression steel provided, and bars developed into the joint?
- [ ] Is the beam shear designed for the capacity-based shear from the expected moment strengths and the gravity, not the analysis demand?
- [ ] Is the beam-column joint designed for shear and confined with the hoops at the required spacing, with the beam bar sizes limited for bond?
- [ ] Are the plastic hinge regions confined with hoops at the code spacing, with 135-degree seismic hooks and extensions, extending beyond the hinge length?
- [ ] Are seismic irregularities identified, avoided where possible, and where present, are the penalties, amplified forces, dynamic analysis, and special detailing, applied?
- [ ] Does the construction specification require special inspection of the joints and hinge regions, proper consolidation in the congested joints, and the execution of the development lengths and splices?
- [ ] Is the response modification coefficient used in the analysis matched to the detailing provided, so that the ductility the coefficient assumes is delivered?
