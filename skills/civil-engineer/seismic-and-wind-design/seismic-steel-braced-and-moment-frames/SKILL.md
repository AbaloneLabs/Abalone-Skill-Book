# Seismic Steel Braced And Moment Frames

Steel seismic lateral systems, special moment frames, concentrically and eccentrically braced frames, and buckling-restrained braces, are designed to yield in a ductile mode that dissipates the earthquake's energy, and each system has a designated fuse, the beam hinge, the brace yielding, the link shear, or the core yield, that must form before any connection, column, or foundation fails, and the system's performance depends on capacity design that protects every non-fuse element. Agents who size the system for the seismic force and stop miss that the force is reduced by the response modification coefficient precisely because the detailing guarantees the ductile fuse, and that without the capacity design and the connection qualification the system cannot develop the ductility the coefficient assumes. This skill covers the judgment exercised while designing steel seismic systems, with the goal that the fuse yields, the connections and columns are protected by overstrength, and the system sustains the cyclic demand without a brittle fracture.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Ductile Fuse And Capacity-Design Everything Else

Every steel seismic system has a designated ductile fuse, the element or the mechanism that yields and dissipates the energy, and the capacity design principle requires that every other element, the connections, the columns, the collectors, and the foundations, be stronger than the fuse so that the fuse forms first and the others remain elastic. Identify the fuse for the chosen system: the beam's flexural hinge in a special moment frame, the brace's axial yield in tension in a concentrically braced frame, the link's shear in an eccentrically braced frame, the core's axial yield in a buckling-restrained brace. Design every non-fuse element for the fuse's expected strength, using the overstrength factor, so that the fuse yields before the connections fracture or the columns buckle. Capacity design is the organizing principle of steel seismic systems, and identifying the fuse and protecting it is the first and most critical design act.

### Design Special Moment Frames With Qualified Beam-Column Connections

Special moment frames rely on the beam's flexural hinging at the column face, and the connection must be strong enough to develop the beam's plastic moment through the cyclic loading, which requires a qualified connection, pre-qualified per the code or proven by testing, because a standard connection fractures under the cyclic demand. Select a pre-qualified connection, the reduced beam section or the reinforced connection, that forces the hinge away from the column face and protects the connection's weld, because the connection's failure is brittle and pre-empts the beam's ductile hinge. Apply the strong-column weak-beam check, with the column's plastic capacity exceeding the beam's, to enforce the beam-hinging mechanism over the column hinge. Detail the panel zone for the shear from the beam moments, with the doubler plates or the continuity plates, because the panel zone's yielding or its excessive deformation degrades the connection. The special moment frame's performance is in the qualified connection and the capacity design, and both are mandatory.

### Design Concentrically Braced Frames For Brace Yielding And Tension

Concentrically braced frames rely on the brace's axial yielding in tension, because the brace buckles in compression at a fraction of its tension capacity, and the system's ductility comes from the tension yielding and the post-buckling behavior of the compression braces. Design the braces for the tension yield, and check the compression buckling, because the compression capacity is far lower and the brace's post-buckling behavior degrades, and the system must tolerate the asymmetry. Apply the overstrength to the connections, designing them for the brace's expected tension strength rather than the analysis demand, because a connection that fractures before the brace yields pre-empts the ductile behavior. Limit the slenderness and the compactness of the brace, because a too-slender brace buckles elastically with poor post-buckling behavior, and a non-compact section buckles locally. Detail the gusset plates for the brace's end rotation during buckling, with the clearance line, because the brace rotates as it buckles and the gusset must allow it without fracturing. The concentrically braced frame's ductility is in the tension yield and the post-buckling tolerance, and the detailing supports both.

### Design Eccentrically Braced Frames For Link Shear Yielding

Eccentrically braced frames place an active link, a short beam segment between the brace and the column or the brace and the beam, that yields in shear and dissipates the energy, combining the stiffness of a braced frame with the ductility of a moment frame. Design the link for shear yielding, with the link's length determining whether it yields in shear, flexure, or both, because a short link yields in shear, the most ductile mode, and a long link yields in flexure, and the design targets the shear-yielding short link. Design the link's web reinforcement for the shear, and the flanges for the flexure, with the stiffeners that prevent the web from buckling during the cyclic shear, because the link's stability under the reversed shear is what allows it to sustain the ductility. Apply the overstrength to the elements outside the link, the braces, the beam segments, and the columns, designing them for the link's expected shear strength, so that the link yields before anything else. The eccentrically braced frame's ductility is in the link's shear yield, and the capacity design protects the rest.

### Design Buckling-Restrained Braces For Symmetrical Yielding

Buckling-restrained braces are composed of a steel core that yields in both tension and compression, confined by a casing that prevents buckling, and they achieve a symmetrical, ductile hysteresis without the compression buckling that limits ordinary braces. Design the brace's core area for the required axial capacity, and select the casing that provides the buckling restraint, because the core yields in compression as well as tension only if the casing prevents the buckling, and the manufacturer's qualification establishes the restraint. Apply the overstrength to the connections and the surrounding frame, designing them for the brace's expected core strength, because the connection must develop the core's full compression and tension capacity without fracturing. Detail the connection and the gusset for the brace's capacity, not the analysis demand, because a buckling-restrained brace develops its full strength in both directions and the connection must match. The buckling-restrained brace's advantage is the symmetrical yielding, and the capacity design ensures the surrounding frame can deliver it.

### Apply Overstrength To Columns, Collectors, And Foundations

The columns, the collectors that drag the seismic force into the braces, and the foundations must be designed for the system's overstrength, because the lateral system's fuses develop forces above the analysis demand, and these elements must remain elastic to maintain the load path. Apply the overstrength factor to the axial and the flexural demand in the columns, with the system's overstrength combinations, so that the columns do not buckle or hinge under the fuse's actual force. Apply the overstrength to the collectors and their connections, designing them for the brace's or the beam's expected strength, because a collector that yields or fractures interrupts the load path and the lateral system fails. Apply the overstrength to the foundations, designing them for the system's maximum force, because a foundation that yields or tips under the fuse's force undermines the lateral system. The overstrength design of the non-fuse elements is what completes the capacity design, and it is mandatory for the system's integrity.

### Qualify And Inspect The Seismic Connections

Steel seismic connections, the moment connections, the brace connections, and the link connections, are highly stressed and their performance depends on the welding and the detailing, and they must be qualified and inspected to a higher standard than ordinary connections. Use pre-qualified connections, proven by testing and codified, or qualify the connection by project-specific testing, because a connection designed without qualification may not perform under the cyclic demand. Specify the welding procedures, the electrode, the preheat, the backing bar removal, and the weld tabs, matching the pre-qualified detail, because the connection's performance depends on the welding execution and a deviation undermines it. Require the ultrasonic or magnetic particle inspection of the critical welds, the groove welds at the connection, because the visual inspection cannot find the defects that cause fracture. The seismic connection's qualification and inspection are what ensure it performs as tested, and both are mandatory.

## Common Traps

### The System Sized For The Analysis Force, Capacity Design Ignored

The engineer sizes the braces, the beams, and the columns for the seismic analysis force, without applying the capacity design and the overstrength to the non-fuse elements. The mechanism is that the analysis force is reduced by the response modification coefficient, which assumes the ductile fuse forms, and the fuse develops a force above the analysis demand due to overstrength, and the non-fuse elements designed for the analysis demand are weaker than the fuse and fail before it forms. The false signal is that the elements "meet the code force." The harm is that a connection fractures, a column buckles, or a collector yields before the fuse forms, pre-empting the ductile behavior the coefficient assumed, and the system performs far below its intended capacity. The capacity design must be applied, with the overstrength to the connections, columns, collectors, and foundations, because the fuse must form first, and that requires the rest to be stronger.

### The Moment Connection Not Qualified

The engineer designs a standard moment connection, with a complete-joint-penetration weld of the beam flanges to the column, without using a pre-qualified detail or qualifying the connection by testing. The mechanism is that a standard connection's weld, at the beam's flange to the column face, is subject to the full plastic moment and the cyclic demand, and it fractures because the weld's notch toughness and the connection's geometry cannot sustain the demand, and the pre-qualified connections, the reduced beam section or the reinforced connection, were developed precisely to avoid this fracture. The false signal is that the connection "is welded full-strength." The harm is that the connection fractures in the earthquake, the beam's hinge cannot form, and the moment frame's ductility is lost, because the connection that was not qualified failed in the brittle weld fracture that the pre-qualified details prevent. The moment connection must be pre-qualified or tested, with the welding procedures and the inspection matched, because a standard connection fractures under the cyclic demand.

### The Concentric Brace Connection Designed For Demand, Not Expected Strength

The engineer designs the brace's gusset plate and its connection for the analysis demand, without applying the overstrength to the brace's expected tension strength. The mechanism is that the brace yields in tension at its expected strength, which exceeds the analysis demand, and the connection designed for the demand is weaker than the brace, and the connection fractures before the brace yields, pre-empting the ductile tension yielding. The false signal is that the connection "meets the code demand." The harm is that the connection fractures, the brace's ductility is never mobilized, and the braced frame's energy dissipation is lost, because the connection that should have been stronger than the brace was weaker. The brace connection must be designed for the brace's expected tension strength with the overstrength, because the fuse is the brace, and the connection must develop it.

### The Link Without Stiffeners Or With Wrong Length

The engineer designs an eccentrically braced frame's link without the web stiffeners, or with a length that yields in flexure rather than shear, and the link's ductility is compromised. The mechanism is that the link's ductility depends on its shear yielding, which requires a short link, and on its stability, which requires the web stiffeners that prevent the web from buckling during the reversed shear, and a link without stiffeners or with a flexure-yielding length does not sustain the cyclic shear and degrades. The false signal is that the link "is designed for the force." The harm is that the link buckles or yields in flexure, its ductility is lost, and the eccentrically braced frame's performance falls below its intended capacity, because the link's shear-yielding ductility was not achieved. The link must be detailed with the proper length for shear yielding and the web stiffeners at the required spacing, because the link is the fuse and its stability is its ductility.

### The Column Not Checked For Overstrength Axial

The engineer designs the columns for the analysis axial demand, without applying the overstrength from the braces' or the beams' expected strength, and the columns buckle under the fuse's actual force. The mechanism is that the lateral system's fuses develop axial forces in the columns above the analysis demand, and the columns designed for the demand are under-sized for the actual force, and they buckle when the fuses form. The false signal is that the columns "meet the axial demand." The harm is that a column buckles in the earthquake, the load path is lost, and the lateral system collapses, because the column that should have remained elastic under the fuse's force was under-designed. The columns must be designed for the overstrength axial, from the system's expected strength, because the column is a non-fuse element and it must remain elastic to maintain the load path.

### The Seismic Weld Not Inspected To The Required Standard

The engineer specifies the seismic connection but requires only visual inspection of the welds, without the ultrasonic or magnetic particle inspection that the pre-qualified detail requires. The mechanism is that the critical welds, the groove welds at the moment connection or the brace connection, can have internal defects, lack of fusion or cracks, that the visual inspection cannot detect, and the pre-qualified detail's performance assumes the welds are sound, which requires the NDT to verify. The false signal is that the welds "are inspected." The harm is that a defective weld passes the visual inspection, the connection fractures in the earthquake at the defect, and the system's performance is lost, because the inspection that would have found the defect was not performed. The seismic welds must be inspected by the ultrasonic or magnetic particle method, at the required extent, because the visual inspection cannot find the internal defects that cause fracture.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the ductile fuse identified for the chosen system, and is every non-fuse element, connections, columns, collectors, foundations, capacity-designed to be stronger than the fuse?
- [ ] For special moment frames, are the connections pre-qualified or tested, with the strong-column weak-beam check and the panel zone design?
- [ ] For concentrically braced frames, are the braces checked for tension yield and compression buckling, with the connections designed for expected strength and the gussets detailed for end rotation?
- [ ] For eccentrically braced frames, is the link detailed for shear yielding with the proper length and web stiffeners, and are the elements outside the link designed for overstrength?
- [ ] For buckling-restrained braces, is the core selected for the capacity and the casing for the restraint, with the connections and frame designed for the core's expected strength?
- [ ] Are the columns, collectors, and foundations designed for the system's overstrength, so they remain elastic when the fuses form?
- [ ] Are the seismic connections pre-qualified or project-tested, with the welding procedures, backing bar removal, and weld tabs matched to the qualification?
- [ ] Are the critical welds inspected by ultrasonic or magnetic particle methods at the required extent, beyond the visual inspection?
