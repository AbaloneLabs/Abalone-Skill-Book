# Seismic Detailing Of Shear Walls

Seismic shear walls are the stiff, strong elements that resist most of a building's lateral force, and their performance in an earthquake depends on the detailing of the wall's compressive toe, the boundary element, and on the coupling of walls by beams that must yield and dissipate energy without shearing, and a wall that is strong in shear but whose toe crushes or whose coupling beam fails cannot perform its intended role. Agents who provide uniform web reinforcement and stop miss that the wall's survival depends on the boundary element at the compressed toe, that the coupling beam's ductility governs the coupled wall's performance, and that a slender wall can buckle out of plane or fail in shear before it yields in flexure. This skill covers the judgment exercised while detailing seismic shear walls, with the goal that the wall yields in flexure at its base, the boundary element confines the toe, the coupling beam yields and dissipates, and the wall sustains the cyclic demand without brittle failure.

## Core Rules

### Determine The Boundary Element Requirement From The Demand

The boundary element, the confined region at the wall's compressive toe, is required where the seismic demand produces high compressive strain or stress in the toe, and the requirement is determined by the code's triggers, not by the engineer's discretion. Compute the wall's flexural demand under the seismic load, including the axial load, and determine the compressive strain at the toe, using the code's strain-based trigger, or the compressive stress using the stress-based trigger, because the trigger identifies whether the toe requires confinement. Where the trigger is exceeded, detail the boundary element with the confined length, the transverse reinforcement, and the vertical extent, treating it as a column within the wall, because the toe crushes without confinement and the wall's flexural capacity degrades. Where the trigger is not exceeded, the wall may use the less stringent detailing, but confirm the trigger at the design axial and flexural demand, because the requirement depends on the demand. The boundary element is the wall's most critical seismic detail, and its requirement must be explicitly checked.

### Design The Wall For Flexural Ductility At The Base

The seismic wall's intended mechanism is flexural yielding at its base, where the wall's full height is engaged and the plastic hinge forms, and the ductility is achieved through the vertical reinforcement and the confinement. Provide the vertical reinforcement distributed across the wall's length, with the minimum ratios and the maximum spacing, to resist the flexural demand and to distribute the cracking. Concentrate additional vertical reinforcement at the wall's ends, the boundary zones, to provide the flexural capacity, and detail the boundary elements with confinement where required, because the flexural hinge forms at the base and the toe must be confined to sustain the rotation. Check the wall's curvature ductility against the demand implied by the response modification coefficient, because the coefficient assumes a ductility that the wall's detailing must deliver. The wall's flexural ductility is in the vertical reinforcement and the boundary confinement, and the base hinge is the mechanism the seismic design relies on.

### Design Shear For The Capacity, With Web Reinforcement And Confinement

The wall's shear must be designed for the capacity-based shear, the shear produced by the wall's flexural yielding, not the analysis demand, because once the wall yields in flexure its shear is determined by its flexural capacity. Compute the capacity-based shear from the wall's expected flexural strength and the axial load, using the code's method, because this is the maximum shear the wall can experience, and designing below it allows a shear failure that pre-empts the flexural yielding. Provide the horizontal web reinforcement, at the required ratio and spacing, to resist the shear, with the reinforcement distributed across the wall's length and anchored at the ends, because the horizontal steel carries the shear and a bar not anchored at the end does not contribute. Provide the confinement in the boundary elements, because the confinement preserves the web concrete that contributes to the shear, and an unconfined web degrades under the cyclic shear. Capacity-based shear design ensures the wall yields in flexure before it shears, and it is a separate computation from the analysis shear.

### Design Coupling Beams For Ductile Yielding And Energy Dissipation

Coupling beams, the beams that connect two wall piers and force them to act as a unit, experience high shear and moment under the seismic load, and they must yield in a ductile mode and dissipate energy, because the coupling is what makes the wall system efficient and the coupling beam's ductility is what the system relies on. Design the coupling beam for the seismic demand, with the diagonal reinforcement where the demand is high and the aspect ratio is low, because the diagonal reinforcement provides the shear and the moment capacity and the ductility that conventional reinforcement cannot in a short, heavily loaded beam. Detail the diagonal reinforcement with the proper development into the walls and the confinement, because the diagonals are the beam's primary reinforcement and they must be anchored and confined. Limit the coupling beam's depth and the wall's opening geometry, because the beam's behavior depends on its aspect ratio and a too-deep or too-shallow beam does not perform. The coupling beam is the ductile fuse of the coupled wall, and its detailing determines the system's performance.

### Check The Wall's Drift, Stability, And Out-Of-Plane Behavior

The wall's drift under the design seismic, the inelastic drift amplified by the deflection factor, must be within the code's limit, and a wall that exceeds the drift limit damages the non-structural components and may become unstable. Check the wall's drift against the limit, using the amplified inelastic value, because the elastic drift under-predicts the real movement. Check the wall's stability under the P-delta, the gravity acting on the displaced wall, because a slender wall that drifts can become unstable, and the stability coefficient must be within the code's limit. Check the wall's out-of-plane behavior, because a slender wall can buckle out of plane under the axial load combined with the lateral displacement, and the wall's thickness and its out-of-plane reinforcement must prevent it. The wall's drift, stability, and out-of-plane behavior are the checks beyond strength, and they govern the wall's geometry and its performance.

### Detail Wall Piers, Openings, And Discontinuities

Walls rarely exist as simple rectangles; they have piers, openings, and discontinuities, and each is a location of stress concentration and potential failure that must be detailed. Detail the wall piers, the vertical segments between openings, as columns where their aspect ratio and their demand warrant, with the confinement and the shear reinforcement, because a pier is a column-like element subject to high demand. Detail the openings' edges, with the diagonal and the edge reinforcement to resist the cracking and the stress concentration that the opening creates, because an un-detailed opening cracks and propagates the damage. Detail the discontinuous walls, where a wall terminates at a floor and does not continue below, with the transfer of the force into the supporting element, because a discontinuous wall's force must go somewhere and the transfer is a critical detail. The wall's geometry, with its piers and openings, is part of the seismic detailing, and each discontinuity must be addressed.

### Specify Construction And Inspection For Dense Wall Reinforcement

Seismic walls are built with dense reinforcement, the boundary element confinement, the web steel, and the coupling beam diagonals, and the construction must place and consolidate the concrete in this density, because a wall built with misplaced or honeycombed reinforcement does not perform. Require special inspection of the wall reinforcement, especially the boundary elements and the coupling beams, because the congestion is high and the placement must be verified. Require the concrete to be consolidated in the dense regions, with the aggregate size and the vibration matched to the congestion, because honeycombed concrete in a boundary element destroys its confinement. Require the splices and the development lengths to be executed as detailed, with the staggered splices and the confined lap zones, because a deviation undermines the wall's capacity. The construction specification is what delivers the seismic wall, and it must require the practices that preserve the dense detailing.

## Common Traps

### The Boundary Element Requirement Not Checked

The engineer designs the wall for axial, flexure, and shear with uniform web reinforcement, without checking the code trigger that requires a boundary element at the compressed toe. The mechanism is that the boundary element requirement is triggered by the compressive strain or stress at the toe, and a wall focused on overall capacity can miss that the toe requires confinement, and the toe crushes in the earthquake because the confinement was never provided. The false signal is that the wall "has enough steel." The harm is that the toe crushes, the wall's flexural capacity degrades, and the lateral system fails to perform, because the boundary element that would have confined the toe and preserved the capacity was never detailed. The boundary element trigger must be explicitly checked at the design demand, and where triggered, the confinement detailed, because a wall without its required boundary element is a wall designed to crush at its toe.

### Shear Designed For The Analysis Demand, Not The Capacity

The engineer designs the wall's horizontal shear reinforcement for the seismic analysis demand, without computing the capacity-based shear from the wall's flexural yielding. The mechanism is that once the wall yields in flexure, its shear is determined by its flexural capacity, which exceeds the analysis demand due to overstrength, and designing for the analysis demand under-designs the shear, allowing a shear failure that pre-empts the flexural yielding. The false signal is that the shear "meets the code demand." The harm is that the wall shears before it yields in flexure, in a sudden, brittle mode, because the shear capacity was below the capacity-based demand, and the ductile flexural mechanism the seismic coefficient assumed never develops. The wall's shear must be designed for the capacity, computed from the expected flexural strength, because the capacity-based shear is the maximum the wall can experience.

### The Coupling Beam Without Diagonal Reinforcement

The engineer designs a short, heavily loaded coupling beam with conventional longitudinal and transverse reinforcement, without the diagonal reinforcement the code requires for high-demand coupling beams. The mechanism is that a short coupling beam's behavior is governed by shear, and conventional reinforcement cannot provide the ductility under the reversed cyclic shear, and the diagonal reinforcement, which carries the shear and the moment through the diagonals, provides the ductility that conventional steel cannot, and the code requires it above a demand threshold. The false signal is that the beam "has the required steel area." The harm is that the coupling beam fails in shear under the cyclic load, the coupling is lost, and the coupled wall system reverts to two independent walls with far less capacity, because the beam's ductility that the system relied on was not provided. The coupling beam must use the diagonal reinforcement where the demand or the aspect ratio triggers it, with the diagonals developed and confined, because a conventional beam in a high-demand coupling location fails.

### The Slender Wall That Buckles Out Of Plane

The engineer designs a slender wall for in-plane flexure and shear, without checking the out-of-plane buckling under the axial load and the lateral displacement. The mechanism is that a slender wall, displaced laterally by the seismic load and loaded axially by the gravity, can buckle out of plane, like a column, and the in-plane design does not address the out-of-plane instability. The false signal is that the wall "is designed for the in-plane load." The harm is that the wall buckles out of plane during the earthquake, losing its in-plane capacity and its lateral resistance, because the out-of-plane stability was never checked. The wall's thickness and its out-of-plane reinforcement must be checked against the buckling, with the slenderness and the axial load, because a slender wall can fail out of plane regardless of its in-plane design.

### The Discontinuous Wall's Force Transfer Not Detailed

The engineer designs a wall that terminates at a floor, a discontinuous wall, without detailing the transfer of the wall's force into the supporting element below. The mechanism is that a discontinuous wall's force, shear and overturning, must transfer into the supporting beam, slab, or wall below, and the transfer is a tension-and-compression mechanism that requires specific reinforcement, and an un-detailed transfer allows the force to follow an unintended path, cracking and failing the supporting element. The false signal is that the wall "is supported below." The harm is that the supporting element fails under the transferred force, the discontinuous wall loses its support, and the lateral system is compromised, because the transfer that the discontinuity requires was never designed. The discontinuous wall's force transfer must be detailed, with the tension ties and the compression paths, because a discontinuity is a load path change that must be engineered.

### Honeycombed Concrete In The Congested Boundary Element

The engineer details the dense boundary element confinement, but the construction specification does not address the consolidation of concrete in the congested cage, and the built boundary element is honeycombed. The mechanism is that the boundary element's reinforcement is dense, the hoops and the vertical bars, and the concrete must flow through and around it, and without proper aggregate size and vibration the concrete honeycombs, and the honeycombed concrete has neither the confinement's benefit nor the strength, because the unconsolidated concrete is defective. The false signal is that the boundary element "is reinforced as detailed." The harm is that the toe crushes in the earthquake despite the confinement on paper, because the actual concrete in the toe was honeycombed and the confinement enclosed defective material. The construction specification must require the aggregate size, the vibration, and the inspection that ensure consolidation in the dense boundary element, because a detailed confinement around honeycombed concrete is no confinement.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the boundary element requirement explicitly checked at the design axial and flexural demand, and where triggered, is the toe confined with the length, transverse reinforcement, and extent required?
- [ ] Is the wall detailed for flexural ductility at the base, with distributed vertical reinforcement, concentrated boundary reinforcement, and confinement?
- [ ] Is the wall's shear designed for the capacity-based shear from the expected flexural strength, with horizontal web reinforcement anchored at the ends?
- [ ] Are coupling beams designed with diagonal reinforcement where the demand or aspect ratio triggers, with the diagonals developed and confined?
- [ ] Is the wall's amplified inelastic drift checked against the code limit, and is the P-delta stability and the out-of-plane buckling checked?
- [ ] Are wall piers, openings, and discontinuities detailed, with piers treated as columns, openings' edges reinforced, and discontinuous walls' force transfers designed?
- [ ] Does the construction specification require special inspection, proper aggregate and vibration for consolidation in the dense regions, and the execution of splices and development lengths?
- [ ] Is the response modification coefficient used in the analysis matched to the wall's detailing, so that the ductility assumed is delivered?
