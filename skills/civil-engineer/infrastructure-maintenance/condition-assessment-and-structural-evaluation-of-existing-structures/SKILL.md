# Condition Assessment and Structural Evaluation of Existing Structures

Condition assessment and structural evaluation is the engineering of the existing structure: the field investigation of the distress and the deterioration, the determination of the in-place material properties and the as-built configuration, and the evaluation of the current capacity and the remaining service life against the current and the changed loads. The existing structure has the history (the original design, the construction, the modifications, the deterioration) that the new structure does not, and the evaluation must account for this history. The harm this skill prevents is a repair that treats the symptom and not the cause (the patch over the active corrosion, the support under the settled foundation), a capacity evaluation that uses the original design properties instead of the in-place properties (the overestimate of the strength and the unsafe conclusion), or a conclusion of "safe" or "unsafe" without the investigation that supports it (the unexamined structure that fails or the sound structure that is condemned). Because the existing structure is the high-stakes (the public safety, the cost of the repair or the replacement, the disruption of the use), the evaluation must be the thorough, the cause-based, and the evidence-based, and the conclusion must be the conservative.

## Core Rules

### Conduct the Document Review and the History Investigation

The evaluation begins with the document review: the original design drawings, the structural calculations, the shop drawings, the material test reports, the previous inspection and repair reports, and the record of the modifications and the changes of the use. Establish the original design (the code, the loads, the material strengths), the as-built (the construction, the deviations), the history (the modifications, the damage, the repairs), and the current use (the loads, the occupancy). The document review directs the field investigation (where to look, what to verify), and the absence of the documents increases the investigation scope, because the unknown structure requires the more thorough field work.

### Perform the Field Investigation for the Distress and the As-Built

The field investigation documents the distress (the cracks, the spalls, the corrosion, the settlement, the leakage, the deformation) and the as-built configuration (the member sizes, the reinforcement, the connections, the material condition). Use the visual inspection, the sounding, the cover meters, the half-cell potential, the ultrasonic, the coring and the sampling, and the survey, and document the distress with the location, the size, the orientation, and the pattern. The distress pattern is the diagnostic: the crack that follows the flexural tension, the spall over the corroding rebar, the settlement crack that widens with the time. An investigation that documents the distress without the pattern, or the as-built without the distress, is the incomplete, and the cause and the capacity cannot be determined.

### Determine the In-Place Material Properties and the As-Built Capacity

The in-place material properties (the concrete strength, the steel yield, the weld quality, the timber grade) and the as-built configuration (the member size, the reinforcement, the connection) determine the actual capacity, and these must be determined, not assumed from the original design. Test the in-place properties (the core strength, the rebound hammer, the coupon test, the weld inspection), and verify the as-built (the cover meter, the exposed reinforcement, the measurement), because the original design may differ from the as-built (the lower strength, the missing bars, the deviation). Use the in-place properties in the capacity evaluation, and account for the deterioration (the section loss, the corrosion), because the original design properties overestimate the capacity of the deteriorated structure.

### Diagnose the Cause of the Distress, Not Just the Symptom

The distress has the cause (the overload, the corrosion, the settlement, the thermal, the chemical attack, the design deficiency), and the cause must be diagnosed, not just the symptom. The crack is the symptom; the cause may be the differential settlement, the shrinkage, the overload, or the corrosion, and the diagnosis requires the pattern, the history, and the investigation. Distinguish the structural distress (the active, the load-related, the capacity-affecting) from the non-structural (the cosmetic, the stable, the unrelated to the capacity). A repair that treats the symptom (the epoxy injection of the crack) without the cause (the ongoing settlement) will fail, because the cause continues and the distress returns.

### Evaluate the Capacity Against the Current and the Changed Loads

Evaluate the structure's capacity against the current loads (the actual dead, live, and environmental) and the changed loads (the new use, the increased occupancy, the new equipment, the code update). Use the in-place properties and the as-built configuration, and the appropriate evaluation method (the allowable stress, the strength design, the reliability-based, the code for the existing structures). Account for the deterioration in the capacity, and the load test where the calculation is uncertain. The evaluation determines the capacity ratio (the demand over the capacity), and the structure with the ratio above the unity is the unsafe, and the repair or the strengthening is required. An evaluation with the original design properties and the current code loads may conclude the unsafe where the in-place capacity is the adequate, or the safe where the deterioration has reduced the capacity.

### Determine the Remaining Service Life and the Repair Strategy

The remaining service life (the time before the deterioration reaches the limit) and the repair strategy (the do-nothing, the maintenance, the repair, the strengthening, the replacement) are determined from the condition, the capacity, and the cause. Estimate the remaining service life with the deterioration model (the corrosion propagation, the fatigue, the service-life model), and select the repair strategy based on the capacity, the remaining life, and the cost. The repair addresses the cause (the corrosion prevention, the settlement stabilisation, the strengthening), not just the symptom, and the strategy is the life-cycle cost-effective. A repair without the remaining-life and the strategy is the short-term, and the structure returns to the distress, because the cause was not addressed.

### Document the Evaluation and the Recommendation With the Evidence

The evaluation report documents the investigation, the in-place properties, the cause diagnosis, the capacity evaluation, the remaining service life, and the recommendation, with the evidence (the photos, the test results, the calculations). The report is the basis for the repair design, the owner's decision, and the engineer's liability, and it must be the clear, the evidence-based, and the conservative. State the limitations (the inaccessible areas, the assumptions), and the recommended actions (the repair, the monitoring, the load test), and the urgency. A report without the evidence and the limitations is the unsupported, and the conclusion is the challengeable, because the basis was not documented.

## Common Traps

### The Original Design Properties Assumed As The In-Place

The capacity evaluation uses the original design strengths (the 28 MPa concrete, the 420 MPa steel) instead of the in-place properties, and the capacity is overestimated, especially for the deteriorated structure. The trap is that the original design properties are the available and the convenient, while the in-place properties may be the lower (the low-strength concrete, the corroded steel), and the assumption overestimates the capacity. The false signal is the design-based calculation; the harm is the unsafe conclusion, because the in-place properties were not determined.

### The Symptom Treated Without The Cause

The repair addresses the distress (the crack, the spall) without the cause (the ongoing corrosion, the settlement, the overload), and the distress returns. The trap is that the symptom is the visible and the treatable, while the cause is the hidden and the ongoing, and the symptom-only repair fails. The false signal is the repaired crack; the harm is the recurring distress, because the cause was not diagnosed and addressed.

### The Cosmetic Distress Mistaken For The Structural

The cosmetic distress (the shrinkage crack, the surface spall) is mistaken for the structural distress, and the structure is condemned or the unnecessary repair is prescribed. The trap is that the distress looks the alarming, while it is the non-structural, and the overreaction wastes the resource or the condemns the sound structure. The false signal is the visible distress; the harm is the unnecessary cost or the lost use, because the structural versus the cosmetic was not distinguished.

### The Structural Distress Mistaken For The Cosmetic

The structural distress (the flexural crack, the shear crack, the settlement crack) is mistaken for the cosmetic, and the structure continues in the unsafe condition. The trap is that the distress looks the minor, while it is the structural, and the underreaction allows the continued deterioration and the failure. The false signal is the minor-looking crack; the harm is the undetected structural deficiency, because the structural versus the cosmetic was not diagnosed.

### The Investigation Incomplete For The Unknown Structure

The structure with the missing documents is evaluated with the limited investigation, and the unknown configuration and the properties are assumed, and the conclusion is the unsupported. The trap is that the investigation is the limited, while the structure is the unknown, and the assumption is the unsupported. The false signal is the completed evaluation; the harm is the unsupported conclusion, because the investigation was not scaled to the unknown.

### The Conclusion Without The Evidence And The Limitations

The evaluation concludes the safe or the unsafe without the evidence (the photos, the tests, the calculations) and the limitations (the inaccessible areas, the assumptions), and the conclusion is the challengeable. The trap is that the conclusion is the stated, while the evidence and the limitations are not, and the conclusion is the unsupported. The false signal is the definitive conclusion; the harm is the challengeable report, because the basis was not documented.

## Self-Check

- Is the document review complete (the original design, the as-built, the history, the current use), and does it direct the field investigation?
- Does the field investigation document the distress (the location, the size, the pattern) and the as-built configuration (the sizes, the reinforcement, the connections)?
- Are the in-place material properties determined (the core strength, the coupon, the weld), and not assumed from the original design?
- Is the cause of the distress diagnosed (the overload, the corrosion, the settlement), and not just the symptom documented?
- Is the capacity evaluated against the current and the changed loads, with the in-place properties and the deterioration, and the demand-to-capacity ratio determined?
- Is the remaining service life estimated, and the repair strategy (the maintenance, the repair, the strengthening, the replacement) based on the cause and the life-cycle cost?
- Does the report document the investigation, the evidence, the limitations, and the recommendation, with the conservative and the clear conclusion?
- Are the structural and the cosmetic distress distinguished, and the active versus the stable distress identified?
