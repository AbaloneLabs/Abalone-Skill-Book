# Below-Grade And Plaza Waterproofing Design

Below-grade and plaza waterproofing is the barrier that keeps water out of occupied or usable space below ground, and it is engineered against hydrostatic pressure, groundwater chemistry, and the structural and construction conditions of the below-grade enclosure. Unlike above-grade envelope work, below-grade waterproofing is largely inaccessible after construction: a leaking basement wall cannot be inspected or repaired from the exterior without excavation, and the cost and disruption of remediation are extreme. This inaccessibility makes the design and the installation quality decisive, because the system must perform for the life of the structure without maintenance access.

The harm this skill prevents is a below-grade waterproofing system selected for the wrong water condition (a dampproofing applied where hydrostatic pressure requires waterproofing), a system installed on the wrong side (negative-side waterproofing where positive-side is required), a joint or penetration detail that creates a leak path under pressure, or a drainage system that is absent or undersized and allows hydrostatic pressure to build against the wall. Below-grade leaks are among the most expensive building defects to remediate, and they are almost always failures of system selection, joint detailing, or drainage integration, not failures of the field membrane.

## Core Rules

### Determine The Groundwater Condition And Design For The Worst Case

The waterproofing system must be designed for the actual groundwater condition, and the condition must be determined from the geotechnical investigation, not assumed. The key parameters are the historic high groundwater table (not the current table, which may be lower), the perched water table, the surface water infiltration, and the potential for the table to rise over the structure's life due to rainfall, construction, or climate change.

Design for the worst-case water condition: if the historic high groundwater table is at or above the basement level, design for full hydrostatic pressure. Do not design for the current or the dry-season table, because groundwater rises and a system designed for the low table fails under the high. Confirm the design water level with the geotechnical engineer and document the basis.

### Select The Waterproofing System For The Side, The Pressure, And The Construction

Waterproofing systems are classified by the side of application: positive-side (exterior, the side the water presses against), negative-side (interior), and blind-side (exterior, applied before the structural wall is poured, against the shoring). Positive-side waterproofing is the standard for new construction because it resists the water pressure directly; negative-side waterproofing is a remedial measure, limited in pressure capacity; blind-side waterproofing is used where excavation access prevents exterior application after the wall is built.

Select the system for the side available, the hydrostatic pressure, and the construction method. For a basement wall against a property line (no exterior access after the wall is poured), blind-side waterproofing applied to the shoring is required. For a new basement with exterior access, positive-side waterproofing applied to the exterior of the wall is standard. Match the system to the construction sequence and the access, because a system that cannot be installed in the available access is not an option.

### Detail Every Joint As A Potential Leak Path

Joints are the dominant leak path in below-grade waterproofing, and every joint (construction joints, expansion joints, control joints, cold joints) must be detailed with a waterstop or a waterproofing reinforcement. A membrane continuous in the field of the wall but discontinuous or unreinforced at a construction joint leaks at the joint under hydrostatic pressure.

Detail every joint with a waterstop (PVC, hydrophilic, metal) embedded in the concrete at the joint, or with a waterproofing membrane reinforcement (a membrane strip over the joint). For expansion joints, use a waterstop designed to accommodate movement. The joint detail is more important than the field membrane, because the joints are where the water enters.

### Integrate The Waterproofing At Every Penetration

Every penetration through the below-grade enclosure (a pipe, a conduit, a duct, a tieback, a soil nail) is a potential leak path, and the waterproofing must be detailed to seal around it. A penetration that pierces the membrane without a seal or a boot leaks under pressure, because the water follows the penetration through the wall.

Detail every penetration with a seal, a boot, or a prefabricated penetration flashing compatible with the membrane. For structural tiebacks or soil nails that cannot be sealed (because they are tensioned), evaluate the leak risk and the mitigation. Coordinate the penetrations with the structural and the MEP design, because a penetration added after the waterproofing is installed compromises the system.

### Provide Drainage To Relieve Hydrostatic Pressure

Drainage is the system that relieves hydrostatic pressure by collecting and conveying groundwater away from the structure, and it reduces the load on the waterproofing. A drainage system (a perimeter drain, a footing drain, a geocomposite drainage board) lowers the water table at the wall and reduces the pressure the waterproofing must resist. For structures below the groundwater table, drainage alone may not eliminate hydrostatic pressure, but it reduces it and provides redundancy.

Design the drainage for the expected water flow, with a perforated pipe at the footing, a filter fabric to prevent clogging, a drainage board on the wall to convey water to the pipe, and a discharge to a sump or a storm system. Verify that the drainage discharge is reliable (a gravity discharge to a storm system, or a sump pump with backup power), because a drainage system that cannot discharge allows the water to rise and the pressure to build.

### Verify Material Compatibility And Chemical Resistance

Below-grade environments expose the waterproofing to groundwater chemistry (chlorides, sulfates, acids) and to the concrete and the drainage materials, and the system must be chemically compatible and resistant. A membrane degraded by groundwater chemistry, or a drainage board that reacts with the concrete, fails prematurely.

Verify the chemical resistance of the membrane for the site groundwater chemistry (from the geotechnical report), and the compatibility of the membrane with the concrete, the drainage board, and the sealants. Specify materials rated for the exposure, and avoid known incompatibilities.

### Plan The Installation Quality And Inspection For An Inaccessible System

Below-grade waterproofing is inaccessible after the wall is backfilled, and the installation quality is decisive because there is no maintenance access. The installation must be inspected before backfill, with the seams tested, the penetrations verified, and the protection board installed. A system backfilled without inspection cannot be verified or corrected.

Plan the inspection protocol: the substrate preparation, the membrane application, the seam testing, the penetration sealing, and the protection board, all inspected and documented before backfill. Specify the protection board (to prevent damage during backfill) and the backfill material (free of debris that could puncture the membrane). An inaccessible system installed without inspection is a latent defect that surfaces as a leak after occupancy.

## Common Traps

### Designing For The Current, Not The Historic High, Groundwater Table

A system designed for the current or dry-season table fails when the table rises to the historic high. The trap is using the observed table instead of the design table from the geotechnical investigation.

### Dampproofing Where Waterproofing Is Required

Dampproofing resists soil moisture but not hydrostatic pressure, and applying it where the water table exerts pressure guarantees leaks. The trap is confusing the two systems.

### Negative-Side Waterproofing For New Construction

Negative-side waterproofing is a remedial measure with limited pressure capacity, inadequate for new below-grade construction under hydrostatic pressure. The trap is using it to avoid exterior access.

### Joints Without Waterstops

A membrane continuous in the field but without a waterstop at the construction joint leaks at the joint. The trap is detailing the field and ignoring the joints.

### Penetrations Without Seals

A pipe or conduit that pierces the membrane without a seal leaks under pressure. The trap is treating penetrations as the MEP trade's responsibility.

### Drainage Absent Or With No Discharge

A drainage system that cannot discharge (a sump without backup power, a pipe to nowhere) allows the water to rise and the pressure to build. The trap is installing drainage without verifying the discharge.

### Backfill Without Inspection

An inaccessible system backfilled without inspection cannot be verified, and defects surface as leaks after occupancy. The trap is treating waterproofing as a finish rather than a critical, inaccessible barrier.

## Self-Check

- Is the waterproofing designed for the historic high groundwater table and full hydrostatic pressure, as confirmed by the geotechnical investigation?
- Is the system (positive-side, negative-side, blind-side) selected for the side available, the pressure, and the construction method?
- Is every joint (construction, expansion, control) detailed with a waterstop or a membrane reinforcement?
- Is every penetration sealed with a compatible boot, seal, or flashing, coordinated with the structural and MEP design?
- Is drainage provided to relieve hydrostatic pressure, with a filter, a discharge to a reliable sump or storm system, and backup power if pumped?
- Are the membrane and all materials verified for chemical resistance to the site groundwater and compatibility with the concrete and drainage?
- Is an installation inspection protocol planned for substrate, seams, penetrations, and protection board, all before backfill?
- Is the system designed for the life of the structure, given that it is inaccessible after construction?
