# Asphalt and Aggregate Quality Testing

Asphalt pavement performance is governed by a set of interlocking volumetric and aggregate properties that are easy to report individually but difficult to read as a system. A mix that meets the binder content on the JMF can still fail if the voids in mineral aggregate (VMA) are too low, if the aggregate is susceptible to degradation, or if the in-place density falls short of the design intent. The civil engineer responsible for asphalt and aggregate quality is reading a mixture design and a field control program simultaneously, and the persistent failure is treating each test result as an isolated pass/fail rather than as a signal about the mix behavior. Hot mix asphalt (HMA) and Superpave design tie binder grade, gradation, and volumetrics to performance, and field density and the indirect tensile (IDT) strength confirm that the placed pavement matches the design. Aggregate quality—soundness, abrasion, and degradation—determines whether the skeleton will survive traffic and weather. This skill forces the decisions that determine whether the pavement will perform: correct volumetric interpretation, correct density method and target, aggregate durability verification, and field control that links plant production to the placed mat.

## Core Rules

### Read Volumetric Properties as a System, Not as Individual Targets

Superpave mix design establishes the binder content at the design number of gyrations (Ndesign) to achieve 4 percent air voids, and the engineer must verify that the voids in mineral aggregate (VMA) and the voids filled with asphalt (VFA) are within the Superpave criteria for the nominal maximum aggregate size. VMA is the space available for asphalt and air, and if VMA is too low there is insufficient binder for durability, leading to cracking and raveling; if VMA is too high, the mix is unstable and prone to rutting. The engineer must not accept a JMF that meets the 4 percent air void target but violates VMA, because the VMA governs the effective binder film thickness and thus the durability. Dust-to-binder ratio (the percentage of material passing the 0.075 mm sieve divided by the effective binder content) must be checked, because a high dust ratio produces a brittle mix and a low dust ratio produces a tender, rut-prone mix. The engineer must read the volumetric report as a balanced system where air voids, VMA, VFA, and dust ratio interact, and a single property within range does not confirm a durable mix.

### Match Binder Grade and Gradation to the Climate and Traffic

Performance-graded (PG) binder is specified by the high and low pavement temperatures it must resist, and the engineer must verify that the binder grade matches the project climate and the traffic level, including any grade bump for slow or heavy traffic (e.g., increasing the high-temperature grade for a high-volume route to resist rutting). Polymer-modified binders may be required for intersections, bus routes, or airport pavements where stopping and standing loads cause rutting. The gradation must match the Superpave design with the appropriate control points and restricted zone, and the engineer must verify that the aggregate blend from the plant matches the JMF gradation within tolerance, because a plant drift in the fine fraction changes the VMA and the volumetrics. The engineer must require gradation verification on plant-produced mix, not just on the design blend, because production stockpiles differ from the design materials. Binder content must be verified by ignition oven or extraction, because a plant drift in binder content directly changes the air voids and the field density target.

### Verify In-Place Density Against the Correct Reference and Method

In-place density is the single most important field property for asphalt performance, and the engineer must specify the reference density correctly: percent of maximum theoretical density (Gmm) is the Superpave standard, and a typical specification requires 92 to 94 percent of Gmm at the core, with rejection below approximately 90 percent. The engineer must not allow percent compaction to be reported against a Marshall or a field bulk density that obscures the air void content, because the performance relationship is to the in-place air voids. Density is verified by cores (ASTM D1188 or D2726 for bulk specific gravity) as the referee method, with nuclear or non-nuclear gauge (ASTM D6938 or D7757) used for rapid field control. The engineer must recognize that gauge readings on thin lifts, on open-graded friction courses, or on mixes with high absorption are unreliable and must be correlated to cores. Joint density is a frequent failure point: longitudinal joints are typically 2 to 3 percent lower in density than the mat center, and the engineer must specify and verify joint density separately, because a low-density joint is where water enters and raveling begins.

### Confirm Aggregate Durability and Resistance to Degradation

Aggregate is the load-carrying skeleton, and the engineer must verify that the aggregate source meets the durability requirements for the application: Los Angeles abrasion (ASTM C131/C535) for resistance to degradation, sodium or magnesium sulfate soundness (ASTM C88) for freeze-thaw resistance, and the appropriate deleterious materials limits. For Superpave, the coarse aggregate angularity (fractured faces), fine aggregate angularity, flat and elongated particle limits, and clay content (sand equivalent) must meet the criteria for the traffic level, because rounded or flat particles produce a mix that rutts under traffic. The engineer must verify that the aggregate tested is the aggregate being produced, because a quarry face can change and a previously sound source can begin producing degraded material. Degradation during production and compaction is a hidden risk: a mix designed on sound aggregate can lose its gradation and VMA if the aggregate breaks down under the compactor, and the engineer should require field verification of gradation on the compacted mat when degradation is suspected.

## Common Traps

### Accepting a JMF on Air Voids Alone

The false signal is 4 percent air voids at Ndesign; the mechanism is that VMA can be below criteria while air voids are met, leaving insufficient binder film for durability. The harm is a mix that designs correctly but cracks and ravels prematurely, because the volumetric balance was never checked.

### Reporting Density Against a Non-Theoretical Reference

The false signal is a percent compaction that meets specification; the mechanism is that the reference is a Marshall or field bulk density that hides the actual in-place air voids. The harm is a pavement accepted as compacted that has air voids above 7 percent and will ravel and permeate water, because the density was not tied to Gmm.

### Ignoring Joint Density

The false signal is a mat density that passes; the mechanism is that longitudinal joints compact poorly and run 2 to 3 percent lower density than the mat center, and joint density is not tested separately. The harm is raveling and water intrusion at the joint, because the weakest location was averaged into a passing mat number.

### Trusting Aggregate Soundness on an Old Source Test

The false signal is a soundness report from the design phase; the mechanism is that the quarry face changes and the produced aggregate degrades differently, breaking down during compaction and altering the gradation and VMA. The harm is a mix that meets the JMF on paper but loses its aggregate skeleton in the field, because the aggregate was not re-verified during production.

## Self-Check

- Does the JMF meet all Superpave volumetric criteria (air voids, VMA, VFA, dust-to-binder ratio) for the nominal maximum aggregate size, not just the 4 percent air void target?
- Is the PG binder grade matched to the project climate and traffic, including any high-temperature grade bump for slow or heavy loading and polymer modification where required?
- Is in-place density reported as a percent of maximum theoretical density (Gmm), with a typical 92 to 94 percent acceptance and rejection below approximately 90 percent?
- Are cores used as the referee density method, with nuclear or non-nuclear gauge readings correlated to cores on thin lifts, open-graded, or high-absorption mixes?
- Is joint density specified and verified separately from the mat, recognizing that longitudinal joints run lower density and are the primary water-intrusion and raveling location?
- Are aggregate durability properties (LA abrasion, soundness, fractured faces, flat and elongated, sand equivalent) verified on the produced aggregate, with field gradation checks when degradation is suspected?
