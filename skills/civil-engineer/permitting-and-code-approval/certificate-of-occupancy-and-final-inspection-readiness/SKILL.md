# Certificate Of Occupancy And Final Inspection Readiness

The certificate of occupancy (CO) is the legal instrument that authorizes use and occupancy of a building, and it is the final regulatory gate of a construction project. It is issued only after every required inspection passes, every life-safety system is tested and commissioned, every approval condition is satisfied, and the as-built record matches the approved permit set. A project can be physically complete and legally unoccupiable, and the gap between physical completion and CO issuance is where schedule and revenue are lost.

The harm this skill prevents is a final inspection that fails on a life-safety defect that blocks occupancy, a closeout package that is incomplete and forces re-inspection, a CO that is issued with conditions or a temporary CO that expires before the conditions are met, or a building that is occupied without a CO and exposes the owner to fines, insurance invalidation, and liability. Engineers and contractors tend to treat closeout as the end of the project, but it is a distinct engineering and coordination phase with its own critical path, its own authority dependencies, and its own failure modes. The cost of a delayed CO is measured in lost rent, extended construction loans, and liquidated damages, and it is almost always avoidable.

## Core Rules

### Build The Closeout And CO Critical Path At Project Start, Not At Project End

The documents, tests, inspections, and approvals required for a CO are determined at project start, because many of them must be scheduled and sequenced during construction, not assembled at the end. Identify every required inspection (foundation, framing, rough-in, insulation, final building, final electrical, final mechanical, final plumbing, fire), every required test (fire alarm, sprinkler, smoke control, elevator, backflow, pressure), every required certification (special inspection reports, manufacturer certifications, energy compliance), and every required closeout document (record drawings, O&M manuals, warranties, commissioning reports).

Build a closeout schedule that sequences these items to the construction schedule, because some tests (fire alarm, sprinkler) require the systems to be energized and commissioned, which requires the power and water to be on, which requires the utility releases. A closeout critical path built at the end always misses dependencies that should have been sequenced months earlier.

### Resolve Every Approval Condition From The Building Permit

The building permit and its plan-check corrections often carry conditions: a required special inspection, a required field test, a required easement, a required off-site improvement. These conditions must be tracked and resolved, because the CO will not be issued until each is closed.

Maintain a conditions log from the permit issuance, recording each condition, the evidence required to close it, the responsible party, and the status. Conditions discovered at final inspection, when they should have been resolved during construction, are the most common cause of CO delay.

### Complete And Verify The Life-Safety System Commissioning Before Final Inspection

Life-safety systems (fire alarm, sprinkler, smoke control, fire-rated assemblies, means of egress, emergency lighting, exit signage) are the systems that the final inspection scrutinizes most closely, and a defect in any of them blocks the CO. These systems must be fully installed, tested, and commissioned, with the test reports and certifications in the closeout package, before the final inspection is requested.

Coordinate the commissioning sequence: the fire alarm test requires the alarm system installed and powered, the sprinkler test requires the water supply and the fire pump, the smoke control test requires the HVAC controls. Sequence these tests so that each is ready when the final inspection occurs, because a failed life-safety test reschedules the final inspection and the CO.

### Assemble A Complete And Internally Consistent Closeout Package

The closeout package is the evidence that the building was built as approved, and it must be complete and internally consistent before it is submitted. A typical package includes record (as-built) drawings, specifications with addenda, special inspection reports, material and equipment certifications, test reports, warranties, O&M manuals, the commissioning report, the energy compliance documentation, and the manufacturer instructions.

Verify that the record drawings reflect every approved change, every RFI, and every field deviation, and that they are sealed where required. An inconsistent closeout package, where the record drawings do not match the approved set or the special inspection reports reference tests not in the package, triggers re-inspection and delay.

### Coordinate The Final Inspection Sequence Across Authorities

The final inspection is rarely a single inspection. The building department, the fire marshal, the health department, and the utility authorities each conduct a final inspection, and they have dependencies. The utility release (electric, gas, water) may be required before the fire alarm and sprinkler tests, which are required before the building final, which is required before the fire marshal final.

Sequence the final inspections to their dependencies and schedule them so that each authority inspects when its prerequisite is satisfied. A final inspection scheduled before its prerequisite is complete fails and reschedules, extending the CO.

### Distinguish A Temporary CO From A Final CO And Manage Its Conditions

A temporary or partial certificate of occupancy (TCO, PCO) allows occupancy before all work is complete, but it carries conditions and an expiration date. A TCO is useful when the core life-safety systems are complete but finishing work remains, but it creates a legal obligation to complete the conditions by the expiration.

If a TCO is requested, identify the conditions, the expiration, and the plan to close each condition before expiration. Occupancy under an expired TCO, or occupancy of areas not covered by the TCO, is unpermitted occupancy and carries the same risks as no CO.

### Maintain The Record Of Approved Deviations For The Final Set

Every field deviation, substitution, and RFI-driven change that was approved by the AHJ must be reflected in the record drawings and available at final inspection. The inspector verifies construction against the approved set plus approved changes; an undocumented deviation, even an approved one not reflected in the record, is treated as unpermitted work.

Maintain the approved revision log throughout construction and reconcile it to the record drawings before final inspection.

## Common Traps

### Treating Closeout As The End Rather Than A Sequenced Phase

Assembling the closeout package and scheduling tests at the end, rather than sequencing them during construction, misses dependencies and extends the CO by weeks. The trap is treating closeout as paperwork rather than a critical-path phase.

### Requesting The Final Inspection Before Life-Safety Commissioning

A final inspection requested before the fire alarm, sprinkler, or smoke control tests are complete and documented fails on the life-safety system and reschedules the entire CO. The false signal is that the systems are "installed"; the requirement is that they are tested and certified.

### An Inconsistent Closeout Package

Record drawings that do not match the approved set, or special inspection reports that reference tests not in the package, signal incomplete work and trigger re-inspection. The trap is assembling the package quickly without internal verification.

### Missing A Permit Condition At Final Inspection

A condition from the building permit, such as a required off-site improvement or a special inspection, that is discovered at final inspection blocks the CO. The trap is not tracking permit conditions throughout construction.

### Occupying Under An Expired Or Out-Of-Scope TCO

Occupying beyond a TCO's expiration, or occupying areas not covered by it, is unpermitted occupancy. The false signal is that occupancy was "allowed"; the harm is fines and insurance invalidation.

### Undocumented Approved Deviations

An approved field change not reflected in the record drawings is treated as unpermitted work at final inspection. The trap is treating the approval as sufficient without updating the record.

## Self-Check

- Is the closeout and CO critical path built at project start, with inspections, tests, certifications, and documents sequenced to the construction schedule?
- Is every building permit condition tracked in a conditions log with the evidence required to close it?
- Are all life-safety systems (fire alarm, sprinkler, smoke control, egress) installed, tested, commissioned, and documented before the final inspection is requested?
- Is the closeout package complete and internally consistent, with record drawings reconciled to every approved change?
- Is the final inspection sequence coordinated across authorities (building, fire, health, utility) with dependencies satisfied before each inspection?
- If a temporary CO is used, are its conditions, expiration, and closure plan documented and on track?
- Does the record drawing set reflect every approved deviation, substitution, and RFI-driven change?
- Is the building unoccupied until the CO (or a valid TCO covering the occupied area) is issued?
