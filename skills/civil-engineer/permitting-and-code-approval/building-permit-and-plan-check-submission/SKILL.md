# Building Permit And Plan Check Submission

Permitting is the regulatory gateway between a completed design and lawful construction, and it is governed by multiple independent authorities with overlapping jurisdictions, separate submittal requirements, and different review clocks. A building permit is rarely a single approval; it is usually a coordinated set of permits (grading, foundation-only, shell, full building, mechanical/electrical/plumbing trades, fire suppression, occupancy) issued by different departments on different timelines, some of which can run in parallel and some of which are sequential dependencies.

The harm this skill prevents is a permit package that is rejected for an avoidable intake defect, a design that fails plan check because it does not satisfy the specific code edition and amendments adopted by the AHJ, a critical-path approval that stalls construction because it was submitted in the wrong sequence, or an approval that is technically granted but carries conditions the project cannot meet. Engineers tend to treat permitting as administrative paperwork rather than engineering work, but the quality of the submittal directly determines schedule risk, cost risk, and legal exposure. Construction without a valid permit, or construction that deviates from the approved permit, creates stop-work orders, fines, mandatory removal of completed work, and difficulty obtaining a certificate of occupancy.

## Core Rules

### Identify Every Approval Authority And Its Governing Code Edition Early

Before assembling any package, enumerate every authority that must approve the project and the specific code edition each enforces. A single project can require approval from a zoning board, a building department, a fire marshal, a health department, a public works or utility authority, a historic preservation commission, a homeowners or architectural review board, and an environmental agency.

For each authority, confirm:

- which code edition and local amendments are currently adopted and enforced (the International Building Code edition, the state amendments, the local jurisdictional overlay);
- whether the authority accepts the IBC, the IRC, or a state-specific code;
- which professional seals are required and in which disciplines;
- the intake format, the submittal portal, and the fee schedule;
- the review cycle length and whether concurrent or sequential review is allowed.

Do not assume the latest published code is in force; many jurisdictions enforce an older edition with amendments, and designing to the wrong edition is a guaranteed plan-check failure.

### Match The Submittal Package To Each Authority's Intake Requirements

Each authority defines its own required contents, and a package that satisfies one will fail another. The building department typically requires sealed drawings, structural calculations, a soils report, an energy compliance path, and a project manual. The fire marshal may require a separate life-safety plan, a fire access route plan, and suppression calculations. The health department may require plumbing fixture counts and grease interceptor sizing.

Build a submittal matrix that maps each authority to its required documents, required seals, sheet sizes, digital format, and quantity. Verify intake requirements against the current published checklist from each authority, because checklists change. A package rejected at intake for a missing form or an unsealed sheet restarts the review clock from zero, which is the most common and most avoidable schedule loss.

### Resolve Zoning And Entitlement Before Detailed Design

Zoning and entitlement approvals (variances, special-use permits, site plan approval, historic review) govern what may be built and must be resolved before detailed structural and architectural design, because they set the building envelope, the height, the setbacks, the use, and the density. Designing a structure that violates an unresolved zoning constraint wastes the engineering effort and forces redesign.

Confirm that the approved zoning envelope, height limit, FAR, and use classification are reflected in the design before the structural and MEP coordination begins. If a variance is required, sequence it first, because variance denial can kill the project regardless of the engineering quality.

### Pre-Submit For Code Interpretation Where The Code Is Ambiguous

Where a code provision is ambiguous, where a proposed system is not explicitly addressed, or where an alternative material or method is proposed, request a formal code interpretation or an alternative-means-and-methods approval from the AHJ before finalizing the design. Building officials have the authority to approve alternatives that meet the intent of the code, but only if the request is documented with engineering justification.

A pre-submittal meeting with the plan examiner resolves interpretations before they become plan-check corrections. Document the meeting outcome in writing, because verbal interpretations are not binding and are frequently contradicted later. An alternative approved in writing during pre-submittal becomes part of the record and protects against a different examiner reversing it.

### Track Plan-Check Corrections And Resubmittals Disciplinarily

Plan-check corrections are normal, but each correction cycle adds weeks. Track every correction item in a log that records the comment, the responsible discipline, the proposed response, the drawing or calculation revision, and the resubmittal status. Respond to corrections with revised sheets and a written response keyed to each comment number, not with blanket statements.

Distinguish between corrections that require redesign (a structural framing change) and corrections that require documentation only (a missing note or calculation page). Redesign corrections must be checked for downstream effects on other disciplines before resubmittal, because a structural fix can trigger MEP coordination changes that create new corrections.

### Sequence Permits To The Critical Path Of Construction

Permit strategy should sequence approvals to the construction critical path. A foundation-only or early-start permit allows site work and foundations to begin while the full building permit is under review, saving weeks or months. A grading permit allows earthwork to begin. Trade permits (mechanical, electrical, plumbing, fire) are often pulled by the subcontractor after the shell permit is issued.

Identify which permits can be issued in parallel, which are dependencies, and which gate the start of construction. Coordinate the permit sequence with the construction schedule so that an approval is not on the critical path when it could have been parallelized. A full-building permit that takes twelve weeks should not gate foundation work that could have started under a four-week foundation permit.

### Verify Field Compliance Matches The Approved Permit

The approved permit drawings are the legal basis of construction, and field construction must match them. Any deviation, substitution, or RFIs that changes the approved design requires a documented change submitted to and approved by the AHJ before the change is built. Construction that deviates from the approved permit, even if the deviation is an improvement, is unpermitted work and can be ordered removed.

Maintain the approved set on site, log all approved changes, and ensure that the as-built record reflects every approved revision. The certificate of occupancy is issued against the approved set plus approved changes, and undocumented deviations block occupancy.

## Common Traps

### Designing To The Wrong Code Edition Or Without Local Amendments

Using the current published IBC when the AHJ enforces an older edition with state amendments produces a design that fails plan check on the load criteria, the seismic category, or the detailing. The trap is assuming the latest code governs; the fix is confirming the adopted edition and amendments before design.

### Treating Intake Rejection As A Minor Delay

An intake rejection for a missing form, an unsealed sheet, or the wrong digital format restarts the review clock entirely. The trap is treating intake as a formality; the harm is a full review cycle lost. The false signal is that the package was "submitted on time," when it was never accepted for review.

### Skipping The Pre-Submittal Meeting On A Nonstandard Design

A nonstandard structural system, a tall building, a performance-based design, or an alternative material is exactly the case where a pre-submittal meeting prevents a plan-check rejection. Skipping the meeting to save a week can cost a month when the examiner rejects the approach.

### Letting Zoning Issues Surface During Building Permit Review

Zoning and entitlement problems discovered during building permit review are the most expensive, because the structural and architectural design is already complete. The trap is assuming zoning is resolved when only a preliminary approval exists.

### Responding To Corrections Piecemeal

Submitting partial correction responses, or responding to some comments and deferring others, fragments the resubmittal and extends the cycle. Each resubmittal should be complete and internally consistent.

### Building Deviations Before The Change Is Approved

Constructing a field change before the AHJ approves the revised drawing creates unpermitted work. The false signal is that the RFI is internally approved; the harm is a stop-work order and possible removal.

## Self-Check

- Has every approval authority been identified, and is the governing code edition and local amendment confirmed for each?
- Does the submittal matrix map each authority to its required documents, seals, format, and intake checklist?
- Are zoning and entitlement approvals resolved and reflected in the design envelope before detailed engineering?
- Have ambiguous or alternative code provisions been resolved through a documented pre-submittal interpretation or meeting?
- Is every plan-check correction logged with a keyed written response and a revised sheet, with redesign corrections checked for cross-discipline effects?
- Is the permit sequence aligned to the construction critical path, with parallelizable permits identified and foundation or early-start permits considered?
- Are all field deviations documented and approved by the AHJ before construction, with the approved set maintained on site?
- Does the as-built record reflect every approved revision so that the certificate of occupancy is not blocked?
