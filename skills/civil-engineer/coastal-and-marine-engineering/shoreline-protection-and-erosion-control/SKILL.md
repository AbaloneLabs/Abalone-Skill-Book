# Shoreline Protection and Erosion Control

Shoreline protection and erosion control is the engineering of measures that stabilize an eroding coast, defend infrastructure from wave attack and storm surge, and where possible preserve or restore the natural sediment budget. It draws on the USACE Coastal Engineering Manual (EM 1110-2-1100), the CIRIA/CUR/CETMEF Rock Manual, FEMA coastal construction guidance, and state coastal management manuals, and it forces a fundamental choice between hard structures (revetments, seawalls, bulkheads, groins, breakwaters), soft or nature-based measures (beach nourishment, dune restoration, vegetation, living shorelines), and managed retreat. The harm this skill prevents is twofold and opposite in sign: a hard structure built without understanding the littoral drift that transfers erosion downdrift, leaving a seawall that protects one property while starving the next; or a soft solution chosen for environmental preference that is undersized and washes out in the first nor'easter, wasting public funds and exposing the assets it was meant to shield. The judgment is difficult because erosion is a sediment-budget problem, not a wave-force problem alone, and because the design horizon must span decades of sea level rise, storm climate, and reduced sediment supply. Agents must treat every recommendation as advisory to a licensed coastal engineer and must ground each choice in measured shoreline change, sediment transport direction and magnitude, and the project's tolerance for movement and repair.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose the Erosion Mechanism Before Choosing a Countermeasure

Before selecting any structure or nourishment, establish why the shoreline is eroding: a structural sediment deficit from a blocked littoral drift (an inlet, a harbor, or an updrift armoring), a cross-shore loss during storms that returns slowly or never, an increase in water level that drowns the beach profile, or human disruption such as dredging or inlet stabilization. Use historical shoreline change maps, lidar or profile surveys, sediment grain size analysis, and wave hindcasts to estimate the net littoral transport rate and direction, because the countermeasure must address the dominant loss mechanism or it will fail. A revetment stops land loss but does not create beach; a groin traps sand updrift and starves downdrift unless filled; nourishment adds volume but is lost at the background erosion rate plus storm losses. Misdiagnosing the mechanism is the most expensive error in coastal engineering, because it produces a structure that is correctly built for the wrong problem and accelerates the very loss it was meant to stop.

### Match the Countermeasure Type to the Setting and the Objective

Select the countermeasure from the setting and the objective together. Revetments and seawalls defend a fixed shoreline where landward infrastructure cannot move and where a beach is secondary; they are designed for wave runup, overtopping, toe scour, and foundation stability, and they reflect wave energy that can steepen the fronting beach. Bulkheads and retaining walls hold earth against quiet water and are not wave defenses; using a bulkhead where waves act invites failure. Groins and detached breakwaters interrupt or reduce littoral transport to retain a beach, but they must be sized for the transport rate and filled at construction to avoid downdrift starvation. Beach nourishment and dune restoration add sediment to the system and are the only measures that widen the beach itself, but they require compatible borrow sand, a renourishment plan, and realistic estimates of losses. Living shorelines (marsh sills, oyster reefs, hybrid treatments) suit low-to-moderate energy settings and provide habitat and wave attenuation, but they are not substitutes for hard armor in high-energy reaches. Do not default to the hardest structure or the softest preference; match the measure to the wave climate, the sediment budget, and the ecological objective.

### Size Hard Structures for Wave Loading, Runup, Scour, and Toe Stability

For revetments and seawalls, design the armor layer for the design wave at the toe using the Hudson or Van der Meer equations, set the crest elevation from wave runup and overtopping limits (EuroTop or CEM), and provide an underlayer and filter or geotextile that prevents core and backfill loss. Critically, design the toe for scour: every vertical wall and steep slope intensifies near-bed velocities and lowers the seabed, so predict the scour depth from CEM or local guidance and extend the toe or apron below it, because toe undermining is the dominant failure mode for seawalls and revetments. Check global stability of the wall or slope (sliding, overturning, bearing, and deep-seated circular failure) under the design water level and rapid drawdown, because coastal structures fail in geotechnical modes as often as in hydraulic modes. Account for wave-induced pore pressures and uplift on the structure, and detail drainage through the wall or revetment so that retained water cannot build up behind it and blow out the armor when the tide recedes.

### Design Beach Nourishment and Dunes with Compatible Sand and a Realistic Loss Plan

For nourishment, select borrow sand that is compatible with the native beach in grain size and gradation, because finer borrow sand equilibrates to a steeper profile and is lost faster, and overfill ratios must account for the difference. Design the nourishment profile (berm width, elevation, and advanced fill or profile equilibration) using equilibrium profile concepts, and estimate the project life from the background erosion rate, the storm losses, and the renourishment interval, because a nourished beach is a consumable asset, not a permanent fix. For dunes, size the crest elevation and width to withstand the design storm surge and wave runup with a residual freeboard, provide a planted and fenced vegetation cover to build aeolian stability, and protect the dune toe where necessary, because a dune breached in a storm loses its protective function for the developed coast behind it. Document the renourishment volume, interval, and monitoring plan, because presenting nourishment as a one-time construction hides the lifecycle cost and sets up a funding failure.

### Incorporate Sea Level Rise, Sediment Continuity, and Downdrift Impacts

Coastal protection must be designed for the sea level rise projected over the project life, because rising water levels deepen the nearshore, raise wave runup and overtopping, drown marsh and dune vegetation, and accelerate erosion of both natural and nourished beaches. Adjust the design water level, structure crest, and nourishment volume for the relative sea level rise scenario appropriate to the project's risk class and planning horizon, and check whether the structure or beach remains functional at mid-century and end-of-century conditions. For any structure that intercepts littoral drift (groins, breakwaters, inlet jetties), perform a sediment continuity analysis and provide bypassing, initial fill, or downdrift nourishment to offset the starvation the structure causes, because transferring erosion to a neighboring reach is a foreseeable harm that creates liability and recurring conflict. Recognize that hard structures fronting a rising sea progressively lose the beach in front of them, and that a long-term strategy (nourishment, retreat, or elevation) is usually required alongside the structure.

## Common Traps

### Armoring Without Diagnosing the Sediment Deficit

A seawall or revetment is built to stop land loss, but the cause is a blocked updrift sediment supply, so the structure reflects wave energy, steepens the fronting beach, and accelerates the loss of any remaining sand. The false signal is that land loss stops at the wall; the harm is that the beach disappears and downdrift erosion worsens, producing a defended bluff with no beach and escalating neighbor conflicts.

### The Groin That Starves the Downdrift Shore

A groin field is built to widen the beach updrift without initial fill or bypassing, so it traps sand that would otherwise feed the downdrift reach. The mechanism is that littoral drift is a conveyor belt, so the false signal of a wider updrift beach hides accelerated downdrift erosion; the harm is that the protected property gains sand while the downdrift owner loses it, often triggering litigation and removal orders.

### Nourishment with Incompatible Borrow Sand

Borrow sand finer than the native beach is placed because it is cheap and available, so the fill equilibrates to a steeper, narrower profile and erodes far faster than predicted. The mechanism is that fine sand is mobilized at lower wave energy, so the false signal of a wide constructed beach hides a short project life; the harm is a renourishment cycle that drains the budget and a public beach that disappears before the next funding round.

### The Living Shoreline Placed in a High-Energy Reach

A marsh sill or oyster reef is specified in a setting exposed to storm waves beyond its design range, on the assumption that nature-based measures are universally safer. The mechanism is that the measure is sized for aesthetics rather than wave climate, so the false signal of an environmentally preferred design hides an under-capacity; the harm is that the installation is destroyed in the first design storm and the bank it protected erodes as if untreated.

## Self-Check

- Has the erosion mechanism been diagnosed from shoreline change data, surveys, grain size, and a littoral transport estimate, rather than assumed to be a simple wave-attack problem?
- Is the countermeasure type matched to the wave climate, sediment budget, infrastructure constraint, and ecological objective, rather than defaulted to the hardest or softest option?
- For revetments and seawalls, are the armor sized for the design wave, the crest set from runup and overtopping, the toe extended below predicted scour, and global and drawdown stability checked?
- For nourishment, is the borrow sand compatible with the native gradation, the profile designed with equilibration, and the renourishment volume, interval, and monitoring plan documented?
- For dunes, is the crest elevation and width sufficient for the design surge and runup with residual freeboard, with vegetation and toe protection specified?
- Has the design water level, runup, and nourishment volume been adjusted for projected relative sea level rise over the project life, with functionality checked at mid- and end-of-life conditions?
- For any structure intercepting littoral drift, is a sediment continuity analysis performed with bypassing, initial fill, or downdrift nourishment to offset starvation?
