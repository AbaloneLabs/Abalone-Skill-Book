# Wave and Tidal Load Analysis

Wave and tidal load analysis is the hydrodynamic basis for every coastal and marine structure, defining the forces, water levels, and runup that a breakwater, seawall, pile-supported wharf, offshore platform, or bridge crossing must survive. It is governed by the USACE Coastal Engineering Manual (EM 1110-2-1100), the Shore Protection Manual lineage, Goda wave pressure methods, and standards such as API RP 2A for offshore and AASHTO for coastal bridges, and it requires the engineer to move correctly between deep-water, intermediate, and shallow-water wave theory, between deterministic design waves and spectral sea states, and between tidal datums and design still water levels that include storm surge. The harm this skill prevents is a structure designed for forces that do not represent the real sea: a wave height taken as a maximum instead of significant height and then mis-factored, a force computed with deep-water theory in a depth where the wave has broken, a pile load that ignores wave-current interaction, or a tidal datum confused with a storm-surge-inclusive design level that under-sets a deck elevation. Because wave forces scale with the square or higher of wave height and can change by an order of magnitude across the breaking transition, a single misapplied theory or datum can produce a structure that fails in its design storm or, conversely, one that is grotesquely overbuilt. Agents must treat this work as decision support for a licensed coastal or structural engineer and must never finalize a design wave, force, or water level without documenting the data source, the transformation path, and the theory applied.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Design Wave Through Data, Return Period, and Transformation

The design wave is not a single number picked from a map; it is the product of a met-ocean dataset (measured buoy records or validated hindcast), an extreme value analysis at a chosen return period, and a transformation from deep water to the point of interest. Begin with the significant wave height Hs and the spectral peak period Tp at the offshore point, fit an extreme value distribution (Gumbel, Weibill, or peaks-over-threshold) to derive the design return-period wave, and confirm the wave steepness and period are physically consistent, because an Hs without its companion Tp is unusable for runup, breaking, and force calculations. Transform the deep-water wave to the site by applying refraction (depth-induced change in direction that concentrates or spreads energy), shoaling (depth-induced change in height), and where relevant diffraction around headlands and structures and wave setup shoreward of breaking. Identify whether the governing wave at the structure is breaking or non-breaking, because a depth-limited breaking wave at the toe during storm surge is often the controlling case and requires the maximum breaking height Hmax roughly 0.78 times the local depth, not the offshore Hs.

### Apply the Correct Wave Theory for the Water Depth and Steepness

Wave theory selection determines the kinematics and pressure field used in force calculations, and the wrong theory produces the wrong force. Use the Ursell number, the wave steepness, and the relative depth (h/L) to choose among linear (Airy) theory for deep and intermediate water with small amplitude, Stokes higher-order theory for steeper waves in intermediate depth, cnoidal or stream-function theory for long waves in shallow water, and solitary wave theory as an approximation for very shallow breaking waves. For force calculations on slender piles, use the Morison equation, which separates the inline force into an inertia term (proportional to fluid acceleration and valid when the pile is small relative to wavelength) and a drag term (proportional to velocity squared and dominant in shallow or breaking waves), and confirm the Keulegan-Carpenter number to know which term governs. For large bodies where the diameter is a significant fraction of the wavelength, use diffraction theory rather than Morison, because the structure modifies the wave field and inertia dominates. Never apply deep-water wave kinematics to a shallow-water force calculation, because the velocity and acceleration profiles differ fundamentally across the regimes.

### Compute Forces on Walls, Decks, and Piles with the Right Method and Load Case

For vertical walls and breakwaters, use the Goda method or the CEM pressure distributions, which give the impulsive and pulsating wave pressure profile on the wall as a function of the design wave, water depth, and crest elevation, and check both the overall sliding and overturning stability and the local bending of wall elements. For breaking wave impact on walls and decks, recognize that the impulsive pressure can be an order of magnitude larger than the pulsating pressure and acts over a very short duration, so quasi-static design under-predicts the peak; where breaking is possible, apply breaking-impact factors or explicit impact pressure methods. For pile-supported wharves, piers, and offshore structures, compute wave forces on individual piles with Morison, integrate over the pile to the wave crest, account for wave-current interaction (which changes both the kinematics and the effective Reynolds and Keulegan-Carpenter numbers), and combine inline and transverse (lift) forces. For coastal bridge decks vulnerable to wave uplift and slamming, apply the AASHTO coastal bridge vulnerability methods or the CEM deck-loading guidance, because wave-induced uplift on a low deck is the dominant failure mode in surge events and is missed by gravity-only design.

### Set Tide Datums, Storm Surge, and Design Still Water Levels Correctly

The water level is as consequential as the wave height, because it sets the depth that limits breaking, the lever arm for forces, and the runup and overtopping that determine crest elevation. Establish the tidal datum on a consistent vertical reference (mean lower low water, mean sea level, NAVD88, or local chart datum) and never mix datums across inputs, because a one-foot datum error shifts every subsequent calculation. Derive the design still water level by combining the astronomical tide (typically mean high water springs or the high tide coincident with the storm) with the storm surge from the design event, plus wave setup where the structure is inside or near the surf zone, and consider relative sea level rise over the project life. For freeboard and deck elevation, evaluate the total water level (surge plus setup plus wave runup) against the structure crest or deck soffit, and confirm that a low-lying deck or roadway is not within the wave-impact zone during the design event, because decks set by astronomical tide alone are routinely destroyed in surges.

### Combine Waves, Currents, Wind, and Water Level in the Governing Load Case

The design load case is rarely a single wave at mean water level; it is the joint occurrence of the design wave, the storm-surge water level, the design current, and wind setup that produces the worst combination of force, runup, and stability. Define load cases that envelope the realistic joint probabilities: the maximum wave with the surge water level for armor and wall stability, the maximum water level for overtopping and deck uplift, the breaking wave at the toe for impulsive wall loading, and the rapid drawdown case for landside slope and wall stability where the surge recedes faster than pore water drains. Check each load case for each failure mode (sliding, overturning, bearing, scour, uplift, runup), because the governing case differs by mode and a single envelope case hides the real critical combination. Document the load cases, the joint occurrence assumptions, and the partial safety factors applied, so that the force basis can be audited and re-evaluated as climate and sea level conditions evolve.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Significant and Maximum Wave Height

The significant wave height Hs is used where the maximum wave height Hmax (roughly 1.8 to 2.0 times Hs in a storm) is required, or vice versa, so the force is computed for the wrong statistical level. The mechanism is that Hs and Hmax differ by a factor approaching two, so the false signal of a documented wave height hides a force that differs by a factor of four or more; the harm is an under-strength or wildly overbuilt element.

### Applying Deep-Water Theory in the Breaking Zone

Wave kinematics and pressures are computed with deep-water linear theory at a depth where the wave has already broken and is depth-limited, so the predicted orbital velocities and forces are wrong. The mechanism is that breaking fundamentally changes the velocity profile and introduces impulsive impact, so the false signal of a tidy linear analysis hides a force regime the theory cannot represent; the harm is a wall or pile designed for half the real loading.

### Mixing Tidal Datums Across Inputs

The wave height is referenced to mean sea level, the structure elevation to NAVD88, and the surge to a local chart datum, with no common conversion, so every elevation is offset by the datum difference. The mechanism is that the arithmetic looks consistent but the vertical origins disagree, so the false signal of a complete water-level analysis hides a one- to two-foot error; the harm is a crest or deck set too low and overtopped or uplifted in the design event.

### Ignoring Wave-Current Interaction on Piles and Walls

The design wave is combined with the design current by simple superposition, ignoring the change in wave kinematics and the modified drag and inertia coefficients. The mechanism is that an opposing current steepens and slows the wave and raises the force, so the false signal of an added current velocity hides a non-linear interaction; the harm is a pile or wall that experiences forces well above the superposed estimate, often at the breaking transition.

## Self-Check

- Is the design wave derived from measured or hindcast data by extreme value analysis at a documented return period, with both Hs and the companion Tp, and transformed to the site by refraction, shoaling, diffraction, and breaking?
- Is the wave at the structure classified as breaking or non-breaking, and where breaking governs, is the depth-limited maximum wave height used rather than the offshore significant height?
- Is the wave theory (Airy, Stokes, cnoidal, stream-function, or solitary) selected from the Ursell number, steepness, and relative depth, with Morison used for slender piles and diffraction theory for large bodies?
- For vertical walls, are Goda or CEM pressure distributions used with explicit treatment of breaking-wave impulsive loading, and are sliding, overturning, and local bending all checked?
- Are all water levels, wave heights, and structure elevations on a single documented vertical datum, with the design still water level combining astronomical tide, storm surge, wave setup, and relative sea level rise?
- Are the governing load cases defined as joint occurrences of wave, water level, current, and wind, with each failure mode checked against its critical combination rather than a single envelope case?
- For coastal bridge decks and low-crested structures, is wave-induced uplift and slamming evaluated under AASHTO or CEM deck-loading guidance, not gravity-only design?
