# Tunnel Lining and Waterproofing Design

Tunnel lining and waterproofing design is the engineering of the permanent enclosure of a tunnel: the structural lining that carries the long-term ground and water loads and the waterproofing system that keeps groundwater out. The lining may be a single-pass precast concrete segmental ring installed directly behind a TBM, a double-shell system with an initial shotcrete lining, a waterproofing membrane, and a cast-in-place final lining, or a composite lining. The core judgment problem is that the lining and waterproofing are interdependent: a fully tanked membrane imposes full hydrostatic pressure on the lining, the segment joints and gaskets govern watertightness in single-pass systems, and the lining must resist not only ground load but water pressure, fire, and the chemical environment. The harm this skill prevents is a leaking tunnel that floods or corrodes, a lining that cracks under water pressure or thermal fire loads, and a waterproofing system that fails at joints or details and is essentially impossible to repair once the tunnel is in service. Underground structures are difficult and costly to access after construction, so the lining and waterproofing must be right the first time; agents support the licensed engineer and must defer final lining and waterproofing decisions to that team.

## Core Rules

### Select the Lining System for the Method, Ground, and Service Requirements

Choose between single-pass segmental lining (efficient for TBM drives, with the segments acting as both initial and final lining, designed for the full ground and water load), double-shell lining (initial shotcrete support, membrane, and cast-in-place final lining, favored for mined tunnels and where a dry, high-quality interior finish is required), and composite or steel-concrete linings for special loads. The choice depends on the excavation method, the ground and water conditions, the required service life and watertightness, the fire and crash loads, and the aesthetics and maintenance access. A single-pass segmental lining demands high segment quality, precise geometry, and reliable gaskets because it is the final product; a double-shell lining allows tolerance and a cleaner final surface but adds thickness and cost. Match the system to the method and the service requirement, and do not default to one system across a tunnel with varying conditions.

### Design the Lining for Ground, Water, and Long-Term Loads

The final lining must be designed for the ground load (which may increase over time as the ground relaxes onto the lining, especially in rock where the initial support may shed load to the final lining over years), the full hydrostatic water pressure if the tunnel is tanked, the long-term effects of creep and shrinkage in concrete, and the thermal and structural effects of fire. For segmental linings, design the ring for the ground and water loads, check the segment capacity under the thrust and bending, design the circumferential and longitudinal joints (including the bolted or dowelled connections and the joint opening under load), and account for the ovalization and the effects of the TBM shield tail and grouting pressure. For cast-in-place linings, design for the ground and water loads on the completed ring, account for shrinkage and thermal cracking, and detail construction and expansion joints. A lining designed for ground load alone, ignoring full water pressure on a tanked tunnel, is under-designed and will crack or leak.

### Engineer the Waterproofing Strategy for the Required Watertightness

Waterproofing strategy ranges from fully tanked (a continuous membrane or sheet that excludes all groundwater, used where a bone-dry tunnel is required, such as for transit or electrical facilities) to partially tanked (membrane only in the crown or at specific zones) to drainable (the lining drains groundwater to a collection system, used in rock tunnels where the water inflow is manageable). For a fully tanked cast-in-place system, use a high-quality sheet membrane (PVC, HDPE, or spray-applied membrane) with welded seams, continuous detailing at joints, penetrations, and the invert, and a drainage layer to relieve any water that bypasses it. For segmental linings, watertness is governed by the segment concrete quality (low permeability, adequate cover, crack control) and the gasketed joints, with ethylene-propylene or hydrophilic gaskets designed for the joint gap and water pressure. Specify the watertightness class explicitly and detail every joint, penetration, and transition, because waterproofing fails at the details, not in the field of the membrane.

### Control Crack Width, Permeability, and Durability

Concrete lining durability is governed by crack width and permeability, because cracks are the primary path for water and chloride to reach reinforcement. Limit crack width per the applicable code (ACI 318 or the tunnel-specific criteria, commonly 0.2 to 0.3 mm for the exposure class), using adequate reinforcement, low heat-of-hydration mix designs, contraction joints, and curing. Specify low-permeability concrete with appropriate cementitious content, water-cement ratio, and admixtures, and provide the cover required for the underground exposure and fire exposure. For segmental linings, control handling and installation cracks through segment design, reinforcement, and careful erection, because cracked segments are the final lining and cannot be easily repaired. A lining with uncontrolled cracking will leak and deteriorate regardless of the waterproofing, so crack control is a structural and durability requirement, not a finishing detail.

### Design for Groundwater Pressure and Buoyancy

A tanked tunnel below the water table must resist the full hydrostatic pressure, and the design water head must be based on the credible long-term groundwater level, not the level at the time of construction (which may rise after the tunnel interrupts drainage). Check the lining for the external water pressure, check the invert and crown for the differential, and check the whole tunnel for buoyancy (uplift) during construction and in service, because an empty or partially empty tunnel below the water table can float if the uplift exceeds the weight and the anchorage. Provide ballast, tension piles, or reliance on ground shear to resist uplift where required. Ignoring the long-term water level or buoyancy has caused tunnel failures, so both are explicit design checks.

### Provide Fireproofing and Fire Life Safety for the Lining

Tunnel fires (especially fuel-fed vehicle or tanker fires) can spall and weaken concrete linings, and the lining must be protected to preserve structural capacity and life safety during and after a fire. Provide fireproofing proportioned to the design fire curve (such as the RWS or HCM hydrocarbon fire curves), using either passive fire protection (boards or spray-applied cementitious or intumescent coatings) or polypropylene fibers in the concrete mix to prevent explosive spalling. Coordinate the fireproofing with the waterproofing (a membrane that melts or burns can compromise both watertightness and fire protection), and detail the lining to survive the design fire without collapse. Fireproofing is a life-safety and structural requirement, not an optional finish, and its interaction with waterproofing must be explicitly resolved.

## Common Traps

### Lining Designed Without Full Water Pressure

A tanked tunnel lining is designed for ground load but not the full hydrostatic pressure, on the assumption that the membrane carries the water, so the lining cracks and leaks. The false signal is a lining that works in the dry; the harm is cracking and leakage once the full water head acts. Always apply full water pressure to a tanked lining.

### Waterproofing Fails at Details and Joints

The membrane field is specified correctly, but the seams, penetrations, invert transitions, and segment gaskets are poorly detailed, so water enters at the details. The false signal is a specified high-quality membrane; the harm is a leaking tunnel that cannot be repaired in service. Detail every joint and penetration and verify the watertightness class.

### Crack Width and Permeability Uncontrolled

The lining concrete is placed without crack-width control or low-permeability mix design, so cracks open and admit water and chloride, defeating the waterproofing. The false signal is a sound-looking lining; the harm is leakage and reinforcement corrosion over the service life. Limit crack width and specify low-permeability concrete.

### Fire and Waterproofing Interaction Ignored

Fireproofing and waterproofing are specified independently, and a membrane that degrades in fire compromises both systems, or the fireproofing detaches from a membrane-backed surface. The false signal is both systems specified; the harm is lining failure in a fire. Coordinate the two systems and detail their interaction.

## Self-Check

- Is the lining system (single-pass segmental, double-shell, composite) matched to the method, ground, water, and service requirements, with the basis documented?
- Is the final lining designed for ground load, full hydrostatic water pressure for a tanked tunnel, long-term creep and shrinkage, and thermal fire effects?
- Is the waterproofing strategy (fully tanked, partially tanked, drainable) matched to the required watertightness class, with all joints, penetrations, and transitions detailed?
- Are crack width (per ACI 318 or tunnel criteria), concrete permeability, cover, and mix design specified to control leakage and durability?
- Is the design water head based on the credible long-term groundwater level, and is the tunnel checked for buoyancy during construction and in service?
- Is fireproofing provided for the design fire curve (RWS/HCM), using passive protection or polypropylene fibers, and coordinated with the waterproofing?
- Are segment joints and gaskets (for segmental lining) or membrane seams and invert detailing (for cast-in-place) designed for the joint gap and water pressure?
