# Gravity Sewer and Pipe Hydraulics

Gravity sewer and pipe hydraulics is the engineering of a buried conduit that carries wastewater or drainage by gravity, where the slope and the diameter must produce a velocity high enough to resuspend and transport the solids at the low flow and a capacity high enough to pass the peak wet-weather flow without surcharging. A gravity sewer is self-cleaning only if the velocity at the minimum flow scours the deposited solids, and it is adequate only if it passes the peak flow including the infiltration and inflow that enter through joints, manholes, and illegal connections. The harm this skill prevents is a sewer laid at a slope that meets the minimum criteria on paper but deposits solids at the actual low flow, a main sized for the dry-weather peak that surcharges into basements at the wet-weather peak, or a lift station whose wet well cycles the pump to failure or whose firm capacity cannot pass the peak. Because sewer backups are a public-health and property hazard and the system is buried and hard to access, the design must be defensible against the low-flow scour and the wet-weather peak over the design life.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Flow, Peaking, and Infiltration and Inflow

The sewer design begins with the average dry-weather flow from the contributing population and land use, projected over the design life, and the peaking factor that converts the average to the peak dry-weather flow, applied with recognised criteria that reflect the service population because small systems peak far higher than large ones. Add the infiltration and inflow allowance, the groundwater and rainwater that enter the system through pipe joints, manhole covers, and illegal connections, sized per the local criteria (commonly a flow per unit length of pipe per day or a fraction of the peak dry-weather flow), because the I and I often dominates the wet-weather peak and governs the sizing. The design flow is the peak wet-weather flow, the dry-weather peak plus the I and I, and the minimum flow is the nighttime dry-weather flow that governs the scour. A sewer sized for the dry-weather peak alone surcharges at the first wet event, because the I and I was never included.

### Set the Slope, Diameter, and Manning n for Capacity and Self-Cleaning

Set the sewer slope and diameter so that the pipe passes the design peak flow at the accepted depth ratio (commonly half-full for sanitary sewers up to d/D of 0.7 to 0.8) and so that the velocity at the design flow meets the minimum self-cleaning criterion. Compute the capacity and velocity by Manning's equation with the n appropriate to the material and age (commonly 0.013 for PVC and HDPE, 0.013 to 0.015 for concrete and older pipe), because the roughness governs the capacity and the wrong n overstates it. Confirm that the minimum velocity at the minimum or average flow meets the scour criterion (commonly 0.6 m/s), because a sewer that meets the velocity at the peak but deposits solids at the low flow clogs over time. Where the slope cannot achieve the scour velocity at the low flow, provide a steeper initial slope, an oversized pipe with a deflected flow line, or a flushing program, because the deposition is a maintenance burden the design created. Confirm the maximum velocity does not erode the pipe or the manhole, providing drop manholes or energy dissipation where the velocity is excessive.

### Locate and Space Manholes for Access and Hydraulics

Locate manholes at every change in direction, slope, diameter, or pipe material, at every pipe junction, and at intervals that allow access for inspection and cleaning, commonly 90 to 150 m for smaller sewers and longer for larger trunk sewers. Size the manhole for the connecting pipes and the channel, and design the bench and the channel so that the flow passes without turbulence or a hydraulic jump that would back up the upstream line. Confirm the drop across the manhole accounts for the entrance and exit losses and the change in velocity, providing an additional drop where the downstream velocity is lower, because a manhole without the energy loss allowance raises the upstream hydraulic grade and risks surcharge. Provide a watertight frame and cover, and where the manhole is in a flood-prone area, a sealed cover to prevent inflow, because manhole inflow is a major I and I source.

### Control Infiltration and Inflow at the Source

Design the sewer to minimise infiltration and inflow over its life by specifying watertight pipe joints (gasketed PVC, HDPE, or concrete pipe with the appropriate joint), watertight manhole construction with sealed frames and covers, and low-pressure or grouted testing of the installed line to verify the exfiltration or infiltration is within the limit. Identify and address the inflow sources: direct connections of downspouts, sump pumps, and area drains that are illegal in most sanitary systems, and manhole covers in flood-prone areas that admit surface water. A new sewer built with watertight joints and tested still admits I and I over time as joints age and the system is connected to, so the design flow must include the allowance and the inspection and rehabilitation program must be planned. A sewer designed without I and I control and allowance surcharges within years of construction.

### Size Lift Stations for Firm Capacity, Wet-Well Cycling, and Redundancy

Where the gravity flow cannot be maintained, size the lift station to pump the peak wet-weather flow with the firm capacity, the capacity with the largest pump out of service, so that the station continues to operate during a pump failure. Size the wet well for the active volume that produces a pump cycle time within the motor manufacturer limit (commonly a minimum 5 to 10 minutes between starts for constant-speed pumps) to avoid motor damage, and small enough to prevent septicity from long residence. Select the pump for the actual system head curve, with the operating point at or near the best efficiency point across the range of wet-well levels, and provide variable-speed or staged pumping where the flow varies widely. Provide standby power, a backup level control, an alarm, and a bypass connection so that the station operates through a power or instrument failure, because a lift station failure backs up the collection system and can flood upstream basements.

## Common Traps

### The Sewer Sized for Dry-Weather Peak

The sewer is sized for the peak dry-weather flow, and the infiltration and inflow allowance is omitted or understated. The trap is that the pipe passes the dry-weather peak, while the wet-weather peak including the I and I is far higher and the sewer surcharges, backing up into upstream services and manholes. The false signal is the adequate dry-weather capacity; the harm is basement and street surcharge during wet weather, traced to the I and I that was never included in the design flow.

### The Velocity Met at Peak but Not at Low Flow

The sewer slope meets the minimum self-cleaning velocity at the design peak flow, but the velocity at the minimum or average flow is below the scour criterion. The trap is that the sewer passes the peak cleanly, while at the low nighttime flow the velocity is too low to resuspend the solids, the solids deposit, and the line clogs or generates odor and corrosive sulfide over time. The false signal is the compliant peak velocity; the harm is a sewer that requires frequent cleaning and corrodes because the low-flow scour was never verified.

### The Manning n for New Smooth Pipe

The capacity is computed with the new-pipe n for PVC, and the sewer is sized on that basis. The trap is that the capacity is correct at installation, while the pipe accumulates slime, grease, and deposits that raise the effective n over the design life, reducing the capacity and raising the hydraulic grade. The false signal is the adequate new-pipe capacity; the harm is a sewer that surcharges earlier than designed because the aged roughness was never considered.

### The Lift Station Without Firm Capacity or Wet-Well Sizing

The lift station pumps are selected for the peak flow with all pumps running, and the wet well is sized for storage without checking the pump cycle time. The trap is that the station passes the peak on paper, while with the largest pump out the firm capacity is below the peak and the station overtops, and the undersized wet well cycles the pump to motor failure. The false signal is the rated station capacity; the harm is a station that fails service during a pump outage and burns out pumps from short cycling.

## Self-Check

- Is the design flow the peak wet-weather flow including the infiltration and inflow allowance, and is the minimum dry-weather flow established for the scour check?
- Is the slope and diameter set so the pipe passes the peak flow at the accepted depth ratio and meets the minimum self-cleaning velocity at the low flow, with Manning n representing the aged condition?
- Are manholes located at every change in direction, slope, diameter, and junction, spaced for access, with the bench, channel, and drop accounting for the energy losses?
- Are watertight joints, manhole construction, and line testing specified to control infiltration, and are inflow sources such as downspouts and flood-prone covers addressed?
- Does the lift station provide firm capacity with the largest pump out, a wet well sized for the pump cycle time and to avoid septicity, and a pump selected for the actual system curve?
- Are standby power, backup level control, alarm, and bypass provided so the station operates through a power or instrument failure?
