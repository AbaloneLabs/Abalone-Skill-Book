# Bridge Inspection and NBI Condition Rating

Bridge inspection is the systematic field evaluation of an existing bridge to detect deterioration, damage, and capacity-reducing conditions, and to record the results in the National Bridge Inventory through condition and appraisal ratings. It is mandated and standardized by the National Bridge Inspection Standards (23 CFR 650 Subpart C), implemented through the FHWA Specifications for the National Bridge Inventory (SNBI), and executed using the AASHTO Manual for Bridge Element Inspection (MBEI) for element-level condition states. The harm this skill prevents is a bridge that is structurally deficient or scour-critical going undetected because the inspection was too infrequent, too shallow, or performed by unqualified staff, leading to sudden collapse or emergency closure; and the opposite harm of an over-conservative rating that triggers needless posting and replacement. Inspection findings drive load ratings, posting decisions, and capital programming, so a misread condition state or a missed fracture-critical member propagates into wrong engineering and wrong spending. This is public-safety work performed under the authority of a licensed program manager; agents support that work and must defer final condition ratings and safety-flag decisions to qualified inspection team leaders.

## Core Rules

### Set Inspection Type, Frequency, and Scope per the NBIS

The NBIS requires a routine inspection at intervals not exceeding 24 months for most bridges, with longer intervals (up to 48 months) only where documented criteria and FHWA approval allow. Beyond routine inspection, establish the need for and frequency of fracture critical member inspections (hands-on, typically 24-month), underwater inspections (for substructures below water, at intervals not exceeding 60 months), and in-depth inspections for complex or damage events. Determine scope from the previous inspection report, the bridge file, and the structure type: a steel bridge with welded tension details needs close fatigue and fracture-critical attention; a water crossing needs scour and channel assessment; a prestressed bridge needs end-region and strand corrosion checks. Setting the wrong interval or omitting a required inspection type is a regulatory violation and a safety gap, so map every bridge to its required inspection suite before fieldwork begins.

### Staff Inspections with Qualified Personnel

The NBIS defines qualifications: the program manager (a licensed professional engineer with bridge inspection experience), the team leader (a licensed PE, or an individual with the specified education, training, and certification such as FHWA-approved NHI courses), and qualified inspectors for underwater (diver certification and team leader oversight) and fracture critical work. The team composition must match the bridge complexity, and every inspection must be led by a qualified team leader. An inspection conducted by unqualified staff is not a valid inspection under the NBIS, and its ratings cannot be relied upon, so verify credentials and team composition before deploying.

### Conduct Element-Level Inspection per the AASHTO MBEI

Element-level inspection records the quantity of each bridge element (decks, girders, pier caps, abutments, bearings, culverts) in each of four condition states, from good to severe, using the MBEI element definitions. Condition states are defined by specific, observable distress thresholds (crack width, delamination, section loss, corrosion, scour exposure), not by impression, and they must be quantified by field measurement. Supplement the national elements with agency-defined elements where local practice requires, and tie the element quantities to the photographs and notes so the record is auditable. Element-level data feeds bridge management systems and load ratings, so inaccurate or impressionistic condition states corrupt every downstream decision.

### Assign NBI Condition Ratings Consistently and Defensibly

NBI condition ratings (0 through 9 for the deck, superstructure, and substructure, and channel and culvert ratings where applicable) summarize the condition of the major components, and they must be assigned per the SNBI definitions and consistent with the element-level data. The ratings reflect the worst condition state present in significant quantity, not the average: a 4 (poor) means advanced section loss, deterioration, or spalling, and a 3 (serious) means deterioration affecting primary components. Document the specific distress that justifies each rating with photographs and measurements, and ensure the NBI ratings are reconciled with the element condition states so the two records agree. A rating drift upward, to avoid triggering management action, is the classic inspection failure and must be resisted.

### Identify Fracture Critical, Fatigue, and Scour Conditions

During inspection, actively look for the conditions that drive sudden failure: fracture critical members in tension (where failure of a single non-redundant member could cause collapse), fatigue-prone welded details (especially on older steel bridges and at cover plate terminations), and scour at piers and abutments (evidence of channel degradation, local scour holes, or undermined footings). Record fracture critical findings with hands-on visual and tactile inspection, document fatigue cracks with location and length, and assess scour using channel cross-sections, probe and sonar data, and comparison to prior inspections. Flag any condition that warrants immediate action (a crack in a fracture critical member, a scour hole undermining a footing) so the owner can impose emergency restriction. Missing a fracture critical or scour condition is the failure mode that kills people, so these checks are never optional.

### Produce a Defensible, Actionable Report

The inspection report must include the inspection type and date, team and qualifications, element-level quantities and condition states, NBI condition and appraisal ratings, photographs with locations and descriptions, identified deficiencies, and recommended actions with priorities and timeframes. Recommendations must distinguish maintenance, repair, and follow-up engineering (such as a load rating update or a scour evaluation), and must flag anything requiring immediate action. The report is the legal record and the input to load rating and programming, so it must be complete, consistent, and traceable from field observation to rating.

## Common Traps

### Rating Drift Toward the Favorable

Over successive inspections, ratings creep upward because the inspector compares to the prior report rather than to the SNBI definitions, masking real deterioration. The false signal is a stable rating; the harm is a bridge that crosses from fair to poor to serious without ever triggering action. Anchor every rating to the written definition and the measured distress, not to last year's number.

### Impressionistic Condition States

Element condition states are assigned by eye without measuring crack widths, delamination areas, or section loss, so the quantities are unreliable. The false signal is a complete element table; the harm is a bridge management system and load rating built on unmeasured data. Require field measurement and photograph the distress that defines each state.

### Omitting Required Special Inspections

A water crossing gets a routine inspection but no underwater inspection, or a steel bridge with fracture critical members gets no hands-on fracture critical inspection, because the special inspection was not scheduled. The false signal is a current routine inspection on file; the harm is a scour or fracture critical failure that the routine inspection could not have detected. Map every bridge to its full required inspection suite and track each one.

### Unqualified or Understaffed Teams

An inspection is signed by a team leader who lacks the NBIS qualifications, or a complex bridge is inspected by a single inspector without the needed expertise. The false signal is a completed and signed report; the harm is an invalid inspection whose ratings cannot be trusted. Verify program manager and team leader qualifications before each inspection.

## Self-Check

- Is the inspection type and interval set per the NBIS (24-month routine, with fracture critical, underwater, and in-depth inspections scheduled where required)?
- Is the team led by a qualified team leader under a qualified program manager, with credentials appropriate to fracture critical and underwater work?
- Are element-level condition states assigned per the AASHTO MBEI using measured distress thresholds, with quantities tied to photographs and notes?
- Are NBI condition and appraisal ratings assigned per the SNBI definitions and reconciled with the element-level data, reflecting the worst significant condition?
- Are fracture critical members, fatigue-prone details, and scour conditions actively evaluated and any immediate-action condition flagged?
- Does the report document qualifications, element and NBI data, photographs, deficiencies, and prioritized recommendations with timeframes?
- Are findings that change capacity (section loss, scour, cracking) carried into a load rating update and any needed posting or restriction?
