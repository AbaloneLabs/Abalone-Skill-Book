# Bridge Rehabilitation and Strengthening

Bridge rehabilitation and strengthening is the engineering of interventions that restore, improve, or increase the load capacity of an existing bridge beyond simple maintenance, extending its service life without full replacement. Rehabilitation covers deck replacement, overlay and waterproofing, joint elimination, bearing replacement, and substructure repair; strengthening covers capacity increases through section enlargement, external post-tensioning, fiber-reinforced polymer (FRP) wrapping, steel plate attachment, and member replacement. The decision space includes widening an adequate bridge to meet current lane or shoulder standards versus replacing a structurally marginal one. The harm this skill prevents is a costly intervention that fails to address the root cause (a patch over active corrosion, an FRP wrap over ongoing settlement), a strengthening that does not achieve the required capacity gain, and a rehab chosen on first cost that ignores the remaining service life and life-cycle cost. These works are safety-critical and capital-intensive, and they are usually performed under traffic, so the judgment must be matched to the cause, the condition, the capacity, and the constructability. Agents support the licensed engineer who seals the design and must defer final strategy and capacity decisions to that engineer.

## Core Rules

### Base the Strategy on Condition Assessment and Cause Diagnosis

Every rehabilitation or strengthening decision must start from the condition assessment (the latest inspection, element-level data, and any special inspection) and a diagnosis of the cause of distress: chloride-induced corrosion, alkali-silica reaction, fatigue cracking from out-of-plane distortion, overload damage, impact damage, scour, or inherent design deficiency. The intervention must address the cause, not just the symptom, because a repair applied over an active cause will fail: a deck patch over actively corroding rebar spalls again, an FRP flexural wrap over a settlement-induced crack delaminates as movement continues. Review the load rating to confirm whether the bridge is capacity-deficient and by how much, and use the remaining service life estimate to judge whether strengthening is justified or whether replacement is the rational choice. A strategy selected without cause diagnosis and capacity context is generic and will waste money.

### Match the Deck and Wearing Surface Strategy to the Condition

The deck is the most rapidly deteriorating element, and the strategy ranges from crack sealing and thin polymer or methyl methacrylate overlays for early distress, to latex-modified or low-slump dense concrete overlays for moderate chloride contamination, to partial-depth and full-depth deck replacement when delamination and corrosion are advanced. Consider chloride content and half-cell potential mapping to judge whether the reinforcement is actively corroding; an overlay over heavily contaminated concrete with active corrosion only delays failure. Evaluate deck replacement alternatives (cast-in-place, precast panel, exothermic or rapid-strength concrete) against traffic control and closure windows, and use joint elimination (integral or semi-integral conversion) where feasible because joints are the primary chloride entry path. Selecting an overlay when the deck needs replacement is the classic false economy that returns the bridge to distress within a few years.

### Select Strengthening Methods for the Specific Deficiency and Required Gain

Strengthening must target a defined deficiency (flexural, shear, axial, seismic, fatigue) and a verified capacity gain. FRP wrapping is efficient for shear and confinement and for modest flexural gains, but it is brittle, sensitive to surface preparation and bond, and limited by concrete crushing strain; it is poor where large flexural gain or ductile failure is required. Section enlargement (jacketing columns, adding concrete to girders) gives large flexural and axial gain with composite action but adds dead load and requires shear transfer detailing. External post-tensioning gives large flexural and shear gain with controllable force but requires durable anchorages and corrosion protection. For fatigue, the retrofit is detail-specific: peening, hole drilling (crack-arrest holes), grinding weld toes, or replacing the offending detail, and removing the distortion that drives out-of-plane fatigue. Select the method that achieves the required gain with constructable, durable detailing, and verify the gain by calculation of strain compatibility, debonding, and failure mode, not by assumption.

### Address the Active Cause and the Load Path Before Strengthening

Before any strengthening is installed, arrest the active cause: cathodic protection or chloride extraction for ongoing corrosion, underpinning or grouting for continuing settlement, joint elimination and drainage repair for chloride entry, and scour countermeasures for undermining foundations. Verify the existing load path and member capacity, because strengthening that adds capacity to one member can shift load to a weaker member or connection, creating a new deficiency. For seismic strengthening, follow capacity design principles so the intended plastic hinge forms and brittle elements are capacity-protected. Strengthening installed over an unaddressed cause, or without checking the redistributed load path, fails or simply relocates the weakness.

### Decide Widening, Rehabilitation, or Replacement on Life-Cycle and Risk

Compare the alternatives on life-cycle cost (initial construction, maintenance, future repair, user cost from traffic disruption) and on the remaining service life the intervention buys. Widening an otherwise sound bridge to add lanes or shoulders may be cost-effective, but if the superstructure or substructure is near the end of life, widening commits the owner to a soon-redundant structure. Replacement eliminates the backlog of deficiencies, meets current design and seismic standards, and can be staged, but it carries the highest first cost and the largest traffic impact. Use a structured alternatives analysis with life-cycle cost, risk (fracture critical, scour, seismic), and functional adequacy, and document why the chosen alternative is preferred. Choosing rehab on first cost alone, when replacement is life-cycle superior, is a recurring and costly error.

### Engineer Constructability Under Traffic and Verify Quality

Most bridge rehabilitation is performed under traffic with lane closures, temporary barriers, and staged construction, so constructability and sequence are first-order design inputs. Design for the construction stages: each stage must be stable and must not leave a partially strengthened member overloaded, and live loads during construction must be checked against the temporary capacity. Specify quality control tied to performance: surface preparation profile (ICRI) for bonded overlays and FRP, bond and pull-off testing, material certification, and post-installation inspection. A strengthening design that ignores staging or omits quality control is a failed project regardless of the calculated capacity, because the as-built condition will not match the design.

## Common Traps

### Rehab That Does Not Address the Cause

An overlay or patch is applied over actively corroding reinforcement or ongoing chloride ingress, and the distress returns within a few years. The false signal is a fresh new surface; the harm is repeated, escalating repair cost and a deck that ultimately needs replacement anyway. Always diagnose and arrest the cause before the cosmetic repair.

### FRP or Overlay With Unverified Capacity Gain

FRP or an overlay is installed and assumed to provide the design gain, but strain compatibility, debonding at termination, and the brittle failure mode were never calculated, so the member remains under-capacity. The false signal is an installed, good-looking strengthening; the harm is a bridge still below the required rating. Verify the gain by calculation and, where required, by load testing.

### Strengthening That Relocates the Weakness

A girder is strengthened, shifting load to a now-undersized connection, bearing, or substructure element that was not checked, creating a new deficiency. The false signal is a stronger member; the harm is failure at the unstrengthened link in the load path. Always re-check the full load path and capacity-protected elements after any strengthening.

### Rehab Chosen on First Cost Over Life-Cycle

Rehabilitation is chosen because it is cheaper now, even though the bridge is near end of life and replacement is life-cycle superior, locking in future repair and disruption. The false signal is a low bid price; the harm is repeated spending on a structure that should have been replaced. Use life-cycle cost and remaining service life in the alternatives analysis.

## Self-Check

- Is the strategy based on the condition assessment, cause diagnosis, and current load rating, and does it address the root cause rather than the symptom?
- Is the deck and wearing surface strategy matched to chloride content, active corrosion, and the real deck condition, with joint elimination considered?
- Is the strengthening method selected for the specific deficiency and verified for capacity gain (strain compatibility, debonding, failure mode), not assumed?
- Are active causes (corrosion, settlement, scour, chloride entry) arrested and is the full load path re-checked so strengthening does not relocate the weakness?
- Are widening, rehabilitation, and replacement compared on life-cycle cost, remaining service life, risk, and functional adequacy, with the choice documented?
- Is the design staged for construction under traffic, with each stage checked for stability and temporary capacity, and live loads during construction verified?
- Are quality control (surface preparation, bond testing, material certification) and post-installation inspection specified so the as-built matches the design?
