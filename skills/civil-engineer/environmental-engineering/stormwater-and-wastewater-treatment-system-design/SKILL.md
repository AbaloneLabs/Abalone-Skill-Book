# Stormwater and Wastewater Treatment System Design

Stormwater and wastewater treatment system design is the engineering of the unit processes (the sedimentation, the filtration, the biological treatment, the disinfection) that remove the pollutants from the water before it is discharged to the receiving environment. The stormwater treatment system removes the sediment, the nutrients, and the metals from the urban runoff, and the decentralized wastewater system removes the organic matter, the nitrogen, and the pathogens from the domestic sewage. The harm this skill prevents is a treatment system that is undersized for the loading and fails to meet the effluent quality (a permit violation and a downstream impact), that clogs or short-circuits from a poor hydraulic design (a system that stops treating), or that is unmaintainable and abandoned (a system that fails after the first year). Because the treatment system is a long-term infrastructure with a permit and a maintenance obligation, it must be designed against the loading, the hydraulics, and the maintenance reality, and verified against the effluent target.

## Core Rules

### Characterise the Loading and the Effluent Target

The treatment system must be designed against the pollutant loading (the stormwater's event mean concentration and the runoff volume, or the wastewater's flow and the influent concentration) and the effluent target (the regulatory limit for the sediment, the nutrients, the metals, or the pathogens). Establish the design storm or the design flow (the water quality storm for the stormwater, the average and peak daily flow for the wastewater), the influent concentration (from the literature, the monitoring, or the land use), and the required removal efficiency (the target effluent divided by the influent). A system designed without the loading and the target is a system designed to an unknown standard, and the effluent quality is unverified.

### Select the Unit Process and the Treatment Train

Select the unit process that achieves the required removal: the sedimentation (the wet pond, the sediment forebay) for the settleable solids; the filtration (the sand filter, the bioretention, the media filter) for the particulate and the dissolved pollutants; the biological treatment (the constructed wetland, the aerobic or anaerobic unit) for the nutrients and the organic matter; the disinfection (the UV, the chlorination) for the pathogens. Combine the processes in a treatment train, where each unit removes a fraction and the next unit addresses the remainder, because no single unit achieves the high removal for the broad pollutant suite. A system with a single unit for a multi-pollutant loading will fail the target for the pollutants the unit does not remove.

### Size the System for the Volume, the Residence Time, and the Loading Rate

Size the stormwater system by the water quality volume (the runoff from the water quality storm, commonly the 25-mm or the 90-percent event) and the residence time (the drawdown time, commonly 24 to 48 hours for the extended detention), with the surface area and the depth that provide the settling and the treatment. Size the wastewater system by the hydraulic residence time (the volume divided by the flow, commonly 2 to 5 days for the aerobic unit) and the organic loading rate (the mass per volume per day, for the biological process). Verify that the sizing provides the treatment time and the area for the design loading, because the undersized system has insufficient time and area and fails the effluent target.

### Design the Hydraulics for the Uniform Flow and the Avoidance of Short-Circuit

The treatment system's hydraulics determine whether the water flows uniformly through the treatment volume or short-circuits from the inlet to the outlet, bypassing the treatment. Design the inlet (the forebay, the level spreader, the diffuser) to distribute the flow across the width, the internal geometry (the length-to-width ratio, commonly 3:1 to 5:1, the baffles) to prevent the short-circuit, and the outlet (the reverse-slope pipe, the skimmer, the weir) to draw the clean water from the appropriate depth. Use the tracer or the nominal residence time to verify the internal flow, because a system that short-circuits treats only a fraction of the volume and fails the effluent target despite the adequate nominal size.

### Specify the Media, the Vegetation, and the Underdrain for the Filtration and the Bioretention

For the filtration and the bioretention systems, specify the media (the sand, the engineered soil, the compost) with the gradation, the permeability (commonly 25 to 100 mm per hour for the bioretention), and the depth (commonly 0.5 to 0.75 metres), the vegetation (the plants suited to the inundation and the drought), and the underdrain (the perforated pipe in the aggregate, with the outlet control) or the infiltration (the design that infiltrates to the underlying soil). Verify that the media's permeability and the area provide the design drawdown, and that the underdrain or the infiltration prevents the standing water and the system failure. A media that is too fine clogs; a media that is too coarse does not treat; the wrong vegetation does not establish.

### Design the Outlet and the Emergency Spillway for the Storm Event

The stormwater treatment system must pass the design storm (the 10-year or the 100-year event) without failing, and the outlet and the emergency spillway must be sized for this storm. Design the principal outlet (the riser, the orifice, the weir) for the water quality and the smaller storms, with the multi-stage configuration that controls the multiple frequencies, and the emergency spillway (the vegetated or the lined channel) for the extreme storm that exceeds the principal outlet. Verify that the embankment (for the pond) is stable, that the spillway has the freeboard and the lining, and that the system does not fail at the design storm, because the storm failure releases the accumulated sediment and the pollutants in a concentrated slug.

### Specify the Long-Term Maintenance and the Monitoring

The treatment system requires the long-term maintenance (the sediment removal from the forebay, the media replacement, the vegetation management, the outlet cleaning) and the monitoring (the inspection, the effluent sampling for the permit), and these must be specified in the design. Identify the maintenance access (the road, the ramp, the bench), the maintenance frequency (the annual or the event-based), and the responsible party (the owner, the HOA, the municipality), and provide the operations and maintenance manual. A system without the maintenance plan will fail within the first years, because the sediment accumulates, the media clogs, and the vegetation dies, and the treatment stops.

## Common Traps

### The System Sized To The Volume Without The Residence Time

The stormwater system is sized for the water quality volume, but the outlet draws the volume down in a few hours, and the residence time is insufficient for the settling and the treatment. The trap is that the volume is met, while the time is not, and the system passes the water through too quickly for the treatment to occur. The false signal is the volume criterion met; the harm is an effluent that fails the target, because the residence time was the actual treatment driver, and it was not verified.

### The Single Unit For The Multi-Pollutant Loading

The treatment system relies on a single unit (the wet pond) for a loading that includes the dissolved nutrients and the metals, which the pond does not remove. The trap is that the pond removes the sediment and the particulate pollutants, while the dissolved pollutants pass through, and the effluent fails the nutrient or the metal target. The false signal is the visible sediment removal; the harm is the dissolved pollutant in the effluent, because the treatment train did not include the unit for the dissolved fraction.

### The Internal Geometry That Short-Circuits

The pond or the wetland has a length-to-width ratio of 1:1 or less, or the inlet and the outlet are aligned, and the flow short-circuits from the inlet to the outlet, bypassing the treatment volume. The trap is that the nominal volume and the residence time are met, while the effective volume is a fraction of the nominal, because the flow takes the direct path. The false signal is the nominal sizing; the harm is a system that treats only a fraction of the water, because the hydraulics short-circuited the treatment volume.

### The Media Too Fine Or Too Coarse

The bioretention media is specified with the fines content that is too high (the clogging) or the permeability that is too high (the insufficient treatment), and the system fails. The trap is that the media meets a generic specification, while the gradation and the permeability are wrong for the design drawdown and the treatment, and the system either clogs or does not treat. The false signal is the specification met; the harm is a system that fails, because the media's hydraulic and treatment properties were not verified.

### The Outlet That Fails At The Design Storm

The principal outlet is sized for the water quality storm, and the emergency spillway is absent or undersized, and the system fails at the larger storm. The trap is that the outlet passes the water quality storm, while the larger storm overtops the embankment or fails the spillway, and the system is destroyed. The false signal is the water quality outlet; the harm is the catastrophic failure at the design storm, because the emergency spillway was not provided for the extreme event.

### The Maintenance Plan Absent Or Vague

The maintenance plan is a one-line note ("maintain as required"), without the access, the frequency, the responsible party, or the budget, and the system is not maintained. The trap is that the system is designed and built, while the maintenance is left to the owner without the specifics, and the owner does not maintain, because the plan did not tell them what to do. The false signal is the built system; the harm is a system that fails within the first years, because the maintenance was not specified and not performed.

## Self-Check

- Is the system designed against the pollutant loading (the design storm or flow, the influent concentration) and the effluent target (the regulatory limit, the required removal)?
- Is the treatment train selected to address the full pollutant suite, with the multiple units for the particulate and the dissolved fractions?
- Is the system sized for the volume, the residence time, and the loading rate, with the treatment time and the area verified for the design loading?
- Are the hydraulics designed for the uniform flow, with the inlet distribution, the internal geometry (the length-to-width ratio, the baffles), and the outlet that prevent the short-circuit?
- Is the media specified with the gradation, the permeability, and the depth that provide the design drawdown and the treatment, and the underdrain or the infiltration that prevents the standing water?
- Are the outlet and the emergency spillway sized for the design storm, with the embankment stability, the freeboard, and the lining that prevent the storm failure?
- Is the long-term maintenance specified, with the access, the frequency, the responsible party, and the operations and maintenance manual?
- Is the effluent quality verified against the target, with the monitoring that confirms the treatment and the permit compliance?
