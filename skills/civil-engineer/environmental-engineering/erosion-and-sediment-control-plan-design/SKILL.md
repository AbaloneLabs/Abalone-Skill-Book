# Erosion and Sediment Control Plan Design

Erosion and sediment control (ESC) plan design is the engineering of how a construction site, which disturbs the vegetated cover and exposes the soil to rain and runoff, prevents the soil from leaving the site and polluting the receiving waters during the period before the permanent stabilisation is established. The disturbed construction site can erode at rates tens to hundreds of times the undisturbed condition, and the sediment-laden runoff, if uncontrolled, deposits in the downstream channels, the storm sewers, and the receiving waters, with environmental, regulatory, and neighbour consequences. The harm this skill prevents is a site that pollutes the receiving water with sediment (a permit violation and an enforcement), that deposits sediment on the downstream roads and properties (a nuisance and a liability), or that erodes the site itself to the point of cutting channels and undermining the work. Because the ESC plan is a permit requirement and a construction-phase safety and environmental control, it must be designed against the site, the soil, and the phasing, and it must be maintained and adapted as the construction proceeds.

## Core Rules

### Design the ESC Plan to the Permit and the Site Conditions

The ESC plan must comply with the governing permit (the national pollutant discharge elimination system general permit for construction activity, or the state or local equivalent), which establishes the requirements based on the disturbed area (commonly the 0.4-hectare or the 4-hectare threshold for the different levels of the plan), the receiving water, and the duration of the disturbance. Establish the disturbed area, the soil type (from the soil survey, with the erodibility and the rock content), the slope and the slope length, the drainage area and the runoff pattern, and the proximity to the sensitive receiving water (a stream, a wetland, a listed water). Design the plan against these conditions, with the controls selected and sized for the actual site, not for a generic template, because the site's soil, slope, and drainage determine which controls will work and which will fail.

### Sequence the Controls With the Construction Phasing

The ESC plan is not a static drawing; it is a sequence of controls that changes as the construction phasing changes, and the plan must specify the controls for each phase. The initial phase, before the mass grading, installs the perimeter controls (the silt fence, the construction entrance, the inlet protection) and the sediment basin or trap. The mass grading phase exposes the large area and requires the temporary stabilisation, the diversion of the upland runoff, and the sediment basin at the low point. The construction phase, with the building pads and the roads, requires the inlet protection, the check dams in the swales, and the temporary cover on the exposed slopes. The final phase establishes the permanent stabilisation (the vegetation, the paving, the landscaping) and removes the temporary controls as the area is stabilised. A plan that shows the controls for one phase and does not sequence them will be wrong for the other phases, and the controls will be absent or inadequate when the construction needs them.

### Select and Size the Perimeter, the Inlet, and the Channel Controls

The perimeter controls (the silt fence, the brush barrier, the diversion berm) intercept the sheet flow at the site boundary and prevent the sediment from leaving the site. Select and install the silt fence along the contour (not across it, where it would concentrate the flow), with the proper trenching and the support, and size it for the drainage area (commonly limited to 0.1 hectare per 30 metres of fence). The inlet protection prevents the sediment from entering the storm drain at the drop inlets and the curb inlets, with the filter appropriate to the inlet type and the flow. The channel controls (the check dams, the rock ditch checks) slow the flow in the swales and the ditches and trap the sediment, with the spacing and the rock size suited to the channel slope and the flow. A control that is selected for the wrong application or sized for the wrong drainage area will fail at the first storm, because the control's capacity is exceeded.

### Size the Sediment Basin or Trap for the Disturbed Drainage Area

The sediment basin (for the drainage area above 2 hectares) and the sediment trap (for the smaller area) are the primary sediment controls, and they must be sized for the disturbed drainage area and the required trapping efficiency. Size the basin by the regulatory criterion (commonly 125 cubic metres of storage per hectare of disturbed drainage area, or the equivalent), with the surface area and the depth that provide the settling time for the design sediment, and the principal spillway (the skimmer or the perforated riser) that decants the clean water from the surface and the emergency spillway that passes the design storm. The basin must be in place before the mass grading, and it must be maintained (the sediment removed at the 50-percent-full threshold) through the construction. A basin that is undersized or absent allows the sediment-laden runoff to leave the site at the first storm, because the primary control is missing.

### Specify the Temporary and the Permanent Stabilisation

The exposed soil is the source of the sediment, and the stabilisation is the control that addresses the source. Specify the temporary stabilisation (the seed and the mulch, the erosion-control blanket, the polyacrylamide, the temporary cover) for the areas that will be exposed for more than the regulatory period (commonly 14 to 21 days for the short-term and 60 days for the long-term), and apply it as soon as the area is idle. Specify the permanent stabilisation (the permanent vegetation, the paving, the landscaping, the riprap) for the areas that have reached the final grade, and apply it as soon as the grade is achieved, because the longer the area is exposed, the greater the erosion and the sediment. A site that exposes the area and defers the stabilisation erodes at the maximum rate, and the downstream controls are overwhelmed by the sediment that the stabilisation would have prevented.

### Inspect, Maintain, and Adapt the Plan During the Construction

The ESC controls degrade from the moment they are installed: the silt fence is overtopped and torn, the sediment basin fills, the inlet protection clogs, the mulch washes away. Require the inspection (by the qualified installer, at the frequency the permit requires, commonly every 7 days and within 24 hours of a 12-mm rain), the maintenance (the sediment removal, the repair, the replacement), and the adaptation (the additional controls where the plan is inadequate, the revised plan where the phasing changes). Document the inspection and the maintenance, because the record is the permit's compliance evidence and the defence in any enforcement. A plan that is installed and not inspected will fail within weeks, and the failure is silent until the downstream water turns brown or the inspector arrives.

### Address the Special Conditions: Steep Slopes, Karst, Wetlands, and the Sensitive Receiving Water

For the special conditions, the standard controls are inadequate, and the plan must address them. For the steep slopes, the additional measures (the slope interrupter, the track walking, the blanket, the temporary bench) control the rill and the gully erosion. For the karst, the infiltration is avoided (the sinkhole formation) and the controls are designed to prevent the concentrated flow. For the wetlands and the sensitive receiving water, the buffer, the enhanced treatment, and the additional sediment basin are provided. A plan that applies the standard controls to the special conditions will fail at the condition that the standard controls cannot address, because the special condition requires the special measure.

## Common Traps

### The Plan Copied From A Template

The ESC plan is copied from a generic template, with the standard symbols and the standard notes, without the site-specific layout, the soil, the slope, and the phasing. The trap is that the plan looks complete and compliant, while the controls are not located for the actual drainage pattern, not sized for the actual disturbed area, and not sequenced for the actual phasing. The false signal is the complete-looking plan; the harm is a site that pollutes the receiving water at the first storm, because the plan was a template, not a design.

### The Controls Not Sequenced With The Phasing

The plan shows the controls for the initial phase, and the construction proceeds to the mass grading and the building phases without the updated controls, because the plan was treated as a one-time drawing. The trap is that the initial controls are in place, while the later phases expose the large areas without the corresponding controls, and the sediment leaves the site from the uncontrolled phases. The false signal is the installed initial controls; the harm is a site that pollutes from the later phases, because the plan did not sequence the controls with the construction.

### The Silt Fence Across The Contour

The silt fence is installed across the contour or across the drainage path, where it concentrates the flow and overtops, rather than along the contour, where it intercepts the sheet flow. The trap is that the fence is installed and appears to be in place, while it is in the wrong orientation and it fails at the first flow, because it concentrates rather than intercepts. The false signal is the installed fence; the harm is a perimeter control that fails immediately, because the orientation was wrong.

### The Sediment Basin Undersized Or Absent

The sediment basin is sized for less than the regulatory storage, or it is omitted because the drainage area "looked small," and the mass grading proceeds. The trap is that the basin is in place or the area is small, while the disturbed drainage area produces the sediment-laden runoff that the undersized or absent basin cannot trap, and the runoff leaves the site. The false signal is the basin or the small area; the harm is the sediment leaving the site at the first storm, because the primary control was undersized or missing.

### The Stabilisation Deferred

The exposed area is not stabilised within the regulatory period, because the construction schedule prioritises the next activity over the stabilisation, and the area erodes. The trap is that the area is exposed, while the stabilisation is deferred, and the erosion produces the sediment that the downstream controls must trap, and the controls are overwhelmed. The false signal is the active construction; the harm is a site that erodes at the maximum rate, because the source control (the stabilisation) was deferred.

### The Plan Installed And Not Inspected

The ESC plan is installed at the start of the construction, and the inspection and the maintenance are not performed, because the plan was treated as a one-time installation. The trap is that the controls are in place at the start, while they degrade and fail within weeks, and the failure is silent until the downstream water turns brown or the regulator arrives. The false signal is the installed plan; the harm is a site that pollutes after the first weeks, because the inspection and the maintenance that would have kept the controls functional were not performed.

## Self-Check

- Does the ESC plan comply with the governing permit, and is it designed against the site's disturbed area, soil, slope, drainage, and receiving water?
- Is the plan sequenced with the construction phasing, with the controls specified for the initial, the mass grading, the construction, and the final phases?
- Are the perimeter, the inlet, and the channel controls selected and sized for the actual application and drainage area, with the silt fence along the contour?
- Is the sediment basin or trap sized for the disturbed drainage area and the required storage, in place before the mass grading, and maintained at the 50-percent-full threshold?
- Is the temporary and the permanent stabilisation specified for the exposed areas, applied within the regulatory period, and prioritised as the grade is achieved?
- Is the plan inspected at the permit frequency, maintained (sediment removed, controls repaired), and adapted (additional controls, revised plan) as the construction proceeds?
- Are the special conditions (steep slopes, karst, wetlands, sensitive receiving water) addressed with the additional measures the standard controls cannot provide?
- Are the inspection and the maintenance documented, as the permit's compliance evidence and the defence in any enforcement?
