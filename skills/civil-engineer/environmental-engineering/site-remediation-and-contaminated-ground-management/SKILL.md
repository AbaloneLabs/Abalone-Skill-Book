# Site Remediation and Contaminated Ground Management

Site remediation and contaminated ground management is the engineering of how a civil or structural project deals with the contamination that exists in the soil or the groundwater before and during construction, and it sits at the intersection of the geotechnical, the environmental, and the construction disciplines. The contamination (petroleum hydrocarbons, solvents, metals, methane, or contaminated fill) affects the safety of the workers, the integrity of the building (vapour intrusion, sulfate attack on concrete, corrosion of steel), the cost and the schedule of the earthwork, and the regulatory and the liability position of the owner. The harm this skill prevents is a construction that exposes workers to contaminated soil or vapour without protection, a building built over a contaminated plume that intrudes vapour into the indoor air, a foundation concrete attacked by contaminated groundwater, or an earthwork that excavates contaminated soil and disposes of it improperly, triggering regulatory enforcement and cost overruns. Because contamination is hidden, regulated, and liability-laden, the civil and structural engineer must coordinate with the environmental specialist and integrate the remediation into the design and the construction.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Contamination Condition Before the Earthwork Design

Before the earthwork, the foundation, or the building design is finalised, establish the contamination condition through a phase II site assessment (soil and groundwater sampling, vapour sampling, and the laboratory analysis), and interpret the results against the regulatory screening levels and the risk-based cleanup goals. Identify the contaminants of concern, the horizontal and vertical extent, the concentration, the phase (soil, groundwater, vapour, non-aqueous phase liquid), and the exposure pathways (the worker contact, the vapour intrusion, the groundwater migration, the ecological receptor). Establish the cleanup goal (the risk-based concentration for the exposure scenario and the land use, or the regulatory threshold), and confirm whether the goal is a generic screening level or a site-specific risk-based target. A civil or structural design that proceeds without the contamination condition established may encounter the contamination during the earthwork, with no plan and no budget for the management.

### Coordinate the Remediation Strategy With the Civil and Structural Design

The remediation strategy (excavation and disposal, in-situ treatment, monitored natural attenuation, engineered barriers, vapour mitigation, or a combination) must be coordinated with the civil and the structural design, because each strategy affects the design differently. An excavation and disposal removes the contamination but requires shoring, dewatering, and backfill, and it changes the foundation geometry and the cost. An in-situ treatment (chemical oxidation, bioremediation) treats the contamination in place but may require a treatment period before the construction and may affect the soil properties. An engineered barrier (a cap, a vapour barrier, a sub-slab depressurisation) leaves the contamination in place and relies on the building and the site infrastructure to break the exposure pathway, and the barrier must be designed, constructed, and maintained for the life of the building. Select the strategy with the civil and the structural implications understood, because a remediation that is designed without the coordination may conflict with the foundation, the utilities, or the building envelope.

### Design the Vapour Intrusion Mitigation for the Building

For sites with volatile contaminants in the soil or the groundwater, the vapour intrusion pathway (the migration of the vapour from the soil through the foundation into the indoor air) is a primary exposure route, and the mitigation must be designed into the building. The mitigation options include a passive vapour barrier (a membrane below the slab), an active sub-slab depressurisation system (a fan that draws the vapour from below the slab and vents it above the roof), or a combination, with the design based on the vapour concentration, the building use, and the regulatory requirement. Coordinate the vapour barrier with the structural slab (the membrane below the slab, the penetrations sealed, the sump and the utility entries detailed), and the depressurisation system with the architectural and the mechanical design (the vent pipe routing, the fan location, the discharge above the roof and away from the intakes). A building built over a volatile plume without the vapour mitigation exposes the occupants to the indoor air contamination, and the mitigation added after the construction is far more expensive and less effective.

### Protect the Construction Workers and Manage the Contaminated Excavation

The earthwork on a contaminated site exposes the workers to the soil, the groundwater, and the vapour, and the construction must be planned and executed with the protection. Prepare a health and safety plan (the personal protective equipment, the air monitoring, the exclusion zones, the decontamination), and require the competent person and the monitoring during the excavation. For the contaminated excavation, characterise the soil before the excavation (so that the disposal route is known), manage the excavation to minimise the generation of the contaminated dust and the vapour, and transport and dispose of the contaminated soil at the permitted facility with the manifest and the documentation. For the contaminated groundwater (the dewatering), treat the water before the discharge to the required standard, and manage the treatment residual. A construction that excavates contaminated soil without the plan exposes the workers and creates a regulatory and a disposal violation, with the cost and the delay far exceeding the planning.

### Protect the Building Materials From the Contaminated Environment

The contamination can attack the building materials: the sulfate and the acid in the contaminated groundwater attack the concrete (the sulfate attack, the acid attack), the contaminants and the low-pH groundwater corrode the steel (the piles, the reinforcement, the utilities), and the methane and the volatile vapours accumulate in the enclosed spaces (the basements, the utility vaults) and create an explosion hazard. Specify the concrete for the exposure (the sulfate-resistant cement, the low-permeability mix, the protective coating), the steel for the corrosion (the corrosion allowance, the coating, the cathodic protection, the non-metallic alternative), and the building for the vapour (the vapour barrier, the ventilation, the gas detection in the enclosed spaces). A foundation or a basement built with the standard materials in a contaminated environment will degrade far short of its design life, and the degradation is hidden until the failure.

### Establish the Long-Term Monitoring and the Institutional Controls

For the remediation that leaves the contamination in place (the engineered barrier, the in-situ treatment, the monitored natural attenuation), the long-term monitoring and the institutional controls are part of the remedy, and they must be established and documented. The monitoring (the groundwater, the vapour, the indoor air) confirms that the remedy is performing, and the institutional controls (the land use restriction, the building use restriction, the excavation permit) prevent the future exposure. Establish the monitoring program (the locations, the frequency, the parameters, the action levels), and the institutional controls in the legal instrument (the environmental covenant or the deed restriction), and document the responsibility for the monitoring and the maintenance. A remedy that relies on the barrier or the in-situ treatment without the monitoring and the controls is a remedy whose performance is unverified and whose future exposure is unmanaged.

### Coordinate the Regulatory Closure and the Liability

The remediation and the construction must be coordinated with the regulatory program (the voluntary cleanup, the brownfield, the corrective action, or the permit), and the closure must be documented for the liability. Confirm that the remediation meets the regulatory cleanup goal, that the closure documentation (the no-further-action letter, the certificate of completion, the permit modification) is obtained, and that the liability (the owner's, the contractor's, and the engineer's) is allocated by the contract and the environmental insurance. A remediation that is performed without the regulatory coordination may not achieve the closure, and the liability for the residual contamination remains, with the cost and the risk falling on the owner and the project team.

## Common Traps

### The Contamination Encountered Without The Plan

The earthwork begins, and the excavation encounters the contaminated soil or the contaminated groundwater that the phase II assessment did not characterise or that the design did not plan for. The trap is that the excavation proceeds, while the contamination has no management plan, no disposal route, and no worker protection, and the work stops, the regulator is notified, and the cost and the delay accumulate. The false signal is the started earthwork; the harm is a project stopped by the contamination that the assessment and the plan should have addressed, because the condition was not established before the design.

### The Vapour Mitigation Added After The Building

The building is designed and built over a volatile plume, and the vapour mitigation is added after the indoor air testing reveals the intrusion, because the vapour pathway was not evaluated in the design. The trap is that the building is occupied, while the indoor air is contaminated, and the mitigation must be retrofitted through the slab and the occupied space, at a far higher cost and disruption than the design-stage mitigation. The false signal is the completed building; the harm is an occupied building with the indoor air contamination, and a mitigation that is less effective and more expensive than the design-stage system.

### The Concrete Attacked By The Sulfate

The foundation concrete is specified as the standard mix, and the contaminated groundwater contains the sulfate or the acid that the geotechnical or the environmental report identified. The trap is that the concrete meets the structural strength, while the sulfate or the acid attacks the cement matrix over the years, and the concrete expands, cracks, and loses strength. The false signal is the strength-tested concrete; the harm is a foundation that degrades far short of its design life, because the concrete was not specified for the contaminated exposure.

### The Contaminated Soil Disposed Improperly

The contaminated soil is excavated and transported to a disposal facility that is not permitted for the contaminant, or the soil is characterised inadequately and the disposal is mis-classified, because the disposal was managed by the schedule rather than the regulation. The trap is that the soil is removed from the site, while the disposal is improper, and the regulator traces the soil to the facility and the project, and the enforcement and the re-excavation follow. The false signal is the removed soil; the harm is a regulatory violation and a cost that the improper disposal created, because the characterisation and the disposal were not managed to the standard.

### The Remedy Without The Monitoring

The engineered barrier or the in-situ treatment is installed, and the remedy is declared complete, without the long-term monitoring or the institutional controls, because the monitoring was treated as a future concern. The trap is that the remedy is in place, while its performance is unverified, and the contamination may migrate or the barrier may degrade, and the exposure returns without detection. The false signal is the installed remedy; the harm is a remedy that fails silently, because the monitoring that would have detected the failure was never established.

### The Regulatory Closure Not Obtained

The remediation is performed, and the construction proceeds, but the regulatory closure documentation is not obtained, because the closure was treated as the regulator's concern. The trap is that the work is done, while the closure is not documented, and the liability for the residual contamination remains with the owner, and a future transaction (the sale, the financing, the redevelopment) cannot proceed without the closure. The false signal is the completed remediation; the harm is a liability that the closure would have released, because the regulatory coordination was not completed.

## Self-Check

- Is the contamination condition established by a phase II assessment before the earthwork and the foundation design, with the contaminants, the extent, and the cleanup goals documented?
- Is the remediation strategy coordinated with the civil and the structural design, with the implications for the foundation, the utilities, and the building envelope understood?
- For volatile contaminants, is the vapour intrusion mitigation designed into the building (the vapour barrier, the sub-slab depressurisation), coordinated with the slab and the mechanical design?
- Is the contaminated excavation planned with the health and safety plan, the soil characterisation, the disposal route, and the dewatering treatment, before the earthwork begins?
- Are the building materials (the concrete, the steel, the enclosed-space ventilation) specified for the contaminated exposure, with the sulfate-resistant, the corrosion-protected, and the gas-detected design as required?
- For the remedies that leave the contamination in place, are the long-term monitoring and the institutional controls established, documented, and assigned to the responsible party?
- Is the regulatory closure coordinated and documented, with the no-further-action or the certificate of completion obtained for the liability release?
- Are the remediation, the construction, and the closure documented in a record that supports the regulatory compliance and the future transaction?
