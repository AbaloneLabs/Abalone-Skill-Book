# Construction Sequence and Temporary Works Design

Construction sequence and temporary works design is the engineering of the structure during the phase when it is most vulnerable: the period when it is being built, when the permanent lateral system is incomplete, when floors are loaded before they are connected, when gravity elements are shored on floors below that have not yet reached strength, and when wind and construction loads act on a frame that was never analysed in its partial state. The completed structure is designed and analysed; the partially completed structure is often neither, and the construction loads, the temporary works, and the sequence determine whether the structure survives to completion. The harm this skill prevents is a collapse during construction, when a floor loaded before its strength is reached gives way, when a frame without its permanent bracing rackets under wind, or when a temporary shore or formwork fails under the load it was never engineered for. Construction failures are sudden and often fatal, and the temporary works and the sequence are as safety-critical as the permanent design.

## Core Rules

### Analyse the Structure at Every Critical Stage of Construction

The permanent structure is designed for the completed condition; the construction analysis must evaluate the structure at every stage where the load, the strength, or the stability differs from the completed condition. Identify the critical stages: the excavation and the foundation, the erection of the frame before the lateral system is complete, the placement of concrete on floors shored by the floors below, the removal of formwork and shoring, the installation of cladding and finishes, and the demolition or modification of existing structures. For each stage, confirm that the structure (as it exists at that stage) can carry the construction loads (the dead weight, the construction live load, the material storage, the equipment, the wind, and the seismic if the construction period spans a season) with the strength available at that stage (the concrete that may be days old, the steel that may not be fully connected). A structure that is adequate when complete but not at the critical construction stage has failed during construction.

### Engineer the Temporary Works: Shoring, Formwork, Falsework, and Bracing

Temporary works are structures, and they must be engineered to the same standard as the permanent works. Design shoring and reshoring to carry the wet concrete and the construction loads without overloading the floors below, accounting for the concrete strength at the time of loading and the number of floors over which the load is distributed. Design formwork for the lateral pressure of the fresh concrete (which depends on the placement rate, the temperature, the mix, and the height), the deflection limits for the surface finish, and the safe removal time. Design falsework for the span, the load, the wind, and the foundation, with the lateral bracing and the tie-downs to prevent collapse under wind or eccentric load. Design temporary bracing for the frame before the permanent lateral system is installed, sized for the construction wind and the erection tolerances. A temporary work that is sized by rule of thumb or by the contractor's experience, without engineering, is a structure whose capacity is unknown, and the failure is a collapse.

### Evaluate Lifting, Rigging, and Crane Operations

Lifting operations place temporary loads on the structure, the crane, and the ground, and each must be engineered. For tower and mobile cranes, confirm the crane loads (the lifted load, the rigging, the dynamic effects, the wind on the load and the boom), the crane foundation or the supporting structure (a tower crane on a mat or tied to the frame, a mobile crane on the ground or the completed floor), and the reach and the clearances. For the lifted element, confirm the lifting points, the rigging, the element's capacity during the lift (a precast element or a steel member lifted at points that induce different moments than the in-service condition), and the temporary bracing until the element is connected. For the structure that supports the crane or the lifted load, confirm that the construction load is within the available capacity at the time of the lift. A lift that is engineered only for the crane capacity, without the supporting structure and the lifted element, is a lift that may overload the structure or fail the element.

### Control the Sequence to Maintain Stability and the Load Path

The construction sequence must maintain the stability and the load path at every step, not just at the completed structure. Confirm that the permanent lateral system is installed in step with the frame erection, so that no floor is left without a lateral load path for longer than the construction wind duration allows. Confirm that the gravity load path is continuous at every stage, that the columns and the walls are connected before the floor above is loaded, and that the foundations are complete and capable before the superstructure load is applied. Confirm that the formwork and shoring sequence does not overload the floors below, that the reshoring distributes the load to floors of adequate strength, and that the removal sequence does not leave a floor carrying its full weight plus the floor above before it has reached its strength. A sequence that builds the structure without maintaining the load path at each step is a sequence that invites a progressive collapse.

### Address Demolition, Modification, and Existing-Structure Integration

For projects that demolish, modify, or integrate with existing structures, the construction sequence must engineer the existing structure through the change. Confirm that the demolition sequence does not remove a load-bearing element before the temporary or permanent replacement is in place, that the modification (an opening cut in a wall or slab, a column removed and replaced) is engineered for the temporary and the permanent condition, and that the integration (a new addition connected to an existing frame) addresses the differential movement, the load transfer, and the connection capacity. Existing structures carry latent conditions (deterioration, as-built deviations, materials below the current standard) that the demolition or modification may expose, and the sequence must anticipate and engineer around them.

### Address Wind, Seismic, and Environmental Loads During Construction

The construction period exposes the partially completed structure to wind, and in seismic regions to seismic loads, that the permanent lateral system may not yet resist. Establish the design wind for the construction period (commonly the wind for a return period matched to the construction duration, such as the 10- or 25-year event for a year of construction, or the seasonal hurricane exposure), and confirm that the temporary bracing and the partially completed frame resist it. For seismic regions, recognise that the construction period carries a real, if lower, seismic risk, and that the structure should not be left in a configuration that is disproportionately vulnerable (a soft story, an unbraced frame) for longer than necessary. For environmental loads (snow, ice, rain, temperature), confirm that the temporary works and the partially completed structure (an open excavation, an unroofed frame, a slab exposed to freezing) are protected.

### Document the Construction Method Statement and the Temporary Works Design

The construction sequence and the temporary works must be documented in a method statement and a calculation set, reviewed by the engineer of record and the contractor's engineer, and approved before the work begins. The method statement establishes the sequence, the loads, the temporary works, the inspection, and the contingency, and it is the reference for the field execution and the record in any incident investigation. A construction that proceeds without the documented method and the engineered temporary works is a construction whose stability is assumed, not verified, and the first indication of the assumption's failure is the collapse.

## Common Traps

### The Floor Loaded Before Its Strength

A concrete floor is loaded with the floor above's wet concrete and shoring before it has reached the strength to carry the load, on the schedule's pressure to keep the pour cycle moving. The trap is that the floor carries the load visibly, while the concrete is days or weeks short of its design strength, and the floor deflects, cracks, or fails under a load it would have carried at 28 days. The false signal is the floor that holds the shoring; the harm is a slab that is overloaded in its early strength, with cracking, deflection, or collapse that the schedule's pace created.

### The Frame Without The Permanent Bracing

A steel or concrete frame is erected several floors ahead of the permanent lateral system (the shear walls, the braced bays), because the frame erection is faster than the lateral system. The trap is that the frame stands and appears stable, while it has no designed lateral load path for the construction wind, and the next storm loads it beyond the capacity of the temporary bracing or the erection bolts. The false signal is the standing, plumb frame; the harm is a frame that rackets or collapses under a wind that the permanent lateral system would have resisted, because the lateral system lagged the erection.

### The Formwork Pressured Beyond Its Design

A formwork system is designed for a placement rate, and the pour proceeds faster, or the mix is more fluid, or the temperature is higher, and the lateral pressure exceeds the design. The trap is that the formwork holds initially, while the pressure rises with the rate and the height, and the formwork bulges, leaks, or bursts. The false signal is the formwork that holds the initial pour; the harm is a blowout that spills concrete and injures workers, because the placement exceeded the rate the formwork was engineered for.

### The Lift Engineered For The Crane, Not The Structure

A heavy lift is planned, and the crane capacity is verified, but the supporting floor or the supporting ground is not checked for the crane's outrigger or track load. The trap is that the crane can lift the load, while the floor or the ground beneath the outriggers cannot support the crane's load, and the outrigger punches through the floor or the ground fails and the crane tips. The false signal is the verified crane capacity; the harm is a collapse of the support, with the crane and the load, because the support was not engineered with the lift.

### The Demolition Sequence That Removes The Support

A demolition removes a load-bearing wall or column before the temporary or permanent replacement is in place, on the assumption that the structure will "stand for a while." The trap is that the structure stands momentarily, while it has lost its load path, and the next load (a wind, a vibration, a minor addition) triggers a progressive collapse. The false signal is the structure that stands after the removal; the harm is a collapse that the sequence created, because the demolition removed the support before the replacement carried the load.

### The Temporary Works Without Engineering

A shoring, formwork, or bracing system is built by the contractor's experience, without an engineering calculation, because "we have done it this way before." The trap is that the system holds under the average condition, while it has not been engineered for the actual load, the actual material, or the actual support condition, and the first deviation (a heavier pour, a stronger wind, a softer support) exceeds its unknown capacity. The false signal is the system that has worked before; the harm is a collapse of a temporary structure whose capacity was never calculated, because the temporary works were treated as craft rather than engineering.

## Self-Check

- Has the structure been analysed at every critical construction stage for the construction loads and the strength available at that stage?
- Are the temporary works (shoring, formwork, falsework, bracing) engineered to the same standard as the permanent works, with calculations and drawings?
- Are lifting and crane operations engineered for the crane, the lifted element, and the supporting structure, with the rigging and the temporary bracing detailed?
- Does the construction sequence maintain the stability and the load path at every step, with the lateral system and the gravity connections in step with the erection?
- Are demolition, modification, and existing-structure integration engineered for the temporary and permanent conditions, with latent conditions anticipated?
- Are the construction wind, seismic, and environmental loads established for the construction period, and do the temporary bracing and the partial structure resist them?
- Is the construction method statement and the temporary works design documented, reviewed, and approved before the work begins?
- Are the critical stages and the temporary works inspected in the field, with deviations referred for engineering before they are built over?
