# Construction Scheduling and Critical Path Management

Construction scheduling is the engineering of time on the project, where the sequence, the duration, and the dependencies of the activities determine whether the project finishes on time, and where the critical path, the float, and the risk concentration determine where a delay propagates and where a recovery is possible. The schedule is not a calendar; it is a network of dependencies that reflects the construction method, the resource availability, the weather, and the supply chain, and a schedule that does not reflect these is a list of dates that the project will not meet. The harm this skill prevents is a schedule whose critical path is misidentified, whose float is consumed by the wrong activities, or whose risk is concentrated at the end, leading to a project that appears on time until the final activities slip and the completion is impossible to recover. Because time is money and delay is the dominant source of construction claims, the schedule must be engineered, monitored, and managed as a safety- and cost-critical instrument.

## Core Rules

### Build the Schedule From the Construction Method and the Activity Logic

A defensible schedule begins with the construction method: the sequence of the major activities (the site preparation, the foundations, the structure, the enclosure, the systems, the finishes), the logic that connects them (which activity must finish before the next begins, which can run in parallel, which is constrained by a resource or a delivery), and the durations that reflect the actual production rates and the crew sizes. Build the network from the activities and the logic, with the dependencies (finish-to-start, start-to-start, finish-to-finish) that represent the real construction relationships, and the constraints (the delivery dates, the permit dates, the weather windows) that bound the activities. A schedule built from target dates backward, without the method and the logic, is a wish list that the construction will not follow, and the gap between the schedule and the method is where the delay grows.

### Identify the Critical Path and the Near-Critical Paths

The critical path is the longest sequence of dependent activities through the network, and it determines the project duration; a delay on a critical path activity delays the project, while a delay on a non-critical activity consumes float before it delays the project. Identify the critical path by the network analysis, and identify the near-critical paths (the paths with float less than a threshold, commonly 5 to 10 days) that can become critical with a small delay. Manage the critical and the near-critical paths with the most attention, because they are where the project's time risk is concentrated. A schedule whose critical path is misidentified (because the logic is wrong or the float is miscomputed) leads the team to manage the wrong activities, and the real critical path slips while the team focuses elsewhere.

### Estimate Durations Realistically, With the Production Rate and the Risk

The activity durations must reflect the actual production rate (the quantity divided by the crew's daily output), the crew size, the workday and the workweek, the weather allowance, the learning curve, and the risk of interruption. Estimate the durations from the quantity and the production rate, not from a target or a default, and document the basis. For the activities with high variability (the foundations in uncertain ground, the steel erection in high wind, the concrete in cold weather), carry a duration that reflects the variability, or a contingency in the schedule. A schedule built from optimistic durations, without the production rate and the risk, is a schedule that the project will overrun, because the durations do not represent the real work.

### Account for Weather, Season, and External Constraints

The schedule must account for the weather and the season that the construction will encounter, because civil and structural work is weather-sensitive (the earthwork in rain, the concrete in cold or hot weather, the steel and the crane in wind, the paving in temperature). Build the weather allowance into the durations for the weather-sensitive activities, based on the historical weather for the location and the season, and sequence the weather-sensitive work into the favourable season where the schedule allows. For the external constraints (the permit, the delivery, the utility, the inspection), build the constraint into the logic with the date and the dependency, and manage the constraint as a milestone. A schedule that ignores the weather and the constraints is a schedule that will slip at the first weather event or the first delayed delivery, because the real conditions were not in the plan.

### Manage the Float and the Contingency Deliberately

The float on the non-critical activities is a resource, and it must be managed deliberately, not consumed by the early activities or by the contractor's preference. Identify the float on each path, and manage the consumption: the owner's changes should consume the float only with the owner's awareness, the contractor's delays should not consume the owner's float, and the remaining float should be preserved for the risks that have not yet materialised. For the schedule contingency (the time buffer at the project or the phase level), set it deliberately against the risk profile, and do not present the schedule without the contingency as if it were the expected duration. A schedule whose float is consumed early has no buffer for the risks that arrive late, and the project finishes late because the buffer was spent before it was needed.

### Monitor the Progress, Update the Schedule, and Manage the Delays

The schedule is a living instrument, and it must be monitored and updated against the actual progress. At each update, record the actual start and finish of the completed activities, the percent complete of the in-progress activities, and the remaining duration, and recalculate the critical path and the float. Identify the activities that are slipping, the paths that are becoming critical, and the impact on the project completion, and take the recovery action early, while the recovery is still possible. For the delays (the weather, the change, the supply, the differing site condition), document the cause, the impact, and the recovery, and evaluate the time extension or the acceleration against the contract and the schedule. A schedule that is set at the start and never updated is a schedule that the project outgrows, and the team manages the work without a current reference.

### Evaluate Delay, Acceleration, and Time-Extension Claims Against the Schedule

Delay claims are evaluated against the schedule, and the analysis (the time impact analysis, the as-built but-for analysis, or the windows analysis) must be performed on the schedule that represents the work. Identify the delay event, the activities affected, the dependency and the float, and the impact on the critical path, and distinguish the excusable delay (the weather, the differing condition, the owner's change) from the non-excusable (the contractor's mismanagement, the late procurement). For the acceleration (the recovery of the delay by added resources or extended hours), evaluate the feasibility, the cost, and the impact on the work. A delay claim without the schedule analysis is an assertion, not a demonstration, and the schedule is the instrument that converts the assertion into the quantified impact.

## Common Traps

### The Schedule Built From Target Dates

The schedule is built backward from a target completion date, with the durations compressed to fit, without regard to the construction method or the production rate. The trap is that the schedule meets the target on paper, while the durations are shorter than the work requires, and the project slips from the start, because the schedule did not reflect the real work. The false signal is the target-meeting schedule; the harm is a project that is behind from the first activity, with no recovery plan, because the schedule was a target, not a plan.

### The Critical Path Misidentified

The schedule's critical path is identified by the longest duration or the latest dates, without the correct dependency logic, and the real critical path (through a different sequence of activities) is hidden. The trap is that the team manages the apparent critical path, while the real critical path slips unnoticed, and the project delays on a path that no one was watching. The false signal is the highlighted critical path on the schedule; the harm is a project that delays on the unmanaged path, because the logic error hid the real critical sequence.

### The Optimistic Duration Without The Production Rate

The activity durations are set at the optimistic, without the production rate or the crew size, because the optimistic is the target. The trap is that the schedule is tight, while the real production takes longer, and every activity runs over its duration, and the float is consumed across the network. The false signal is the tight, efficient schedule; the harm is a project that consumes its float early and delays on the cumulative overrun of the optimistic durations.

### The Weather Not In The Schedule

The schedule is built without the weather allowance, on the assumption that the weather will be average, and the weather-sensitive activities are scheduled into the adverse season. The trap is that the schedule is feasible in average weather, while the real weather (a wet excavation season, a cold concrete winter, a windy steel erection) delays the activities, and the float is consumed by the weather. The false signal is the weather-independent schedule; the harm is a project that delays on the weather, because the real conditions were not in the plan.

### The Float Consumed Early

The non-critical activities have float, and the contractor uses the float to start the activities late or to extend them, consuming the buffer before the risks arrive. The trap is that the schedule has float on paper, while the float is consumed by the early activities, and the late risks (the delivery delay, the change, the weather) have no buffer, and the project delays. The false signal is the float on the schedule; the harm is a project with no buffer when the risks materialise, because the float was spent rather than preserved.

### The Delay Claim Without The Schedule Analysis

A delay claim is asserted without the schedule analysis, on the basis of the delay event and the asserted impact, without the time impact analysis or the as-built but-for demonstration. The trap is that the claim is asserted, while the schedule does not support the impact (the delayed activity had float, the dependency was not as claimed, the concurrent delay offset the impact), and the claim is not demonstrable. The false signal is the asserted delay; the harm is a claim that cannot be substantiated, or an owner who pays a claim that the schedule does not support, because the analysis was not performed.

## Self-Check

- Is the schedule built from the construction method, the activity logic, and the realistic durations, with the dependencies and the constraints that represent the real work?
- Is the critical path identified by the network analysis, with the near-critical paths identified and managed?
- Are the durations estimated from the production rate and the crew size, with the weather, the learning curve, and the risk reflected?
- Are the weather, the season, and the external constraints (permit, delivery, utility, inspection) built into the durations and the logic?
- Is the float on each path identified, and is its consumption managed deliberately, with the contingency set against the risk profile?
- Is the schedule monitored and updated at regular intervals, with the actual progress, the recalculated critical path, and the recovery action taken early?
- Are the delay, acceleration, and time-extension claims evaluated by the schedule analysis (time impact, as-built but-for, windows), with the cause, the impact, and the recovery documented?
- Is the schedule a living instrument, with the baseline, the updates, and the analyses documented for the project record?
