# Contract Administration and Change Management

Contract administration is the engineering of the contract during construction, where the design is converted into the built work through the contract documents, the submittals, the requests for information, the changes, and the field observations, and where the engineer's role is to interpret the design, to verify the compliance, and to manage the changes without compromising the design intent or the contract balance. The contract is the instrument that allocates the risk, the responsibility, and the payment, and the administration is the day-to-day management of that instrument against the messy reality of construction. The harm this skill prevents is a change that alters the structural design without the engineer's evaluation, a submittal that deviates from the design without being caught, a differing site condition that is absorbed without the contract's risk allocation, or a dispute that escalates because the documentation and the communication failed. Because the contract administration is the engineer's field responsibility and a liability surface, it must be disciplined, documented, and consistent with the contract and the design intent.

## Core Rules

### Administer the Contract as Written, With the Design Intent Preserved

The contract documents (the drawings, the specifications, the general and the special conditions, the addenda) define the work and the obligations, and the administration must enforce them as written, with the design intent preserved through every interpretation and every change. Review every submittal, every request for information (RFI), and every change against the contract documents, and confirm that the response is consistent with the design and the contract. Do not interpret the contract to favour one party or to expedite the work at the expense of the design, because the engineer's role is the faithful administration, not the rewriting of the contract. Where the contract is ambiguous or silent, interpret it in consultation with the owner and the contractor, with the design intent and the contract's risk allocation as the guide, and document the interpretation.

### Review Submittals and RFIs for Compliance and for Design Impact

Submittals (shop drawings, product data, samples) are the contractor's translation of the design into the fabricated and the supplied product, and the review must confirm that the submittal complies with the design. Review the submittal against the contract documents, with the focus on the conformance to the specified standards, the dimensions, the connections, the materials, and the performance, and return the submittal with the approval, the revision, or the rejection, with the specific reasons. For the RFIs, interpret the design and the contract, and respond with the clarification, the correction, or the direction, with the response consistent with the design intent. The review is not a re-design; it is the verification, and a submittal that deviates from the design must be caught and corrected, because the deviation propagates into the built work.

### Process Changes by the Contract, With the Design and the Cost Evaluated

Changes (change orders, construction change directives, field orders) are inevitable, and each must be processed by the contract, with the design impact, the cost, and the schedule evaluated. For a proposed change, evaluate the structural and the civil impact (does the change alter the load, the capacity, the durability, the system), the cost (the quantity, the unit cost, the credit for the deleted work), and the schedule (the time impact, the critical path), and document the evaluation. For the owner-directed change, confirm that the design is revised by the engineer and the cost and the time are adjusted by the contract. For the contractor-proposed change (a substitution, a value engineering, a constructability change), evaluate the impact and the equivalence before the acceptance. A change that is processed without the design evaluation may alter the structure without the engineer's review, and the change becomes a latent defect.

### Document the Differing Site Condition and the Risk Allocation

Differing site conditions (the subsurface or the site condition that differs from the contract's indication) are a primary source of claims, and the contract's differing-site-condition clause allocates the risk. When a differing condition is encountered, document the condition (the nature, the extent, the location, the photographs, the contemporaneous records), notify the owner and the contractor per the contract's notice requirement, and evaluate the impact on the work, the cost, and the schedule. Apply the contract's risk allocation: a Type I condition (differing from the contract's indication) is generally the owner's risk, and a Type II condition (differing from the ordinary in the locality) is allocated by the contract. Do not absorb the differing condition by a field decision or a quiet acceptance, because the risk allocation is the contract's, and the documentation is the basis of the cost and the time adjustment.

### Maintain the Record: Field Observations, Correspondence, and the Document Control

The contract administration is only as defensible as its record, and the record must be maintained contemporaneously, accurately, and completely. Document the field observations (the daily reports, the inspection records, the photographs, the nonconformance reports), the correspondence (the RFIs, the submittals, the change requests, the directives), the meetings (the minutes, the decisions, the action items), and the document control (the revisions, the addenda, the as-builts). The record is the basis of the payment, the change, the claim, and any future investigation, and a missing or a late record cannot be reconstructed reliably. Maintain the record as the work proceeds, not after the fact, because the contemporaneous record is the evidence and the reconstruction is the assertion.

### Manage the Payment Application and the Certification

The engineer's certification of the payment application is the verification that the work has been performed and the materials have been incorporated, and it must be based on the field observation and the record. Review the payment application against the schedule of values, the completed work, the stored materials, and the retainage, and certify the amount that the work supports. Do not certify the work that has not been performed or the materials that have not been incorporated, because the certification is the engineer's representation to the owner and the lender, and an over-certification is a liability. For the work that is nonconforming, withhold the payment or certify with the reservation, and require the correction before the full payment.

### Manage the Dispute and the Claim by the Contract

Disputes and claims are inevitable, and the contract provides the procedure (the notice, the documentation, the negotiation, the mediation, the arbitration, or the litigation). Manage the claim by the contract: require the notice and the documentation, evaluate the merit against the contract and the record, negotiate the resolution in good faith, and escalate per the procedure if the resolution fails. Do not let the dispute fester or the claim accumulate, because the unresolved dispute grows in cost and in disruption, and the early evaluation and the resolution are the engineer's and the owner's best defence. Document the claim, the evaluation, and the resolution, because the record is the basis of the negotiation and the escalation.

## Common Traps

### The Submittal Deviation Not Caught

A submittal (a shop drawing, a product data) deviates from the design in a connection, a material, or a dimension, and the review does not catch the deviation, because the review was cursory or the reviewer was not the design engineer. The trap is that the submittal is approved, while it deviates from the design, and the deviation is built into the work, and the structure does not match the design. The false signal is the approved submittal; the harm is a latent defect that the review was meant to catch, surfacing only when the deviation affects the performance.

### The Change Without The Design Evaluation

A change is processed for the cost and the schedule, but the structural and the civil impact is not evaluated by the engineer, because the change appeared minor. The trap is that the change is approved, while it alters the load, the capacity, or the durability, and the structure is affected in a way that the design did not evaluate. The false signal is the cost- and schedule-evaluated change; the harm is a structural change made without the engineering review, with the impact surfacing as a defect or a failure.

### The Differing Condition Absorbed Without The Notice

A differing site condition is encountered, and the field team absorbs it by a direction or a quiet acceptance, without the notice and the documentation per the contract. The trap is that the work proceeds, while the condition that warrants a cost and a time adjustment is not documented, and the contractor's claim, when it comes, lacks the contemporaneous record. The false signal is the work that proceeds; the harm is a claim that cannot be evaluated or a risk that the owner absorbs without the contract's allocation, because the notice and the documentation were not made.

### The Record Maintained After The Fact

The field observations, the correspondence, and the meetings are documented after the fact, from memory or from scattered notes, because the contemporaneous record was not maintained. The trap is that the record exists, while it is a reconstruction, not a contemporaneous account, and the reconstruction is less reliable and less defensible in a claim or an investigation. The false signal is the complete-looking record; the harm is a record that cannot be relied upon, because it was not made at the time of the events.

### The Payment Certified Without The Verification

The payment application is certified based on the contractor's representation, without the field verification that the work was performed and the materials were incorporated. The trap is that the payment is certified, while the work was not performed or the materials were not incorporated, and the owner and the lender pay for the work that does not exist. The false signal is the certified payment; the harm is an over-payment that is the engineer's liability, and a project whose financial control is lost.

### The Dispute Left To Accumulate

A dispute or a claim is not addressed when it arises, and it is left to accumulate, on the hope that it will resolve itself or that the resolution at the end will be easier. The trap is that the dispute appears to be deferred, while it grows in cost, in disruption, and in resentment, and the resolution at the end is far harder and more expensive. The false signal is the deferred dispute; the harm is a dispute that escalates beyond the contract's manageable resolution, because the early evaluation and the negotiation were not undertaken.

## Self-Check

- Is the contract administered as written, with the design intent preserved through every interpretation and every change?
- Are submittals and RFIs reviewed against the contract documents, with the conformance to the standards, dimensions, materials, and performance verified?
- Are changes processed by the contract, with the structural and civil impact, the cost, and the schedule evaluated and documented?
- Are differing site conditions documented and noticed per the contract, with the risk allocation applied and the impact evaluated?
- Is the record (field observations, correspondence, meetings, document control) maintained contemporaneously, accurately, and completely?
- Is the payment application certified based on the field verification, with the nonconforming work withheld or reserved?
- Are disputes and claims managed by the contract procedure, with the notice, the documentation, the evaluation, and the negotiation undertaken early?
- Is the contract administration documented in a record that supports the payment, the change, the claim, and any future investigation?
