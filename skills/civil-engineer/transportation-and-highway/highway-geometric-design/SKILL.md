# Highway Geometric Design

Highway geometric design fixes the three-dimensional path that vehicles will follow for the life of the facility, and the geometry is the single largest determinant of whether drivers can perceive, react to, and safely traverse the road at the design speed. Once the alignment is built, the curve radii, grades, sight lines, and cross-sections are essentially permanent, and any deficiency becomes a contributing factor in every crash that occurs there for decades. The harm this skill prevents is a road that meets individual dimension criteria but is internally inconsistent, that surprises the driver with a sharp curve after a long tangent, that hides an intersection over a crest, or that places a fixed object where an errant vehicle will leave the road. Geometric design is not a cataloguing of minimum dimensions; it is the engineering of driver expectation and vehicle dynamics into a continuous, forgiving alignment.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Design Speed and Context First, and Design to Consistency

The design speed, selected for the facility class, terrain, and land-use context, governs the minimum radius, superelevation, sight distance, and grade criteria for the entire alignment. More important than any single minimum is consistency: a road designed for 110 km/h must not contain a curve that only supports 70 km/h without abundant warning, because the driver's expectation, set by miles of higher-speed geometry, will not adapt in time. Check design consistency by evaluating the operating speed variation between successive elements; large drops in design or operating speed between adjacent elements are a primary crash predictor and require mitigation, not just compliance with the minimum.

### Design Horizontal Alignment for Continuity and Driver Expectation

The horizontal alignment is a sequence of tangents and curves that must flow. Confirm that curve radii meet or exceed the minimum for the design speed and superelevation rate, that transitions to and from superelevation are gradual and within the runoff length criteria, and that compound curves (where one curve flows into another of different radius) avoid large ratios that surprise the driver. Avoid short tangents between reverse curves that prevent proper steering recovery. Where a curve must be below the design speed minimum, provide advance signing, chevrons, and pavement marking, and document it as a design exception with the engineering justification, because a substandard curve on a high-speed road is a known crash pattern.

### Design Vertical Alignment for Grade, Sight Distance, and Drainage

Vertical alignment is the sequence of grades connected by vertical curves (crest and sag). Confirm that grades are within the maximum for the design speed and terrain, with truck climbing lanes provided where sustained grades reduce heavy-vehicle speeds below the threshold that creates unsafe speed differentials. Confirm that crest vertical curves provide the required stopping sight distance for the design speed, because a crest that hides an obstacle or a queue is a primary crash location. Confirm that sag vertical curves provide headlight sight distance at night and that the curve is long enough to avoid a jarring profile. Ensure that the profile provides positive drainage at the low point of every sag, with inlet capacity sized for the design storm, because a sag that ponds water will hydroplane vehicles in rain.

### Verify Stopping, Decision, Passing, and Intersection Sight Distance

Sight distance is the engineering of the driver's ability to see and react. Stopping sight distance, required at every point on the alignment, must be available to a driver at the design speed to perceive an obstacle and stop; check it across crest curves, into horizontal curves obstructed by walls or foliage, and at night in sags. Decision sight distance, longer than stopping, is required where the driver must make a complex decision, such as a route choice at an interchange or a surprise intersection. Passing sight distance governs the marking of no-passing zones on two-lane roads and must be verified, not assumed. Intersection sight distance must provide a driver on the minor approach enough view of the major road to enter or cross safely; verify the sight triangle is clear of buildings, signs, and landscaping for the design speed.

### Design the Cross-Section for the Facility and the Roadside

Lane width, shoulder width, cross-slope, median, and roadside all contribute to safety and capacity. Confirm lane and shoulder widths meet the criteria for the facility class and traffic, that cross-slope drains water off the travelled way (commonly 1.5 to 2 percent on tangent, with the high side of superelevated curves checked for drainage and ice), and that medians or barriers are provided where warranted by traffic, speed, and crash history. The roadside is the forgiving zone: where a vehicle leaves the road, the clear recovery area should be free of fixed objects (poles, trees, culvert ends) for the recommended distance, with breakaway features, shielding by guardrail or barrier where objects cannot be removed, and side slopes flat enough to prevent rollover. A roadside designed for forgiveness converts run-off crashes into recoverable events.

### Design Intersections and Interchanges for Conflict and Capacity

Intersections concentrate conflicts and are the dominant crash location on most networks. For at-grade intersections, confirm that the layout provides adequate sight distance, turning templates for the design vehicles (including the largest permitted vehicle, with offtracking), channelisation to guide the driver, and storage lengths for turning lanes sized for the design traffic. For interchanges and grade-separated intersections, confirm that the ramp geometry meets the design speed for each ramp type, that the gore area and signing give the driver time to decide, and that acceleration and deceleration lanes provide sufficient length for speed change. Roundabouts, where selected, must be designed for the inscribed circle diameter, entry and exit geometry, and deflection that control speed through the circle; a roundabout with inadequate deflection allows high-speed circulating crashes.

### Document and Justify Design Exceptions

Where a project cannot meet a design criterion, the exception must be documented with the criterion, the constraint that prevents compliance, the alternative considered, the mitigation provided, and the engineering justification. Design exceptions are not failures; they are engineering decisions, but they must be made deliberately and recorded, because an undocumented substandard element is indistinguishable from an error in a later crash investigation. For context-sensitive solutions, balance the geometric criteria against community, environmental, and historical constraints, and document the tradeoffs explicitly so that the residual risk is owned, not hidden.

## Common Traps

### The Curve After The Long Tangent

A road is designed with a long, high-speed tangent and then a curve that meets the minimum radius for a lower speed, because the terrain forced it and the minimum is technically met. The trap is that the curve complies with a criterion, while the driver's expectation, set by the tangent, is for continued high speed and the curve arrives without adequate warning or speed reduction. The false signal is the compliant radius; the harm is a curve with a disproportionate run-off-road crash history, because the geometry failed to manage driver expectation, which is the real safety variable.

### The Hidden Intersection Over The Crest

A vertical crest curve is sized to the minimum stopping sight distance for the design speed, and an intersection is placed just beyond the crest. The trap is that the crest meets the criterion for an unexpected obstacle, while an intersection is a decision point that requires the longer decision sight distance, and the driver cresting the hill has no time to choose a lane or react to a queue. The false signal is the compliant stopping sight distance; the harm is a rear-end and conflict crash pattern at the intersection that no signing can fully correct.

### The Clear Zone That Hides A Rigid Object

A roadside is reviewed for clear width and appears to meet the criteria, but a drainage culvert headwall, a luminaire pole, or a sign foundation sits within the recovery area, accepted because "it is only one object." The trap is that the clear zone reads as compliant in plan, while a single rigid object in the recovery area converts recoverable run-off crashes into severe fixed-object crashes. The false signal is the average clear width; the harm is a roadside that is forgiving everywhere except the one location where an errant vehicle is statistically likely to arrive.

### The Sag That Ponds

A sag vertical curve is sized for headlight sight distance and appears adequate, but the low point has no inlet or an undersized inlet, and the cross-slope is minimal. The trap is that the profile meets the geometric criterion, while the low point collects stormwater and ponds across the travelled way, because the drainage design was treated as separate from the geometry. The false signal is the compliant curve; the harm is recurring hydroplaning crashes in rain at a location the geometry said was safe.

### The Roundabout Without Deflection

A roundabout is selected for an intersection and sized for capacity, but the entry geometry allows a straight-through path through the circle without deflection. The trap is that the roundabout "works" for flow, while the absence of deflection permits vehicles to circulate at high speed, defeating the speed-control and low-conflict purpose that justifies the roundabout. The false signal is the adequate capacity; the harm is high-speed circulating and merging crashes that are more severe than the signalised intersection the roundabout replaced.

### The Substandard Element Without A Documented Exception

A project cannot meet sight distance or lane width at one location because of a building or a right-of-way constraint, the design proceeds with a reduced value, and the reduction is never documented as an exception. The trap is that the road is built and operates, while the substandard element is invisible in the record, and a future crash investigation cannot distinguish an engineering decision from an oversight. The false signal is the completed, operating road; the harm is unowned residual risk that surfaces only when a crash occurs and the project's design choices are scrutinised.

## Self-Check

- Is the design speed established for the facility class and terrain, and is the alignment checked for operating-speed consistency between successive elements?
- Do all horizontal curves meet or exceed the minimum radius for the design speed and superelevation, with transitions, runoff, and compound-curve ratios within criteria?
- Are grades within limits, with climbing lanes where heavy-vehicle speed differential warrants, and are crest and sag vertical curves sized for stopping and headlight sight distance?
- Is stopping sight distance available at every point, with decision sight distance at complex decision points and intersection sight triangles verified clear?
- Does the cross-section meet lane, shoulder, cross-slope, and median criteria, and is the roadside designed for forgiveness with clear zones, breakaway features, and shielding?
- Are intersections and interchanges designed for conflict, sight distance, design-vehicle turning templates, storage, and ramp speed-change lengths?
- For roundabouts, is the inscribed circle, entry and exit geometry, and deflection verified to control circulating speed?
- Are all substandard elements documented as design exceptions with the constraint, alternatives, mitigation, and engineering justification recorded?
