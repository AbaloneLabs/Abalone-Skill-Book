# Steel And Metal Materials Specification

Specifying steel is the act of choosing the grade, the toughness, the weldability, the corrosion protection, and the fatigue class of the metal that will carry the structure's load, and the choices interact: a high-strength grade may lack the toughness for a cold or seismic application, a weldable grade may be required where welding is used, and a fatigue-critical detail demands a class of steel and a class of fabrication that a generic spec will not deliver. Agents who specify "structural steel, grade as available" miss that the grade governs the strength and the ductility, the toughness governs the resistance to brittle fracture at low temperature or high strain rate, the weldability governs whether the connections can be made, and the corrosion protection governs the service life in the environment. This skill covers the judgment exercised while specifying steel and metals, with the goal that the specified material has the strength, toughness, weldability, and protection the application demands, and that the mill certificates and fabrication verify it.

## Core Rules

### Specify The Steel Grade Against Strength, Ductility, And Application

The steel grade, its yield strength, its ultimate strength, and its elongation, must be specified against the application's demands, because a grade's strength is only one of its properties and the ductility, the elongation, and the ratio of ultimate to yield govern the member's behavior beyond first yield. Select a grade with adequate yield strength for the design, but also with adequate ductility, expressed as minimum elongation, for the application, because a high-strength, low-ductility steel may be strong but brittle, and a seismic or fatigue application demands a ductile steel. Avoid high-strength steels where ductility or weldability governs, because the higher strength often comes at the cost of toughness and weldability, and the benefit of the higher strength is lost if the connections cannot be welded or the member fractures. The grade is selected against the full set of demands, not against strength alone.

### Require Toughness For Cold, Impact, And Seismic Service

Steel's toughness, its resistance to brittle fracture, is critical in cold climates, in impact-loaded structures, and in seismic systems, and a steel without adequate toughness can fracture suddenly at a stress below its yield. Specify the Charpy V-notch toughness requirement, with the test temperature and the minimum energy, matched to the application's lowest service temperature and its loading rate, because toughness drops with temperature and with strain rate, and a steel tough at room temperature may be brittle at the service low. Require the toughness for the primary load-carrying members, the tension members, the connection elements, and the seismic fuse elements, because a brittle fracture in any of these is catastrophic. Do not assume a generic grade has adequate toughness, because toughness varies with the heat, the thickness, and the producer, and it must be specified and certified. Toughness is the defense against sudden fracture, and it must be specified where the application demands it.

### Ensure Weldability Through Carbon Equivalent And Preheat

A steel's weldability, its ability to be welded without cracking, is governed by its chemistry, expressed as the carbon equivalent, and by the welding procedure, the preheat, the heat input, and the filler metal, and a steel with a high carbon equivalent requires preheat and controlled procedures to avoid hydrogen-induced cracking. Specify the maximum carbon equivalent for the project, or require the producer to report it, and develop the welding procedure specifications against the actual chemistry, because a generic procedure applied to a high-carbon-equivalent steel cracks the heat-affected zone. Require preheat for thick members, for high-carbon-equivalent steels, and for low-ambient-temperature welding, because preheat slows the cooling and allows the hydrogen to diffuse out, preventing the delayed cracking that can occur days after the weld. Weldability is a property of the steel and the procedure together, and both must be specified and verified, because a steel that cannot be welded soundly cannot be joined soundly.

### Specify Bolt Grades And Connection Materials Consistently

Bolts come in grades with different strengths, different ductilities, and different tightening requirements, and the connection design assumes a specific grade, so the specification and the procurement must deliver that grade. Specify the bolt grade, the A325 or A490 or equivalent, the finish, the galvanizing or the weathering, and the tightening method, the pretension or the snug-tight, matching the connection design, because a bolt of the wrong grade or the wrong finish has different strength and different corrosion behavior. Specify the washers, the nuts, and the installation requirements, because the bolt's pretension depends on the nut's lubrication and the washer's bearing, and a connection assembled with mismatched hardware does not develop its design pretension. Require the lot certification, because bolt strength varies and the design assumes the certified minimum. The connection materials are part of the structural specification, and they must be specified as completely as the steel.

### Design Corrosion Protection For The Service Environment

Steel corrodes, and the corrosion protection, paint, galvanizing, weathering steel, or cathodic protection, must be designed for the service environment and the intended life, because a protection system suited to a dry interior fails in a marine exterior within years. Classify the environment, rural, urban, industrial, marine, and the exposure, sheltered or open, and select the protection system with the number of coats, the thickness, and the surface preparation that the environment demands, because the surface preparation is as important as the paint and a poorly prepared surface sheds the best coating. Consider weathering steel for atmospheric exposure where the patina forms and protects, but not where the steel is wet continuously or buried, because weathering steel needs wet-dry cycling to form its patina and corrodes like ordinary steel without it. The corrosion protection is part of the structural design, because an unprotected steel loses section and capacity over its life.

### Control Fatigue Through Detail Class And Material Quality

Fatigue, the initiation and growth of cracks under cyclic load, is governed by the detail's stress concentration and the number of load cycles, and a fatigue-critical application demands both a fatigue-classified detail and a material free of initial defects. Design the details to a fatigue class appropriate to the loading, avoiding the geometric stress concentrations that lower the class, because a detail's fatigue life is set by its geometry and a sharp transition or a poor weld profile drops the class by an order of magnitude. Specify the weld profile, the NDT inspection, and the material's freedom from laminations and inclusions for fatigue-critical members, because an initial defect shortens the fatigue life and the material and the fabrication must not introduce one. Fatigue is a detail and material problem, and both must be controlled, because a fatigue failure is sudden and occurs at stresses well below yield.

### Review Mill Test Reports Against The Specification

The steel arrives with mill test reports, certifying the chemistry and the mechanical properties of each heat, and the engineer must review the reports against the specification before the steel is accepted into the work. Verify the yield strength, the ultimate strength, the elongation, the carbon equivalent, and the Charpy toughness against the specified minimums, heat by heat, because the reports certify the heat and the steel's properties vary within the heat. Reject steel whose reports do not meet the specification, or require additional testing, because steel accepted below spec is built into the structure and the deficiency surfaces only under load. Track the heat numbers to the members, so that a deficient heat can be identified and remediated if a later test reveals a problem. The mill report review is the verification that the steel is what was specified, and it must be done before fabrication, because steel built into the structure cannot be returned.

## Common Traps

### High-Strength Steel Specified Where Ductility Or Weldability Governs

The engineer specifies a high-strength steel to reduce member sizes and weight, without checking the ductility, the toughness, or the weldability, and the higher strength comes at the cost of the properties that govern the application. The mechanism is that high-strength steels achieve their strength through alloying or heat treatment that can reduce the elongation, the toughness, and the weldability, and a seismic, fatigue, or welded application demands those properties more than the strength. The false signal is that the steel "is stronger." The harm is that the member fractures in a seismic event because it lacks ductility, or the welds crack because the steel is not weldable, or the connection fails by brittle fracture because the toughness is inadequate, and the strength benefit is lost in the failure. The grade must be selected against the full set of demands, with ductility, toughness, and weldability verified, because a strong but brittle or unweldable steel fails in the modes the design assumed away.

### Toughness Not Specified For Cold Or Seismic Service

The engineer specifies the steel grade without a Charpy toughness requirement, assuming the grade's nominal properties suffice, in a cold climate or a seismic application where brittle fracture is a risk. The mechanism is that toughness varies with the heat, the thickness, and the producer, and a steel that meets the grade's strength may have low toughness, and the low toughness combined with cold temperature or seismic strain rate produces brittle fracture at a stress below yield. The false signal is that the steel "meets the grade." The harm is that a member or a connection fractures suddenly in a cold snap or an earthquake, with no warning, because the toughness that would have prevented the brittle fracture was never specified or certified. The Charpy toughness must be specified with the test temperature and minimum energy for cold, impact, or seismic service, and certified per heat, because toughness is the defense against sudden fracture and it must be required, not assumed.

### Generic Welding Procedure Applied To A High-Carbon Steel

The engineer or the fabricator applies a generic welding procedure, developed for ordinary carbon steel, to a high-carbon-equivalent steel or a thick member, without adjusting the preheat or the heat input. The mechanism is that a high-carbon-equivalent steel or a thick section cools quickly after welding, trapping hydrogen and forming a brittle, cracked heat-affected zone, and the generic procedure's preheat and heat input are inadequate to prevent it. The false signal is that the weld "looks sound." The harm is hydrogen-induced cracking, which can appear days after the weld, propagating through the heat-affected zone and fracturing the connection under load, because the procedure did not account for the steel's chemistry or the thickness. The welding procedure must be developed against the actual carbon equivalent and thickness, with preheat and heat input qualified by procedure qualification records, because a generic procedure applied to a high-carbon steel cracks the weld.

### Bolt Grade Or Finish Substituted Without Recalculation

The contractor substitutes a different bolt grade or finish, a lower grade for economy or a galvanized finish for a weathering-steel connection, without engineering review, and the connection's capacity or behavior changes. The mechanism is that the connection design assumed a specific bolt grade's strength and a specific finish's behavior, and a substitute changes the shear or tensile capacity, the slip resistance, or the corrosion compatibility, and the change is invisible until the connection is loaded. The false signal is that the bolts "fit and are tightened." The harm is that a lower-grade bolt shears under design load, or a galvanized bolt in a weathering connection creates a galvanic cell that accelerates corrosion, because the substitute was not evaluated against the design. Bolt substitutions must be reviewed and approved, with the connection recalculated, because a bolt of a different grade or finish is a different connection.

### Corrosion Protection Under-Specified For The Environment

The engineer specifies a generic paint system, primer and one coat, regardless of the environment, and the system is applied in a marine or industrial exposure where it fails within years. The mechanism is that the coating system's life is governed by the environment, the surface preparation, and the number and thickness of coats, and a system suited to a mild interior provides inadequate protection in an aggressive exterior, and the steel corrodes beneath the failing coating. The false signal is that the steel "is painted." The harm is that the coating fails, the steel corrodes, the section loses capacity, and the structure requires blasting and recoating within a fraction of its design life, at a cost far exceeding the original coating. The corrosion protection must be designed for the environment, with the surface preparation, the coat count, the thickness, and the system type matched to the exposure and the intended life, because a generic coating in an aggressive environment is no protection.

### Fatigue Detail Class Ignored On A Cyclically Loaded Member

The engineer designs a connection or a detail on a cyclically loaded member, a bridge, a crane runway, or a machine support, without classifying the detail for fatigue or controlling the geometry that sets the class. The mechanism is that fatigue life is governed by the detail's stress concentration, and a sharp transition, an attachment, or a poor weld profile lowers the fatigue class by an order of magnitude, reducing the life from decades to months, and the design's nominal stress check does not capture the detail's class. The false signal is that the member "is adequate for the stress." The harm is that a crack initiates at the detail, grows under the cyclic load, and fractures the member suddenly, because the detail's fatigue class was the governing variable and it was never considered. Fatigue-critical details must be classified, the geometry controlled to maintain the class, and the weld profile and NDT specified, because fatigue is a detail problem and a poorly classified detail fails regardless of the nominal stress.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the steel grade selected against strength, ductility, and application, with high-strength steels avoided where ductility or weldability governs?
- [ ] Is Charpy V-notch toughness specified with test temperature and minimum energy for cold, impact, or seismic service, and certified per heat?
- [ ] Is the carbon equivalent controlled or reported, and are welding procedures qualified against the actual chemistry with preheat for thick or high-carbon members?
- [ ] Are bolt grades, finishes, washers, nuts, and tightening methods specified to match the connection design, with substitutions requiring recalculation?
- [ ] Is the corrosion protection designed for the environment and intended life, with surface preparation, coat count, and thickness matched to the exposure?
- [ ] Are fatigue-critical details classified, geometry controlled to maintain the class, and weld profile and NDT specified for cyclically loaded members?
- [ ] Are mill test reports reviewed heat by heat against the specified minimums before fabrication, with heat numbers tracked to members?
- [ ] Is weathering steel used only where wet-dry cycling occurs, and not in continuous wet or buried conditions?
