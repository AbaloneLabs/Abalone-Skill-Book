# Asphalt Pavement And Geosynthetics

Pavement design is the layering of materials, asphalt, base, subbase, and subgrade, each with a stiffness and a strength, to distribute the wheel load to the soil without exceeding any layer's capacity over the design traffic, and geosynthetics, geotextiles and geogrids, are the tools that separate, filter, drain, and reinforce those layers when the soils or the drainage would otherwise fail. Agents who size a pavement by rule of thumb and stop miss that the real questions are about the traffic's equivalent single-axle loads, the subgrade's resilient modulus, the base quality, the drainage that preserves the subgrade strength, and the geosynthetic's function, separation, filtration, drainage, or reinforcement, which determines the product and the specification. This skill covers the judgment exercised while designing pavements and specifying geosynthetics, with the goal that the pavement carries the design traffic to its design life, the drainage preserves the subgrade, and the geosynthetic performs its intended function rather than a function it was not selected for.

## Core Rules

### Design The Pavement Against Traffic, Subgrade, And Climate

The pavement section is designed against three inputs: the design traffic, expressed as equivalent single-axle loads over the design life; the subgrade's support, expressed as the resilient modulus or the California bearing ratio; and the climate, which governs the temperature that affects the asphalt's stiffness and the moisture that affects the subgrade's strength. Determine the design traffic from the traffic count, the axle loads, the growth, and the design life, because the traffic is the load and the section must carry it. Determine the subgrade support from testing across the alignment, because the subgrade varies and the weak locations govern, not the average. Account for the climate, because a pavement in a hot climate needs a stiffer asphalt to resist rutting, and a pavement in a wet climate needs drainage to preserve the subgrade. The section is designed against the specific traffic, subgrade, and climate, not against a generic standard.

### Select The Layer Materials For Their Function And Quality

Each pavement layer has a function, the asphalt to distribute the load and provide a wearing surface, the base to provide stiffness and drainage, the subbase to extend the support, and the materials must be selected for their function and quality. Specify the asphalt mix with the binder grade matched to the climate, the aggregate gradation, and the volumetric properties, because the binder grade governs the rutting and cracking resistance and the gradation governs the durability. Specify the base as crushed, well-graded aggregate with the required CBR or resilient modulus, because the base's stiffness distributes the load and a weak base fails under traffic. Specify the subbase where the subgrade is weak, with lower quality but adequate material, because the subbase extends the support economically. The layer materials are engineered, not defaulted, and their quality determines the pavement's performance.

### Provide Drainage To Preserve The Subgrade Strength

Water is the enemy of pavement, softening the subgrade, degrading the base, and stripping the asphalt, and the drainage is as important as the section thickness, because a saturated subgrade has a fraction of its dry strength and the pavement designed for the dry strength fails when the subgrade is wet. Design the surface drainage to shed runoff from the pavement, the subsurface drainage to intercept and carry the groundwater, and the internal drainage to drain the base, because each addresses a different water source. Slope the surface, provide ditches and underdrains, and use a free-draining base or a drainage layer, because water that stands in the pavement destroys it within a season. Lower the design subgrade strength to the wet condition, because the pavement must perform when the subgrade is at its weakest, which is when it is saturated. Drainage is a primary pavement element, because a well-drained pavement on a moderate subgrade outlasts a thick pavement on a saturated one.

### Select The Geosynthetic For Its Intended Function

Geosynthetics perform different functions, separation between a soft subgrade and a granular base, filtration to allow water flow while retaining soil, drainage to convey water within the plane, and reinforcement to add tensile strength to the soil, and the product must be selected for the intended function, because a product suited to one function may not perform another. Select a geotextile separator where a soft subgrade would contaminate the base, with the apparent opening size and the permittivity for the filtration that accompanies the separation. Select a geotextile filter where water flows from a fine soil into a coarse drain, with the opening size small enough to retain the soil and the permittivity large enough to pass the water. Select a geogrid or a high-strength geotextile for reinforcement where the soil's tensile strength is inadequate, with the tensile modulus and the pullout resistance to mobilize the reinforcement. The function determines the product and the specification, and a geosynthetic selected for the wrong function does not perform.

### Design The Geosynthetic Reinforcement For The Mechanism

Where the geosynthetic provides reinforcement, the design must address the mechanism: the reinforcement mobilizes its tensile strength through the soil-reinforcement interaction, and the strength, the modulus, the pullout resistance, and the spacing must be designed for the load and the failure mechanism. Design the reinforcement for the bearing capacity improvement, the lateral spreading restraint, the slope stability, or the traffic-load distribution, depending on the application, because each mechanism mobilizes the reinforcement differently and requires different properties. Check the pullout resistance at the reinforcement's ends, where the embedment must be long enough to anchor the tensile force, and the creep of the polymer over the design life, because geosynthetics creep under sustained load and the long-term strength is below the short-term. The reinforcement is a designed element, with its properties and geometry engineered to the application, not a generic layer added for "extra support."

### Specify Construction Practices That Preserve The Design

The pavement and the geosynthetic are built, and the construction practices must preserve the designed properties, because a well-designed pavement built on a rutted subgrade or a contaminated base fails regardless of the design. Specify the subgrade preparation, the compaction to the required density and moisture, and the proof rolling to detect soft spots before the base is placed, because a soft subgrade rutted under construction fails the pavement. Specify the base placement and compaction to the required density, because a loose base does not provide its design stiffness. Specify the geosynthetic installation, the overlap, the anchoring, and the protection from damage during fill placement, because a torn or displaced geosynthetic does not perform. Specify the asphalt placement temperature, compaction, and joint construction, because a cold or under-compacted asphalt ruts and cracks. The construction specification is what delivers the design, and it must require the practices that preserve the layers' properties.

### Review Test Results And Accept Against The Specification

The materials are tested, the subgrade density, the base gradation and density, the asphalt volumetrics and the in-place density, and the geosynthetic's certification, and the results must be reviewed against the specification before acceptance, because the test is the verification that the built pavement matches the design. Review the subgrade and base density tests against the required, location by location, because the weak locations govern and a section accepted with low densities fails under traffic. Review the asphalt mix volumetrics, the binder content, the air voids, and the in-place density, because the volumetrics govern the durability and the density governs the strength. Review the geosynthetic's certification against the specified properties, because the product delivered must be the product designed. The acceptance is the gate that ensures the built pavement is the designed pavement, and it must be done against the project's specification, not approved by rote.

## Common Traps

### The Rule-Of-Thumb Section For A Specific Traffic And Subgrade

The engineer applies a standard section, a fixed thickness of asphalt and base, regardless of the traffic or the subgrade, and the section is over-designed for light traffic on good soil and under-designed for heavy traffic on weak soil. The mechanism is that the pavement's required thickness is proportional to the traffic and inversely proportional to the subgrade strength, and a fixed section ignores both, providing either wasted material or premature failure. The false signal is that the section "is standard." The harm is that the heavy-traffic pavement on a weak subgrade fails within a fraction of its design life, rutting, cracking, and requiring reconstruction, because the section was not designed for the actual loads and support. The section must be designed against the specific traffic and subgrade, with the equivalent single-axle loads and the resilient modulus, because a rule-of-thumb section is a guess that fails where it matters.

### Drainage Omitted, Subgrade Strength Assumed Dry

The engineer designs the pavement for the subgrade's dry strength, without providing drainage, and the subgrade saturates in the wet season and loses most of its strength. The mechanism is that the subgrade's resilient modulus drops dramatically when saturated, and a pavement designed for the dry modulus is under-designed for the wet condition that will occur, because water enters the pavement from rain, groundwater, and capillary action. The false signal is that the subgrade "was tested at a strength." The harm is that the pavement ruts and fails in the first wet season, because the subgrade strength that the design relied on is gone when the subgrade is saturated, and the failure is rapid under traffic. The drainage must be provided, surface and subsurface, and the design subgrade strength must reflect the wet condition, because a pavement designed for a dry subgrade that becomes wet is a pavement designed for a condition that will not persist.

### The Geosynthetic Selected Without A Function

The engineer specifies "a geotextile" or "a geogrid" without defining its function, and the contractor supplies whatever is available, which may not perform the intended separation, filtration, or reinforcement. The mechanism is that geosynthetics are function-specific, and a heavy separation geotextile may have inadequate permittivity for filtration, or a filtration fabric may lack the tensile strength for reinforcement, and a product supplied without a function specification may serve none of them well. The false signal is that a geosynthetic "is specified." The harm is that the subgrade contaminates the base because the separator's opening size is wrong, or the drain clogs because the filter retains no soil, or the reinforcement pulls out because its strength or embedment is inadequate, and the pavement or the embankment fails in the mode the geosynthetic was meant to prevent. The function must be defined and the product selected for it, with the properties specified, because a geosynthetic without a function is a layer that does nothing.

### Reinforcement Designed For Short-Term Strength, Creep Ignored

The engineer designs a geosynthetic-reinforced slope or retaining wall using the reinforcement's short-term tensile strength, without reducing it for creep over the design life. The mechanism is that geosynthetics are polymers that creep under sustained load, and the long-term strength available over a 50- or 100-year design life is a fraction of the short-term ultimate, and designing at the short-term strength over-estimates the available reinforcement by a large factor. The false signal is that the reinforcement "has the strength." The harm is that the reinforced slope or wall creeps over the years, the reinforcement elongates, the face bulges, and the structure eventually fails, because the long-term strength was the governing property and it was not used. The reinforcement must be designed with the long-term strength, derived from creep testing and reduced for the design life, because a polymer's strength over decades is far below its short-term test value.

### Subgrade Compaction And Proof Roll Skipped

The engineer specifies the pavement section and the contractor places the base and asphalt, without adequately compacting or proof-rolling the subgrade, and a soft spot remains beneath the pavement. The mechanism is that the subgrade's strength assumes a compacted, uniform condition, and a soft spot, an uncompacted area or a wet pocket, has a far lower strength and the pavement fails locally over it under traffic, because the design assumed a uniform support that the construction did not deliver. The false signal is that the subgrade "is graded." The harm is that the pavement ruts and cracks over the soft spot within the first traffic season, requiring patching or reconstruction, because the local failure was caused by an unverified subgrade condition. The subgrade must be compacted to the required density and proof-rolled to detect soft spots before the base is placed, because the pavement's performance depends on the uniform support the proof roll verifies.

### Asphalt Density Accepted Without Volumetric Review

The inspector accepts the asphalt based on in-place density alone, without reviewing the mix volumetrics, the binder content, and the air voids, and a dense mix with the wrong binder or gradation is accepted. The mechanism is that the asphalt's performance depends on both the density and the volumetrics, and a mix that is dense but has low binder or high air voids is brittle and cracks, while a mix with high binder or low voids ruts, and the density alone does not reveal these. The false signal is that the density "meets spec." The harm is that the pavement cracks or ruts prematurely, because the volumetrics were wrong even though the density was right, and the failure surfaces within the warranty period or shortly after. The asphalt must be accepted on both the in-place density and the mix volumetrics, with the binder content, air voids, and gradation reviewed against the job mix formula, because density without volumetrics is half the verification.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the pavement section designed against the specific design traffic, subgrade resilient modulus, and climate, not a rule-of-thumb standard?
- [ ] Are the layer materials, asphalt binder grade and gradation, base quality, and subbase, selected for their function and quality?
- [ ] Is drainage provided, surface and subsurface, and is the design subgrade strength reduced to the wet condition?
- [ ] Is the geosynthetic selected for a defined function, separation, filtration, drainage, or reinforcement, with the properties specified for that function?
- [ ] Where reinforcement is the function, is the design based on the mechanism, with pullout resistance, embedment, and long-term creep-reduced strength?
- [ ] Do the construction specifications require subgrade compaction, proof rolling, base density, geosynthetic installation, and asphalt placement practices that preserve the design?
- [ ] Are subgrade and base densities, asphalt volumetrics and in-place density, and geosynthetic certifications reviewed against the specification before acceptance?
- [ ] Is the asphalt accepted on both density and volumetrics, with the binder content, air voids, and gradation checked against the job mix formula?
