# Subsurface Investigation Program Planning

Subsurface investigation planning is the engineering of how a project will learn what lies beneath the surface before it commits a foundation, an excavation, or an earthwork to the ground, and it is the single most important determinant of geotechnical risk on the project. The investigation is the only window into the soil that will support the structure, and the number, depth, spacing, and method of the borings and soundings determine whether that window is wide enough to see the controlling condition or whether it misses the soft layer, the buried organics, the perched water, or the rock that will govern the design and the cost. The harm this skill prevents is an investigation that is too sparse to detect the variable or critical condition, leading to a design based on assumed ground that does not match the real ground, with claims, delays, and in the worst cases failures when the construction encounters what the investigation should have found. Because the investigation is the foundation of every geotechnical decision, the program must be planned against the project's actual uncertainty, not against a default spacing or a budget.

## Core Rules

### Define the Investigation Objectives Against the Project and the Risk

Before any field work, define what the investigation must establish: the soil profile and the bearing stratum for the foundation type; the depth to rock and the rock quality; the groundwater level, the perched water, and the seasonal fluctuation; the presence of weak, compressible, expansive, collapsible, or contaminated soil; the slope stability and the earthwork borrow or fill characterisation; and any project-specific geohazard (karst, liquefiable soil, fault rupture). Rank the objectives by the consequence of missing them, because the investigation must be densest and deepest where the unknown matters most. An investigation planned without explicit objectives will default to a generic spacing that may be adequate for a simple building and badly inadequate for a complex or high-risk site.

### Determine Boring Number, Spacing, and Layout by the Project Scale and the Soil Variability

The number and spacing of borings must reflect the project footprint, the structural arrangement, and the expected soil variability, not a fixed default. For a building, locate borings at the corners and the centre of the footprint and at the heavily loaded elements, with a spacing commonly 15 to 30 metres for uniform sites and closer for variable sites or critical structures; for a bridge or a retaining wall, locate borings at each substructure unit; for a linear project (road, pipeline, levee), space borings along the alignment with closer spacing at the crossings and the known variable zones. Confirm that the layout covers the footprint and the zone of influence of the foundations, not just the building outline, because the settlement and the stability depend on the soil within and beyond the footprint. Increase the density where the geology, the prior land use, or the preliminary soundings indicate variability, because uniform spacing on variable ground will miss the controlling condition between borings.

### Determine Boring Depth by the Foundation Type and the Stress Influence Zone

The depth of each boring must extend below the zone that the foundation will influence, so that the bearing stratum and any weaker layer below it are characterised. For shallow foundations, extend borings to a depth of 1.5 to 2 times the footing width below the bearing elevation, or to a competent stratum, whichever is deeper, so that the settlement analysis can evaluate the full stress bulb. For deep foundations, extend borings below the anticipated pile or shaft tip, plus a margin to confirm the bearing stratum thickness and to detect a weaker layer below. For excavations and retaining walls, extend borings below the excavation base and the wall toe to evaluate base stability, heave, and the passive resistance. A boring that stops at the bearing elevation has not verified the stratum that the foundation depends on, and a weaker layer below the tip is a common cause of settlement and bearing failure that the shallow investigation missed.

### Select Drilling, Sampling, and In-Situ Testing Methods for the Soil and the Design Parameter

Select the drilling method (hollow-stem auger, mud rotary, casing advance, or sonic) suited to the soil, the groundwater, and the access, and the sampling method that will recover the soil in the condition the design needs. For cohesive soil, take undisturbed Shelby-tube samples for strength and consolidation testing; for cohesionless soil, rely on the standard penetration test (SPT) or the cone penetration test (CPT) for in-situ density and strength; for rock, core with the appropriate barrel and assess recovery and RQD. Add in-situ tests where laboratory testing is insufficient: vane shear for soft clay, pressuremeter or dilatometer for in-situ modulus, CPT for continuous profiling and liquefaction assessment, and pumping or packer tests for permeability. Match the method to the design parameter: a settlement analysis needs undisturbed samples for consolidation, a bearing analysis needs in-situ strength, and a liquefaction analysis needs CPT or SPT data at the relevant depth.

### Characterise Groundwater Deliberately and Over Time

Groundwater is among the most consequential and most poorly characterised site conditions, and a single reading at the end of drilling is often misleading because the drilling fluid masks the true level and because the level fluctuates seasonally. Install piezometers or observation wells at selected borings, let them equilibrate, and read them over time to establish the seasonal high and the perched water condition. Confirm the measurement against the regional hydrogeology and the evidence of seepage or staining in the samples. A design that uses the end-of-drilling water level as the design groundwater will, in many cases, underestimate the seasonal high and design the foundation, the drainage, or the uplift for a water condition that the site exceeds each year.

### Address Project-Specific Hazards and Contamination

For sites with known or suspected hazards, add the investigation elements that address them: liquefaction assessment (CPT or SPT with the soil type and the groundwater) in seismic regions; karst investigation (closely spaced borings, geophysics, and proof-boring at each footing) on soluble rock; expansive or collapsible soil identification (index tests and swell-collapse tests on the surficial soil); fault rupture investigation (trenching and dating) near active faults; and contamination assessment (phase II sampling and laboratory analysis) on sites with prior industrial or storage use. These hazards carry consequences far beyond the routine settlement and bearing, and the investigation must be tailored to detect them, because a routine investigation will not.

### Document the Investigation and Carry the Uncertainty Into the Design

The investigation report must document the objectives, the methods, the locations and depths, the soil and rock encountered, the groundwater measurements, the laboratory and in-situ test results, and the geotechnical interpretation, with the cross sections that show the interpreted profile between borings. Critically, the report must state the uncertainty that remains: the conditions inferred between borings, the variability observed, and the parameters that carry wider uncertainty. The design must then carry that uncertainty, by conservative parameters, by field verification during construction, or by additional investigation if the construction exposes conditions that the program did not find. An investigation that reports a single, definitive profile without acknowledging the interpolation between borings has concealed the uncertainty that the design must manage.

## Common Traps

### The Default Spacing On Variable Ground

The investigation uses a default boring spacing (for example, 30 metres) on a site whose geology or prior use indicates high variability, and the design proceeds on the resulting profile. The trap is that the spacing meets a nominal standard, while the variable ground between the borings contains a soft layer, a buried channel, or an organic deposit that no boring found, and the foundation encounters it during construction. The false signal is the compliant, regular boring layout; the harm is a design based on ground that does not represent the real site, with claims, delays, and redesign when the construction exposes what the investigation missed.

### The Boring That Stops At The Bearing Elevation

The borings extend to the anticipated bearing elevation and stop, on the assumption that the stratum at that depth is the design bearing layer. The trap is that the stratum at the bearing elevation is characterised, while a softer or more compressible layer below it, within the stress influence zone, is not, and the foundation settles or punches into the uncharacterised layer. The false signal is the boring at the design depth; the harm is settlement or bearing failure driven by a layer the investigation did not reach, because the depth was set by the foundation elevation rather than the stress influence zone.

### The End-Of-Drilling Water Level

The groundwater is recorded at the end of drilling, and that level is used as the design groundwater, without piezometers or seasonal measurement. The trap is that the level reflects the drilling fluid and the short time since circulation, while the true seasonal high water table is higher, often substantially, and the foundation, the drainage, or the uplift is designed for a water condition the site exceeds each year. The false signal is the recorded water level; the harm is a foundation that floods, an uplift that exceeds the design, or a bearing capacity that drops below the design value at the seasonal high.

### The Wrong Method For The Parameter

The investigation uses SPT and visual classification for a settlement analysis on soft clay, without undisturbed sampling or consolidation testing. The trap is that the SPT and the visual classification give a rough strength, while the settlement on soft clay is governed by the consolidation parameters that only an undisturbed sample and an oedometer test can provide, and the predicted settlement carries an uncertainty of a factor of two or more. The false signal is the completed, reported investigation; the harm is a settlement prediction that is far from the real settlement, because the method did not match the parameter the design needed.

### The Routine Investigation On A Hazard Site

A site in a seismic region, on karst, or with prior industrial use is investigated with a routine program, and the project-specific hazard is not assessed. The trap is that the investigation characterises the routine bearing and settlement, while the liquefaction, the sinkhole, or the contamination, which carry the dominant risk and cost, are not evaluated. The false signal is the complete-looking routine report; the harm is a hazard that surfaces during or after construction, with consequences (liquefaction settlement, sinkhole collapse, contamination discovery) that the routine investigation was never designed to find.

### The Definitive Profile That Hides The Interpolation

The investigation report presents a single, interpreted soil profile across the site, with layers drawn between borings, and the design uses the profile as if it were measured at every point. The trap is that the profile looks continuous and definitive, while the layers between borings are interpolated, and the real ground may differ in ways the interpolation cannot capture. The false signal is the smooth cross section; the harm is a design that treats an interpretation as a measurement, with no allowance for the uncertainty that the interpolation concealed.

## Self-Check

- Are the investigation objectives defined against the project type, the foundation, and the consequence of missing each condition?
- Are the boring number, spacing, and layout set by the project footprint, the structural arrangement, and the expected variability, with increased density where variability or risk is high?
- Do the boring depths extend below the foundation stress influence zone (1.5 to 2 times the footing width for shallow, below the tip for deep, below the base for excavations)?
- Are the drilling, sampling, and in-situ testing methods selected to provide the design parameters (strength, consolidation, density, modulus, liquefaction resistance) the analysis requires?
- Is the groundwater characterised by piezometers read over time, with the seasonal high and perched water established rather than inferred from end-of-drilling readings?
- Are project-specific hazards (liquefaction, karst, expansive or collapsible soil, fault rupture, contamination) addressed by tailored investigation elements?
- Does the report document the objectives, methods, locations, results, and interpretation, with cross sections showing the interpolated profile?
- Does the report and the design carry the residual uncertainty, with conservative parameters, field verification, or additional investigation where the construction may expose uncharacterised conditions?
