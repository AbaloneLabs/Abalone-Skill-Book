# Fall Protection and Working at Heights

Falls are the leading cause of construction fatalities, and the judgment problem is that fall protection is governed by a hierarchy of controls that most crews apply backwards — reaching first for a harness (active, failure-prone, dependent on the user) instead of a guardrail (passive, reliable) or elimination of the hazard altogether. Agents tend to treat fall protection as "wear a harness," but a harness is the last line of defense, and a harness misused (a lanyard too long, an anchor too weak, a D-ring over the edge creating swing) provides an illusion of protection while delivering a lethal fall or suspension trauma after the fall is arrested. The harm this skill prevents is the fall fatality and the post-arrest death from suspension trauma that follows an otherwise successful catch. This skill covers the 6-foot construction trigger, the hierarchy of controls, guardrail and safety-net specifications, personal fall arrest system design (anchor strength, free fall, deceleration, swing fall), the distinction between arrest, positioning, and restraint, controlled access zones, leading edge and roof work, overhead hoisting, and prompt rescue planning. It is a high-stakes domain: errors kill, and the agent must defer to a qualified person (per OSHA, one with a degree or extensive knowledge in the relevant field) for engineered anchors and lifelines, and to competent persons and OSHA-authorized trainers for program and inspection.

## Core Rules

### Apply the 6-Foot Trigger and the Hierarchy of Controls in Order

In construction, OSHA 29 CFR 1926 Subpart M requires fall protection at heights of 6 feet or more to a lower level, and the protection must come from a guardrail, safety net, or personal fall arrest system (PFAS), or a combination. The hierarchy of controls dictates the order of preference: first eliminate the hazard (do the work at ground level, prefabricate, use a tool from below); then passive systems (guardrails, covers, parapets) that protect without user action; then active systems (PFAS, positioning, restraint) that depend on correct user behavior; then administrative controls (controlled access zones, monitors) that depend on discipline. The disciplined approach is to push up the hierarchy: a guardrail is more reliable than a harness because it cannot be forgotten or mis-worn. The trap is defaulting to PFAS because it is cheaper or faster, when a guardrail or engineered platform would protect every worker passively.

### Specify Guardrails, Safety Nets, and Hole Covers to Standard

A guardrail system must have a top rail at 42 inches (plus or minus 3 inches), a midrail midway between the top rail and the working surface, and a toeboard where tools or material could fall, with the top rail capable of withstanding 200 pounds of outward/downward force and the midrail 150 pounds. Hole and opening covers must support at least twice the weight of employees and equipment, be secured against displacement, and be marked "Hole" or "Do Not Remove." Safety nets must be installed as close under the work as practical and within 30 feet below, with mesh and border rope meeting strength criteria, and must be drop-tested or certified. The trap is a guardrail built of substandard material (PVC pipe, undersized lumber), a cover that is unmarked and gets walked over, or a net placed too far below the work. The disciplined rule is to build passive systems to spec and to inspect them.

### Design the Personal Fall Arrest System for the Actual Fall Conditions

A PFAS has three load-bearing elements: the anchor, the body support (harness), and the connecting means (lanyard or self-retracting lifeline), and each must be matched to the fall. Anchors must support 5,000 pounds per worker attached (or be designed by a qualified person to a two-times safety factor), and must be above the worker to minimize free fall. Free fall must be limited to 6 feet (or less where the lanyard or SRL specifies), and deceleration distance is limited to 3.5 feet; the total fall clearance must be calculated as free fall plus deceleration plus harness stretch plus the worker's height below the D-ring plus a safety margin, or the worker will hit the lower level even though the system "caught" the fall. Swing fall (the pendulum hazard when the anchor is not directly overhead) must be eliminated by keeping the anchor within 30 degrees of plumb from the worker. The trap is a fixed-length lanyard on a low anchor that produces a swing into a structure, or a fall clearance calculation that omits the safety margin. The disciplined approach is to calculate fall clearance for every setup and to keep anchors overhead.

### Distinguish Fall Arrest From Positioning, Restraint, and Suspension

The four harness-based systems are different and not interchangeable. Fall arrest stops a fall after it begins (anchor 5,000 lb, full-body harness, shock-absorbing lanyard or SRL). Positioning holds the worker in place hands-free (e.g., on a pole or rebar) but must be backed by arrest. Restraft (travel restraint) prevents the worker from reaching the leading edge so a fall cannot begin — the lanyard is too short to reach the edge, which is the safest active system because there is no fall to arrest. Suspension is used for rope access and window cleaning and is a work support, not a fall arrest. The trap is using a positioning belt or a restraint lanyard as if it were an arrest system, or vice versa; a positioning belt alone does not arrest a fall. The disciplined rule is to specify which system is in use for each task and to use the correct equipment for that function.

### Control Leading Edge, Roof, and Overhead Hoisting Work

Leading edge work (where steel decking or other material is being placed at the advancing edge of a floor) and low-slope roof work have special hazards. For leading edge and precast concrete erection, a controlled access zone (CAZ) with a warning line 15 feet back from the edge, plus a safety monitor, may be used where conventional protection is infeasible — but only under the specific Subpart M conditions, and the CAZ is not a substitute for guardrails or arrest where those are feasible. For low-slope roofs (4:12 or less), a warning line 15 feet from the edge combined with a monitor is permitted for work outside the line; inside 6 feet, arrest or a guardrail is required. Overhead hoisting creates a struck-by hazard: never work under a suspended load, and use tag lines to control loads. The trap is invoking a CAZ or warning line as a generic "we don't need fall protection" device when the specific conditions are not met. The disciplined approach is to apply the specific rule for each edge and roof type.

### Plan Prompt Rescue to Prevent Suspension Trauma

A worker who falls and is suspended in a harness can die of suspension (orthostatic) intolerance within as little as 10 to 30 minutes, because the harness straps trap blood in the legs, reducing return flow to the heart and brain, even though the fall itself was arrested. OSHA requires that workers be rescued promptly, and a self-rescue capability or a rescue plan with trained rescuers and equipment (descent devices, aerial lifts, ladder rescue) must be in place before work begins. The trap is assuming that "arrested" equals "saved" and having no rescue plan, so the worker hangs until suspension trauma kills them or until a slow emergency response arrives. The disciplined rule is to plan and rehearse rescue, to have the means to reach a suspended worker within minutes, and to obtain medical evaluation after any suspension because the physiological effects can persist.

### Inspect Equipment and Retire Damaged Gear

Harnesses, lanyards, SRLs, and anchors must be inspected by the user before each use (for cuts, burns, chemical damage, bent hardware, missing tags, and retracted SRLs that lock) and by a competent person periodically. Equipment subjected to a fall arrest must be removed from service immediately. The trap is a harness that has been in service for years, has a frayed lanyard or a cracked D-ring cover, and is still in use because "it looks okay." The disciplined rule is to inspect before every use, tag and remove any gear that has arrested a fall or shows damage, and follow the manufacturer's service life.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to a Harness When a Guardrail Would Protect Passively

The crew chooses PFAS for an open-sided floor because it is faster to set up than a guardrail, exposing every worker to an active system that depends on correct donning and anchoring. The trap is that passive systems protect regardless of user behavior, and active systems do not. The mechanism is that a harness must be worn correctly, anchored to a 5,000-pound point, and kept within fall clearance — any single failure produces a fall. The false signal is "everyone is tied off." The harm is a fall from a setup that a guardrail would have prevented entirely. The defense is to apply the hierarchy of controls and prefer passive protection wherever feasible.

### Anchoring Below the Worker and Creating a Swing Fall

The worker clips a fixed-length lanyard to an anchor at foot level (a rebar mat, a low beam) and moves laterally, so a fall produces both a longer free fall and a pendulum swing into a column or wall. The trap is that a low or offset anchor converts a survivable arrest into a swing-impact injury or a fall clearance failure. The mechanism is that the pendulum geometry is invisible until the fall occurs. The false signal is "I am tied off." The harm is a swing impact or a lower-level strike. The defense is to keep the anchor overhead and within 30 degrees of plumb, and to use an SRL or a horizontal lifeline designed by a qualified person.

### Miscalculating Fall Clearance So the Worker Hits the Lower Level

The setup uses a 6-foot shock-absorbing lanyard on an anchor 6 feet above the D-ring, and the worker is 6 feet tall, but the deceleration distance (3.5 ft), harness stretch (1 ft), and a safety margin are omitted, so the worker strikes the floor below even though the lanyard deployed. The trap is that "arrested" is meaningless if the clearance is not there. The mechanism is that the total fall distance exceeds the available clearance. The false signal is "the lanyard is rated for this." The harm is a ground-strike fatality. The defense is to calculate fall clearance for every setup (free fall + deceleration + harness stretch + worker height below D-ring + margin) and to use an SRL where clearance is tight.

### Using a Positioning Belt or Restraint Lanyard as an Arrest System

A worker wears a positioning belt (or a short restraint lanyard) and assumes it will catch a fall, when positioning equipment is not designed to arrest and a restraint lanyard is too short to allow a fall but will not absorb the energy if one occurs. The trap is that the equipment looks similar but serves a different function. The mechanism is that arrest requires a full-body harness distributing load across the torso and a shock-absorbing connector; a belt concentrates load on the abdomen. The false signal is "I have fall gear on." The harm is internal injury from a belt or an unarrested fall from a restraint lanyard. The defense is to specify the system function for each task and use arrest-rated gear for arrest.

### No Rescue Plan After an Arrested Fall

The crew has a compliant PFAS but no means to retrieve a fallen worker, so the worker hangs in the harness for 30 minutes waiting for the fire department and dies of suspension trauma. The trap is that arrest without prompt rescue is a delayed fatality. The mechanism is that suspension traps venous blood in the legs, causing shock. The false signal is "the fall was arrested, so they are safe." The harm is death from suspension intolerance within minutes to tens of minutes. The defense is to plan and rehearse rescue, to have self-rescue or assisted-rescue equipment on site, and to obtain medical evaluation after any suspension.

### Continuing to Use Damaged or Post-Fall Equipment

A harness that arrested a previous fall, or a lanyard with a deployed shock pack, is returned to service because it "looks fine," and fails in the next fall. The trap is that the internal energy absorber or webbing has been compromised invisibly. The mechanism is that single-use energy absorbers and stressed webbing lose capacity. The false signal is visual intactness. The harm is equipment failure in a subsequent fall. The defense is to remove from service immediately any equipment that has arrested a fall or shows damage, tag it, and destroy or return it to the manufacturer per policy.

## Self-Check

- Is fall protection provided at 6 feet or more to a lower level, and has the hierarchy of controls been applied (eliminate, passive guardrail/cover/net, active PFAS, administrative) so that passive systems are preferred over active ones?
- Do guardrails meet the 42-inch top rail, midrail, 200-pound top rail / 150-pound midrail capacity, and toeboard where needed; are hole covers marked, secured, and rated for twice the load; and are safety nets within 30 feet and drop-tested or certified?
- For PFAS, is each anchor rated 5,000 pounds per worker (or qualified-person designed at a 2x factor), positioned overhead to limit free fall to 6 feet, and within 30 degrees of plumb to prevent swing fall?
- Has fall clearance been calculated for every setup (free fall + deceleration + harness stretch + worker height below D-ring + safety margin), and is an SRL used where clearance is tight?
- Is the correct system specified for each task — arrest (full harness + shock-absorbing lanyard/SRL), positioning (backed by arrest), restraint (lanyard too short to reach the edge), or suspension — with no cross-use of belts or restraint lanyards as arrest gear?
- For leading edge, precast, and low-slope roof work, are the specific Subpart M conditions met for any controlled access zone or warning line plus monitor, and is conventional protection used wherever feasible?
- Is a prompt rescue plan in place (self-rescue or trained rescuers, descent/lift equipment) so a suspended worker is retrieved within minutes, and is medical evaluation obtained after any suspension to address suspension trauma?
- Is all PFAS equipment inspected by the user before each use and by a competent person periodically, with any gear that has arrested a fall or shows damage immediately removed, tagged, and destroyed or returned?
