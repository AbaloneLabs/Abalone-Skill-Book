# OSHA Construction Safety Standards and Programs

Construction safety is governed by a single, large body of federal regulation — 29 CFR 1926, the OSHA Construction Standards — and the judgment problem is not merely "know the rules," but knowing which subpart applies to which hazard, who is responsible for compliance on a shared site, and how a written program translates into field behavior that survives an inspection or an accident. Agents tend to treat OSHA compliance as a paperwork exercise: a binder of programs, a roster of training, a poster on the wall. The harm this skill prevents is the gap between a documented program and actual protection — the fall-protection plan that exists on paper while workers tie off to inadequate anchors, the "competent person" who has a title but no authority to stop work, the recordkeeping that under-reports injuries and triggers a willful citation when the truth emerges. This skill covers the structure of 1926, written program elements, competent-person designation, the recurring top citations, training and recordkeeping, and the multi-employer doctrine that extends liability beyond the direct employer. It is high-stakes: errors produce fatalities, willful citations, and criminal referrals, and the agent must defer to a licensed safety professional (CSP, CIH), an OSHA-authorized trainer, and the authority having jurisdiction when work exceeds its competence.

## Core Rules

### Know the Structure of 29 CFR 1926 and Find the Right Subpart

29 CFR 1926 is organized into subparts lettered A through Z, each covering a hazard family: Subpart C sets general safety and health provisions (including the employer's duty to initiate and maintain programs), Subpart E is personal protective equipment, Subpart K is electrical, Subpart L is scaffolding, Subpart M is fall protection, Subpart N is cranes and derricks, Subpart P is excavation, Subpart Q is concrete and masonry, Subpart R is steel erection, Subpart V is power transmission, and Subpart Z lists toxic and hazardous substances (including lead and asbestos). The disciplined habit is to identify the hazard first, then locate the specific subpart and section that governs it, rather than relying on memory of a single rule. The trap is applying a general-industry rule (1910) to construction work, or citing a 1926 section that was redesignated or removed; always confirm the current text. When a state operates its own OSHA-approved plan (a "state plan state"), the state standard may be stricter than federal and must be checked against the federal baseline.

### Build a Written Safety Program That Is Field-Operable, Not Decorative

A construction safety program should contain, at minimum, a hazard assessment, a statement of management commitment and employee involvement, a system for hazard prevention and control (engineering, administrative, PPE), a training element, and a means of evaluating effectiveness. For hazards with dedicated OSHA rules — fall protection, excavation, confined space, lockout/tagout, respiratory protection, hazard communication — the program must address each specific requirement (exposure assessment, competent/qualified person, equipment selection, inspection, rescue). The judgment problem is that a program written to satisfy an auditor is useless if the field cannot execute it; write procedures a foreman can actually follow, name the competent persons, and tie each program element to an observable behavior and a record. The trap is a generic, downloaded program that names hazards the project does not have and omits the ones it does. Review the program against the actual scope of work, and revise it when the scope changes.

### Designate the Competent Person With Real Authority, Not Just a Title

OSHA defines a "competent person" as one who is capable of identifying existing and predictable hazards in the surroundings or working conditions that are unsanitary, hazardous, or dangerous to employees, and who has authorization to take prompt corrective measures to eliminate them. The two halves — capability (knowledge and training) and authority (power to stop work and correct) — are both required, and the designation must be explicit and documented. The trap is naming a competent person on paper who lacks either the technical knowledge or the authority to halt work; under inspection or after an incident, OSHA will test whether the person actually understood the hazard and actually could stop the job. Distinguish the "competent person" (a field authority for a specific hazard, e.g., excavation) from the "qualified person" (one with formal training or a degree in the relevant technical area, required for engineered systems such as fall-protection anchor design), and from a "certified" person (e.g., a certified industrial hygienist for air monitoring). Do not blur these roles.

### Prioritize the Top Citations Because They Drive Enforcement and Fatalities

The recurring OSHA top-ten construction citations are a reliable map of where workers die and where inspectors look: fall protection (Subpart M, the perennial leader), scaffolding (Subpart L), respiratory protection, ladders, excavation (Subpart P), fall protection training, eye and face protection, and scaffolding training. These map directly to the Focus Four hazards (falls, struck-by, caught-in/between, electrocution) that account for the majority of construction fatalities. The disciplined approach is to build the program and the daily inspections around these hazards first, because a defensible program demonstrably controls the hazards that actually kill people. The trap is spending disproportionate effort on low-probability, exotic hazards while the leading killers are uncontrolled; an inspector will notice the same inversion.

### Verify Training Is Specific, Documented, and In a Language the Worker Understands

OSHA requires training for most high-hazard work (fall protection, excavation competent person, scaffolding erection, forklift operation, hazard communication, respirator use) and the training must be provided in a language and vocabulary the worker understands, by a person qualified to deliver it. Documentation should record the topic, the trainer's qualification, the date, and the attendees, and should be retrievable. The trap is "training" that is a signed sign-in sheet for a toolbox talk in English delivered to workers who speak another language, or a forklift "certification" bought online without hands-on evaluation; under scrutiny these collapse, and they provide no real protection. For OSHA-recognized outreach (10- and 30-hour cards), training must come from an OSHA-authorized trainer; do not represent informal training as outreach.

### Maintain Recordkeeping That Is Accurate and Complete (300, 300A, 301)

Covered employers must record each new work-related injury or illness that meets recording criteria on the OSHA 300 log, file a 301 incident report (or equivalent workers' compensation form) within seven calendar days, and post the 300A annual summary from February 1 to April 30 in a visible location, signed by a company executive. Severe incidents (fatality, in-patient hospitalization, amputation, loss of an eye) must be reported to OSHA directly and quickly — fatality within 8 hours, the others within 24 hours. The trap is under-reporting (treating a recordable injury as first aid to keep the log clean) or over-reporting (logging clearly non-work-related cases), both of which create liability; the disciplined approach is to apply the recording criteria mechanically and document the decision logic. Keep medical records and exposure records for the durations OSHA specifies (30 years for employee exposure records, duration of employment plus 30 years for medical).

### Apply the Multi-Employer Worksites Doctrine to Shared Liability

On a construction site with a controlling employer (general contractor or construction manager), creating employers (subcontractors whose employees are exposed), exposing employers (whose own employees are exposed), and correcting employers (brought in to correct a hazard), OSHA may cite more than one employer for the same hazard under the multi-employer worksite doctrine. The controlling employer's duty is to exercise reasonable care to detect and have violating employers correct violations, through a combination of site-specific safety program, inspections, and enforcement. The trap is a general contractor assuming that because the hazard "belongs" to a subcontractor, the GC has no exposure; the doctrine often reaches the GC for failing to exercise reasonable care. The disciplined approach is to establish, in writing, the site safety program, the subcontractor obligations, the inspection frequency, and the escalation path, and to document corrective actions.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Safety Program as Paperwork Rather Than a Field System

The employer assembles a thick safety program from templates, posts it, and considers compliance achieved. The trap is that OSHA and, more importantly, workers measure protection by field behavior, not by binder thickness. The mechanism is that a program disconnected from daily inspections, competent-person authority, and training decays into theater. The false signal is "we have a written program," which feels like control but is not. The harm is that the leading hazards remain uncontrolled, and after an incident the program itself becomes evidence of the gap between intent and execution. The defense is to tie every program element to an observable behavior, a named responsible person, and a record, and to audit the field against the program.

### Naming a Competent Person Without Granting Stop-Work Authority

A supervisor is listed as the excavation competent person but has never been told, in writing, that they may halt work, and believes they must ask the superintendent first. The trap is that the OSHA definition requires authority to take prompt corrective measures, and a person who must escalate cannot act promptly. The mechanism is that the authority half of the definition is invisible until an incident, at which point the designation fails. The false signal is "we have a competent person on the org chart." The harm is an uncorrected hazard that injures a worker, and a citation for failure to designate a true competent person. The defense is to designate in writing, train the person, and explicitly grant and communicate stop-work authority.

### Under-Reporting Injuries to Keep the 300 Log Clean

A supervisor records a laceration requiring stitches as "first aid" or discourages reporting to keep the recordable rate low. The trap is that the 300 log is discoverable and a discrepancy between medical records and the log is evidence of willful misconduct. The mechanism is that under-reporting converts an ordinary recordable into a recordkeeping violation and, after a serious incident, into evidence of a cover-up that escalates penalties and invites criminal referral. The false signal is a low DART rate that looks good to management. The harm is a recordkeeping citation layered on the underlying injury, and loss of credibility with OSHA. The defense is to apply the recording criteria mechanically, train supervisors not to discourage reporting, and reconcile the log with medical and workers' compensation records.

### Misapplying General-Industry (1910) Rules to Construction (1926) Work

An agent cites a 1910 standard for a construction activity, or a state-plan construction rule, and builds compliance around the wrong requirement. The trap is that 1910 and 1926 differ in trigger heights, training, and competent-person requirements, and applying the wrong one produces either over- or under-compliance. The mechanism is that the two parts of the code look similar but govern different work. The false signal is "the rule number exists and seems to fit." The harm is a program that fails inspection or, worse, leaves a hazard uncontrolled. The defense is to confirm the work is construction (1926) and to verify the current text of the specific section, checking any state-plan overlay.

### Assuming the General Contractor Has No Liability for Subcontractor Hazards

The GC relies on subcontractor safety programs and conducts no independent inspections, believing the creating employer alone is exposed. The trap is the multi-employer doctrine, under which the controlling employer can be cited for failing to exercise reasonable care. The mechanism is that the doctrine allocates liability by role, not by whose employee was hurt. The false signal is "it is the sub's hazard." The harm is a citation to the GC and, in a fatality, potential criminal exposure. The defense is to operate a site-specific program, inspect at a frequency commensurate with the GC's actual or constructive knowledge of the hazard, and document correction.

### Buying Online "Certifications" Without Hands-On Evaluation

An employer purchases a forklift or scaffolding "certification" online that issues a card without observing the worker operate the equipment. The trap is that OSHA requires hands-on evaluation for powered industrial trucks and competent erection for scaffolding, and a card without observation is void. The mechanism is that the documentation overstates the training. The false signal is a laminated card. The harm is an untrained operator who injures someone, and a citation for inadequate training that the card cannot defend. The defense is to require qualified trainers, documented hands-on evaluation, and refresher training tied to observed performance.

## Self-Check

- Have I identified the specific 29 CFR 1926 subpart and section governing each hazard on the project, confirmed the current text, and checked for a stricter state-plan standard?
- Does the written safety program address each high-hazard activity actually present (fall protection, excavation, confined space, lockout/tagout, hazard communication), name a competent person, and tie each element to an observable field behavior and a record?
- Is each competent person designated in writing, trained for the specific hazard, and explicitly granted stop-work authority — and is the competent person distinguished from a qualified person or a certified person where the rule requires one?
- Does the program and the daily inspection effort prioritize the top citations and the Focus Four (falls, struck-by, caught-in/between, electrocution) rather than disproportionately low-probability hazards?
- Is training delivered by a qualified person, in a language and vocabulary the workers understand, with documented hands-on evaluation where required, and is outreach training (10/30-hour) provided only by an OSHA-authorized trainer?
- Are the 300 log, 301 reports, and 300A summary maintained accurately, with recordable cases applied per criteria, and are severe incidents reported to OSHA within 8 hours (fatality) or 24 hours (hospitalization, amputation, loss of eye)?
- Under the multi-employer doctrine, has the controlling employer established a site-specific program, an inspection frequency reflecting actual or constructive knowledge, a subcontractor obligation clause, and a documented correction and escalation path?
- Have I deferred to a licensed safety professional (CSP/CIH), an OSHA-authorized trainer, and the authority having jurisdiction where the work exceeds my competence or licensing?
