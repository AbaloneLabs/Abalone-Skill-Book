# Soil Properties And Bearing Capacity

Bearing capacity and settlement are the two judgments that decide whether a shallow foundation will stand or sink, and both depend on soil properties that are inferred, not measured directly, from a limited number of borings through a ground mass that varies in ways the borings cannot fully reveal. Agents who take a reported bearing value from a geotechnical report and use it as a fixed number miss that the value is an engineering opinion derived from scattered data, that the failure mode it protects against, shear failure of the soil, is sudden and catastrophic, and that the settlement it tolerates governs the structural performance far more than the bearing number itself. This skill covers the judgment exercised while interpreting soil data and selecting bearing pressures, with the goal that the foundation neither shears the soil nor settles beyond what the structure can tolerate, and that the engineer treats the ground's variability and the report's uncertainty with appropriate conservatism.

## Core Rules

### Read The Geotechnical Report As An Interpretation, Not A Measurement

A geotechnical report presents design parameters, a recommended bearing value, and a settlement estimate, but every number in it is an inference from a small number of samples through a heterogeneous mass. Read the report's boring logs, the lab test results, and the stratigraphy, and understand how the recommended values were derived, because a single recommended bearing value is the report's summary of a range of possible conditions, not a guaranteed property of the ground. Identify the soil types, their consistency or density, their moisture and groundwater conditions, and the depth to competent bearing, and confirm the report's recommendations are consistent with the raw data. Where the report's values seem aggressive relative to the data, or where the data is sparse relative to the foundation's importance, request additional investigation or apply additional conservatism. The report is professional opinion; the engineer's responsibility is to use it critically.

### Distinguish Bearing Capacity From Settlement As Separate Checks

Bearing capacity is the soil's resistance to a shear failure that punches the foundation into the ground, and it is a stability check with a factor of safety; settlement is the soil's vertical deformation under load, and it is a serviceability check against the structure's tolerance. A foundation can have adequate bearing capacity and still settle enough to damage the structure, and a foundation that satisfies a bearing-pressure limit can still exceed the settlement tolerance if the bearing value was selected without a settlement check. Compute both: the bearing capacity with the appropriate shear strength parameters and factor of safety, and the settlement with the appropriate deformation modulus and the load, over the foundation's footprint and the stressed soil zone. The two checks use different soil parameters and protect against different failures, and both must pass.

### Select Shear Strength Parameters For The Design Condition

The soil's shear strength, cohesion and friction angle for drained conditions or undrained shear strength for cohesive soils under rapid loading, governs the bearing capacity, and the parameters must match the design loading condition and the groundwater condition. Use effective stress parameters with the friction angle and cohesion for long-term, drained loading of granular and cohesive soils; use undrained shear strength for short-term loading of saturated cohesive soils, because the pore pressures have not dissipated. Account for the groundwater, because buoyancy reduces the effective stress and the bearing capacity, and a perched or seasonal water table that the report's snapshot missed changes the capacity. Do not mix drained and undrained parameters, because they represent different failure conditions and mixing them produces a capacity that corresponds to no real state of the soil.

### Apply The Factor Of Safety Against Shear Failure

Bearing capacity against shear failure is checked with a factor of safety, typically three for static loads under ordinary foundations, applied to the ultimate capacity to obtain the allowable pressure. The factor of safety reflects the variability of the soil, the uncertainty in the strength parameters, and the catastrophic nature of a shear failure, which is sudden and without warning. Do not reduce the factor of safety without a documented reason, such as extensive testing and load proof on a specific project, because a reduced factor trades margin for cost and increases the risk of the sudden failure the factor exists to prevent. Where the loading includes transient or seismic components, apply the appropriate load factors and the permitted capacity increase, but confirm the increase is justified by the soil's behavior under transient load. The factor of safety is the engineer's defense against the ground's variability, and it should not be eroded for economy.

### Check Settlement Against Structural Tolerance

Settlement must be checked against the structure's tolerance, both total settlement, the downward movement of the whole foundation, and differential settlement, the relative movement between adjacent foundations or across a single mat, because differential settlement is what damages the structure. Compute the immediate settlement, the consolidation settlement for cohesive soils, and the secondary settlement over the structure's life, and sum them, using the appropriate deformation moduli and the drainage conditions. Compare the differential settlement to the structure's tolerance, typically expressed as an angular distortion, and confirm the structure can accommodate the predicted movement, or stiffen the foundation or the structure if it cannot. A foundation that meets bearing capacity but settles differentially beyond the tolerance cracks the structure, tilts the floors, and jams the doors, and the settlement check is what prevents this.

### Account For Groundwater And Buoyancy

Groundwater reduces the effective stress in the soil, which reduces both the bearing capacity and the resistance to uplift, and a foundation designed without accounting for the groundwater can fail in bearing when the water rises and can float when the water exceeds the foundation's weight. Determine the design groundwater level, considering seasonal fluctuation, perched water, and long-term trends including climate-driven rise, because a single boring's water reading is a snapshot, not the design condition. Check the bearing capacity with the reduced effective stress, check the uplift or flotation with the full buoyant force, and design the foundation's weight or tension anchors to resist the uplift where the buoyancy exceeds the weight. Groundwater is the variable most likely to differ between investigation and reality, and the design must assume the credible worst case, not the boring's reading.

### Consider The Stressed Soil Zone Beyond The Footing

The bearing capacity and the settlement depend not only on the soil immediately beneath the footing but on the soil within the stressed zone, which extends to a depth of one-and-a-half to two times the footing width, and a weak or compressible layer within that zone can govern the design even if the surface soil is competent. Review the stratigraphy to the full depth of the stressed zone, identify any weak or compressible layers, and check the capacity and settlement against those layers, because a footing bearing on dense sand over soft clay can fail or settle excessively through the soft clay even though the sand is adequate. Where a weak layer is present, the foundation may need to be widened to spread the load, deepened to bypass the layer, or the structure designed to tolerate the resulting settlement. The ground beneath a footing is a profile, not a point, and the design must consider the whole profile.

## Common Traps

### The Single Recommended Bearing Value Treated As Fixed

The engineer takes the report's recommended allowable bearing value, say 200 kilopascals, and uses it for every footing, treating it as a fixed property of the site. The mechanism is that the report's summary number is convenient and authoritative-looking, but it is derived from limited data and represents a design recommendation for typical footings of a typical size at a typical depth, not a guaranteed capacity for every footing on the site. The false signal is that the number "is in the report." The harm is that a large footing, whose stressed zone reaches a weak layer the small test footing did not, settles far more than the report's estimate, or a footing at a different depth or near a slope has a lower capacity than the recommended value. Every footing's capacity and settlement must be checked against its own size, depth, and the stratigraphy beneath it, because a single recommended value is a starting point, not a guarantee for every foundation.

### Bearing Capacity Checked, Settlement Ignored

The engineer confirms the footing pressure is below the allowable bearing value and declares the foundation adequate, without computing the settlement. The mechanism is that bearing capacity feels like the "real" check and settlement feels secondary, but on granular soils and even on many cohesive soils, settlement, not shear failure, is the governing concern, and a footing at the allowable bearing pressure can settle far beyond the structure's tolerance. The false signal is that the bearing check "passes." The harm is that the structure settles differentially, cracks, tilts, and the failure surfaces after construction when correction, underpinning or jacking, is expensive and disruptive. Settlement must be computed and checked against the structural tolerance, because a foundation that satisfies bearing but fails settlement is a foundation that fails.

### Drained Parameters Used For Short-Term Undrained Loading

The engineer uses the drained friction angle and cohesion of a saturated clay for a foundation loaded rapidly, such as a silo filled quickly or a foundation loaded during construction, when the clay's undrained shear strength governs because the pore pressures cannot dissipate in the loading time. The mechanism is that saturated cohesive soils behave differently under drained and undrained conditions, and the undrained strength, which is often lower, governs for rapid loading before consolidation. The false signal is that the soil "has a friction angle." The harm is that the foundation shears the soil under the rapid load at a stress below the drained capacity, because the real short-term capacity is the lower undrained value. The shear strength parameters must match the loading condition, drained or undrained, because the soil's behavior depends on whether the pore pressures can dissipate during the load.

### Groundwater Assumed At The Boring Reading

The engineer designs the foundation with the groundwater at the level recorded in the borings, which may have been taken in a dry season or before a perched water table developed, and the real groundwater is higher. The mechanism is that the boring's water reading is a snapshot at one time, and groundwater fluctuates seasonally, responds to rainfall, and can rise over the structure's life due to development or climate, and a higher water table reduces the bearing capacity and increases the uplift. The false signal is that the water level "was measured." The harm is that the foundation's bearing capacity is lower than designed because the effective stress is reduced, or the foundation floats because the buoyancy exceeds the weight, and the failure occurs when the water rises, which may be years after construction. The design groundwater must be the credible worst case, considering fluctuation and trends, not the boring's reading, because groundwater is the variable most likely to differ from the investigation.

### The Weak Layer Beneath The Footing Ignored

The engineer checks the bearing capacity against the soil immediately beneath the footing, which is dense sand, and declares the foundation adequate, without checking the soft clay layer a meter below. The mechanism is that the footing's stressed zone extends well beyond its own depth, and a weak or compressible layer within that zone can govern the capacity or the settlement, even if the surface soil is competent. The false signal is that the surface soil "is dense." The harm is that the footing punches through the dense sand into the soft clay, or settles excessively as the clay consolidates, because the weak layer controls the real behavior. The stratigraphy to the full stressed depth must be reviewed, and any weak layer checked, because a footing is supported by the whole profile beneath it, not just the soil at its base.

### Factor Of Safety Reduced For Economy Without Justification

The engineer, under cost pressure, reduces the factor of safety against shear failure from three to two, or uses a higher bearing value than the report recommends, to shrink the footings, without additional investigation or load testing to justify the reduction. The mechanism is that the factor of safety exists to cover the ground's variability and the investigation's uncertainty, and reducing it trades that margin for cost, increasing the probability of the sudden, catastrophic shear failure the factor exists to prevent. The false signal is that the soil "is well characterized." The harm is that a local variation in the soil, a soft pocket or a higher water table, leads to a shear failure that the original factor would have absorbed, and the failure is sudden and without warning. The factor of safety should be reduced only with documented justification, additional testing, or load proof, because eroding the margin for economy is eroding the defense against a catastrophic failure.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the geotechnical report been read as an interpretation, with the recommended values checked against the raw boring and lab data?
- [ ] Have both bearing capacity and settlement been checked, recognizing that settlement often governs and that a footing at the allowable bearing pressure can still fail by excessive settlement?
- [ ] Are the shear strength parameters matched to the design loading condition, drained or undrained, and to the groundwater condition?
- [ ] Is the factor of safety against shear failure applied at the code value, with any reduction documented and justified by additional investigation?
- [ ] Has the differential settlement been computed and checked against the structure's angular distortion tolerance?
- [ ] Is the design groundwater level the credible worst case, accounting for seasonal fluctuation, perched water, and long-term trends, with bearing checked at reduced effective stress and uplift checked at full buoyancy?
- [ ] Has the stratigraphy been reviewed to the full depth of the stressed zone, with any weak or compressible layer checked for capacity and settlement?
- [ ] Has each footing's capacity and settlement been checked against its own size, depth, and underlying profile, rather than a single site-wide bearing value?
