# Embodied Carbon and Life Cycle Assessment of Structures

Embodied carbon and life cycle assessment (LCA) is the quantification of the greenhouse gas emissions associated with the materials, the construction, the use, and the end-of-life of a structure, and the engineering of the design choices that reduce these emissions. The structural system and the materials are the dominant contributor to the building's embodied carbon (commonly 50 to 80 percent for the upfront, stage A), and the engineer's decisions on the system, the span, the material, and the efficiency determine the carbon for the 60-to-100-year life of the structure. The harm this skill prevents is a design that optimises the construction cost or the architectural form at the expense of the embodied carbon (a structure with the excessive material or the high-carbon material that locks in the emissions for the century), a design that substitutes the material without verifying the structural and the durability equivalence (the low-carbon choice that fails or degrades), or an LCA that is reported without the boundary and the data transparency (a number that cannot be checked or compared). Because the embodied carbon is a measurable, the design decisions must be made against the LCA, the structural requirement, and the data quality, and the report must be transparent.

## Core Rules

### Define the Functional Unit and the System Boundary

The LCA must be defined against the functional unit (the structure's function: the load-bearing floor area, the bridge span, the pipeline length, for the defined service life) and the system boundary (the lifecycle stages: A1-A3 the product, A4-A5 the construction, B1-B7 the use, C1-C4 the end-of-life, D the beyond-the-system). The functional unit allows the comparison of the alternatives on the equivalent basis (the carbon per square metre of floor, per metre of span), and the boundary defines what is included (the foundation, the superstructure, the envelope, the MEP) and what is excluded. A LCA without the functional unit and the boundary is a number without a context, and the alternatives cannot be compared, because the scope and the basis are undefined.

### Quantify the Stages and the Material Breakdown

Quantify the embodied carbon by the lifecycle stage and by the material, using the environmental product declarations (EPDs), the LCA databases (the industry averages), or the project-specific data. The stage A1-A3 (the product stage: the extraction, the manufacture, the transport to the factory) is commonly the dominant contributor for the structure, and the material breakdown (the concrete, the steel, the timber, the masonry, the aluminium) identifies the high-carbon materials and the reduction opportunities. Report the carbon by the stage and the material, because the breakdown is the basis for the reduction strategy, and the total without the breakdown does not indicate where to act.

### Reduce the Carbon Through the System, the Efficiency, and the Material

The embodied carbon reduction follows the hierarchy: the build less (the design out the unnecessary structure, the span optimisation, the adaptive reuse), the build efficiently (the structural optimisation, the material efficiency, the low-waste), and the build with the low-carbon material (the low-carbon concrete, the recycled steel, the timber). The system selection (the frame type, the span, the grid) has the largest impact, because it determines the material quantity and the type, and the early decisions lock in the carbon. The material substitution (the cement replacement, the recycled content, the timber for the steel) reduces the carbon per unit, but it must be verified for the structural capacity and the durability. A reduction strategy that targets only the material and not the system misses the largest opportunity, because the system determines the material.

### Verify the Structural Equivalence of the Low-Carbon Alternative

The low-carbon alternative (the timber for the steel, the low-carbon concrete for the standard, the recycled steel) must be verified for the structural equivalence: the strength, the stiffness, the fire resistance, the durability, and the serviceability. The timber has the lower strength and the fire considerations; the low-carbon concrete may have the slower strength gain and the durability implications; the recycled steel has the same properties but the supply availability. Verify the alternative against the structural requirement, and do not assume the equivalence, because the substitution that fails the structural or the durability test creates the larger carbon and the safety risk over the life.

### Address the Durability and the Service Life in the Whole-Life Carbon

The whole-life carbon includes the use stage (B1-B7: the repair, the replacement, the maintenance) and the end-of-life (C1-C4), and the durability determines the use-stage carbon. A material with the low upfront carbon but the short service life (the frequent replacement) may have the higher whole-life carbon than the higher-upfront, longer-life alternative. Evaluate the alternatives on the whole-life basis, with the service life and the maintenance schedule, and do not optimise the upfront carbon at the expense of the durability, because the replacement carbon may exceed the upfront saving.

### Report the LCA With the Transparency and the Data Quality

The LCA report must be transparent: the functional unit, the boundary, the stages, the data sources (the EPDs, the databases, the specific data), the assumptions, and the limitations. Report the data quality (the EPD versus the database average, the regional versus the global, the specific versus the generic), because the data quality determines the confidence in the result, and the comparison of the alternatives is only valid on the equivalent data basis. A LCA reported as a single number without the transparency is not checkable, not comparable, and not credible, because the boundary and the data are hidden.

### Set the Target and the Benchmark

Set the embodied carbon target (the absolute or the per-unit target, against the benchmark or the regulatory requirement) and benchmark the design against the industry data (the Structural Engineer's Carbon Metric, the industry databases). The target drives the reduction, and the benchmark provides the context for the design's performance. A design without the target and the benchmark has no measure of the carbon performance, and the reduction is not verifiable, because the baseline and the goal are undefined.

## Common Traps

### The LCA Without The Functional Unit And The Boundary

The LCA is reported as a total carbon number, without the functional unit and the system boundary, and the alternatives are compared on the different scope. The trap is that the numbers are reported, while the scope and the basis are different, and the comparison is invalid, because the alternatives do not cover the same function and the same lifecycle. The false signal is the precise number; the harm is a comparison that misleads the design decision, because the boundary and the functional unit were not defined.

### The Material Substitution Without The Structural Verification

The low-carbon material is substituted (the timber for the steel, the low-carbon concrete) without the verification of the structural capacity, the fire, and the durability. The trap is that the carbon is reduced on the paper, while the structural or the durability requirement is not met, and the substitution creates the safety risk or the premature replacement. The false signal is the carbon reduction; the harm is a structure that fails or degrades, because the equivalence was assumed and not verified.

### The Upfront Carbon Optimised At The Expense Of The Whole-Life

The upfront carbon (stage A) is minimised with the material that has the short service life, and the replacement carbon (stage B) is ignored, and the whole-life carbon is higher. The trap is that the upfront carbon is low, while the whole-life carbon is high, because the frequent replacement adds the carbon that the upfront saving did not account. The false signal is the low upfront number; the harm is a higher whole-life carbon, because the durability and the replacement were not in the LCA.

### The Database Average Treated As The Specific Data

The LCA uses the database average for the material (the global steel average, the regional concrete average), and the result is reported as the project-specific carbon, while the actual material may be the higher or the lower carbon. The trap is that the average is used, while the specific material (the actual supplier, the actual mix) may differ, and the result does not reflect the project's actual carbon. The false signal is the precise number; the harm is a carbon estimate that is not the project's actual, because the specific data (the EPD) was not used.

### The Reduction Target Without The Benchmark

The design reports the carbon reduction (the 20-percent reduction from the baseline), but the baseline is the engineer's own alternative, not the industry benchmark, and the reduction is not meaningful. The trap is that the reduction is reported against the self-selected baseline, while the industry benchmark shows the design is still the high-carbon, because the baseline was set to make the reduction look good. The false signal is the percentage reduction; the harm is a design that claims the reduction but is the high-carbon in the absolute, because the benchmark was not used.

### The System Optimised After The Carbon Is Locked In

The structural system is selected for the architectural or the cost reasons, and the LCA is performed after the system is fixed, and the carbon is locked in. The trap is that the LCA is performed, while the system decision (the largest carbon driver) is already made, and the LCA can only optimise the material, not the system. The false signal is the completed LCA; the harm is a high-carbon design, because the system was selected before the carbon was evaluated.

## Self-Check

- Is the LCA defined against the functional unit (the structure's function and service life) and the system boundary (the lifecycle stages A through D)?
- Is the embodied carbon quantified by the stage and the material, using the EPDs or the LCA databases, with the breakdown that identifies the reduction opportunities?
- Is the reduction strategy following the hierarchy (build less, build efficiently, build low-carbon), and is the system selection addressed, not just the material?
- Is the low-carbon alternative verified for the structural equivalence (the strength, the stiffness, the fire, the durability), and not assumed?
- Is the whole-life carbon evaluated, with the use stage (the repair, the replacement) and the durability, and not just the upfront carbon?
- Is the LCA reported with the transparency (the functional unit, the boundary, the data sources, the assumptions, the data quality)?
- Is the embodied carbon target set and the design benchmarked against the industry data, and is the reduction measured against the meaningful baseline?
- Is the LCA performed before the system selection is fixed, so the largest carbon driver (the system) can be optimised?
