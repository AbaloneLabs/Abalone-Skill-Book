# Climate Adaptation and Resilience of Civil Infrastructure

Climate adaptation and resilience design is the engineering of the civil infrastructure (the stormwater, the coastal, the water supply, the transportation, the building) to perform under the changing climate, where the historical design conditions (the precipitation, the sea level, the temperature, the extreme wind) are no longer the reliable predictor of the future. The climate change alters the intensity and the frequency of the extremes (the heavier precipitation, the higher sea level, the hotter heat waves, the stronger storms), and the infrastructure designed to the historical record is under-designed for the future. The harm this skill prevents is an infrastructure that is designed to the past and fails in the future (the stormwater that floods, the coastal road that inundates, the bridge that scour-fails), a design that uses the single climate projection as the certain future (the over- or the under-design), or an adaptation that is the hard infrastructure only and ignores the nature-based and the adaptive pathway. Because the climate is a changing, uncertain condition, the design must be against the projection, the uncertainty, and the adaptive pathway, and the resilience investment must be justified against the consequence.

## Core Rules

### Use the Climate Projections, Not the Historical Record, for the Design

The design conditions must be based on the climate projections (the downscaled global climate models, the regional studies, the sea-level rise scenarios), not the historical record, because the historical record underestimates the future extremes. Establish the design precipitation (the Atlas 14 with the climate factor, or the climate-modelled precipitation), the sea-level rise (the regional projection for the design year and the scenario), the temperature (the projected heat extremes), and the wind (the projected storm intensity), with the scenario (the low, the intermediate, the high emissions) and the design year (the 2050, the 2100, the service life). A design to the historical record is a design to the past, and it will be under-designed for the future climate.

### Address the Uncertainty Through the Scenario and the Adaptive Pathway

The climate projection has the uncertainty (the emissions scenario, the model, the downscaling), and the design must address this uncertainty, not ignore it or treat the single projection as the certain future. Use the multiple scenarios (the low to the high) and the multiple models, and evaluate the design's performance across the range. Use the adaptive pathway (the design that can be upgraded in the future, as the climate and the projection evolve), where the initial design provides the resilience for the near-term and the provision for the future upgrade (the additional flood freeboard, the raiseable wall, the expandable stormwater). A design to the single projection is the over- or the under-design, depending on which projection, and the adaptive pathway provides the flexibility for the uncertain future.

### Select the Adaptation Measure for the Hazard and the Consequence

The adaptation measure is selected for the specific hazard (the precipitation flood, the coastal inundation, the heat, the wind, the scour) and the consequence (the life safety, the critical function, the property, the environment). The measures include the protect (the levee, the seawall, the flood barrier), the accommodate (the elevation, the wet floodproofing, the increased capacity), the retreat (the relocation, the set-back), and the nature-based (the living shoreline, the wetland restoration, the green infrastructure). Select the measure suited to the hazard, the site, and the consequence, and combine the measures (the hard and the soft, the protect and the accommodate) for the layered defence. A single measure for the multi-hazard site will fail the hazard it does not address, because the adaptation was not matched to the risk.

### Design for the Service Life and the Lifecycle Performance

The infrastructure's service life (the 30, the 50, the 100 years) determines the climate it will experience, and the design must be for the climate over the service life, not the climate at the construction. The coastal road with the 50-year life will experience the sea-level rise of the next 50 years, and the design must account for the rise at the end of the life, not the start. Evaluate the lifecycle performance (the cumulative exposure, the maintenance, the upgrade) under the changing climate, and design for the end-of-life condition, because the design to the start-of-life climate will be under-designed by the end of the life.

### Justify the Resilience Investment Against the Avoided Consequence

The resilience investment (the higher levee, the larger culvert, the elevated building) has the cost, and the investment must be justified against the avoided consequence (the avoided damage, the avoided downtime, the avoided life loss). Use the benefit-cost analysis, with the present value of the avoided damages (discounted over the service life) against the cost of the resilience measure, and the risk-based prioritisation (the highest risk first). The resilience investment is not the unlimited, and the justification must be the quantitative, because the resources are finite and the priority must be the risk-based. A resilience investment without the justification is the gold-plating or the under-investment, because the cost and the benefit were not balanced.

### Integrate the Nature-Based and the Hybrid Solutions

The nature-based solutions (the living shoreline, the wetland, the dune, the green infrastructure) and the hybrid solutions (the nature-based with the hard element) provide the resilience with the co-benefits (the habitat, the recreation, the water quality, the carbon sequestration) and the adaptability (the natural system that adjusts to the changing conditions). Integrate the nature-based where the site and the hazard allow, because the nature-based provides the resilience and the co-benefits that the hard infrastructure does not, and the hybrid provides the layered defence. A resilience design that is the hard infrastructure only misses the nature-based opportunity and the co-benefits, because the hard-only was the default.

### Coordinate the Resilience Across the Infrastructure and the Scales

The climate resilience is a system property, not a single-asset property, and the design must coordinate the resilience across the infrastructure and the scales. The stormwater system, the transportation, the water supply, and the buildings are the interconnected, and the failure of one (the flooded road) cascades to the others (the blocked access, the lost power). Coordinate the resilience across the assets and the scales (the site, the neighbourhood, the watershed, the region), because the single-asset resilience that ignores the system creates the false sense of the security, and the cascade failure defeats the resilience.

## Common Traps

### The Historical Record Treated As The Future

The design uses the historical precipitation or the sea level as the design condition, and the infrastructure is under-designed for the future climate. The trap is that the historical record is the available, the familiar, and the code-based, while the future climate is the higher, and the design to the past is the under-design for the future. The false signal is the code-compliant design; the harm is the infrastructure that fails in the future climate, because the historical record was treated as the future.

### The Single Projection As The Certain Future

The design uses the single climate projection (the high scenario, the specific model) as the certain future, and the design is the over- or the under-designed, depending on which projection. The trap is that the single projection is the precise, while the future is the uncertain, and the design to the single projection does not account for the range. The false signal is the precise projection; the harm is the over- or the under-design, because the uncertainty was not addressed.

### The Hard Infrastructure Only

The resilience design is the hard infrastructure (the seawall, the levee, the flood wall), and the nature-based and the adaptive options are not evaluated. The trap is that the hard infrastructure is the familiar and the quantifiable, while the nature-based is the less familiar and the harder to quantify, and the hard-only is the default. The false signal is the engineered solution; the harm is the missed co-benefits and the lost adaptability, because the nature-based and the hybrid were not considered.

### The Design To The Start-Of-Life Climate

The design is to the current climate or the near-term projection, and the service life of 50 to 100 years is ignored, and the infrastructure is under-designed by the end of the life. The trap is that the start-of-life climate is the current and the familiar, while the end-of-life climate is the higher, and the design to the start is the under-design for the end. The false signal is the design-year climate; the harm is the under-design, because the service life was not in the climate criterion.

### The Resilience Without The Justification

The resilience measure is added (the higher freeboard, the larger capacity) without the benefit-cost or the risk-based justification, and the investment is the gold-plating or the under-investment. The trap is that the resilience is the added, while the cost and the benefit are not quantified, and the investment is not the risk-based. The false signal is the resilient design; the harm is the misallocated resource, because the justification was not performed.

### The Single-Asset Resilience Without The System

The single asset (the building, the road, the pump station) is designed for the resilience, while the interconnected system (the access, the power, the water) is not, and the cascade failure defeats the resilience. The trap is that the single asset is resilient, while the system is not, and the failure of the upstream or the downstream defeats the single-asset resilience. The false signal is the resilient asset; the harm is the system failure, because the resilience was not coordinated across the infrastructure.

## Self-Check

- Is the design based on the climate projections (the precipitation, the sea level, the temperature, the wind), not the historical record, for the design year and the scenario?
- Is the uncertainty addressed through the multiple scenarios and the adaptive pathway, and not the single projection as the certain future?
- Is the adaptation measure selected for the specific hazard and the consequence, with the layered defence (the protect, the accommodate, the retreat, the nature-based)?
- Is the design for the climate over the service life (the end-of-life condition), and not just the start-of-life climate?
- Is the resilience investment justified against the avoided consequence, with the benefit-cost or the risk-based prioritisation?
- Are the nature-based and the hybrid solutions integrated, and not just the hard infrastructure?
- Is the resilience coordinated across the infrastructure and the scales (the site, the neighbourhood, the watershed), and not just the single asset?
- Is the lifecycle performance (the cumulative exposure, the maintenance, the upgrade) evaluated under the changing climate?
