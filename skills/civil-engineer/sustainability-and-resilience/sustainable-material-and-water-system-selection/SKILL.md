# Sustainable Material and Water System Selection

Sustainable material and water system selection is the engineering of the choices that reduce the environmental impact of the civil project: the recycled and the regional materials, the water reuse and the harvesting, the low-impact development (LID), and the rating system compliance (LEED, Envision, BREEAM). The material and the water systems determine the project's resource consumption, its stormwater impact, and its environmental footprint, and the engineer's specifications drive the contractor's procurement and the project's sustainability performance. The harm this skill prevents is a "sustainable" material that fails the structural or the durability requirement (the recycled aggregate that lacks the strength, the reclaimed wood that rots), a water system that is the undersized or the unmaintainable (the rainwater harvesting that runs dry, the bioretention that clogs), or a rating-driven selection that optimises the credit at the expense of the actual performance (the local material that is the higher carbon, the water reuse that is the energy-intensive). Because the sustainability is the engineering performance, not the label, the selection must be against the structural, the hydraulic, and the lifecycle requirement, and verified for the actual environmental benefit.

## Core Rules

### Define the Sustainability Goal and the Rating System

The sustainability goal (the carbon reduction, the water conservation, the waste diversion, the habitat) and the rating system (LEED, Envision, BREEAM, the state or the local green code) must be defined before the material and the system selection, because the goal and the rating determine the criteria. Establish the target (the rating level, the credit pursuit, the performance metric), the documentation requirement (the EPD, the recycled content, the regional distance, the water calculation), and the verification (the commissioning, the monitoring). A selection without the defined goal and the rating is the ad-hoc, and the project's sustainability is the uncoordinated and the unverifiable, because the criteria and the documentation were not established.

### Verify the Structural and the Durability Equivalence of the Sustainable Material

The sustainable material (the recycled aggregate, the fly ash concrete, the reclaimed timber, the recycled steel) must be verified for the structural and the durability equivalence to the conventional material. The recycled concrete aggregate may have the lower strength and the higher shrinkage; the high-fly-ash concrete may have the slower strength gain and the lower early strength; the reclaimed timber may have the reduced section and the hidden defects; the recycled steel has the same properties but the supply. Verify the material against the structural design (the strength, the stiffness, the serviceability) and the durability (the exposure, the service life), and adjust the design or the specification to account for the differences. A sustainable material assumed equivalent without the verification creates the structural or the durability failure, because the assumption was not checked.

### Evaluate the Lifecycle, Not Just the Upfront, Environmental Benefit

The sustainable material and the water system must be evaluated on the lifecycle environmental benefit, not just the upfront or the single-attribute. The recycled material avoids the virgin extraction but may require the additional processing; the regional material reduces the transport but may be the higher manufacturing carbon; the water reuse conserves the water but may require the energy for the treatment and the pumping. Evaluate the lifecycle (the carbon, the water, the energy, the waste) and the trade-offs, and do not assume the sustainable label equals the lifecycle benefit, because the single attribute may hide the higher impact in the other stage.

### Size the Water Reuse and the Harvesting for the Supply and the Demand

The water reuse and the harvesting system (the rainwater, the greywater, the stormwater capture) must be sized for the supply (the catchment area and the precipitation) and the demand (the irrigation, the toilet flushing, the cooling tower), with the storage that balances the two. Size the catchment (the roof area, the contributing area), the storage (the tank volume for the design dry period, the overflow for the extreme storm), and the treatment (the filtration, the disinfection for the end use), and verify the reliability (the frequency of the empty tank, the percentage of the demand met). A system sized for the supply without the demand, or the demand without the supply, is the mismatched, and the tank is the empty or the overflowing, because the supply and the demand were not balanced.

### Design the Low-Impact Development for the Site and the Maintenance

The LID (the bioretention, the permeable pavement, the green roof, the vegetated swale) must be designed for the site's soil (the infiltration rate, the groundwater), the drainage area, and the maintenance. Verify the soil's infiltration (the field test, not the estimate), the separation from the groundwater and the bedrock, the contributing area's loading (the sediment, the oil), and the maintenance access and the responsibility. Design the underdrain where the infiltration is inadequate, the pretreatment (the forebay, the filter strip) where the loading is high, and the overflow for the extreme storm. An LID designed without the site's soil and the maintenance will fail, because the infiltration was assumed and the maintenance was not planned.

### Balance the Rating Credit Against the Engineering Performance

The rating credit (the LEED point, the Envision credit) is the driver, but the engineering performance (the structural, the hydraulic, the durability) is the requirement, and the two must be balanced. Pursue the credit that aligns with the engineering performance, and do not pursue the credit that compromises it: the regional material credit that selects the higher-carbon local material over the lower-carbon distant; the water reuse credit that selects the energy-intensive system; the open space credit that selects the infiltration that fails the soil. A credit pursued at the expense of the engineering performance creates the failure or the higher impact, because the credit was the goal and the performance was the secondary.

### Document and Verify the Compliance

The sustainability selection must be documented and verified for the rating compliance: the EPD for the material, the recycled content and the regional distance calculation, the water calculation, the commissioning report. Document the selection in the specification and the submittal, and verify the compliance through the review and the commissioning, because the rating requires the evidence, and the unverified selection does not earn the credit. A selection made but not documented or verified does not achieve the rating, because the evidence was not provided.

## Common Traps

### The Sustainable Material Assumed Equivalent

The sustainable material (the recycled aggregate, the fly ash concrete) is specified without the verification of the structural and the durability equivalence, and the material fails or underperforms. The trap is that the material has the sustainable label, while the structural and the durability properties differ, and the assumption of the equivalence creates the failure. The false signal is the sustainable label; the harm is the structural or the durability failure, because the equivalence was assumed and not verified.

### The Single-Attribute Sustainability

The material is selected for the single attribute (the recycled content, the regional distance) without the lifecycle evaluation, and the material is the higher impact in the other stage. The trap is that the single attribute is the rating credit, while the lifecycle (the carbon, the energy, the waste) is the higher, and the single-attribute selection is the higher impact. The false signal is the credit earned; the harm is the higher lifecycle impact, because the single attribute was the only criterion.

### The Water System Sized For The Supply Without The Demand

The rainwater harvesting system is sized for the roof catchment and the precipitation, but the demand (the irrigation, the toilet flushing) is not quantified, and the tank overflows or the system is the oversized. The trap is that the supply is calculated, while the demand is not, and the system is the mismatched. The false signal is the sized tank; the harm is the system that overflows or is the oversized, because the demand was not in the sizing.

### The LID Without The Soil Verification

The bioretention or the permeable pavement is designed for the infiltration, but the soil's infiltration rate is estimated, not tested, and the soil is the clay or the high groundwater, and the system ponds and fails. The trap is that the infiltration is assumed, while the soil is the low-infiltration, and the system fails, because the soil was not tested. The false signal is the designed infiltration; the harm is the standing water and the system failure, because the soil verification was not performed.

### The Credit Pursued At The Expense Of The Performance

The rating credit is pursued (the water reuse, the regional material) and the engineering performance is compromised, because the credit was the goal. The trap is that the credit is earned, while the performance (the structural, the hydraulic, the energy) is the lower, and the credit-driven selection is the higher impact or the failure. The false signal is the credit earned; the harm is the compromised performance, because the credit was prioritised over the engineering requirement.

### The Selection Not Documented Or Verified

The sustainable material or the water system is selected, but the documentation (the EPD, the calculation, the commissioning) is not provided, and the rating credit is not earned. The trap is that the selection is made, while the evidence is not, and the rating requires the evidence. The false signal is the selected material; the harm is the credit not earned, because the documentation and the verification were not performed.

## Self-Check

- Is the sustainability goal and the rating system defined, with the target, the criteria, and the documentation requirement established before the selection?
- Is the sustainable material verified for the structural and the durability equivalence (the strength, the stiffness, the service life), and not assumed?
- Is the lifecycle environmental benefit evaluated (the carbon, the water, the energy, the waste), and not just the single attribute?
- Is the water reuse and the harvesting system sized for the supply and the demand, with the storage that balances the two and the reliability verified?
- Is the LID designed for the site's soil (the field-tested infiltration), the drainage area, the pretreatment, and the maintenance?
- Is the rating credit balanced against the engineering performance, and not pursued at the expense of the structural, the hydraulic, or the energy requirement?
- Is the selection documented (the EPD, the calculation) and verified (the commissioning), and does the evidence support the rating compliance?
