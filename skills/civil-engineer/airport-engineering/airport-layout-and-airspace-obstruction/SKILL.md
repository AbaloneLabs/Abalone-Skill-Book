# Airport Layout and Airspace Obstruction

Airport layout planning and airspace obstruction analysis define the safe operational envelope of the airfield in three dimensions: the planimetric arrangement of runways, taxiways, aprons, and facilities on the Airport Layout Plan (ALP), and the invisible volumetric surfaces above and around the airport that must be kept clear of objects so that aircraft can approach, depart, and maneuver without collision. The governing references are FAA AC 150/5300-13 for airport design surfaces and the Code of Federal Regulations Title 14 Part 77 (FAR Part 77), Safe, Efficient Use, and Preservation of the Navigable Airspace, for the imaginary surfaces used to evaluate obstructions. The harm this skill prevents is an airport where a new building, tower, terrain, vegetation, or land use encroaches on a critical surface, causing an instrument approach procedure to be raised or withdrawn, increasing weather minimums, or creating a collision hazard that surfaces only after construction when correction is extraordinarily expensive. The ALP, the Part 77 surfaces, the threshold siting surfaces, and the runway protection zone (RPZ) land-use controls are interdependent, and the designer must evaluate any proposed development or obstruction against all of them, because an object that clears one surface may penetrate another and may invalidate an approach procedure even when it does not physically block a flight path.

## Core Rules

### Maintain the FAR Part 77 Imaginary Surfaces and Evaluate Every Object Against Them

FAR Part 77 defines the imaginary surfaces (primary, approach, transitional, horizontal, and conical) that surround every public airport, and any object that penetrates these surfaces is an obstruction that must be evaluated, marked, lighted, or removed, and that may require notice to the FAA for an airspace determination. Establish the Part 77 surfaces for each runway based on its approach category (visual, nonprecision instrument, or precision instrument) and the approach surface dimensions, and evaluate every existing and proposed object, including buildings, antennas, terrain, and vegetation, against the surfaces using an FAA-approved airspace analysis. Recognize that a penetration does not automatically require removal, but it triggers a regulatory process: the FAA issues a determination of hazard or no hazard, and a hazard determination can force the object's reduction, can raise instrument approach minimums, or can require marking and lighting. Evaluate proposed development against Part 77 before construction, because an object built into a surface and later determined a hazard is far more expensive to resolve than one caught in planning.

### Control Land Use in the Runway Protection Zone and Safety Areas

The runway protection zone (RPZ) is the trapezoidal area off the runway end whose function is to enhance the protection of people and property on the ground, and its land-use compatibility must be controlled because an aircraft that overruns or lands short most often comes to rest within it. Per AC 150/5300-13, control the RPZ (ideally by fee-simple acquisition, or by aviation easement) to clear it of incompatible land uses such as residences, places of public assembly, fuel storage, and structures, and apply the land-use compatibility guidance to the RPZ interior, because land uses that concentrate people in the RPZ elevate the risk of a ground-casualty accident. Likewise, protect the runway safety area (RSA), runway object free area (ROFA), and the corresponding taxiway areas from objects, parking, and incompatible development, because these areas exist to contain aircraft excursions and to keep objects clear of operating aircraft. Document the RPZ land-use control method and the current land uses, because an RPZ that is not controlled will accumulate incompatible development over time and will eventually constrain the runway's operation.

### Verify Threshold Siting and NAVAID Clearance for Each Runway End

The runway threshold must be located so that the threshold siting surface (defined in AC 150/5300-13 by the approach type and the aircraft served) is clear of objects, because the threshold position determines where aircraft cross the runway end on approach, and an object that penetrates the threshold siting surface may require the threshold to be displaced, reducing the available landing distance. For each runway end, verify the threshold siting surface against terrain, buildings, and vegetation, and confirm that any required threshold displacement is reflected in the declared distances (takeoff run available, takeoff distance available, accelerate-stop distance available, and landing distance available), because a displaced threshold that is not reflected in the declared distances misleads operators about the available runway. Verify clearance for air navigation aids (NAVAIDs) such as glide slope antennas, localizers, and visual approach slope indicators, which have their own critical areas and siting requirements, because an object that interferes with a NAVAID signal can invalidate the instrument approach procedure that depends on it.

### Assess Land Use Compatibility, Noise, and the ALP as an Integrated Plan

The ALP is the controlling planning document for the airport, and it must depict the existing and planned airfield geometry, the RPZs, the Part 77 surfaces, the NAVAIDs, and the land-use and noise compatibility boundaries as an integrated plan, because development on and around the airport is reviewed against the ALP for consistency. Conduct or reference the noise exposure analysis (using the FAA's Aviation Environmental Design Tool or equivalent) to map the day-night average sound level contours, and apply land-use compatibility guidance so that noise-sensitive uses (residences, schools, hospitals) are not placed within the incompatible noise contours, because incompatible land use around an airport generates pressure to constrain aircraft operations and is a leading cause of operational restrictions. Keep the ALP current and FAA-approved, because an out-of-date ALP that does not reflect actual or planned development can cause proposed projects to be found inconsistent, delaying or denying federal funding and development approval.

## Common Traps

### The Building Approved Without a Part 77 Evaluation

A building or tower near the airport is approved and constructed without an airspace analysis, because the local zoning process did not require it. The false signal is the permitted, constructed building; the harm is that the building penetrates a Part 77 approach or transitional surface, the FAA subsequently determines it a hazard, the instrument approach minimums are raised or the procedure withdrawn, and the airport's all-weather capability is degraded by an object that could have been caught and redesigned before construction.

### The RPZ Allowed to Accumulate Incompatible Use

The RPZ is not acquired or controlled, and over time residences, parking, or a public facility are built within it because the land was available and the control lapsed. The false signal is the unchanged runway; the harm is that the RPZ now concentrates people and property in the area where an overrun or undershoot is most likely to come to rest, elevating ground-casualty risk and potentially constraining the runway's operation or requiring expensive land acquisition and relocation to restore compatibility.

### The Displaced Threshold Not Reflected in Declared Distances

A runway threshold is displaced to clear an object in the approach, but the declared distances on the charts and signage are not updated to reflect the displacement. The false signal is the corrected threshold; the harm is that operators compute takeoff and landing performance against the full runway length when the usable length is shorter, and an aircraft may plan a takeoff or landing on a distance that is not actually available, with overrun risk on takeoff or landing.

### The Out-of-Date ALP That Misrepresents the Airport

The ALP has not been updated to reflect recent construction, planned development, or revised Part 77 surfaces, so proposed projects are reviewed against a plan that does not match reality. The false signal is the existing ALP on file; the harm is that a project found consistent with the stale ALP may in fact conflict with the actual or required surfaces, federal funding is delayed or denied when the inconsistency surfaces, and the airport's planning basis diverges from its operational reality.

## Self-Check

- Are the FAR Part 77 imaginary surfaces established for each runway and approach type, and is every existing and proposed object (building, antenna, terrain, vegetation) evaluated against them with an FAA airspace determination where required?
- Is the runway protection zone controlled (ideally by fee acquisition or aviation easement) and cleared of incompatible land uses such as residences, public assembly, and fuel storage, with the control method and land uses documented?
- Are the runway safety area, object free area, and corresponding taxiway areas kept clear of objects, parking, and incompatible development that would compromise their containment and clearance function?
- For each runway end, is the threshold siting surface verified clear of objects, and is any threshold displacement correctly reflected in the declared distances (TORA, TODA, ASDA, LDA) on charts and signage?
- Are NAVAID critical areas and siting requirements verified clear of interference, so that the instrument approach procedures dependent on those NAVAIDs remain valid?
- Is the ALP current and FAA-approved, depicting the planned airfield, RPZs, Part 77 surfaces, NAVAIDs, and noise compatibility boundaries, and is land-use compatibility around the airport assessed against current noise contours?
