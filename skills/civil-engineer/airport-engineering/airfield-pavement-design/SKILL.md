# Airfield Pavement Design

Airfield pavement design differs fundamentally from highway pavement design because aircraft apply concentrated, very high wheel loads through complex multi-wheel gears, and the consequences of pavement failure on a runway are catastrophic rather than merely costly. The governing standard in the United States is FAA Advisory Circular (AC) 150/5320-6, Airport Pavement Design and Evaluation, which provides the methods for flexible and rigid airfield pavement thickness based on the design aircraft's gear configuration and weight, the subgrade strength, the cumulative traffic (coverages or passes), and the climate. The harm this skill prevents is a runway, taxiway, or apron pavement that fails under the gear loading of the design aircraft, producing rutting, shoving, spalling, or loose material (foreign object debris, FOD) that can damage aircraft engines and tires and that can cause loss of control at high speed. The designer must characterize the subgrade, select the design aircraft and its gear loading, size the section by the FAA method, provide for drainage and frost, and report the pavement classification number (PCN) so that operators can confirm their aircraft's aircraft classification number (ACN) does not exceed the pavement's capacity. Airfield pavements are not highway pavements scaled up; their load spectra, gear geometry, and failure criteria are distinct, and the FAA methods, not highway methods, govern.

## Core Rules

### Characterise the Subgrade by CBR or k-Value at the Weakest Realistic Condition

The subgrade strength governs the required pavement thickness, and it must be characterized at the weakest realistic field condition, because the design must survive the seasonal wet or thaw period, not the average day. For flexible airfield pavements, characterize the subgrade by California Bearing Ratio (CBR), determined by laboratory testing on samples compacted to expected field density and tested at the in-service moisture, or correlated from other indices, and design to the lowest annual CBR rather than the average, because the pavement must perform when the subgrade is softest. For rigid airfield pavements, characterize the subgrade by the modulus of subgrade reaction (k-value), from plate load testing or correlation. Identify and address soft zones, expansive soils, and frost-susceptible subgrades, because a single weak pocket under a wheel path will localize rutting or slab cracking regardless of the average subgrade quality, and frost-susceptible soils under airfield pavements require non-frost-susceptible replacement to the depth determined by the freezing index.

### Identify the Design Aircraft and Compute Thickness From Gear Loading and Cumulative Traffic

Airfield pavement thickness is governed by the most damaging aircraft in the traffic mix, identified not only by weight but by gear configuration, because a heavy aircraft with a well-distributed multi-wheel gear may be less damaging than a lighter aircraft with a concentrated dual or single-wheel gear. Use the FAA design method (FAARFIELD for flexible and rigid design) to compute the required section for the design aircraft at its maximum weight, applied over the design life in terms of coverages or departures, accounting for the traffic distribution across runways, taxiways, and aprons, because taxiways and aprons receive channelized slow-moving loads that produce different distress than runways. Confirm that the gear geometry (wheel spacing, tire pressure, and gear footprint) is correctly input, because the overlapping stress bulbs of multi-wheel gears determine the effective stress in the subgrade and base, and an incorrect gear input produces a section that does not match the actual loading. The design must also consider the mix of aircraft, using the cumulative damage factor approach where the traffic includes multiple aircraft types.

### Select Flexible or Rigid Sections With Materials and Layer Quality Appropriate to Airfield Loads

For flexible airfield pavements, the asphalt surface and binder courses, base, and subbase must meet FAA material specifications (P-401 for hot-mix asphalt, P-209 for crushed aggregate base, and the appropriate subbase) appropriate to the aircraft weight and tire pressure, because high tire pressures of commercial jets demand a high-quality, rut-resistant asphalt mix and a stiff crushed base. For rigid airfield pavements, the concrete must meet the flexural strength requirement and the slab thickness must be designed for the gear loading, with dowels at joints in the wheel paths for load transfer on runways and taxiways carrying heavy aircraft, because joint faulting and corner cracking from gear loads are dominant rigid distresses. Confirm that the material specifications and layer thicknesses used in the design match the FAA item specifications, because a base or asphalt that meets highway standards but not airfield P-series specifications may lack the stability and durability required under aircraft gear loads and tire pressures.

### Report PCN, Provide Drainage and Frost Protection, and Specify Surface Friction

The pavement's load-carrying capacity must be reported as a Pavement Classification Number (PCN) with the subgrade category, tire pressure category, and pavement type, so that operators can compare it to their aircraft's Aircraft Classification Number (ACN) and confirm compatibility, because operating an aircraft whose ACN exceeds the pavement's PCN damages the pavement and risks failure. Provide drainage of the base and subbase through daylighted layers or underdrains, with filter criteria met, because water in the base under aircraft gear loads produces pumping and rapid loss of support, and address frost by providing non-frost-susceptible material to the required depth where the subgrade is susceptible. For runways, specify the surface texture and grooving required to maintain friction and to prevent hydroplaning, because runway surface friction is safety-critical and a polished or poorly textured surface extends landing distances and contributes to overrun and veer-off accidents, and maintain the grooving through overlay design.

## Common Traps

### The Highway Pavement Method Applied to an Airfield

The airfield section is sized by a highway method (AASHTO 1993 with ESALs) rather than the FAA method, because the designer is familiar with highway design. The false signal is a completed thickness calculation; the harm is that highway ESAL methods do not represent concentrated aircraft gear loads, overlapping stress bulbs, or the coverages concept, so the section may be inadequate for the design aircraft's gear and the pavement fails or produces FOD under jet operations.

### The Subgrade CBR Taken at Average Condition

The subgrade CBR is taken at average or optimum moisture rather than the seasonal weak value, producing a thinner section. The false signal is the compliant design; the harm is that the subgrade softens each wet or thaw season to a fraction of the design CBR, the pavement ruts and shoves under gear loads during that period, and the section that met the average condition fails seasonally.

### The Design Aircraft Selected by Weight Alone

The design aircraft is chosen as the heaviest aircraft, without checking gear configuration, and a lighter aircraft with a more concentrated gear governs the design but is missed. The false signal is the defensible-looking heaviest-aircraft design; the harm is that the concentrated gear of the lighter aircraft applies higher stress to the subgrade and base, and the section designed for the heavy but well-distributed aircraft is inadequate for the actual governing gear.

### The PCN Not Reported or Exceeded Silently

The pavement is designed and built but the PCN is not computed or reported, or operators routinely use aircraft whose ACN exceeds the PCN without evaluation. The false signal is the operating runway; the harm is that repeated overload by high-ACN aircraft damages the pavement progressively, accelerating rutting, cracking, and FOD, and the pavement's capacity is not documented for operators to make compatible-use decisions.

## Self-Check

- Is the subgrade characterized by CBR (flexible) or k-value (rigid) at the weakest realistic seasonal condition, with soft zones, expansive soils, and frost-susceptible soils identified and addressed?
- Is the design aircraft identified by weight and gear configuration, and is the section computed by the FAA method (FAARFIELD) using the correct gear geometry, tire pressure, and cumulative coverages or departures?
- Do the material specifications and layer thicknesses match the FAA P-series items (P-401, P-209, and subbase) appropriate to the aircraft weight and tire pressure, rather than highway-standard materials?
- For rigid airfield pavements, are dowels provided at joints in the wheel paths for load transfer, and is the concrete designed for the flexural strength and slab thickness required by the gear loading?
- Is the pavement classification number (PCN) reported with subgrade, tire pressure, and pavement type categories, and is the ACN of operating aircraft confirmed not to exceed it?
- Is drainage provided for the base and subbase with filter criteria met, frost protection by non-frost-susceptible material where required, and runway surface texture and grooving specified and maintained for friction and hydroplaning control?
