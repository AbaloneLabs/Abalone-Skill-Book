# Runway and Taxiway Geometric Design

Runway and taxiway geometric design fixes the airfield's operational geometry for decades, and because aircraft operate at high speed on runways and in confined, low-speed maneuvers on taxiways and aprons, the dimensional standards are driven by safety-of-flight considerations that leave little room for engineering discretion to compromise. The governing standard in the United States is FAA Advisory Circular (AC) 150/5300-13, Airport Design, which defines the dimensional criteria as a function of the Airport Reference Code (ARC), the Aircraft Approach Category (AAC) and Airplane Design Group (ADG) of the design aircraft, and the approach visibility minimums. The harm this skill prevents is an airfield whose runway length is inadequate for the design aircraft at critical conditions, whose runway safety area (RSA) cannot contain an overrun or excursion, or whose taxiway geometry creates confusion, wingtip clearance conflicts, or runway-incursion risk. Aircraft performance, wind coverage, and the physical protection areas are interdependent, and the designer must size each element to the design aircraft and the critical aircraft operating at its maximum takeoff weight in the most demanding atmospheric condition, because an airfield designed for average conditions will be marginal or unsafe when a heavily loaded aircraft departs on a hot day at high elevation.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Design Aircraft and Determine Runway Length for Critical Conditions

Runway length is governed by the most demanding aircraft that uses the airport regularly (the design or critical aircraft), and it must be determined for that aircraft at its maximum takeoff or landing weight under the critical atmospheric conditions, not average conditions. Use the aircraft manufacturer's performance charts or FAA AC 150/5325-4 runway length guidance, adjusted for the airport elevation, the mean daily maximum temperature of the hottest month, and the runway gradient, because takeoff and landing distances increase with temperature (density altitude), elevation, and gradient. Account for the runway's effective gradient and for the runway length required for both takeoff (accelerate-stop distance and takeoff distance) and landing, and provide the runway safety area and runway object free area beyond the runway ends. A runway length based on standard-day sea-level performance will be inadequate for a loaded aircraft departing a hot, high-elevation airport, forcing payload or fuel restrictions that make the airport commercially unviable or operationally marginal.

### Apply ARC-Based Dimensional Standards for Runway Width, Separations, and Safety Areas

The Airport Reference Code combines the Aircraft Approach Category (based on approach speed) and the Airplane Design Group (based on wingspan), and it determines the dimensional standards for runway width, runway-to-taxiway and runway-to-object separation, taxiway width and fillets, and the runway safety area, runway object free area (ROFA), and runway protection zone (RPZ). Apply the AC 150/5300-13 standards for the current or planned ARC of the design aircraft, and confirm that every dimensional element meets the standard for that code, because the separations are set to protect aircraft operations and to keep objects and other aircraft clear of the operational areas. The runway safety area must be cleared, graded, and drained to support an aircraft that overruns or veers off the runway without catastrophic damage; it is not a buffer for parking objects. Where the full RSA cannot be provided, an engineered materials arresting system (EMAS) may be considered for the overrun, but it is a documented exception, not a substitute for the standard where the standard is achievable.

### Design Taxiway Geometry to Prevent Incursions and Wingtip Conflicts

Taxiway design is dominated by two risks: runway incursions (an aircraft or vehicle entering the runway environment without clearance) and wingtip collisions between taxiing or parked aircraft. Lay out taxiways to provide direct, unambiguous routing that minimizes the need for aircraft to cross active runways, because each runway crossing is an incursion opportunity, and use taxiway geometry (right-angle turns, holding positions, and runway guard lights) to force pilots to slow and orient before entering a runway. Apply the ADG-based taxiway width and taxiway safety area and object free area standards, and verify wingtip clearance between parallel taxiways and between taxiways and fixed objects or parked aircraft, using the design aircraft wingspan, because inadequate wingtip clearance produces collision risk in low-visibility and congested operations. Design fillets at intersections to accommodate the cockpit-oversteer geometry of large aircraft so that the landing gear tracks remain on the pavement, and locate holding positions at the standard separation from the runway centerline so that a holding aircraft's tail does not penetrate the runway safety area or the obstacle-free zone.

### Use the Wind Rose to Establish Runway Orientation and Wind Coverage

Runway orientation must provide the wind coverage required for the design aircraft, because crosswind and tailwind components beyond the aircraft's limits prevent safe operations. Perform a wind rose analysis using at least ten consecutive years of local wind data, computing the crosswind and tailwind components for candidate orientations against the allowable crosswind component for the AAC (typically 10.5, 13, 15, or 16 knots depending on approach speed), and confirm that the runway provides the required wind coverage (commonly 95 percent plus an additional allowance for instrument conditions). Where a single runway cannot achieve the required coverage, a crosswind runway may be justified, but the justification rests on the wind analysis and the crosswind component limit of the critical aircraft, not on convenience. Document the wind coverage and the allowable crosswind component used, because the runway orientation is defensible only against the wind data and the aircraft limits.

## Common Traps

### The Runway Length Based on Standard-Day Performance

The runway length is sized from the design aircraft's performance at standard sea-level conditions without adjusting for the airport's elevation, the hot-month temperature, or the runway gradient. The false signal is a runway length that meets the aircraft's nominal requirement; the harm is that on a hot day at elevation, the loaded aircraft's takeoff distance increases substantially, the runway is marginal or inadequate, and the aircraft must reduce payload or fuel, making operations commercially marginal or unsafe.

### The Object Stored in the Runway Safety Area

The runway safety area is graded and levelled, and because it is flat and empty it is used to store equipment, materials, or vehicles during construction or operations. The false signal is the apparent order and the cleared runway; the harm is that the RSA exists to contain an overrun or excursion, and an object within it converts a survivable overrun into a catastrophic collision, defeating the entire purpose of the safety area.

### The Taxiway Routing That Forces Runway Crossings

A taxiway layout routes aircraft across an active runway to reach the apron or runway end, because it is the shortest path, without considering incursion risk. The false signal is the efficient routing; the harm is that each runway crossing is an incursion opportunity, and a layout that maximizes crossings maximizes the probability of a pilot or vehicle entering the runway environment without clearance, a leading cause of the most severe aviation incidents.

### The Wingtip Clearance Checked Only for the Current Fleet

Wingtip clearance is verified for the aircraft currently using the airport but not for the larger design aircraft the airport is planning to serve, or the ADG standard is applied without checking the actual wingspan. The false signal is the compliant clearance for today's fleet; the harm is that when the larger aircraft arrives, the parallel taxiway or apron separation is inadequate, producing wingtip collision risk in congested or low-visibility operations that the design should have anticipated.

## Self-Check

- Is the design (critical) aircraft established by make and model, and is the runway length determined from manufacturer performance or AC 150/5325-4 guidance adjusted for airport elevation, hot-month temperature, and runway gradient?
- Are all dimensional standards (runway width, separations, taxiway width, RSA, ROFA, RPZ) applied per AC 150/5300-13 for the current or planned Airport Reference Code and approach visibility minimums?
- Is the runway safety area cleared, graded, and drained to support an overrun or excursion, with no objects stored within it, and is an EMAS used only as a documented exception where the full RSA is unachievable?
- Does the taxiway layout minimize runway crossings, provide unambiguous routing, and use holding positions, runway guard lights, and fillets designed for the cockpit-oversteer geometry of the design aircraft?
- Is wingtip clearance between parallel taxiways, between taxiways and objects, and on the apron verified for the design aircraft wingspan, not just the current fleet?
- Is the runway orientation justified by a wind rose analysis using at least ten years of data against the allowable crosswind component for the AAC, with the wind coverage and any crosswind runway need documented?
