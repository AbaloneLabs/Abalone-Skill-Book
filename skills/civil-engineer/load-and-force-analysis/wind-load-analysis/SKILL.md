# Wind Load Analysis

Wind load analysis converts a regional wind speed into the pressures and suctions that act on a building's surfaces, and the conversion depends on the exposure, the building's geometry, the height, and the surface, so that two buildings in the same wind region can experience very different loads, and a building's roof can experience a suction far larger than its walls' pressure. Agents who apply a uniform wind pressure and stop miss that wind is a pressure-and-suction phenomenon governed by the building's shape, that the components and cladding experience far higher localized loads than the main frame, and that the exposure category, which reflects the surrounding terrain, can change the load by a factor of two. This skill covers the judgment exercised while determining wind loads, with the goal that the main wind force-resisting system is loaded for the overall response, the components and cladding are loaded for the localized pressures, and the building's geometry and exposure are correctly reflected in the pressures used.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Basic Wind Speed, Exposure, And Directionality

The wind load begins with the basic wind speed for the site, the regional design speed from the code's maps, and the exposure category, which reflects the terrain's roughness and changes the wind's profile with height. Establish the basic wind speed from the code, accounting for the risk category of the occupancy, because a higher-risk occupancy uses a higher-speed map. Determine the exposure category, B for urban, C for open, D for flat coastline, by examining the terrain in the prevailing wind directions, because the exposure governs the velocity pressure and a building in exposure C experiences higher loads than the same building in exposure B. Apply the directionality factor, which accounts for the reduced probability of the worst wind from the worst direction, only where the code permits, because the factor reduces the load and its use is conditional. The basic speed, exposure, and directionality are the inputs that scale the entire wind load, and they must be established correctly for the site.

### Compute Main Wind Force-Resisting System Pressures By Zone And Surface

The main wind force-resisting system, the overall frame, walls, and roof that resist the building's lateral and uplift response, is loaded by pressures that vary across the building's surfaces, with higher pressures at the edges and corners where the flow separates. Compute the pressures for each surface, the windward wall in pressure, the leeward wall in suction, the side walls in suction, and the roof in suction or pressure depending on its slope, using the code's pressure coefficients for each zone. Account for the higher coefficients at the edges and corners of the walls and roof, the end zones, because the flow separation at these zones produces higher local pressures that the main frame must resist. Combine the pressures into the load cases that produce the overall response, the shear, the overturning, and the torsion, because the windward and leeward pressures combine into the net pressure and the asymmetric pressures produce torsion. The main frame's wind load is the integrated surface pressure, and it must reflect the zoning and the load cases.

### Compute Component And Cladding Pressures For Localized Loads

The components and cladding, the wall panels, the windows, the doors, the roof deck, and the fasteners, experience localized pressures that are far higher than the main frame's average, because the flow separation at edges and corners produces intense, small-area suctions that the main frame's larger tributary averages out. Compute the component-and-cladding pressures using the code's higher coefficients for small effective areas, because the pressure coefficient increases as the area decreases, and a small-area element like a corner window or a roof edge panel experiences a coefficient several times the main frame's. Select the element's effective area correctly, because the coefficient depends on it, and a small element uses the high coefficient while a large element uses a lower one. The component-and-cladding pressures govern the design of the envelope and its fasteners, and they are a separate computation from the main frame, because the envelope's failure, a blown-off roof panel or a broken window, is the most common wind damage.

### Evaluate Uplift On Roofs And Canopies

Wind produces uplift, an upward suction, on roofs, canopies, and overhangs, and the uplift can exceed the roof's dead weight and peel the roof off, because the suction on the windward roof slope and the flow acceleration over the roof reduce the pressure below the ambient. Compute the uplift pressures for the roof zones, with the higher pressures at the corners and edges where the suction is intense, and design the roof's attachment, the deck, the fasteners, and the connections, to resist the uplift, because a roof not designed for uplift peels off in a storm. Check the canopies and overhangs, which experience uplift on both the top and the bottom surfaces, doubling the effect, because the wind flows under and over the canopy and both surfaces are in suction. Check the parapets, which experience pressure on the inside and suction on the outside, producing a large net load. Uplift is a separate and often governing wind effect, and it must be computed and resisted for every roof and overhead element.

### Consider Vortex Shedding And Across-Wind Response On Slender Buildings

Wind on a slender, flexible building can produce across-wind response, perpendicular to the wind direction, through vortex shedding, where the alternating vortices in the building's wake impose a transverse oscillation that can exceed the along-wind response. Evaluate the building's slenderness and flexibility, the height-to-width ratio and the fundamental frequency, because a slender, flexible building is susceptible to vortex shedding and a squat, stiff building is not. Compute the across-wind response where the building is susceptible, using the code's methods or a wind tunnel, because the across-wind base moment and the acceleration at the upper floors can govern the lateral design and the occupant comfort. Consider the vortex shedding frequency relative to the building's natural frequency, because resonance occurs when they coincide and the response amplifies dramatically. The across-wind response is a real and sometimes governing effect on tall, slender buildings, and it must be evaluated where the geometry is susceptible.

### Account For Internal Pressure And Enclosure Classification

The wind pressure on a building's envelope combines with the internal pressure, the pressure inside the building, which depends on the enclosure classification and the openings, and the internal pressure can add to or subtract from the external. Classify the building's enclosure, enclosed, partially enclosed, or open, based on the openings, because the classification governs the internal pressure coefficient, and a partially enclosed building, with a large opening on one wall, experiences a large positive internal pressure that adds to the external suction on the opposite wall, doubling the net load. Account for the openings, the doors, the windows, and the breach potential, because a breached opening in a storm converts an enclosed building to a partially enclosed one with the higher internal pressure. The internal pressure is a component of every surface's net load, and it must be classified and applied, because an enclosure misclassification under-designs the envelope.

### Use Wind Tunnel Testing For Complex Or Important Structures

For buildings with complex geometry, unusual height, or high importance, the code's analytical methods may be inadequate, and a wind tunnel test provides the actual pressures and the dynamic response that the simplified methods approximate. Recommend a wind tunnel for buildings beyond the code's height or geometry limits, for buildings with unusual shapes that the code's coefficients do not cover, and for buildings where the occupant comfort or the cladding performance is critical, because the wind tunnel captures the specific geometry's flow and the code's generic coefficients do not. Use the wind tunnel results for both the main frame and the cladding, because the test provides both, and the cladding pressures from the test are often lower than the code's conservative values, allowing economical cladding design. The wind tunnel is the tool for the structures the code's methods do not cover, and it should be used where the geometry or the importance warrants.

## Common Traps

### A Uniform Wind Pressure Applied To All Surfaces

The engineer applies a single wind pressure to the building's surfaces, treating wind as a uniform load, without zoning the walls and roof or distinguishing pressure from suction. The mechanism is that wind pressure varies across the building, with pressure on the windward wall, suction on the leeward and side walls, and intense suction at the roof edges and corners, and a uniform pressure averages these, missing the high local loads and the net pressure combinations. The false signal is that the building "has a wind load." The harm is that the end zones and the roof corners are under-designed, the main frame's torsion from asymmetric pressure is missed, and the building's wind response is under-predicted. The wind pressure must be computed by zone and surface, with the pressure coefficients and the load cases, because wind is a varying, directional phenomenon, not a uniform load.

### Component And Cladding Pressures Missed

The engineer designs the envelope and its fasteners for the main frame's wind pressure, without computing the higher component-and-cladding pressures for the small-area elements. The mechanism is that the localized pressures at edges and corners are far higher than the main frame's average, because the small area does not average the intense suction, and designing the cladding for the main frame's pressure under-designs the panels and the fasteners. The false signal is that the wind load "is applied." The harm is that the roof panels, the windows, and the doors fail in a storm, blown in or sucked off, because the localized pressure that governed them was never computed, and the envelope breach leads to internal pressurization and further damage. The component-and-cladding pressures must be computed separately, with the small-area coefficients, and the envelope designed for them, because the envelope's failure is the most common and most damaging wind failure.

### The Exposure Category Assumed Without Terrain Review

The engineer assumes exposure C, the open terrain, or exposure B, the urban, without examining the actual terrain around the site, and the exposure is wrong for the prevailing wind directions. The mechanism is that the exposure governs the velocity pressure's profile with height, and an open exposure produces higher loads than an urban one, and an assumed exposure that does not match the terrain either over-designs or under-designs. The false signal is that the exposure "is standard." The harm is that an urban building assumed in exposure C is over-designed, wasting material, or an open building assumed in exposure B is under-designed and fails in a storm, because the terrain's effect on the wind was not reflected. The exposure must be determined by examining the terrain in the prevailing wind directions, with the category confirmed for the site's actual surroundings, because the exposure scales the entire wind load.

### Uplift On The Roof Not Resisted

The engineer designs the roof for gravity, the dead and snow, without checking the wind uplift, and the roof's attachment is inadequate for the suction. The mechanism is that wind produces an upward suction on the roof, especially at the corners and edges, and the suction can exceed the roof's dead weight, and a roof not designed for uplift peels off when the suction exceeds the attachment's capacity. The false signal is that the roof "is designed for gravity." The harm is that the roof deck or the roofing membrane lifts off in a storm, the building is breached, and the subsequent internal pressurization blows out the other walls, because the uplift that initiated the failure was never resisted. The roof must be designed for the wind uplift, with the deck, the fasteners, and the connections checked for the suction, because a roof that holds gravity but releases uplift fails in the first storm.

### Internal Pressure Classification Wrong

The engineer classifies the building as enclosed, with the low internal pressure coefficient, when it has large openings or a breach potential that makes it partially enclosed, with the high coefficient. The mechanism is that a partially enclosed building, with a dominant opening on one wall, experiences a large positive internal pressure that adds to the external suction on the opposite surfaces, and the net load on those surfaces is far higher than the enclosed classification predicts. The false signal is that the building "is enclosed." The harm is that the leeward wall and the roof, designed for the enclosed internal pressure, fail under the partially enclosed net load, because the opening that creates the partial enclosure was not considered. The enclosure classification must reflect the actual openings and the breach potential, because a misclassification under-designs the surfaces that the internal pressure affects.

### Vortex Shedding Ignored On A Slender Tower

The engineer designs a slender, flexible tower for the along-wind response only, without checking the across-wind vortex shedding, and the across-wind response governs but is never computed. The mechanism is that vortex shedding imposes a transverse oscillation on a slender building, and at resonance the response amplifies, and the across-wind base moment and the upper-floor acceleration can exceed the along-wind values, governing the lateral design and the occupant comfort. The false signal is that the along-wind analysis "covers the wind." The harm is that the tower's lateral system is under-designed for the across-wind response, the upper floors accelerate beyond the comfort threshold, and the occupants perceive unacceptable motion, because the transverse effect was never evaluated. The across-wind vortex shedding must be evaluated for slender, flexible buildings, because the along-wind response alone does not capture the transverse oscillation that may govern.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are the basic wind speed, exposure category, and directionality established for the site, with the exposure confirmed by terrain review in the prevailing directions?
- [ ] Are the main wind force-resisting system pressures computed by zone and surface, with the end zones and the load cases for shear, overturning, and torsion?
- [ ] Are the component-and-cladding pressures computed separately for the small-area elements, with the higher coefficients, and is the envelope designed for them?
- [ ] Is the wind uplift on roofs, canopies, overhangs, and parapets computed and resisted by the deck, fasteners, and connections?
- [ ] Is the across-wind vortex shedding evaluated for slender, flexible buildings, with the resonance and the occupant comfort checked?
- [ ] Is the enclosure classification, enclosed, partially enclosed, or open, correctly determined from the openings and breach potential, with the internal pressure applied?
- [ ] Is a wind tunnel test recommended or used for buildings with complex geometry, unusual height, or critical performance, where the code's methods are inadequate?
- [ ] Are the wind load cases combined into the response, with the torsion from asymmetric pressure included in the main frame design?
