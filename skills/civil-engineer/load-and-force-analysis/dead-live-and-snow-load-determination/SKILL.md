# Dead, Live, And Snow Load Determination

Gravity load determination is the foundation of all structural design, and it is the place where a missing load, a wrong unit weight, or a misunderstood occupancy category propagates silently through the entire analysis, because every member sized from the loads inherits their error. Agents who look up a live load table and apply it uniformly miss that dead loads depend on the actual construction, including the finishes, the partitions, and the equipment that the design assumes, that live loads depend on the occupancy and must be patterned to capture the worst case, and that snow loads depend on the roof geometry and drift at every discontinuity. This skill covers the judgment exercised while determining gravity loads, with the goal that the dead loads reflect the actual construction, the live loads reflect the actual use and its patterns, the snow loads reflect the roof's geometry and exposure, and the load combinations envelope every credible governing case.

## Core Rules

### Compute Dead Loads From The Actual Construction, Including Hidden Weight

Dead load is the weight of the structure and its permanent components, and it must be computed from the actual construction, layer by layer, including the elements that are easy to overlook. Account for the structural frame, the slab, the deck, the finishes, the ceilings, the partitions, the roofing, the facades, and the permanent equipment, using the actual unit weights of the specified materials, because a generic unit weight misses the heavy finishes, the terrazzo or the stone, and the permanent equipment, the HVAC or the tanks, that add substantial load. Add the partition allowance where partitions are movable, because the code requires a minimum load for movable partitions, and add the allowance for future finishes or overlays where they are anticipated. The dead load is the weight of everything that is permanently there, and it must be enumerated completely, because a missing dead load is a missing load for the structure's life.

### Establish Live Loads From The Occupancy And The Use

Live load is the load from the occupancy, the people, the furniture, the stored materials, and the vehicles, and it is established from the code's occupancy-specific values, not from a generic default. Classify the occupancy per the code, residential, office, assembly, storage, industrial, or vehicle, and apply the corresponding live load, because the values differ by an order of magnitude, a storage occupancy carries far more than an office. Consider the actual use within the occupancy, because a file storage room in an office carries the storage load, not the office load, and a library's stack area carries the dense storage load. Apply the live load reduction where permitted, accounting for the tributary area and the occupancy, because large tributary areas have a low probability of full live load, but do not reduce where the code prohibits, in assembly occupancies or for specific heavy uses. The live load is the occupancy's load, and it must reflect the actual use, not a generic value.

### Pattern The Live Load To Capture The Worst Case

Live load is not applied uniformly; it is patterned to produce the maximum demand in each member, because the worst case for a beam's positive moment is loaded adjacent spans, the worst case for a column's moment is a checkerboard, and the worst case for a continuous system is a specific pattern. Apply the patterned live load cases, skip-loading alternate spans and bays, and envelop the results, because a uniformly loaded model under-predicts the demand that a real, partial loading produces. Include the pattern that maximizes each effect, positive moment, negative moment, shear, axial, because different patterns govern different effects, and a single pattern cannot capture all. The patterned live load is what produces the design demand, and a uniformly loaded analysis is a service-load approximation, not a strength-load design.

### Calculate Snow Loads Including Drifts At Every Discontinuity

Snow load on a roof is not uniform; it drifts at every discontinuity, parapets, valleys, roof steps, and equipment, accumulating to several times the balanced load, and the drift loads often govern the roof design. Compute the balanced snow load from the ground snow, the exposure, the thermal, and the importance factors, and then compute the drift loads at every roof discontinuity, using the code's drift geometry and the snow source area, because a roof with a parapet or a step drifts and the drift load can exceed the balanced load by a factor of several. Check the rain-on-snow surcharge and the ponding instability where the roof is flat, because the rain adds load and the ponding, the accumulation of water in the deflected roof, can become unstable and collapse the roof. Identify every discontinuity on the roof, because each is a drift location, and the roof must be designed for the drift at each, not just the balanced load.

### Account For Special Loads: Equipment, Fluids, And Impact

Beyond the standard dead and live loads, structures carry special loads that must be identified and applied: mechanical equipment with its operating weight and vibration, tanks and vessels with their fluid weight, cranes and machinery with their impact and cyclic loads, and the construction loads during building. Identify the equipment and its weight, location, and vibration characteristics, because a piece of rotating equipment imposes dynamic loads that a static analysis misses. Compute the fluid loads for tanks and vessels at their full capacity, because the design must hold the full fluid, and account for the surge and the sloshing where the fluid is mobile. Apply the impact factors for cranes and machinery, because the moving load's impact exceeds its static weight. The special loads are part of the load determination, and they must be identified from the project's specific equipment and use, not assumed absent.

### Assemble The Load Combinations Per The Governing Code

The individual loads, dead, live, snow, and special, are combined per the code's load combinations, which apply factors to each load to envelope the strength and the serviceability checks, and the combinations must be assembled completely. Use the strength combinations, with the dead and live load factors, and the alternative combinations where permitted, and include the combinations with snow, with wind, and with seismic, because each produces a different demand and a different member may govern. Include the patterned live load within each combination, because the pattern and the factor combine. Check the serviceability combinations, the unfactored dead plus live for deflection, and the specific combinations for vibration and ponding. The load combinations are the envelope, and a member designed against a subset of the combinations may be governed by one that was omitted, so the full set must be assembled and checked.

### Document The Load Assumptions For Future Verification

The load determination is the basis of the entire design, and it must be documented so that the assumptions can be verified, revised, and re-checked if the use changes or the structure is re-evaluated. Document the dead load takeoff, material by material, the live load occupancy classification and the values, the snow load calculation with the factors and the drifts, the special loads with their sources, and the load combinations with their factors. A design whose loads are undocumented cannot be re-checked, because the loads are invisible in the final drawings, and a future change, an added floor finish or a revised occupancy, cannot be evaluated against the original assumptions. The load documentation is the design's provenance, and it must be as complete as the calculations it supports, because the loads are the foundation of every member size.

## Common Traps

### The Generic Unit Weight That Misses The Heavy Finishes

The engineer applies a generic dead load per area, based on a typical slab and finish, without enumerating the actual construction, and the heavy finishes, the stone flooring or the green roof, are missed. The mechanism is that the dead load is the sum of many layers, and a generic value captures the typical but misses the specific, and the heavy specific layers, which can add tens of percent to the dead load, are invisible in the generic number. The false signal is that the dead load "is estimated." The harm is that the structure is under-designed for its own weight, the deflections exceed the limits, the members are stressed beyond capacity, and the failure can occur under dead load alone, before any live load is applied. The dead load must be computed from the actual construction, layer by layer, with the actual unit weights, because a generic dead load is a guess that misses the heavy reality.

### The Office Live Load Applied To A Storage Room

The engineer applies the office live load uniformly across a floor, including to a file storage room or a library stack area, where the actual use carries a far higher load. The mechanism is that the occupancy-specific live loads reflect the actual use, and a storage room within an office carries the storage load, which can be several times the office load, and a uniform office load under-designs the storage area. The false signal is that the floor "is an office." The harm is that the storage area's framing is under-designed, the floor deflects excessively or fails when the storage is loaded, because the live load that governed was the storage value, not the office average. The live load must reflect the actual use within each area, with storage, assembly, or vehicle loads applied where those uses occur, because a uniform office load across a mixed-use floor under-designs the heavy-use areas.

### Uniform Live Load Without Patterning

The engineer applies the live load uniformly to all spans, because it is simpler than patterning, and the model reports moments and shears that are below the patterned worst case. The mechanism is that the patterned live load, loading alternate spans, produces the maximum positive moment in a span and the maximum pattern at a support, and a uniformly loaded model averages these, under-predicting the demand that a real partial loading produces. The false signal is that the model "is fully loaded." The harm is that the beams and columns are sized for forces below the patterned demand, and the deficiency is invisible until a loading pattern close to the design pattern occurs, overloading the member. The live load must be patterned, with skip-loaded cases enveloped, because uniform loading is a service approximation, not a strength design.

### The Drift Snow Load At A Parapet Or Step Ignored

The engineer computes the balanced snow load and applies it uniformly to the roof, without computing the drift at the parapet, the roof step, or the equipment, where the snow accumulates to several times the balanced value. The mechanism is that snow drifts at every vertical discontinuity, driven by the wind, and the drift geometry and the snow source area produce a triangular load that can be far higher than the balanced load, and a uniform balanced load misses the drift entirely. The false signal is that the roof "has the snow load." The harm is that the roof framing at the drift location is under-designed, and the roof collapses or is damaged in a winter storm when the drift forms, because the drift load that governed was never computed. The snow load must include the drifts at every discontinuity, with the geometry and the source area, because the drift, not the balanced load, often governs the roof design.

### The Equipment Or Fluid Load Assumed Absent

The engineer designs the structure for dead, live, and snow, without identifying the mechanical equipment, the tanks, or the vessels that the building will house, and the special loads are absent from the design. The mechanism is that the equipment and fluid loads are project-specific, and a generic load determination does not capture them, but a building with a rooftop tank, a mechanical penthouse, or a process vessel carries loads that can exceed the roof's live load. The false signal is that the loads "are covered by the live load." The harm is that the structure is under-designed for the equipment or the fluid, the framing deflects or fails when the equipment is installed or the tank is filled, because the special load was never identified or applied. The special loads must be identified from the project's specific equipment and use, with their weights and locations, because they are real loads that the structure must carry.

### Load Combinations Subsetted, The Governing Case Omitted

The engineer checks a few load combinations, dead plus live and dead plus live plus snow, and omits the combinations with wind or seismic, or omits the patterned live load within the combinations, and the governing case is never analyzed. The mechanism is that each combination produces a different demand, and a different member may govern under each, and a subset of combinations can miss the one that produces the maximum demand in a specific member. The false signal is that the combinations "are checked." The harm is that a member governed by the omitted combination is under-designed, and the failure occurs under the load case that was never analyzed, because the envelope was incomplete. The full set of code load combinations must be assembled and checked, with the patterned live load included, because the governing combination is the one that produces the maximum demand, and it can only be found by checking them all.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the dead load computed from the actual construction, layer by layer, with the actual unit weights, including finishes, partitions, and permanent equipment?
- [ ] Is the live load established from the occupancy classification, with the actual use within each area, including storage, assembly, or vehicle loads where they occur?
- [ ] Is the live load patterned, with skip-loaded cases enveloped, to capture the maximum demand in each member?
- [ ] Are snow loads, balanced and drifted, computed at every roof discontinuity, with the rain-on-snow and ponding checks where applicable?
- [ ] Are special loads, equipment, fluids, impact, and construction, identified from the project's specific use and applied at their locations?
- [ ] Is the full set of code load combinations assembled and checked, with the patterned live load included in each, covering strength and serviceability?
- [ ] Are the load assumptions documented completely, so that the determination can be verified and revised if the use changes?
- [ ] Has the live load reduction been applied only where permitted, and not in assembly occupancies or for specific heavy uses?
