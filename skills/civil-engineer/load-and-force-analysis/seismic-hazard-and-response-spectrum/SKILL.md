# Seismic Hazard And Response Spectrum

Seismic design begins with the hazard, the ground motion that the site is expected to experience, and the hazard is a probabilistic estimate derived from the regional seismicity, the site's distance to faults, and the site's soil, and it is the input that scales the entire seismic design. Agents who take a seismic coefficient from a table and apply it miss that the coefficient is derived from the spectrum, which is derived from the mapped ground motion modified by the site's soil, and that a site on soft soil experiences a different, often higher, motion than a site on rock, and that a site near a fault experiences near-fault effects that the generic spectrum does not capture. This skill covers the judgment exercised while determining the seismic input, with the goal that the design ground motion reflects the site's seismicity, its soil, and its proximity to faults, and that the lateral system designed against it has a defensible basis for the life-safety and collapse-prevention performance the code intends.

## Core Rules

### Determine The Mapped Ground Motion For The Site

The seismic hazard begins with the mapped ground motion, the spectral acceleration at short periods and at one second, for the site's location, derived from the code's seismic maps or the national seismic hazard model. Determine the mapped values for the site's coordinates, accounting for the risk category of the occupancy, because a higher-risk occupancy uses a higher-hazard, lower-probability map. Understand that the mapped values are for a reference rock site, a firm rock with a specific shear wave velocity, and they will be modified by the site's actual soil, because the soil amplifies or de-amplifies the motion. The mapped ground motion is the regional input, and it must be determined for the specific site, because the seismicity varies across a region and a site a few kilometers from another can have a different hazard.

### Account For Site-Specific Soil Amplification

The mapped ground motion is modified by the site's soil through the site coefficients, which amplify the motion for soft soils and de-amplify it for very hard rock, and the modification can change the design spectral acceleration by a factor of two or more. Classify the site's soil per the code, based on the shear wave velocity, the standard penetration resistance, or the undrained shear strength in the upper 30 meters, because the site class, A through F, determines the coefficients. Apply the site coefficients to the mapped values, with the appropriate coefficients for the short-period and the one-second periods, and account for the cap that limits the amplification at high ground motion, because the soft soil's amplification saturates and decreases at very high input. Identify Site Class F, the special soils requiring site-specific analysis, peat, highly plastic clay, and liquefiable soils, because the generic coefficients do not apply and a site-specific response analysis is required. The soil amplification is the site's modification of the regional hazard, and it must be determined from the actual soil profile.

### Establish The Seismic Design Category

The seismic design category, which governs the permitted structural systems, the height limits, the detailing, and the analysis requirements, is determined from the design spectral acceleration and the risk category, and it is the regulatory classification that scopes the entire seismic design. Determine the seismic design category from the short-period and the one-second spectral accelerations and the risk category, using the code's tables, because the category combines the hazard and the occupancy into a single classification that drives the requirements. Understand that the seismic design category imposes limits: it restricts the permitted lateral systems, it caps the building's height for certain systems, it triggers the irregularity penalties, and it determines the analysis method permitted. The seismic design category is the gate that scopes the design, and it must be determined correctly, because a misclassification permits a system or a height that the hazard does not justify.

### Select The Analysis Method Appropriate To The Building

The seismic analysis method, equivalent lateral force, modal response spectrum, or seismic response history, must be selected based on the seismic design category, the building's height, and its regularity, because the code permits simpler methods for regular, low-category buildings and requires more rigorous methods for irregular, high-category, or tall buildings. Use the equivalent lateral force method for regular buildings in the lower categories, where the simplified static procedure is adequate, because it is efficient and conservative. Use the modal response spectrum analysis for irregular buildings, tall buildings, or where the equivalent method's assumptions fail, because the modal method captures the higher modes and the torsional response that the static method approximates. Use the seismic response history analysis for very irregular, very tall, or base-isolated buildings, where the spectrum's modal combination is inadequate, because the time history captures the nonlinear and the isolation behavior. The analysis method must match the building's complexity, because a method too simple for the building misses the response and a method too complex wastes effort.

### Account For Near-Fault And Directivity Effects

Sites near an active fault experience near-fault effects, the forward directivity that produces a coherent, long-period velocity pulse in the fault-normal direction, and the fling step, the permanent ground displacement, and these effects are not captured by the generic spectrum. Identify sites within the near-fault distance, typically a few kilometers from the active fault, because the near-fault effects are significant and the generic spectrum under-predicts the long-period motion. Apply the near-fault factors or use a site-specific spectrum that captures the directivity, because the velocity pulse can impose a large demand on a long-period building that the generic spectrum does not reflect. Consider the fault rupture, the surface displacement, because a building straddling the fault rupture is torn apart and no structural design can resist it, and the fault setback must be respected. Near-fault effects are real and significant, and they must be accounted for where the site is close to an active fault.

### Account For Liquefaction And Seismic Settlement

The seismic ground motion can liquefy saturated, loose, cohesionless soils, causing a loss of strength and a settlement, and the liquefaction and its consequences are geotechnical hazards that must be evaluated separately from the structural seismic design. Evaluate the liquefaction potential of the site's soils, using the SPT or CPT data and the ground motion, because a liquefiable layer beneath the foundation can cause the foundation to settle, tilt, or lose bearing, and the structural design assumes a stable foundation. Evaluate the seismic settlement, the densification of the soil during shaking, and the lateral spreading, the downslope movement of liquefied ground, because these are the consequences of liquefaction that damage the foundation. Where liquefaction is a risk, mitigate it by ground improvement or design the foundation to tolerate it, because a foundation on liquefiable ground fails in the earthquake regardless of the superstructure's seismic design. Liquefaction is a site hazard that must be evaluated and addressed, because the seismic design of the structure is meaningless if the foundation fails.

### Consider A Site-Specific Ground Motion Study For Important Or Unusual Sites

For critical facilities, tall buildings, soft soil sites, or near-fault sites, the code's generic spectrum may be inadequate or overly conservative, and a site-specific ground motion study provides the actual design motion for the site's conditions. Recommend a site-specific study for critical facilities, where the performance objective exceeds the code's life safety, for tall buildings where the long-period motion governs and the generic spectrum may not capture the site's soil, for soft or deep soil sites where the generic coefficients are uncertain, and for near-fault sites where the directivity is significant. Use the site-specific spectrum, developed from the probabilistic and the deterministic hazard and the site's soil profile, for the structural analysis, because it reflects the site's actual conditions. The site-specific study is the tool for the sites the generic spectrum does not adequately cover, and it should be used where the importance or the soil warrants.

## Common Traps

### The Mapped Values Used Without Site Soil Modification

The engineer takes the mapped spectral accelerations and uses them directly as the design values, without applying the site coefficients for the site's soil. The mechanism is that the mapped values are for a reference rock, and the site's actual soil, especially if soft, amplifies the motion, and using the unmodified values under-predicts the design motion by a large factor. The false signal is that the values "are from the code map." The harm is that the lateral system is designed for a motion below the site's actual motion, and the building is under-designed for the earthquake it will experience, because the soil's amplification was never applied. The site coefficients must be applied, with the site class determined from the soil profile, because the mapped values are the rock motion and the site's motion is the rock motion modified by the soil.

### Site Class Assumed Without Geotechnical Data

The engineer assumes Site Class D, stiff soil, as a default, without geotechnical data to confirm the shear wave velocity or the penetration resistance, and the actual site class is different, often softer. The mechanism is that the site class determines the coefficients, and a softer site class amplifies the motion more, and an assumed class that is stiffer than the actual under-predicts the design motion. The false signal is that Site Class D "is typical." The harm is that the design motion is below the site's actual motion, and the lateral system is under-designed, because the assumed site class did not reflect the actual soil. The site class must be determined from geotechnical data, the shear wave velocity, the SPT, or the undrained strength, because the class drives the coefficients and the coefficients drive the design motion.

### Seismic Design Category Determined, Then Ignored

The engineer determines the seismic design category and then proceeds with a lateral system or a height that the category does not permit, or skips the irregularity penalties the category triggers. The mechanism is that the seismic design category imposes limits on the permitted systems, the heights, and the detailing, and these limits are mandatory, and treating the category as a paperwork step rather than a scoping gate permits designs that the hazard does not justify. The false signal is that the category "is on the drawing." The harm is that a building is constructed with a system or a height not permitted for its category, and the building's seismic performance falls below the code's intent, because the limits that the category imposed were not respected. The seismic design category's limits must be followed, with the permitted systems, heights, and detailing confirmed against the category, because the category is the regulatory scope of the seismic design.

### Equivalent Lateral Force Used Where Modal Analysis Is Required

The engineer uses the equivalent lateral force method for an irregular or tall building where the code requires a modal response spectrum analysis, because the static method is simpler. The mechanism is that the equivalent method assumes a regular building with a dominant first mode, and an irregular or tall building has significant higher modes and torsional responses that the static method does not capture, and the code requires the modal method precisely to capture them. The false signal is that the equivalent method "gives a base shear." The harm is that the building's actual seismic response, the higher-mode forces in the upper floors and the torsional amplification, is missed, and those elements are under-designed, because the method used was too simple for the building. The analysis method must match the building's complexity, with the modal method used where the equivalent method's assumptions fail, because a simpler method on a complex building misses the response.

### Liquefaction Evaluated As A Structural Issue, Not A Geotechnical One

The engineer treats liquefaction as a structural concern, designing the superstructure for the liquefaction, without addressing the foundation's stability on the liquefied soil. The mechanism is that liquefaction is a foundation and ground failure, not a superstructural response, and a superstructure designed for the seismic motion still fails if its foundation settles, tilts, or loses bearing on liquefied ground, because the foundation is the support and its failure cannot be resisted structurally. The false signal is that the structure "is designed for seismic." The harm is that the foundation fails in the earthquake, the building settles or tilts, and the superstructure is damaged or collapses, because the liquefaction that caused the foundation failure was never mitigated. Liquefaction must be evaluated as a geotechnical hazard and mitigated by ground improvement or deep foundations, because the seismic design of the structure is meaningless if the ground beneath it liquefies.

### Near-Fault Effects Ignored For A Site Close To A Fault

The engineer designs a long-period building near an active fault using the generic spectrum, without accounting for the near-fault directivity pulse. The mechanism is that the near-fault directivity produces a coherent, long-period velocity pulse in the fault-normal direction, and the generic spectrum, developed for average ground motion, does not capture the pulse, and a long-period building resonates with the pulse and experiences a demand far above the generic prediction. The false signal is that the spectrum "is from the code." The harm is that the building is under-designed for the near-fault pulse, the lateral system is overloaded, and the building's drift and the forces exceed the design, because the directivity that governs was never considered. Near-fault effects must be accounted for sites close to active faults, with the directivity factors or a site-specific spectrum, because the generic spectrum under-predicts the long-period motion at near-fault sites.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are the mapped ground motion values determined for the site's coordinates and the risk category, from the code's maps or the national hazard model?
- [ ] Is the site class determined from geotechnical data, shear wave velocity, SPT, or undrained strength, and are the site coefficients applied to the mapped values?
- [ ] Is the seismic design category determined from the spectral accelerations and the risk category, and are its limits on systems, heights, and detailing respected?
- [ ] Is the analysis method, equivalent lateral force, modal, or response history, selected based on the category, height, and regularity, with the modal method used where required?
- [ ] Are near-fault directivity and fling effects accounted for sites close to active faults, with factors or a site-specific spectrum?
- [ ] Is liquefaction evaluated as a geotechnical hazard, with mitigation by ground improvement or deep foundations where it is a risk?
- [ ] Is a site-specific ground motion study recommended or used for critical facilities, tall buildings, soft soil, or near-fault sites where the generic spectrum is inadequate?
- [ ] Is the design response spectrum, modified for site soil, used consistently in the structural analysis, with the period and the drift checked against the spectrum?
