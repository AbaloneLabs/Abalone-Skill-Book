# Change Order and Extra Work Management

Change order and extra work management is the day-to-day administration of work that departs from the contract scope, whether by formal direction, by differing conditions, by design defect, or by the cumulative effect of many small disruptions. It is governed by the contract's changes and differing site conditions clauses (AIA A201 Articles 7 and 15, EJCDC C-700, FIDIC Red Book Clause 13 and Clause 4.12), by the common law of cardinal change and constructive change, and by the documentation discipline of contemporary project management. The harm this skill prevents is twofold: a contractor that performs extra work without documenting entitlement and pricing, and ends up absorbing the cost because it cannot prove the work was directed or outside the scope; or an owner that pays inflated or duplicated change-order prices, or that faces a large accumulated claim at the end because small changes were never reconciled as they occurred. Because change orders are where most construction money is won and lost, and because the contemporaneous record made at the time of the change is almost always decisive, the management of extra work is a discipline of documentation, classification, pricing, and timing, not merely of negotiation. Agents must treat this work as decision support for a project manager, contract administrator, or counsel of record, and must never finalize a change order price or time adjustment without the contract clauses, the scope basis, and the contemporaneous record.

## Core Rules

### Classify the Change Correctly: Directed, Constructive, Differing Condition, or Scope

The classification of a change determines who initiates it, how it is priced, and what notice is required, and misclassification creates both cost and entitlement risk. A directed change is work the owner or architect explicitly orders under the changes clause, documented by a written directive or a request that clearly exceeds the contract scope. A constructive change is work that, although not formally directed, the contractor was effectively required to perform because of defective or ambiguous plans and specifications, an interpretation forced by the design professional, or an owner-imposed method that differs from the contract; constructive changes require the contractor to prove that the work was outside the contract and that it had no reasonable alternative. A differing site condition (Type 1 or Type 2) is a subsurface or latent physical condition at the site that differs materially from what the contract indicated or from what an experienced contractor would reasonably expect, and it carries its own notice and documentation requirements under the differing site conditions clause. A scope or cardinal change is so far beyond the contract that it may constitute a breach or an abandonment of the contract, with different remedies; do not casually label a large change as cardinal, because the consequences are severe and the threshold is high. Classify each change explicitly, because the classification drives the notice, the pricing method, and the entitlement analysis.

### Price the Change with the Correct Basis: Unit, Lump Sum, or Time and Materials

The contract's changes clause sets the permissible pricing methods, and the chosen method must match the nature of the work and the available data. Unit prices specified in the contract govern where the changed work is measurable in the same units; negotiated unit prices apply where the work is measurable but no contract unit price exists; lump sum (firm fixed price) applies where the scope of the change is well enough defined to estimate; and time and material (T&M) applies where the scope cannot be defined in advance, typically with agreed labor rates, equipment rates, and material at cost plus a documented markup. Apply the contract's overhead and profit markup consistently and per the clause (often 10 to 20 percent on added work, with different or no markup on credits and on work performed by subcontractors), and never double-apply markup that a subcontractor has already included. Document the quantity takeoff, the labor and equipment rates, the material quotes, and the subcontractor backup for every priced change, because an unsupported number is the first thing the owner will challenge, and a change order priced on a single line cannot be audited or defended.

### Evaluate the Time Impact of Every Change, Not Just the Cost

Every change has a potential schedule impact, and a change order that adjusts cost without adjusting time forfeits the contractor's time entitlement for that change, often permanently. For each change, perform a time impact assessment: does the changed work fall on the critical path or on a near-critical path, does it add duration, does it disrupt sequencing, and does it require rework of completed or in-progress work? Where the change affects time, request a contract time extension in the change order itself, supported by a schedule fragnet or a time impact analysis, because merging the time claim into the change order is far stronger than asserting delay separately and later. Where the owner contends the change has no time impact because float exists, resolve the float ownership question explicitly and document the basis, because consuming float under a change order without acknowledgment converts a later delay into an extension dispute. Do not sign a change order that is marked time-neutral when the work is on the critical path, because the time waiver is very difficult to undo.

### Document the Change Contemporaneously and Preserve Entitlement

The contemporaneous record made at the time of the change is almost always the deciding evidence in a later dispute, and the discipline of documentation is what preserves entitlement. From the first indication of a change, create and preserve the notice, the request for information or clarification that established the scope difference, the directive or field order, the daily reports that record the extra work, the time and material tickets, the photographs, and the correspondence that establishes what was directed and what was performed. Distinguish between work performed at the owner's direction (entitled to compensation) and work performed at the contractor's own election or to correct its own error (not entitled), and document the distinction at the time, because reconstructing it months later is unreliable. Track cumulative changes against the contract scope to identify the cumulative impact or disruption that no single change order captures, because the effect of many small changes on productivity is a real and compensable impact that is lost if not documented as it accumulates.

### Administer the Process to the Contract's Sequence and Time Limits

The changes clause prescribes a sequence and time limits that condition entitlement, and failure to follow the process weakens or bars recovery. Provide the written notice of the change within the clause's time limit, submit the cost and time proposal within the required period, proceed with the work as directed (especially under AIA's proceed clause or FIDIC's variation procedure) while the price is resolved, and document the agreement or the dispute at each step. Use the contract's prescribed instruments (change directive, construction change directive, variation order, proposal request) rather than informal channels, because an undocumented directive that the owner later disavows leaves the contractor with a constructive change it must prove. Resolve small changes promptly through the minor change or field order mechanism where the contract allows, and escalate disputed changes to the claims process before they age out of the notice window, because a change left unresolved becomes a claim that is harder to prove the longer it sits.

## Common Traps

### Performing Extra Work Without Written Direction

The contractor performs work the owner's representative orally requested or implied, without a written directive or a documented notice, and later cannot prove the work was outside the contract scope. The mechanism is that entitlement requires proof of direction or of constructive compulsion, so the false signal of work actually performed hides an unproven directive; the harm is that the cost is absorbed because the owner denies it was ever ordered.

### The Time-Neutral Change on the Critical Path

The change order is signed as cost-only with a time waiver, even though the changed work is on the critical path and adds duration, because the contractor did not want to delay agreement on the money. The mechanism is that the time waiver is binding, so the false signal of a settled change hides a forfeited extension; the harm is that the resulting late completion is charged as liquidated damages or non-excusable delay.

### Duplicative or Inflated Markup

The change order price layers the prime contractor's markup on top of the subcontractor's already-marked-up cost, or applies overhead and profit to materials and credits where the clause excludes them. The mechanism is that markup compounds, so the false signal of a documented price hides double recovery; the harm is an inflated change that the owner audits, rejects, and uses to discredit the rest of the pricing.

### Missing the Cumulative Impact

Each small change is priced and signed individually as a discrete cost, with no record of how the accumulation of changes disrupts productivity and sequencing across the project. The mechanism is that disruption is a cumulative effect, so the false signal of fully priced individual changes hides the unquantified total impact; the harm is a large loss of productivity that cannot be recovered because it was never documented as it occurred.

## Self-Check

- Is each change classified explicitly as directed, constructive, differing site condition, or scope/cardinal change, with the classification driving the notice and pricing method?
- Is the change priced by the method the clause permits (contract unit, negotiated unit, lump sum, or T&M), with a documented takeoff, rates, material quotes, and subcontractor backup, and without duplicative markup?
- Has the time impact of the change been assessed against the current critical path, and where time is affected, is an extension requested in the change order with a supporting fragnet or TIA?
- Where the owner asserts no time impact due to float, is the float ownership question resolved and documented rather than left to a later dispute?
- Is the contemporaneous record (notice, RFI, directive, daily reports, T&M tickets, correspondence) created and preserved for every change from first indication?
- Are cumulative changes tracked against the contract scope so that cumulative impact and disruption are documented as they accumulate, not reconstructed at the end?
- Is the change administered through the contract's prescribed instruments and time limits, with disputed changes escalated to the claims process before the notice window closes?
