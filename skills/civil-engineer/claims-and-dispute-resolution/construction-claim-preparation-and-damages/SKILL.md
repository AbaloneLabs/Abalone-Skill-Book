# Construction Claim Preparation and Damages

Construction claim preparation is the disciplined assembly of a contractor's or owner's request for additional compensation or time, built on causation, entitlement, and quantification, and governed by the contract's claim and changes clauses (AIA A201, EJCDC C-700, FIDIC Red or Yellow Book), the common law of construction, and the quantification practice of AACE International and the Society of Construction Law. The harm this skill prevents is a claim that is legally colorable but evidentially empty, or numerically large but causally unsupported: a demand that asserts delay and disruption without linking either to a specific contract breach, that quantifies lost productivity with a global percentage instead of a measured study, or that throws the entire cost overrun into a total cost calculation that collapses the moment it is cross-examined. Because claims are decided on the preponderance of contemporaneous evidence and on the contractor's burden of proof, a claim assembled retrospectively from memory or from a single spreadsheet is far weaker than one built from the daily reports, correspondence, and schedules that existed when the events occurred. Agents must treat claim preparation as decision support for a claims consultant, quantity surveyor, or counsel of record, and must never assert final entitlement or damages without the contract clauses, the notices, and the contemporaneous record.

## Core Rules

### Establish Causation and Entitlement Before Quantifying Anything

A construction claim stands on three legs: liability (a breach or contractual entitlement), causation (a documented link from the breach to the injury), and damages (a defensible quantification). Begin with liability and causation, not with the number, because a well-quantified claim with a broken causal chain fails entirely. Identify the specific contract clause that confers entitlement (the changes clause, the differing site conditions clause, the suspension or delay clause, the defective plans and specifications or implied warranty clause), the specific act or omission that triggered it (a directed change, a constructive change, a differing condition, a delay, defective or incomplete design), and the chain of events that connects the trigger to the cost or time impact. Articulate causation against the contemporaneous record, because the burden of proof requires the claimant to show not merely that the cost was incurred but that it was incurred because of the owner's act or breach and would not have been incurred otherwise; a claim that asserts impact without tracing it event by event is a global assertion, not a proven claim.

### Satisfy the Notice and Documentation Conditions to Entitlement

Most contracts condition entitlement on written notice given within a stated period after the triggering event is discovered, and many claims are barred or severely weakened not by their merits but by late, oral, or deficient notice. Verify the notice clause (the days allowed, the required content, the required recipient), confirm whether notice was given in time and in the required form, and where it was not, evaluate whether the owner waived the notice requirement through conduct (directing the work, processing the change, paying related costs) and document the waiver rather than asserting it. Build the claim on the contemporaneous record: daily reports, inspection records, schedule updates, request-for-information and submittal logs, correspondence, meeting minutes, time and material tickets, photographs, and weather records, because contemporaneous documents are given far more weight than retrospective statements, and a claim without them reads as reconstruction. Preserve and organize the record so that each cost and each day of delay can be tied to a specific document, because the opposing party and the tribunal will test every link.

### Quantify Lost Productivity with a Defensible Measured Method

Lost labor productivity, often called inefficiency or disruption, is usually the largest and the most contested element of a construction claim, and it must be quantified with a method whose rigor matches its size. Prefer measured-mile or earned-value methods, which compare the contractor's productivity on the impacted work to its productivity on an unimpacted period or area of the same work, isolating the productivity loss attributable to the impact rather than to the contractor's own performance. Where a true measured mile is impossible, use the modified total cost method (adjusting the total cost for the contractor's own inefficiency, bid error, and unrelated costs) and acknowledge its lower evidentiary weight, and reserve the unmodified total cost method for the rare case where the nature of the work makes any other method impossible and where the contractor can prove that its bid was reasonable and its costs actual. Avoid industry-average studies (MCAA, NECA, Leonard) as the primary quantification, because they are generic factors, not measures of the specific project's loss, and tribunals give them little weight absent a project-specific baseline. Document the productivity baseline, the impacted period, the cause of the impact, and the calculation, so that the loss is traceable and auditable.

### Quantify Extended Overhead, Escalation, and Time-Related Costs Correctly

Extended field and home office overhead compensate the contractor for the cost of maintaining its organization during a compensable delay period, and it must be quantified only for the excusable and compensable portion of the delay, not for the entire overrun. For home office overhead, apply the Eichleay formula (or the equivalent Canadian or British formula where applicable) with documented contract revenue, total home office overhead, and the billings during the delay period, and be prepared to defend the formula's assumptions; for field overhead, use the actual daily or weekly cost of the field office, supervision, utilities, and equipment extended by the compensable delay days. Quantify labor and material escalation using published indices (BLS, ENR, regional indices) tied to the actual procurement dates shifted by the delay, not to a generic percentage, and separate escalation that would have occurred anyway from escalation caused by the delay. Apply the same rigor to extended insurance, bonds, and equipment rental, and net out any costs saved by the delay (standby versus full mobilization) so that the recovery reflects actual loss rather than a grossed-up figure.

### Test the Claim Against the Burden of Proof, Mitigation, and Alternatives

Before a claim is submitted, test it against the standard the tribunal will apply: the claimant bears the burden of proving liability, causation, and damages by a preponderance of the evidence, and each element must stand on its own proof. Identify and address the defenses the owner will raise: the contractor's own concurrent delay or inefficiency, failure to mitigate, notice default, comparative fault, and the no-damage-for-delay clause, and where a defense has merit, concede and adjust rather than overstate. Evaluate whether mitigation was attempted and documented, because a failure to mitigate (not re-sequencing, not accelerating where reasonable, not seeking alternative materials) can reduce or bar recovery. Consider the alternative quantifications and the alternative theories, because a claim presented in only one way, with no acknowledgment of its weaknesses, signals advocacy over analysis and undermines credibility; a claim that openly addresses its limits and still supports the recovery is far more persuasive.

## Common Traps

### The Global or Total Cost Claim

The contractor dumps the entire cost overrun into a single total cost calculation, subtracting the bid from the actual, without isolating the owner-caused portion from its own inefficiency, bid error, or unrelated cost. The mechanism is that total cost hides causation in a single subtraction, so the false signal of a large, documented number hides an unproven causal link; the harm is a claim that fails in its entirety because it cannot meet the strict conditions for total cost recovery.

### Productivity Loss by Industry Study Alone

Lost productivity is claimed as a percentage pulled from a generic industry study (MCAA, NECA) applied to the impacted labor, with no project-specific measured baseline. The mechanism is that the studies are averages, not measures of this job, so the false signal of a cited authority hides an unquantified loss; the harm is that the productivity claim is given little or no weight and the largest element of the claim collapses.

### Extended Overhead for the Full Overrun

Home office and field overhead are claimed for the entire period of project overrun, including the contractor's own non-excusable delay. The mechanism is that overhead is time-related, so the false signal of a documented overrun hides the compensable-only window; the harm is an inflated claim that is partially barred and that discredits the rest of the submission.

### Causation Asserted but Not Traced

The claim states that the owner's changes "caused delay and disruption" but does not trace each cost or each delay day to a specific event, clause, and contemporaneous document. The mechanism is that the burden of proof requires the link, so the false signal of a narrative of impact hides an unproven chain; the harm is a claim that reads as advocacy and fails for want of evidence even where the underlying impact was real.

## Self-Check

- Are liability, causation, and damages each established separately, with the specific contract clause, the triggering act, and the documented link from breach to cost or time impact?
- Is the notice requirement verified for timing, form, content, and recipient, and where notice was late or deficient, is waiver documented through the owner's conduct?
- Is the claim built on the contemporaneous record (daily reports, updates, RFI and submittal logs, correspondence, T&M tickets), with each cost and delay day tied to a specific document?
- Is lost productivity quantified by a measured-mile or earned-value method with a project-specific baseline, with industry studies used only as corroboration rather than the primary basis?
- Is extended field and home office overhead quantified only for the excusable and compensable delay period, using Eichleay or equivalent with documented inputs and net of costs saved?
- Is labor and material escalation tied to published indices and shifted procurement dates, separating delay-caused escalation from escalation that would have occurred anyway?
- Has the claim been tested against the burden of proof, the owner's likely defenses (concurrent delay, failure to mitigate, notice default), and alternative quantifications, with weaknesses acknowledged rather than concealed?
