# Prestress Losses And Transfer Design

Prestress losses and transfer design is the work of estimating how the prestress force decreases over time from a series of distinct mechanisms, and of detailing how that force is introduced into the concrete at the transfer or anchorage zone, and it is where prestressed design most often diverges from reality. The difficulty is not subtracting a lump-sum percentage from the jacking force; it is the judgment that losses occur through several time-dependent and immediate mechanisms that interact, that the effective prestress governs the service behavior, and that the transfer of the force at the ends is a concentrated, bursting-prone event that can fail the member before it ever sees service load. A member designed with an assumed lump-sum loss can have an effective prestress far below the design if the actual losses are higher, a transfer length that is too short leaves strands unbonded and the force undeveloped, and an anchorage zone without confinement bursts at release. ACI 318 and the PCI design handbook govern the loss estimation and the transfer zone detailing, and the engineer cannot treat losses as a single discount or the transfer zone as a formality. This skill covers the judgment exercised while estimating prestress losses and designing the transfer and anchorage zones, with the understanding that final design, sealing of drawings, and code interpretations require a licensed professional engineer, and that prestressed design is a specialty that may require a structural specialist.

## Core Rules

### Estimate Each Loss Mechanism Separately And In The Correct Sequence

Begin every loss estimate by identifying the distinct mechanisms and computing each separately, because lumping them into a single percentage obscures the governing contributors and can be badly wrong. Elastic shortening occurs when the prestress is applied and the concrete shortens elastically, reducing the strand strain, and it depends on the concrete stress at the centroid of the strands and the modular ratio; for post-tensioned members it is reduced by sequential stressing. Creep is the time-dependent shortening of the concrete under the sustained prestress, and it depends on the concrete stress, the creep coefficient, and the age at loading. Shrinkage is the time-dependent volumetric reduction of the concrete from drying, and it depends on the member size, the humidity, and the concrete mix. Relaxation is the time-dependent reduction of the strand stress under constant strain, and it depends on the strand type, low-relaxation or stress-relieved, and the initial stress level. Anchorage seating, or wedge set, is the immediate loss when the wedges grip the strand at release of the jack, and it depends on the wedge set distance and the strand length. Friction, in post-tensioned members, is the loss along the duct from curvature and wobble, and it depends on the tendon profile and the friction coefficients. Compute each mechanism and sum them to the total loss, and recognize that they interact, because creep and shrinkage change the concrete strain that affects the strand, and the sequence of application matters for the estimate.

### Compute Friction Losses For Post-Tensioned Tendons

In post-tensioned members, the tendon force decreases along the length due to friction from the intended curvature of the profile and the unintended wobble of the duct, and the loss must be computed from the friction theory. Compute the curvature friction loss from the cumulative angular change of the tendon profile and the curvature friction coefficient, because each change in tendon direction generates friction that reduces the force, and the loss accumulates with the total angle. Compute the wobble friction loss from the unintended deviation of the duct from the intended profile and the wobble coefficient, because real ducts are not perfectly placed and the wobble generates friction even in nominally straight sections. Determine the force at each point along the tendon from the jacking force reduced by the friction, and verify that the force at the far end is adequate, because a tendon that loses too much force to friction does not provide the assumed prestress at the far end. Where the friction loss is excessive, stress from both ends or use a tendon profile with less curvature, because the far-end force must be adequate for the design. Verify the friction estimate by the elongation during stressing, because the measured elongation confirms the assumed friction coefficients and the actual force, and a discrepancy indicates a duct deviation or a friction assumption that must be investigated.

### Design The Transfer Length And Development For Pretensioned Strands

In pretensioned members, the strand force is transferred to the concrete through bond over the transfer length, and the design must ensure the transfer length is available and the development length is achieved at the critical sections. The transfer length is the distance over which the effective prestress is developed by bond at release, and it depends on the strand diameter and the concrete strength, because the bond transfers the force from the strand to the concrete through bearing and adhesion over that length. The development length is the distance required to develop the full nominal strength of the strand at ultimate, and it is the transfer length plus an additional length for the bond to develop the higher stress at nominal, because at ultimate the strand stress exceeds the effective prestress and more bond is required. Verify that the transfer and development lengths are available at the critical sections, because a section within the transfer length does not have the effective prestress, and a section within the development length does not have the full nominal strength. Where the available length is insufficient, use debonding or larger concrete cover to extend the transfer, or revise the strand layout, because a strand that is not developed cannot carry its force and the section capacity is below the design.

### Detail The Anchorage And Transfer Zones For Force Introduction

The transfer or anchorage zone is where the concentrated prestress force is introduced into the concrete, and it must be detailed to distribute the force without bursting or splitting. In pretensioned members, the force is introduced gradually through bond over the transfer length, but the concentration of strands at the ends can still create bursting forces perpendicular to the strands, and confinement reinforcement is required to resist them. In post-tensioned members, the force is introduced at the anchorage as a concentrated load through the bearing plate, and the anchorage zone must be designed with the bearing plate, the spiral or confinement reinforcement, and the edge reinforcement to distribute the force into the member, because without confinement the concrete bursts or spalls ahead of the anchorage. Use the strut-and-tie method or the ACI 318 anchorage zone provisions to design the confinement and the force path, because the anchorage zone is a disturbed region where the standard section assumptions do not apply. Detail the confinement reinforcement with the spacing and amount required by the force, and verify the bearing stress on the concrete at the anchorage, because the local bearing stress under the plate can exceed the concrete capacity without the confinement and the plate area. Do not treat the anchorage zone as a standard detail, because the force path and the confinement depend on the specific force and geometry, and an under-confined anchorage bursts at stressing.

## Common Traps

### Using A Lump-Sum Loss Without Computing The Mechanisms

The engineer applies a lump-sum loss percentage, such as a fixed fraction of the jacking force, without computing the individual mechanisms. The mechanism is that the actual losses depend on the concrete properties, the member geometry, the strand type, and the environment, and a lump sum can be badly wrong in either direction. The false signal is that the lump sum is a conservative estimate. The harm is a member with effective prestress far from the design, leading to excessive deflection and cracking if the loss is under-estimated, or to over-compression and waste if over-estimated. Each loss mechanism must be computed separately and summed, because the lump sum obscures the contributors and the accuracy.

### Underestimating Friction In Long Or Curved Tendons

The engineer estimates the friction loss with assumed coefficients without verifying, for a long or sharply curved post-tensioned tendon. The mechanism is that curvature and wobble friction accumulate along the length, and a long or curved tendon can lose a large fraction of its force to friction. The false signal is that the jacking force is adequate at the jack. The harm is inadequate force at the far end, because the friction loss was underestimated, leading to insufficient prestress and service problems. Friction must be computed from the curvature and wobble with the correct coefficients, and verified by elongation, because the far-end force governs the design.

### Ignoring The Transfer Length At Critical Sections

The engineer checks the member stresses and strength at sections without verifying they are beyond the transfer length. The mechanism is that within the transfer length the strand has not developed its effective prestress, and the section capacity is below the design. The false signal is that the section passes the stress and strength checks. The harm is overstress or inadequate capacity at sections near the ends, because the transfer length was not credited. The transfer and development lengths must be verified at all critical sections, because a section within the transfer length does not have the assumed prestress.

### Under-Confined Anchorage Zone That Bursts At Stressing

The engineer details the anchorage zone with standard reinforcement without designing the confinement for the specific force. The mechanism is that the anchorage introduces a concentrated force that bursts or spalls the concrete without confinement, and standard reinforcement is often inadequate. The false signal is that the anchorage is shown on the drawing. The harm is bursting or splitting failure at stressing, a sudden and dangerous failure, because the confinement was inadequate. The anchorage zone must be designed with the confinement and bearing verification for the specific force, using strut-and-tie or the ACI provisions, because the force path depends on the force and geometry.

## Self-Check

- Are the loss mechanisms, elastic shortening, creep, shrinkage, relaxation, anchorage seating, and friction, computed separately and summed, rather than lumped into a single percentage?
- For post-tensioned members, are curvature and wobble friction computed with the correct coefficients, and is the far-end force adequate, verified by elongation during stressing?
- Are the transfer and development lengths verified at all critical sections, so that no section within the transfer length is assumed to have the effective prestress?
- Is the anchorage or transfer zone designed with confinement reinforcement and bearing verification for the specific force, using strut-and-tie or the ACI provisions?
- Is the effective prestress used at service and final, with the total losses deducted from the jacking force, and are the transfer-stage stresses checked with the higher initial force?
- Are the loss estimates consistent with the concrete properties, member geometry, strand type, and environment of the specific project, rather than generic values?
- Is the design sealed and reviewed by a licensed professional engineer, with specialist involvement for prestressed design?
