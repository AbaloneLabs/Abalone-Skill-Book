# Post-Tensioned Slab And Beam Design

Post-tensioned slab and beam design is the work of introducing high-strength tendons into cast-in-place concrete slabs and beams after the concrete has set, so that the floor system carries gravity and lateral loads with less depth, less cracking, and longer spans than ordinary reinforced concrete, and it requires judgment that ordinary flat slab design does not. The difficulty is not computing an equivalent load from the tendon drape; it is the judgment that the tendon layout must balance the load without creating restraint, that the anchorage and stressing sequence determine whether the floor shortens and cracks, that unbonded and bonded systems behave differently at ultimate, and that the interaction between the post-tensioning and the supports, the walls, and the columns can lock in forces that crack the slab. A slab that satisfies service stresses can still crack at re-entrant corners or at stiff supports due to restraint to shortening, a tendon layout that balances the dead load can still fail in strength if the secondary moments are ignored, and an anchorage detail that is not accessible cannot be stressed or inspected. ACI 318, the PTI Post-Tensioning Manual, and the IBC govern, and the engineer cannot treat post-tensioning as an equivalent uniform load on an ordinary slab. This skill covers the judgment exercised while designing post-tensioned slabs and beams, with the understanding that final design, sealing of drawings, and code interpretations require a licensed professional engineer, and that post-tensioned design is a specialty that may require a structural specialist.

## Core Rules

### Choose Bonded Or Unbonded Systems And Design Each Appropriately

Begin every post-tensioned design by selecting the system, bonded or unbonded, and applying the provisions specific to that system. In unbonded systems, the tendons are coated and sheathed and move freely relative to the concrete, so the strand force is transferred only at the anchorages, and the ultimate strength depends on the anchorage and the strand stress increase through deformation of the whole member, which is limited; unbonded systems are economical and common in residential and office flat slabs. In bonded systems, the tendons are grouted into ducts after stressing, so the force is transferred along the length through bond, and the ultimate strength is higher with a localized strand stress increase at the critical section; bonded systems are used where corrosion protection, fire resistance, or higher ultimate capacity is required, and in infrastructure and parking structures. Design each system with its specific provisions: unbonded systems require minimum bonded reinforcement for crack control and ductility, and bonded systems require duct and grouting provisions for corrosion protection and bond. Do not treat the two systems interchangeably, because their behavior at ultimate, their corrosion protection, and their detailing differ fundamentally, and a design that mixes their provisions is invalid.

### Lay Out Banded And Distributed Tendons For Two-Way Slab Action

In two-way post-tensioned slabs, the tendon layout determines the load path and the service stresses, and the banded-distributed layout is the standard approach for column-strip and middle-strip action. Lay out banded tendons in one direction, concentrated in the column strip over the supports, and distributed tendons in the perpendicular direction, spread uniformly across the slab, so that the banded tendons act as the supporting beams and the distributed tendons span between them, creating a clear two-way load path. Design the tendon profiles with the drape required to balance the desired portion of the load, typically the dead load or a fraction of the live load, and verify the service stresses at all critical sections including the column and middle strips and the spans. Provide the minimum average prestress required by the code to ensure compression across the slab and to control cracking, but avoid excessive prestress that increases shortening and restraint forces. Coordinate the tendon layout with the column locations, the openings, and the slab edges, because tendons cannot pass through columns or openings without deviation, and a poorly coordinated layout creates congestion and constructability problems.

### Detail Anchorages, Stressing Pockets, And Access

The anchorages are where the tendon force is introduced into the concrete, and their detailing determines whether the force can be developed and whether the tendons can be stressed and inspected. Detail the anchorage zones with the bearing plates, the spiral or confinement reinforcement, and the edge reinforcement required to distribute the concentrated force and prevent bursting and spalling at the anchorage, because the anchorage introduces a large concentrated load at the slab edge that can burst the concrete without confinement. Locate stressing pockets at the slab edges or at interior stressing points where the jack can access the anchorage, and size the pockets for the jack and the strand extension, because a pocket that is too small or inaccessible cannot be stressed. Detail the dead-end anchorages where the tendon is fixed and only the live end is stressed, and verify the force transfer at both ends. Require stressing records and elongation verification, because the tendon force is verified by the elongation and the gauge pressure, and an unstressed or under-stressed tendon does not provide the assumed prestress. Do not detail anchorages that are inaccessible or un-inspectable, because a post-tensioned system whose tendons cannot be stressed or verified is a system whose force is unknown.

### Control Shortening, Restraint Cracking, And Stressing Sequence

Post-tensioned slabs shorten under the prestress, and the restraint to that shortening by walls, columns, and foundations creates forces that can crack the slab and reduce the effective prestress. Analyze the slab for the effects of restraint, and detail construction joints, expansion joints, or pour strips to allow the shortening to occur before the restraints are engaged, because a slab that is restrained before it shortens develops tension that cracks the concrete and wastes the prestress. Sequence the stressing in stages, with an initial partial stress to secure the tendons and a final stress after the concrete reaches the required strength, and verify the strength before each stressing stage, because stressing at low strength can damage the anchorage zone. Detail the connections to stiff vertical elements, such as shear walls and elevator cores, to accommodate the slab shortening, using slotted inserts, flexible connections, or isolation joints, because rigid connections to stiff elements lock in restraint forces. Recognize that shortening and restraint are the most common cause of distress in post-tensioned slabs, and that the design must address them explicitly through jointing, sequencing, and connection detailing rather than assuming the slab will shorten freely.

## Common Traps

### Ignoring Restraint To Shortening From Stiff Elements

The engineer designs the slab prestress without considering the restraint from shear walls, cores, or stiff columns that prevent the slab from shortening. The mechanism is that the prestress force shortens the slab, and restraint to that shortening converts the prestress into tension that cracks the slab and reduces the effective compression. The false signal is that the service stresses pass on a freely shortening slab. The harm is cracking at re-entrant corners, at stiff supports, and along restraint lines, because the shortening was restrained. Restraint must be analyzed and accommodated through joints, pour strips, and flexible connections, because a restrained slab does not achieve the assumed prestress.

### Mixing Bonded And Unbonded Provisions

The engineer designs an unbonded system using bonded-system ultimate strength provisions, or vice versa, without recognizing the behavioral difference. The mechanism is that unbonded tendons develop lower stress increase at ultimate because the deformation is distributed over the whole member, while bonded tendons develop a localized stress increase at the critical section. The false signal is that the strength check passes. The harm is a slab with ultimate capacity below the design, because the wrong strand stress at nominal was used. The system-specific provisions and strand stress at nominal must be applied, because bonded and unbonded systems behave differently at ultimate.

### Detailing Anchorages That Cannot Be Stressed Or Inspected

The engineer details anchorages at slab edges or interior points without verifying access for the stressing jack or inspection. The mechanism is that stressing requires physical access for the jack and the operator, and an inaccessible anchorage cannot be stressed or its elongation verified. The false signal is that the anchorage is shown on the drawing. The harm is a tendon that is unstressed or under-stressed, providing no prestress, because it could not be stressed. Anchorages must be detailed with accessible stressing pockets sized for the jack, and stressing records must be required, because the force must be verified.

### Omitting Minimum Bonded Reinforcement In Unbonded Systems

The engineer designs an unbonded post-tensioned slab with tendons only, omitting the minimum bonded reinforcement required by the code. The mechanism is that unbonded tendons alone do not provide crack control or ductility at overload, and the minimum bonded reinforcement exists to ensure crack distribution and ductile behavior. The false signal is that the tendon capacity exceeds the demand. The harm is brittle behavior and uncontrolled cracking at overload, because the bonded reinforcement was omitted. Minimum bonded reinforcement must be provided in unbonded systems per the code, because it provides the crack control and ductility that the unbonded tendons cannot.

## Self-Check

- Is the system, bonded or unbonded, selected and designed with its specific provisions for strand stress at nominal, minimum reinforcement, and corrosion protection?
- Is the tendon layout, banded and distributed, coordinated with columns, openings, and edges, with the drape balancing the intended load and service stresses checked at all critical sections?
- Are anchorage zones detailed with bearing plates, confinement reinforcement, and edge reinforcement, and are stressing pockets accessible and sized for the jack?
- Are stressing records and elongation verification required, with stressing sequenced in stages and concrete strength verified before each stage?
- Has restraint to shortening from shear walls, cores, and stiff columns been analyzed, and are construction joints, pour strips, and flexible connections detailed to accommodate shortening?
- Is minimum bonded reinforcement provided in unbonded systems, and are connections to stiff vertical elements detailed to avoid locking in restraint forces?
- Is the design sealed and reviewed by a licensed professional engineer, with specialist involvement for post-tensioned design?
