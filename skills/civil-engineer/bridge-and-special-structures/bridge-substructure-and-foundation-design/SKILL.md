# Bridge Substructure and Foundation Design

Bridge substructure and foundation design is the engineering of the elements that transfer the superstructure loads to the ground: the piers (the columns, the caps, the footings), the abutments (the stem, the seat, the footing, the wall), and the foundations (the spread footing, the pile, the drilled shaft, the caisson). The substructure carries the vertical load, the lateral load (the wind, the seismic, the braking, the earth pressure), and the movement (the thermal, the creep, the settlement), and the foundation carries these to the ground. The harm this skill prevents is a substructure that is the under-capacity for the combined load and the seismic (the shear or the flexural failure), a foundation that is the under-capacity for the bearing or the pile capacity (the excessive settlement or the bearing failure), or a foundation that is the scour-vulnerable (the undermining and the collapse). Because the bridge failure is the catastrophic and the public-safety-critical, the substructure and the foundation must be designed against the AASHTO LRFD, the scour, and the seismic, with the conservative foundation and the verified ground conditions.

## Core Rules

### Transfer the Superstructure Loads and Movements to the Substructure

The substructure design begins with the transfer of the superstructure loads (the dead, the live, the wind, the braking, the seismic, the earth pressure) and the movements (the thermal, the creep, the shrinkage) to the substructure through the bearings. Establish the load paths and the boundary conditions at the bearings (the fixed, the expansion), and the loads at each substructure unit, with the load combinations per the AASHTO LRFD. Account for the construction sequence (the staged construction, the creep and the shrinkage) and the secondary effects (the prestress, the temperature gradient). A substructure designed with the incomplete or the wrong loads from the superstructure is the under-designed, because the load transfer was not correct.

### Select the Substructure Type for the Site and the Geometry

The substructure type (the pier: the solid wall, the column bent, the pile bent, the hammerhead; the abutment: the stub, the full-height, the integral, the semi-integral) must be selected for the site (the clearance, the geometry, the aesthetics, the navigation), the foundation (the spread, the deep), and the seismic (the ductility, the stiffness). The solid wall pier for the low clearance and the navigation, the column bent for the medium, the hammerhead for the urban and the limited footprint. The integral abutment for the short-to-medium bridge (the jointless, the low-maintenance), the stub abutment for the medium-to-long. Select the type that is the suited to the site and the foundation, and consider the seismic and the maintenance, because the type determines the performance and the cost.

### Design the Pier for the Strength, the Ductility, and the Stability

The pier (the column and the cap) must be designed for the strength (the axial, the flexural, the shear against the AASHTO load combinations), the ductility (the seismic, the capacity-protected, the confinement), and the stability (the slenderness, the P-delta). For the seismic, design the pier for the ductility (the plastic hinge at the base, the confinement, the capacity-protected shear), or the essentially elastic (the low seismic), per the AASHTO seismic provisions. Check the slenderness (the P-delta) for the tall pier, and the foundation flexibility (the soil-structure interaction). A pier designed for the strength without the ductility and the stability may fail the brittle or the buckling in the seismic, because the multiple limit states were not checked.

### Design the Abutment for the Earth Pressure, the Load, and the Movement

The abutment must be designed for the earth pressure (the active, the at-rest, the passive, the surcharge), the superstructure load (the vertical, the lateral), and the movement (the thermal, the settlement). For the stub abutment, design the stem for the earth pressure and the bearing seat for the load, with the footing for the bearing and the sliding. For the integral abutment, design for the cyclic earth pressure (the thermal movement) and the pile flexibility (the horizontal movement), and limit the length for the integral (the movement and the pile stress). Account for the approach slab and the settlement, because the approach settlement creates the bump and the load on the abutment. An abutment designed without the earth pressure and the movement is the under-designed, because the earth and the thermal were not in the load.

### Design the Foundation for the Bearing, the Lateral, and the Settlement

The foundation (the spread footing, the pile, the drilled shaft) must be designed for the bearing (the vertical capacity against the allowable bearing pressure or the pile capacity), the lateral (the shear capacity, the passive resistance, the pile lateral), and the settlement (the total and the differential). For the spread footing, verify the bearing (the factored load against the factored bearing resistance), the sliding, the eccentricity, and the settlement. For the pile and the drilled shaft, verify the axial capacity (the skin friction and the end bearing, with the group effect), the lateral capacity (the p-y analysis, the group), and the settlement. Use the site-specific geotechnical data (the borings, the tests), and the conservative capacity, because the foundation is the hidden and the difficult to inspect. A foundation designed without the site-specific data and the conservative capacity may fail the bearing or the excessive settlement, because the ground was the assumed.

### Evaluate the Scour and the Foundation Depth

For the bridge over the water, the scour (the local at the pier, the contraction at the bridge, the long-term degradation) must be evaluated, and the foundation depth must account for the scour. Evaluate the scour with the HEC-18 (the hydraulic analysis, the sediment, the pier and the abutment geometry), and design the foundation for the total scour (the local plus the contraction plus the long-term), with the pile or the drilled shaft below the scour depth, or the spread footing below the scour (the less common). The scour is the leading cause of the bridge failure, and the foundation that does not account for the scour is the undermining risk, because the scour was not in the depth.

### Verify the Global Stability and the Seismic Performance

The global stability (the slope, the abutment, the overall) and the seismic performance (the displacement, the ductility) must be verified. For the slope and the abutment, verify the global stability (the circular and the non-circular failure) under the static and the seismic (the pseudo-static). For the seismic, verify the substructure displacement (the seismic displacement against the seat width), the ductility (the plastic hinge), and the liquefaction (the foundation). A substructure that is the stable under the static but the unstable under the seismic or the liquefaction may fail, because the global and the seismic were not verified.

## Common Traps

### The Load Transfer Incomplete

The substructure is designed with the incomplete loads (the missing seismic, the missing thermal, the missing earth pressure), and the demand is underestimated. The trap is that the primary loads are the transferred, while the secondary or the extreme loads are the missing, and the demand is the underestimated. The false signal is the designed substructure; the harm is the under-designed element, because the load transfer was not complete.

### The Foundation Without The Site-Specific Data

The foundation is designed with the assumed bearing pressure or the pile capacity, without the site-specific borings and the tests, and the capacity is the overestimated. The trap is that the assumption is the convenient, while the actual ground is the weaker, and the capacity is the overestimated. The false signal is the designed foundation; the harm is the bearing failure or the excessive settlement, because the site-specific data was not used.

### The Scour Not Evaluated

The bridge over the water is designed without the scour evaluation, and the foundation depth does not account for the scour, and the foundation is undermined. The trap is that the scour is the not evaluated, while it is the leading cause of the bridge failure, and the foundation is the vulnerable. The false signal is the designed foundation; the harm is the undermining and the collapse, because the scour was not in the depth.

### The Seismic Ductility Not Provided

The pier is designed for the strength, but the seismic ductility (the confinement, the plastic hinge) is not provided, and the pier fails the brittle. The trap is that the strength is the provided, while the ductility is the not, and the seismic demand exceeds the capacity. The false signal is the strong pier; the harm is the brittle failure, because the ductility was not designed.

### The Abutment Without The Earth Pressure And The Movement

The abutment is designed for the vertical load, but the earth pressure and the thermal movement are the not accounted, and the abutment is the under-designed. The trap is that the vertical load is the designed, while the earth and the thermal are the missing, and the abutment is the under-designed. The false signal is the designed abutment; the harm is the under-capacity, because the earth and the thermal were not in the load.

### The Liquefaction Not Evaluated

The bridge in the seismic area with the saturated sand is designed without the liquefaction evaluation, and the foundation loses the support in the seismic. The trap is that the seismic is the considered for the inertia, while the liquefaction is the not evaluated, and the foundation is the vulnerable. The false signal is the seismic design; the harm is the liquefaction-induced failure, because the liquefaction was not assessed.

## Self-Check

- Are the superstructure loads and movements transferred to the substructure through the bearings, with the complete load combinations per the AASHTO LRFD?
- Is the substructure type (the pier, the abutment) selected for the site, the geometry, the foundation, and the seismic?
- Is the pier designed for the strength, the ductility (the seismic confinement and the plastic hinge), and the stability (the slenderness, the P-delta)?
- Is the abutment designed for the earth pressure, the load, and the movement (the thermal, the settlement, the approach slab)?
- Is the foundation designed for the bearing, the lateral, and the settlement, with the site-specific geotechnical data and the conservative capacity?
- Is the scour evaluated (the HEC-18) and the foundation depth set below the total scour, for the bridge over the water?
- Is the global stability (the slope, the abutment) verified under the static and the seismic, and the liquefaction evaluated for the saturated sand?
- Is the seismic displacement verified against the seat width, and the ductility and the confinement provided for the pier?
