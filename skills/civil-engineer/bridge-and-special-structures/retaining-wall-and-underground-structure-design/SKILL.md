# Retaining Wall and Underground Structure Design

Retaining wall and underground structure design is the engineering of the elements that retain the soil and the water and the elements that are buried: the retaining walls (the gravity, the cantilever, the mechanically stabilised earth, the anchored, the sheet pile, the secant pile), the underground structures (the basement, the tunnel, the culvert, the vault), and the excavation support (the soldier pile, the lagging, the tieback, the strut). These elements carry the earth pressure, the water pressure, and the surcharge, and they interact with the ground and the adjacent structures. The harm this skill prevents is a wall that fails the external stability (the sliding, the overturning, the bearing), a wall that fails the global stability (the deep-seated failure), an underground structure that leaks (the flooded basement, the deteriorated tunnel), or an excavation support that fails (the collapse, the damage to the adjacent). Because the retaining and the underground are the ground-dependent and the failure-impactful, the design must be against the site-specific geotechnical data, the conservative earth pressure, and the verified stability, with the drainage and the waterproofing.

## Core Rules

### Determine the Earth Pressure and the Surcharge for the Wall and the Movement

The earth pressure (the active, the at-rest, the passive) depends on the wall movement, and the surcharge (the traffic, the building, the construction) depends on the adjacent load. Determine the earth pressure for the wall type and the movement: the active for the wall that can move (the cantilever, the MSE), the at-rest for the wall that cannot (the basement, the restrained), the passive for the embedded portion. Determine the surcharge (the Boussinesq or the equivalent strip), and the seismic earth pressure (the Mononobe-Okabe). Use the site-specific soil parameters (the unit weight, the friction angle, the cohesion), and the conservative value, because the earth pressure is the dominant load and the soil is the variable. A wall designed with the wrong earth pressure (the at-rest used for the active, the surcharge missing) is the under- or the over-designed, because the pressure and the movement were not matched.

### Select the Wall Type for the Height, the Soil, and the Constraint

The wall type must be selected for the height, the soil, the site constraint, and the construction. The gravity and the cantilever for the low-to-medium height (to 6 to 8 metres), the MSE for the medium-to-high (to 20 metres and above), the anchored for the high and the constrained, the sheet pile and the secant pile for the excavation and the waterfront. Select the type that is the suited to the height and the soil, and the constructible, and consider the property line, the utility, the traffic, and the aesthetics. A wall type that is the wrong for the height or the constraint is the uneconomical or the unbuildable, because the type was not matched.

### Verify the External Stability: Sliding, Overturning, and Bearing

The external stability (the sliding, the overturning, the bearing) must be verified for the wall. Verify the sliding (the resisting force over the driving force, with the factor of safety), the overturning (the resisting moment over the driving moment), and the bearing (the factored bearing pressure against the allowable). Account for the water pressure (the hydrostatic, the buoyancy) and the surcharge, and the passive resistance (the toe, the key). A wall that fails the external stability slides, overturns, or settles, because the stability was not verified.

### Verify the Global Stability and the Deep-Seated Failure

The global stability (the deep-seated failure surface that passes below or through the wall) must be verified, especially for the MSE, the anchored, and the wall on the slope. Perform the slope stability analysis (the circular and the non-circular, the static and the seismic), with the soil parameters and the water, and verify the factor of safety. A wall that is the externally stable but the globally unstable fails the deep-seated, because the global stability was not checked.

### Design the Internal Stability and the Structural Capacity

The internal stability (the reinforcement, the anchor, the stem, the footing) must be designed for the structural capacity. For the MSE, design the reinforcement (the length, the spacing, the pullout, the rupture), and the connection. For the anchored, design the anchor (the capacity, the spacing, the corrosion protection, the proof test). For the cantilever, design the stem (the flexural and the shear), the footing (the flexural, the shear, the key), and the reinforcement. For the sheet pile, design the section modulus (the bending), the embedment (the passive), and the anchor or the strut. A wall that is the externally and the globally stable but the internally under-designed fails the structural, because the internal capacity was not verified.

### Design the Drainage and the Waterproofing

The drainage (the wall, the subdrain, the weep hole) and the waterproofing (the membrane, the waterstop, the joint) must be designed, because the water pressure and the leakage are the primary cause of the wall and the underground failure. Provide the drainage (the granular backfill, the geotextile, the perforated pipe at the heel, the weep holes) to prevent the hydrostatic buildup, and the waterproofing for the underground (the membrane, the waterstop at the construction joint, the crystalline). Design the wall for the water pressure if the drainage fails (the conservative), because the drainage may clog. A wall or an underground without the drainage and the waterproofing fails the water, because the water pressure or the leakage was not addressed.

### Coordinate With the Ground and the Adjacent Structures

The retaining wall and the underground structure interact with the ground and the adjacent structures, and the coordination is required. For the excavation support, evaluate the deformation (the lateral and the vertical) and the impact on the adjacent (the building, the utility, the road), and the monitoring (the inclinometer, the settlement point). For the basement, coordinate with the foundation and the superstructure, and the buoyancy (the uplift). For the tunnel, evaluate the ground loss and the surface settlement. A wall or an underground that is designed in isolation, without the adjacent impact, may damage the adjacent, because the coordination was not performed.

## Common Traps

### The Earth Pressure Mismatched To The Movement

The wall is designed with the active earth pressure, but the wall is the restrained (the basement, the integral abutment) and cannot move, and the actual pressure is the at-rest or the higher. The trap is that the active is the used, while the wall is the restrained, and the pressure is the underestimated. The false signal is the designed wall; the harm is the under-designed wall, because the pressure and the movement were not matched.

### The Global Stability Not Checked

The wall is verified for the external stability (the sliding, the overturning, the bearing), but the global stability (the deep-seated) is not checked, and the wall fails the deep-seated. The trap is that the external is the checked, while the global is the not, and the failure is the deep-seated. The false signal is the externally stable wall; the harm is the global failure, because the deep-seated was not analysed.

### The Water Pressure Not Accounted

The wall is designed for the dry condition, but the drainage fails or the water table is the higher, and the hydrostatic pressure is the not accounted. The trap is that the dry is the assumed, while the water is the present, and the pressure is the underestimated. The false signal is the designed wall; the harm is the sliding or the overturning from the water pressure, because the water was not in the load.

### The Adjacent Impact Not Evaluated

The excavation support is designed for the stability, but the deformation and the impact on the adjacent are the not evaluated, and the adjacent building or the utility is damaged. The trap is that the support is the stable, while the deformation is the excessive, and the adjacent is the damaged. The false signal is the stable support; the harm is the adjacent damage, because the deformation and the impact were not evaluated.

### The MSE Reinforcement Under-Designed

The MSE wall is designed for the external stability, but the reinforcement (the length, the pullout, the rupture) is the under-designed, and the wall fails the internal. The trap is that the external is the verified, while the internal is the not, and the reinforcement fails. The false signal is the externally stable wall; the harm is the internal failure, because the reinforcement was not verified.

### The Waterproofing Inadequate

The underground structure is designed for the structural capacity, but the waterproofing (the membrane, the waterstop) is the inadequate, and the structure leaks. The trap is that the structural is the designed, while the waterproofing is the not, and the structure leaks. The false signal is the strong structure; the harm is the leakage and the flooding, because the waterproofing was not detailed.

## Self-Check

- Is the earth pressure (the active, the at-rest, the passive) matched to the wall movement, and the surcharge and the seismic included, with the site-specific soil parameters?
- Is the wall type selected for the height, the soil, the site constraint, and the construction?
- Is the external stability (the sliding, the overturning, the bearing) verified, with the water pressure and the surcharge?
- Is the global stability (the deep-seated) verified, with the slope stability analysis and the factor of safety?
- Is the internal stability (the reinforcement, the anchor, the stem, the footing) designed for the structural capacity?
- Is the drainage (the granular backfill, the subdrain, the weep hole) and the waterproofing (the membrane, the waterstop) designed, and the wall checked for the water pressure if the drainage fails?
- Is the coordination with the ground and the adjacent structures (the deformation, the impact, the monitoring) performed, for the excavation support and the underground?
- Is the buoyancy (the uplift) evaluated for the basement, and the ground loss and the settlement for the tunnel?
