# Bridge Superstructure and Deck Design

Bridge superstructure and deck design is the engineering of the structural system that spans between the supports and carries the highway or the railway traffic: the deck slab that distributes the wheel loads, the primary members (the girders, the trusses, the arches, the boxes) that span to the supports, and the secondary members (the diaphragms, the cross-frames) that brace and distribute. The bridge is the high-stakes structure: the public safety, the heavy dynamic live load, the fatigue, the harsh exposure (the deicing salt, the freeze-thaw, the marine), and the long service life (the 75-to-100-year design). The harm this skill prevents is a deck that is the under-thickness or the under-reinforced and cracks and spalls (the most common bridge deterioration), a primary member that is the under-capacity for the permit load or the fatigue (the catastrophic failure), or a superstructure that is the difficult to inspect and maintain (the hidden deterioration that progresses to the failure). Because the bridge is the safety-critical and the long-life, the design must be against the AASHTO LRFD, the fatigue, and the durability, and verified for the constructability and the inspectability.

## Core Rules

### Select the Superstructure Type for the Span, the Load, and the Site

The superstructure type (the slab, the girder, the box girder, the truss, the arch, the cable-stayed, the suspension) must be selected for the span range, the load (the highway, the railway, the pedestrian), the site (the clearance, the aesthetics, the foundation), and the constructability. The slab for the short span (to 15 metres), the girder (the steel or the prestressed concrete) for the medium span (15 to 50 metres), the box girder and the truss for the long span (50 to 200 metres), and the cable-stayed and the suspension for the very long span (above 200 metres). Select the type that is the economical and the constructible for the span and the site, and consider the maintenance and the inspectability, because the type determines the life-cycle cost and the inspection access.

### Apply the AASHTO LRFD Load Combinations and the Live Load

The AASHTO LRFD Bridge Design Specifications define the load combinations (the Strength I to V, the Service I to IV, the Extreme Event) and the design live load (the HL-93: the design truck or the tandem, plus the design lane load). Apply the load combinations with the appropriate load factors, and the live load with the dynamic load allowance (the impact) and the multiple presence factor. Distribute the live load to the girders with the lever rule or the AASHTO distribution factors (the S over D method or the refined method), and account for the skew, the curvature, and the continuity. A design that uses the simplified distribution without the verification, or the wrong live load, underestimates the demand, because the AASHTO method was not applied correctly.

### Design the Deck Slab for the Wheel Load and the Durability

The deck slab must be designed for the wheel load (the punching shear, the flexure) and the durability (the thickness, the cover, the reinforcement, the cracking). Use the empirical design (the AASHTO method for the concrete deck with the adequate depth and the four layers of the isotropic reinforcement) or the conventional design (the strip method with the wheel load), and provide the minimum thickness (commonly 200 to 225 mm), the cover (the 50 to 65 mm for the top mat, the 25 to 40 mm for the bottom), and the reinforcement (the two layers, the epoxy-coated or the stainless for the corrosion). Provide the deck drainage and the waterproofing membrane, because the standing water and the chloride penetration are the primary cause of the deck deterioration. A deck that is the under-thickness or the under-cover cracks and spalls, because the durability was not in the design.

### Design the Primary Member for the Strength, the Fatigue, and the Serviceability

The primary member (the girder, the truss member) must be designed for the strength (the flexural and the shear capacity against the Strength load combination), the fatigue (the fatigue load and the stress range against the AASHTO fatigue category), and the serviceability (the deflection, the cracking, the vibration). For the steel girder, verify the flexural strength (the compact or the non-compact section), the shear (the unstiffened or the stiffened web), the fatigue (the detail category and the stress range), and the serviceability (the live load deflection, commonly the span over 800 to 1000). For the prestressed concrete girder, verify the flexural strength, the shear, the serviceability (the camber, the deflection, the cracking), and the prestress loss. A member designed for the strength without the fatigue and the serviceability may fail the fatigue or the poor serviceability, because the multiple limit states were not checked.

### Detail the Diaphragms, the Cross-Frames, and the Lateral Bracing

The diaphragms and the cross-frames brace the girders against the lateral-torsional buckling during the construction and the service, distribute the load between the girders, and stabilise the system. Detail the diaphragms at the supports and the intermediate points (the spacing per the AASHTO), with the connection to the girders that transfers the lateral force, and the lateral bracing (the bottom flange) for the long span or the construction. A superstructure without the adequate bracing may buckle laterally during the construction or the service, because the bracing was the under-detailed.

### Detail the Joints, the Bearings, and the Appurtenances for the Movement and the Maintenance

The deck joints (the expansion, the fixed) accommodate the thermal and the live load movement, and the bearings transfer the load to the substructure while allowing the rotation and the movement. Detail the joints for the movement range (the thermal, the creep, the shrinkage, the seismic), with the seal and the drainage, and select the bearings (the elastomeric pad, the pot, the disc) for the load and the movement. Provide the appurtenances (the railing, the barrier, the lighting, the drainage), and the access for the inspection and the maintenance. The joints and the bearings are the high-maintenance elements, and the poor detailing (the leaking joint, the frozen bearing) causes the substructure deterioration and the maintenance burden, because the movement and the maintenance were not in the detailing.

### Address the Curved and the Skew Bridge Effects

For the curved and the skew bridge, the load distribution and the torsion differ from the straight and the square bridge, and the effects must be accounted. The curved girder has the torsion and the lateral load, and requires the refined analysis (the grid, the finite element) and the bracing. The skew bridge has the load distribution that concentrates at the obtuse corner and the differential deflection, and the deck and the bearing design must account for the skew. A design that uses the straight-bridge simplification for the curved or the skew bridge underestimates the torsion and the concentration, because the geometry effects were not accounted.

## Common Traps

### The Deck Under-Thickness Or Under-Cover

The deck is designed with the thin slab or the reduced cover, and the deck cracks and the chloride penetrates, and the deck spalls and deteriorates. The trap is that the thin deck or the reduced cover is the cost saving, while the durability is the compromised, and the deck deteriorates. The false signal is the adequate structural capacity; the harm is the rapid deck deterioration, because the durability was not in the design.

### The Live Load Distribution Oversimplified

The live load distribution uses the simplified S over D method for the bridge that does not qualify (the skew, the curvature, the wide girder spacing), and the demand is underestimated. The trap is that the simplified method is the convenient, while the bridge does not qualify, and the distribution is the wrong. The false signal is the calculated distribution factor; the harm is the under-designed girder, because the simplified method was used outside its range.

### The Fatigue Not Checked

The primary member is designed for the strength, but the fatigue (the stress range and the detail category) is not checked, and the member develops the fatigue crack. The trap is that the strength is the checked, while the fatigue is the not, and the cyclic live load causes the crack. The false signal is the strength capacity; the harm is the fatigue crack, because the fatigue limit state was not verified.

### The Joint Leaking And The Bearing Frozen

The deck joint is detailed for the inadequate movement range or without the seal, and the joint leaks, and the water and the chloride damage the substructure. The trap is that the joint is the installed, while the seal and the movement are the inadequate, and the joint leaks. The false signal is the installed joint; the harm is the substructure deterioration, because the joint leaked and the water reached the bearing and the pier.

### The Curved Or Skew Treated As Straight

The curved or the skew bridge is analysed with the straight, square-bridge method, and the torsion and the concentration are the not accounted. The trap is that the straight method is the used, while the bridge is the curved or the skew, and the effects are the not accounted. The false signal is the completed analysis; the harm is the under-designed member, because the geometry effects were not in the analysis.

### The Inspectability Not Considered

The superstructure is designed without the access for the inspection, and the critical members (the bearing, the bottom flange, the connection) are the inaccessible, and the deterioration is the hidden. The trap is that the design is the completed, while the access is the not provided, and the inspection is the difficult. The false signal is the completed design; the harm is the hidden deterioration, because the inspectability was not in the design.

## Self-Check

- Is the superstructure type selected for the span, the load, the site, and the constructability, with the maintenance and the inspectability considered?
- Are the AASHTO LRFD load combinations and the HL-93 live load applied, with the dynamic allowance, the multiple presence, and the distribution factors appropriate for the geometry?
- Is the deck slab designed for the wheel load and the durability (the thickness, the cover, the reinforcement, the drainage, the waterproofing)?
- Are the primary members designed for the strength, the fatigue (the stress range and the detail category), and the serviceability (the deflection, the cracking, the vibration)?
- Are the diaphragms, the cross-frames, and the lateral bracing detailed for the lateral stability and the load distribution?
- Are the joints, the bearings, and the appurtenances detailed for the movement, the seal, the drainage, and the maintenance access?
- For the curved or the skew bridge, are the torsion, the concentration, and the differential deflection accounted with the refined analysis?
- Is the inspectability provided, with the access to the bearings, the flanges, and the connections?
