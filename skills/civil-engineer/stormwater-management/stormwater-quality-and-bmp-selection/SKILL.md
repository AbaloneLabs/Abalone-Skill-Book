# Stormwater Quality and BMP Selection

Stormwater quality and BMP selection is the engineering of the treatment practices that remove the pollutants development generates from the runoff before it reaches the receiving water, so that the discharge satisfies the water-quality conditions of the MS4 or NPDES stormwater permit and does not degrade the stream, lake, or estuary that receives it. Stormwater carries sediment, nutrients, metals, hydrocarbons, bacteria, and trash from pavement and lawns, and a total maximum daily load may impose a waste-load allocation that the site must meet. The harm this skill prevents is a BMP selected because it is on the approved list but sized for the wrong volume, an infiltration practice placed in impermeable soil or above a high water table where it fails silently, or a maintenance plan so vague that the practice clogs and ceases to treat within a few years. Because post-construction stormwater permits are enforceable and the receiving water quality is a public resource, the BMP selection must be defensible against the pollutant of concern, the actual site soil, and the maintenance commitment over the facility's life.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Permit, the Pollutants of Concern, and Any TMDL Allocation

Before selecting any practice, establish the governing stormwater regulation: the MS4 permit or the construction and post-construction NPDES stormwater requirements, and any total maximum daily load that assigns a waste-load allocation to the site's discharge. Identify the pollutants of concern from the receiving water impairment list, because the BMP must target those pollutants: total suspended solids for nearly all sites, nutrients where the receiving water is nutrient-impaired, metals and hydrocarbons where road runoff dominates, and bacteria where recreational contact is the use. Confirm the required water-quality volume or flow (commonly the runoff from the first 25 to 38 mm of rainfall, or the one-year storm volume) and the target removal efficiency the rule requires. These requirements define which practices are eligible and how they must be sized; a BMP selected without the pollutants of concern and the TMDL established may treat the wrong constituent or fail the allocation.

### Size the Practice for the Water-Quality Volume and the Documented Removal

Size each structural BMP for the water-quality volume or the water-quality flow using the approved method, and confirm that the sizing meets the design criteria for the practice type, not merely the intent. Bioretention requires a media depth, an area, and a drawdown time that pond and filter the volume through the planting soil; vegetated swales require a length, slope, and residence time that settle and filter the flow; permeable pavement requires a reservoir depth and subgrade infiltration that store the volume; and wet ponds and constructed wetlands require a permanent pool and extended detention volume sized to the contributing area. Use the documented pollutant removal percentages from the approved reference, not optimistic vendor claims, and confirm that the removal targets the pollutant of concern at the percentage the permit requires. A practice sized for the wrong volume or credited with an unverified removal fails the water-quality requirement regardless of how well it is built.

### Verify Infiltration Feasibility Against Soil, Groundwater, and Hotspots

For infiltration practices (bioretention, permeable pavement, infiltration trenches and basins), verify the actual site conditions before specifying the practice, because an infiltration facility in the wrong soil fails immediately and silently. Perform infiltration testing or confirm the hydrologic soil group, and confirm the seasonal high groundwater table and bedrock depth provide the required separation (commonly 0.6 to 1.0 m below the facility bottom). Exclude infiltration from stormwater hotspots such as fueling areas, vehicle service yards, and industrial sites where infiltrating contaminated runoff would pollute groundwater, and from sites with contaminated soils. Provide pretreatment (a forebay, grass filter, or hydrodynamic separator) to remove sediment before it reaches the infiltration surface, because sediment loading clogs the infiltrating media and shortens the facility life. An infiltration practice placed without soil and groundwater verification ponds, kills vegetation, breeds mosquitoes, and provides no treatment.

### Provide Pretreatment, Flow Distribution, and Overflow for Every Practice

Every treatment practice needs pretreatment to trap sediment and trash where it can be removed economically, flow distribution that spreads the runoff evenly across the treatment area, and an overflow that passes the large storms without scouring the media or washing out the vegetation. Provide a forebay or pretreatment cell sized to a fraction of the water-quality volume and accessible for cleanout, a level spreader or distribution pipe that prevents channelisation, and an overflow weir or grate set at the design ponding depth that bypasses the design storm to a stable conveyance. Confirm that the bypass does not short-circuit untreated flow around the practice and that the overflow discharge is to a stable point. A practice without pretreatment clogs at the inlet, and one without proper distribution channels the flow and treats only a fraction of its area.

### Specify Long-Term Maintenance and the Responsible Party

Stormwater BMPs fail without maintenance: bioretention media clogs, permeable pavement surfaces seal, swales erode, and wet ponds fill with sediment. Specify the maintenance requirements for each practice (the inspection frequency, the sediment removal trigger, the media replacement interval, and the vegetation management) and identify the responsible party and the funding mechanism, because a practice that cannot be maintained will fail within a few years regardless of how well it was designed. Provide maintenance access to the forebay, the outlet, and the treatment area, and require an operation and maintenance agreement or easement where the regulation demands it. A BMP designed without a maintenance plan is a liability that the owner inherits and the regulator eventually cites.

## Common Traps

### The BMP Sized for the Wrong Volume

The practice is sized using the peak flow or a rainfall depth different from the rule's water-quality volume, and the sizing is declared compliant. The trap is that the practice treats the wrong storm, while the small frequent storms that carry most of the pollutant load pass through partially treated or bypassed, and the receiving water receives the load the rule was meant to capture. The false signal is the completed sizing; the harm is water-quality noncompliance traced to a volume definition that did not match the permit.

### The Infiltration Practice in Impermeable Soil

An infiltration or bioretention practice is specified because it is the preferred low-impact-development option, but the site soil is clay or the water table is high and no infiltration test was performed. The trap is that the facility meets the design intent on paper, while in the field it ponds, kills the vegetation, breeds mosquitoes, and provides no infiltration or treatment. The false signal is the approved practice on the plan; the harm is a failed facility that provides neither treatment nor amenity and must be rebuilt at additional cost.

### The Vendor Removal Claim Accepted Without Verification

The BMP is credited with a pollutant removal percentage from a manufacturer's literature that exceeds the verified performance in the approved reference. The trap is that the design shows compliance with the removal target, while the actual removal is lower and the discharge exceeds the allocation or the TMDL waste-load. The false signal is the compliant removal credit; the harm is a receiving water that continues to be impaired because the practice never performed as claimed.

### The Practice Without a Maintenance Plan

The BMP is designed and built but the maintenance requirements, the access, and the responsible party are not specified. The trap is that the facility treats runoff when new, while within a few seasons the media clogs, the vegetation fails, and the practice ceases to function, and no one is obligated to restore it. The false signal is the functioning new facility; the harm is a practice that fails silently and leaves the site in noncompliance for years until the regulator inspects.

## Self-Check

- Are the governing MS4 or NPDES stormwater requirements established, including the pollutants of concern, the water-quality volume or flow, and any TMDL waste-load allocation?
- Is each structural BMP sized for the rule's water-quality volume or flow, with the documented removal percentages from the approved reference targeting the pollutant of concern?
- For infiltration practices, are the soil permeability, the seasonal high groundwater and bedrock separation, and hotspot exclusions verified, with pretreatment provided?
- Does every practice have pretreatment, even flow distribution, and an overflow that passes large storms without scouring media or short-circuiting untreated flow?
- Are the maintenance requirements, access, responsible party, and funding mechanism specified for each practice, with an agreement or easement where required?
- Does the selected BMP suite target all the pollutants of concern and meet the removal or allocation the permit imposes, not just the suspended solids?
