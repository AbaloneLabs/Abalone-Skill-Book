# Stormwater Detention and Water Quality Design

Stormwater management design is the engineering of how a developed site stores, releases, and treats the runoff that development generates, so that downstream flooding is not worsened and the water leaving the site is clean enough to protect the receiving stream. Development replaces vegetated, permeable ground with roofs and pavement, multiplying the volume and peak rate of runoff and introducing pollutants that the pre-development watershed never produced. The detention basin, the outlet structure, and the water-quality treatment are the engineered compensation for that change. The harm this skill prevents is a basin that passes the design storm on paper but overtops at the real storm, an outlet that releases more than the pre-development rate and floods downstream owners, or a treatment system that does not actually remove the pollutants the permit requires. Because stormwater permits are enforceable and downstream flooding is a liability, the design must be defensible against both the design storm and the regulator.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Permit Release Rates and Water-Quality Requirements First

Before any sizing, establish the governing permit and the release-rate and water-quality requirements it imposes. Most stormwater rules require that the post-development peak rate of discharge not exceed the pre-development rate for one or more design storms (commonly the 1-, 2-, 10-, and 25-year events), and some require attenuation of the 100-year event or control of the channel-forming flow. Water-quality requirements typically mandate treatment of a defined volume (the water-quality volume, often the runoff from the first 25 mm of rainfall, or the one-year storm) by a method on the approved list, with a target removal efficiency for total suspended solids and, in some jurisdictions, nutrients or metals. These requirements are the design constraints; without them established and documented, the sizing is arbitrary.

### Characterise Pre- and Post-Development Hydrology Separately

Compute the pre-development and post-development peak flows and volumes by the approved hydrologic method, with separate curve numbers or runoff coefficients, times of concentration, and drainage areas for each condition. The pre-development condition is the regulatory baseline, and it must be characterised realistically: existing impervious area, actual soil and cover, and the meadow or woods condition that the rule specifies, not an artificially high baseline that makes the post-development condition look compliant. The post-development condition must include all proposed impervious area and the actual proposed cover, with the curve numbers and times of concentration updated. The required storage is the difference between the post-development inflow hydrograph and the allowable release, and it cannot be computed without both hydrographs.

### Route the Inflow Hydrograph Through the Basin Storage

Size the basin by routing the post-development design-storm hydrograph through the proposed storage, using the storage-indication (level-pool) routing method, not by a simplified peak-flow subtraction. The routing accounts for the fact that the basin fills as it releases, that the outlet discharge varies with the rising and falling head, and that the storage available at each elevation determines the attenuation. Confirm that the routed peak outflow does not exceed the allowable release rate at each design storm, that the basin does not overtop at the largest design storm, and that the maximum water surface stays within the basin footprint with freeboard. Provide an emergency spillway sized to pass the design storm (or the storm beyond it) safely if the principal outlet clogs, because outlet clogging is a common failure and the spillway is the last line of defence against catastrophic embankment failure.

### Design the Outlet Structure for Multi-Stage Control

Most basins must control multiple design storms with one outlet structure, achieved by a multi-stage arrangement: a low-flow orifice or water-quality outlet at the base, an orifice or weir at the 1- or 2-year stage, a larger opening or weir at the 10-year stage, and the emergency spillway at the 100-year stage. Size each opening by the orifice or weir equation at the head appropriate to its stage, and confirm that the combined discharge at each design-storm peak does not exceed the allowable release for that storm. Verify that the low-flow orifice is sized to release the water-quality volume over the required drawdown time (commonly 24 to 48 hours) to settle pollutants and to avoid prolonged standing water. Protect each orifice against clogging with a trash rack or screen sized so that the open area far exceeds the orifice area, because a clogged orifice converts a controlled basin into an uncontrolled one.

### Provide Water-Quality Treatment Suited to the Pollutant and the Site

Select the water-quality treatment from the approved list and size it for the water-quality volume or flow. Wet ponds, extended detention basins, constructed wetlands, sand filters, bioretention, vegetated swales, and hydrodynamic separators each have design criteria for area, volume, residence time, vegetation, and media, and each removes different pollutant fractions with different efficiency. Confirm that the selected treatment targets the pollutants of concern (sediment, nutrients, metals, oil and grease, bacteria) and that the sizing meets the criteria, not just the intent. For infiltration practices, confirm the soil permeability and the separation from groundwater and bedrock, because an infiltration basin built in impermeable soil or above a high water table will fail immediately and silently.

### Address Conveyance, Overflow, and Downstream Effects

The basin is one element in a system. Confirm that the conveyance to the basin (channels, pipes, swales) carries the design flow without erosion or surcharge, that the basin overflow path to the receiving stream is stable and does not concentrate flow onto a downstream property, and that the release does not worsen flooding downstream by synchronising with peak flow from another part of the watershed. Perform a downstream analysis to the point where the site discharge no longer affects the receiving water, because a basin that controls the site peak but synchronises it with a downstream tributary can increase, not decrease, the downstream peak.

### Provide for Maintenance, Access, and Safety

Stormwater facilities fail without maintenance: basins fill with sediment, orifices clog, vegetation overgrows, and inlets erode. Design for maintenance by providing all-weather access to the forebay, the outlet structure, and the sediment removal area; by specifying a forebay to trap sediment where it can be removed economically; and by documenting the maintenance requirements and the responsible party. Address safety by grading side slopes to be survivable (commonly 1V:3H or flatter above and below the permanent pool), by providing a safety bench around wet ponds, and by fencing or signing where warranted. A basin that cannot be maintained will fail within a few years, regardless of how well it was designed.

## Common Traps

### The Peak Subtraction That Ignores Routing

The required storage is computed as the simple difference between the post-development peak flow and the allowable release, multiplied by the storm duration, without routing the hydrograph through the storage. The trap is that the arithmetic produces a storage volume that looks defensible, while the actual basin must store the entire rising hydrograph above the release rate, which is far larger than the peak-subtraction estimate. The false signal is the completed, dimensioned basin; the harm is a basin that overtops at the design storm because it was undersized by a method that does not represent the physics of storage.

### The Pre-Development Baseline Inflated

The pre-development condition is characterised with a high curve number or an already-developed cover, so that the pre-development peak is high and the required attenuation is small. The trap is that the analysis "complies" with the release-rate rule, while the baseline is inflated and the post-development release actually exceeds the true pre-development rate, worsening downstream flooding. The false signal is the compliant-looking release rate; the harm is downstream property that floods more severely than before development, with the liability traced to the mischaracterised baseline.

### The Orifice That Clogs

The low-flow or water-quality orifice is small (often 50 to 100 mm) and is protected by a trash rack with insufficient open area, or by none at all. The trap is that the outlet passes the design flow when clean, while in service the orifice clogs with debris, sediment, or vegetation, and the basin fills to the next stage or to the spillway, releasing at a rate far above the permitted value or overtopping. The false signal is the hydraulic adequacy of the clean orifice; the harm is an uncontrolled basin that floods downstream or fails the embankment at a storm the design was supposed to pass.

### The Infiltration Basin In Impermeable Soil

An infiltration basin or bioretention facility is specified because it is the preferred low-impact-development practice, but the site soil is clay or the water table is high, and no permeability test was performed. The trap is that the facility meets the design intent on paper, while in the field it ponds, kills the vegetation, breeds mosquitoes, and provides no infiltration or treatment. The false signal is the approved practice on the plan; the harm is a failed facility that provides neither detention nor water quality and that must be rebuilt at additional cost.

### The Single-Storm Design That Misses The Water-Quality Volume

The basin is sized and routed for the 10- or 25-year peak attenuation, but the water-quality volume and its drawdown time are not separately checked. The trap is that the basin controls the large storm, while the small, frequent storms that carry most of the pollutant load pass through too quickly for treatment, and the receiving stream receives the full pollutant load of the development. The false signal is the compliant peak control; the harm is water-quality permit noncompliance and the degradation of the receiving stream that the water-quality requirement was meant to prevent.

### The Downstream Synchronisation

A basin controls the site peak and releases it at the pre-development rate, but the release timing synchronises with the peak from a downstream tributary, increasing the combined peak downstream. The trap is that the site "complies" with the release-rate rule, while the downstream effect is an increase in flooding, not a decrease. The false signal is the on-site compliance; the harm is downstream owners who flood more than before the development, with the cause invisible without a downstream routing analysis.

## Self-Check

- Are the permit release rates and water-quality requirements established and documented, including the design storms and the water-quality volume?
- Are the pre- and post-development hydrology computed separately, with the pre-development baseline characterised realistically and not inflated?
- Is the basin sized by routing the design-storm hydrograph through the storage, with the routed outflow verified against the allowable release at each storm?
- Is an emergency spillway provided and sized for the design storm or beyond, with the embankment protected against overtopping if the principal outlet clogs?
- Is the outlet structure multi-stage, with each orifice or weir sized for its design storm and protected by an adequately sized trash rack?
- Is the water-quality volume drawn down over the required time, and is the treatment practice sized and suited to the pollutants of concern and the site soil?
- Is the conveyance to and from the basin adequate, the overflow path stable, and the downstream effect checked to the point of no site impact?
- Are maintenance access, sediment forebay, side slopes, safety bench, and responsible party documented so the facility can be maintained through its design life?
