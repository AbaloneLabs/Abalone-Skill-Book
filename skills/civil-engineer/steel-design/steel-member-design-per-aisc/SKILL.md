# Steel Member Design Per AISC

Steel member design is the work of selecting a rolled or built-up steel section that satisfies strength, stability, and serviceability under the governing load combinations, using the AISC 360 Specification under either the LRFD or ASD method. The apparent simplicity of selecting a shape from a table conceals the real engineering, which is in the stability checks that govern most steel members. Steel is strong and ductile but slender, and most steel failures are stability failures: lateral-torsional buckling of a beam that is not braced, local buckling of a slender flange or web, flexural buckling of a column, or web crippling at a concentrated load. AISC 360 codifies these checks through the classification of sections as compact, noncompact, or slender, through the unbraced length and effective length factors, and through the design strengths for each limit state. The agent working in this domain must understand that a steel member is governed by its weakest limit state, that the bracing assumptions determine the capacity, and that serviceability often governs the member size even when strength is satisfied. Final design and sealing of drawings require a licensed professional engineer; special structures, plate girders, and seismic systems may require a structural specialist.

## Core Rules

### Choose LRFD Or ASD And Apply It Consistently

AISC 360 permits two design methods, LRFD which compares factored loads to design strength, and ASD which compares service loads to allowable strength, and both are calibrated to produce comparable reliability. Choose one method for a given design and apply it consistently throughout, because mixing factored and service load combinations within a single check produces nonsensical results. Understand that the underlying nominal strength is the same in both methods; the difference is in the resistance factor applied in LRFD and the factor of safety applied in ASD, and the ratio between them is roughly constant across limit states. Do not assume one method is always more economical; for many members the two methods produce the same design decision, and the choice often follows office practice, client preference, or the governing code. The key discipline is consistency and a correct understanding of which loads are factored and which are service, because the most common error is applying ASD allowable strength to factored loads or vice versa.

### Select Flexural Members By Plastic Moment, LTB, And Local Buckling

The flexural strength of a steel beam is governed by the lowest of three limit states: the plastic moment of the fully yielded section, lateral-torsional buckling for an unbraced compression flange, and local buckling of the flange or web. For a compact section fully braced against lateral buckling, the strength reaches the plastic moment, which is the maximum. For an unbraced or partially braced beam, lateral-torsional buckling reduces the strength below the plastic moment as the unbraced length increases, because the compression flange wants to buckle sideways and twist before the section can fully yield. For sections with noncompact or slender flanges or webs, local buckling limits the strength below the plastic moment even with full bracing, because the plate elements buckle locally. Determine the section classification first, then the unbraced length of the compression flange, then compute the design flexural strength for each limit state and use the lowest. Bracing of the compression flange is the single most powerful tool for increasing beam capacity, so the bracing assumption must be real and documented, not assumed.

### Check Shear And Web Limit States At Concentrated Loads

Shear strength in a steel beam is governed by shear yielding or shear buckling of the web, and for most rolled W-shapes with compact webs the shear strength is high relative to flexural capacity, but it must still be checked, especially for short, heavily loaded beams and for built-up sections. Beyond uniform shear, concentrated loads, reactions at bearings, point loads from other framing, and column base forces, introduce a family of web limit states: web yielding, web crippling, web buckling, and sidesway web buckling. These limit states govern at points of concentrated compression because the load is delivered to the web through a small length of flange, and a thin web can yield, cripple, or buckle locally before the beam reaches its flexural capacity. Check each applicable web limit state at every concentrated load and reaction, and where the web is inadequate, provide stiffeners, bearing plates, or a heavier section. Concentrated load checks are commonly overlooked and are a frequent source of connection and bearing failures in steel framing.

### Design Columns For Flexural, Torsional, And Local Buckling

Steel columns are compression members whose capacity is governed by the lowest of flexural buckling, torsional buckling, and local buckling limit states. Flexural buckling is the dominant mode for most rolled sections and is governed by the effective length, the product of the unbraced length and the effective length factor K, which depends on the end restraints and the framing. Determine the effective length carefully, because an unbraced frame has K greater than one while a braced frame has K of one or less, and the difference dramatically affects column capacity. Torsional buckling governs for thin-walled open sections and is checked through the torsional flexural buckling provisions. Local buckling of flanges and webs limits capacity for slender sections, classified through the width-thickness ratios. For columns carrying combined axial load and bending, check the interaction through the AISC interaction equations, which combine axial and flexural demand against axial and flexural capacity, because a column that is adequate in pure compression may fail under combined loading. Column design is dominated by stability, so the bracing and end-restraint assumptions are as important as the section properties.

### Establish Effective Length And Bracing From The Real Framing

The effective length of a column and the unbraced length of a beam depend entirely on the actual bracing provided by the structure, and these assumptions directly determine the design capacity. A column braced against sidesway by shear walls or braced frames has an effective length factor near one, while a column in an unbraced moment frame has a factor greater than one that reflects the amplification of deflection under load. A beam's compression flange must be braced at intervals short enough to develop the required flexural strength, and the source of that bracing, a concrete slab, joists, or discrete braces, must be capable of restraining the flange. Document the bracing assumption on the drawings and verify that the detail can deliver the restraint, because a beam designed for full plastic moment on the assumption of continuous bracing that is not actually provided will buckle at a fraction of its assumed capacity. Bracing is not a minor detail; it is the foundation of the stability calculation, and an unverified bracing assumption is a hidden reduction in capacity.

### Check Serviceability Through Deflection And Vibration

Strength and stability are necessary but not sufficient, because a steel member that satisfies strength limits can still deflect excessively or vibrate objectionably, both of which are serviceability failures. Steel is ductile and slender, so deflection often governs beam depth even when strength is satisfied by a shallower section, particularly for long-span floor and roof beams. Check deflection under service loads against code and project limits, considering both total and live-load deflection and the effect on finishes, and check ponding on flat roofs where deflection can accumulate water that increases load. Check floor vibration for long-span or lightweight floors and for occupancies sensitive to motion, using the applicable criteria, because a floor that meets strength and deflection limits can still be perceptibly bouncy and unacceptable to occupants. Cambering can offset dead-load deflection but must be specified with realistic values and understood not to address live-load or vibration issues. Serviceability is part of member selection, not an afterthought, because serviceability failures surface as occupant complaints and damage to finishes even when the structure is safe.

### Verify Material Specification And Availability

AISC member capacities depend on the steel grade, typically specified yield strengths of 36, 50, or 65 ksi for structural steel, and the design must use the grade actually specified and available. Select the grade appropriate to the application, recognizing that higher grades reduce section size for strength but may affect weldability, ductility, and cost. Confirm that the selected W-shape or other section is available, because the AISC listing includes many sections that are rarely rolled or special-order, and specifying an unavailable section causes delays and redesign. For built-up members, plate gages and welds must be specified and the local buckling limits checked for the actual plate proportions. Material specification and availability are part of design, because a member that cannot be procured in the specified grade or shape is a member that cannot be built.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Full Lateral Bracing Without A Real Brace

The engineer designs a beam for its full plastic moment on the assumption that the compression flange is continuously braced, when in fact no brace exists or the assumed brace cannot restrain the flange. The mechanism is that lateral-torsional buckling reduces flexural capacity as the unbraced length increases, and without real bracing the beam will buckle sideways and twist at a fraction of its assumed capacity. The false signal is that the plastic moment capacity is listed for the section. The harm is that the beam fails by buckling under a load far below its assumed capacity, with little warning, because the assumed brace was never there. The compression flange bracing must be provided by a real, capable element and documented, and the unbraced length used in the design must reflect the actual brace spacing, because lateral-torsional buckling is the limit state that most often governs steel beams.

### Using The Wrong Effective Length Factor For The Frame

The engineer uses an effective length factor of one for a column in an unbraced moment frame, treating it as braced, when the frame relies on the column bending for stability and the factor should be greater than one. The mechanism is that in an unbraced frame the columns deflect laterally under load and the effective length is amplified, reducing capacity; using a braced factor overstates the column capacity. The false signal is that the column satisfies the buckling check with K equal to one. The harm is that the column can buckle in a sidesway mode at a load below its design capacity, potentially triggering progressive collapse of the unbraced frame. The effective length factor must reflect the actual frame behavior, braced or unbraced, and the sidesway stability of the frame must be checked, because the bracing condition determines column capacity.

### Overlooking Web Limit States At Concentrated Loads

The engineer checks flexure and shear and assumes the beam is adequate, without checking web yielding, web crippling, or web buckling at the concentrated loads and reactions. The mechanism is that concentrated loads deliver high local compression to the web through a small length of flange, and a thin web can yield, cripple, or buckle locally before the beam reaches its flexural or shear capacity. The false signal is that the beam satisfies flexure and shear. The harm is local buckling or crippling of the web at a support or point load, which can precipitate a connection failure or beam settlement under service load. Every concentrated load and reaction must be checked against the web limit states, with stiffeners or bearing plates provided where the web is inadequate, because these local failures are common and often missed.

### Ignoring Local Buckling For Slender Elements

The engineer uses the plastic moment capacity for a section with noncompact or slender flanges or webs, without checking the width-thickness ratio limits that reduce capacity for local buckling. The mechanism is that slender plate elements buckle locally before the section can reach its plastic moment, and AISC reduces the design strength for noncompact and slender sections. The false signal is that the section is a W-shape and therefore assumed compact. The harm is that the section buckles locally at a moment below its assumed capacity, and the failure can be sudden. The section must be classified by its width-thickness ratios, and the design strength must reflect the classification, because many deeper and lighter sections and most built-up sections are noncompact or slender and cannot reach the plastic moment.

### Sizing For Strength And Ignoring Deflection Or Vibration

The engineer selects the lightest section that satisfies strength and stability and reports it as economical, without checking deflection or vibration. The mechanism is that steel is slender and the lightest strength-satisfying section is often shallow and flexible, so it deflects or vibrates beyond acceptable limits under service load. The false signal is that the section satisfies strength. The harm is floors that sag, crack finishes, pond water on roofs, or bounce objectionably under foot traffic, generating occupant complaints and expensive remediation even though the structure is technically safe. Deflection and vibration must be checked during member selection, and the governing section is often controlled by serviceability rather than strength, because a serviceability failure is still a failure.

### Mixing LRFD Loads With ASD Strengths Or Vice Versa

The engineer applies factored LRFD loads to an ASD allowable strength, or service ASD loads to an LRFD design strength, producing a check that is either dangerously unconservative or unnecessarily conservative. The mechanism is that the two methods use the same nominal strength but different load and resistance factors, and mixing them defeats the calibration that gives both methods their reliability. The false signal is that the check produces a number and a pass or fail. The harm is either an unsafe member that fails under design load or an overdesigned member that wastes material, and the error is hard to detect without tracing each load and strength to its method. One method must be chosen and applied consistently, with every load combination and every strength matched to that method, because mixing methods is a silent and consequential error.

### Assuming A Section Is Available Without Checking Producers

The engineer selects a W-shape from the full AISC listing for its efficiency, without checking that the section is commonly rolled and available. The mechanism is that the AISC listing includes many sections that are rarely produced or special-order, and specifying them causes procurement delays and redesign. The false signal is that the section appears in the table. The harm is schedule delays, redesign under pressure, or substitution of a less efficient section that may not satisfy the original design intent. Commonly available sections should be preferred, and availability should be confirmed before specifying, because a member that cannot be procured is a member that cannot be built on schedule.

## Self-Check

- Has one design method, LRFD or ASD, been chosen and applied consistently, with every load combination and strength matched to that method?
- Is the flexural strength governed by the lowest of plastic moment, lateral-torsional buckling, and local buckling, with the unbraced length reflecting real compression flange bracing?
- Have shear and all web limit states, web yielding, web crippling, web buckling, been checked at every concentrated load and reaction?
- Are columns checked for flexural, torsional, and local buckling, with the effective length factor reflecting the actual braced or unbraced frame behavior?
- Is combined axial and bending in columns checked through the AISC interaction equations?
- Are deflection, ponding, and floor vibration checked under service loads, with camber specified realistically where used?
- Are the specified steel grade and section shape available from producers, avoiding rare or special-order sections?
- Is the design sealed by a licensed professional engineer, with specialist involvement for plate girders, slender elements, or seismic systems beyond ordinary competence?
