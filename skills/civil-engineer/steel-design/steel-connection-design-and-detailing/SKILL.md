# Steel Connection Design And Detailing

Steel connection design is the work of detailing the joints between members so that they transfer the forces from the analysis with adequate strength, ductility, and rotation capacity, and it is where steel structures most often fail. Members are rolled sections with predictable strengths; connections are assemblies of bolts, welds, plates, and angles where force paths turn, concentrate, and introduce secondary effects that the analysis rarely captures directly. A beam selected for flexure can still pull its connection apart, a column can be torn by a moment connection's flange force, and a shear tab can fail in block shear or buckling before the bolts reach capacity. AISC 360 governs ordinary connections, and AISC 341 governs seismic connections with far more demanding requirements because seismic connections must sustain large inelastic rotations while carrying the forces from yielding members. The agent working in this domain must understand that the connection is designed for the actual force path through its elements, not for the abstract member force, and that detailing, bolt and weld placement, edge distances, and access for installation and inspection determine whether the connection as built matches the connection as designed. Connection design for major structures and all seismic connections must be performed and sealed by a licensed professional engineer; seismic connections in special systems require a structural specialist and prequalification.

## Core Rules

### Design Each Connection Element Along The Real Force Path

A connection is not a single check but a chain of elements, each of which must carry the force from the member into the support: the bolts or welds to the beam, the connecting element such as a shear tab or angle, the bolts or welds to the column, the column elements such as the flange and web, and the limit states at each interface. Design each element for its actual demand and check each limit state, because the connection capacity is governed by the weakest link. For a shear connection, check bolt shear, bearing and tear-out at the bolt holes, shear yielding and shear rupture of the connecting element, block shear rupture of the beam web and the connecting element, and the weld to the support. For a moment connection, check the flange weld or plate for the flange force, the web connection for shear, the column flange and web for the delivered forces, and the panel zone for shear. A connection designed only for the bolt capacity, ignoring the plate, the welds, or the supporting member, is a connection with an unverified and often inadequate force path.

### Choose Bearing Versus Slip-Critical Bolting Deliberately

AISC recognizes two bolted connection types: bearing connections, where the connected plies slip into bearing against the bolts at service load and the bolts resist shear through bearing and shear, and slip-critical connections, where the bolts are pretensioned and the friction between plies prevents slip at service load. Bearing connections are economical and appropriate for most shear connections where limited slip is acceptable. Slip-critical connections are required where slip would compromise serviceability or the load path, such as in connections with oversized or slotted holes, in connections subject to fatigue or significant load reversal, and in seismic connections where slip would change the mechanism. Slip-critical connections require pretensioned bolts, prepared faying surfaces with a defined class, and verification of the slip capacity, all of which add cost and inspection. Choose the type based on the service condition and document the faying surface requirement, because a bearing connection used where slip-critical is required will slip under load and change the structure's behavior.

### Check Bolts For Shear, Tension, And Combined Loading

Bolts in connections resist shear, tension, or both, and combined loading reduces capacity below the pure-shear or pure-tension value. For shear, check the bolt shear capacity and the bearing and tear-out capacity of the connected material, because in thin material the material controls rather than the bolt. For tension, check the bolt tensile capacity and, where the connected element tends to pry, check prying action, which increases the bolt tension beyond the direct applied force. For combined shear and tension, use the AISC interaction equation that reduces the available strength in each direction as the other increases. Select the bolt grade appropriate to the demand, A325 or A490 for high-strength structural bolting, and recognize that A490 provides higher capacity but has different pretension and galvanizing limitations. Do not assume the bolt capacity governs; in many connections the connected material, the plate, angle, or beam web, controls through bearing, tear-out, or block shear, so all limit states must be checked.

### Design Welds With The Correct Strength, Size, And Detail

Welded connections transfer force through fillet or groove welds, and the design must select the weld type, size, length, and electrode with the correct strength and detail the weld for access and inspection. Fillet weld strength is governed by the throat area and the electrode strength, and the size must be sufficient for the demand and within the limits for the connected material thickness. Groove welds, complete or partial joint penetration, develop the full or partial capacity of the connected material and require proper preparation, qualification, and inspection, with complete joint penetration welds used where full-strength transfer is required. Match the electrode strength class to the base metal and the demand, using matching or undermatching electrodes deliberately. Detail welds so that they can be deposited and inspected, avoiding details that trap contamination or are inaccessible to the welder or inspector. Welding is governed by the AWS D1.1 Structural Welding Code, and complete joint penetration welds used in seismic connections require weld procedure specifications and welder qualification.

### Detail Shear Tabs And Single-Plate Connections For Ductile Rotation

Shear tabs and single-plate connections are the most common simple shear connections, and their design must provide not only shear strength but also the ductility to accommodate the end rotation of the simply supported beam. AISC provides design procedures for these connections that check bolt shear, bearing and tear-out, shear yielding and rupture of the plate, block shear, weld strength to the support, and the buckling and yielding of the plate acting as an unsupported element. The plate thickness and the bolt layout must allow the connection to flex and rotate without developing excessive moment that could fracture the welds or the bolts. Limit the number of bolts and the plate proportions per the AISC procedure, because a connection that is too stiff for its rotation demand will attract moment it was not designed for and can fail. Standardize connection details within a project for economy and inspection, but verify that each standardized connection is adequate for its actual demand, because standard details are starting points, not certifications.

### Design Moment Connections For The Full Beam And Column Forces

Moment connections transfer flange forces from the beam into the column and must be designed for the full moment, typically delivered as a couple in the flanges, plus the shear through the web connection. The flange force can be very large, and the connection must deliver it into the column flange through complete joint penetration welds, flange plates, or similar means, and the column must resist the force without flange bending, web yielding, web crippling, or panel zone shear failure. Check the column for the need for continuity plates, transverse stiffeners at the flange force, and doubler plates in the panel zone where the column web cannot resist the panel zone shear. Detail the connection so that the flange welds have adequate access and backing, the web bolts do not interfere with the flange welds, and the entire connection can be inspected. Moment connections are complex, force-concentrating details, and a moment connection designed without checking the column-side limit states is a connection that may tear the column apart under the beam's moment.

### Apply AISC 341 Seismic Connection Requirements For Ductile Systems

Seismic connections in special steel moment frames and special concentrically braced frames must satisfy AISC 341, which imposes requirements far beyond AISC 360 because these connections must sustain large inelastic rotations while carrying the forces from yielding members. For special moment frame connections, AISC 341 requires prequalified connections, such as the reduced beam section, RBS, or welded flange plate connection, or connections demonstrated by testing, and requires the connection to develop the probable plastic moment of the beam with strong column-weak beam proportioning. The RBS connection deliberately reduces the beam flange at a distance from the column to force the plastic hinge away from the weld, protecting the vulnerable welded flange-to-column joint. Seismic welds require notch-tough electrodes, backing removal or treatment, and complete joint penetration with qualification. Connections are designated by their performance, such as SC for seismically compliant connections in special moment frames, with specific testing and detailing requirements. Seismic connection design is a specialty that requires the structural specialist, prequalified or tested connections, and rigorous inspection, because these connections are where steel moment frames have failed catastrophically in past earthquakes.

## Common Traps

### Designing Only The Bolts And Ignoring The Connected Material

The engineer checks the bolt shear capacity, finds it adequate, and stops, without checking bearing, tear-out, or block shear in the connected plate or beam web. The mechanism is that in many connections the connected material is thinner or weaker than the bolts, so the material controls through bearing, tear-out, or block shear rupture, and a connection adequate in bolt capacity fails in the material. The false signal is that the bolts are adequate. The harm is a connection that tears through the plate or web under load, often in a sudden, brittle block shear mode, because the weakest link was never checked. Every connection must be checked for all limit states along the force path, including the connected material, because the connection capacity is the capacity of its weakest element.

### Using A Bearing Connection Where Slip-Critical Is Required

The engineer specifies a bearing bolted connection with standard holes and no pretension, in a location subject to load reversal, fatigue, or oversized holes where slip would change the load path. The mechanism is that a bearing connection allows the plies to slip into bearing at service load, and where slip is detrimental, this slip changes the geometry, introduces impact under reversal, or compromises the intended mechanism. The false signal is that the bolts have adequate shear capacity. The harm is that the connection slips under service load, causing objectionable movement, fatigue damage, or a change in the structural behavior that can precipitate failure. Slip-critical connections with pretensioned bolts and prepared faying surfaces must be specified where slip is detrimental, because the connection type must match the service condition.

### Neglecting Prying Action In Tension Connections

The engineer designs a tee stub or angle connection for the direct applied bolt tension and does not account for prying action, the additional force created as the connected leg flexes and prys on the bolts. The mechanism is that the connecting element bends under tension, and the reaction at its edge adds force to the bolts beyond the direct pull, so the bolts see more tension than the applied force. The false signal is that the bolts have adequate tensile capacity for the direct force. The harm is bolt fracture or connection failure under a tension load below the design value, because prying was not included. Prying action must be checked per the AISC procedure for any connection where the connected element is flexible and the bolts are in tension, with the element stiffened or the bolts increased where prying is excessive.

### Underdesigning The Column Side Of A Moment Connection

The engineer designs the beam-side flange welds and web bolts for the beam moment and assumes the column can take the forces, without checking column flange bending, web yielding, web crippling, or panel zone shear. The mechanism is that the flange force delivered to the column is large and concentrated, and the column flange can bend, the web can yield or cripple locally, and the panel zone can shear under the opposing beam moments. The false signal is that the beam-side connection is detailed. The harm is column flange or web failure, panel zone yielding, or fracture of the connection under the beam's moment, because the column-side limit states were not checked. Continuity plates and doubler plates must be provided where required, and the column must be checked for all the forces the moment connection delivers, because a moment connection is only as strong as the column that receives it.

### Specifying Welds That Cannot Be Deposited Or Inspected

The engineer details complete joint penetration welds in locations inaccessible to the welder or inspector, or fillet welds in joints too tight for the torch or the ultrasonic probe. The mechanism is that welds require access for deposition and for the nondestructive examination that verifies them, and a detail without access produces defective welds that cannot be detected or repaired. The false signal is that the weld is shown on the drawing. The harm is a weld with incomplete fusion, porosity, or cracks that is undocumented and undetected, failing under load where it matters most. Weld details must provide access for deposition and inspection, backing must be detailed where required, and inaccessible welds must be redesigned as bolted or reconfigured, because a weld that cannot be made or inspected is a weld that does not exist.

### Applying Ordinary AISC 360 To A Seismic Connection

The engineer designs a moment connection in a special steel moment frame using only AISC 360 provisions, without the AISC 341 requirements for prequalification, strong column-weak beam, notch-tough welds, and connection testing. The mechanism is that seismic connections must sustain large inelastic rotations, and ordinary AISC 360 design does not ensure the ductility and detailing required, so the connection can fracture at the weld under the design earthquake. The false signal is that the connection satisfies AISC 360 strength checks. The harm is brittle fracture of the connection in an earthquake, the failure mode that caused widespread damage in past earthquakes, because the seismic detailing was never applied. Seismic connections must satisfy AISC 341 with prequalified or tested details, notch-tough electrodes, and the strong column-weak beam and panel zone provisions, designed by a specialist, because ordinary strength design is not sufficient for ductile seismic performance.

### Omitting Continuity Plates Or Doubler Plates Where Required

The engineer designs a moment connection without checking whether the column requires transverse stiffeners, continuity plates, at the beam flange forces or doubler plates in the panel zone. The mechanism is that the concentrated flange forces and panel zone shear can exceed the capacity of the unstiffened column, and without stiffeners or doublers the column deforms locally or yields in shear. The false signal is that the column section is adequate for axial and flexure. The harm is local column failure at the connection under the beam moment, because the column was never checked for the concentrated connection forces. Continuity plates and doubler plates must be provided wherever the column checks require them, and their absence is a common and consequential connection defect, because the column is part of the connection.

## Self-Check

- Is each connection designed along the full force path, with every element, bolt, weld, plate, and supporting member, checked for its applicable limit states?
- Is the bolted connection type, bearing or slip-critical, matched to the service condition, with pretension and faying surface requirements specified where slip-critical?
- Are bolts checked for shear, tension, and combined loading, with prying action included where the connected element is flexible and in tension?
- Are welds designed with the correct type, size, electrode, and length, detailed for deposition and inspection per AWS D1.1?
- Are shear tabs and single-plate connections checked for all limit states including block shear and plate buckling, with proportions allowing ductile rotation?
- Are moment connections checked on the column side for flange bending, web yielding, web crippling, and panel zone shear, with continuity and doubler plates where required?
- For seismic connections, are AISC 341 requirements satisfied, including prequalified or tested connections, strong column-weak beam, and notch-tough welds?
- Is connection design sealed by a licensed professional engineer, with specialist involvement and prequalification for seismic connections and major structures?
