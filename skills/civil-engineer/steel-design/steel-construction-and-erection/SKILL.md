# Steel Construction And Erection

Steel construction and erection is the work of turning a designed steel structure into a built one, and it is governed by a reality that the design drawings do not fully capture: a steel frame is assembled piece by piece in the air, and during erection the partially completed structure is far less stable than the finished frame. Members that will later be braced by the completed system must be temporarily braced or guyed, connections that will later be fully tightened may be snug only, and the sequence of picks determines whether any intermediate configuration is stable under wind and construction load. Beyond stability, the quality of the built structure depends on bolt tightening and inspection, on welding and its nondestructive examination, and on holding the structure within the AISC tolerances that the design assumed. The agent working in this domain must understand that erection is a stability-critical and quality-critical activity, that the erector's methods must be planned and reviewed, and that the special inspector's role is to verify that the built structure matches the designed one. Erection engineering, temporary works design, and inspection planning must be performed under the supervision of a licensed professional engineer; the authority having jurisdiction and the project specifications define the required inspection scope and qualifications.

## Core Rules

### Plan The Erection Sequence For Stability At Every Stage

The erection sequence must be planned so that the structure is stable at every intermediate stage, not only in its final configuration, because during erection the lateral system is incomplete and wind and construction loads act on a partial frame. Sequence the erection so that braced bays or moment frames are completed early to provide a stable core, then erect adjoining framing tied into that stable core, rather than spreading the frame in all directions with no completed lateral path. Consider the crane reach and pick weights, the delivery sequence of fabricated members, and the need for temporary bracing or guy cables to hold columns and beams plumb and stable until the permanent bracing is installed. Review the sequence against wind load on the partially erected frame, because a frame that is stable when complete may be unstable at a fraction of its members. The erection sequence is a stability engineering problem, and an unplanned or improvised sequence is a common source of erection collapses, so the sequence must be developed, reviewed, and followed.

### Provide Temporary Bracing And Guying For Partially Framed Structures

Temporary bracing and guying are required to hold columns plumb, to resist wind on the partial frame, and to stabilize members until the permanent lateral system is connected. Design temporary braces and guys for the wind and construction loads on the partial frame, with adequate capacity and anchorage, because an undersized or poorly anchored brace fails and the frame it was holding collapses. Recognize that columns freed of their shipping supports and not yet tied into the frame have very little lateral capacity, and that long-span beams hanging from a few bolts can swing or rotate. Coordinate the removal of temporary bracing with the completion of the permanent lateral system, because removing braces before the permanent system is functional leaves the frame unstable. Temporary works are engineered structures in their own right, and their design must be performed or reviewed by a qualified engineer, because an erection collapse from inadequate temporary bracing is among the most catastrophic construction failures.

### Specify And Verify Bolt Tightening Per The Required Method

High-strength bolts in structural connections must be tightened to the required level, which may be snug-tight, pretensioned, or slip-critical depending on the connection type and the specification. Snug-tight is the minimum and is achieved by the full effort of an ironworker with an impact wrench bringing the plies into firm contact. Pretensioned and slip-critical connections require a specified minimum bolt tension, achieved and verified by one of the AISC-recognized methods: turn-of-nut, calibrated wrench, twist-off-type tension-control bolts, or direct tension indicators. Specify the method and the required tension, and require inspection verifying that the method was applied correctly, because a bolt that is snug only where pretension is required produces a connection that slips under load and fails to perform. For slip-critical connections, verify that the faying surfaces were prepared to the required class before the plies were drawn together, because surface preparation cannot be inspected after bolting. Bolt tightening and its verification are documented through the inspection record, and the absence of verification is the absence of the connection.

### Define Welding Inspection Scope By Connection Criticality

Welding inspection must be scoped to the criticality of the connection and the governing code, because not every weld requires the same level of examination. Visual inspection by a qualified welding inspector is the baseline for all structural welds and verifies weld size, length, profile, and visible defects such as undercut, porosity, and cracks. Nondestructive testing, ultrasonic testing, magnetic particle testing, and dye penetrant testing, is required for complete joint penetration welds and other critical welds, particularly in seismic connections, and the extent and timing must be specified per AWS D1.1 and AISC 341 where applicable. Ultrasonic testing detects internal discontinuities such as incomplete fusion and cracks that are not visible, magnetic particle testing detects surface and near-surface cracks in ferromagnetic steel, and dye penetrant testing detects surface cracks in any material. Match the method to the weld type and criticality, and require the testing at the correct time, after the weld has cooled and before it is covered. Welding inspection is the verification that the welds meet the code, and an uninspected critical weld is an unverified weld.

### Verify Compliance With AISC Tolerances

AISC publishes tolerances for the fabrication and erection of structural steel, including plumbness of columns, sweep and camber of members, cross-section variation, and the alignment of connections, and these tolerances are the assumptions on which the design is based. The structure must be erected within these tolerances, because exceeding them introduces forces and eccentricities the design did not account for and can make connections impossible to fit. Measure plumbness of columns at specified intervals, sweep of beams and trusses, and the fit-up of connections, and document compliance through the erection survey. Where tolerances are exceeded, the condition must be evaluated by the engineer of record and corrected or accepted by documented engineering decision, because out-of-tolerance members affect the structure's behavior and appearance. Tolerances are not cosmetic; they are engineering limits, and a structure erected outside tolerance is a structure that may not perform as designed.

### Coordinate The Special Inspector's Role And Scope

The special inspector, where required by the building code or project specification, verifies that the steel construction conforms to the approved drawings and the code, with particular attention to the items that most affect structural integrity. The scope typically includes verification of shop and mill certifications, observation of bolting and welding, verification of bolt tightening for pretensioned and slip-critical connections, and monitoring of welder qualifications and weld procedures. For seismic systems, the special inspection requirements are more extensive and include continuous observation of certain welding and verification of the AISC 341 connection provisions. Define the inspector's scope, qualifications, and reporting in the project specifications, and require that inspection be performed by an accredited agency or qualified individual independent of the contractor. The special inspector is the project's independent verification, and the inspection record is the evidence that the built structure matches the designed one, so the role must be scoped, staffed, and heeded.

### Document Erection Tolerances, Corrections, And Deviations

Every erection project produces deviations from the ideal, members slightly out of plumb, connections that required reaming or shimming, and field modifications, and these must be documented and resolved through engineering decision. Require the erector to report deviations that exceed tolerances, field modifications, and damaged members, and route them to the engineer of record for evaluation and direction. Do not allow undocumented field modifications, because a hole enlarged, a member cope added, or a weld changed without engineering review can compromise the connection or member. Maintain a record of all inspections, bolt tightening verification, weld testing reports, and deviation resolutions, because this record is the documentation of compliance and the basis for future evaluation of the structure. Documentation is part of construction, not an afterthought, because a structure without a construction record is a structure whose as-built condition is unknown.

## Common Traps

### Erecting Without A Planned Stable Sequence

The erector proceeds member by member without a planned sequence, assuming that the frame will be stable once enough members are in place, when in fact intermediate configurations may be unstable. The mechanism is that the completed frame is stable but the partial frame lacks its lateral system, and wind or construction load on the partial frame can collapse it before completion. The false signal is that the members are being placed and the frame is taking shape. The harm is an erection collapse that can kill workers and destroy the partially built structure, because stability during erection is a separate problem from stability of the completed frame. The erection sequence must be planned for stability at every stage, with temporary bracing provided, because an improvised sequence is a gamble with stability.

### Removing Temporary Bracing Before The Permanent System Is Complete

The erector removes temporary braces to clear the work area or because the frame looks complete, before the permanent lateral system, bracing, or moment connections are fully installed and tightened. The mechanism is that temporary bracing is holding the frame stable, and removing it before the permanent system is functional leaves the frame without a lateral path. The false signal is that the frame appears complete. The harm is sudden collapse under wind or even under self-weight when the temporary restraint is removed, because the permanent stability path was not yet in place. Temporary bracing must remain until the permanent lateral system is complete and verified, and its removal must be coordinated with the engineer's assessment, because premature removal is a direct cause of erection collapses.

### Leaving Bolts Snug-Tight Where Pretension Is Required

The ironworker snug-tightens bolts and moves on, where the connection requires pretensioned or slip-critical bolts, and the inspection does not catch the shortfall. The mechanism is that snug-tight is far below the required pretension, and a connection that relies on pretension for slip-critical behavior or for seismic performance will slip or loosen under load. The false signal is that the bolts are in place and the plies are in contact. The harm is that the connection slips under service or seismic load, changing the structure's behavior and potentially failing, because the pretension that the design assumed was never achieved. Bolt tightening must be verified by the specified method, turn-of-nut, calibrated wrench, tension-control bolts, or direct tension indicators, and the verification must be documented, because snug-tight is not pretension.

### Skipping Nondestructive Testing On Critical Welds

The project does not perform the ultrasonic or magnetic particle testing required for complete joint penetration and seismic welds, on the assumption that visual inspection is sufficient or that the welds look sound. The mechanism is that critical welds can contain internal discontinuities, incomplete fusion, slag, or cracks, that are not visible, and only nondestructive testing can detect them. The false signal is that the weld passes visual inspection. The harm is a critical weld with an internal defect that fails under load, particularly under seismic load where brittle fracture of welds has caused catastrophic failures, because the defect was never detected or repaired. Nondestructive testing must be performed on the critical welds per the code and specification, at the correct time and by qualified technicians, because visual inspection alone cannot verify the integrity of a complete joint penetration weld.

### Exceeding Tolerances Without Engineering Evaluation

The erector forces an out-of-tolerance member into place, reaming holes, shimming, or bending members, without reporting the deviation or obtaining engineering direction. The mechanism is that out-of-tolerance conditions introduce forces and eccentricities the design did not account for, and forcing members into place can damage them or their connections. The false signal is that the members fit after the forcing. The harm is a structure with hidden stresses, damaged members, or compromised connections that may not perform as designed, and the deviations are undocumented so future evaluation is impossible. Deviations beyond tolerance must be reported and evaluated by the engineer of record, with corrections or documented acceptance, because forcing steel to fit is not a substitute for engineering.

### Allowing Undocumented Field Modifications

The erector or fabricator modifies a connection or member in the field, enlarging a hole, adding a cope, changing a weld, or substituting a material, without engineering review or documentation. The mechanism is that field conditions often differ from the drawings, and undocumented modifications are made to make the piece fit, but the modification can reduce capacity or introduce a defect. The false signal is that the modified piece fits and the connection is made. The harm is a connection or member below its designed capacity, undocumented and unknown to the engineer, that may fail under load. All field modifications must be submitted to the engineer of record for review and documented in the record drawings, because an undocumented modification is a hidden change to the structure.

### Treating Special Inspection As A Formality

The project treats the special inspector as a paperwork requirement rather than an independent verification, and inspection findings are not heeded or resolved. The mechanism is that special inspection exists to verify that the built structure conforms to the design and code, and treating it as a formality defeats that purpose, allowing defects to pass uncorrected. The false signal is that the inspection paperwork is complete. The harm is a structure with uncorrected bolting, welding, or tolerance defects that the inspection was meant to catch, because the findings were ignored. The special inspector must be qualified, independent, and heeded, with findings resolved through engineering decision and documented, because inspection that is not heeded is inspection that does not protect the structure.

## Self-Check

- Is the erection sequence planned so that the structure is stable at every intermediate stage, with a completed lateral path developed early?
- Is temporary bracing and guying designed for wind and construction load on the partial frame, with removal coordinated with permanent system completion?
- Are bolts tightened to the required level, snug-tight, pretensioned, or slip-critical, with the method and verification specified and documented?
- Is welding inspection scoped by connection criticality, with visual inspection for all welds and UT, MT, or PT for complete joint penetration and seismic welds?
- Is the structure verified within AISC tolerances for plumbness, sweep, and connection fit, with deviations beyond tolerance reported and evaluated?
- Is the special inspector's role, scope, and qualifications defined, with the inspector independent and findings resolved and documented?
- For seismic systems, are the AISC 341 special inspection requirements satisfied, including continuous observation of critical welding?
- Is erection engineering and temporary works design performed under a licensed professional engineer, with inspection scope meeting the authority having jurisdiction requirements?
