# Standard of Care and Seal Responsibility

Standard of care and seal responsibility is the professional engineering practice of the engineer's legal and ethical obligation for the work that is sealed and issued: the standard of care (the level of the skill and the diligence that the competent engineer in the same circumstances would provide), the responsible charge (the personal supervision and the control of the work), and the seal (the engineer's certification and the assumption of the responsibility). The civil engineer's seal on the drawings, the calculations, and the reports is the legal assertion that the engineer is in the responsible charge of the work and that the work meets the standard of care. The harm this skill prevents is the engineer sealing the work that they did not personally supervise (the seal without the responsible charge, the license risk), the work that does not meet the standard of care (the negligence, the liability), or the work without the documentation that supports the seal (the undefendable claim). Because the civil engineering work is the public-safety-critical and the seal is the legal and the personal liability, the engineer must exercise the responsible charge, the standard of care, and the documentation, and the conservative judgment when the work is the uncertain.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Scope and the Responsible Charge Before the Work

The scope of the work (the deliverables, the code, the loads, the criteria) and the responsible charge (the engineer's personal supervision and the control) must be established before the work begins, because the seal asserts the responsible charge. Define the scope in the contract (the deliverables, the exclusions, the code edition), and the responsible charge (the engineer who supervises and the controls the work, the delegation to the qualified staff, the review and the approval). Do not seal the work that the engineer did not personally supervise, because the seal without the responsible charge is the license violation. A seal on the work without the established scope and the responsible charge is the undefined and the undefendable, because the responsibility was not established.

### Apply the Standard of Care and the Conservative Judgment

The standard of care (the level of the skill and the diligence that the competent civil engineer would provide, in the same circumstances and the same time and the same locality) is the benchmark, and the engineer must meet or exceed it. Apply the standard of care through the code (the IBC, the ASCE 7, the ACI, the AISC, the AASHTO), the reference (the ASCE, the manuals), the peer review (the independent check), and the judgment (the conservative, when the code is the silent or the ambiguous). The standard of care is the not the perfection, but the competent, and the engineer who falls below is the negligent. Apply the conservative judgment when the work is the uncertain (the soil, the load, the condition), because the conservative protects the public and the engineer, and the standard of care requires the prudent.

### Document the Work to Support the Seal

The work must be documented to support the seal: the calculations (the assumptions, the loads, the code, the results), the drawings (the design, the details, the notes), the specifications (the materials, the methods, the quality), and the reports (the investigation, the evaluation, the recommendation). The documentation is the evidence of the standard of care and the basis of the seal, and the engineer must retain the documentation (commonly 10 years or more, per the state). Document the work with the clarity and the completeness, because the documentation is the defence in any claim, and the undocumented work is the undefendable. A seal on the work without the documentation is the unsupported, because the basis of the seal was not retained.

### Verify the Work Before the Seal

The work must be verified before the seal: the independent check (the peer or the senior review), the QA (the quality assurance), the coordination (the architect, the MEP, the contractor), and the constructability. Verify the calculations (the check by the independent engineer), the drawings (the review for the completeness and the coordination), and the specifications (the review for the consistency). The verification is the standard of care, and the engineer who seals without the verification is the negligent. A seal without the verification is the unverified, because the work was not checked.

### Address the Uncertainty and the Conservative Approach

The civil engineering work has the uncertainty (the soil, the load, the construction, the condition), and the engineer must address the uncertainty with the conservative approach. Use the conservative load (the upper bound), the conservative capacity (the lower bound), the conservative assumption (the worst case), and the factor of safety (the code or the higher). Document the uncertainty and the conservative approach, because the standard of care requires the prudent handling of the uncertainty. An engineer who designs to the optimistic (the lower load, the higher capacity, the best case) is the negligent, because the uncertainty was not addressed conservatively.

### Coordinate With the Team and the Authority

The civil engineer coordinates with the team (the architect, the MEP, the geotechnical, the contractor) and the authority (the building official, the fire marshal, the DOT), and the coordination is the standard of care. Document the coordination (the meeting, the RFI, the review), and the resolution (the design change, the clarification). The coordination that is the not documented is the dispute-prone, and the uncoordinated design is the error-prone. A seal on the work without the coordination is the conflict-laden, because the team and the authority were not coordinated.

### Respond to the Construction and the Field

The civil engineer responds to the construction and the field: the shop drawing review, the RFI response, the observation (the periodic or the full-time), and the design change. The response is the standard of care, and the engineer who seals and does not respond to the construction is the exposed. Document the response (the review, the observation report, the design change), and the conservative approach to the field condition that differs from the design. A seal without the construction response is the abandoned, because the engineer did not follow the work to the construction.

## Common Traps

### The Seal Without The Responsible Charge

The engineer seals the work that they did not personally supervise, and the seal is the without the responsible charge, and the license is the risk. The trap is that the seal is the applied, while the supervision is the not, and the seal is the violation. The false signal is the sealed work; the harm is the license action, because the responsible charge was not exercised.

The trap is that the work is the completed, while the standard is the not met, and the work is the negligent. The false signal is the completed work; the harm is the liability, because the standard of care was not met.

The trap is that the optimistic is the attractive, while the conservative is the required, and the design is the under-capacity. The false signal is the economical design; the harm is the under-capacity, because the uncertainty was not addressed conservatively.

The trap is that the seal is the applied, while the construction is the not followed, and the construction deviates. ## Self-Check

- Is the scope (the deliverables, the code, the loads, the criteria) and the responsible charge (the personal supervision) established before the work, and documented in the contract?
- Does the work meet the standard of care (the code, the reference, the peer review, the conservative judgment), and the engineer who falls below is the negligent?
- Is the work documented (the calculations, the drawings, the specifications, the reports) to support the seal, and the documentation retained?
- Is the work verified (the independent check, the QA, the coordination, the constructability) before the seal, and the verification documented?
- Is the uncertainty (the soil, the load, the condition) addressed with the conservative approach (the upper-bound load, the lower-bound capacity, the factor of safety)?
- Is the coordination with the team (the architect, the MEP, the geotechnical) and the authority (the building official, the fire marshal) documented, and the resolution recorded?
- Does the engineer respond to the construction (the shop drawing, the RFI, the observation, the design change), and the response documented?
- Is the seal applied only to the work that the engineer is in the responsible charge of, and the work that meets the standard of care?
