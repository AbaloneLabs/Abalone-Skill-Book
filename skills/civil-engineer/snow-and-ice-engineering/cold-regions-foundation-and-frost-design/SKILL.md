# Cold Regions Foundation and Frost Design

Cold regions foundation design is the engineering of foundations where seasonal frost or permafrost governs the bearing and movement behavior of the ground, and where the conventional assumption of a stable, unfrozen bearing stratum does not hold. The governing references include ASCE 32 for frost-protected shallow foundations (FPSF), ACI 332 for residential concrete in cold climates, and the cold-regions geotechnical literature for permafrost, frost heave, and adfreeze, supplemented by the IBC and IRC prescriptive frost provisions for conventional construction. The harm this skill prevents is a foundation that heaves when ice lenses grow in frost-susceptible soil, that drops when the lenses thaw, that is jacked by adfreeze bond to frozen pile shafts, or that settles catastrophically when warm-building heat degrades the permafrost it was meant to rest on. Because frozen-ground behavior is governed by water, temperature, and soil type in ways unfounded ground is not, the engineer who applies temperate-zone foundation logic to a cold site — deeper footing, ignore the heat flow, assume the ground is stable — will produce a foundation that moves with every freeze-thaw cycle and, in permafrost, may settle irreversibly the first winter.

## Core Rules

### Determine the Frost Depth and Frost Susceptibility of the Site

The frost depth (the maximum depth to which the ground freezes in winter) sets the minimum depth at which a conventional foundation must bear to avoid frost heave, and it must be established from local frost depth maps, freeze index data, or site-specific observation rather than a generic regional number. Equally important is the frost susceptibility of the soil: silts and silty sands with access to water are highly frost-susceptible because they grow ice lenses that heave the ground, while clean gravels and sands that drain freely are generally non-frost-susceptible because water cannot be held to feed lens growth. Determine both the frost depth and the soil's frost susceptibility from the geotechnical investigation, and recognize that frost heave requires three conditions acting together: freezing temperatures, a frost-susceptible soil, and a water supply. A site that has the soil and the cold but no water will not heave, and a site with water and cold but clean granular soil will not heave, so the design response depends on which conditions are present and which can be removed.

### Apply ASCE 32 Frost-Protected Shallow Foundations Where Appropriate

ASCE 32 permits shallow foundations placed above the frost depth when insulation is used to manipulate the ground thermal regime so that the soil beneath the footing does not freeze, allowing foundations far shallower than conventional frost-depth footings and avoiding deep excavation. The FPSF method sizes and places vertical and horizontal insulation (extruded polystyrene) based on the air freezing index, the building's heated or unheated condition, and the desired footing depth, and it is widely used in residential and light commercial construction in cold climates. Apply ASCE 32 within its scope: it assumes a heated building that contributes heat to the ground, specific insulation R-values and placement details, and proper drainage so that water does not accumulate under the insulated zone. Do not improvise the insulation layout or reduce the R-value to save cost, because the FPSF works only if the insulation configuration keeps the sub-frost isotherm below the footing, and a thin or misplaced layer can let frost reach the bearing soil and heave the foundation the system was meant to protect.

### Control Water and Drainage to Prevent Ice Lens Growth

Because frost heave requires water, the most effective frost defense is often drainage: remove the water and the frost-susceptible soil cannot grow ice lenses even when it freezes. Provide foundation drainage (perimeter drains, free-draining backfill, granular mat under floor slabs) that lowers the groundwater below the frost zone, and replace frost-susceptible soils at and below the footing with non-frost-susceptible granular fill where the native soil is a silt or silty sand. Confirm that the drainage path is functional year-round, because a drain that is blocked, frozen, or daylighted into a frozen ditch will let water rise into the frost zone and feed lens growth. A foundation on frost-susceptible soil with no water control will heave every winter regardless of footing depth, because capillary action can draw water up from a water table well below the frost line to feed the lenses; the drainage and the granular fill break that capillary supply.

### Design for Permafrost: Preserve the Frozen State or Accept Thaw

In permafrost regions, the foundation strategy is either to preserve the frozen state of the ground (so it retains its frozen strength and does not settle) or to design for the thawed condition (accepting that the permafrost will degrade and providing a foundation that works thawed). Preserve the frozen state by ventilating the foundation (open pile-and-grade-beam, or elevated structures with airflow beneath to prevent warm-building heat from reaching the permafrost), by using thermosyphons or heat pipes that extract heat from the ground in winter, and by insulating to limit heat ingress. Where thaw is accepted, design for the thawed soil strength, which is often very low in ice-rich permafrost, and consider pre-thawing or ground improvement. The single most dangerous failure mode in permafrost is warm-building heat degrading the bearing layer, so the thermal design of the foundation-building interface is as important as the structural design, and a foundation that is structurally adequate but thermally uncontrolled will settle as the permafrost it rests on melts.

### Evaluate Adfreeze, Lateral Frost, and Ice Lens Forces on Deep Foundations

Deep foundations (piles, shafts, drilled piers) in frost-susceptible or frozen ground are subject to adfreeze — the bond between the frozen soil and the foundation shaft that can jack the foundation upward as the surrounding soil heaves — and to lateral frost pressures that can push on walls and piles. Compute the adfreeze uplift force from the adfreeze shear strength and the frozen contact area, and resist it with embedment below the frost zone, with a surcharge or anchorage, or with slip sleeves and surface treatments that reduce the bond where uplift must be avoided. Confirm that the foundation can resist the seasonal uplift-and-drop cycle without accumulating permanent displacement, because a small annual ratchet can lift a pile out of bearing over years. For foundation walls and retaining structures in frost-susceptible backfill, account for the lateral frost pressure, which can be large, and either provide the structure to resist it or use non-frost-susceptible, free-draining backfill so the pressure does not develop.

## Common Traps

### The Conventional Frost-Depth Footing On Frost-Susceptible Soil With Water

The footing is placed at the code frost depth in a frost-susceptible silt with a high water table, on the assumption that depth alone prevents heave. The mechanism is that capillary water feeds ice lens growth below the footing, so the false signal of a "code-depth footing" hides that frost reaches the bearing soil; the harm is seasonal heave and thaw settlement that cracks the foundation and superstructure.

### The FPSF Built Outside Its Scope

The frost-protected shallow foundation is built with reduced insulation, no drainage, or for an unheated building, on the assumption that "any insulation helps." The mechanism is that the FPSF relies on a specific thermal balance, so the false signal of a "shallow insulated foundation" hides that the frost isotherm reaches the footing; the harm is a shallow foundation that heaves because the insulation configuration did not keep the ground unfrozen as ASCE 32 requires.

### The Permafrost Foundation That Lets Building Heat In

A slab-on-grade or shallow foundation is placed on permafrost without ventilation or thermal break, on the assumption that the ground "stays frozen." The mechanism is that warm-building heat conducts into the permafrost and degrades it, so the false signal of a "frozen-ground foundation" hides that the bearing layer is melting; the harm is differential settlement that progresses through the winter and can render the building uninhabitable.

### The Deep Foundation Without Adfreeze Resistance

A pile or shaft is embedded below the frost zone for bearing but not checked for the adfreeze uplift from the frozen upper soil. The mechanism is that the frozen bond jacks the foundation upward each winter, so the false signal of an "adequate embedment" hides the net uplift that exceeds the anchorage; the harm is a foundation that ratchets upward over seasons and loses bearing contact.

## Self-Check

- Has the frost depth been established from local data or site observation, and has the frost susceptibility of the soil been determined from the geotechnical investigation?
- Where ASCE 32 frost-protected shallow foundations are used, is the insulation R-value, placement, and configuration within the standard's scope, with heated-building and drainage conditions satisfied?
- Has foundation drainage and non-frost-susceptible granular fill been provided to remove the water supply that feeds ice lens growth in frost-susceptible soils?
- In permafrost, is the strategy explicit (preserve frozen state or design for thaw), with ventilation, thermosyphons, or insulation to prevent warm-building heat from degrading the bearing layer?
- Have adfreeze uplift and lateral frost pressures been evaluated for deep foundations and walls, with embedment, anchorage, slip sleeves, or free-draining backfill provided to resist them?
- Does the foundation design account for the seasonal heave-and-thaw cycle without accumulating permanent displacement over years?
- Is the thermal design of the foundation-building interface treated as equal in importance to the structural design, given that frozen-ground behavior is governed by heat flow and water?
