# Roof Snow Load and Ice Dam Analysis

Roof snow load and ice dam analysis is the diagnosis and prevention of the cold-climate roof failures that occur not from the static weight of snow alone but from the cycle of melting and refreezing that ice dams create, and from the localized snow and slush accumulations that roof geometry and heat loss produce. Where ASCE 7 Chapter 7 supplies the structural design snow load, this skill addresses the service and durability failures — the ice dam that backs water under the shingles, the valley drift that overloads a local frame, the frozen gutter that tears off the fascia, and the membrane punctured by refrozen slush — that drive the majority of cold-climate roof callbacks and leaks. The harm this skill prevents is a roof that meets the structural snow load on paper but leaks every winter because heat loss from the building melts snow at the eave, refreezes it as an ice dam, and ponds meltwater behind it; or a roof whose geometry or thermal path was never evaluated for the drifts and dams the local climate will impose. Because ice dams are a heat-flow and drainage problem as much as a load problem, the engineer who evaluates only the snow weight has missed the failure mode that will actually appear.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose Ice Dams Through the Heat-Loss and Refreeze Mechanism

An ice dam forms when snow on the upper, warm part of a roof melts, runs down to the cold eave, and refreezes, building a ridge of ice that traps subsequent meltwater behind it. The root cause is almost always a warm roof deck driven by heat loss from the building below — inadequate or displaced ceiling insulation, air leaks from the conditioned space into the attic, bypasses at plumbing and chimneys, or a heating source in the attic itself — combined with a cold eave that is outside the heated envelope. Diagnose the dam by tracing the heat path: identify where the insulation is thin or compressed, where air sealing is absent, and where the roof surface temperature crosses the freezing line before the eave. Do not treat ice dams as a roofing-material problem solvable by a "better shingle," because the membrane and shingle only resist the water the dam has already ponded; the dam itself must be eliminated by lowering the roof deck temperature to match the ambient snow temperature across the whole roof.

### Design Ventilation and Insulation to Keep the Roof Cold

The durable remedy for ice dams is a cold roof: a roof deck whose temperature stays at or near ambient so snow does not melt unevenly. Achieve this with a continuous, well-installed insulation layer at the ceiling or roof plane that meets or exceeds the climate-appropriate R-value, combined with rigorous air sealing of every penetration and top-plate, and with soffit-to-ridge ventilation that flushes the underside of the deck with cold outside air to carry away any heat that reaches it. Confirm that the ventilation net free area meets code ratios (commonly 1/300 of the vented attic area, balanced between intake and exhaust), that the intake soffit vents are not blocked by insulation (use baffles), and that the ceiling plane is truly airtight, because a single large bypass can deliver more meltwater than a whole under-insulated ceiling. For cathedral and hot roofs where ventilation is not used, the insulation and air sealing must be even more rigorous, and the assembly must be evaluated for condensation risk, because there is no vent path to dry out accumulated moisture.

### Evaluate Roof Geometry for Drift, Slush, and Ponding

Roof geometry concentrates snow and meltwater at predictable locations: valleys collect drifting snow from two opposing slopes, parapets and roof steps create leeward drifts, low-slope areas trap slush that cannot drain, and internal drains or scuppers that freeze shut pond meltwater behind them. Evaluate each roof for the geometry-specific load and water path: compute valley and parapet drifts consistent with ASCE 7, confirm that low-slope areas have positive drainage (at least 1/4 inch per foot) to a drain that will function under snow and ice, and confirm that secondary (emergency) scuppers or drains are provided so that a frozen or blocked primary drain does not overload the roof with ponded water. Pay particular attention to the transition between steep and low-slope roof sections, where sliding snow from the steep section can overload the low-slope membrane below, and to locations where downspouts discharge onto lower roofs or walkways and refreeze. A roof whose geometry ponds meltwater behind an ice dam or a frozen drain will fail at that location regardless of the nominal snow load.

### Specify Membrane, Flashing, and Drainage Detailing for Ice and Water

In cold climates, the roof assembly must be detailed to resist the water that ice dams and freeze-thaw will deliver, even after the thermal and ventilation remedies are in place. Specify a self-adhering ice and water membrane (or equivalent) extending from the eave edge up the roof a distance that covers the region where dams can form (commonly to at least 24 inches inside the warm wall line, and farther in severe climates), and extend it fully in valleys, around penetrations, and at parapets where water can back up. Confirm that gutters and downspouts are sized for the meltwater and snow load, are structurally attached to carry ice, and are located or heated so they do not freeze and tear off the fascia; consider snow guards or retention on metal roofs to prevent dangerous snow and ice slides that overload gutters and endanger occupants below. Do not rely on the underlayment or shingle alone to resist dammed water, because standing water behind an ice dam will find any seam or fastener, and the membrane is the element that actually keeps the water out when the dam is present.

### Address Slush, Freeze-Thaw, and the Thermal Factor in the Structural Load

Beyond the service failures, the structural snow load must reflect the thermal condition of the roof: ASCE 7 applies a higher thermal factor (Ct) to cold, unheated, or freezer roofs because they retain the full snowpack, while warm roofs shed some snow by melting. Confirm that the structural load uses the correct Ct and that the slush load from rain-on-snow and freeze-thaw is considered, because a saturated, refrozen snowpack can weigh far more than the fresh snow load, and a roof that carries the design snow can be overloaded when rain falls on the snow and the drains freeze. Evaluate the sequence of loading: snow, then rain, then refreeze, then more snow, because the cumulative weight on an older roof can exceed any single design event. A roof designed for the dry snow load that ignores the saturated and refrozen condition is designed for a load the climate will not actually deliver.

## Common Traps

### The Ice Dam Treated As A Roofing-Material Problem

The owner or designer responds to ice dam leaks by upgrading the shingle or adding underlayment, without addressing the heat loss that creates the dam. The mechanism is that the membrane resists the ponded water but the dam keeps forming, so the false signal of a "fixed leak" hides the continuing thermal cause; the harm is a roof that leaks again at the next dam location or when the membrane ages, because the melt-refreeze cycle was never interrupted.

### The Ventilation Added Without Air Sealing

Soffit and ridge vents are added to an attic that has major air leaks from the conditioned space, on the assumption that ventilation alone will keep the roof cold. The mechanism is that warm moist air bypasses the insulation through leaks, so the false signal of a "vented roof" hides the localized melting the leaks cause; the harm is persistent ice dams over the leak locations and, often, condensation and frost in the attic that rots the sheathing.

### The Frozen Drain That Ponds Water Behind A Dam

The roof is designed with positive drainage to internal drains or scuppers, but the drainage path freezes shut in winter, and meltwater ponds behind the blockage. The mechanism is that the nominal slope does not help when the drain is blocked, so the false signal of a "well-drained roof" hides a deepening pond; the harm is a roof overloaded by the weight of ponded water and ice that the snow load calculation never included.

### The Valley And Parapet Drifts Ignored In The Geometry

The roof is checked for uniform snow but the valleys, parapets, and step changes that collect drifts are not evaluated, because the geometry "looks simple." The mechanism is that these features concentrate snow and slush, so the false signal of a uniform load hides the local overload; the harm is a valley or parapet framing member that fails under a drift the uniform load never represented.

## Self-Check

- Has the ice dam been diagnosed through the heat-loss and refreeze mechanism, identifying the insulation gaps, air leaks, and bypasses that warm the roof deck?
- Does the roof assembly provide a continuous, climate-appropriate insulation layer with rigorous air sealing and balanced soffit-to-ridge ventilation sufficient to keep the deck cold?
- Has every valley, parapet, roof step, and low-slope area been evaluated for drift, slush, and ponding consistent with the actual roof geometry?
- Does the detailing include a self-adhering ice and water membrane extending past the warm wall line and through valleys, with gutters and downspouts sized and located to resist ice?
- Does the structural snow load use the correct ASCE 7 thermal factor Ct for the roof's actual heated or unheated condition, and is the saturated and refrozen slush load considered?
- Are primary and secondary (emergency) roof drains provided so that a frozen or blocked primary drain does not overload the roof with ponded water?
- Has the cumulative loading sequence (snow, rain-on-snow, refreeze, additional snow) been considered rather than a single dry-snow design event?
