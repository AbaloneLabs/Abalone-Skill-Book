# Deicing and Anti-Icing Operations

Deicing and anti-icing operations are the winter maintenance practices that keep transportation surfaces — highways, bridge decks, sidewalks, and airfields — safe and serviceable during snow and ice events, by applying chemicals and abrasives to break the bond between frozen precipitation and the pavement. The governing references include FHWA's snow and ice control guidance, AASHTO winter maintenance publications, and agency-specific snow and ice control plans, and the practice hinges on the distinction between anti-icing (applying chemical before or at the start of an event to prevent the ice-pavement bond) and deicing (applying chemical after the bond has formed to break it). The harm this skill prevents is a maintenance operation that applies the wrong chemical for the pavement temperature, that over-applies salt where it cannot work, that corrodes bridge steel and contaminates adjacent water bodies, or that relies on reactive deicing when proactive anti-icing would have kept the surface clear at a fraction of the chemical and labor cost. Because salt effectiveness is sharply temperature-dependent and the window for anti-icing closes once the bond forms, the engineer or operator who treats all events the same will waste chemical, damage infrastructure, and still lose the road.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Anti-Icing From Deicing and Apply Each in Its Window

Anti-icing is the proactive application of liquid chemical (typically brine, sometimes blended with agricultural additives) to the pavement before or at the very start of a snow or frost event, so that the chemical prevents the ice from bonding to the pavement and allows plowing or traffic to clear the light accumulation easily. Deicing is the reactive application of solid or liquid chemical after ice has already bonded, which must then melt through the bond layer to release the ice — a process that requires more chemical, more time, and more traffic disruption, and that often fails once a thick bond has formed. Plan operations around the anti-icing window: monitor the forecast and pavement temperature, pre-wet or apply brine ahead of the event, and reserve deicing for cases where the bond has already formed or where anti-icing was not possible. The false economy of waiting to "see if it sticks" before applying chemical converts a cheap anti-icing pass into an expensive, often unsuccessful deicing operation, because the bond, once formed, resists the chemical that would have prevented it.

### Select the Chemical by Pavement Temperature and Eutectic Limit

Sodium chloride (rock salt and brine) is the workhorse chemical, but its effectiveness drops sharply below about 20 to 25 F pavement temperature and it is essentially ineffective below 15 F, because its eutectic temperature (about -6 F) is theoretical and the rate of melting becomes impractically slow well above it. Match the chemical to the pavement temperature: NaCl for warmer events, with magnesium chloride or calcium chloride for colder pavement (effective to lower temperatures because of their lower eutectic points), and dry abrasives (sand) for very cold conditions where no chemical will work and traction is the only goal. Do not apply NaCl in quantity at very low pavement temperatures expecting it to melt, because it will not, and the applied salt will simply accumulate to corrode the bridge deck, contaminate runoff, and be tracked off the road. Confirm that the chemical form (solid, pre-wetted solid, or liquid) suits the temperature and the spreader: liquids for anti-icing and adherence, pre-wetted solids to keep the chemical on the road and speed melting, and dry solids only where they will not scatter or blow off.

### Set Application Rates to the Event, Not to a Default

Application rates must be matched to the pavement temperature, the precipitation type and rate, and the traffic level, not applied at a single default rate regardless of conditions. Anti-icing brine rates are low (commonly 20 to 60 gallons per lane mile depending on conditions), because the goal is a thin preventive film, while deicing solid rates are higher and increase as the pavement temperature falls and the chemical becomes less efficient. Use the agency's calibrated rate tables or FHWA guidance to set the rate, confirm that spreaders are calibrated so the applied rate matches the target, and adjust during the event as pavement temperature and precipitation change, because a rate that is right at the start of the storm is often wrong an hour later as conditions fall. Over-application does not melt faster in proportion; it wastes chemical, accelerates corrosion and environmental damage, and leaves residue, while under-application loses the road. Bridge decks deserve special attention: because they freeze first and stay frozen longer than approach pavements, they often need earlier and higher-rate treatment, and the corrosion cost of chloride on bridge steel means the rate must be justified, not reflexive.

### Manage Corrosion, Environmental, and Infrastructure Impacts

Chloride chemicals corrode reinforcing steel in concrete bridge decks, vehicles, and metal infrastructure, and they run off into adjacent soils and water bodies where they persist and accumulate, harming vegetation and aquatic life. Manage the corrosion risk by using the minimum effective rate, by considering corrosion-inhibited chemicals or alternatives (acetates, formates, agricultural blends) on sensitive structures such as bridge decks and parking garages where chlorides are prohibited, and by protecting reinforcing steel through epoxy coating, increased cover, and sealers as part of the structure's design. Manage the environmental impact by calibrating spreaders, avoiding over-application, using liquids and pre-wetting to keep chemical on the road rather than in the ditch, and by respecting environmental sensitivity zones (water supply areas, wetlands, sensitive vegetation) where chloride limits or alternative chemicals apply. A deicing operation that ignores corrosion and runoff will keep the road open this storm while shortening the life of the bridge and degrading the watershed it crosses, and the cumulative cost is paid over years.

### Integrate RWIS, Forecasting, and Decision Support

Modern snow and ice control relies on Road Weather Information Systems (RWIS) — pavement and atmospheric sensors, forecast services, and maintenance decision support systems — to time and target chemical application to the actual pavement temperature and condition, not to the air temperature or a fixed schedule. Use RWIS pavement temperature data to decide when to anti-ice (pavement near or below freezing with precipitation forecast), when to deice, and when to stand down, because pavement temperature, not air temperature, governs chemical effectiveness and bridge deck freezing. Confirm that operators are trained to read and act on the RWIS and forecast information, that the decision support system's recommendations are calibrated to local conditions and equipment, and that the snow and ice control plan documents the level of service, the trigger conditions for each action, and the chemicals and rates to be used. A maintenance operation run on habit and air temperature, without RWIS and forecast integration, will mis-time its applications, waste chemical, and lose roads that a data-driven plan would have kept clear.

## Common Traps

### The Reactive Deicing Default

The operation waits until ice has bonded before applying chemical, because anti-icing "might not be needed." The mechanism is that the bond resists chemical once formed, so the false signal of a "wait and see" economy hides a far more expensive deicing operation; the harm is a road that closes for hours and consumes many times the chemical an anti-icing pass would have used.

### The Salt Applied Below Its Effective Temperature

NaCl is spread at pavement temperatures of 10 to 15 F expecting it to melt, because salt "works in the cold." The mechanism is that NaCl's melting rate collapses below 20 F, so the false signal of an applied chemical hides that nothing is melting; the harm is accumulated salt that corrodes the deck and contaminates runoff without clearing the road, where a colder-temperature chemical or abrasives were required.

### The Default Rate Applied To All Events

The spreader is set to a single rate regardless of pavement temperature, precipitation, or storm phase. The mechanism is that conditions change through the storm, so the false signal of a "standard application" hides that the rate is wrong for the current condition; the harm is over-application that corrodes and pollutes during warm events and under-application that loses the road during cold ones.

### The Bridge Deck Treated Like The Approach Pavement

The bridge is treated at the same time and rate as the adjacent road, ignoring that it freezes first. The mechanism is that the bridge deck loses heat from above and below, so the false signal of a uniform treatment hides that the bridge is already frozen while the road is wet; the harm is a bridge that becomes the first location to lose friction and cause crashes, because it was not treated early or heavily enough for its thermal behavior.

## Self-Check

- Is the operation planned around anti-icing (proactive, before the bond forms) with deicing reserved for cases where the bond has already formed?
- Is the chemical selected by pavement temperature and eutectic limit, with NaCl for warmer events and magnesium or calcium chloride or abrasives for colder pavement where NaCl is ineffective?
- Are application rates set from calibrated tables matched to pavement temperature, precipitation type and rate, and traffic, with spreaders calibrated and rates adjusted during the event?
- Are bridge decks treated earlier and at appropriate rates, given that they freeze first and that chloride corrosion is a real cost to the structure?
- Are corrosion and environmental impacts managed through minimum effective rates, corrosion-inhibited or alternative chemicals on sensitive structures, and respect for environmental sensitivity zones?
- Is RWIS pavement temperature data and forecasting integrated into the timing and targeting of applications, rather than relying on air temperature or a fixed schedule?
- Does the snow and ice control plan document the level of service, trigger conditions, chemicals, and rates, and are operators trained to act on the decision support information?
