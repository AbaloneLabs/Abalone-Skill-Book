# Legal Description and Easement Determination

A legal description is the instrument by which a parcel of land is defined for conveyance, and an easement is the instrument by which a non-owner's right to use the land is created, and the judgment problem is that both look like routine text but are legal documents whose sufficiency, closure, and terms determine whether title is marketable and whether the intended rights exist. Agents tend to treat a description as a surveyor's formality and an easement as a line on a plat, and miss that a description that does not close or that conflicts with the plat creates an unmarketable title, that an easement appurtenant runs with the land while one in gross is personal, that a dedication on a plat conveys title to the public, and that a title commitment's exceptions define the encumbrances that bind the land. The harm this skill prevents is a conveyance that fails to transfer the intended parcel, an easement that is unenforceable or overbroad, an encumbrance that forecloses development, and the title defects and litigation that follow. This skill covers metes and bounds, lot and block, and PLSS descriptions, easements (appurtenant and in gross), encumbrances and the title commitment, and dedications and reservations. It is a legal-surveying discipline, and the agent must engage a licensed land surveyor for recordable descriptions and counsel for the legal sufficiency of conveyances and easements.

## Core Rules

### Draft Metes and Bounds, Lot and Block, and PLSS Descriptions to Standard

A legal description takes one of three principal forms, and each must be drafted to its system's requirements. A metes and bounds description begins at a point of beginning (POB) tied to a monument, traces the boundary by courses (bearing and distance, with curve data for arcs) around the perimeter, and returns to the POB, citing the monuments found or set and the adjoiners. A lot and block description references a recorded subdivision plat (for example, "Lot 7, Block 3, Sunset Hills Subdivision, recorded in Plat Book 12, Page 45, County Records"), and its sufficiency depends on the plat's clarity and recordation. A PLSS description uses aliquot parts of a section (for example, "the NE 1/4 of the SW 1/4 of Section 14, Township 2 North, Range 3 East"), and must follow the rules for irregular sections, government lots, and fractional parts. The disciplined rule is to read the description completely (POB, basis of bearing, courses, area, recording references), to confirm the courses close mathematically, and to reconcile the description with the plat and the field monuments, because a description that does not close or that conflicts with the plat is a defect. The trap is a description copied from an old deed whose courses no longer match the current monuments or plat, creating an ambiguity at conveyance.

### Determine Easements as Appurtenant or in Gross and Define Their Scope

An easement is a non-possessory right to use another's land for a specific purpose, and its type and scope determine its enforceability and its transferability. An easement appurtenant benefits a dominant tenement (the parcel that benefits) and burdens a servient tenement (the parcel burdened), runs with the land, and transfers automatically with the dominant estate, and it is the typical form for access, drainage, and utility easements that serve a parcel. An easement in gross benefits a person or entity rather than a parcel, is personal unless assigned, and is the typical form for utility company easements, and its commercial form may be assignable. Define the easement's scope precisely: the purpose (access, utility, drainage, conservation), the location and width, the parties with rights, the maintenance responsibility, the term (perpetual or temporary), and the rights of assignment, because an overbroad or ambiguous easement invites disputes and can over-burden the servient land. The disciplined rule is to read the recorded easement instrument for its actual terms, not to rely on the plat label, and to draft new easements with a defined purpose and width tied to a description or exhibit. The trap is treating an easement label on a plat as the full grant, when the recorded instrument grants broader rights or imposes conditions the plat does not show.

### Read the Title Commitment to Identify Encumbrances and Exceptions

A title commitment is the insurer's offer to insure title subject to the exceptions listed in Schedule B, and those exceptions (recorded easements, reservations, restrictions, covenants, liens, and prior conveyances) are the encumbrances that bind the land and that the survey and the development must account for. Read Schedule B item by item, obtain the recorded instrument for each exception, and map each easement, reservation, and restriction against the parcel and the proposed development, because an exception that looks routine can prohibit a building, a use, or a utility connection. Distinguish the standard exceptions (taxes, general assessments) from the specific exceptions (a named easement or covenant), and consider whether an endorsement or a removal of an exception is available to clarify coverage. The disciplined rule is to obtain the title commitment before completing the design and to update it through closing, because encumbrances discovered late can foreclose the development, and to coordinate the surveyor's mapping of the exceptions with the insurer's requirements. The trap is relying on the plat and the deed without the title commitment, missing recorded easements and restrictions that the plat does not show and that bind the land.

### Prepare Dedications and Reservations on Plats Correctly

A dedication on a subdivision plat conveys land to the public (streets, alleys, parks, easements) for public use, and a reservation withholds a right or an area from the grant (a reservation of an easement, or of a building setback line). Draft the dedication language to clearly identify what is dedicated, to whom (the public, the county, the utility), and for what purpose, because ambiguous dedications create disputes over whether title passed and who maintains the dedicated land. Confirm that the dedication meets the local subdivision regulations and the acceptance requirements, because a dedication not accepted by the governing body may not vest the public right, and record the plat to make the dedication effective. For reservations, state the reserved right, the area, and the beneficiary, and ensure the reservation does not inadvertently burden the lots in a way the regulations prohibit. The disciplined rule is to coordinate the plat's dedications and reservations with the title commitment and the easement instruments, so the recorded documents are consistent and the encumbrances are fully disclosed. The trap is a plat dedication that is ambiguous or unaccepted, leaving streets or easements in limbo, or a reservation that over-burdens the lots and blocks development.

### Verify Closure, Sufficiency, and Marketability Before Recordation

A legal description must be sufficient to locate the parcel on the ground and to identify it without ambiguity, and its sufficiency is tested by closure, by reconciliation with the plat and monuments, and by the absence of conflict with the record. Check that a metes and bounds description closes mathematically within an acceptable tolerance, that the courses reconcile with the recorded plat and the field monuments, and that the area is consistent with the conveyance. Check that a lot and block description references a recorded plat that is clear and that the lot exists as described, and that a PLSS description is valid for the section's subdivision. Confirm that the description, the plat, the easements, and the title commitment are mutually consistent, because an inconsistency is a defect that clouds title and that a buyer or lender will require cured. The disciplined rule is to engage a licensed land surveyor for the preparation and certification of any recordable description or plat, and counsel for the legal sufficiency of the conveyance, because sufficiency and marketability are legal conclusions. The trap is recording a description or plat with a misclosure or a conflict, creating a title defect that must be cured by a corrective instrument or a quiet-title action.

## Common Traps

### The Description That Does Not Close or Conflicts With the Plat

A metes and bounds description's courses do not close, or the description conflicts with the recorded plat, and it is used for conveyance anyway. The mechanism is that the misclosure or conflict signals a transcription, survey, or conveyance error. The false signal is a description that looks complete. The harm is an unmarketable title, a boundary dispute, or construction in the wrong location, requiring a corrective deed.

### The Easement Label Treated as the Full Grant

A plat shows a utility easement label, and the team relies on it without reading the recorded easement instrument, which grants broader rights or imposes conditions. The mechanism is that the plat label summarizes but does not contain the grant's terms. The false signal is a clear easement line on the plat. The harm is an overbroad easement that forecloses development or conditions that restrict use, discovered after the design is fixed.

### The Title Commitment Obtained Late or Not at All

The design proceeds on the plat and deed without the title commitment, and recorded easements and restrictions in Schedule B are missed. The mechanism is that the title commitment's exceptions are the encumbrances that bind the land, and missing them leaves them unaddressed. The false signal is a clean-looking plat. The harm is an encumbrance discovered late that prohibits the building or the use, forcing redesign or killing the project.

### The Ambiguous or Unaccepted Plat Dedication

A plat dedicates streets or easements with ambiguous language, or the dedication is not accepted by the governing body, leaving the public right in limbo. The mechanism is that a dedication must be clear and accepted to vest the public right. The false signal is a recorded plat with dedication language. The harm is streets or easements whose title or maintenance is unresolved, blocking permits and development.

## Self-Check

- For a metes and bounds description, did I read it completely (POB, basis of bearing, courses, curve data, area, recording references), confirm it closes mathematically, and reconcile it with the plat and the field monuments?
- For a lot and block or PLSS description, did I confirm the referenced plat is recorded and clear, that the lot or aliquot part exists as described, and that the description follows the system's rules for irregular sections and government lots?
- For every easement, did I determine whether it is appurtenant or in gross, read the recorded instrument for its purpose, location, width, term, maintenance, and assignment rights, and avoid relying on the plat label alone?
- Did I obtain the title commitment, read Schedule B item by item, obtain and map each recorded exception against the parcel and the proposed development, and update the commitment through closing?
- For plat dedications and reservations, did I draft clear language identifying what is dedicated or reserved, to whom, and for what purpose, confirm local subdivision and acceptance requirements, and coordinate with the title commitment and easement instruments?
- Did I engage a licensed land surveyor for any recordable description or plat, defer to counsel for the legal sufficiency and marketability of the conveyance, and confirm the description, plat, easements, and title commitment are mutually consistent before recordation?
