# Boundary Survey and Retracement

A boundary survey is the determination of a property line's location on the ground, and a retracement is the re-establishment of a previously created boundary where the original monuments and evidence must be found, weighed, and followed rather than re-measured and corrected. The judgment problem is that a boundary looks like a geometric exercise but is a legal reconstruction governed by a hierarchy of evidence and by the intent of the parties at the time the boundary was created, and agents tend to treat the deed distances as controlling and miss that a found original monument in a different location governs, that the senior deed prevails over the junior, and that the surveyor's job is to follow the footsteps of the original surveyor, not to improve on them. The harm this skill prevents is a line established in the wrong place, an encroachment built on a neighbor's land, a corner reset by measurement that overthrows a controlling monument, and the litigation, removal, and liability that follow. This skill covers the priority of calls, senior and junior rights, monuments and evidence, the intent of the parties, ALTA/NSPS land title surveys, riparian and littoral boundaries, and PLSS section subdivision. It is a strictly licensed domain: boundary determination, monument setting, and retracement are reserved to a licensed land surveyor, and the agent must defer to and engage one for any boundary opinion or corner reset.

## Core Rules

### Apply the Priority of Calls and the Hierarchy of Evidence

The boundary is controlled by a hierarchy of evidence, codified in the rules of construction and tracing to common law, and the surveyor must weigh the evidence in order: (1) natural monuments (rivers, trees, enduring natural features), (2) artificial monuments set by the original surveyor, (3) calls in the deed for adjoiners or monuments, (4) courses (distance and direction), and (5) area, with the senior right prevailing where two deeds conflict. A physical monument found in the ground controls over a bearing-and-distance description, because the monument is what the parties stood at when the boundary was created, and the measurements were only a way to find it. The disciplined rule is to never move, destroy, or "correct" a controlling monument, to record its found condition, and to recognize that measurements are evidence of the monument's location, not the reverse. The trap is recalculating a corner position from the deed distances and resetting it where the math says it should be, when a found original monument a few feet away controls, because the surveyor's role in retracement is to follow, not to improve.

### Resolve Senior and Junior Rights and the Intent of the Parties

When two deeds overlap or conflict, the senior (earlier) deed prevails over the junior (later) deed, and the senior grantee takes the land described, leaving the junior grantee whatever remains, a doctrine known as "first in time, first in right." The intent of the parties at the time of conveyance governs the construction of the description, and the surveyor seeks that intent from the deed language, the contemporaneous plats, the monuments called for, and the surrounding circumstances, because the deed is interpreted as the parties understood it then, not as the math reads now. Apply the doctrine of practical location and acquiescence only where the law and the evidence support a boundary by agreement or by long possession, and never substitute a later agreement for the original conveyance without legal basis. The disciplined rule is to gather the complete chain of title and the senior and junior conveyances before opining on a conflict, because the resolution depends on the sequence and the intent, and a boundary determined without the chain of title is incomplete. The trap is resolving an overlap by splitting the difference, which has no basis in the rules of construction and creates a new defect.

### Locate and Evaluate Monuments and All Available Evidence

The retracement surveyor must find and evaluate every category of evidence: the original monuments called for in the deed or plat, the bearing trees and witness marks, the fences and occupation lines (which are evidence, not control, unless they establish a boundary by acquiescence), the adjoiner surveys, the prior retracements, and the record measurements. Document each item of evidence with its nature, its found condition, its position, and its weight, and reconcile the evidence through the hierarchy rather than selecting the evidence that fits a preferred position. Where an original monument is not found, search for collateral evidence (bearing trees, accessory monuments, line trees) and apply proportionate measurement or other restoration methods only as the last resort and per the controlling manual or statute. The disciplined rule is to treat "not found" as a problem to resolve with evidence, not a gap to fill with measurement, and to record the search and the basis of the conclusion. The trap is accepting the first monument found and building the retracement on it without searching for corroborating or conflicting evidence that would change the weight.

### Conduct ALTA/NSPS Land Title Surveys to the Standard

An ALTA/NSPS land title survey is a comprehensive boundary and land title survey meeting the jointly published ALTA/NSPS standards, performed for a commercial real estate transaction, and it integrates the boundary determination with the title commitment's Schedule B exceptions. Table A is the optional table of survey requirements and specifications, and the client and the surveyor select the items (such as Table A Item 2, address; Item 3, zoning; Item 6(c), utilities; Item 7(a)(b)(c), flood zone; Item 16, evidence of use by others) that the survey must address. The surveyor must show the monuments found and set, the recorded easements and rights-of-way from the title commitment, the observed improvements, the encroachments, and the gaps and overlaps, and must certify to the title insurer, the lender, and the parties. The disciplined rule is to read the title commitment's Schedule B exceptions and to map each one, because an ALTA survey that does not address the title exceptions fails its purpose, and to certify only to the parties and the standard, not to additional parties without amendment. The trap is performing an ALTA survey as a routine boundary survey without the title integration, leaving the title exceptions unaddressed and the certification incomplete.

### Handle Riparian Boundaries and PLSS Section Subdivision

Riparian and littoral boundaries, formed by water, move with the water under the doctrines of accretion (gradual change, boundary follows the water), erosion (gradual loss), and avulsion (sudden change, boundary stays at the former channel), and the surveyor must determine which doctrine applies from the history of the change. The ordinary high water mark, the mean high water line, and the navigability determination affect whether the bed is privately or publicly owned and where the boundary lies, and these are governed by state law and federal navigability doctrine. For PLSS section subdivision, follow the BLM Manual of Surveying Instructions for the order of restoration (single corner, lost corner, proportionate measurement, and the subdivision of sections into aliquot parts and government lots), because the manual's methods are the controlling procedure for federal land. The disciplined rule is to apply the correct legal doctrine for water boundaries and the correct manual for PLSS, because both are governed by specific law and procedure, not by general measurement. The trap is treating a moved riverbank as a fixed boundary or subdividing a section by simple quartering without the manual's rules, producing lines that do not match the official survey.

## Common Traps

### Resetting a Corner by Measurement Over a Found Monument

A surveyor recalculates a corner from the deed distances and resets it where the math places it, ignoring or removing a found original monument a short distance away. The mechanism is the hierarchy of evidence placing original monuments above measurements. The false signal is the mathematically clean position. The harm is a line that overthrows the controlling monument, creating overlaps, gaps, and encroachments against adjoiners.

### Splitting the Difference in an Overlapping Deed Conflict

Two deeds overlap, and the surveyor resolves the conflict by placing the line midway between the competing positions. The mechanism is that the senior right prevails, and a midpoint has no legal basis. The false signal is a compromise that looks fair. The harm is a boundary that does not match either conveyance, creating defects for both parcels and inviting litigation.

### Building a Retracement on the First Monument Found

The surveyor finds one monument and proceeds without searching for bearing trees, accessory monuments, adjoiner evidence, or prior retracements that might shift the weight. The mechanism is that a single item of evidence, uncorroborated, may be misidentified or disturbed. The false signal is a found monument that looks right. The harm is a retracement built on a wrong or weak identification, with the error propagating to every dependent corner.

### Treating a Moved Riverbank as a Fixed Boundary

A riparian boundary is set at a historical channel position, ignoring that the river moved by accretion and the boundary followed it, or that it moved by avulsion and the boundary stayed. The mechanism is that the doctrine of accretion versus avulsion determines whether the boundary moves. The false signal is a clear channel line on an old map. The harm is a boundary that does not reflect the legal doctrine, placing land in the wrong ownership.

## Self-Check

- Did I apply the priority of calls and hierarchy of evidence (natural monuments, artificial monuments, calls, courses, area), placing found original monuments above measurements, and did I avoid moving or correcting any controlling monument?
- For any overlapping or conflicting deeds, did I establish the chain of title, determine the senior and junior rights, and seek the intent of the parties at the time of conveyance rather than splitting the difference?
- Did I locate and evaluate every category of evidence (called monuments, bearing trees, fences and occupation, adjoiner surveys, prior retracements, record measurements), document each item's weight, and reconcile the evidence through the hierarchy?
- For an ALTA/NSPS survey, did I read the title commitment's Schedule B exceptions, select the applicable Table A items, map each exception and observed improvement, and certify only to the parties and the standard?
- For riparian or littoral boundaries, did I determine whether the change was accretion, erosion, or avulsion, locate the ordinary high water mark, and apply the state-law navigability and ownership rules?
- For PLSS work, did I follow the BLM Manual of Surveying Instructions for corner restoration and section subdivision, and did I engage a licensed land surveyor for the boundary determination, corner setting, and recordable survey?
