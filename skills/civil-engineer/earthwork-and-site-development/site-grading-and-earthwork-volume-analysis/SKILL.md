# Site Grading and Earthwork Volume Analysis

Site grading and earthwork volume analysis is the engineering of the ground reshaping for the development: the establishment of the design elevations, the balancing of the cut and the fill, the design of the slopes and the benches, and the specification of the compaction and the shrink-swell. The grading determines the drainage, the foundation support, the earthwork cost, and the site's integration with the surrounding. The harm this skill prevents is a grading plan that creates the unbalanced earthwork (the expensive borrow or the waste), the slopes that are the unstable (the failure and the property damage), the drainage that is the unmanaged (the flooding and the erosion), or the fill that is the uncompacted and the unsuitable (the settlement and the foundation failure). Because the earthwork is the large-volume, the early, and the foundational, the grading must be against the site, the soil, and the drainage, with the balanced volume, the stable slope, and the verified compaction.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Design Elevations and the Constraints

The design elevations (the building pads, the roads, the parking, the detention) must be established against the constraints: the existing grade, the off-site drainage (the inflow and the outflow), the foundation (the bearing and the settlement), the utilities (the gravity flow), the property line, the code (the freeboard, the slope), and the aesthetics. Establish the design elevations that meet the constraints, with the minimum earthwork, the positive drainage, and the integration with the surrounding. A grading that ignores the constraints (the off-site drainage, the property line) creates the problems that the regrading cannot fix, because the constraints were not in the elevation.

### Balance the Cut and the Fill With the Shrink and the Swell

The earthwork balance (the cut volume versus the fill volume) must account for the shrink (the fill compacts to less than the cut volume) and the swell (the rock or the dense soil expands when excavated), because the bank volume (the in-place) differs from the loose and the compacted. Calculate the cut and the fill (the cross-section, the grid, the TIN), and adjust for the shrink (commonly 10 to 20 percent for the soil) and the swell (commonly 20 to 40 percent for the rock), and balance the cut and the fill to minimise the borrow or the waste. A balance that ignores the shrink and the swell will be the wrong, because the volume change was not accounted.

### Design the Slopes, the Benches, and the Drainage for the Stability

The slopes (the cut and the fill) must be designed for the stability (the soil, the height, the water), with the benches (the intermediate, for the tall slope) and the drainage (the crest and the toe, the bench drains). Design the slope for the soil's friction and cohesion, the height, and the groundwater, with the conservative slope ratio (commonly 2:1 to 3:1 for the soil, the flatter for the weak or the wet), and the bench at the vertical intervals (commonly every 7 to 10 metres). Provide the drainage at the crest (the diversion of the upland runoff) and the toe (the collection), and the bench drains, because the water on the slope is the primary cause of the failure. A slope that is the steep for the soil, or the unbenched for the height, or the undrained, fails, because the stability and the drainage were not in the design.

### Verify the Slope Stability and the Global Stability

The slope stability (the circular and the non-circular, the static and the seismic) and the global stability (the deep-seated, the wall, the building) must be verified for the cut and the fill slope. Perform the slope stability analysis with the site-specific soil parameters and the groundwater, and verify the factor of safety (commonly 1.3 to 1.5 for the static, the higher for the seismic). For the fill on the soft ground, verify the global stability (the deep-seated failure through the soft layer), and the settlement. A slope that is the stable in the dry but the unstable in the wet or the seismic fails, because the water and the seismic were not in the analysis.

### Specify the Compaction and the Material Suitability

The fill compaction and the material suitability must be specified for the fill that supports the structures, the roads, and the slopes. Specify the compaction (the percent of the maximum dry density, commonly 95 percent for the structural fill, the 90 percent for the landscape), the moisture (the optimum plus or minus 2 percent), and the material (the gradation, the plasticity, the organic-free). Specify the test (the Proctor, the field density), and the frequency, and the unsuitable material (the organic, the expansive, the contaminated) for the removal or the treatment. A fill that is the uncompacted or the unsuitable settles or fails, because the compaction and the suitability were not specified.

### Coordinate the Grading With the Drainage, the Foundations, and the Erosion Control

The grading must be coordinated with the drainage (the surface and the subsurface), the foundations (the bearing, the settlement, the depth), and the erosion control (the temporary and the permanent). The grading establishes the surface drainage (the swales, the inlets, the detention), and the foundation elevations (the bearing stratum, the frost depth), and the erosion control (the staged disturbance, the stabilisation). A grading that is designed in isolation, without the drainage, the foundation, and the erosion, creates the conflicts that the redesign cannot fix, because the coordination was not performed.

### Plan the Phasing and the Staging

The earthwork must be phased and staged with the construction: the clearing and the grubbing, the rough grading, the utility installation, the fine grading, and the stabilisation. Plan the phasing to minimise the exposed area (the erosion control), the sequence the cut and the fill (the balance), and the staging (the stockpile, the borrow, the waste). A grading that is the unphased exposes the large area to the erosion, and the unbalanced earthwork, because the phasing was not planned.

## Common Traps

### The Unbalanced Earthwork

The grading plan is designed without the volume balance, and the site has the large borrow or the waste, and the cost and the schedule increase. The trap is that the elevations are the set, while the volume balance is the not checked, and the site is the unbalanced. The false signal is the completed grading plan; the harm is the expensive borrow or waste, because the balance was not verified.

### The Shrink And Swell Ignored

The earthwork balance is calculated without the shrink and the swell, and the actual fill volume is the different, and the balance is the wrong. The trap is that the bank volume is the used, while the compacted and the loose differ, and the balance is the wrong. The false signal is the balanced volume; the harm is the wrong balance, because the shrink and the swell were not accounted.

### The Slope Too Steep Or Unbenched

The slope is designed too steep for the soil, or the tall slope is the unbenched, and the slope fails. The trap is that the slope is the steep for the aesthetics or the area, while the soil and the height require the flatter or the benched, and the slope fails. The false signal is the designed slope; the harm is the slope failure, because the stability and the benching were not in the design.

### The Water On The Slope

The slope is designed without the crest and the toe drainage, and the water flows over and through the slope, and the slope fails. The trap is that the slope is the designed, while the drainage is the not, and the water causes the failure. The false signal is the stable-looking slope; the harm is the water-induced failure, because the drainage was not provided.

### The Fill Uncompacted Or Unsuitable

The fill is placed without the compaction specification, or the unsuitable material (the organic, the expansive) is the used, and the fill settles or fails. The trap is that the fill is the placed, while the compaction and the suitability are the not specified, and the fill is the poor. The false signal is the placed fill; the harm is the settlement or the failure, because the compaction and the suitability were not verified.

### The Grading Without The Coordination

The grading is designed without the coordination with the drainage, the foundation, and the erosion control, and the conflicts arise. The trap is that the grading is the completed, while the coordination is the not, and the conflicts are the unresolvable. The false signal is the completed grading; the harm is the conflicts and the rework, because the coordination was not performed.

## Self-Check

- Are the design elevations established against the constraints (the existing grade, the off-site drainage, the foundation, the utilities, the property line, the code)?
- Is the cut and fill balanced with the shrink and the swell, and the borrow or the waste minimised?
- Are the slopes designed for the stability (the soil, the height, the water), with the benches and the crest and toe drainage?
- Is the slope stability (the circular, the non-circular, the static, the seismic) and the global stability verified, with the site-specific parameters and the factor of safety?
- Is the compaction (the percent, the moisture) and the material suitability (the gradation, the plasticity, the organic-free) specified, with the test and the frequency?
- Is the grading coordinated with the drainage (the surface, the subsurface), the foundations (the bearing, the frost), and the erosion control (the staged disturbance, the stabilisation)?
- Is the earthwork phased and staged, with the clearing, the rough grading, the utility, the fine grading, and the stabilisation sequenced?
- Are the unsuitable materials (the organic, the expansive, the contaminated) identified for the removal or the treatment?
