# Concrete Testing and Strength Acceptance

Concrete strength acceptance is a domain governed by statistical criteria, not by single-test pass-fail, where a single low cylinder is not necessarily a failure and a single high cylinder is not necessarily a success, and where the investigation of a low-strength test must distinguish between a concrete quality problem, a sampling and curing problem, and a testing problem before any remediation is decided. The recurring failure is to treat each cylinder break as a pass-fail verdict, to over-react to a single low break with core drilling and rejection before investigating the cause, or to under-react to a pattern of low breaks by averaging it away, and to confuse the strength of the standard-cured cylinders (which measure the concrete quality) with the strength of the in-place concrete (which is affected by placement, curing, and age). The engineer's job is to apply the statistical acceptance criteria correctly, to investigate low-strength tests systematically to find the cause, and to decide on acceptance, rejection, or remediation based on the in-place strength and the structural consequence. This skill covers the judgment used to interpret concrete tests and make strength acceptance decisions, with the overriding principle that acceptance is statistical and the investigation of a low test must find the cause before the remedy.

## Core Rules

### Apply the Statistical Acceptance Criteria, Not Single-Test Pass-Fail

Concrete strength acceptance per ACI 318 is statistical: the average of any three consecutive tests must equal or exceed the specified strength (f'c), and no individual test may fall below f'c by more than 500 psi (or by more than 0.10 f'c for f'c over 5000 psi). A single test below f'c but above the minimum is not a failure; it is within the statistical variation the criteria are designed to tolerate. Apply the criteria as written, tracking the running average of three consecutive tests and the individual minimum, and do not declare a failure or a pass on a single test. The criteria assume a consistent concrete supply and a consistent testing process, and they are the basis for acceptance; overriding them with a stricter single-test rule rejects acceptable concrete, and overriding them with a looser rule accepts non-compliant concrete.

### Distinguish Standard-Cured Cylinders From In-Place Concrete

Standard-cured cylinders (cured under lab conditions and tested at 28 days) measure the potential strength of the concrete as supplied and batched; they do not measure the strength of the concrete in the structure, which is affected by placement, consolidation, curing temperature, moisture, and age. Field-cured cylinders (cured alongside the structure) approximate the in-place strength for formwork removal and loading decisions, and in-place methods (rebound hammer, Windsor probe, ultrasonic pulse velocity, and especially core testing) measure the actual in-place strength. When a standard-cured cylinder test is low, it indicates a problem with the concrete supply or the cylinder sampling and curing, not necessarily a problem with the structure. When the in-place strength is in question, core the structure and test the cores, because the cylinder and the structure can have very different strengths.

### Investigate Low-Strength Tests Systematically to Find the Cause

When a low-strength test occurs, investigate the cause before deciding on remediation, because the cause determines the remedy. Check the concrete supply (batch tickets, mix design, water content, admixtures, cementitious materials), the sampling and curing of the cylinders (did the field technician sample correctly, were the cylinders cured properly in the lab), the testing (was the cylinder capped or ground correctly, was the break a good break or an eccentric or cap failure), and the placement and curing in the structure. A low break caused by a sampling or testing error does not indicate a concrete problem and does not require structural remediation; a low break caused by a concrete supply problem may indicate a widespread issue requiring investigation of all placements from the affected batches. The investigation must precede the remedy.

### Use Core Testing to Evaluate In-Place Strength When It Matters

When the standard-cured cylinder tests indicate a potential concrete problem, or when the in-place strength is in question for structural reasons, core the structure and test the cores per ASTM C42 to evaluate the actual in-place strength. Take cores from locations representative of the suspect concrete, from locations of low stress if possible (to minimize damage), and in sufficient number (commonly at least three cores from the suspect area) to be statistically meaningful. Interpret the core strengths per ACI 318 (the average of at least three cores equalling 85 percent of f'c and no single core below 75 percent is the equivalent-strength acceptance criterion), and use the result to decide on acceptance or remediation. Core testing is the definitive measure of in-place strength, and it is the basis for the structural decision when the cylinders are low.

### Evaluate In-Place Strength for Formwork Removal and Loading

The strength required for formwork removal, post-tensioning, and load application is the in-place strength at the age of the operation, not the 28-day f'c. Determine the in-place strength using field-cured cylinders, maturity methods, or in-place tests, and confirm it meets the required percentage of f'c (commonly 70 to 100 percent depending on the operation and the span) before proceeding. Removing formwork or applying load before the in-place strength is adequate causes deflection, cracking, or collapse, and the 28-day f'c is not the relevant value for an operation at 3 or 7 days. The in-place strength at the age of the operation is the governing value.

### Review the Mix Design and the Batch Tickets for Compliance

The concrete quality starts with the mix design and the batching, and the review of the mix design (proportions, cementitious content, water-cement ratio, admixtures, aggregate gradation) and the batch tickets (actual batch weights, water added, admixture dosages, delivery time) is part of quality management. Confirm the mix design meets the durability and strength requirements, confirm the batch tickets show the concrete supplied matches the mix design (no excess water, correct admixtures), and track the consistency across deliveries. A concrete supply that drifts from the mix design — extra water on site, wrong admixture dosage — produces low-strength concrete that the cylinders will eventually catch, but the batch ticket review catches it earlier.

### Manage the Sampling, Curing, and Testing Process for Valid Results

The validity of the cylinder test depends on the sampling, the initial curing in the field, the transport, the lab curing, and the testing all being performed per ASTM standards, and deviations in any step produce low breaks that do not reflect the concrete quality. Ensure the field technician samples per ASTM C172 (from the middle of the load, after some discharge), the initial field curing per ASTM C31 (at 60 to 80 degrees F, in roto-mold or single-use molds), the lab curing and testing per ASTM C192 and C39, and the capping or end grinding per ASTM C617 or C1231. A low break from a sampling or curing deviation is a testing problem, not a concrete problem, and managing the testing process is essential to getting valid results.

## Common Traps

### Single Low Break Treated as a Failure, Immediate Core Drilling

The engineer treats a single cylinder break below f'c as a failure and immediately cores the structure or rejects the concrete, without applying the statistical criteria or investigating the cause. The mechanism is that a single number below the spec feels like a failure. The false signal is a break "below spec." The harm is that the single break may be within the acceptable statistical variation, or may be a testing error, and the over-reaction wastes cores, delays the project, and damages the structure unnecessarily. The statistical criteria and the cause investigation must precede the remedy.

### Pattern of Low Breaks Averaged Away

The engineer has a pattern of low breaks but focuses on the running average and dismisses the trend, without recognizing that the concrete supply has drifted or the testing process has a problem. The mechanism is that the average is within criteria and each individual break is "not that low." The false signal is an average that "passes." The harm is that the concrete supply is systematically low or the testing is flawed, the structure is built with sub-standard concrete, and the pattern is not addressed until a structural problem appears. A pattern of low breaks must be investigated even when the average passes.

### Standard-Cured Cylinder Low Result Interpreted as In-Place Weakness

A low standard-cured cylinder break is interpreted as a weakness in the structure, leading to remediation of the structure, when the low break reflects a cylinder sampling, curing, or testing problem, not the in-place concrete. The mechanism is that the cylinder is seen as representing the structure. The false signal is a cylinder break "for that placement." The harm is that the structure, which may be perfectly adequate, is cored, repaired, or rejected based on a cylinder that does not represent it, while the actual concrete problem (if any) goes unaddressed. The cylinder and the structure must be distinguished, and the in-place strength confirmed by cores when it matters.

### In-Place Strength for Formwork Removal Based on 28-Day f'c

The contractor removes formwork or applies load based on the assumption that the concrete has reached f'c, without checking the in-place strength at the actual age. The mechanism is that f'c is the strength and the age feels secondary. The false signal is concrete that "has reached design strength." The harm is that at 3 or 7 days, the in-place strength is well below f'c, the formwork is removed too early, and the concrete deflects, cracks, or collapses under the construction or service load. The in-place strength at the age of the operation must be confirmed.

### Core Strength Interpreted Without the ACI Equivalent-Strength Criteria

The engineer takes cores, averages the strengths, and compares the average directly to f'c, without applying the ACI equivalent-strength criteria (85 percent average, 75 percent individual). The mechanism is that the core average compared to f'c feels like the right check. The false signal is a core average "below f'c." The harm is that the criteria account for the inherent lower strength of cores (due to drilling damage, the smaller size, the in-place curing), and comparing directly to f'c rejects acceptable concrete, while the criteria would accept it. The core strengths must be interpreted per the ACI criteria.

### Sampling or Curing Deviations Producing Low Breaks, Attributed to Concrete

The field sampling, initial curing, or lab testing deviates from the ASTM standards, producing low breaks that are attributed to the concrete supply rather than to the testing process. The mechanism is that the cylinder is assumed to represent the concrete and the testing process is assumed correct. The false signal is a low break "for that batch." The harm is that the concrete supply is blamed and possibly rejected when the actual problem is the testing, and the real concrete quality issue (if any) is masked by the testing error. The sampling, curing, and testing process must be managed per ASTM.

### Mix Design or Batch Ticket Non-Compliance Not Caught

The mix design or the batch tickets show non-compliance — excess water, wrong admixture, low cementitious content — and the discrepancy is not caught in the review, so the low-strength concrete is supplied and placed. The mechanism is that the batch ticket review feels like paperwork. The false signal is a delivered concrete load. The harm is that the non-compliant concrete produces low strengths that are discovered only at the 28-day break, by which time the concrete is in the structure and the remediation is costly. The mix design and batch tickets must be reviewed for compliance.

## Self-Check

- Are the strength acceptance criteria (average of three consecutive tests, individual minimum) applied statistically, rather than treating each cylinder break as a pass-fail verdict?
- Are standard-cured cylinders (measuring concrete supply quality) distinguished from in-place concrete strength (measured by field-cured cylinders or cores), with the distinction applied in the interpretation?
- When a low-strength test occurs, is the cause investigated systematically (supply, sampling, curing, testing, placement) before any remediation is decided?
- Is core testing used to evaluate in-place strength when it matters, with the cores taken representatively and interpreted per the ACI equivalent-strength criteria (85 percent average, 75 percent individual)?
- Is the in-place strength at the age of formwork removal, post-tensioning, or load application confirmed by field-cured cylinders or maturity, rather than assumed to equal the 28-day f'c?
- Are the mix design and the batch tickets reviewed for compliance (water content, cementitious content, admixtures, delivery time), with discrepancies caught before placement?
- Is the sampling, initial field curing, transport, lab curing, and testing managed per ASTM standards, so that the cylinder results are valid and reflect the concrete rather than the testing process?
