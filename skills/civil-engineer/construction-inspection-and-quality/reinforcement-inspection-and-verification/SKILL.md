# Reinforcement Inspection and Verification

Reinforcement inspection is the last verification point before liquid concrete locks the structural skeleton into its final, unmodifiable state. Once the pour is complete, the bars cannot be moved, added, or re-lapped, and defects become extremely expensive to find and to fix. The structural engineer's calculations assume a specific bar size at a specific location with a specific embedment and clear cover, and they assume that the bars will be in place and properly bonded when the load arrives. The inspector's job is to confirm that the as-built cage matches the design, that the cover that protects the steel from corrosion is actually present, and that the laps and hooks that transfer force between bars are detailed as drawn. The harm this skill prevents is a structure that is weaker than calculated because a critical bar was omitted, short-cut, or displaced, or that corrodes decades early because the cover was missing or the ties were too widely spaced to confine the core.

## Core Rules

### Verify the Approved Shop Drawings Against the Design First

Reinforcement inspection begins on paper, not in the field. Confirm that the rebar fabricator's shop drawings have been reviewed and stamped by the structural engineer of record, that every bar mark is traceable to a design detail, and that any approved deviations (substitutions, rebar grade changes, lap length increases) are flagged on the drawings the field is using. The most dangerous field error is installing to an old, unapproved revision while the approved revision sits in the trailer. Verify bar grade (commonly 420 or 520 MPa), coating (black, epoxy, or galvanised), and any special seismic or fatigue requirements before any steel reaches the site.

### Confirm Bar Identity, Size, Grade, and Condition

At the cage, verify that each bar mark matches the shop drawing in diameter (bar size), grade (mill marking and grade designation), and length. Reject bars with deep rust pitting that reduces section, mill scale that is loose and flaking, or oil, mud, or form-release agent that would impair bond. Light surface rust is generally acceptable and even beneficial for bond, but anything that flakes off under a light wipe, or any contamination that cannot be brushed off, must be cleaned. For epoxy-coated bars, inspect for coating holidays with a pinhole detector and patch any damage, because a single bare spot becomes the anode for accelerated corrosion in chloride exposure.

### Verify Location, Spacing, and Clear Cover

The single most important field measurement is clear cover, the distance from the concrete surface to the nearest bar surface, because cover governs both fire rating and corrosion protection. Confirm cover against the project specification and the exposure class in the governing code (for example, ACI 318 cover requirements by element and exposure). Use bar supports, chairs, and spacers of the correct height and material (concrete, plastic, or coated steel suited to the exposure), and confirm they are spaced closely enough to keep the cage from sagging under worker traffic and concrete placement weight. Verify bar spacing against the drawings, because a bar omitted or a spacing opened up to ease placement reduces flexural and shear capacity below the design. Confirm that top steel in slabs and beams is held at the correct height by suitable bolsters, because top steel that sinks to the bottom under foot traffic turns a designed T-beam or cantilever into a lightly reinforced section that will fail in service.

### Verify Development Length, Lap Splices, Hooks, and Anchorage

Force transfer into and between bars depends on development length and anchorage, and these are the details most often "rounded off" in the field. Confirm that straight development lengths, standard hooks, and headed bars meet the dimensions and clear spacing assumed in the design, and that any reductions for excess reinforcement or confinement are only those the engineer has explicitly approved. For lap splices, verify lap length against the bar size, grade, concrete strength, cover, and spacing, and confirm that lapped bars are in contact or within the code-allowed gap and securely wired. Verify that splice locations are staggered as detailed, because a row of laps at the same section creates a local weakness and a congestion point that prevents consolidation. Check hook orientation, because a 90-degree or 180-degree hook turned the wrong way does not develop the force the design assumed.

### Resolve Congestion and Interference Before the Pour

Reinforcement congestion, especially at beam-column joints, column-to-footing connections, and coupling-beam boundaries, is where placement and consolidation failures originate. Walk every joint before the pour and confirm that there is a clear path for the vibrator and for the aggregate size specified, that ties and hoops do not block the joint core, and that the joint hoops required for seismic confinement are actually installed and not omitted because they were "too tight to fit." If congestion prevents sound consolidation, raise it immediately with the engineer of record; do not let the crew resolve it by leaving out bars or by vibrating around the outside of the joint.

### Verify Mechanical Couplers, Welded Splices, and Post-Tensioning

For mechanical couplers, confirm the coupler type and manufacturer are on the approved list, that bars are fully engaged to the manufacturer's marked depth, and that couplers are staggered. For welded splices, confirm the welder is qualified for the procedure, that the weld and inspection (visual, magnetic particle, or ultrasonic as required) are documented, and that the heat-affected zone is acceptable for the rebar grade. For post-tensioning, verify duct placement, support spacing, tendon profile (drape) against the design, anchorages, and the stressing sequence and elongation records, because a tendon draped to the wrong profile develops the wrong force distribution in the slab.

### Document and Sign Off the Pre-Pour Inspection

The pre-pour inspection must be recorded against the element ID, with photographs of congested areas, a checklist of bar marks verified, a record of any deviations and their disposition, and a sign-off by the responsible inspector before the concrete order is released. A pour that proceeds without this sign-off is a pour whose reinforcement state cannot later be proven, and any future defect investigation will be unable to distinguish an as-built deviation from a construction error.

## Common Traps

### The Top Steel That Sank

In a slab or beam pour, the top mat of reinforcement is held up on bolsters, but workers walk on the cage during placement, the bolsters tip or crush, and the top bars settle toward the bottom. The trap is that the cage looks correct before the pour and the bars are all present, while the effective depth used in the flexural calculation has been reduced by 30 to 50 mm. The false signal is the presence of the bars; the harm is a slab or cantilever whose moment capacity is 20 to 40 percent below design, which will sag or crack under service load and may fail under full design load.

### The Lap That Is Too Short Because Cover Was Reduced

A field crew, trying to fit a lap into a tight form, butts the bars with a shorter overlap than detailed, reasoning that "a lap is a lap." The trap is that lap length depends on cover and spacing, and a lap installed with less cover than assumed has less bond area and may not develop the bar. The false signal is that the bars are touching and wired; the harm is a splice that pulls out under load, turning a continuous member into two disconnected pieces at the splice location.

### The Beam-Column Joint Hoops Omitted For Fit

At a heavily reinforced joint, the closely spaced confinement hoops required by the seismic provisions are difficult to install, and the crew leaves out several, intending to "make up the area" with extra longitudinal bars. The trap is that the joint looks full of steel, while the confinement that gives the joint its ductility and shear capacity under seismic loading is missing. The false signal is the apparent density of reinforcement; the harm is a joint that is brittle and will fail in shear or bond-slip during an earthquake, precisely when it is most needed.

### The Cover That Disappears At The Edges

Bar supports are placed along the centre of a slab or along the form face of a wall, but at the edges and corners, where workers lean tools and where the form has slight bow, the bars rest directly against the form. The trap is that the cover reads correctly in the accessible middle of the element while the edges, which are the zones most exposed to moisture and chlorides, have zero cover. The false signal is the spot-checked cover at the easy locations; the harm is corrosion initiation at the edges within years of service, with spalling and section loss that propagate inward.

### The Epoxy Coating Damaged In Handling

Epoxy-coated bars are delivered intact, but during placement they are dragged across form tie wires, stacked on bare ground, and walked on, producing hundreds of small coating holidays. The trap is that the bars look coated at a glance, while the pinhole damage turns each bare spot into a concentrated corrosion cell in chloride service. The false signal is the visible green or grey coating; the harm is accelerated localised corrosion that perforates the bar far faster than the uncoated condition the durability design assumed.

### The Rebar Substituted Without Recalculation

A bar size is unavailable, the fabricator substitutes the next larger size "for strength," and the crew installs it without flagging the change to the engineer. The trap is that the larger bar is stronger in area but changes the development length, the splice length, the crack width, and the fit at the joint, and a substitution that increases area can still reduce performance if it no longer fits the joint or develops its force. The false signal is "more steel is always stronger"; the harm is a detail that no longer matches the calculations and may be unbuildable or underperform in bond and crack control.

## Self-Check

- Are the shop drawings in the field the latest approved revision, and are all bar marks traceable to the engineer-approved design?
- Has every bar mark been verified for size, grade, length, and condition, with epoxy-coated bars checked for holidays and repaired?
- Is clear cover verified at multiple locations per element, including edges and corners, against the specification and exposure class, with supports of correct height and spacing?
- Is top steel in slabs and beams confirmed at design height, with bolsters that will not crush or tip under placement traffic?
- Are development lengths, lap splices, hooks, and couplers verified against the bar size, grade, cover, and spacing, with splices staggered as detailed?
- At every beam-column joint and congested region, is there confirmed space for the vibrator and the specified aggregate, and are all confinement hoops installed?
- Are mechanical couplers fully engaged, welded splices qualified and inspected, and post-tensioning ducts profiled and stressed per the approved sequence?
- Is there a documented, photographed pre-pour sign-off for the element before concrete is ordered, including disposition of every deviation?
