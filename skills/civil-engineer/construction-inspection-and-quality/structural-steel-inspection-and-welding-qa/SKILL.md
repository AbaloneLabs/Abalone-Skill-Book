# Structural Steel Inspection and Welding QA

Structural steel inspection is the discipline of verifying that the frame built in the field matches the calculated capacity assumed on paper, connection by connection and weld by weld. Steel design depends critically on the integrity of the connections, and the connections depend on the integrity of the welds and bolts that make them. A perfectly designed moment frame is only as good as the field-welded joint at the column face, and a slip-critical braced connection is only as good as the bolt pretension and faying-surface preparation. The inspector's job is to verify material identity, fit-up, weld procedures and welder qualifications, the sequence and quality of bolting and welding, and the nondestructive testing that confirms soundness. The harm this skill prevents is a connection that looks complete but contains a crack, a lack of fusion, or an undertorqued bolt, any of which can trigger a brittle or progressive failure under the seismic or gravity load the connection was designed to resist.

## Core Rules

### Verify Material Identity and Mill Test Reports Before Erection

Every primary structural member must be traceable, via heat number and mill test report, to the grade specified in the design (commonly ASTM A992 for wide-flange shapes, A500 for HSS, A36 for plates and angles, or project-specific high-strength grades). Confirm that the certified mill test reports have been reviewed and accepted, that heat numbers are transferred to the erection drawings, and that members are tagged or otherwise traceable in the field. A member erected with the wrong grade, or a high-strength bolt lot mixed with a lower-strength lot, can silently reduce the capacity of the connection or member below the design value.

### Verify Fit-Up, Geometry, and Erection Tolerance Before Welding or Bolting

Before any connection is made, confirm that the members are positioned within the AISC Code of Standard Practice tolerances for alignment, plumbness, and joint fit-up. Welded joints require the specified root opening, bevel angle, and gap; a joint forced together with come-alongs or spacers to compensate for poor fit-up will lock in residual stresses and may produce a weld that looks complete but is internally flawed. Bolted joints require correct hole size and alignment; holes enlarged by burning, or slots cut in the field to make misaligned members fit, reduce bearing area and net section and are prohibited by the specification unless explicitly approved by the engineer.

### Verify Welding Procedure Specifications and Welder Qualification

Every welded joint must be performed under an approved Welding Procedure Specification (WPS) that references a qualified Procedure Qualification Record (PQR), and every welder must be qualified for the process, position, and thickness range being welded (per AWS D1.1 for buildings, or the project-specific bridge code AWS D1.5). Before welding begins, confirm that the WPS in use matches the joint, that the welder's qualification is current and covers the deposition variables, and that the essential variables (process, base metal, filler metal, preheat, interpass temperature, and position) are within the qualified ranges. A weld made outside the WPS is an unqualified weld regardless of how it looks.

### Control Preheat, Interpass Temperature, and Environmental Conditions

For most structural steels and especially for thick members, high-strength grades, and low ambient temperatures, preheat and interpass temperature control are essential to prevent hydrogen-induced cracking in the heat-affected zone. Verify that preheat is applied and measured at the correct distance from the weld, that interpass temperature is maintained within the WPS range, and that welding is not performed in high wind, rain, or temperatures below the WPS minimum without shelter and heating. The most common field crack source is a weld started on cold, damp steel with inadequate preheat, producing a hydrogen crack that may not surface until the connection is loaded.

### Verify Bolting: Snug-Tight, Pretensioned, and Slip-Critical

Confirm the bolt type and grade (ASTM A325 or A490, or their F1852 and F2280 twist-off equivalents), the joint type (bearing, pretensioned, or slip-critical), and the installation method (turn-of-nut, calibrated wrench, twist-off, or direct tension indicator). For snug-tight joints, verify that all plies are in firm contact. For pretensioned and slip-critical joints, verify that the faying surfaces are free of paint, oil, rust, and mill scale (or that only approved coatings with tested slip coefficients are present), that bolts are installed in the correct sequence to avoid relaxation, and that the specified minimum bolt tension is achieved and verified by the project's method. A slip-critical joint installed over an unqualified painted faying surface will slip under service load.

### Witness and Review Nondestructive Testing

Nondestructive testing (NDT) is the verification that weld soundness matches the appearance. Confirm that the NDT scope (which welds, what percentage, what method) follows the specification and the engineer's requirements, that the technician is qualified (commonly to a recognised certification scheme), and that the method is appropriate: ultrasonic testing for thick complete-joint-penetration groove welds, magnetic particle for surface and near-surface flaws in ferromagnetic material, radiographic where the geometry suits it, and liquid penetrant for surface flaws on non-magnetic material. Review every indication against the acceptance criteria; a rejectable indication is not a paperwork item but a weld that must be gouged out, repaired under an approved repair WPS, and re-tested.

### Manage Sequence, Distortion, and Sequential Loading

Welding shrinks as it cools, and the sequence in which joints are welded determines whether the frame pulls into tolerance or out of it. Verify that the welding sequence respects the contractor's approved sequencing plan, that shrinkage compensation is built into the fit-up, and that members are not loaded beyond their erected capacity before the connections are completed. For seismically loaded moment connections, confirm that the weld backing bar is removed and the weld toe reinforced where the detail requires, because the backing-bar notch at the column face was a primary initiator of the brittle fractures observed in past earthquakes.

### Document, Reject, and Disposition Nonconformance

Every rejected weld, every misfit, every damaged faying surface, and every out-of-tolerance member must be documented with location, photographs, NDT reports, and a disposition signed by the engineer of record. Repairs are not field decisions; they are engineering decisions, because a gouged-out weld changes the heat input, a flame-straightened member changes its material properties, and an enlarged hole changes the net section. The inspector's role is to ensure that no nonconformance is absorbed silently and that every repair is performed under an approved procedure and re-verified.

## Common Traps

### The Good-Looking Weld With Lack of Fusion

A weld is run with insufficient heat input or too fast a travel speed, producing a bead that is uniform and clean to the eye but has not fused to the side wall or root. The trap is that the weld passes visual inspection and may even pass surface NDT, while ultrasonic testing reveals a planar lack-of-fusion defect that has effectively no toughness. The false signal is the cosmetic appearance; the harm is a connection that fails in brittle fracture under seismic overload, precisely the load case the weld was specified to resist.

### The Slip-Critical Joint Over Paint

A slip-critical connection is specified because the load reverses or is seismic, but the fabricator applied a primer to all steel including the faying surfaces, and the erector did not mask or grind the contact area. The trap is that the bolts are torqued to full pretension and the connection "looks tight," while the slip coefficient of the painted surface is far below the bare-steel value the design assumed. The false signal is the achieved bolt tension; the harm is a connection that slips under service or seismic load, shedding load to other members and potentially initiating progressive redistribution.

### The Cold Weld On Thick Steel

On a cold morning, a welder starts a groove weld on a thick column without bringing the steel to the required preheat, because preheat takes time and the schedule is tight. The trap is that the weld is deposited and looks complete, while the rapid cooling has produced a hard, brittle microstructure in the heat-affected zone and may have initiated hydrogen cracks that propagate under load. The false signal is the completed bead; the harm is a crack that grows over the structure's life and may suddenly fracture the connection under a future overload.

### The Backing Bar Left In Place

On a moment connection, the weld backing bar at the bottom flange is left in place because removal is slow and difficult, and the detail is "almost" the prequalified one. The trap is that the backing bar creates a notch and a crack initiator at the point of highest stress demand, exactly the condition that caused widespread brittle fractures in moment frames during the Northridge earthquake. The false signal is that the weld "has backing like the detail shows"; the harm is a connection with a fraction of the intended seismic ductility.

### The Field-Enlarged Hole

Members arrive slightly misaligned, and rather than report the fit-up problem, the crew burns out the bolt holes to a larger size or cuts slots so the bolts will go through. The trap is that the connection is now "bolted up" and plumb, while the bearing area is reduced, the bolt may now be in a slot that allows slip, and the net section of the connected plate may be below the design value. The false signal is the assembled, plumb connection; the harm is a joint that does not develop its design shear or bearing capacity.

### The NDT Sampled But Not Representative

The specification calls for 10 percent ultrasonic testing of a weld class, and the contractor selects the easiest, most accessible welds for testing, leaving the overhead and congested welds untested. The trap is that the NDT report shows "no rejectable indications" while the welds most likely to contain defects, the difficult ones, have not been examined. The false signal is the clean NDT report; the harm is a population of uninspected welds that may contain the very defects the testing was meant to catch.

## Self-Check

- Are all primary members traceable via heat number to accepted mill test reports, and are bolt lots verified for grade and not mixed?
- Before welding or bolting, is fit-up verified within tolerance, with correct root opening, bevel, hole size, and no field-burned holes or unapproved slots?
- Is every welded joint performed under an approved WPS, by welders qualified for the position and thickness, with essential variables within range?
- Are preheat and interpass temperature controlled per the WPS, and is welding suspended or sheltered in conditions of wind, rain, or low temperature?
- For slip-critical joints, are faying surfaces verified bare or with approved coatings, and is bolt pretension verified by the specified method?
- Does the NDT scope match the specification and engineer's requirements, is the technician qualified, and is every indication dispositioned against the acceptance criteria?
- For seismic moment connections, are backing bars removed and weld toes reinforced where detailed, and is the welding sequence approved and followed?
- Is every nonconformance documented with location, photographs, and engineer-approved repair procedure, with repairs re-inspected before sign-off?
