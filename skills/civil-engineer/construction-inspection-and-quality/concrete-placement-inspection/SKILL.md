# Concrete Placement Inspection

Concrete placement inspection is the field-level control point that determines whether a mix designed to survive fifty years actually performs like it. The structural calculations, the mix submittal, and the rebar drawings all assume sound, fully consolidated, properly cured concrete in every region of every element. The inspector's job is to verify that assumption against messy reality: trucks arriving late, water added on site to chase slump, vibrating crews stretched thin across a large mat foundation, ambient temperatures that flash set, and finishing operations that seal bleed water under the surface. The harm this skill prevents is not cosmetic. A column with a honeycomb void at its base, a beam with a cold joint at mid-depth, or a slab cured without moisture control can all pass a visual walk-through and then fail under design load, corrode the reinforcement prematurely, or trigger a costly removal-and-replacement order after the forms come off.

## Core Rules

### Verify the Placement Plan Before Any Truck Is Accepted

Before the pour begins, confirm that the approved mix design matches the element being placed: strength class, exposure category, air content for freeze-thaw, maximum aggregate size relative to rebar spacing and clear cover, and any special requirements such as low heat of hydration for mass concrete or corrosion inhibitor for chloride exposure. Check that the placement sequence is documented and that it respects initial and final set times given the anticipated ambient temperature and haul distance. Confirm that the crew, vibrators, finishing tools, curing materials, and weather protection are all on site and adequate for the element volume. A pour that starts without these confirmations is a pour that will be rescued reactively, and reactive rescues on structural concrete rarely produce sound results.

### Control Acceptance at the Point of Delivery

At the truck, the first verification is identity: the batch ticket must match the approved mix design in cement type, admixtures, source, design strength, and slump range, and the truck number and ticket time must be within the allowable discharge window (commonly 90 minutes or 300 revolutions, whichever comes first, per ASTM C94, with project-specific overrides). Reject or require documented engineering disposition for any load that arrives outside these limits. The second verification is fresh properties: slump (or slump flow for self-consolidating concrete), air content where specified, concrete and ambient temperature, and unit weight. These are not bureaucratic checkboxes; a load that is 25 mm over the approved slump, or that arrives at 35 degrees Celsius on a hot day with retarding admixture, is a load whose strength and durability may already be compromised before it leaves the chute.

### Reject On-Site Water Addition Except Under Explicit Approved Procedure

The single most common and most damaging field deviation is adding water to the truck to restore slump. Every litre of added water beyond the design water-cement ratio reduces compressive strength, increases permeability, and raises shrinkage cracking risk. The only acceptable path is the ASTM C94 procedure: water may be added, once, to the truck drum up to the maximum approved mix water, the drum must be mixed at the manufacturer's recommended mixing speed for a minimum number of rotations, and slump and a fresh cylinder sample must then be taken. Any water addition not following this exact procedure, and any addition beyond the design water, is a nonconformance that must be documented and reported to the engineer of record, not absorbed silently into the day's work.

### Observe Placement, Consolidation, and Finishing

During placement, the inspector verifies that concrete is placed in horizontal layers of appropriate depth, that successive layers are placed before the prior layer sets to avoid cold joints, and that drop heights are controlled to prevent segregation (commonly limited to roughly one metre for conventional mixes, with chute, pump, or tremie used for greater heights). Consolidation by internal vibrator must be observed for insertion depth (penetrating into the previous layer), spacing, and duration: too little leaves entrapped air voids and honeycomb, too much causes segregation and sand streaks. Vibrator head diameter must suit the rebar congestion; a large head cannot fit between tight ties in a column. Finishing operations must not begin until bleed water has dissipated, because finishing that traps bleed water under a dense surface skin causes delamination, scaling, and dusting.

### Enforce Curing Immediately After Finishing

Curing is the step most often shortened or skipped, and it has the largest single effect on whether the element reaches its design strength and durability. Confirm that the specified curing method (moist curing, ponding, burlap, curing compound, or insulated forms for mass concrete) is applied as soon as final set permits and maintained for the specified duration, commonly seven days for most structural concrete and longer for high-performance or mass placements. For hot, windy, or low-humidity conditions, verify evaporation control (monomolecular film, fogging, wind breaks) to prevent plastic shrinkage cracking in the first hours. For cold weather, verify thermal protection so the concrete does not freeze before it gains strength and so maturity is tracked rather than assumed.

### Manage Break-Strength Results and Nonconformance

Cylinder or core test results must be evaluated against the project's statistical acceptance criteria, typically the ACI 318 requirement that individual tests are within 3.5 MPa of the design and that running averages of three consecutive tests meet or exceed the design strength. A low break is not automatically a failure of the structure, but it is a trigger for documented engineering evaluation: the engineer of record must determine the affected volume, whether the in-place strength can be verified by cores, whether load capacity is affected, and whether removal, strengthening, or acceptance-as-built is warranted. The inspector's role is to ensure the trigger fires, the data is clean, and no low-strength concrete is quietly accepted.

## Common Traps

### The Slump Recovery That Silently Drops Strength

A truck arrives at the low end of its slump range, the placement crew complains it is stiff and slow to pump, and the batch plant authorises a water addition over the radio. The trap is that slump and workability recover visibly within minutes, so everyone sees a "good" load going down the chute, while the water-cement ratio of that specific load has climbed above the design and the 28-day cylinders for that truck will under-break by 15 to 25 percent. The false signal is the immediate workability; the harm is a structural element that does not meet the strength the calculations assumed, discovered only weeks later when the cylinders break, by which point several floors may already be placed above it.

### The Cold Joint Disguised As A Construction Joint

A pour is delayed by a breakdown, weather, or a late truck, and the surface of the previously placed concrete begins to set. The crew, under schedule pressure, continues placing on top of it rather than stopping to form a clean construction joint. The trap is that the surface looks monolithic once finished, and a cold joint formed against setting concrete has negligible bond and no shear transfer across the interface, behaving structurally as an unplanned hinge or slip plane inside a member that was designed to be continuous. The harm appears only under load or seismic action, when the member cracks and mobilises exactly along that weak plane.

### The Vibrator That Over-Consolidates

An inexperienced operator, taught that "more vibration is safer than less," leaves the vibrator head in one spot too long or inserts it too slowly. The trap is that the concrete looks excellent, flat and free of voids, while the coarse aggregate has sunk and the cement paste and water have risen, producing a weak laitance-rich top layer, sand streaks along the form, and a segregated section that is weaker and more permeable than the design. The false signal is the smooth, void-free surface; the harm is reduced strength and durability in exactly the cover zone that protects the reinforcement.

### The Early Finish That Traps Bleed Water

On a slab pour running behind schedule, the finishers begin trowelling as soon as the concrete supports their weight, before bleed water has stopped rising. The trap is that the surface looks hard and dense immediately, while bleed water is sealed beneath a thin dense skin, producing blisters and delamination that appear days or weeks later as the slab is trafficked. The false signal is the rapid set and glossy finish; the harm is a slab surface that scales and delaminates, requiring costly remediation of large areas.

### The Curing That Stops At The End Of The Shift

Curing is specified for seven days, but the crew that finishes on Friday evening applies curing compound once and does not return, and the weekend's heat and wind desiccate the surface. The trap is that the concrete "looks cured" because it has a membrane, while the hydration of the surface zone has been arrested and the cover concrete that protects the rebar never reaches its intended strength or impermeability. The harm is a structure that meets the average cylinder strength but has a weak, permeable skin that will carbonate or admit chlorides years earlier than the durability design assumed.

### The Low Break Treated As A Paperwork Problem

A cylinder under-breaks, the testing agency reports it, and the field team files the report without escalating to the engineer of record because "it's only one cylinder and the rest are fine." The trap is that acceptance criteria are statistical precisely because individual loads can be weak, and a single low break may represent a full truckload of concrete now cast into a critical column or beam. The harm is that the affected element never receives the engineering evaluation that would determine whether it can carry load, and the weakness is buried under subsequent construction until it surfaces as a crack, a deflection, or, in the worst case, a failure.

## Self-Check

- Does the approved mix design in hand match the element's strength, exposure, air-content, and aggregate-size requirements, and is the placement sequence documented against set time and haul distance?
- For every truck, is there a batch ticket confirming mix identity, discharge within the time or revolution limit, and fresh-property tests (slump, air, temperature) within the approved range?
- Was any on-site water addition performed strictly per the ASTM C94 procedure, with a post-addition slump test and cylinder sample, and was any deviation reported to the engineer of record rather than absorbed?
- Did placement observation confirm controlled drop height, layer depth, vibrator insertion into the previous layer, and vibrator spacing and duration appropriate to the rebar congestion?
- Did finishing begin only after bleed water dissipated, and were evaporation control or thermal protection measures applied for the actual ambient conditions?
- Is the specified curing method applied immediately after finishing and maintained for the full required duration, with records of who applied it and when?
- For every low cylinder break, is there a documented engineering evaluation of affected volume, in-place strength verification, and load-capacity impact, rather than a silent acceptance?
- Are cold joints, honeycomb, segregation, or finishing defects documented with location and photographs and referred for nonconformance disposition before the element is loaded?
