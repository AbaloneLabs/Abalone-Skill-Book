# Pavement Rehabilitation and Overlay Design

Pavement rehabilitation is the engineering of a treatment that restores a deteriorated pavement to acceptable structural and functional condition, and it succeeds only when the design is matched to the actual distress mechanism rather than applied as a standard thickness over whatever exists. The temptation, and the dominant failure mode, is to treat rehabilitation as a resurfacing operation: mill a little, lay a standard overlay, restore ride and skid, and declare the road rebuilt. But an overlay placed over an unresolved structural defect, a pumping subgrade, a fatigue-cracked base, or a working concrete joint will reflect that defect through the new surface within one or two seasons, consuming the rehabilitation budget without extending the pavement's structural life. The harm this skill prevents is the repeated premature failure of overlays that look new on completion but fail quickly because the cause of the original distress was never diagnosed or addressed. The correct approach is forensic: characterise the existing pavement's condition, remaining life, and structural capacity, identify whether the distress is structural or functional, and select the treatment, whether preservation, overlay, or reconstruction, that addresses the actual mechanism.

## Core Rules

### Characterise the Existing Pavement Before Selecting Any Treatment

No overlay or rehabilitation design is valid without a defensible characterisation of the existing pavement's condition, because the treatment must address the actual distress and structural state. Conduct a condition survey to determine the Pavement Condition Index (PCI) and, critically, identify the type, severity, and extent of each distress, because the distress type reveals the mechanism: alligator (fatigue) cracking indicates structural failure of the base or subgrade, longitudinal wheel-path cracking may indicate fatigue or poor construction, transverse cracking indicates thermal or reflective mechanisms, and rutting indicates instability in the asphalt or densification of the base. Determine the remaining structural life by falling-weight deflectometer (FWD) testing and backcalculation of the existing layer moduli, supplemented by coring to confirm layer thicknesses and to see whether cracks originate at the surface or from the bottom up. A rehabilitation design that proceeds without this characterisation is a guess, and the overlay thickness will be chosen by habit rather than by the existing pavement's actual capacity.

### Distinguish Structural From Functional Distress and Match the Treatment Accordingly

The treatment must match whether the pavement needs structural enhancement, functional restoration, or both. Functional distress (rutting, raveling, bleeding, loss of skid resistance, roughness) may be addressed by milling and a thin overlay or surface treatment that restores ride and surface without adding structural capacity. Structural distress (fatigue cracking indicating base or subgrade failure, widespread alligator cracking, loss of support) requires a treatment that adds structural capacity, such as a thick overlay designed by the AASHTO 1993 overlay equation using the condition factor or the effective thickness method, or full-depth reclamation, or reconstruction. Applying a functional overlay over structural distress is the most common rehabilitation error: it restores the surface while the underlying fatigue continues, and the cracks reflect through within a season or two. Confirm that the selected treatment's structural contribution matches the deficiency found in the condition survey and FWD backcalculation.

### Control Reflective Cracking When Overlaying Over Cracked or Concrete Pavement

Reflective cracking, the propagation of existing cracks or joints through a new asphalt overlay, is the dominant failure mode of overlays placed over jointed concrete or cracked asphalt, and it must be addressed explicitly in the design rather than hoped away. When overlaying jointed concrete pavement, evaluate whether the underlying slabs are stable; if they move under traffic or temperature, control reflection by rubblizing the concrete (fracturing it into interlocking pieces that behave as a high-quality base), by crack-and-seat (cracking the slabs into segments and seating them to restore support), or by providing a thicker overlay with a stress-absorbing membrane interlayer. When overlaying cracked asphalt, the existing fatigue cracks will reflect unless the underlying structural problem is addressed or an interlayer, geogrid, or increased overlay thickness is provided. Document the expected reflective-cracking performance and the chosen control measure, because an overlay placed over working joints or active fatigue cracks with no reflection control will crack through in one to three years regardless of the overlay thickness.

### Use FWD Backcalculation and Remaining Life to Size the Overlay Defensively

For a structural overlay, size the thickness using the AASHTO 1993 overlay design method or an equivalent, which compares the structural number required for future traffic against the effective structural number of the existing pavement, with the difference provided by the overlay. The effective structural number of the existing pavement is determined from FWD backcalculation of layer moduli adjusted by a condition factor that reflects the distress, or by the effective thickness method summing the remaining contribution of each layer. Estimate the remaining life of the existing pavement from the backcalculated moduli and the past traffic, because a pavement with little remaining life contributes almost nothing structurally and the overlay must carry the full future loading. For whitetopping (concrete overlay over asphalt), confirm that the underlying asphalt provides a stable base and that the concrete thickness and jointing are designed for the bonded or unbonded condition assumed. A defensive overlay design uses the backcalculated capacity, not an assumed or optimistic value, and documents the basis for the effective structural number.

## Common Traps

### The Standard Overlay Over Uncharacterised Pavement

An existing pavement is rough and cracked, and the agency applies a standard overlay thickness to restore ride and surface, without a condition survey, FWD testing, or distress mechanism identification. The false signal is the smooth new surface that looks like a rebuilt road; the harm is that the underlying fatigue cracking or loss of support reflects through the overlay within one or two seasons, because the structural defect was never diagnosed or addressed, and the rehabilitation budget is consumed without extending the pavement's life.

### The Functional Treatment Over Structural Distress

Alligator cracking indicates base or subgrade fatigue, but the road receives a thin mill-and-overlay that addresses only the surface. The false signal is the restored surface and improved PCI; the harm is that the fatigue mechanism continues beneath, the cracks reflect and multiply, and the pavement returns to failure quickly because the treatment added no structural capacity to a structurally failed section.

### The Overlay Over Working Concrete Joints With No Reflection Control

An asphalt overlay is placed over a jointed concrete pavement that is still moving under traffic and temperature, with no rubblization, crack-and-seat, interlayer, or reflection control. The false signal is the continuous new surface; the harm is that every underlying joint reflects through the asphalt within one to three years, because the joints continue to move and concentrate stress, reproducing the concrete joint pattern in the overlay and destroying its ride and integrity.

### The Effective Structural Number Assumed Rather Than Backcalculated

The overlay thickness is sized using an assumed effective structural number for the existing pavement, without FWD backcalculation or remaining-life analysis. The false signal is a completed overlay calculation that looks defensible; the harm is that the assumed capacity may be far higher than the deteriorated pavement actually provides, so the overlay is underdesigned for the future traffic and fails prematurely because it was asked to supplement a capacity the existing pavement no longer has.

## Self-Check

- Has the existing pavement been characterised by a condition survey (PCI with distress type, severity, and extent), FWD backcalculation of layer moduli, and coring to confirm thicknesses and crack origin?
- Has the distress mechanism been identified, and is the selected treatment matched to whether the deficiency is structural (requiring added capacity) or functional (requiring surface restoration)?
- For overlays over jointed concrete or cracked asphalt, is reflective cracking controlled by rubblization, crack-and-seat, interlayers, geogrids, or increased thickness, with the expected performance documented?
- Is the structural overlay thickness sized from the difference between required and effective structural number, with the effective number based on backcalculated moduli and condition factors rather than assumed values?
- Has the remaining life of the existing pavement been estimated and used to determine how much future loading the existing layers can still carry versus what the overlay must provide?
- For whitetopping, is the underlying asphalt confirmed as a stable base, and are the concrete thickness and jointing consistent with the bonded or unbonded condition assumed in the design?
