# Rigid Pavement Design and Jointing

Rigid pavement design is the engineering of a concrete slab that distributes wheel loads by beam action over a wide area of subgrade, and the slab's performance is governed not only by its thickness but by the joints and reinforcement that control where and how the concrete cracks. Unlike flexible pavement, where the layers yield and distribute stress, a concrete slab is stiff and brittle: it will crack from temperature and moisture gradients, from traffic loads at the edges and corners, and from loss of subgrade support, and the entire design philosophy is to control that cracking through joint location, load transfer, and base support rather than to prevent it. The harm this skill prevents is a concrete pavement that develops mid-slab random cracking, joint faulting from pumping, or corner breaks within the first decade, defects that destroy ride quality and load transfer and that are extraordinarily expensive to repair because concrete cannot simply be overlaid like asphalt. The jointing plan, the dowel design, the base type, and the slab thickness are interdependent decisions that must be made together, because a slab that is thick but poorly jointed will crack unpredictably, and a slab that is well jointed but too thin will fault and crack at the joints.

## Core Rules

### Select the Pavement Type and Design the Slab Thickness for Flexure

Choose between jointed plain concrete pavement (JPCP), jointed reinforced concrete pavement (JRCP), and continuously reinforced concrete pavement (CRCP) based on traffic, climate, agency experience, and the tolerance for maintenance, because each type controls cracking differently. For AASHTO rigid design, compute the required slab thickness from the modulus of subgrade reaction (k-value), the concrete flexural strength (modulus of rupture, not compressive strength, because slab failure is in flexure), the load safety factor, the cumulative ESALs, the reliability, and the serviceability loss. The concrete must be designed by mix design for the required flexural strength at 28 days, with attention to aggregate durability, shrinkage, and freeze-thaw resistance, because a slab with high compressive strength but low flexural strength will still crack under traffic. Confirm that the assumed edge condition (tied shoulder, curb, or unsupported edge) matches the as-built condition, because an unsupported slab edge carries far higher stress than an interior load and governs the required thickness.

### Control Cracking Through Joint Spacing Based on Slab Dimensions and Climate

For JPCP, the transverse joint spacing must be short enough that the daily and seasonal temperature and moisture gradients (curling and warping) do not build tensile stress sufficient to crack the slab between joints. A common guideline limits the transverse spacing to roughly 24 times the slab thickness in inches (roughly 15 feet for a typical slab), with shorter spacing in climates with large daily temperature ranges, because long slabs curl more and crack mid-slab. Match the longitudinal joint spacing to the lane width and confirm that the slab aspect ratio (length to width) avoids slabs that are too long and narrow, which warp and crack along the long dimension. For CRCP, the continuous reinforcement holds shrinkage cracks tightly together so that aggregate interlock maintains load transfer, and the steel percentage (commonly around 0.6 to 0.8 percent of the slab cross-sectional area) is designed to keep crack spacing and crack width within limits; the steel must be placed at the correct depth with proper lap and cover, because misplaced steel produces wide cracks that lose interlock and spall.

### Provide Load Transfer at Joints Through Dowels and Aggregate Interlock

Load transfer across transverse joints is what prevents differential vertical movement (faulting) between adjacent slabs under heavy traffic, and it is the single most important detail in JPCP performance on high-volume roads. For arterial and interstate traffic, provide round, epoxy-coated dowel bars sized by slab thickness (commonly 1.25 to 1.5 inch diameter, 12 to 18 inches long, spaced at 12 inches) at every transverse joint, placed at mid-depth and lubricated to allow free movement, because dowels transfer shear between slabs without restricting horizontal thermal movement. Do not rely on aggregate interlock alone for load transfer where the traffic is heavy or the joint openings are wide, because interlock degrades as cracks widen and the slabs then fault under repeated loads. For longitudinal joints between lanes, use tie bars (deformed bars, not smooth dowels) to hold the lanes together while allowing the joint to act as a hinge, sized by the slab thickness and the distance to the nearest free edge.

### Design the Base, Subbase, Drainage, and Joint Sealants as a System

The layer immediately beneath the slab (the base or subbase) provides uniform support and drainage, and its quality directly controls faulting and pumping, the dominant distress when water and fines are present at the interface. Use a stabilised (cement- or asphalt-treated) or dense-graded crushed base for high-traffic pavements, with a positive drainage system (edgedrains, daylighted base) to remove water that enters through joints and cracks, because water plus fines plus traffic produces the pumping that erodes support and faults the joints. Seal all joints against incompressibles and water, because incompressible debris that enters a joint in summer is compressed when the slab contracts in winter and spalls the joint faces, and water that enters degrades the base and subgrade. Address frost-susceptible subgrades by providing non-frost-susceptible material to the required depth, because differential frost heave under a rigid slab produces uneven support and cracking that no slab thickness can prevent.

## Common Traps

### The Joint Spacing Stretched to Reduce Maintenance

JPCP transverse joints are spaced longer than the guideline, often to reduce the number of joints to seal and maintain, without recalculating curling and warping stresses. The false signal is the reduced joint count and the cleaner-looking slab; the harm is that the long spacing allows daily temperature curling to build tensile stress that initiates mid-slab random cracking, converting the designed JPCP into an unjointed and unpredictably cracked slab that destroys load transfer and ride and costs far more to maintain than the joints it replaced.

### The Dowels Omitted or Undersized for Lighter Traffic

A JPCP is designed for a road expected to carry moderate trucks, and dowels are omitted to save cost, relying on aggregate interlock for load transfer. The false signal is the cost saving and the compliant slab thickness; the harm is that the joint openings widen over time, aggregate interlock degrades, and the slabs fault differentially under repeated truck loads, producing a stepped ride that requires grinding or full slab replacement within a decade.

### The Compressive Strength Substituted for Flexural Strength

The mix design is specified by compressive strength because it is cheaper to test, and a high compressive value is assumed to imply adequate slab performance. The false signal is the strong-looking concrete; the harm is that flexural strength, which actually governs slab cracking, may be inadequate even when compressive strength is high, and the slab cracks under traffic and curling stresses that the compressive value did not represent.

### The Unsupported Edge Designed as if Supported

The thickness calculation assumes a tied shoulder or curb that provides edge support, but the as-built condition leaves an unsupported or only loosely connected edge. The false signal is the compliant thickness on paper; the harm is that the unsupported edge carries far higher stress under wheel loads near the lane edge, producing edge and corner cracking that the design never accounted for, because the assumed and actual edge conditions did not match.

## Self-Check

- Has the pavement type (JPCP, JRCP, or CRCP) been selected deliberately for traffic, climate, and agency maintenance capability, and is the slab thickness computed from k-value, flexural strength, ESALs, reliability, and serviceability loss?
- Is the concrete specified by flexural strength (modulus of rupture) from a mix design, not by compressive strength alone, with aggregate durability and freeze-thaw resistance addressed?
- For JPCP, is the transverse joint spacing within roughly 24 times the slab thickness (adjusted for climate), with a slab aspect ratio that avoids excessive curling and mid-slab cracking?
- Are dowel bars sized, placed at mid-depth, lubricated, and provided at every transverse joint for heavy traffic, with tie bars at longitudinal joints, rather than relying on aggregate interlock alone?
- Does the base or subbase provide uniform support and drainage (stabilised or dense-graded with edgedrains or daylighted base), with joints sealed against incompressibles and water?
- Does the assumed edge condition (tied shoulder, curb, or unsupported) match the as-built detail, and are frost-susceptible subgrades addressed with non-frost-susceptible material to the required depth?
