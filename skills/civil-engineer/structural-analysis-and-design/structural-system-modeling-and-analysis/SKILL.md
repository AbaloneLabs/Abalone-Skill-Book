# Structural System Modeling And Analysis

Structural analysis is the act of reducing a real, continuous, three-dimensional building into a discrete mathematical model whose internal forces can be computed, and every reduction is a place where a conservative assumption can become an unconservative one. Agents often treat modeling as a software-input task and miss that the model's validity is governed by the idealizations chosen before any node is drawn: how joints are released, how stiffness is aggregated, how floors act as diaphragms, how foundations are restrained, and how second-order effects are captured. A model that runs and produces smooth, plausible-looking diagrams can still be wrong in ways that under-predict demand by 20 percent or more, and in a high-stakes domain that margin is the difference between a member that is adequate and one that fails. This skill covers the judgment an engineer exercises while building, running, and sanity-checking a structural analysis model, with the goal that the demand used to size members is a defensible upper bound on what the real structure will experience.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Analysis Purpose Before Modeling

Before drawing a single member, define what the analysis must answer, because the purpose determines the required fidelity. A preliminary sizing pass may accept a two-dimensional plane frame with gross-section stiffness; a final design of a seismic lateral system requires a three-dimensional model with cracked-section properties, diaphragm behavior, and explicit consideration of P-delta. State whether the model is for gravity only, wind, seismic, vibration, progressive collapse, or construction-stage analysis, because each imposes different requirements on geometry, mass, stiffness, and load input. An analysis whose purpose is not stated tends to inherit whatever defaults the software ships with, and those defaults are calibrated to no specific project.

### Idealize Geometry Without Losing Force Paths

Translate the physical structure into analysis members and nodes, but preserve every load path the designer intends. Columns and beams become line elements located at their centroid or at a consistent reference plane; walls become shell elements or equivalent frame elements; slabs become plates, membranes, or diaphragms. Decide explicitly how wide a wall or slab strip each element represents, because an over-wide strip steals stiffness and demand from a neighbor and an under-wide strip leaves a real load path unmodeled. Where members frame in at different elevations or with offsets, model the offsets explicitly rather than forcing them onto a single grid line, because rigid-zone and offset behavior changes the moments at the joint. The discipline is to ask, for every element drawn, what physical concrete or steel it represents, and to confirm the answer covers the intended force path.

### Assign Boundary Conditions And Releases Deliberately

Boundary conditions and member end releases are where models most often diverge from reality, and they must be assigned by intent, not by default. Decide whether each foundation support is a true pin, a fixed base, a rotational spring representing soil flexibility, or a pile group with six-degree-of-freedom stiffness; the choice changes moments and base shears dramatically. Decide whether beam-to-column connections are rigid, pinned, or semi-rigid, and reflect that in the model, because a connection assumed pinned that is actually stiff draws moment it was not designed for, and a connection assumed rigid that is actually flexible redistributes moment to members that were not checked. Release the minor-axis bending, torsion, and shear where the connection cannot transmit them, and do not release where it can. Document each assumption, because the connection design must later match the analysis assumption or the model is fiction.

### Model Floor Diaphragms According To Their Real Behavior

Floor slabs usually act as rigid or semi-rigid diaphragms tying the vertical lateral elements together, and the diaphragm assumption governs how lateral force is distributed to shear walls and frames. Use a rigid diaphragm where the slab is deep and stiff relative to the lateral system and the plan is compact; use a semi-rigid or flexible diaphragm where the slab is long and slender, has large openings, or is of wood or metal deck without a concrete topping. A rigid-diaphragm assumption applied to a flexible floor forces all lateral elements to move together and misallocates force to the stiff elements at the expense of the flexible ones. Where the diaphragm is modeled with shell elements, mesh it finely enough to capture in-plane shear, and confirm that chord forces and collectors are extracted from the model and designed, because diaphragm design is part of the lateral system, not an afterthought.

### Account For Cracked-Section And Effective Stiffness

Concrete cracks under service loads, and its effective stiffness is far below the gross-section value, which changes both the force distribution and the drift. Use code-specified effective stiffness factors for seismic design, where the lateral system's period, drift, and force distribution depend on cracked properties, and apply them consistently to beams, columns, and walls. Do not mix gross-section stiffness for some elements and cracked stiffness for others without a reason, because stiffness governs force attraction and an inconsistent model sends force to whichever element was modeled stiffer. For steel, use gross properties but account for panel-zone deformation and composite action where the slab engages the beam. The stiffness you assign is the stiffness the structure will approximately have at the load level analyzed, not the stiffness of uncracked concrete at first loading.

### Capture Second-Order Effects Explicitly Or By Qualified Method

Gravity loads acting on a laterally displaced frame produce additional moments, the P-delta effect, and a model that ignores it under-predicts demand in slender frames and tall buildings. Use a second-order (geometric nonlinear) analysis where the frame is slender, the drift is large, or the code requires it, or apply a qualified amplification method with the moment magnifier or the B2 factor, but do not simply run first-order and assume second-order is negligible. Confirm that the P-delta included is both the member-level (P-little-delta) and the story-level (P-big-delta) effect, because omitting the member-level effect under-predicts moments in slender columns and long beams. Where a direct second-order analysis is used, ensure the notional loads, stiffness reduction, and geometric imperfections required by the code are applied, because a bare second-order run without the code's required inputs is not a compliant analysis.

### Run Sanity Checks Against Independent Hand Methods

No analysis output should be accepted into design without an independent sanity check, because software bugs, modeling errors, and unit mistakes produce smooth but wrong results. Compute the total base shear from the applied loads and compare it to the model's base reactions; they must agree to within a tolerance. Compute the period of the fundamental mode by a hand approximation and compare it to the model's modal period; a model period that is wildly off signals a mass or stiffness error. Sketch the expected bending moment diagram for a representative beam and compare it to the model output; if the model shows a shape that contradicts statics, the model is wrong. The engineer's obligation is to prove the model approximately right by an independent path before trusting any single number it produces.

### Extract And Document Demand With Load Case Provenance

For every member sized from the analysis, document which load combination governed and what the demand ratio was, because a member designed against an unnamed "envelope" cannot be rechecked if the loads change. Use load combinations from the governing code, not arbitrary factors, and confirm that patterned live load cases are included so that the worst-case loading of each span is captured. Where strength and serviceability are checked against different combinations, track them separately, because a beam adequate for strength may fail deflection and a column adequate for gravity may fail under seismic axial demand. The deliverable is not the model file; it is a documented set of demands, each tied to a load combination and a code reference, that another engineer can reproduce.

## Common Traps

### The Default Boundary Condition That Is Not Reality

The engineer accepts the software's default foundation fixity or connection rigidity because the model runs and the results look smooth, but the default reflects no decision about the real structure. The mechanism is that defaults are invisible inputs: they are set, not chosen, and the engineer's eye passes over them because they carry no warning flag. The false signal is that the model produced numbers, and numbers feel like evidence. The harm is that a foundation assumed fully fixed when the soil is soft under-predicts column moments by a large factor, or a connection assumed pinned when it is stiff draws moment into a connection designed for zero moment, and either error travels silently into member and connection design. Every boundary condition and release must be a recorded decision, justified against the real detail, because an unexamined default is an unexamined assumption about how the building will behave.

### The Rigid Diaphragm Imposed On A Flexible Floor

The engineer applies a rigid-diaphragm constraint to every floor because it simplifies the model and accelerates the run, including to a long narrow slab with a central atrium or to a wood-framed floor. The mechanism is that rigidity is a modeling convenience that forces all nodes on the floor to translate and rotate as one rigid plate, which suppresses the real in-plane flexibility. The false signal is that the lateral elements share force in proportion to their stiffness under rigid translation, which looks orderly. The harm is that the flexible diaphragm in reality cannot drag the distant frames along, so those frames see less force and the central walls see more than the model predicts, and the under-designed central wall or the over-designed distant frame both represent a latent failure. The diaphragm assumption must match the real floor's in-plane stiffness, and where the floor is genuinely flexible, the model must let it flex.

### Gross-Section Stiffness Used For Seismic Analysis

The engineer models concrete with gross-section stiffness because it is the default and because it makes the building stiffer, the periods shorter, and the base shear in many codes smaller. The mechanism is that stiffness drives period, period drives the code's response coefficient, and a stiffer model can yield a lower design base shear, which feels like efficiency. The false signal is that the model is "conservative" because it is stiffer. The harm is that the real cracked building is more flexible, drifts more, attracts force differently, and the seismic design based on gross stiffness under-predicts drift and may misallocate force among walls and frames; in a strong earthquake the building behaves unlike the model. Seismic models must use code-specified cracked-section effective stiffness, applied consistently, because the design must reflect the structure's real degraded state at the demand level.

### Omitting P-Delta In A Slender Frame

The engineer runs a first-order analysis and accepts the moments because the building is "not that tall," without quantifying the second-order amplification. The mechanism is that P-delta grows with slenderness and axial load, and a frame that looks modest can have a meaningful amplification when the gravity axial load is large relative to the lateral stiffness. The false signal is that the first-order moments look reasonable in isolation. The harm is that the real building, displaced by wind or seismic load, develops additional moments the model never reported, and the columns and beams are sized for forces below what they will experience; in the limit, a frame with high P-delta can become unstable under a load the first-order model said was acceptable. Second-order effects must be either computed directly or qualified by a checked amplification factor, and the decision to neglect them must be supported by a calculated stability coefficient, not by intuition.

### Trusting Smooth Output As Evidence Of Correctness

The engineer sees a clean, continuous bending moment diagram and a deflected shape that looks plausible, and concludes the model is right. The mechanism is that software produces aesthetically convincing graphics, and the visual plausibility is mistaken for physical correctness, when in fact a model with a wrong stiffness, a wrong unit, or a missed load case still deflects and still draws smooth diagrams. The false signal is the smoothness itself. The harm is that no independent check is performed, and the error propagates into design until it surfaces during construction or review, when the cost of correction is high. Smooth output is a necessary but not sufficient condition; the engineer must verify the model against an independent hand computation of at least base shear, period, and a representative moment diagram, because the absence of an obvious glitch is not evidence of correctness.

### Inconsistent Load Patterns That Miss The Worst Case

The engineer applies live load uniformly to all spans because it is simpler than patterning, and the model reports moments that are lower than the true worst case for any single span. The mechanism is that patterned live load, loading alternate spans to maximize moment, is what produces the design demand, and a uniformly loaded model under-predicts the moment at the face of supports and over the spans. The false signal is that the model is fully loaded and therefore "conservative." The harm is that beams and columns are sized for forces below the patterned worst case, and the deficiency is invisible until a loading pattern close to the design pattern occurs. Live load must be patterned, skip-loaded, and enveloped so that every span's governing demand is captured, because uniform loading is an analysis simplification, not a design-safe assumption.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the analysis purpose been stated, and do the model fidelity, stiffness, and load inputs match that purpose?
- [ ] Does every modeled element correspond to a defined physical member, and are offsets, rigid zones, and member widths documented rather than defaulted?
- [ ] Are all boundary conditions and member end releases recorded decisions justified against the real connection and foundation detail, with no unexamined defaults?
- [ ] Does the diaphragm assumption match the real floor's in-plane stiffness, and where semi-rigid or flexible, is the model allowed to deform accordingly?
- [ ] Are cracked-section or effective stiffness properties applied consistently for seismic analysis, per the governing code?
- [ ] Are second-order effects captured by direct analysis or qualified by a calculated amplification, with both P-big-delta and P-little-delta considered?
- [ ] Has the model been sanity-checked against independent hand computations of base shear, fundamental period, and at least one representative moment diagram?
- [ ] Is patterned live load included so that the governing demand of each span is captured, and is every design demand tied to a documented load combination and code reference?
