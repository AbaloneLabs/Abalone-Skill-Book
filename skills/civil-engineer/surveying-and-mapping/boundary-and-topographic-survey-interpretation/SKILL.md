# Boundary and Topographic Survey Interpretation

A survey plat or topographic map is the legal and geometric foundation of a civil project, and the judgment problem is that these documents look like simple drawings but encode a hierarchy of evidence (monuments over measurements, senior rights over junior, original over retracement) that an untrained reader cannot decode from the lines alone. Agents tend to read a plat the way they read a floor plan — as an authoritative picture of where things are — and miss that a bearing and distance on a plat is a description subordinate to the physical monuments in the ground, that an easement drawn lightly can block an entire utility corridor, and that a topo contour interval too coarse for the design will hide a drainage problem until construction. The harm this skill prevents is building in the wrong place, encroaching on a neighbor or an easement, designing to a topography that does not match the field, and the litigation and rework that follow. This skill covers reading plats and maps, monuments and property corners, easements and encumbrances, topographic contours and spot elevations, legal descriptions, resolving discrepancies, and when to engage a licensed land surveyor. It is a strictly licensed domain: boundary determination, monument setting, and certifiable surveying are reserved to a licensed land surveyor, and the agent must defer to and engage one for any boundary opinion, corner reset, or recordable survey.

## Core Rules

### Read a Plat or Map as a Structured Document, Not a Picture

A survey plat has standard elements that must be read in order: the title block (surveyor, client, date, purpose, certification), the north arrow (true, magnetic, or grid, with declination or basis of bearing noted), the scale (graphical and representative fraction, with a bar scale to detect reproduction distortion), the legend (symbols for monuments, fences, utilities, contours), the body (bearings and distances along lines, curve data, areas), and the notes and certifications (basis of bearing, datum, references to recorded plats and deeds). The disciplined habit is to read the title block and notes first, because they define the survey's purpose, datum, and limitations, before interpreting the geometry. The trap is reading the lines and ignoring the notes, which is where the surveyor states the basis of bearing, the datum, and the critical caveats (e.g., "corners not found," "area subject to tidal influence"). Always confirm the datum (e.g., NAVD88 vs. NGVD29 for vertical, and the coordinate system for horizontal) because mixing datums shifts everything.

### Understand Monuments and Property Corners and Their Hierarchy of Evidence

In boundary surveying, the hierarchy of evidence (codified in the rules of construction, tracing to common law) governs what controls when sources conflict: (1) natural monuments (rivers, trees), (2) artificial monuments set by the original surveyor, (3) calls in the deed for adjoiners or monuments, (4) distance and direction (bearings and distances), and (5) area, with senior (older) rights prevailing over junior in conflicts between deeds. A physical monument found in the ground controls over a bearing-and-distance description, and the original surveyor's monuments set during the original subdivision control over a later retracement surveyor's measurements. The disciplined rule is to never move or destroy a monument, to record its found condition, and to recognize that a corner "not found" is a problem for a licensed surveyor to resolve, not a gap to fill by measurement. The trap is assuming the deed distances control; they do not, when a controlling monument is found in a different location.

### Identify Easements, Encumbrances, and Rights That Bind the Land

A plat or title commitment typically shows easements (drainage, utility, access, conservation), reservations, and restrictions that limit what the owner may do with the land. An easement grants another party the right to use a strip of the property (a utility company to maintain a line, a neighbor to access a landlocked parcel, the public for drainage), and the owner may not build structures that interfere with it. The disciplined approach is to map every easement and encumbrance against the proposed development before design, because an easement that looks incidental can foreclose a building pad, a driveway, or a utility connection. The trap is treating a thin dashed easement line as insignificant; it is a property right, and building within it can require removal at the owner's cost. Read the title commitment and the recorded easement instruments, not just the plat, for the actual terms.

### Read Topographic Contours and Spot Elevations for the Design's Needs

Topographic contours are lines of equal elevation, and the contour interval (the vertical distance between adjacent contours) determines the resolution: a 1-foot interval shows detail suitable for grading and drainage design; a 5- or 10-foot interval is adequate for regional planning but hides features critical to site design. Closely spaced contours indicate steep slopes; widely spaced contours indicate gentle slopes; concentric closed contours indicate hills or depressions (with the depression often marked with hatchurs or a depression contour). Spot elevations are individual point elevations (rim of a catch basin, high point of a road, top of curb) that supplement contours where detail is needed. The disciplined rule is to confirm the contour interval is adequate for the design before relying on the topo, and to field-verify critical elevations. The trap is designing drainage or grading to a coarse-interval topo that hides a swale or a high point, producing a ponding or a cut that appears only at construction.

### Draft and Interpret Legal Descriptions by Their System

Legal descriptions take three main forms. Metes and bounds describes a parcel by starting at a point of beginning (POB) and tracing the boundary by courses (bearing and distance) around the perimeter back to the POB, referencing monuments. Lot and block describes a parcel by reference to a recorded subdivision plat (e.g., "Lot 7, Block 3, Sunset Hills, recorded in Plat Book 12, Page 45"). The Public Land Survey System (PLSS) describes land by aliquot parts of a section (e.g., "the NE 1/4 of the SW 1/4 of Section 14, T2N, R3E"), with sections, townships, and ranges as the framework, and with special lots and government lots for irregular sections. The disciplined habit is to read the description completely (including the POB, the basis of bearing, and the recording references), to check that the courses close mathematically, and to reconcile the description with the plat and the field monuments. The trap is a description that does not close, or that conflicts with the plat or the monuments, which signals a defect requiring a surveyor.

### Resolve Discrepancies Between Record and Field Evidence Through the Surveyor

Discrepancies arise constantly: a deed distance does not match the plat, a called-for monument is not found, an adjoiner's fence is off the deed line, or two surveys disagree on a corner. The rules of construction give the surveyor a method to weigh evidence (monuments over measurements, senior over junior, original over retracement), but the resolution is a professional judgment that only a licensed surveyor may make, documented in a surveyor's report and, where required, a record of survey. The disciplined rule is to treat a discrepancy as a flag, to gather the record evidence (deeds, plats, prior surveys, title), and to engage the surveyor for the resolution rather than choosing a position by guess. The trap is "splitting the difference" between two conflicting positions, which has no legal basis and creates a new defect.

### Engage a Licensed Land Surveyor for Boundary Determination and Certification

Boundary determination, monument setting or reset, subdivision platting, legal description preparation for recordation, and certifiable surveying are the licensed practice of land surveying. An engineer, contractor, or owner may read and interpret surveys for their own work, but may not opine on a boundary, set or reset a corner, certify a survey, or record a plat without a license. The disciplined rule is to engage a licensed land surveyor for any boundary question, any corner reset, any recordable description or plat, and any conflict between record and field evidence, and to rely on the surveyor's certification for the boundary. The trap is treating a boundary question as an engineering or construction problem and resolving it without a surveyor, which produces an unlicensed practice, an uncertifiable result, and exposure to liability.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Plat Lines as Authoritative Over the Ground Monuments

A reader assumes the bearings and distances on the plat define the property line and measures from the plat, ignoring that a found original monument in a different location controls over the plat measurements. The trap is that the plat is a description subordinate to the monuments it calls for. The mechanism is the hierarchy of evidence placing monuments above measurements. The false signal is "the plat shows the line here." The harm is building to a plat position that is not the legal boundary, encroaching on or giving up land. The defense is to locate and respect the controlling monuments and to engage a surveyor when they conflict with the plat.

### Ignoring the Notes, Datum, and Basis of Bearing

A reader works from the plat geometry without reading the notes, and mixes datums (e.g., uses NGVD29 elevations from the plat in a NAVD88 design) or uses a bearing basis different from the design's, shifting the work. The trap is that the notes carry the critical context — datum, basis of bearing, caveats — that the geometry assumes. The mechanism is that the same numbers mean different things under different datums or bearing bases. The false signal is "the numbers are on the plat." The harm is systematic offset or elevation error across the project. The defense is to read the title block and notes first, confirm the datum and basis of bearing, and convert where they differ from the design's.

### Treating a Light Easement Line as Insignificant

A reader sees a thin dashed easement line crossing a proposed building pad and dismisses it as minor, when it is a recorded utility or drainage easement that prohibits the structure. The trap is that the line's visual weight does not reflect its legal weight. The mechanism is that an easement is a property right enforceable by removal. The false signal is "it is just a line." The harm is a building or utility placed in an easement that must be removed or relocated at cost. The defense is to map every easement and encumbrance against the design and to read the recorded easement instrument for its actual terms.

### Designing to a Topo Whose Contour Interval Is Too Coarse

A designer grades a site and designs drainage from a 5-foot contour interval topo, which hides a shallow swale and a high point, and the design ponds water or requires unexpected cut at construction. The trap is that the contour interval sets the resolution, and a coarse interval conceals the features that matter. The mechanism is that interpolated detail between contours is not real measurement. The false signal is "the topo shows the ground." The harm is a grading or drainage failure revealed only at construction. The defense is to confirm the interval is adequate for the design (commonly 1-foot for site design) and to field-verify critical elevations.

### Using a Legal Description That Does Not Close or Conflicts With the Plat

A deed or description's courses do not close mathematically, or the description conflicts with the recorded plat, and the reader uses it anyway, creating an ambiguous or unmarketable boundary. The trap is that a non-closing or conflicting description is a defect, not a rounding error. The mechanism is that the misclosure or conflict signals a transcription, survey, or conveyance error. The false signal is "it is close enough." The harm is a boundary dispute, an unmarketable title, or construction in the wrong location. The defense is to check that descriptions close, reconcile them with the plat and monuments, and engage a surveyor to correct defects.

### Resolving a Boundary Conflict Without a Licensed Surveyor

An owner or contractor finds that a fence, a neighbor's structure, or a prior survey conflicts with the deed line and "settles" the boundary by measurement or by agreement, without a licensed surveyor's determination. The trap is that boundary determination is the licensed practice of surveying, and an informal resolution is not legally defensible and may itself create a defect. The mechanism is that only a surveyor's weighed-evidence determination and certification establish the boundary. The false signal is "we agreed on the line." The harm is an unenforceable boundary, an unrecordable result, and liability for unlicensed practice. The defense is to engage a licensed land surveyor for any boundary determination, corner reset, or recordable description.

## Self-Check

- Did I read the title block, north arrow (and basis of bearing), scale, legend, and notes before interpreting the plat geometry, and did I confirm the horizontal coordinate system and vertical datum (e.g., NAVD88) match the design's?
- For any boundary question, did I locate and respect the controlling monuments (natural and artificial, original over retracement), apply the hierarchy of evidence (monuments over measurements, senior over junior), and avoid moving or destroying any monument?
- Have I mapped every easement, reservation, and encumbrance from the plat and the title commitment against the proposed development, and read the recorded easement instruments for their actual terms, before finalizing the design?
- Is the topographic contour interval adequate for the design's purpose (commonly 1-foot for site grading and drainage), and have I field-verified critical spot elevations rather than relying on interpolated contours?
- For any legal description, did I read it completely (POB, courses, basis of bearing, recording references), confirm it closes mathematically, and reconcile it with the plat and the field monuments?
- Where record and field evidence conflict (a deed distance vs. a found monument, an adjoiner's fence, two surveys), did I treat it as a flag, gather the record evidence, and engage a licensed land surveyor for a weighed-evidence resolution rather than guessing or splitting the difference?
- Have I engaged a licensed land surveyor for every boundary determination, corner reset, recordable legal description, subdivision plat, and certifiable survey, and refrained from opining on boundaries or certifying surveys without a license?
