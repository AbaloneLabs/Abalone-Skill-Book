# GPS and Total Station Surveying Techniques

Modern surveying runs on two instrument families — Global Positioning System / Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) receivers and total stations — and the judgment problem is that each has a different accuracy profile, a different failure mode, and a different appropriate use, and a crew that picks the wrong tool, or that runs the right tool without the checks that expose error, will produce coordinates that look precise on the data collector but are wrong by inches or feet. Agents tend to treat instrument output as truth because the readout shows many decimal places, and miss that GPS degrades silently under tree canopy or poor satellite geometry, that a total station accumulates error through an unchecked traverse, and that "closed" and "checked" are not the same as "accurate." The harm this skill prevents is layout and mapping that is confidently wrong — a storm line built to RTK coordinates shifted by multipath, a control network that does not close, a boundary retraced with insufficient checks — and the cost and liability that follow. This skill covers RTK-GPS, total station operation, control networks, traverse and adjustment, accuracy standards and error sources, checks and closures, and the decision of when to use which instrument. It is a licensed domain: control establishment and certifiable surveying are the practice of land surveying, and the agent must defer to and engage a licensed land surveyor for certifiable work and follow manufacturer and standards guidance for instrument operation.

## Core Rules

### Understand RTK-GPS Operation and Its Accuracy Limits

Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) GPS uses a base receiver at a known point and a rover receiver at the point to be measured, with the base computing correction errors and transmitting them to the rover in real time (by radio or cellular link), yielding centimeter-level positions (commonly 1-2 cm horizontal, 2-3 cm vertical) under good conditions. RTK depends on a sufficient number of satellites (commonly at least 5, ideally 6 or more), good satellite geometry (a low Position Dilution of Precision, PDOP), an open sky view, a reliable correction link, and a base coordinate that is correct. The disciplined habit is to check the satellite count and PDOP before observing, to confirm the rover is "fixed" (ambiguity resolved) rather than "float," and to observe and check known points at the start and end of the session (an "occupy and check") to confirm the system is within tolerance. The trap is observing in "float" or under poor geometry and accepting the readout; a float solution can be off by decimeters or more while displaying a confident-looking coordinate.

### Diagnose GPS Error Sources: Multipath, Canopy, and Sky Visibility

GPS accuracy degrades from several sources: multipath (the satellite signal reflecting off buildings, pavement, water, or vehicles before reaching the antenna, producing a wrong range), canopy and obstruction (trees, buildings, and cut slopes blocking satellites and reducing the constellation), ionospheric and tropospheric delay (especially during solar storms or over long base-rover distances), and poor satellite geometry (high PDOP). Multipath and canopy are the most common field problems, and they are insidious because the receiver may still show "fixed" while the position is shifted. The disciplined approach is to avoid observing near reflective surfaces and under canopy, to use a ground plane or choke-ring antenna in reflective environments, to observe when the constellation is strong, and to check redundant points. The trap is trusting a fixed RTK position taken against a building wall or under heavy tree cover; the position can be off by several centimeters to decimeters with no warning on the readout. Where canopy or obstruction is unavoidable, switch to a total station.

### Operate the Total Station for Angle and Distance Measurement

A total station measures horizontal and vertical angles (by electronic encoding to 1 arc-second or finer) and slope distance (by infrared or laser to a prism or to a reflective surface in reflectorless mode), and computes coordinates from an occupied point, a backsight, and the measured angles and distances. Setup discipline is critical: the instrument must be precisely centered over the point (within 1 mm), leveled, and the height of instrument (HI) and height of target (HR) measured and entered correctly, because a height error propagates directly into the elevation. The backsight must be observed and checked (the measured direction should match the known direction within tolerance) before any work proceeds. The disciplined rule is to always backsight, to check the backsight and a second known point before observing, and to re-check at the end of the setup. The trap is a setup error (off-center, mis-leveled, wrong HI/HR) that shifts every observation from that setup, or skipping the backsight check because "it looked fine."

### Establish Horizontal and Vertical Control Networks

Control is the framework that all layout and mapping depend on: primary horizontal control (points of known coordinate, established by GPS static or traverse) and vertical control (benchmarks of known elevation, established by differential leveling or GPS). Control must be established to an accuracy standard appropriate to the work (e.g., Third-Order, Class I for typical construction layout; Second-Order for major infrastructure), referenced to the project datum and coordinate system, and documented with adjustment results and closures. The disciplined approach is to establish redundant control (at least two references for every point, so a disturbed point can be detected and recovered), to close the control network, and to adjust it by least squares or a recognized method. The trap is a single, unclosed control point that every subsequent layout depends on; if it is wrong or disturbed, the entire project inherits the error with no way to detect it.

### Run Open and Closed Traverses and Adjust to Closure

A traverse is a series of connected survey points where angles and distances are measured sequentially. An open traverse has no check (it does not close back to a known point), and its error is undetectable, so it is acceptable only for low-accuracy work. A closed traverse (loop traverse closing back to the start, or a traverse between two known points) allows the angular and linear misclosure to be computed and distributed by an adjustment (compass/Bowditch, transit, or least squares). The disciplined rule is to always close the traverse, to compare the misclosure to the accuracy standard (e.g., a ratio of 1:10,000 or better for construction layout), and to adjust only if the closure is within tolerance — a large misclosure signals a blunder that must be found and fixed, not adjusted away. The trap is adjusting a traverse that fails the closure standard, which distributes a blunder across all points and hides the error rather than correcting it.

### Classify and Control Error Sources: Instrument, Natural, Personal

Survey errors fall into three categories. Instrumental errors include collimation (line of sight not truly horizontal), vertical-index error, and distance calibration; these are removed by calibration, by face-averaging (measuring in face left and face right), and by regular servicing. Natural errors include atmospheric refraction and earth curvature (corrected by the instrument or software), temperature and pressure effects on the infrared distance measurement (entered into the instrument), and GPS atmospheric delay. Personal errors include mis-centering, mis-leveling, misreading the HI/HR, and observing the wrong target; these are controlled by procedure and by redundant checks. The disciplined approach is to calibrate instruments on a known baseline, to enter temperature and pressure, to face-average angles for higher-order work, and to build redundant checks into every observation. The trap is treating any one source as negligible; the combined effect of small uncorrected errors can exceed the tolerance.

### Apply Checks and Closures and Match the Method to the Accuracy Required

Every survey must include independent checks: a GPS observation checked against a known point, a total station setup checked by backsight and a second known point, a traverse that closes, a level loop that returns to its start. The check must be independent of the method being checked (e.g., checking GPS control with a total station traverse, not with more GPS). The accuracy standard (First-, Second-, Third-Order, or a project-specific ratio) must be chosen for the work and verified by the closure; if the closure fails, the error is found and re-observed, not hidden. The disciplined rule is to design the survey to the required accuracy, to build in independent checks, and to document the closures. The trap is the single, unchecked observation that looks precise but has no verification, or adjusting a failed closure to make it "pass."

### Choose GPS or Total Station by the Site and the Accuracy Required

The choice of instrument depends on site conditions and accuracy. RTK-GPS excels in open sky, over large sites, for control and topographic work where 1-2 cm is acceptable, and where speed matters; it fails under canopy, near buildings, and where multipath or poor geometry dominates. A total station excels where GPS cannot see the sky (underground, indoors, under canopy, in urban canyons), where higher accuracy or redundancy is needed, and for fine layout (building corners, column lines); it is limited by line of sight and by the time to traverse. The disciplined approach is to use GPS for open-site control and topo, total station for canopy and fine layout, and often both (GPS for primary control, total station for secondary control and layout) on the same project. The trap is forcing one tool onto a job it cannot do — RTK under heavy canopy, or a long open traverse where GPS would be faster and better.

## Common Traps

### Accepting a "Float" or Poor-Geometry RTK Solution

The crew observes under marginal satellite geometry or with an unresolved ambiguity, the data collector shows a coordinate, and they accept it, when a float solution can be off by decimeters. The trap is that the readout looks authoritative regardless of solution quality. The mechanism is that "fixed" vs. "float" and the PDOP are the quality indicators, and ignoring them removes the safeguard. The false signal is "I got a position." The harm is layout or mapping shifted by centimeters to decimeters. The defense is to confirm a fixed solution, check the satellite count and PDOP, and occupy and check a known point before and after observing.

### Trusting RTK Under Canopy or Against a Reflective Wall

The crew takes RTK positions under heavy tree canopy or against a glass-and-steel building, where multipath and blocked satellites shift the position, and the readout still shows "fixed." The trap is that multipath and canopy degrade accuracy without an obvious warning. The mechanism is that reflected or blocked signals corrupt the ranges used to compute position. The false signal is "the solution is fixed." The harm is a position off by several centimeters to decimeters. The defense is to avoid canopy and reflective surfaces, use a ground-plane antenna where needed, and switch to a total station where GPS cannot see clean sky.

### Total Station Setup Error From Mis-Centering or Wrong HI/HR

The instrument is set up off-center over the point, mis-leveled, or the height of instrument or target is measured or entered wrong, and every observation from that setup inherits the error. The trap is that setup errors propagate invisibly into all data from the setup. The mechanism is that the centering, level, and height are the foundation of every angle and distance. The false signal is "the instrument is on and reading." The harm is systematically shifted coordinates and elevations. The defense is to center precisely (within 1 mm), level carefully, measure and enter HI and HR correctly, and backsight and check a known point before observing.

### Skipping the Backsight Check or Re-Check

The crew sets up, sights the backsight, and starts observing without checking that the measured direction matches the known direction, or without re-checking at the end of the setup. The trap is that the backsight check is the verification that the setup is oriented correctly, and skipping it removes the detection of orientation error. The mechanism is that an unchecked orientation propagates into every subsequent point. The false signal is "I sighted the backsight." The harm is points rotated or shifted by the orientation error. The defense is to always backsight, check the measured direction against the known, observe and check a second known point, and re-check at the end.

### Adjusting a Traverse That Fails the Closure Standard

The traverse misclosure exceeds the project tolerance, and the crew distributes it by adjustment rather than finding the blunder, hiding a measurement or transcription error across all points. The trap is that adjustment is valid only for random error within tolerance, not for blunders. The mechanism is that a blunder distributed by adjustment contaminates every point rather than being isolated. The false signal is "the traverse now closes." The harm is a control network that appears to pass but contains a distributed blunder. The defense is to compare the misclosure to the standard, find and re-observe the blunder when the closure fails, and adjust only when the closure is within tolerance.

### Using an Open Traverse With No Independent Check

The crew runs an open traverse (no closure back to a known point) for layout or mapping, so any error is undetectable, and builds from it. The trap is that an open traverse has no closure to expose error. The mechanism is that without a closure, angular and linear errors accumulate with no check. The false signal is "the coordinates look consistent." The harm is layout or mapping that drifts from the true position with no detection. The defense is to close every traverse (loop or between known points), check the closure against the standard, and provide an independent check for any open traverse that must be used.

## Self-Check

- For RTK-GPS, did I confirm a fixed (ambiguity-resolved) solution, check the satellite count (at least 5-6) and PDOP, verify the base coordinate, and occupy and check a known point at the start and end of the session?
- Did I avoid observing under canopy, against reflective surfaces (buildings, pavement, water), and during poor satellite geometry or ionospheric activity, and switch to a total station where the sky view is inadequate?
- For total station work, did I center precisely over the point (within 1 mm), level carefully, measure and enter the height of instrument and height of target correctly, and backsight and check the measured direction against the known?
- Is horizontal and vertical control established to an accuracy standard appropriate to the work (e.g., Third- or Second-Order), referenced to the project datum and coordinate system, redundant (at least two references per point), closed, and documented with adjustment results?
- For any traverse, did I run a closed traverse (loop or between known points), compare the misclosure to the accuracy standard (e.g., 1:10,000 or better), find and re-observe any blunder when the closure fails, and adjust only when within tolerance?
- Have I classified and controlled the error sources — instrumental (calibration, face-averaging, servicing), natural (temperature, pressure, refraction, GPS atmospheric delay), and personal (centering, leveling, HI/HR, target identification)?
- Does every observation include an independent check (GPS checked by total station, total station checked by a second known point, level loop returning to start), and is the check independent of the method being verified?
- Did I choose the instrument by the site and the accuracy required (RTK for open-site control and topo, total station for canopy, urban canyons, and fine layout), and engage a licensed land surveyor for certifiable control and survey work?
