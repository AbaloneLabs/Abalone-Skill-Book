# Intersection and Signal Design

Intersection design is the engineering of the point where traffic streams cross, merge, and diverge, and it concentrates more conflict, delay, and crash risk than any other element of the network. The design must simultaneously move vehicles, protect pedestrians and cyclists, provide clear right-of-way, and manage the tradeoff between vehicle delay and safety, because every choice that speeds vehicles (longer cycles, permissive left turns, wide turning radii) tends to increase conflict severity for vulnerable users, and every choice that protects vulnerable users (protected phases, pedestrian intervals, tight radii) tends to add vehicle delay. The harm this skill prevents is an intersection that meets vehicle capacity on paper while producing preventable angle and pedestrian crashes, or a signal that is installed where it is not warranted and thereby adds delay and rear-end risk without safety benefit. The Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices (MUTCD) governs signal warrants, signing, and marking in the United States, and the design must respect its warrants, its clearance interval guidance, and its consistency requirements, while the geometric design must serve the design vehicles and the vulnerable users together.

## Core Rules

### Justify Signal Installation Through Documented MUTCD Warrants

A traffic signal is not installed because an intersection feels busy or because a community requests one; it is installed when one or more of the MUTCD signal warrants is satisfied based on engineering study, because an unwarranted signal adds delay and can increase rear-end and angle crashes. Conduct the warrant analysis using the MUTCD warrant categories (eight-hour vehicle volume, four-hour vehicle volume, peak hour, pedestrian volume, school crossing, coordinated signal system, crash experience, and roadway network), applying the correct volume thresholds for the intersection's speed and lane configuration, and document which warrants are met and with what data. Recognize that meeting a warrant makes a signal candidate, not a requirement, and that an engineering judgment may still reject a signal if it would degrade safety or operations; conversely, a crash-experience or gap-based warrant may justify a signal even when volumes are marginal. Never install a signal without the warrant study on file, because an unwarranted signal that later contributes to a crash will be scrutinized for its justification.

### Design Phasing to Balance Safety and Efficiency for the Dominant Movements

Signal phasing determines which movements move together and how left turns and pedestrians are protected, and it is the primary lever for intersection safety. Decide for each left turn whether to use protected-only phasing (a dedicated green arrow, safest but highest delay), permitted phasing (turn on a circular green after yielding, lowest delay but higher angle-crash risk), or protected-permitted (a green arrow followed by a permitted phase, a common compromise), based on the left-turn volume, opposing speed, sight distance, and crash history. Use protected-only phasing where opposing speeds are high, sight distance is limited, or there is a documented angle-crash pattern, because permissive left turns into high-speed opposing traffic are a leading cause of severe angle crashes. Provide protected pedestrian phases (exclusive or leading pedestrian interval) where turning vehicles conflict with high pedestrian volumes, because a leading pedestrian interval of a few seconds lets pedestrians enter the crossing before turning drivers and sharply reduces pedestrian conflicts.

### Compute Cycle Length, Splits, and Clearance Intervals From Defensible Methodology

The cycle length, green splits, and clearance intervals determine capacity and safety, and each must be computed rather than guessed. Set the cycle length by balancing the competing goals of minimizing total delay (which favors shorter cycles at lower volumes) and providing enough green to serve critical movements (which sets a minimum cycle); Webster's method or an equivalent optimization provides a defensible starting point, with longer cycles justified at high-volume coordinated corridors. Allocate green time to phases in proportion to their critical-lane volume, ensuring that every phase receives at least its minimum green and its pedestrian crossing time. Compute the yellow change and red clearance intervals by the MUTCD-recommended kinematic formula based on approach speed, deceleration rate, and intersection width, because a yellow interval set too short for the approach speed produces red-light running and angle crashes, and one set too long encourages drivers to treat it as additional green. Document the methodology and inputs for every interval, because clearance intervals that are not defensible are a known crash and liability risk.

### Design Geometry, Channelization, and Roundabouts to Serve All Users

The geometric layout must serve the design vehicles (including the largest permitted vehicle, with offtracking checked by turning templates), guide the driver through channelization, and protect vulnerable users. Provide left-turn lanes with adequate storage length for the design queue and taper length for deceleration, because a turn lane that is too short spills its queue into the through lane and causes rear-end crashes. Use channelization to define vehicle paths and to slow turning vehicles where pedestrians cross, and select curb radii that balance the turning path of the design vehicle against the crossing distance and exposure of pedestrians, because wide radii allow high-speed turns that endanger pedestrians while tight radii may force large vehicles over the curb. For roundabouts, design the inscribed circle diameter, entry and exit geometry, and central island deflection to control circulating speed, because a roundabout without adequate deflection allows high-speed circulating and merging crashes that defeat its safety purpose, and confirm capacity by the HCM roundabout method for the design volumes.

## Common Traps

### The Signal Installed Without a Documented Warrant

A signal is installed at a community's request or because the intersection feels busy, without a formal MUTCD warrant study on file. The false signal is the new signal that appears to improve order; the harm is that the unwarranted signal adds delay to the major street, induces rear-end crashes from stopping free-flowing traffic, and, if a crash later occurs, has no engineering justification to defend it.

### The Permissive Left Turn Into High-Speed Opposing Traffic

A left turn is allowed to proceed on a permissive circular green into opposing traffic moving at high speed, to save delay, without considering the angle-crash risk. The false signal is the reduced delay and the compliant phasing; the harm is that drivers misjudge gaps in high-speed opposing traffic, producing severe right-angle crashes that a protected-only phase would have prevented.

### The Yellow Interval Set Without the Kinematic Formula

The yellow change interval is set to a standard value (often three or four seconds) regardless of approach speed, rather than computed by the MUTCD kinematic formula. The false signal is the uniform, simple interval; the harm is that the interval is too short for the actual approach speed, drivers cannot stop comfortably, red-light running and angle crashes result, and the interval lacks the engineering basis needed to defend it.

### The Roundabout Without Adequate Deflection

A roundabout is selected and sized for capacity, but the entry geometry allows a straight-through path without deflection, so vehicles circulate at high speed. The false signal is the roundabout that handles the flow; the harm is that the absence of deflection permits high-speed circulating and merging crashes that are more severe than the signalized intersection the roundabout was meant to improve.

## Self-Check

- Is the signal installation justified by a documented MUTCD warrant study (volume, crash, pedestrian, or other), with the engineering judgment to accept or reject the signal recorded?
- Is the left-turn phasing for each approach selected based on left-turn volume, opposing speed, sight distance, and crash history, with protected-only phasing where high-speed opposing traffic creates angle-crash risk?
- Are the cycle length, green splits, minimum greens, and pedestrian crossing times computed by a defensible methodology, with green allocated by critical-lane volume?
- Are the yellow change and red clearance intervals computed by the MUTCD kinematic formula for the actual approach speed, intersection width, and deceleration rate, rather than set to a standard value?
- Do turn lanes provide adequate storage and taper for the design queue, and are curb radii and channelization balanced between the design vehicle's turning path and pedestrian crossing distance and exposure?
- For roundabouts, do the inscribed circle diameter, entry and exit geometry, and central island deflection control circulating speed, and is capacity confirmed by the HCM roundabout method for the design volumes?
