# Fall Protection and Prevention Program

Falls are the leading cause of construction fatalities, and the persistent failure in fall protection is not a lack of available equipment but a lack of engineering judgment about which system fits the hazard, whether the anchors and anchorage can actually hold the load, and whether the worker can be rescued before suspension trauma becomes lethal. The civil engineer building a fall protection program under 29 CFR 1926 Subpart M is responsible for a system-level design, not the purchase of harnesses. The six-foot trigger height in construction means that the vast majority of elevated work surfaces require protection, and the choice among guardrails, safety nets, personal fall arrest systems (PFAS), positioning devices, and controlled access zones must be driven by the specific geometry, the leading-edge conditions, the work sequence, and the rescue feasibility. Too often a PFAS is specified without verifying that an anchor rated for 5,000 pounds per worker exists, or without recognizing that a harness that arrests a fall still leaves the worker hanging with a narrow window for rescue. This skill forces the decisions that determine whether the system saves a life or merely creates the appearance of compliance.

## Core Rules

### Match the System to the Hazard and the Work Sequence

Subpart M establishes a hierarchy, and the engineer must select the system that controls the hazard for the actual work being performed, not the system that is easiest to procure. Guardrails (top rail at 42 inches plus or minus 3 inches, capable of withstanding 200 pounds of outward force, with a midrail and toeboard where needed) are preferred because they prevent the fall entirely. Safety nets are appropriate where guardrails are infeasible and where the fall distance permits net deployment. PFAS is the fallback, and it requires a complete arrest system: anchor, body harness, lanyard or lifeline, and a deceleration device. The engineer must confirm the free-fall distance is limited to six feet, that total fall distance accounting for deceleration and harness stretch clears the lower level, and that the system is rigged so a worker cannot swing into an obstruction (swing fall). Positioning device systems are limited to two feet of free fall and are not substitutes for fall arrest. The work sequence matters: leading-edge work, precast concrete erection, and residential construction each have specific provisions and the engineer must not apply a generic PFAS plan to all of them.

### Verify Anchor Strength and Anchorage Independence

The most common engineering failure in PFAS is assuming an anchor exists without verifying its capacity. Subpart M requires anchors to support 5,000 pounds per worker attached, or be designed by a qualified person to a factor of safety of two against the maximum arresting force. The engineer must distinguish between anchorages (structural members evaluated for load) and anchors (manufactured devices), and must confirm that the selected tie-off point is not a conduit, a small-diameter pipe, a guardrail, or any element not rated for the load. Self-retracting lifelines reduce clearance requirements but must be used overhead and within the manufacturer's footprint. The engineer must also verify that horizontal lifelines are designed by a qualified person, because their loads multiply with the number of workers and the sag geometry, and a generic horizontal lifeline can generate forces that exceed the anchor capacity. Anchorage independence from the fall arrest system must be maintained so that failure of one element does not cascade.

### Manage Leading Edges, Holes, and Controlled Access Zones

Leading-edge work, where a deck or slab is being extended, creates a moving hazard that static guardrails cannot address, and the engineer must design for a controlled access zone (CAZ) with a safety monitor, or use a PFAS rigged to overhead anchors that move with the work. Holes, including skylights and floor openings, must be protected by covers rated to twice the load they may receive, secured against displacement, and labeled, because a worker stepping into an unprotected hole is a fall regardless of the surrounding guardrail. The engineer must confirm that covers are not simply plywood laid over an opening, and that they are marked to prevent removal. Controlled access zones are permitted for overhand bricklaying and leading-edge work, but they require a competent safety monitor with no other duties, a defined boundary line, and a plan that restricts entry. The engineer should treat any opening or edge as a fall hazard requiring active management, not as a condition that will be "handled in the field."

### Plan Rescue Before the Fall Occurs

A worker hanging in a harness after an arrested fall can suffer suspension trauma within minutes as blood pools in the lower extremities, and Subpart M requires that workers who use PFAS be rescued promptly. The engineer must ensure the program includes a rescue plan that does not depend on calling 911 and waiting, because the response time often exceeds the survivability window. The plan must identify the rescue equipment, the trained personnel, the self-rescue options, and the means to reach a suspended worker at any location on the structure. The engineer must verify that the rescue plan is feasible for the actual geometry, including confined spaces, leading edges, and high elevations where aerial lifts cannot reach. Rescue planning is a design decision: if the location cannot be reached for rescue, the fall protection system for that location must be reconsidered, because a system that arrests a fall but cannot rescue is incomplete.

## Common Traps

### Specifying PFAS Without a Verified Anchor

The false signal is the presence of a harness and lanyard; the mechanism is that the worker ties off to a structural element never evaluated for 5,000 pounds, and the anchor fails at arrest. The harm is a fall that occurs despite full PPE, because the load path was never engineered, and the system provided only the illusion of protection.

### Ignoring Total Fall Distance and Swing Fall

The false signal is that a PFAS is in use; the mechanism is that deceleration distance, harness stretch, and lanyard length add up to more clearance than exists, or that a tie-off point offset allows a pendulum into a wall. The harm is the worker strikes the lower level or a structure despite the system activating, because clearance and geometry were never calculated.

### Treating Holes and Skylights as Covered by Surrounding Guardrails

The false signal is that guardrails exist around the perimeter; the mechanism is that interior holes and skylights are not part of the perimeter logic, and an unmarked cover is indistinguishable from decking. The harm is a worker steps through an opening that was never protected, because the engineer treated the floor as uniformly safe.

### Relying on 911 for Prompt Harness Rescue

The false signal is that emergency services exist; the mechanism is that suspension trauma can become lethal within 15 to 30 minutes, and fire department response plus access time routinely exceeds that window. The harm is a worker dies after a successfully arrested fall, because rescue was treated as someone else's responsibility rather than a designed response.

## Self-Check

- Does the selected fall protection system match the hazard and work sequence, with guardrails preferred and PFAS backed by a complete arrest system including free-fall and total fall distance calculations?
- Has every PFAS tie-off point been verified to support 5,000 pounds per worker or been designed by a qualified person at a safety factor of two, with no use of unrated structural elements?
- Are leading edges, holes, skylights, and floor openings protected with rated covers or CAZs, and are covers secured, labeled, and capable of twice the anticipated load?
- Does the program include a feasible, prompt rescue plan with trained personnel and equipment that can reach any suspended worker within the suspension trauma survivability window?
- Is the competent person or qualified person for the program identified, trained, and authorized, with the distinction between the two roles respected in design and inspection duties?
- Are controlled access zones, safety monitors, and positioning devices used only within their specific Subpart M provisions, with no generic substitution across work types?
