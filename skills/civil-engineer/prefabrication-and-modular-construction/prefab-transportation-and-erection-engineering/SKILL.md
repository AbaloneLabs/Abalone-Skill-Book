# Prefab Transportation And Erection Engineering

Transportation and erection are the phases where a prefabricated component leaves the controlled factory environment and is subjected to forces, accelerations, and support conditions it will never see again in service. A module designed for gravity and lateral loads in place may be damaged or destabilized by the lifting forces, the transport vibrations, the wind during erection, or the temporary support conditions before it is connected. Transportation and erection engineering is the discipline of verifying that every component survives the journey from factory to final position, that the lifting and rigging are safe, and that the erection sequence maintains stability at every step.

The harm this skill prevents is a module cracked or distorted by lifting forces applied at the wrong points, a component that exceeds the route envelope and is stopped or rerouted, a lift that exceeds the crane capacity or is destabilized by wind, an erection sequence that leaves a partially connected structure unstable, or a site access that cannot accommodate the delivery and crane. Engineers tend to design the component for service loads and assume transportation and erection are the contractor's problem, but the lifting points, the bracing, the crane, and the sequence are engineering determinations that must be made and verified before the component ships.

## Core Rules

### Engineer The Lifting Points For The Handling Forces, Not The Service Loads

A module or component lifted by crane is supported at the lifting points and subjected to its own weight, the dynamic amplification of the lift, and the wind. The lifting forces can induce moments and shears that exceed the service loads, and the lifting points must be designed for the handling condition. A module lifted from corners not designed as lifting points can crack, distort, or fail.

Design the lifting points (cast-in inserts, welded lugs, lifting frames) for the module weight, the dynamic amplification factor (typically 1.5 to 2.0 for crane lifts), and the wind load during the lift. Verify that the module structure can span between the lifting points without distress, and that the lifting hardware is rated for the load. Provide a lifting drawing that specifies the rigging, the sling angles, the lifting sequence, and the capacity requirements.

### Verify Module Stability And Distortion During All Handling Phases

A module is handled in multiple phases: lifted from the factory jig, loaded onto the transport, secured for transit, lifted off the transport, and set in place. Each phase has different support conditions, and a module stable in one phase may distort or overturn in another. Transport vibrations and braking forces induce accelerations that the securing must resist.

Analyze the module for each handling phase: the lifting condition (support at lifting points), the transport condition (support on the trailer, secured against braking and wind), and the setting condition (support on the foundation or the adjacent module before final connection). Verify that the module does not distort beyond tolerance, that the finishes and connections are not damaged, and that the securing resists the transport forces. Specify the transport securing detail and the bracing.

### Design Temporary Bracing And Shoring For The Erection Sequence

During erection, a structure passes through configurations that are less stable than the final state: a module set but not yet connected, a frame erected without its lateral system, a wall before the diaphragm is complete. Temporary bracing and shoring must maintain stability through every intermediate configuration, because a partially erected structure can collapse under wind, seismic, or construction loads before the permanent lateral system is engaged.

Design the temporary bracing for the erection sequence, identifying every configuration the structure passes through and the loads (wind, construction) each must resist. Specify the bracing location, capacity, and sequence of installation and removal. Verify that the bracing is in place before the structure relies on it and removed only after the permanent system is engaged. A structure that is stable in its final configuration but unstable during erection is a collapse risk.

### Select The Crane For The Weight, The Reach, And The Sequence

The crane selection is determined by the heaviest lift, the maximum reach radius (the distance from the crane to the farthest module), and the lift sequence. A crane that can lift the module at close radius may be inadequate at the reach required to set the farthest module, and the crane chart must be checked at every lift position. The crane type (mobile, tower, crawler) depends on the site, the number of lifts, and the reach.

Verify the crane capacity against the load chart at every lift radius, accounting for the rigging weight and the dynamic effects. Confirm the crane setup (the bearing capacity of the crane pad, the overhead clearance from power lines, the tail swing), and the sequence that allows setting every module without repositioning or with minimal repositioning. A crane selected for average conditions that is inadequate for the critical lift is a safety risk.

### Plan The Delivery Sequence To Match The Erection Sequence

The delivery sequence must match the erection sequence, because modules delivered out of order cannot be set, and a constrained site cannot store them. On a site with laydown area, some buffering is possible; on a constrained urban site, delivery must be just-in-time, with the module arriving and being set in a continuous operation.

Plan the delivery schedule to the erection sequence, with the modules numbered and delivered in set order. Coordinate the factory production, the transport, and the erection so that the right module arrives at the right time. A delivery sequence that does not match the erection sequence creates a storage crisis or an idle crane, both of which are costly.

### Assess And Verify Site Access For Delivery And Crane

The site access, the route from the highway to the site, the gate width, the turning radius, the bearing capacity of the access road and the crane pad, and the overhead clearance must all be verified before delivery. An oversize module that cannot navigate the final approach, or a crane that cannot access the lift position, stops the project.

Survey the route and the site access, verify the bearing capacity for the transport and the crane, confirm the overhead and horizontal clearances, and identify any temporary work (road widening, gate removal, utility clearance) required. A site access problem discovered at delivery is a project stoppage.

### Control Wind And Weather Limits For Lifting

Lifting operations are weather-sensitive, and wind is the primary hazard. Every crane and every module has a wind speed beyond which the lift is unsafe, because wind destabilizes the load, swings the module, and can exceed the crane's structural capacity. The wind limit must be established and enforced, and the lift rescheduled when the wind exceeds it.

Establish the wind speed limit for the crane, the module, and the rigging, and monitor the wind at the lift. Plan the lift schedule with weather windows, and do not lift in conditions exceeding the limit. A lift attempted in excessive wind is a catastrophic risk.

## Common Traps

### Lifting From Points Not Designed As Lifting Points

A module lifted from corners or edges not engineered for lifting forces cracks or distorts. The trap is assuming the module is strong enough to lift from any point; the reality is that lifting induces different forces than service.

### Ignoring The Transport And Handling Conditions

A module designed for service but not analyzed for transport vibration, braking, and wind on the trailer arrives damaged. The trap is treating transport as the carrier's responsibility; the securing and the bracing are engineering determinations.

### No Temporary Bracing For Intermediate Erection Configurations

A structure stable in its final state but unstable during erection collapses before the permanent lateral system is engaged. The trap is designing only the final configuration.

### Crane Selected Without Checking The Load Chart At Every Radius

A crane adequate at close radius that is inadequate at the far reach drops the module or tips. The trap is selecting the crane by maximum capacity without checking the radius derate.

### Delivery Sequence Mismatched To Erection Sequence

Modules delivered out of order on a constrained site create a storage crisis and idle the crane. The trap is treating delivery and erection as independent schedules.

### Site Access Discovered Inadequate At Delivery

A route or gate that cannot accommodate the module or the crane stops the project at the most expensive moment. The trap is not surveying the access before committing.

### Lifting In Excessive Wind

A lift attempted above the wind limit swings, destabilizes, or fails. The trap is schedule pressure overriding the weather limit.

## Self-Check

- Are the lifting points designed for the handling forces (weight, dynamic amplification, wind), with a lifting drawing specifying rigging, sling angles, and capacities?
- Is the module analyzed for every handling phase (factory lift, transport, setting), with distortion within tolerance and securing specified?
- Is temporary bracing designed for every intermediate erection configuration, with installation and removal sequenced to maintain stability?
- Is the crane selected and verified against the load chart at every lift radius, with setup, clearance, and sequence confirmed?
- Does the delivery sequence match the erection sequence, with just-in-time or laydown strategy defined for the site?
- Has the site access (route, gate, turning radius, bearing capacity, clearances) been surveyed and verified for delivery and crane?
- Are wind and weather limits established, monitored, and enforced for every lift?
- Has the transportation and erection engineering been completed and documented before any component ships?
