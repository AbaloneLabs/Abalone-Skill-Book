# Modular And Prefab System Selection

Prefabrication and modular construction shift a large share of construction labor from an uncontrolled field environment to a controlled factory environment, and the decision to use them is a structural, logistical, and contractual strategy, not merely a construction method. The systems range along a spectrum from highly prefabricated components (precast planks, structural steel modules) to fully finished volumetric modules that are set in place and connected. Each system has a different structural behavior, a different tolerance regime, a different transportation and erection requirement, and a different contractual and regulatory path.

The harm this skill prevents is a prefab system selected for its factory productivity benefits without accounting for the structural complexity of module-to-module connections, the transportation constraints that cap module size, the tolerance accumulation that prevents modules from fitting, the erection sequence that requires a crane and a site laydown area that may not exist, or the regulatory path that treats a modular building as a factory product, a site-built structure, or both. Engineers tend to treat prefab as a construction productivity decision and defer the structural and logistical implications, but the prefab strategy is determined by structural connections, transportation envelopes, and erection feasibility, and these must be evaluated before the strategy is committed.

## Core Rules

### Match The Prefab System To The Project Geometry And Repetition

The economic case for prefabrication depends on repetition, because the factory setup, the jigging, and the production line are amortized over the number of repeated units. A highly repetitive building (a hotel, a dormitory, an apartment building, a hospital patient tower) favors volumetric modular or panelized systems; a one-off or highly irregular building does not, because the factory cannot achieve the repetition that justifies the setup.

Evaluate the unit mix, the floor plan repetition, and the structural grid regularity. A building with many unit types and irregular geometry may suit component-level prefabrication (precast planks, steel modules) but not volumetric modular. Select the system whose factory productivity is realized by the project's actual repetition, not by an assumed repetition that the design does not deliver.

### Define Module Break Points For Structural, Functional, And Tolerance Reasons

The module break points, where one module ends and the next begins, determine the structural connection locations, the building system interfaces (plumbing, electrical, HVAC), the fire and acoustic separation, and the tolerance accumulation. Break points should be located where the structural connection is buildable, where the building systems can be joined, and where the tolerance between modules can be absorbed.

Coordinate the break points across disciplines: the structural engineer wants the connection at a structurally efficient location, the MEP engineer needs access to join the systems, the architect needs the joint to meet fire and acoustic ratings, and the manufacturer needs the break to suit the factory production. A break point optimized for one discipline and unbuildable for another creates field conflicts.

### Design The Module-To-Module And Module-To-Foundation Connections

The connections between modules, and between the modules and the foundation, are the structural and waterproofing critical points of a prefab building, and they are the most common source of failure. The structural connection must transfer gravity, lateral, and uplift loads between modules, accommodate the erection tolerance, and be buildable by the erection crew working from outside or above the module.

Design the connection for the load transfer, the tolerance accommodation (shims, grout pads, adjustable brackets), the constructability (access for bolting or welding, sequence of erection), and the durability (corrosion protection, fireproofing). A connection that works on paper but cannot be built by the erection crew, or that cannot accommodate the field tolerance, fails in the field. Mock up and test the critical connections before production.

### Verify The Transportation Envelope And Route For Every Module

Every volumetric module must be transportable from the factory to the site, and the transportation constraints cap the module size. The constraints include the maximum width, height, and length permitted on the route (which varies by jurisdiction and requires permits for oversize loads), the bridge clearances, the turning radii, the weight limits, and the site access for the delivery truck and the crane.

Verify the module dimensions against the transportation envelope early, because a module designed larger than the route allows must be redesigned or shipped disassembled, defeating the factory productivity. Confirm the route survey, the permit requirements, and the delivery sequence, because a module that cannot be delivered cannot be erected.

### Plan The Erection Sequence, Crane, And Site Logistics

Modular erection requires a crane, a delivery sequence that matches the erection sequence, and a site laydown area (or a just-in-time delivery strategy if no laydown exists). The crane size is determined by the module weight and the reach radius, and the crane location must allow setting every module without repositioning (or with minimal repositioning). The delivery sequence must match the erection sequence, because modules cannot be stored out of order on a constrained site.

Plan the erection sequence to the structural dependency (which modules must be set before others, how lateral stability is achieved as the building rises), the crane reach, and the delivery logistics. A site with no laydown area requires just-in-time delivery, which requires a tightly controlled schedule and a reliable factory and transport chain. Verify that the crane, the route, and the sequence are feasible before committing to the strategy.

### Establish The Tolerance Regime And The Interface Detailing

Prefabricated buildings have two tolerance regimes: the factory tolerance (tight, controlled) and the site tolerance (looser, affected by foundation and erection accuracy). The interface between modules, and between the modules and the site-built elements (the foundation, the core, the roof), must absorb the difference. Without explicit tolerance accommodation, modules do not fit, connections do not close, and finishes do not align.

Specify the manufacturing tolerance, the erection tolerance, and the interface detail that absorbs the difference (shims, grout, adjustable brackets, oversize holes, sealant joints). Establish the survey control that verifies the foundation and the module positions as erection proceeds, because tolerance accumulation that is not detected and corrected compounds up the building.

### Resolve The Regulatory Path For Modular And Off-Site Construction

Modular buildings are regulated through a path that can involve both factory certification (state modular program, third-party inspection in the factory) and site permitting (the local AHJ for the foundation, the connections, the site work). The path depends on the jurisdiction, the building type, and whether the modules are classified as manufactured housing, industrialized modular, or site-built components.

Confirm the regulatory path early, because it determines who inspects what, where, and when. A modular building inspected in the factory under a state program may not require re-inspection of the module in the field, but the foundation, the connections, and the site work are inspected locally. A misunderstanding of the path leads to double inspection, missed inspection, or a CO block.

## Common Traps

### Selecting Prefab For Productivity Without Repetition

A prefab system chosen for factory productivity on a project without the repetition to amortize the setup costs more than site-built construction. The trap is assuming factory work is always cheaper; the reality is that factory productivity requires repetition.

### Module Break Points Optimized For One Discipline

Break points set for structural efficiency that make MEP joining impossible, or for architectural reasons that create unbuildable structural connections, create field conflicts. The trap is optimizing break points in isolation.

### Connections That Work On Paper But Not In The Field

A module connection that cannot be bolted or welded by the erection crew, or that cannot accommodate the field tolerance, fails during erection. The trap is designing connections without mock-ups or constructability review.

### Exceeding The Transportation Envelope

A module designed larger than the route allows must be redesigned or shipped disassembled, erasing the factory benefit. The trap is sizing modules without a route survey.

### No Laydown Area With A Non-Just-In-Time Schedule

A site with no laydown area and a delivery schedule that is not tightly just-in-time creates a storage crisis and an erection delay. The trap is assuming modules can be stored on a constrained urban site.

### Tolerance Accumulation Without Interface Detailing

Modules that do not fit, connections that do not close, and finishes that do not align result from tolerance accumulation with no interface detail to absorb it. The trap is assuming factory precision eliminates field tolerance.

### Misunderstanding The Regulatory Path

Double inspection, missed inspection, or a CO block result from misunderstanding whether the modules are factory-certified, site-inspected, or both. The trap is assuming the local AHJ inspects everything.

## Self-Check

- Is the prefab system matched to the project's actual geometry and unit repetition, with the factory productivity justified by the repetition?
- Are module break points coordinated across structural, MEP, architectural, and manufacturing disciplines?
- Are the module-to-module and module-to-foundation connections designed for load transfer, tolerance accommodation, constructability, and durability, with critical connections mocked up?
- Has the transportation envelope and route been verified for every module, including permits for oversize loads?
- Is the erection sequence planned for structural dependency, crane reach, and delivery logistics, with a laydown or just-in-time strategy defined?
- Are the manufacturing tolerance, erection tolerance, and interface details specified, with survey control to detect and correct accumulation?
- Is the regulatory path (factory certification, site permitting, or both) confirmed for the jurisdiction and building type?
- Has the prefab strategy been evaluated for total project benefit, including structural, logistical, and contractual implications, not just factory productivity?
