# Engineered Wood And Mass Timber Design

Engineered wood and mass timber design is the work of selecting and designing members made of manufactured wood products, where the material properties, layup, and behavior differ fundamentally from solid sawn lumber and where the governing standards, fire provisions, and construction types are specific to these products. The difficulty is not reading a capacity table; it is the judgment that the product is anisotropic, that its strength depends on orientation and layup, that rolling shear and bearing perpendicular to the panel can govern where they are invisible in solid-sawn design, and that tall mass timber introduces fire, charring, and construction-type considerations that did not exist before the IBC tall mass timber types. A CLT floor that satisfies bending can still fail in rolling shear at its cross layers, a glulam beam can fail at a connection that splits the laminations, and a tall mass timber building can fail to meet its fire rating if the char layer and protection are not correctly accounted for. The NDS, the ANSI PRG 320 standard for CLT, the product-specific qualifications for glulam and LVL, and the IBC Type IV provisions govern, and the engineer cannot treat engineered wood as solid sawn with a different table. This skill covers the judgment exercised while designing with engineered wood and mass timber, with the understanding that final design, sealing of drawings, and code interpretations require a licensed professional engineer, and that tall mass timber and seismic systems may require a structural specialist.

## Core Rules

### Design CLT And Mass Timber For Orientation, Layup, And Rolling Shear

Begin every CLT and mass timber design by recognizing that these are orthotropic, layered products whose strength depends on the direction of load relative to the grain of each layer. CLT is laid up with alternating layers oriented at right angles, so the major-axis strength direction carries the span and the minor-axis layers provide transverse integrity and rolling shear resistance, and the design must use the properties for the correct orientation. Check rolling shear in the cross layers, which is the shear stress that acts on the rolling plane of the perpendicular laminations and can govern the shear capacity of a CLT panel at a fraction of the bending capacity, because rolling shear strength is much lower than the bending or in-plane shear strength. Use the CLT properties from ANSI PRG 320 and the qualified manufacturer's layup, because the properties depend on the lamination thickness, species, and grade, and a generic table does not capture the specific layup. For glulam, distinguish between balanced and unbalanced layups and between the bending combination and the axial combinations, because the tension and compression laminations are placed asymmetrically in unbalanced beams and the capacity differs by loading direction. Do not treat a CLT or glulam member as an equivalent solid-sawn section, because the layup creates properties that solid sawn does not possess.

### Apply The Char Method For Fire Resistance Of Exposed Mass Timber

Mass timber achieves fire resistance through the formation of a char layer that insulates the uncharred core, and the design must account for the char and the elevated-temperature zone beneath it. Compute the effective char depth from the required fire rating using the NDS char rate, add the zero-strength layer that accounts for the heated wood beneath the char, and design the remaining uncharred section for the applied loads during the fire, because the capacity during fire is that of the reduced section. For Type IV-A, IV-B, and IV-C tall mass timber construction, apply the IBC provisions that specify the required protection, the amount of exposed timber, and the fire rating, because these construction types define how much mass timber may be exposed and how much must be protected by noncombustible material. Distinguish between the fire rating of the assembly and the char calculation for the member, because the assembly rating may require protection of connections and joints that the member char calculation does not address. Detail connections and joints to maintain the fire rating, because an exposed steel connection or an unprotected joint can fail before the char-protected timber, and the connection is the weak link in fire as it is in structural loading.

### Design Connections For Mass Timber With Ductility And Tolerances

Connections in mass timber are critical because the members are large and the forces are high, and the connections must transfer load with ductility, tolerance, and fire protection. Use self-tapping screws, bearing-type connections with steel plates, or proprietary connectors qualified for the application, and design them for the actual force path including the group action and geometry factors, because a mass timber connection that fails is catastrophic. Detail connections to accommodate the fabrication and erection tolerances of large panels, because mass timber is manufactured to tight tolerances but the structure and the foundation are not, and a connection without adjustment will not fit. Provide ductility in the connection through yielding fasteners or yielding steel plates, because a connection that is stronger than the connected member can force failure into the wood in a brittle mode. Protect connections for the required fire rating, because exposed steel connectors lose strength in fire, and detail them with concealment, intumescent coating, or additional wood cover as the construction type requires. Recognize that connections in mass timber often govern the design and the cost, and that a connection detail that is elegant on paper but impossible to install has no value.

### Verify Serviceability, Vibration, And Long-Term Behavior Of Engineered Wood

Engineered wood products have high stiffness-to-weight ratios, which makes them efficient but also makes vibration and long-term behavior governing concerns that solid-sawn design rarely faces. Check floor vibration for CLT and wood I-joist floors, because lightweight stiff floors can meet deflection limits and still be perceptibly bouncy, and the vibration check uses the floor frequency, stiffness, and damping rather than the deflection limit alone. Check long-term deflection and creep for glulam, CLT, and LVL under sustained load using the NDS creep factor, because engineered wood creeps under sustained load and the total deflection at end of service life exceeds the immediate value. Check bearing perpendicular to grain at supports and at concentrated loads, because the perpendicular-to-grain capacity of CLT and glulam is much lower than the parallel-to-grain capacity and can govern at supports. For wood I-joists, check web shear, web buckling, and bearing at the ends, because the thin web is the vulnerable element and can buckle or crush where a solid member would not. Treat serviceability and long-term behavior as part of the design, not a courtesy check, because in engineered wood these often govern the member size and the floor performance.

## Common Traps

### Treating CLT As An Equivalent Solid-Sawn Panel

The engineer takes a CLT panel and designs it as a solid-sawn section of equivalent depth, using solid-sawn bending and shear values. The mechanism is that CLT is an orthotropic layered product with rolling shear in the cross layers and orientation-dependent properties that solid sawn does not have. The false signal is that the equivalent section passes the bending check. The harm is rolling shear failure or overstress of the cross layers at a load below the calculated capacity, because the layup was never credited. CLT must be designed with the PRG 320 properties for the correct layup and orientation, with rolling shear checked as an independent limit state.

### Omitting The Zero-Strength Layer In The Fire Char Calculation

The engineer computes the char depth and subtracts only the char layer, designing the full remaining section for the fire load. The mechanism is that the wood beneath the char is heated and loses strength before it chars, and the zero-strength layer accounts for this heated zone. The false signal is that the remaining section after char removal is adequate. The harm is member failure during the fire, because the heated zone was not deducted and the effective section was larger than reality. The char calculation must include both the char depth and the zero-strength layer, and the remaining uncharred section must be designed for the fire load.

### Detailing Connections Stronger Than The Connected Members

The engineer designs a mass timber connection to be stronger than the connected members, on the theory that the connection should not govern. The mechanism is that a connection stronger than the wood forces failure into the wood in a brittle splitting or tear-out mode, and ductility is lost. The false signal is that the connection capacity exceeds the demand. The harm is brittle wood failure at the connection under overload, because the yielding mechanism was forced into the wrong material. Connections should be detailed to yield in the fasteners or steel plates, providing ductility, rather than forcing brittle failure into the wood.

### Ignoring Vibration In Lightweight Engineered Wood Floors

The engineer checks deflection against the code limit, passes, and does not check vibration, for a lightweight CLT or I-joist floor. The mechanism is that lightweight stiff floors can meet deflection limits and still vibrate perceptibly under footfall, because vibration depends on frequency and damping, not deflection. The false signal is that the deflection check passes. The harm is floors that are acceptable on paper but objectionable to occupants, leading to complaints and remediation. Vibration must be checked for lightweight engineered wood floors using the frequency and stiffness criteria, because deflection alone does not capture the dynamic performance.

## Self-Check

- Has the CLT or mass timber member been designed with the PRG 320 or product-specific properties for the correct layup and orientation, with rolling shear checked as an independent limit state?
- For exposed mass timber, has the fire resistance been computed with both the char depth and the zero-strength layer, and does the remaining section carry the fire load?
- Are the IBC Type IV-A, IV-B, or IV-C provisions for protection and exposed timber satisfied, including connection and joint protection for the required rating?
- Are mass timber connections designed for the actual force path with group action and geometry factors, detailed for erection tolerances, and provided with ductility and fire protection?
- Has floor vibration been checked for lightweight engineered wood floors, and has long-term creep deflection been included for sustained load?
- Has bearing perpendicular to grain been checked at supports and concentrated loads, and for I-joists have web shear, web buckling, and end bearing been verified?
- Is the design sealed and reviewed by a licensed professional engineer, with specialist involvement for tall mass timber and seismic systems?
