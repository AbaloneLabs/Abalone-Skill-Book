# Timber Beam And Column Design

Timber beam and column design is the work of selecting a wood member that resists flexural, shear, axial, and bearing demands while accounting for the fact that wood strength is not a single fixed number but a reference value modified by a stack of adjustment factors for load duration, moisture, temperature, geometry, and stability. The difficulty is not solving the bending stress equation; it is the judgment that surrounds the equation, because a beam that satisfies bending can still roll over laterally, split in bearing, creep under sustained load, or lose capacity when wet, and a column that satisfies axial capacity can still buckle as a slender member or fail in combined bending and compression. The NDS governs through an allowable stress design method in which reference design values are multiplied by adjustment factors to produce adjusted design values, with a parallel LRFD format, and the engineer cannot treat capacity as the tabulated value independent of service conditions. This skill covers the judgment exercised while designing and checking ordinary solid-sawn and engineered timber beams and columns, with the understanding that final design, sealing of drawings, and code interpretations require a licensed professional engineer, and that tall mass timber, long-span glulam, and seismic systems may require a structural specialist.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Full NDS Adjustment Factor Stack To Reference Design Values

Begin every timber design from the NDS reference design values published for the species, grade, and size, and recognize that these values are baseline conditions that must be adjusted. The adjusted design value equals the reference value multiplied by the applicable adjustment factors, and the factors differ by stress type: bending has a size factor, a beam stability factor, and an incising factor; compression has a column stability factor; all stress types have load duration, moisture, temperature, and repetitive member factors where applicable. Do not use the published reference value directly as the capacity, because a member in service rarely matches the reference conditions, and omitting the moisture factor for a wet-service member or the load duration factor for a permanent load can overstate capacity by a large margin. Apply the load duration factor that corresponds to the shortest-duration load in the combination, because wood carries short-duration loads at higher stress than permanent loads, but never combine the duration benefit with an unconservative omission of the stability factors. Confirm whether ASD or LRFD is being used and apply the format conversion consistently, because mixing the two methods produces capacities that are neither valid ASD nor valid LRFD values.

### Check Beam Lateral Stability Through The Beam Stability Factor

A beam that satisfies bending stress on the basis of its full section can still fail by lateral-torsional buckling if its compression edge is not laterally supported along its length. Compute the beam stability factor from the slenderness of the compression edge, the unbraced length, the effective length factor accounting for loading and support conditions, and the section depth and width, because a deep narrow beam with an unbraced compression edge has a stability factor well below unity and an adjusted bending capacity far below the reference value. Distinguish between members with the compression edge fully supported, such as a joist with sheathing nailed to its top, and members with an unsupported compression edge, such as a beam with the top edge in tension or a floor beam braced only at the supports. Where the stability factor governs, either reduce the span, add lateral bracing to the compression edge, or select a wider or shallower section, because the geometry of the section and its bracing is the only thing that restores stability. Do not assume that the presence of any bracing eliminates the check, because partial or widely spaced bracing still leaves an effective unbraced length that must be evaluated.

### Design Columns For Slenderness And Combined Axial And Bending

Design columns as members carrying combined axial load and bending, not as pure compression members, because real columns carry eccentric gravity load, frame moments, and lateral drift. Compute the column stability factor from the slenderness ratio, the effective unbraced length in each principal direction, and the section radius of gyration, because a slender column buckles at a stress well below the reference compression value, and the slenderness ratio is the controlling parameter. Check the column for the combined interaction of axial compression and bending using the NDS interaction equation, which adds the axial stress ratio and the bending stress ratio with amplification of the bending term for the second-order effect, because a column that satisfies axial and bending separately can fail the interaction. Evaluate slenderness about both axes and use the larger governing ratio, because a rectangular column or a built-up member can be slender in one direction and stocky in the other, and the weak direction governs. Provide lateral support to the column at the points assumed in the effective length calculation, because a column whose assumed brace points are not actually braced has a longer effective length and a lower capacity than calculated.

### Verify Bearing, Shear, And Deflection As Independent Limit States

Bending is one limit state, but bearing at supports, horizontal shear, and deflection are independent checks that frequently govern and must be evaluated separately. Check bearing perpendicular to grain at the supports and at concentrated load points, because wood is weak perpendicular to grain and a beam can crush into its bearing plate or split at a notched support before it fails in bending. Check horizontal shear at the neutral axis near the supports, paying special attention to notched and tapered members where the shear capacity is reduced, because shear failure in wood is sudden and often occurs at notches and connections. Check deflection against project and code limits using the modulus of elasticity adjusted for the service condition, and include the long-term creep component for sustained load using the NDS creep factor, because wood creeps under sustained load and the total deflection at end of service life is well above the immediate elastic value. Treat each limit state as a gate that must be passed independently, because a member adequate in bending can still fail in bearing, shear, or serviceability.

## Common Traps

### Using The Reference Design Value Without Adjustment Factors

The engineer looks up the published bending or compression value for the species and grade and uses it directly as the capacity, omitting the moisture, duration, size, and stability factors. The mechanism is that the reference value is defined at standard conditions that rarely match service, and a wet, permanent-load, unbraced member has an adjusted capacity far below the tabulated value. The false signal is that the member passes against the reference value. The harm is a member whose real capacity is below the demand, failing in service or under a load the calculation said it could carry, because the adjustment factors that reduced the capacity were never applied. Every design value must be multiplied by the full applicable adjustment factor stack before comparison to demand.

### Ignoring Lateral Stability Of An Unbraced Compression Edge

The engineer checks bending stress against the adjusted value and passes, without computing the beam stability factor for an unbraced compression edge. The mechanism is that a beam whose compression edge is free to move laterally buckles at a bending stress below the reference value, and the stability factor captures this reduction. The false signal is that the bending check passes on the full section. The harm is lateral-torsional buckling under a load below the calculated capacity, because the member was not as stable as the full-section check assumed. The beam stability factor must be computed whenever the compression edge is not continuously braced, and the effective unbraced length must reflect the actual bracing.

### Designing A Column For Axial Load Only

The engineer sizes a column for the axial load and ignores the bending from frame action, eccentricity, or lateral drift. The mechanism is that real columns carry moment as well as axial load, and the NDS interaction equation adds the two effects with second-order amplification, so a column adequate in pure compression can fail the combined check. The false signal is that the axial capacity exceeds the axial demand. The harm is column failure under combined loading at a demand the pure-axial check said was acceptable. Columns must be checked with the full interaction equation including bending from all sources, and the slenderness must be evaluated about both axes.

### Omitting The Creep Component From Long-Term Deflection

The engineer reports the immediate elastic deflection and treats it as the total, ignoring the creep that wood accumulates under sustained load. The mechanism is that wood creeps under sustained load and the long-term deflection can be substantially larger than the immediate value, especially in wet or high-temperature service. The false signal is that the immediate deflection is within limits. The harm is floors and roofs that sag over time, damaging finishes and creating ponding, because the deflection that mattered was the total at end of service life. Long-term deflection must include the creep factor applied to the sustained-load portion of the immediate deflection.

## Self-Check

- Has each design value been multiplied by the full applicable NDS adjustment factor stack, including load duration, moisture, temperature, size, stability, and incising where applicable, with ASD or LRFD applied consistently?
- Has the beam stability factor been computed for any member with an unbraced compression edge, using the correct effective unbraced length and section geometry?
- Are columns checked for slenderness about both axes and for combined axial and bending using the NDS interaction equation with second-order amplification?
- Has bearing perpendicular to grain been checked at all supports and concentrated load points, with special attention to notched members?
- Has horizontal shear been checked near the supports, with the capacity reduction applied at notches and tapered sections?
- Is deflection checked with the modulus of elasticity adjusted for service condition, including the long-term creep component for sustained load?
- Is the design sealed and reviewed by a licensed professional engineer, with specialist involvement for tall mass timber, long-span glulam, or seismic systems?
