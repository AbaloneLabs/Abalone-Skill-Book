# Timber Connections And Fastening

Timber connection design is the work of selecting fasteners and detailing the joints between wood members so that the connection transfers the analysis forces with adequate strength, ductility, and durability, and it is where timber structures most often fail. Members are sized members with predictable strengths; connections are assemblies of bolts, screws, nails, rings, and plates where force paths turn, concentrate, and introduce splitting, row tear-out, and group effects that the member design never captures. A beam selected for flexure can still split at its bolt group, a truss can fail at a heel joint where fasteners cluster, and a connection that satisfies the single-fastener capacity can still fail in block shear or group tear-out because the fasteners act together on a reduced wood volume. The NDS governs through a yield limit method that computes the reference lateral resistance of each fastener from the possible yield modes, then adjusts for duration, geometry, group action, and end and edge distances, and the engineer cannot treat connection capacity as the single-fastener value times the count. This skill covers the judgment exercised while designing and detailing ordinary timber connections, with the understanding that final design, sealing of drawings, and code interpretations require a licensed professional engineer, and that seismic and high-load connections may require a structural specialist.

## Core Rules

### Compute Fastener Capacity From The Full NDS Yield Limit Equations

Begin every lateral connection design from the NDS yield limit equations, which compute the reference lateral design value for a single fastener as the minimum over the possible yield modes. The modes include fastener yielding in one or two plastic hinges, wood bearing or crushing in the main and side members, and combinations of fastener and wood yielding, and the governing mode is the one that produces the lowest value. Do not use a rule-of-thumb single value for a bolt or screw, because the capacity depends on the main and side member thicknesses, the fastener diameter, the fastener bending yield strength, and the dowel bearing strength of the species, all of which vary by geometry and material. Distinguish between double shear and single shear configurations, which have different yield modes and capacities, and between connections with steel side plates and all-wood connections, because steel side plates change the bearing distribution and can increase capacity. Apply the applicable adjustment factors, including load duration, wet service, temperature, geometry, and group action, to the reference value, because the reference value is a baseline that must be reduced for real conditions. For fasteners in withdrawal, such as lag screws or screws loaded in tension, use the withdrawal design value from the NDS tables based on penetration and species, and never rely on withdrawal in connections subject to reversal or vibration without positive anchorage.

### Apply Group Action And Geometry Factors To Multi-Fastener Joints

A connection with multiple fasteners does not develop the sum of the single-fastener capacities, because the fasteners share the load unequally and act on a reduced wood volume that can fail as a group. Compute the group action factor, which reduces the capacity of a row or cluster of fasteners based on the number of fasteners, the spacing, the fastener and member stiffness, and the load-slip characteristics, because the outer fasteners attract more load and the group capacity is below the sum. Compute the geometry factor for fasteners that do not meet the full required end, edge, and spacing distances, because reduced distances lower the capacity through splitting and tear-out, and the geometry factor captures this reduction rather than being a pass-fail threshold. Lay out fasteners to meet the full NDS required end, edge, and spacing distances wherever possible, because meeting the full distances avoids the geometry reduction and reduces the risk of splitting, and where the full distances cannot be met, apply the reduced geometry factor explicitly. Check the connection for row tear-out and block shear where fasteners are in a line parallel to the grain, because the wood between and beyond the fasteners can tear out as a block under shear, and this limit state is independent of the single-fastener yield capacity.

### Select Fastener Type And Detail For The Load And Service Condition

Choose the fastener type based on the magnitude and direction of the load, the member thicknesses, the service condition, and the constructability of the joint. Bolts are used for larger loads and thicker members and require properly sized holes and washers on both sides to distribute bearing and prevent pull-through. Lag screws and structural screws are used where one-sided installation is needed or where withdrawal resistance is required, and their capacity depends on penetration into the main member and on predrilling to prevent splitting. Nails and light-gauge connectors are used for light framing and diaphragms, with capacity governed by the NDS nail tables adjusted for duration, and care to distinguish toe-nailing, end-nailing, and face-nailing which have different capacities. Split rings and shear plates are used for high-load connections in heavy timber, where the ring or plate transfers load through a large bearing area that greatly exceeds the bolt alone, and these require precise grooving and alignment. Detail the connection so that fasteners can be installed, tightened, and inspected, with access for drills and wrenches, and so that the connection does not trap water that promotes decay, because a connection that cannot be built or that rots in service has no real capacity.

### Check Connection Limit States In The Wood And The Fastener

A connection is a chain of limit states, each of which must be satisfied: the fastener lateral or withdrawal capacity, the wood bearing and splitting, the row and group tear-out, the net section of the member at the connection, and the capacity of any steel gusset plates or plates. Check the net section of the wood member at the bolt group, because the holes reduce the cross section and a tension chord or tie can fail at the net section rather than in the connection hardware. Check steel gusset plates and side plates for yielding, rupture, and block shear per the steel provisions, because a wood connection with steel plates is a hybrid that must satisfy both the wood and the steel limit states. Check the connection for the effects of moisture change, because wood shrinks and swells across the grain and can split around fasteners or loosen the fit, and detail with slotted holes or allowance for movement where the differential movement is significant. Treat each limit state as an independent gate, because a connection adequate in fastener yield can still fail in wood tear-out, net section, or plate capacity, and the governing limit state is often one the engineer did not check.

## Common Traps

### Summing Single-Fastener Capacities Without Group Action

The engineer computes the single-fastener capacity and multiplies by the number of fasteners, without the group action factor. The mechanism is that fasteners in a row share load unequally and act on a reduced wood volume, so the group capacity is below the sum, and the group action factor captures this reduction. The false signal is that the sum of fastener capacities exceeds the demand. The harm is connection failure in group tear-out or row tear-out at a load below the summed capacity, because the fasteners acted together on a wood volume smaller than assumed. The group action factor must be applied to any multi-fastener connection, and the row and block tear-out limit states must be checked independently.

### Placing Fasteners Closer Than Required Edge And End Distances

The engineer lays out fasteners to fit the geometry and places them closer to the edge or end than the NDS requires, without applying the geometry factor. The mechanism is that fasteners close to the edge or end split the wood or tear out under load, and the geometry factor reduces the capacity to reflect the reduced distance. The false signal is that the fasteners fit and the single-fastener capacity is adequate. The harm is splitting or tear-out at a load below the calculated capacity, because the geometry was never credited with its reduction. The full required distances should be met wherever possible, and where they cannot, the reduced geometry factor must be applied explicitly.

### Using Withdrawal Resistance In Reversible Or Vibrating Connections

The engineer relies on the withdrawal capacity of lag screws or nails in a connection subject to load reversal, vibration, or cyclic load. The mechanism is that withdrawal resistance depends on friction and bearing that degrade under reversal and vibration, and a fastener that holds in static withdrawal can work loose under cyclic load. The false signal is that the static withdrawal capacity is adequate. The harm is fastener pull-out or loosening under service, because the cyclic condition was never considered. Connections subject to reversal or vibration should use positive lateral bearing or mechanical anchorage rather than withdrawal, and the withdrawal capacity should be discounted where cyclic load is present.

### Checking The Fastener And Forgetting The Member Net Section

The engineer verifies the fastener yield capacity and the wood bearing but does not check the net section of the tension member at the bolt group. The mechanism is that bolt holes reduce the cross-sectional area of the member, and a tension tie or chord can rupture at the net section before the connection hardware reaches capacity. The false signal is that the connection hardware is adequate. The harm is tensile rupture of the member at the connection, because the net section was the governing limit state and was never checked. The net section of every tension member at the connection must be checked against the demand, because the connection includes the member, not only the fasteners.

## Self-Check

- Has each fastener capacity been computed from the full NDS yield limit equations as the minimum over all yield modes, with main and side member thickness, diameter, and dowel bearing strength included?
- Has the group action factor been applied to every multi-fastener connection, and have row tear-out and block shear been checked as independent limit states?
- Do all fasteners meet the full required end, edge, and spacing distances, and where they do not, has the reduced geometry factor been applied explicitly?
- Is the fastener type selected for the load magnitude and direction, member thickness, service condition, and constructability, with washers, predrilling, and access detailed?
- Have all connection limit states been checked, including fastener yield, wood bearing and splitting, net section of the member, and steel gusset plate capacity where applicable?
- Is withdrawal resistance avoided or discounted in connections subject to load reversal, vibration, or cyclic load?
- Is the connection design sealed and reviewed by a licensed professional engineer, with specialist involvement for seismic and high-load connections?
