# Construction Drawing and Specification Coordination

Construction drawings and specifications are the two halves of the contract documents that define the work, and the judgment problem is that they look like separate artifacts but are legally a single integrated instrument whose internal consistency determines whether the contractor can build, price, and claim the work correctly. Agents tend to treat the drawings as the real design and the specifications as boilerplate reference, or vice versa, and miss that a note on a plan can silently override a specification section, that a dimension and a specification can contradict each other, and that the order of precedence among documents decides which controls when they conflict. The harm this skill prevents is a bid built on conflicting documents, a change order or claim rooted in a discrepancy the design team never reconciled, a specification that references a superseded standard, and a drawing set that is missing the seal, the scale, the north arrow, or the datum needed to be contractually valid. This skill covers the structure of the drawing set and the project manual, the CSI MasterFormat and SectionFormat three-part spec, the coordination between plans and specs, the order of precedence, the general notes, and the sealing and sheet standards. It is a document-production discipline that sits at the boundary of design and contract, and the agent must treat every note, every callout, and every specification reference as a contract obligation, not a drafting convenience.

## Core Rules

### Structure the Drawing Set and the Project Manual as One Integrated Contract

The contract documents are the agreement, the general conditions, the supplementary conditions, the specifications (the project manual), the drawings, and the addenda, and each must be complete and internally consistent within itself and with the others. Organize the drawing set with a title sheet, a drawing index, general notes, legend, existing-conditions and demolition sheets, civil sheets (grading, utility, erosion control), and the discipline sheets in a consistent numbering system (for example C-100 series for civil, S-100 series for structural). Organize the project manual by CSI MasterFormat, with Division 00 procurement and contracting requirements, Division 01 general requirements, and the technical divisions 02 through 49, each section independently numbered and titled. The disciplined rule is that a work item defined on the drawings must be defined consistently in the specifications, and a work item specified in a section must appear on the drawings where it is built, because the contractor prices and builds from both together. The trap is a drawing set and a project manual produced by separate teams that never cross-check, leaving a structural element drawn but not specified, or a material specified but not detailed.

### Write Specifications in CSI MasterFormat and Three-Part SectionFormat

CSI MasterFormat is the standard organization of the project manual into divisions and sections, and CSI SectionFormat is the standard internal organization of each section into three parts: Part 1 General (summary, references, submittals, quality assurance, delivery, project conditions), Part 2 Products (manufacturers, materials, fabrication, source quality control), and Part 3 Execution (installation, field quality control, repair, cleaning, protection). The disciplined rule is to write each section as a self-contained instruction for a discrete scope of work, to reference standards by full designation and edition (for example ASTM C150/C150M, AASHTO T 99, ACI 318), and to avoid restating requirements that belong in another section or in Division 01. Use performance specifications where the result matters and the method is the contractor's choice, and prescriptive specifications where the method or material must be controlled, and state which one each section is. The trap is a section that mixes performance and prescriptive language inconsistently, or that references a standard without the edition, because the standard changes between editions and the contractor can bid to a different version than the designer intended.

### Reconcile Plans and Specifications and Resolve Conflicts Before Bid

A conflict between the drawings and the specifications is a defect in the contract documents, and the disciplined rule is to resolve every conflict before the bid, through a coordinated review by the design team and, where needed, an addendum. Common conflicts include a plan note that specifies a different material than the specification section, a dimension that contradicts a detail, a general note that conflicts with a specific note, and a specification that calls for a product no longer manufactured. Run a cross-discipline coordination check: civil grading against structural foundations, utility invert elevations against building connections, structural openings against mechanical duct sizes, and specification references against the drawings. Where a conflict is found after bid, resolve it through the contract's order of precedence and a formal interpretation, never by an informal field direction that leaves the record ambiguous. The trap is assuming the contractor will figure out the intent, because the contractor is entitled to rely on the documents as written and to price them as written, and an unresolved conflict becomes a change order.

### Apply the Order of Precedence and Manage Notes on Plans

The contract documents define an order of precedence that controls when documents conflict, and AIA A201 and EJCDC general conditions typically establish a hierarchy, often placing the specifications above the drawings or stating that the more stringent requirement governs. Read the actual precedence clause in the general and supplementary conditions, because it varies by contract and the supplementary conditions can reorder it, and never assume a default. General notes on a plan sheet are part of the drawings and are subordinate to (or coordinate with) the specifications depending on the precedence clause, and a note that attempts to override a specification may be ineffective or may itself create a conflict. The disciplined rule is to use general notes for information that belongs on the sheet (datum, basis of bearing, abbreviations, sheet-specific conditions) and to avoid restating or contradicting the specifications in notes, because duplicated requirements drift apart during revisions. The trap is a plan note that says "conform to Section 033000" while also restating a requirement with a different value, creating an ambiguity that the precedence clause must then resolve at the contractor's advantage.

### Seal, Sign, Date, and Standardize the Sheets for Contractual Validity

A drawing set issued for construction or permit must bear the professional seal, signature, and date of the licensed engineer or surveyor responsible for that discipline, applied per the licensing board's rules, and the seal attests that the documents were prepared under the responsible charge of a licensed professional. Apply the seal only to sheets within the professional's scope of responsibility, never to sheets outside it, and confirm the licensing jurisdiction's rules because seal requirements vary by state and by document type. Standardize each sheet with a title block (project name, sheet title, sheet number, scale, date, revision block), a north arrow, a graphical scale bar, the datum and basis of bearing, and a legend, because a sheet missing the scale or the north arrow is ambiguous and a sheet missing the revision history is not traceable. The trap is issuing a drawing set with a photocopied or digital seal applied to sheets the professional did not review, which is a false certification and a disciplinary violation, or issuing a set with the revision block blank so the contractor cannot confirm it has the current issue.

## Common Traps

### The Plan Note That Silently Overrides the Specification

A designer adds a note on a plan that specifies a concrete strength or a pipe material different from the specification section, assuming the note clarifies the intent. The mechanism is that the note and the specification are both contract documents but carry different values, and the precedence clause decides which controls. The false signal is that the note looks authoritative on the sheet. The harm is the contractor bidding to the more favorable reading and a change order when the conflict surfaces, or the wrong material installed.

### The Specification That References a Superseded Standard

A specification section references a standard by number without the edition year, or cites an edition that has been superseded, and the contractor bids to the current edition while the designer intended the older one. The mechanism is that standards are revised on cycles and the requirements differ between editions. The false signal is a valid-looking reference. The harm is a product or test method that does not match the design intent, with nonconformance discovered during submittal review or testing.

### The Drawing Set and Project Manual Produced Without Cross-Check

The civil team produces the drawings and the specifications team produces the project manual, and neither cross-checks the other, so a structure is detailed but not specified, or a material is specified but not shown. The mechanism is that the two artifacts are produced separately and integrated only by assumption. The false signal is that each artifact looks complete on its own. The harm is a gap the contractor prices conservatively or claims as a change, and a bid that does not reflect the integrated design.

### The Seal Applied to Sheets Not Under Responsible Charge

A professional applies a digital seal to an entire drawing set, including sheets outside the professional's scope or sheets not actually reviewed, to expedite issuance. The mechanism is that the seal is a certification of responsible charge, and applying it broadly is a false attestation. The false signal is a fully sealed set. The harm is a disciplinary violation, invalid documents, and personal liability for sheets the professional did not control.

## Self-Check

- Is the drawing set organized with a title sheet, index, general notes, legend, and consistent numbering, and is the project manual organized by CSI MasterFormat with Division 00, 01, and the technical sections?
- Is each specification section written in CSI SectionFormat three-part structure (Part 1 General, Part 2 Products, Part 3 Execution), with standards referenced by full designation and edition, and without duplicating Division 01 requirements?
- Have I run a cross-discipline coordination check (civil grading vs. structural foundations, utility inverts vs. building connections, plan notes vs. specification sections) and resolved every conflict before bid, issuing addenda where needed?
- Have I read the actual order-of-precedence clause in the general and supplementary conditions, and do my plan notes avoid restating or contradicting specification requirements in ways the precedence clause must later resolve?
- Does every sheet in the issued set bear the correct professional seal, signature, and date applied only to sheets under the professional's responsible charge, with title block, north arrow, graphical scale, datum, and revision history complete?
- Have I confirmed that no specification references a superseded standard, that every drawn element is specified and every specified element is drawn, and that the documents are internally consistent as a single contract instrument?
