# Section 121 Exclusion

Section 121 allows a taxpayer to exclude up to 250,000 dollars (single) or 500,000 dollars (married filing jointly) of gain on the sale of a principal residence. This is one of the most valuable capital gains exclusions in the tax code, potentially eliminating all federal tax on a home sale. But the exclusion is not automatic; it requires meeting the ownership and use tests, and it has been narrowed by provisions that tax non-qualified use and recapture depreciation. An agent who assumes the exclusion applies without verifying the tests, or who ignores the non-qualified use and depreciation rules, will overstate the benefit and mislead the seller.

Agents commonly miss that the 500,000 dollar amount requires both spouses to meet the use test (not just one), that non-qualified use after 2008 reduces the exclusion, that depreciation recapture on rental use is not excluded, and that the exclusion can generally be used only once every two years. The harm is a seller who expects a full exclusion but faces tax on a portion of the gain.

This skill covers the Section 121 exclusion under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the exclusion rules have specific requirements and limitations, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Determine The Exclusion Amount

The Section 121 exclusion allows a taxpayer to exclude up to 250,000 dollars of gain (single or married filing separately) or 500,000 dollars of gain (married filing jointly) on the sale of a principal residence. For married filing jointly, both spouses must meet the ownership and use tests (or one spouse meets the ownership test and the other meets the use test, with some exceptions for a deceased spouse). The 500,000 dollar amount is not automatic for married couples; it requires both spouses to qualify.

Determine the exclusion amount based on filing status and qualification. For a single filer, the exclusion is up to 250,000 dollars. For a married filing jointly couple, the exclusion is up to 500,000 dollars, provided both spouses meet the use test (lived in the home for 2 of the last 5 years) and at least one meets the ownership test. If only one spouse meets the use test, the exclusion is limited to 250,000 dollars (the qualifying spouse's portion). Compute the gain on the sale (sale price minus adjusted basis minus selling costs). Apply the exclusion to the eligible gain. Any gain above the exclusion amount is long-term capital gain taxed at the 0/15/20% rate.

### Apply The Ownership And Use Tests

The taxpayer must have owned and used the home as a principal residence for at least 2 of the 5 years immediately preceding the sale. The 2 years do not need to be consecutive; they can be aggregated over the 5-year period. The ownership test (the taxpayer owned the home) and the use test (the taxpayer lived in the home) are separate and both must be met. The tests are measured over the 5-year period ending on the sale date.

Verify the ownership and use tests. The taxpayer must have owned the home for at least 2 years and lived in it as a principal residence for at least 2 years during the 5-year period before the sale. The 2 years can be cumulative (for example, living in the home for 6 months at a time over 4 years). Track the ownership and use periods carefully. If the taxpayer does not meet either test, the exclusion is not available, and the full gain is taxable (unless a partial exclusion applies under a special circumstance). Document the ownership and use history.

### Handle Non-Qualified Use After 2008

Non-qualified use is any period after 2008 during which the home was not used as the taxpayer's principal residence. The gain attributable to non-qualified use is not eligible for the exclusion. This rule prevents taxpayers from moving out, renting the home, and then claiming the full exclusion years later. The non-qualified use gain is computed as a fraction: the non-qualified use period divided by the total ownership period, applied to the total gain.

Compute the non-qualified use gain. Identify any period after 2008 during which the home was not the principal residence (for example, a rental period after moving out). The non-qualified use gain is the total gain multiplied by the fraction of non-qualified use years over total ownership years. This portion of the gain is taxable and cannot be excluded. For example, a home owned for 10 years with 2 years of non-qualified use (rental after moving out) has 20% of the gain taxed as non-qualified use gain, and the remaining 80% is eligible for the exclusion. Apply the non-qualified use reduction before the exclusion.

### Exclude Depreciation Recapture From The Exclusion

The Section 121 exclusion does not apply to depreciation recapture attributable to periods after May 6, 1997, during which the home was rented or used for business. If the taxpayer rented the home (or used part of it for business), the depreciation claimed during the rental or business use must be recaptured as unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, taxed at up to 25%. This recapture is taxable even if the total gain is below the exclusion amount.

Compute the depreciation recapture separately. Identify any depreciation claimed after May 6, 1997, during rental or business use of the home. This depreciation is recaptured as unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, taxed at up to 25%, and is not excluded by Section 121. For example, a taxpayer who claimed 30,000 dollars of depreciation during a rental period and sells the home for a 200,000 dollar gain (below the 250,000 dollar exclusion) still pays tax on the 30,000 dollars of depreciation recapture at up to 25%, even though the remaining 170,000 dollars is excluded. Apply the recapture before the exclusion.

### Apply The Two-Year Use Rule

The Section 121 exclusion can generally be used only once every two years. A taxpayer who has used the exclusion within the past two years cannot use it again, even if the ownership and use tests are met for the new home. This rule prevents frequent use of the exclusion for multiple home sales. The two-year period is measured from the sale date of the prior excluded home to the sale date of the current home.

Check the two-year use rule. If the taxpayer used the Section 121 exclusion within the past two years (for a prior home sale), the exclusion cannot be used again for the current sale, even if the tests are met. The two-year period is measured between sale dates. If the prior exclusion was more than two years ago, the exclusion is available for the current sale. Track the prior use of the exclusion. This rule applies per taxpayer, so a married couple where only one spouse used the exclusion may still qualify for a partial exclusion on a joint sale.

### Handle Partial Exclusions For Special Circumstances

If the taxpayer does not meet the two-year ownership and use tests, a partial exclusion may be available if the sale is due to a change in employment, health, or unforeseen circumstances (such as divorce, death of a spouse, multiple births, or natural disaster). The partial exclusion is prorated based on the fraction of the two-year period that was met. For example, if the taxpayer lived in the home for 1 year (half of the 2-year requirement) due to a job relocation, the partial exclusion is 50% of the full amount (125,000 dollars for single, 250,000 dollars for married filing jointly).

Determine if a partial exclusion applies. If the sale is due to employment change (the new job is at least 50 miles farther from the old home than the old job was), health (a doctor recommends the move for medical reasons), or unforeseen circumstances (divorce, death, multiple births, natural disaster), a partial exclusion may be available. The partial exclusion is prorated based on the time met. Compute the prorated amount and apply it to the gain. Document the qualifying reason (job relocation letter, medical documentation, divorce decree). Do not assume the full exclusion if the tests are not met; check for partial exclusion eligibility.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The 500000 Dollar Amount Is Automatic For Married Couples

The 500,000 dollar exclusion requires both spouses to meet the use test. If only one spouse meets the use test, the exclusion is limited to 250,000 dollars.

### Ignoring Non-Qualified Use After 2008

Non-qualified use (rental or non-residence use after 2008) reduces the exclusion. The gain attributable to non-qualified use is taxable and cannot be excluded.

### Forgetting Depreciation Recapture Is Not Excluded

Depreciation claimed during rental or business use after May 6, 1997, is recaptured at up to 25% and is not excluded by Section 121, even if the gain is below the exclusion amount.

### Using The Exclusion More Than Once In Two Years

The exclusion can generally be used only once every two years. Using it for a prior sale within two years disqualifies the current sale.

### Failing The Ownership Or Use Test

Both the ownership test (owned for 2 of 5 years) and the use test (lived in for 2 of 5 years) must be met. Failing either test disqualifies the exclusion (unless a partial exclusion applies).

### Overlooking Partial Exclusions For Job Health Or Unforeseen Circumstances

If the tests are not met, a partial exclusion may be available for employment, health, or unforeseen circumstances. Do not assume zero exclusion if the tests are not met.

- [ ] Has the non-qualified use gain been computed for any non-residence period after 2008, reducing the excludable gain?
