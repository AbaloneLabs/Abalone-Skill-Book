# Partial Exclusions and Special Cases

The full Section 121 exclusion (250,000 dollars single, 500,000 dollars married filing jointly) requires 2 years of ownership and use. When the taxpayer cannot meet the 2-year tests, a partial exclusion may still be available if the sale is triggered by employment change, health, or unforeseen circumstances. Additionally, special rules apply for divorce, separation, inheritance, deceased spouses, and members of the military and foreign service. An agent who applies only the full exclusion, or who ignores the special cases, will misstate the benefit for taxpayers with non-standard situations.

Agents commonly miss that the partial exclusion is prorated based on the time met, that the qualifying reasons have specific definitions, that divorce and inheritance have special use-test relief, and that military members get an extended 5-year period. The harm is a taxpayer who assumes zero exclusion on a short sale or a complex situation and overpays tax.

This skill covers partial exclusions and special cases under Section 121 of U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the rules depend on specific facts, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Compute The Partial Exclusion For Premature Sales

If the taxpayer does not meet the 2-year ownership and use tests, a partial exclusion is available if the sale is due to employment change, health, or unforeseen circumstances. The partial exclusion is prorated based on the fraction of the 2-year period that was met. The proration fraction is the shorter of the ownership period or the use period, divided by 2 years (730 days). The partial exclusion is the full exclusion amount multiplied by the proration fraction.

Compute the partial exclusion. Determine the proration fraction: the shorter of the ownership or use period, divided by 730 days. Multiply the full exclusion (250,000 dollars single, 500,000 dollars married filing jointly) by the fraction. For example, a single taxpayer who lived in the home for 1 year (365 days) due to a job relocation has a proration fraction of 365/730 = 50%, and the partial exclusion is 125,000 dollars (50% of 250,000 dollars). The partial exclusion applies to the gain on the sale. Any gain above the partial exclusion is taxable. Document the qualifying reason and the proration computation.

### Apply The Employment Change Safe Harbor

A sale qualifies for the partial exclusion if it is due to a change in employment. The employment change safe harbor requires the new place of employment to be at least 50 miles farther from the home sold than the former place of employment was from the home. The distance test is similar to the moving expense distance test. The employment change can be a new job, a transfer by the same employer, or a change in the primary place of employment for a self-employed individual.

Apply the employment change safe harbor. Measure the distance from the former home to the former job and from the former home to the new job. The new job must be at least 50 miles farther. For a self-employed individual, the primary place of business changes. Document the employment change (offer letter, transfer notice, self-employment records). The employment change must be the primary reason for the sale. If the distance test is not met, the employment change does not qualify, and another reason (health or unforeseen circumstances) must be relied on.

### Apply The Health Safe Harbor

A sale qualifies for the partial exclusion if it is due to health. The health safe harbor requires a doctor to recommend a change in residence for reasons of health, including the health of a family member. Health includes the diagnosis, cure, mitigation, or treatment of a disease, illness, or injury. A doctor's recommendation to move for health reasons (for example, to a warmer climate for arthritis, or to a one-story home for mobility) qualifies.

Apply the health safe harbor. Obtain a doctor's recommendation or letter stating that the move is recommended for health reasons. The recommendation must be for a change in residence, not just a recommendation for treatment. The health reason can be for the taxpayer, a spouse, a child, or another family member living with the taxpayer. Document the health reason (doctor's letter, medical records). If the move is not primarily for health, the health safe harbor does not apply, and another reason must be relied on.

### Apply The Unforeseen Circumstances Safe Harbor

A sale qualifies for the partial exclusion if it is due to unforeseen circumstances. The IRS provides a list of safe-harbor unforeseen circumstances: involuntary conversion (the home is condemned or destroyed), natural or man-made disaster, death of a qualified individual, divorce or legal separation, multiple births from a single pregnancy, and change in employment or self-employment that leaves the taxpayer unable to pay the mortgage and basic living expenses. Events outside this list may qualify if they are unforeseen and the sale is the primary result.

Apply the unforeseen circumstances safe harbor. Determine if the sale is due to one of the listed events (involuntary conversion, disaster, death, divorce, multiple births, unemployment). Document the event (divorce decree, death certificate, insurance claim, unemployment records). For events outside the list, the taxpayer must show the event was unforeseen and was the primary reason for the sale. Routine events (a desire to upgrade, a change in school district, a divorce that was planned for years) do not qualify. The unforeseen circumstances must be genuine and documented.

### Handle Divorce And Separation Special Rules

Divorce and legal separation have special rules for the Section 121 exclusion. If a home is transferred to one spouse incident to a divorce, the recipient spouse is treated as having owned and used the home for the period the other spouse owned and used it. This allows the recipient spouse to meet the ownership and use tests based on the couple's combined history. Additionally, a spouse who is granted use of the home under a divorce decree can count the period of granted use toward the use test.

Apply the divorce and separation special rules. If the home is transferred to one spouse incident to a divorce, the recipient spouse inherits the ownership and use history of the other spouse. This means a spouse who receives the home in a divorce can meet the tests even if they did not personally own or use the home for the full 2 years. Track the ownership and use history through the divorce. The recipient spouse can then sell the home and claim the exclusion based on the combined history. Document the divorce decree and the transfer.

### Handle Inheritance And Deceased Spouse Special Rules

Inheritance and deceased spouse situations have special rules. If a taxpayer inherits a home, the use test is based on the taxpayer's own use, but the ownership test can include the decedent's ownership period (the taxpayer is treated as having owned the home for the decedent's period). For a deceased spouse, the surviving spouse can use the deceased spouse's ownership and use history for up to 2 years after the death, allowing the surviving spouse to claim the 500,000 dollar exclusion if the tests were met before the death.

Apply the inheritance and deceased spouse special rules. For an inherited home, the taxpayer inherits the decedent's ownership period (but not the use period, unless the taxpayer lived in the home). For a deceased spouse, the surviving spouse can claim the 500,000 dollar exclusion for up to 2 years after the death, provided the couple met the tests before the death and the surviving spouse has not remarried. After 2 years, the surviving spouse is limited to the 250,000 dollar single exclusion. Track the ownership and use history through the death and inheritance. Document the death certificate and the estate records.

### Handle Military And Foreign Service Extensions

Members of the uniformed services, Foreign Service, and intelligence community get an extended period for the ownership and use tests. If the taxpayer is on qualified official extended duty, the 5-year period is extended for up to 10 years (the clock is suspended during the duty period). This allows military members and foreign service officers to meet the tests even if they are deployed or stationed abroad for extended periods.

Apply the military and foreign service extension. If the taxpayer is on qualified official extended duty (generally more than 90 days), the 5-year period is suspended for the duty period, up to a maximum of 10 years. This means the ownership and use periods can extend beyond the normal 5-year window. Track the duty periods and the suspension. The extension is critical for military families who are frequently relocated. Document the duty orders and the suspension computation.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Zero Exclusion When The Tests Are Not Met

A partial exclusion may be available for employment, health, or unforeseen circumstances. Do not assume zero exclusion if the tests are not met. Check for partial exclusion eligibility.

### Miscalculating The Proration Fraction

The proration fraction is the shorter of the ownership or use period divided by 730 days. Using the longer period or miscalculating the fraction overstates the partial exclusion.

### Failing The Distance Test For Employment Change

The employment change safe harbor requires the new job to be at least 50 miles farther from the home than the old job. Failing the distance test disqualifies the employment reason.

### Treating Planned Events As Unforeseen Circumstances

A planned divorce, a desire to upgrade, or a change in school district are not unforeseen. Unforeseen circumstances must be genuine and documented.

### Overlooking Divorce And Inheritance Special Rules

Divorce transfers and inherited homes have special ownership and use rules. The recipient can inherit the prior owner's history. Do not apply the standard tests without the special rules.

### Forgetting The Deceased Spouse Two-Year Window

The surviving spouse can claim the 500,000 dollar exclusion for up to 2 years after the death. After 2 years, the exclusion drops to 250,000 dollars.

### Ignoring Military And Foreign Service Extensions

Military members and foreign service officers get an extended 5-year period (up to 10 years) for the ownership and use tests. The clock is suspended during qualified official extended duty.
