# Harvesting In Retirement Accounts

Tax-loss harvesting inside a tax-deferred retirement account (traditional IRA, 401(k), 403(b)) is almost always futile because losses in those accounts provide no current tax deduction. The reason is structural: gains and losses inside a tax-deferred account are not recognized until distribution, and at distribution the entire traditional account balance is taxed as ordinary income with no separate tracking of investment gains or losses. There is no mechanism to deduct a loss inside the account because the account's internal performance is invisible to the tax code. This fundamental reality is widely misunderstood, and agents frequently recommend harvesting inside an IRA as though it produced a benefit identical to a taxable-account harvest.

The judgment problem is that agents conflate the investment action (selling a loser and buying a replacement) with the tax action (realizing a deductible loss). Inside a tax-deferred account, the investment action may be sound for portfolio reasons, but the tax action produces nothing because there is no taxable event until withdrawal. The harm is wasted effort and, worse, the mistaken belief that a retirement-account loss has been captured for tax purposes, leading the taxpayer to forgo harvesting in their taxable account where it would actually help. The narrow exceptions, after-tax basis in a traditional IRA and the qualified distribution loss rules for Roth IRAs, are rarely applicable and have strict requirements that agents routinely misstate. The most common silent failure is advising someone to harvest in a 401(k) or pre-tax IRA as if it were a taxable account, producing zero tax benefit.

This skill applies to evaluating loss harvesting in IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s, and Roth IRAs, the after-tax basis exception, and the comparison of harvesting across account types. It is not tax advice; the after-tax basis and Roth loss rules are narrow, fact-specific, and have changed over time, and outcomes depend on the taxpayer's full account history. Consult a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) before claiming any deductible loss from a retirement account.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Tax-Deferred Account Losses Are Nondeductible

In a traditional IRA, 401(k), 403(b), or other pre-tax retirement account, investment losses are not deductible because the account's internal gains and losses are never recognized for tax purposes. The account is taxed on distribution, at ordinary income rates, on the entire pre-tax balance regardless of how it was invested. Selling a losing position and buying a replacement inside the account changes the holdings but creates no tax event; the loss simply reduces the account balance, which reduces future taxable distributions, but provides no current deduction.

State this clearly to avoid confusion. A $10,000 loss realized inside a traditional IRA does not appear on the tax return, does not offset capital gains, and does not reduce ordinary income. The only effect is that the account balance is $10,000 lower, so future distributions (and future tax) are lower by the tax rate times $10,000, spread over many years. This is not harvesting; it is just a lower balance. Do not present tax-deferred account harvesting as producing a tax benefit.

### Direct Harvesting To The Taxable Account Where It Works

Tax-loss harvesting produces a current deduction only in a taxable account, where realized losses are reported on Schedule D and offset gains and up to $3,000 of ordinary income. When a taxpayer holds both taxable and retirement accounts, the harvesting discipline should focus on the taxable account, where the benefit is real and immediate. The retirement account can be rebalanced for investment reasons, but not for tax-loss harvesting.

Allocate harvesting effort to the taxable account and explain why the retirement account is not a candidate. If the taxpayer has losses in both account types, realize the taxable losses (for the deduction) and rebalance the retirement account as needed for portfolio reasons, without expecting a tax benefit. The common error is to harvest in the retirement account because it has a larger loss, when the taxable account's smaller loss is the one that produces a deduction.

### Understand The After-Tax Basis Exception For Traditional IRAs

A narrow exception allows a deductible loss on a traditional IRA, but only when the taxpayer has after-tax basis (nondeductible contributions) in the IRA, and only when the entire traditional IRA is distributed and the total distributions are less than the total after-tax basis. Under IRC Section 72, if the taxpayer receives less than their basis in nondeductible contributions across all their traditional IRAs, the difference is a miscellaneous deduction (not subject to the 2% floor) reported on Schedule A. This requires emptying all traditional IRAs and having the total value fall below the after-tax basis.

Recognize how narrow this exception is. It applies only to taxpayers with nondeductible contributions (Form 8606 basis), requires distributing the entire traditional IRA balance across all accounts (the aggregation rule treats all traditional IRAs as one), and produces a loss only if the market value has declined below the after-tax contributions. Most taxpayers with pre-tax IRAs have no after-tax basis and cannot use this rule. Even those with basis rarely want to empty their entire IRA to capture a loss. Document the basis via Form 8606 and confirm the aggregation requirement before claiming this loss.

### Apply The Roth IRA Loss Rules With The QD Requirement

A Roth IRA loss is deductible only if the taxpayer distributes the entire Roth IRA balance and the total distributions are less than the total basis (contributions and conversions). The loss is a miscellaneous itemized deduction, but only if the distribution is a qualified distribution (the account has been open at least 5 years and the taxpayer is 59.5, disabled, or a first-time homebuyer). Non-qualified distributions that produce a loss are not deductible. The aggregation rule treats all Roth IRAs as one for this calculation.

Clarify the double restriction: the Roth must be fully distributed, and the distribution must be qualified. For most Roth owners, the account has grown beyond contributions, so distributing everything produces a gain, not a loss. The loss case arises only when a Roth funded with contributions has declined in value below the contribution basis, which is uncommon. Claiming a Roth loss requires emptying all Roth accounts and meeting the qualified distribution test, which most taxpayers will not do. Do not present Roth loss harvesting as a practical strategy without these conditions.

### Avoid Wash Sales Between Taxable And Retirement Accounts

A subtle but critical interaction: if a taxpayer harvests a loss in their taxable account and the replacement purchase occurs in their IRA (because of dividend reinvestment or an intentional buy), the wash sale rule disallows the taxable loss permanently. Unlike a normal wash sale, where the disallowed loss is added to the replacement's basis, an IRA wash sale has no basis to adjust (the IRA tracks basis at the account level, not the security level), so the loss is destroyed with no recovery.

Treat any IRA purchase of a substantially identical security within 61 days of a taxable harvest as a catastrophic outcome. Before harvesting in a taxable account, check whether the same or a substantially identical security is held in any IRA and whether dividend reinvestment is active there. Disable IRA reinvestment for the harvested security for the full 61-day window. This is the most important cross-account wash sale check, because the IRA context converts a deferral into a permanent loss.

### Compare Account Types Before Recommending Where To Harvest

When a taxpayer holds multiple account types, the harvesting decision should compare the tax treatment of each. Taxable accounts allow current loss deductions (Schedule D, up to $3,000 ordinary offset, carryforward). Traditional pre-tax IRAs and 401(k)s allow no loss deduction. Traditional IRAs with after-tax basis allow a loss only on full distribution below basis. Roth IRAs allow a loss only on full qualified distribution below basis. The taxable account is almost always the correct location for harvesting.

Present the comparison explicitly. If the taxpayer asks about harvesting in their 401(k), explain that the 401(k) loss is not deductible and redirect to the taxable account. If they have a traditional IRA with nondeductible contributions, evaluate the after-tax basis exception only if they are willing to empty all traditional IRAs. Frame the decision around where the tax code recognizes the loss, not where the largest investment loss sits.

### Rebalance Retirement Accounts For Investment Reasons, Not Tax

Retirement accounts can and should be rebalanced for portfolio reasons, such as maintaining target allocation, reducing concentration risk, or adjusting risk as the taxpayer ages. These transactions have no tax cost inside a tax-deferred account (no realization on trade), which is an advantage: rebalancing is tax-free in a traditional IRA or 401(k). The mistake is conflating this tax-free rebalancing with tax-loss harvesting.

Separate the two activities. Rebalance the retirement account freely for portfolio management, because there is no tax friction. Harvest losses in the taxable account for tax benefit, because that is where the code recognizes losses. Do not describe a retirement-account rebalance as harvesting; it is portfolio management, and claiming a tax benefit from it misleads the taxpayer.

## Common Traps

### Recommending Harvesting Inside A Pre-Tax IRA Or 401(k)

The trap is treating a tax-deferred account like a taxable account and expecting a loss deduction. Losses inside pre-tax accounts are not deductible. Redirect harvesting to the taxable account.

### Confusing Account Rebalancing With Tax-Loss Harvesting

Rebalancing inside an IRA has no tax cost but also no tax benefit. The trap is calling it harvesting. Rebalance for portfolio reasons; harvest for tax reasons only in taxable accounts.

### Claiming A Traditional IRA Loss Without After-Tax Basis

The after-tax basis exception requires nondeductible contributions (Form 8606) and full distribution of all traditional IRAs. The trap is claiming a loss without basis or without emptying the accounts. Verify basis and the aggregation rule.

### Overlooking The Qualified Distribution Requirement For Roth Losses

Roth losses are deductible only for qualified distributions (5-year, 59.5). The trap is claiming a Roth loss on a non-qualified distribution. Confirm the qualified status first.

### Triggering An IRA Wash Sale That Permanently Destroys A Taxable Loss

An IRA purchase of a substantially identical security within 61 days of a taxable harvest permanently loses the deduction. The trap is not checking IRA accounts and reinvestments. Check all IRAs before harvesting.

### Assuming A Larger Retirement Loss Is Worth Harvesting

The size of the investment loss is irrelevant if it is in a tax-deferred account. The trap is harvesting where the loss is largest rather than where it is deductible. Harvest in the taxable account.

### Forgetting The Aggregation Rule For IRA Loss Calculations

All traditional IRAs (or all Roth IRAs) are treated as one account for the loss calculation. The trap is computing the loss on a single IRA in isolation. Aggregate all accounts of the same type.

### Misstating That Roth Losses Are Ordinary Deductions

Roth losses are miscellaneous itemized deductions (not subject to the 2% floor), not capital losses, and only for qualified distributions. The trap is treating them as Schedule D capital losses. Report on Schedule A as a miscellaneous deduction.
