# Substantial Omission Threshold

The substantial omission threshold under Section 6501(e) extends the assessment statute from three years to six years when a taxpayer omits from gross income an amount exceeding 25 percent of the gross income stated on the return. This is a high-stakes determination: a finding that the threshold is met gives the IRS an additional three years to assess additional tax, penalties, and interest, while a finding that it is not met closes the year at three years. The judgment problem is that the threshold turns on technical definitions of "omission," "gross income stated," and the treatment of basis overstatements, and the rules changed with the 2015 Bipartisan Budget Act (BBA), which extended the six-year period to cases where a taxpayer overstates basis (thereby understating gain). An agent who misapplies the threshold either advises a client that a year is closed when the IRS can still assess (exposing the client to a surprise assessment) or treats a year as open when it is closed (forfeiting a valid statute-of-limitations defense).

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the 25-percent threshold is measured against the gross income stated on the return (the total income reported), not the adjusted gross income or the taxable income, so a large omission may not meet the threshold if the reported gross income is large. Second, the omission is generally computed gross (the total omitted amount), but certain items (such as a sale of property where the basis is omitted) are treated differently. Third, the 2015 BBA amendment extended the six-year period to overstated basis, but only for basis overstatements that result in an omission of income, and the amendment has been the subject of conflicting case law. Fourth, adequate disclosure of the omitted item on the return or in a statement prevents the six-year period, even if the item was later adjusted. Fifth, the threshold is applied per return, not per item, so multiple omissions are aggregated. Sixth, certain items (such as pass-through items from a partnership or an S corporation) are subject to special rules under the partnership audit regime.

This skill covers the substantial omission threshold and the six-year assessment period under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Measure The 25-Percent Threshold Against Gross Income Stated

The 25-percent threshold is measured against the gross income stated on the return, which is the total gross income reported by the taxpayer before any adjustments. The omission must exceed 25 percent of this amount to trigger the six-year period. For example, if the taxpayer reported gross income of USD 200,000 and omitted USD 45,000, the omission is 22.5 percent, which does not meet the threshold, so the three-year period applies. If the taxpayer omitted USD 55,000, the omission is 27.5 percent, which meets the threshold, so the six-year period applies. The measurement against gross income stated (rather than adjusted gross income or taxable income) means that a large reported income base can absorb a significant omission without triggering the threshold.

Measure the omission against the gross income stated. Identify the gross income reported on the return (the total income before adjustments), and compute the omitted amount. Divide the omission by the gross income stated to determine the percentage. If the omission exceeds 25 percent, the six-year period applies. If the omission is 25 percent or less, the three-year period applies. The measurement is sensitive to the gross income base, so a taxpayer with a large income base may omit a significant amount without triggering the threshold. The agent should compute the percentage carefully, because the threshold is a bright line that determines whether the IRS has three or six years to assess.

### Apply The BBA Amendment For Overstated Basis

The 2015 BBA amendment extended the six-year period to cases where a taxpayer overstates basis in property, which has the effect of omitting income from the sale of the property. Before the amendment, the six-year period applied only to omitted income items, and a taxpayer who reported a sale but overstated the basis (thereby understating the gain) was generally within the three-year period, based on the Supreme Court's decision in United States v. Home Concrete & Steel (2012). The BBA amendment overrode Home Concrete and extended the six-year period to basis overstatements, effective for returns filed after the enactment date (November 2015). The amendment applies to sales of property where the basis is overstated, reducing the reported gain.

Apply the BBA amendment for overstated basis. Determine whether the taxpayer overstated basis in a property sale, and compute the amount of the overstatement. If the overstatement (treated as an omission) exceeds 25 percent of the gross income stated on the return, the six-year period applies. The amendment is effective for returns filed after November 2015, so for earlier returns, the Home Concrete rule (three-year period for basis overstatements) may apply. The agent should confirm the filing date and the applicable law, because the amendment changed the treatment of basis overstatements. The BBA amendment is a significant expansion of the six-year period, so the agent should analyze basis overstatements carefully.

### Evaluate Adequate Disclosure As A Safe Harbor

Adequate disclosure of an item on the return or in a statement attached to the return prevents the six-year period, even if the item is later adjusted. The disclosure must be sufficient to apprise the IRS of the nature and the amount of the item, and it must be made on the return or in a statement filed with the return. The disclosure rules are detailed in Treasury Regulation Section 1.6501(e)-1 and the IRS guidance (such as Revenue Procedure 2015-20 for the basis-overstatement disclosure). If the item is adequately disclosed, the three-year period applies, even if the IRS later adjusts the item. The disclosure safe harbor is valuable for taxpayers who report complex transactions (such as partnership distributions or property sales with uncertain basis).

Evaluate the adequate disclosure safe harbor. Determine whether the omitted item or the basis overstatement was disclosed on the return or in a statement, and whether the disclosure was sufficient to apprise the IRS of the nature and the amount. If the disclosure was adequate, the three-year period applies, even if the item is later adjusted. The disclosure should identify the item, the amount, and the basis (for property sales), and it should be attached to the return. The disclosure safe harbor is a powerful protection, so the agent should review the return and any attached statements to determine whether the disclosure was adequate. A taxpayer who is uncertain about an item should consider disclosing it to secure the three-year period.

### Aggregate Multiple Omissions Per Return

The 25-percent threshold is applied per return, not per item, so multiple omissions are aggregated. If a taxpayer omits income from multiple sources (such as a missing W-2 and a missing 1099), the omissions are added together, and the total is measured against the gross income stated. Similarly, if a taxpayer has multiple basis overstatements, they are aggregated. The aggregation means that several smaller omissions can combine to exceed the threshold, triggering the six-year period. The agent should identify all omissions and basis overstatements on the return and aggregate them.

Aggregate the omissions per return. Identify all omitted income items and all basis overstatements on the return, and add them together. Measure the total against the gross income stated. If the total exceeds 25 percent, the six-year period applies. The aggregation can catch taxpayers who have multiple smaller omissions that individually do not meet the threshold but together do. The agent should not analyze each omission in isolation, because the threshold is applied to the aggregate. The aggregation is a common trap, because a taxpayer may believe that each omission is below the threshold, while the aggregate exceeds it.

### Address Pass-Through Items And The Partnership Audit Regime

Omissions and basis overstatements from pass-through entities (partnerships and S corporations) are subject to special rules under the partnership audit regime (the BBA centralized partnership audit rules, effective for partnership tax years beginning after 2017). Under the BBA, partnership items are audited at the entity level, and the assessment of partnership-related omissions is governed by the partnership audit regime, not the individual partner's return. For an S corporation, the omission flows through to the shareholder's return, and the six-year period is applied at the shareholder level. The pass-through treatment means that the six-year period may apply at the entity level (for a partnership) or at the owner level (for an S corporation).

Address the pass-through items. For a partnership under the BBA, the omission is audited at the entity level, and the assessment is governed by the partnership audit regime (with the partnership's assessment statute, not the partner's). For an S corporation, the omission flows through to the shareholder's return, and the six-year period is applied at the shareholder level, based on the shareholder's gross income stated. The agent should determine whether the omission is a pass-through item and apply the correct regime. The pass-through treatment is complex, because the same omission may be subject to different statutes at the entity and the owner levels, and the agent should coordinate the analysis.

### Document The Threshold Analysis For Each Year

The substantial omission threshold is a fact-intensive determination, and the agent should document the analysis for each year. The documentation should include the gross income stated on the return, the omitted amount (or the basis overstatement), the percentage, the adequate disclosure analysis, and the applicable assessment period (three or six years). The documentation is essential because the taxpayer may need to prove the statute of limitations if the IRS attempts to assess after the three-year period.

Document the threshold analysis. For each year, record the gross income stated, the omitted amount (with a breakdown of each omission and basis overstatement), the percentage, the adequate disclosure analysis (whether the item was disclosed and whether the disclosure was sufficient), and the applicable assessment period. Maintain the documentation with the return and the proof of filing, because the taxpayer may need to raise the statute of limitations as a defense. The documentation should be prepared contemporaneously, because a reconstructed analysis is less persuasive. The threshold analysis is the basis for the statute-of-limitations defense, so it should be thorough and well-documented.

## Common Traps

### Measuring The Threshold Against Adjusted Gross Income

The symptom is an agent who measures the 25-percent threshold against the adjusted gross income or the taxable income. The trap is that the threshold is measured against the gross income stated (the total income before adjustments), so a larger base may absorb the omission. The direction is to measure the omission against the gross income stated on the return.

### Overlooking The BBA Basis-Overstatement Amendment

The symptom is an agent who applies the three-year period to a basis overstatement on a return filed after November 2015. The trap is that the BBA amendment extended the six-year period to basis overstatements, so the IRS has six years to assess. The direction is to apply the BBA amendment for basis overstatements on post-November 2015 returns.

### Failing To Claim Adequate Disclosure

The symptom is a taxpayer who disclosed an item on the return but the agent does not claim the adequate disclosure safe harbor. The trap is that adequate disclosure prevents the six-year period, so the three-year period applies. The direction is to review the return and the attached statements for adequate disclosure and to claim the safe harbor.

### Analyzing Omissions Individually Rather Than Aggregating

The symptom is an agent who analyzes each omission separately and concludes that none meets the 25-percent threshold. The trap is that the omissions are aggregated per return, so the total may exceed the threshold. The direction is to aggregate all omissions and basis overstatements and to measure the total against the gross income stated.

### Applying The Individual Statute To A Partnership Omission

The symptom is an agent who applies the individual partner's six-year period to a partnership omission. The trap is that partnership omissions are audited at the entity level under the BBA, with the partnership's assessment statute. The direction is to apply the partnership audit regime to partnership omissions and the individual statute to non-partnership omissions.

### Assuming The Threshold Is A Net Test

The symptom is an agent who nets the omitted income against offsetting items before measuring the threshold. The trap is that the omission is generally computed gross, so the offsetting items do not reduce the omission. The direction is to compute the omission gross (the total omitted amount) unless a specific rule provides for netting.
