# Three vs Six Year Assessment

The statute of limitations on assessment is the deadline by which the IRS must assess additional tax, and it is the taxpayer's primary protection against perpetual tax exposure. Under Section 6501, the general rule is three years from the date the return was filed, but a six-year period applies when the taxpayer omits from gross income an amount exceeding 25 percent of the gross income stated on the return. The judgment problem is that the three-year and six-year periods turn on technical definitions of "filed," "omission," and "gross income stated," and a misapplication can lead an agent to declare a year closed when the IRS can still assess (exposing the taxpayer to a surprise assessment) or to treat a year as open when it is closed (forfeiting the statute-of-limitations defense). The period can also be extended by agreement (Form 872), tolled by certain events (such as an offer in compromise or a bankruptcy), and affected by the filing of an amended return.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, a return filed before the due date is deemed filed on the due date, so the three-year period runs from the due date, not the actual filing date. Second, the six-year period under the old rule applied only to omitted income, but the 2015 BBA amendment extended it to overstated basis (which reduces reported income), so a substantial overstatement of basis can trigger the six-year period. Third, the six-year period requires a 25-percent omission measured against the gross income stated on the return, and the omission is computed gross (not net of offsetting items) in most cases. Fourth, a Form 872 agreement to extend the assessment period can extend it indefinitely (or to a specified date), and the taxpayer must consent. Fifth, the filing of an amended return does not restart the assessment period for the original return (though it may start a new period for the refund claim). Sixth, a fraudulent return or a failure to file leaves the assessment period open indefinitely.

This skill covers the three-year and six-year assessment statutes of limitations under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Compute The General Three-Year Assessment Period

The general rule under Section 6501(a) is that the IRS must assess additional tax within three years after the return was filed. A return filed before the due date (including extensions) is deemed filed on the due date, so the three-year period runs from the due date. A return filed after the due date (a late return) is filed on the actual date of filing, and the three-year period runs from that date. If no return is filed, the assessment period never begins to run (it is open indefinitely). The three-year period is the default, and the taxpayer should track it to know when a year closes.

Compute the three-year assessment period. Identify the return's filing date, determine the deemed filing date (the due date if filed early, including extensions if the return was filed within the extension period), and add three years. If the return was filed late, use the actual filing date. The three-year date is the deadline for the IRS to assess additional tax, unless an exception (the six-year period, fraud, a Form 872 extension, or a tolling event) applies. The taxpayer should maintain the return and the proof of filing (such as the certified mail receipt or the e-file confirmation) to establish the filing date, because the burden of proving the statute of limitations is on the taxpayer if the IRS claims the return was filed later.

### Apply The Six-Year Period For Substantial Omissions

Under Section 6501(e), the assessment period is extended to six years if the taxpayer omits from gross income an amount exceeding 25 percent of the gross income stated on the return. The omission was traditionally limited to omitted income items (such as a missing W-2 or 1099), but the 2015 BBA amendment extended the six-year period to cases where the taxpayer overstates basis, which has the effect of omitting income. The 25-percent threshold is measured against the gross income stated on the return, and the omission is computed gross (the total omitted amount) in most cases. The six-year period does not apply if the omission is disclosed adequately on the return or in a statement attached to the return.

Apply the six-year period for substantial omissions. Determine whether the taxpayer omitted income exceeding 25 percent of the gross income stated on the return, or overstated basis in a way that reduced reported income by more than 25 percent. If so, the six-year period applies, and the IRS has six years to assess. The omission is measured against the gross income stated on the return (the total income reported), and the 25-percent threshold is a bright line. If the omission was adequately disclosed (such as on a Form 8938 or in a disclosure statement), the six-year period does not apply, even if the item was later adjusted. The six-year period is a major extension, so the agent should analyze whether the omission meets the threshold before advising that a year is closed at three years.

### Account For Extensions And Tolling Events

The assessment period can be extended by agreement (Form 872) or tolled by certain events. A Form 872 agreement extends the assessment period to a specified date (or indefinitely, if the taxpayer consents), and it is often used during an examination to give the IRS more time. The taxpayer must sign the Form 872, and the extension is valid only if the taxpayer consents. Tolling events include the filing of an offer in compromise (which tolls the assessment period during the offer's pendency plus one year), a bankruptcy proceeding (which tolls the assessment period during the automatic stay plus 60 days), and a request for a taxpayer assistance order. The tolling extends the assessment period by the duration of the tolling event plus the specified grace period.

Account for the extensions and the tolling events. If the taxpayer signed a Form 872, determine the extended assessment date. If the taxpayer filed an offer in compromise or a bankruptcy petition, determine the tolling period and add it to the assessment date. The extensions and the tolling events can extend the assessment period significantly, so the agent should obtain the IRS account transcript to confirm the current assessment statute expiration date (ASED). The taxpayer is not obligated to sign a Form 872, so the decision to extend should be evaluated based on the examination's status and the taxpayer's interest in closing the year.

### Understand The Effect Of Amended Returns On The Assessment Period

The filing of an amended return (Form 1040-X) does not restart the assessment period for the original return. The original return's assessment period continues to run from its filing date, and the amended return is treated as a claim for refund (with its own refund statute under Section 6511). However, if the amended return reports additional tax (an upward amendment), the IRS can assess the additional tax, but the assessment is still subject to the original return's assessment period. The amended return does not extend the assessment period for the original return, so the IRS cannot use an amended return to revive a closed year.

Understand the effect of the amended return. The original return's assessment period runs from its filing date, and the amended return does not restart it. If the amended return claims a refund, the refund is subject to the Section 6511 statute (the later of three years from filing or two years from payment). If the amended return reports additional tax, the IRS can assess it within the original return's assessment period, but the amended return does not extend the period. The agent should not assume that an amended return restarts the assessment period, because it does not, and the IRS cannot assess additional tax for a closed year based on an amended return.

### Distinguish The Assessment Statute From The Collection Statute

The assessment statute of limitations (Section 6501) is the deadline for the IRS to assess tax, and it is separate from the collection statute of limitations (Section 6502), which is the deadline for the IRS to collect an assessed tax. The collection statute is generally 10 years from the date of assessment, and it can be extended by certain events (such as an offer in compromise, a bankruptcy, or an installment agreement). The two statutes are independent, so a year may be closed for assessment (the IRS cannot assess additional tax) but open for collection (the IRS can still collect an assessed balance).

Distinguish the assessment statute from the collection statute. The assessment statute (Section 6501) governs the IRS's right to assess additional tax, and the collection statute (Section 6502) governs the IRS's right to collect an assessed tax. The agent should track both, because a year may be closed for assessment but open for collection. The collection statute is 10 years from the assessment date, and it can be extended or tolled by certain events. The agent should obtain the IRS account transcript to confirm both the ASED and the collection statute expiration date (CSED), because the two statutes determine the taxpayer's exposure to additional assessment and to collection.

### Track The Statute For Each Year And Document The Computation

The statute of limitations is determined year by year, and the agent should track the ASED for each tax year. The tracking should include the return's filing date, the deemed filing date, the three-year and six-year dates, any Form 872 extensions, and any tolling events. The computation should be documented, because the taxpayer may need to prove the statute of limitations if the IRS attempts to assess after the period has expired. The documentation should include the proof of filing (the certified mail receipt or the e-file confirmation) and the IRS account transcript.

Track the statute for each year and document the computation. Maintain a statute-of-limitations log for each tax year, with the filing date, the deemed filing date, the three-year and six-year dates, any extensions, and any tolling events. Obtain the IRS account transcript to confirm the current ASED, because the IRS's records may differ from the taxpayer's. If the IRS attempts to assess after the period has expired, the taxpayer can raise the statute of limitations as a defense, but the burden of proof is on the taxpayer. The documentation should be maintained for at least seven years (to cover the six-year period plus a margin), because the statute of limitations is the taxpayer's primary protection against perpetual tax exposure.

## Common Traps

### Counting The Three-Year Period From The Actual Filing Date For An Early Return

The symptom is a taxpayer who filed the 2020 return on February 1, 2021, and believes the three-year period expires on February 1, 2024. The trap is that the return is deemed filed on the due date (April 15, 2021, or May 17, 2021, for the 2020 tax year), so the three-year period expires on that date, not February 1. The direction is to use the deemed filing date (the due date) for an early-filed return.

### Overlooking The Six-Year Period For Overstated Basis

The symptom is a taxpayer who reported a sale with an overstated basis that reduced the gain by more than 25 percent of gross income, and who believes the three-year period applies. The trap is that the 2015 BBA amendment extended the six-year period to overstated basis, so the IRS has six years to assess. The direction is to analyze whether the overstatement of basis meets the 25-percent threshold and to apply the six-year period.

### Assuming An Amended Return Restarts The Assessment Period

The symptom is a taxpayer who files an amended return and believes the original return's assessment period restarts. The trap is that the amended return does not restart the assessment period for the original return, so the IRS cannot use the amended return to revive a closed year. The direction is to understand that the original return's assessment period runs from its filing date and is not affected by the amended return.

### Forgetting A Form 872 Extension

The symptom is a taxpayer who signed a Form 872 during an examination and forgets the extension. The trap is that the extension extends the assessment period to the specified date, so the IRS can assess after the original three-year period. The direction is to obtain the IRS account transcript to confirm the current ASED and to account for any Form 872 extensions.

### Confusing The Assessment Statute With The Collection Statute

The symptom is a taxpayer who believes a year is "closed" because the 10-year collection statute has expired. The trap is that the assessment statute (three or six years) and the collection statute (10 years) are separate, so a year may be closed for assessment but open for collection. The direction is to track both statutes and to confirm the ASED and the CSED on the IRS account transcript.

### Failing To Maintain Proof Of Filing

The symptom is a taxpayer who cannot prove the return was filed by a certain date. The trap is that the burden of proving the statute of limitations is on the taxpayer, so the IRS may claim the return was filed later, extending the assessment period. The direction is to maintain the proof of filing (certified mail receipt or e-file confirmation) for at least seven years.
