# Fraud and No Limitation

Fraud is the most severe exception to the statute of limitations on assessment. Under Section 6501(c), if a taxpayer files a false or fraudulent return with the intent to evade tax, or fails to file a return, the IRS can assess the tax at any time — there is no statute of limitations. The judgment problem is that fraud requires a specific intent to evade a tax known to be owing, which is a higher standard than negligence or a mere understatement, and the IRS bears the burden of proving fraud by clear and convincing evidence. An agent who conflates negligence with fraud, or who advises a client that a year is closed when fraud keeps it open, exposes the client to perpetual assessment exposure (and the client to the 75-percent civil fraud penalty and potential criminal prosecution). Conversely, an agent who overstates the fraud risk may cause unnecessary panic or a premature concession. The no-limitation rule for failure to file is separate: if no return is filed, the assessment period never begins to run, regardless of intent.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the IRS must prove fraud by clear and convincing evidence, which is a higher burden than the preponderance standard for negligence, and the fraud must be proven for each year at issue. Second, the civil fraud penalty under Section 6663 is 75 percent of the underpayment attributable to fraud, and it is separate from the tax and the interest. Third, the fraud penalty applies to the portion of the underpayment attributable to fraud, so if only part of the underpayment is fraudulent, the penalty applies to that part (and the rest may be subject to the accuracy-related penalty). Fourth, the no-limitation rule for a fraudulent return applies only to the fraudulent return, not to a subsequent non-fraudulent amended return. Fifth, the failure-to-file no-limitation rule applies even without fraud, so a taxpayer who never files a return is exposed indefinitely. Sixth, the fraud determination affects the collection statute, the discharge of tax in bankruptcy, and the offer-in-compromise eligibility.

This skill covers the fraud exception and the no-limitation rule under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Fraud Standard And The IRS Burden Of Proof

Fraud for purposes of Section 6501(c) requires a specific intent to evade a tax known to be owing, and the IRS must prove it by clear and convincing evidence. The clear-and-convincing standard is higher than the preponderance standard (which applies to negligence), and it requires the IRS to show that the taxpayer intended to evade the tax, not merely that the taxpayer made an error. The IRS proves fraud through "badges of fraud," which are circumstantial indicators of intent, such as understating income, keeping two sets of books, claiming false deductions, concealing assets, failing to file, and dealing in cash. No single badge is conclusive, but the cumulative pattern supports the fraud finding. The fraud must be proven for each year at issue, and the IRS cannot extrapolate fraud from one year to another without evidence.

Understand the fraud standard and the burden of proof. The IRS must prove fraud by clear and convincing evidence, which requires a showing of specific intent to evade a tax known to be owing. The agent should evaluate the badges of fraud (understating income, false deductions, concealed assets, cash dealings, failure to file) to assess the fraud risk, but the agent should not conclude fraud lightly, because the standard is high and the consequences are severe. If the badges of fraud are present, the agent should advise the client of the no-limitation rule and the fraud penalty, and the client should consult a qualified tax professional and, if appropriate, a criminal tax attorney. The fraud standard is distinct from negligence, and the agent should not conflate the two.

### Distinguish The Civil Fraud Penalty From Negligence

The civil fraud penalty under Section 6663 is 75 percent of the portion of the underpayment attributable to fraud, and it is separate from the tax and the interest. The accuracy-related penalty under Section 6662 is 20 percent (or 40 percent for gross valuation misstatements) of the underpayment attributable to negligence or a substantial understatement, and it is the lesser penalty. The two penalties are mutually exclusive for the same underpayment: if the fraud penalty applies to a portion, the accuracy-related penalty does not apply to that portion, but the accuracy-related penalty may apply to the non-fraudulent portion. The distinction matters because the fraud penalty is nearly four times the negligence penalty, and it is not dischargeable in bankruptcy.

Distinguish the civil fraud penalty from negligence. If the underpayment is attributable to fraud (specific intent to evade), the 75-percent fraud penalty applies to the fraudulent portion. If the underpayment is attributable to negligence or a substantial understatement (without specific intent), the 20-percent (or 40-percent) accuracy-related penalty applies. The agent should evaluate the intent to determine the applicable penalty, and the client should be advised of the severe consequences of a fraud finding. The fraud penalty is not dischargeable in bankruptcy, and it can lead to criminal prosecution, so the distinction is critical. The agent should not concede fraud lightly, but the agent should also not understate the risk if the badges of fraud are present.

### Apply The No-Limitation Rule For Fraudulent Returns

Under Section 6501(c)(1), if a taxpayer files a false or fraudulent return with the intent to evade tax, the IRS can assess the tax at any time. There is no statute of limitations for a fraudulent return, so the year is open indefinitely. The no-limitation rule applies to the fraudulent return, and it covers the entire tax for the year, not just the fraudulent portion. The rule is a severe consequence of fraud, because the taxpayer is exposed to assessment (and the fraud penalty) at any time, even decades later. The no-limitation rule does not apply to a subsequent non-fraudulent amended return, but the original fraudulent return's no-limitation rule continues.

Apply the no-limitation rule for fraudulent returns. If the return was filed with the intent to evade tax, the IRS can assess at any time, and the year is open indefinitely. The agent should advise the client of the perpetual exposure if fraud is present, and the client should consult a qualified tax professional and a criminal tax attorney. The no-limitation rule is a powerful tool for the IRS, because it removes the statute-of-limitations defense, and the taxpayer cannot close the year by waiting. The agent should evaluate the fraud risk honestly, because a false conclusion (either way) exposes the client to significant harm. The no-limitation rule applies to the fraudulent return, so the agent should not assume that a subsequent amended return closes the year.

### Apply The No-Limitation Rule For Failure To File

Under Section 6501(c)(3), if a taxpayer fails to file a return, the IRS can assess the tax at any time. The no-limitation rule for failure to file applies even without fraud, so a taxpayer who never files a return is exposed indefinitely. The rule applies until the taxpayer files a return, at which point the three-year period (or the six-year period for substantial omissions) begins to run from the filing date. If the taxpayer files a return after the IRS has already assessed (such as after an IRS examination or a substitute return), the filing may start the period, but the assessment may already be valid.

Apply the no-limitation rule for failure to file. If no return was filed, the IRS can assess at any time, and the year is open indefinitely. The agent should advise the client of the perpetual exposure if a return was not filed, and the client should file the return to start the statute of limitations. The filing of a return starts the three-year period (or the six-year period), so the client should file even if the return is late, because the filing starts the clock. If the IRS has already filed a substitute return (under Section 6020(b)), the substitute return may or may not start the period, depending on whether the taxpayer later agrees to it, so the agent should confirm the filing status. The no-limitation rule for failure to file is a common trap, because a taxpayer may believe the year is closed after a certain time, while the failure to file leaves it open.

### Evaluate The Effect Of A Substitute For Return

If a taxpayer fails to file, the IRS may prepare a substitute for return (SFR) under Section 6020(b), based on the income reported by third parties (such as W-2s and 1099s). The SFR is an assessment document, and the IRS can assess based on it. However, the SFR is not a "return" for purposes of starting the statute of limitations, unless the taxpayer subsequently files a return or agrees to the SFR. If the taxpayer files a return after the SFR, the filing starts the three-year period (or the six-year period). If the taxpayer does not file, the SFR does not start the period, and the no-limitation rule continues.

Evaluate the effect of a substitute for return. If the IRS prepared an SFR, determine whether the taxpayer subsequently filed a return or agreed to the SFR. If the taxpayer filed a return, the filing starts the statute of limitations. If the taxpayer did not file, the SFR does not start the period, and the no-limitation rule continues. The agent should obtain the IRS account transcript to confirm the filing status and the assessment history, because the SFR's effect on the statute is a technical point. The SFR is often based on the highest tax (without deductions or credits), so the taxpayer should file a return to claim the deductions and the credits and to start the statute of limitations. The agent should not assume that the SFR closes the year, because it generally does not.

### Address The Interaction With Collection, Bankruptcy, And Offers

The fraud determination and the no-limitation rule affect the collection statute, the discharge of tax in bankruptcy, and the offer-in-compromise eligibility. A tax assessed based on a fraudulent return is not dischargeable in bankruptcy (under Section 523(a)(1) of the Bankruptcy Code), and the fraud penalty is also not dischargeable. The collection statute (10 years from assessment) continues to run, but the no-limitation rule for assessment means the IRS can assess at any time, so the collection statute may not start until the assessment. An offer in compromise based on doubt as to collectibility may be available, but the fraud may affect the IRS's view of the taxpayer's credibility.

Address the interaction with the collection, the bankruptcy, and the offers. A tax based on a fraudulent return is not dischargeable in bankruptcy, and the fraud penalty is also not dischargeable, so the taxpayer cannot eliminate the debt through bankruptcy. The collection statute (10 years) runs from the assessment, but the no-limitation rule for assessment means the IRS can assess at any time, so the collection statute may not start until the assessment. An offer in compromise may be available, but the fraud may affect the IRS's view. The agent should advise the client of these interactions, because the fraud determination has consequences beyond the assessment statute. The client should consult a qualified tax professional and, if appropriate, a bankruptcy attorney or a criminal tax attorney.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Negligence With Fraud

The symptom is an agent who treats a substantial understatement as fraud. The trap is that fraud requires specific intent to evade a tax known to be owing, which is a higher standard than negligence, so the no-limitation rule does not apply. The direction is to evaluate the badges of fraud and to distinguish fraud from negligence.

### Assuming A Year Is Closed When Fraud Is Present

The symptom is an agent who advises a client that a year is closed after three years, without evaluating the fraud risk. The trap is that fraud leaves the year open indefinitely, so the IRS can assess at any time. The direction is to evaluate the fraud risk and to advise the client of the no-limitation rule if fraud is present.

### Overlooking The Failure-To-File No-Limitation Rule

The symptom is a taxpayer who never filed a return and believes the year is closed after a certain time. The trap is that the failure to file leaves the year open indefinitely, regardless of intent. The direction is to file the return to start the statute of limitations.

### Assuming A Substitute For Return Starts The Statute

The symptom is a taxpayer who believes the IRS's SFR starts the three-year period. The trap is that an SFR is generally not a return for statute purposes, unless the taxpayer subsequently files or agrees, so the no-limitation rule continues. The direction is to confirm the filing status and to file a return to start the period.

### Understating The Consequences Of The Fraud Penalty

The symptom is an agent who treats the fraud penalty as equivalent to the negligence penalty. The trap is that the fraud penalty is 75 percent of the fraudulent underpayment, it is not dischargeable in bankruptcy, and it can lead to criminal prosecution. The direction is to advise the client of the severe consequences and to recommend consulting a criminal tax attorney.

### Applying The No-Limitation Rule To A Non-Fraudulent Amended Return

The symptom is an agent who applies the no-limitation rule to a subsequent non-fraudulent amended return. The trap is that the no-limitation rule applies to the original fraudulent return, and the amended return may start a new period for the refund claim, but it does not close the original year. The direction is to understand that the original fraudulent return's no-limitation rule continues.
