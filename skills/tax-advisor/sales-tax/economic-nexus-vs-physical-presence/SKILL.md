# Economic Nexus Versus Physical Presence

The central judgment problem in nexus classification is that economic nexus and physical presence nexus are created by completely different activities, measured by completely different tests, and yet produce the same downstream obligation to register and collect. A seller can have one without the other, both at once, or neither, and the correct classification changes how the obligation is timed, how far back it reaches, and how it should be documented. Treating all nexus as "Wayfair nexus" collapses two distinct legal theories and causes agents to miss physical presence triggers that exist regardless of any sales threshold, or to over-claim economic nexus where the threshold was never actually met.

Agents commonly conflate the two categories, apply the South Dakota economic thresholds as though they govern physical presence, and forget that physical presence was never overruled by Wayfair — it remains a standalone, threshold-free basis for nexus. They also misclassify borderline activities such as inventory stored in a third-party warehouse, independent contractors, or a few days at a trade show, and they overlook that some states still maintain click-through or affiliate nexus statutes that are neither purely economic nor purely physical. The harm this prevents is a seller who relies on a sales-threshold test while sitting on undisputed physical presence, or who over-registers based on a misread economic trigger.

This skill covers the classification distinction between economic nexus (post-Wayfair sales and transaction thresholds) and physical presence nexus, plus the related click-through and affiliate categories, under U.S. state and local sales tax principles. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the specific thresholds, which activities count, and how each state applies these doctrines are jurisdiction-dependent and change frequently, and a qualified state-and-local tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Understand That Physical Presence And Economic Nexus Are Independent Theories

Physical presence nexus arises from a tangible connection to the state — property, people, or activity physically located there — and it has no dollar or transaction threshold; any qualifying presence can be enough. Economic nexus arises solely from the volume of sales or transactions into the state, with no physical tie, and only attaches once a state-specific threshold is crossed. These are independent: meeting one does not require the other, and having one does not excuse the analysis of the other.

The practical consequence is that the analysis must always run in two parallel tracks. First, ask whether any physical presence factor exists, because if it does, nexus is established regardless of sales volume — a seller with one employee in a state has nexus even with $1,000 of sales. Second, separately ask whether the economic threshold is met, because a seller with no physical presence can still owe nexus purely on sales volume. Documenting both tracks prevents the common error of stopping the analysis after one test passes or fails. A seller may fail the economic test in a state where it nonetheless has a warehouse, and concluding no-nexus would be wrong.

### Apply Economic Nexus Thresholds As State-Specific, Not Universal

After South Dakota v. Wayfair (2018), states may impose tax collection on remote sellers based solely on economic activity, but each state sets its own threshold and measurement rules. South Dakota's standard was $100,000 of gross revenue or 200 separate transactions in the current or prior calendar year, and many states adopted similar numbers, but the variation is wide and changes over time.

Common economic thresholds include $100,000 in sales (with no transaction count) in states such as California and Texas, $100,000 or 200 transactions in states that kept both prongs, and higher thresholds such as $500,000 in a few states like Tennessee (which has adjusted its threshold over time). Some states dropped the 200-transaction prong after it produced disproportionate compliance burden relative to revenue. Critically, the measurement basis differs — some states count gross sales of tangible personal property, some include services, some count only retail sales, and some look at the prior year while others measure the current year or a trailing twelve months. Never assume the $100,000 or 200-transaction default; pull the specific state's current statute or guidance and confirm what counts toward the threshold and over what period.

### Recognize Physical Presence Activities That Create Nexus Without Any Threshold

Physical presence nexus is created by a long list of activities, none of which depend on sales volume. Owning or leasing real property (an office, warehouse, store, or storage unit), having employees, agents, or independent contractors performing services in the state, maintaining inventory in the state (including inventory held by a third party, such as Amazon FBA fulfillment centers), delivering goods in the seller's own vehicles, and conducting temporary activities such as trade shows or pop-up sales can all establish physical presence nexus.

The key judgment is that even transient or third-party-arranged presence can count. Inventory stored in a fulfillment network is the most commonly missed physical presence trigger, because the seller does not control where the marketplace places its goods yet the goods create property presence in each state where they sit. A two-day trade show booth, installation or repair services performed in-state, or an independent sales representative soliciting orders can each be enough. Build a physical-presence checklist and apply it to every state, because unlike economic nexus there is no safe-harbor threshold — if the activity qualifies, nexus exists from the first day.

### Handle Click-Through And Affiliate Nexus As A Distinct Third Category

Some states maintain click-through nexus or affiliate nexus statutes that sit between pure economic and pure physical presence. Click-through nexus typically attaches when a seller has an agreement with an in-state resident who, for a commission, refers customers via a web link or otherwise, and the seller exceeds a minimum sales amount from those referrals. Affiliate nexus attaches when the seller is related to an in-state business that facilitates its sales or uses similar facilities.

These categories matter because they can create nexus at a lower economic bar than the standard Wayfair threshold, or based on relationships that are neither inventory nor employees. For example, a click-through statute may require a relatively modest level of in-state referral sales (historically thresholds such as $10,000 were cited in some statutes) combined with a commission arrangement. Identify whether the seller has any commission-based referral or affiliate relationships with in-state parties, and check the specific state's statute, because not all states enforce these doctrines and the precise triggers vary. Do not assume Wayfair displaced them; several remain on the books.

### Determine The Measurement Period And Counting Basis For Economic Nexus

The economic threshold is only meaningful when applied to the correct measurement window and the correct base. States differ on whether the threshold is measured over the prior calendar year, the current calendar year, the prior twelve months, or a trailing rolling period, and they differ on what counts toward sales.

Confirm the measurement period before concluding the threshold was met or missed. A seller might cross $100,000 in a calendar year but not on a trailing-twelve-month basis, or vice versa, and the answer flips depending on the state's rule. Also confirm what is included: gross revenue from all sales into the state, only sales of tangible personal property, sales plus certain services, or retail sales net of returns and exempt sales. Marketplace sales may or may not count toward the seller's own threshold depending on whether the marketplace is treated as the seller for threshold purposes. Getting the period and base wrong produces a false positive (over-registration) or a false negative (silent liability).

### Weigh The Different Registration Consequences Of Each Nexus Type

Although both nexus types lead to registration, the consequences differ in timing and exposure. Physical presence nexus is often undisputed and may reach back to when the presence began, which can be years, and the seller has little leverage to dispute it. Economic nexus generally applies prospectively from the date the threshold is crossed and the state's rules take effect, so the look-back is typically shorter and more contained.

Use the classification to shape the remediation strategy. A seller with long-standing physical presence faces a longer potential look-back and may need a voluntary disclosure agreement to cap it, whereas a seller that only recently crossed an economic threshold may have a short, manageable exposure suitable for direct registration. Documenting which nexus type applies, and the date each was established, gives the seller defensible position on the start date and the scope of any back liability. Never blend the two into a single vague "we have nexus" conclusion.

### Account For Marketplace Sales And Facilitator Laws In The Classification

When a marketplace facilitator collects and remits tax on a seller's marketplace sales, the classification of the seller's own nexus does not disappear, but the practical obligation for those specific sales may shift to the facilitator. The seller must still classify its nexus because non-marketplace sales remain its own responsibility and physical presence triggers remain live.

Separate the analysis into marketplace sales versus direct sales. For marketplace sales, confirm the facilitator is registered and collecting in each state, which typically relieves the seller of collection on those transactions. For direct sales, run the full economic and physical presence analysis independently. A common error is to count marketplace sales toward the seller's economic threshold without realizing the state may exclude them, or to assume marketplace collection erases physical presence nexus from FBA inventory. Keep the two channels distinct and document each.

## Common Traps

### Collapsing Both Nexus Types Into A Single Wayfair Threshold Test

The agent applies the $100,000 or 200-transaction test to everything and ignores physical presence. The trap is that physical presence creates nexus with no threshold, so a seller with inventory or an employee owes nexus regardless of sales volume. Always run the physical presence track separately from the economic track.

### Treating Third-Party Inventory As Not The Seller's Problem

The seller does not control where its fulfillment network stores goods, so it assumes the inventory is not its nexus. The trap is that inventory held for the seller by a third party still creates property presence in each state where it sits. Inventory all fulfillment-center states where the seller's goods may be stored.

### Applying The $100,000 Or 200-Transaction Default To Every State

The agent quotes the South Dakota numbers as universal. The trap is that thresholds range from $100,000 to $500,000, some states dropped the transaction prong, and measurement periods differ. Pull each state's current threshold and measurement rule rather than defaulting.

### Forgetting That Click-Through Or Affiliate Nexus May Apply

The seller has commission-based referrers or a related in-state entity, and the agent skips these doctrines. The trap is that several states still enforce click-through or affiliate nexus at lower economic bars than Wayfair. Check for referral and affiliate relationships and the relevant state statutes.

### Measuring The Economic Threshold Against The Wrong Period Or Base

The agent totals annual sales and compares to the threshold without checking the measurement window. The trap is that states use prior-year, current-year, or trailing periods and differ on what counts. Confirm the specific period and base before concluding the threshold was crossed.

### Assuming Marketplace Collection Eliminates The Seller's Nexus Entirely

Because a marketplace collects on platform sales, the agent concludes the seller has no nexus anywhere. The trap is that facilitator collection only covers marketplace sales and does not erase independent economic or physical nexus for direct sales. Run the analysis separately for each channel.

### Over-Registering Based On A Misread Economic Trigger

The seller registers in a state believing it crossed the threshold, when in fact the threshold was measured on the wrong base or period. The trap is unnecessary registration, filing burden, and audit exposure in a state where no obligation existed. Verify the threshold was genuinely met before registering.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has nexus been analyzed on two independent tracks — physical presence (threshold-free) and economic (threshold-based) — for every relevant state?
- [ ] For economic nexus, has the specific state's current threshold (which may be $100,000, $500,000, or another amount) and its transaction-count rule been confirmed rather than assumed?
- [ ] Has the measurement period (prior year, current year, or trailing twelve months) and the counting base (gross sales, tangible property only, retail sales) been verified for each state's economic threshold?
- [ ] Has a physical presence checklist been applied, including owned or leased property, employees, independent contractors, in-state delivery vehicles, trade shows, and pop-up sales?
- [ ] Has third-party inventory (such as fulfillment-network or FBA inventory) been identified across all states where the seller's goods may physically sit?
- [ ] Have click-through and affiliate nexus statutes been checked where the seller has commission-based referrers or related in-state entities?
- [ ] Has the nexus type been documented with the date each was established, so the look-back scope and registration timing are defensible?
- [ ] Have marketplace sales been separated from direct sales, with the facilitator's registration confirmed and independent nexus tested for the direct channel?
- [ ] Has the classification been used to shape remediation — longer look-back via voluntary disclosure for physical presence, shorter prospective exposure for economic nexus?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general sales-tax information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
