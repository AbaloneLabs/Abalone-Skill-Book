# Collection And Remit Obligations

The judgment problem in sales-tax collection is that the obligation to charge tax on a sale depends on three independently variable factors — whether the product or service is taxable, whether the customer is exempt, and where the sale is sourced — and an agent that resolves only one or two of these will collect the wrong amount. A single order can contain taxable goods, exempt goods, and goods bound for a resale customer, each taxed differently, and the seller must apply the correct rule to each line. Remittance is the downstream obligation: once collected, the tax must be reported, reconciled, and paid to the correct jurisdiction on the correct schedule, and a failure to remit collected tax is treated far more seriously than a failure to collect, because the seller is holding money that belongs to the state.

Agents tend to treat collection as a binary on-or-off and miss that taxability is product- and state-specific (clothing may be exempt in one state and taxable in another, and services are taxable in some states and not others). They also mishandle exemption certificates — accepting an invalid or expired certificate, or failing to collect one at all — which shifts the tax burden onto the seller. They forget that shipping and handling charges may or may not be taxable depending on how they are billed and whether they are separately stated, and they conflate collection (charging the customer) with remittance (paying the state), not realizing that collected-but-unremitted tax creates personal liability in many states. The harm this prevents is a seller that systematically over- or under-collects, accepts worthless exemption certificates, or holds unremitted tax and faces trust-fund recovery penalties.

This skill covers the transaction-level collection decision and the remittance and return-filing process, primarily under U.S. state and local sales-tax law. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; taxability rules, exemption rules, and remittance procedures are jurisdiction-dependent and change frequently. A qualified state-and-local tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Resolve Product Taxability, Customer Exemption, And Sourcing Independently

The tax on a transaction is the product of three separate determinations. First, is the product or service taxable in the destination state? Second, is the customer exempt (resale, government, nonprofit, manufacturing)? Third, where is the sale sourced for rate purposes? Each must be resolved independently because a taxable product sold to an exempt customer is not taxed, and a taxable product sold to a taxable customer may be taxed at a different rate depending on sourcing. Collapsing any of these into an assumption produces the wrong result.

For example, a sale of clothing (potentially exempt in some states) to a resale customer (exempt regardless of product) sourced to an origin-rate state (rate set by the seller's location) requires three distinct lookups. Resolve product taxability against the destination state's schedule, confirm the customer's exemption status with a valid certificate, and determine the sourcing rule before applying a rate. Do not apply a blanket "all goods are taxable" or "this customer is always exempt" shortcut.

### Apply Product-Specific And Service-Specific Taxability Rules

Taxability is not uniform across products or across states. Tangible personal property is taxable in most states, but categories such as groceries, clothing, medicine, and textbooks are exempt or taxed at a reduced rate in many states, and the definitions differ (a "grocery" exemption may exclude prepared foods, candy, or soft drinks). Services are taxable in fewer states, and the taxable service list varies — professional services are usually exempt, but services to tangible personal property, lodging, and digital services are often taxable. Digital goods and software-as-a-service are an evolving area where states differ sharply.

For example, a subscription software product may be taxable as a digital good in one state, taxable as a service in another, and exempt in a third. Pull the specific state's treatment of the exact product or service category, and do not assume that because a product is taxable in the seller's home state it is taxable everywhere. Maintain a taxability matrix by state and product category and update it as states issue new guidance.

### Validate Exemption And Resale Certificates Before Honoring Them

An exemption certificate shifts the tax burden from the seller to the buyer, but only if the certificate is valid, complete, and applicable to the transaction. A seller that accepts an invalid, expired, or misapplied certificate can be held liable for the uncollected tax. The certificate must be from the customer claiming the exemption, must state a valid exemption reason (resale, manufacturing, government, nonprofit), must cover the type of goods purchased, and must be retained for the retention period required by the state.

For example, a buyer presenting a resale certificate for office supplies it will consume (not resell) is misusing the certificate, and if audited the seller may owe the tax unless it can show it accepted the certificate in good faith. Collect the certificate before or at the time of sale, verify the exemption reason matches the product, and store it retrievably for audit defense. Never backdate or accept a certificate after an audit has begun.

### Determine The Taxability Of Shipping, Freight, And Handling Charges

Shipping and handling charges are a frequent source of error because their taxability depends on how they are billed and the state's rule. In many states, separately stated transportation charges for delivery by a common carrier are not taxable, but handling, packaging, or delivery charges that are not separately stated, or delivery by the seller's own vehicle, are taxable. Some states tax all delivery charges regardless of how they are stated.

For example, a single "shipping and handling" line that bundles taxable handling with potentially exempt freight may render the entire charge taxable in a state that requires separation. Review how delivery charges are itemized, confirm the destination state's rule on freight and handling, and restructure the billing if needed to preserve the exemption for bona fide transportation.

### Separate Collection From Remittance And Treat Collected Tax As Trust Money

Collecting tax from a customer creates an immediate obligation to remit it to the state, and the collected tax is generally held in trust for the state — it is not the seller's money. Failing to remit collected tax is treated as a serious violation, and in many states the responsible persons (owners, officers) can be held personally liable under trust-fund recovery provisions. Remittance must be reconciled to the amount collected, reported on the correct return, and paid by the due date for the assigned filing frequency.

For example, a seller that collects $10,000 of tax but spends it on operations before the return is due has committed a trust-fund violation, and the owners may be personally liable even if the business dissolves. Reconcile collected tax to remitted tax each period, segregate the funds if cash flow is tight, and never treat sales-tax collections as available working capital.

### Match Remittance Frequency, Rounding, And Return Reconciliation To The Jurisdiction

Each state assigns a filing frequency (monthly, quarterly, annually) based on the seller's tax volume, and the frequency can change as volume grows. Returns must be filed even for zero-tax periods in most states, and the rounding method and discount for timely filing (where offered) vary by state. Reconciliation must tie the tax collected per the books to the tax reported on the return, with discrepancies investigated.

For example, a seller bumped from quarterly to monthly filing that continues filing quarterly will rack up late returns and penalties. Track the assigned frequency for each jurisdiction, file zero returns where required, apply any timely-filing discount, and reconcile collected-versus-reported tax each period so that variances are caught before they compound.

## Common Traps

### Treating Collection As A Binary On-or-off

The symptom is a seller that taxes or exempts an entire order based on a single factor. The trap is that product taxability, customer exemption, and sourcing are independent, so a mixed order requires line-by-line analysis. Resolve each factor separately for each line.

### Assuming A Product Taxable In The Home State Is Taxable Everywhere

The symptom is applying home-state taxability to all destinations. The trap is that groceries, clothing, digital goods, and services are taxed differently across states, so home-state logic over- or under-collects elsewhere. Maintain a state-by-state taxability matrix.

### Accepting An Invalid Or Expired Exemption Certificate

The symptom is a seller honoring an exemption with no documentation or a stale certificate. The trap is that an invalid certificate shifts the tax liability onto the seller, who must collect before or at sale and retain the certificate. Collect valid certificates and verify the exemption reason matches the product.

### Bundling Shipping And Handling Into One Taxable Charge

The symptom is a single "shipping and handling" line that defeats a freight exemption. The trap is that bundling can render the entire delivery charge taxable in states that exempt only separately stated freight. Separate bona fide transportation from handling charges on the invoice.

### Spending Collected Tax Before Remittance

The symptom is a cash-strapped seller using sales-tax collections to fund operations. The trap is that collected tax is trust money, and failure to remit can create personal liability for responsible owners under trust-fund recovery rules. Segregate collected tax and remit it on time.

### Missing A Filing-Frequency Change Or Skipping Zero Returns

The symptom is a seller continuing to file at an old frequency or skipping periods with no sales. The trap is that most states require returns even for zero-tax periods, and missing a frequency bump causes late filings. Track frequency per jurisdiction and file every required return.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has each transaction been analyzed across all three independent factors — product taxability in the destination state, customer exemption status, and sourcing rule — rather than treated as a binary?
- [ ] Has the specific state's taxability treatment of the exact product or service category (including digital goods and services) been confirmed rather than assumed from the home state?
- [ ] Are exemption and resale certificates collected before or at sale, verified for validity and applicability, and stored retrievably for the required retention period?
- [ ] Has the taxability of shipping, freight, and handling charges been determined under the destination state's rule, with separately stated bona fide freight preserved where exempt?
- [ ] Is collected tax treated as trust money, segregated from operating funds, and remitted on time to avoid personal liability under trust-fund recovery provisions?
- [ ] Has the assigned filing frequency for each jurisdiction been confirmed, with zero returns filed where required and any timely-filing discount applied?
- [ ] Is collected tax reconciled to reported tax each period, with variances investigated before they compound?
