# Nexus Determination And Registration Obligations

Determining sales tax nexus is a binary decision with asymmetric consequences, and that asymmetry is the core judgment problem. If a seller with nexus fails to register, it accumulates back tax, penalty, and interest liability that can run for years before discovery, and the liability often falls on the seller rather than the customer. If a seller without a genuine obligation registers prematurely, it takes on filing burden, registration fees, and exposure to audit in a state where it may have had none. The hard part is that nexus is not one rule but a patchwork of state-specific triggers that change frequently, and the moment nexus is established the registration clock starts whether or not the seller notices.

Agents tend to treat nexus as a single Wayfair question, apply the famous $100,000 or 200-transaction threshold mechanically, and skip the registration-decision layer entirely. They forget that physical presence still creates nexus on its own, that marketplace facilitator laws shift the obligation, that the date nexus begins governs how far back a state can reach, and that registering without first assessing exposure can convert a manageable problem into a confessed multi-year liability. The harm this prevents is a seller who registers blindly, waives look-back protections, or conversely one who ignores a real trigger and lets silent liability compound.

This skill covers the lifecycle from nexus discovery through the registration decision, including exposure assessment and voluntary disclosure, under U.S. state and local sales tax principles. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; sales tax nexus rules, thresholds, registration triggers, and voluntary disclosure terms are jurisdiction-dependent and change frequently, and a qualified state-and-local tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Treat Nexus As A State-By-State, Trigger-By-Trigger Inventory

Nexus is not a global status; it must be assessed separately for each state and each type of triggering activity. A seller can have nexus in twenty states and none in the rest, and within a single state it can owe nexus to the state but not to a local jurisdiction, or vice versa. Build an inventory of every state where the seller has any plausible contact, then test each contact against that state's current rules.

The inventory should cover, at minimum, physical presence factors (employees, offices, warehouses, inventory in third-party fulfillment like FBA, trade shows, deliveries in own vehicles), economic activity (sales volume and transaction counts measured against the state's threshold), and affiliate or click-through relationships. Do not collapse this into a single question. A seller may clear the economic threshold in one state while having physical presence in another, and the registration timing differs for each. Document the trigger type and the date it was first satisfied for every state, because that date anchors the exposure calculation.

### Distinguish The Trigger Date From The Registration Deadline

Once nexus exists, most states require registration within a defined window, commonly 30 days, and require collection to begin on a specified future date (often the first day of the next month after registration, or a state-set effective date). The trigger date and the registration deadline are distinct, and confusing them produces either late registration penalties or collection errors.

Identify the exact date nexus was established and the state's required registration window. Some states specify that collection must begin on the first day of the second month after nexus, others tie it to the registration approval date, and a few backdate collection to the nexus date itself. Collecting too early over-collects from customers and creates refund obligations; collecting too late means the seller eats the uncollected tax for the gap period. Map each state's rule explicitly rather than assuming a uniform 30-day standard.

### Quantify Prior-Period Exposure Before Registering Blindly

Registration often requires disclosing a start date, and several states ask whether the seller had prior sales. Registering without first measuring exposure can hand the state a roadmap to back taxes. Before registering in any state where nexus existed for some time, estimate the uncollected tax for the full look-back period, typically up to the state's statute of limitations (commonly three or four years, sometimes longer if fraud is alleged).

Run the exposure calculation on taxable sales into the state from the nexus trigger date forward, applying the correct state and local rates and accounting for any tax the seller already collected. The result determines whether the seller should register directly and self-assess, or instead pursue a voluntary disclosure agreement that caps the look-back period. A seller with three years of uncollected tax has a very different decision than one with two months, and the exposure number should drive the path, not the other way around.

### Use Voluntary Disclosure Agreements To Cap Look-Back When Exposure Is Material

A voluntary disclosure agreement (VDA) is a negotiated settlement with a state's tax authority, typically available before the state has contacted the seller, that commonly limits the look-back period to a defined window such as three or four prior years (often a look-back of three years plus the current year) and frequently waives penalties, though interest usually remains. When prior exposure is large, a VDA can materially reduce the settlement compared to a cold registration.

Evaluate a VDA whenever the seller had nexus for more than a year before discovery, or whenever the dollar exposure is significant relative to the business. The tradeoff is that a VDA requires formal negotiation, takes time, and commits the seller to come forward; the benefit is the capped look-back and penalty waiver. Note that most VDAs require the seller to not already be under audit or contact by that state, so timing matters — once a nexus state sends a questionnaire or notice, the VDA window may close. Also compare against any active amnesty program, which can offer similar or better terms with less negotiation.

### Consider Amnesty Programs As A Time-Limited Alternative

States periodically run tax amnesty programs that waive penalties and sometimes interest for a defined period, usually requiring payment of back tax within the amnesty window. Amnesty can be simpler than a VDA because the terms are standardized and there is no negotiation, but it is only available during the program dates and is often a once-per-taxpayer event.

Check whether any relevant state is currently running an amnesty before defaulting to a VDA or cold registration. Amnesty terms vary — some cover only specific tax types or periods, some exclude taxpayers already under audit, and some bar future amnesty participation. If a state's amnesty window is open and the seller's exposure fits the program, amnesty may be the lowest-friction path. If no amnesty is active, the VDA or direct registration path governs.

### Sequence Multi-State Registration By Exposure And Risk

When a seller has nexus in many states at once, registering everywhere simultaneously can be costly and operationally disruptive. Prioritize by exposure and enforcement risk: states with the largest back-tax liability, states with active enforcement or known outreach (such as nexus questionnaires), and states where physical presence makes the obligation undisputed should generally go first.

Sequence the registrations so the highest-exposure, highest-risk states are resolved through VDAs or direct registration first, while lower-exposure states can follow. However, do not delay registration indefinitely to avoid the obligation, because the look-back period keeps growing. A defensible sequencing plan documents why each state is ordered as it is and sets concrete target dates, rather than leaving registration as an open-ended to-do. For states where the seller is below the economic threshold and has no physical presence, confirm no-nexus status in writing and re-test periodically.

### Treat Marketplace Facilitator Laws As A Shift, Not An Elimination, Of Obligation

Many states require a marketplace facilitator (such as a large online marketplace) to collect and remit tax on behalf of third-party sellers, which can remove the seller's collection obligation for sales made through that marketplace. But this does not remove the seller's nexus for other reasons, and it does not apply to sales made outside the marketplace.

Before concluding a seller has no obligation in a state because a marketplace collects, confirm that the marketplace is a registered facilitator under that state's law, that the specific sales in question were marketplace sales, and that the seller has no independent nexus trigger (own inventory, employees, or its own economic threshold reached through non-marketplace sales). A seller using FBA may have physical presence nexus through inventory stored in a state even when the marketplace collects on the sale, which can create a registration obligation for other sales or for reporting. Do not let marketplace collection create a false sense of total compliance.

## Common Traps

### Applying The $100,000 Or 200-Transaction Threshold As If It Were Universal

Many agents quote the South Dakota Wayfair thresholds and assume every state uses them. The trap is that thresholds vary widely — some states use $100,000 with no transaction count, some use $500,000, several have dropped the 200-transaction component, and a few use prior-year or current-year measurement. Always pull the specific state's current threshold and measurement period rather than applying the default.

### Registering Immediately Without Measuring Exposure First

A seller discovers nexus and registers the next day, listing a start date years in the past. The trap is that registration can trigger a look-back assessment and waive protections a VDA would have preserved. Measure exposure and evaluate VDA or amnesty eligibility before submitting the registration where prior exposure is material.

### Assuming Marketplace Collection Means No Nexus Anywhere

Because a marketplace collects tax on platform sales, the seller concludes it has no state obligations at all. The trap is that marketplace facilitator collection only covers marketplace sales and does not erase independent physical or economic nexus for other channels. Test for independent nexus triggers separately.

### Forgetting Physical Presence Because The Seller Is "Online Only"

An online-only seller assumes it has only economic nexus. The trap is that inventory in third-party warehouses (such as Amazon FBA), remote employees, or a single trade-show booth create physical presence nexus independent of any sales threshold. Inventory the physical presence factors before relying on the economic threshold.

### Treating The Registration Effective Date As The Collection Start Date

The seller registers and assumes collection begins on the approval date. The trap is that many states require collection to begin on a state-specified future date that differs from the registration date, and collecting on the wrong date causes over- or under-collection. Confirm each state's collection-start rule explicitly.

### Ignoring Local Jurisdictions And Special Districts

The seller registers with the state and collects only the state rate. The trap is that many states impose county, city, transit, and special-district taxes that the seller's permit may or may not cover, and registration scope varies. Confirm whether the state registration covers all local jurisdictions or whether separate local registration is needed.

### Letting The Look-Back Period Grow While Deferring The Decision

The seller knows it has nexus but defers registration to manage cash flow, and the exposure compounds. The trap is that the statute of limitations runs from non-filing, which in many states means there is no limit until returns are filed, so liability can grow indefinitely. Set a concrete remediation date rather than leaving the obligation open.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has nexus been inventoried state-by-state and trigger-by-trigger (physical presence, economic, affiliate/click-through), rather than reduced to a single Wayfair question?
- [ ] For each nexus state, has the exact trigger date and the state's specific registration deadline window been identified and documented?
- [ ] Has prior-period exposure been quantified for the full look-back period (typically three to four years, subject to the state's statute) before any registration is submitted?
- [ ] Has a voluntary disclosure agreement been evaluated for any state where nexus existed for more than a year or where dollar exposure is material?
- [ ] Has the agent checked whether any relevant state is currently running a tax amnesty program whose terms fit the seller's exposure?
- [ ] Has multi-state registration been sequenced by exposure and enforcement risk, with concrete target dates rather than open-ended deferral?
- [ ] For marketplace sales, has the agent confirmed the facilitator is registered, the sales were marketplace sales, and no independent nexus trigger exists for other channels?
- [ ] Have physical presence factors (including third-party fulfillment inventory, employees, and trade shows) been checked even for sellers believed to be online-only?
- [ ] Has the collection-start date rule for each state been confirmed against the registration effective date to avoid over- or under-collection?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general sales-tax information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
