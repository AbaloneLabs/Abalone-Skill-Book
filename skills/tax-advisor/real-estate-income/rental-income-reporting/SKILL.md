# Rental Income Reporting

Rental income reporting appears straightforward on the surface: income on Schedule E, expenses deducted against it. But the details of what constitutes rental income, which expenses are deductible and how they are allocated, the treatment of security deposits and advance payments, and the interaction with personal-use rules create numerous traps. An agent who treats rental reporting as a simple income-minus-expenses exercise without examining the specific rules will misstate taxable income, miss deductions, or create examination exposure.

Agents commonly miss that security deposits are not income until forfeit, that advance rent is income in the year received, that expenses must be allocated between rental and personal use, and that certain costs (improvements, points, loan costs) must be capitalized rather than expensed. The harm is a landlord who over-reports income (paying tax on deposits that should not be income) or under-reports (missing the advance rent inclusion), or who misclassifies capital costs as current expenses.

This skill covers rental income reporting under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; rental rules interact with passive activity, depreciation, and personal-use rules, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Identify All Sources Of Rental Income

Rental income includes all amounts received for the use or occupancy of property, not just the monthly rent. This includes advance rent (rent received in advance, which is income in the year received regardless of the period it covers), lease cancellation payments, nonrefundable tenant payments for option consideration, and the fair market value of property or services received in lieu of cash rent. If a tenant provides services (painting, repairs) in exchange for rent reduction, the fair market value of those services is rental income.

Catalog all amounts received from the rental activity and confirm each is included in income. Advance rent is a common omission: a landlord who receives the first and last month's rent plus a security deposit at lease signing must include the first and last month's rent in income in the year received, while the security deposit is not income (assuming it is refundable). Lease cancellation payments and nonrefundable option payments are income. Do not exclude amounts merely because they are labeled as something other than rent; the substance controls.

### Handle Security Deposits Correctly

A refundable security deposit is not rental income in the year received, because the landlord is obligated to return it to the tenant (subject to deductions for damage or unpaid rent). The deposit becomes income only in the year it is forfeited (for example, applied to unpaid rent or retained for damages) or in the year it is applied to the tenant's final rent obligation. If the landlord retains part of the deposit for repairs, the retained amount is income in the year retained, and the corresponding repair cost is a deductible expense.

Distinguish refundable deposits (not income until forfeit) from nonrefundable deposits or fees (income in the year received). A nonrefundable cleaning fee or pet fee is income immediately. Confirm the lease terms to classify each payment correctly. Do not include refundable security deposits in current income, and do include them in the year they are forfeited or applied. Track the deposit liability until resolution.

### Deduct Operating Expenses And Allocate Mixed Use

Operating expenses for rental property are deductible against rental income. These include property taxes, mortgage interest (but not principal), insurance, repairs and maintenance, utilities paid by the landlord, property management fees, advertising, legal and professional fees, travel to the property, supplies, and depreciation. Expenses must be ordinary and necessary for the rental activity and must be allocated between rental use and personal use if the property is used personally by the owner.

For a property rented at fair market value for the entire year, the full operating expenses are deductible. For a property with personal use (such as a vacation home used partly for rental and partly for personal use), expenses must be allocated between rental and personal use based on the days of each use. The allocation method and the personal-use day count determine the deductible rental expenses, and the rules are strict (see the dwelling unit personal use rules). Confirm the use pattern and allocate expenses accordingly. Do not deduct 100% of expenses for a property with significant personal use.

### Capitalize Improvements Versus Expense Repairs

A critical distinction in rental taxation is between repairs (currently deductible) and improvements (capitalized and depreciated). A repair keeps the property in operating condition; an improvement adds value, adapts the property to a new use, or extends its life. Replacing a broken window is a repair; adding a new room is an improvement. Replacing a few damaged roof shingles is a repair; replacing the entire roof is an improvement. The distinction affects timing: repairs are deducted in the year paid, while improvements are depreciated over the property's recovery period (27.5 years for residential rental).

Classify each cost as a repair or improvement based on whether it maintains the property (repair) or betterment it (improvement). The IRS has provided guidance (the safe harbor rules and the tangible property regulations) that can help classify certain costs. Small taxpayer safe harbors may allow current expensing of certain amounts. De minimis safe harbors may allow expensing of low-cost items. Confirm the classification and apply the safe harbors where available. Do not deduct a full roof replacement or a major renovation as a current repair; these must be capitalized and depreciated.

### Depreciate The Property Structure And Improvements

The cost of the rental property (excluding land, which is not depreciable) is depreciated over 27.5 years for residential rental property using the straight-line method. Improvements to the property are depreciated over their applicable recovery period. Furniture and appliances used in the rental are depreciated over shorter periods (typically 5 or 7 years). Depreciation reduces rental income and is a significant deduction for landlords.

Confirm the depreciable basis (cost excluding land, plus capitalized closing costs), the recovery period (27.5 years for residential structure), and the method (straight-line). Begin depreciation when the property is placed in service (ready and available for rental), not when purchased or first rented. Land is never depreciated; allocate the purchase price between land and building. Furniture and appliances have shorter recovery periods. Track depreciation each year and ensure it is claimed, because unclaimed depreciation is considered allowed and reduces basis regardless (the basis is reduced by depreciation allowed or allowable). See the depreciation recapture skill for the consequences on sale.

### Report On Schedule E With Proper Documentation

Rental income and expenses are reported on Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss) for each property. The schedule lists income, deductible expenses by category, and depreciation, arriving at the net rental income or loss. If the taxpayer has multiple rental properties, each is listed separately. The passive activity loss rules may limit the deductibility of net rental losses (see the passive activity loss skill).

Ensure the Schedule E reporting is complete and accurate, with each income and expense category correctly classified. Maintain documentation for all reported amounts: rent receipts or lease records for income, bills and receipts for expenses, and the depreciation schedule. The documentation should support the amounts in the event of examination. Do not report rental activity on Schedule C (which is for a trade or business providing substantial services); standard rental with limited services is Schedule E. If the rental provides substantial services (hotel-like services), it may be a business reported on Schedule C.

## Common Traps

### Including Refundable Security Deposits In Current Income

Refundable deposits are not income until forfeit. Including them in the year received overstates income. Track them as a liability until resolution.

### Excluding Advance Rent From Current Income

Advance rent (including last month's rent paid at lease signing) is income in the year received, not when the period it covers arrives. Excluding it understates income.

### Deducting Improvements As Current Repairs

A roof replacement, room addition, or major renovation is an improvement that must be capitalized and depreciated, not expensed currently. Misclassifying overstates current deductions.

### Failing To Allocate Expenses For Personal-Use Property

A vacation home with personal use requires expense allocation between rental and personal portions. Deducting 100% of expenses for a mixed-use property overstates deductions.

### Not Claiming Depreciation But Still Losing The Basis

Depreciation reduces basis whether or not it is claimed (allowed or allowable). Failing to claim it loses the current deduction but does not avoid the recapture on sale.

### Reporting Rental With Substantial Services On Schedule E

If the rental provides substantial services (daily maid service, concierge), it may be a business on Schedule C, subject to self-employment tax. Standard rental with limited services is Schedule E.

### Forgetting To Exclude Land From Depreciable Basis

Land is not depreciable. The purchase price must be allocated between land and building, and only the building is depreciated over 27.5 years.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has all rental income been identified, including advance rent (income in year received), lease cancellation payments, nonrefundable fees, and fair market value of services received in lieu of rent?
- [ ] Have refundable security deposits been excluded from current income and included only in the year forfeited or applied, with nonrefundable fees included in the year received?
- [ ] Have operating expenses been deducted and allocated between rental and personal use for mixed-use properties based on the use pattern?
- [ ] Have repairs (currently deductible) been distinguished from improvements (capitalized and depreciated), with safe harbors applied where available?
- [ ] Has depreciation been computed on the building (27.5 years, straight-line, excluding land) and on furniture and appliances (shorter periods), beginning when placed in service?
- [ ] Has land been excluded from the depreciable basis, with the purchase price allocated between land and building?
- [ ] Is the rental activity reported on Schedule E (or Schedule C if substantial services are provided), with each property and expense category correctly classified?
- [ ] Is the rental reporting documented with lease records, receipts, bills, and a depreciation schedule supporting the reported amounts?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal rental income information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
