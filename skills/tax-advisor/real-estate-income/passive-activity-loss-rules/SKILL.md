# Passive Activity Loss Rules

The passive activity loss (PAL) rules limit the ability to deduct losses from activities in which the taxpayer does not materially participate. Rental activities are generally per se passive, regardless of the owner's participation, unless an exception applies. Losses from passive activities can only offset income from passive activities; excess losses are suspended and carried forward until the taxpayer has passive income or disposes of their entire interest in the activity. These rules prevent taxpayers from using losses from rental properties or limited partnership interests to shelter wages, portfolio income, or active business income. An agent who ignores the PAL rules, or who applies them without checking material participation and the rental exceptions, will overstate the current deductibility of losses.

Agents commonly miss that rental activities are per se passive, that the real estate professional exception can change the classification, that the active participation exception allows a limited loss deduction for certain landlords, and that suspended losses are released on full disposition of the activity. The harm is a taxpayer who deducts passive losses against active income (creating examination exposure and tax deficiency) or who fails to claim available exceptions (overpaying tax).

This skill covers the passive activity loss rules under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the rules are complex and fact-specific, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Classify Each Activity As Passive Or Nonpassive

The PAL rules divide activities into passive and nonpassive. Passive activities are trade or business activities in which the taxpayer does not materially participate, and rental activities (which are generally per se passive regardless of participation). Nonpassive activities include active businesses in which the taxpayer materially participates, portfolio income (interest, dividends, capital gains), and wage income. Losses from passive activities can only offset income from passive activities; they cannot offset nonpassive income.

Classify each of the taxpayer's activities. A rental property is passive unless an exception applies (real estate professional status or the short-term rental exception). A limited partnership interest is passive (limited partners are generally presumed not to materially participate). An S corporation or sole proprietorship is passive if the taxpayer does not materially participate, and nonpassive if they do. The classification is the first step; it determines whether losses are currently deductible or suspended. Do not assume a business loss is deductible without confirming material participation.

### Apply The Material Participation Tests

Material participation is determined by seven tests, the most common of which are the 500-hour test (the taxpayer participates more than 500 hours in the year), the substantially-all test (the taxpayer's participation constitutes substantially all of the participation in the activity), and the 100-hour-and-more-than-any-individual test (the taxpayer participates more than 100 hours and more than any other individual). Participation by a spouse is counted together for married filing jointly. Work done by an owner-employee in their own business counts, but investor-level work (reviewing financial statements, organizing records) generally does not count.

Document the participation hours for each activity, especially for businesses near the thresholds. The 500-hour test is objective and straightforward to document with a time log. The 100-hour test requires comparing the taxpayer's hours to every other participant, which can be difficult. The historical participation tests (participation in 5 of the preceding 10 years, or in any 3 prior years for a personal service activity) allow material participation based on past involvement. Confirm which test is met and document the basis. Without documentation, the IRS may reclassify the activity as passive, suspending the losses.

### Treat Rental Activities As Per Se Passive

Rental real estate activities are generally per se passive, meaning they are passive regardless of the owner's participation level. This rule exists because Congress viewed rental real estate as inherently an investment activity for most owners. Even a landlord who spends 1,000 hours a year managing a rental property has a passive activity under the default rule, unless an exception applies. This is a critical distinction from business activities, where material participation can make the activity nonpassive.

Confirm that rental activities are classified as passive under the default rule before considering exceptions. The per se passive classification means rental losses are suspended (subject to the active participation exception and the real estate professional exception) unless the taxpayer qualifies for an exception. Do not treat rental losses as deductible against active income merely because the owner participates significantly; the default rule suspends them.

### Apply The Active Participation Exception For Up To 25,000 Dollars

An exception to the per se passive rule allows up to 25,000 dollars of rental losses to offset nonpassive income for taxpayers who actively participate in the rental activity. Active participation is a lower standard than material participation; it requires making management decisions (approving tenants, deciding rental terms, approving repairs) but does not require a specific hour threshold. The 25,000 dollar exception is phased out for taxpayers with adjusted gross income between 100,000 and 150,000 dollars (the phaseout is 50 cents per dollar of AGI over 100,000, so the full 25,000 is available at 100,000 AGI and zero at 150,000).

Confirm whether the taxpayer actively participates (most hands-on landlords do) and apply the 25,000 dollar exception with the AGI phaseout. The exception applies per taxpayer (not per property), so a landlord with multiple properties gets one 25,000 dollar allowance (or 12,500 each for married filing separately under certain conditions). Compute the phaseout based on AGI before the rental loss. The exception allows a meaningful current deduction for small landlords with moderate income, but it disappears for higher-income taxpayers.

### Apply The Real Estate Professional Exception

The real estate professional exception can reclassify rental activities as nonpassive, allowing losses to offset nonpassive income without the 25,000 dollar limit. To qualify, the taxpayer must spend more than half of their personal service time (more than 750 hours) in real property trades or businesses in which they materially participate, and they must materially participate in each rental activity (or group rental activities and materially participate in the group). This is a demanding test that effectively requires real estate to be the taxpayer's primary occupation.

Confirm whether the taxpayer qualifies as a real estate professional before applying the exception. The 750-hour and more-than-half tests are strict and require detailed time records. Material participation in each rental (or a valid grouping election) is also required. A taxpayer with a full-time non-real-estate job generally cannot meet the more-than-half test. See the real estate professional status skill for the detailed requirements. The exception is powerful (it removes the passive classification and the loss limitation) but it is narrowly available.

### Track Suspended Losses And Release On Disposition

Passive losses that are suspended (not deductible in the current year because there is no passive income) are carried forward indefinitely. They are released and deductible in full in the year the taxpayer disposes of their entire interest in the activity in a transaction that fully terminates the passive activity (generally a sale to an unrelated party at fair market value). Partial dispositions or related-party transactions may not release the suspended losses.

Track the suspended losses by activity and by year. When the activity is fully disposed of, release the suspended losses and deduct them against any income (including nonpassive income) in that year. The release is in addition to any gain or loss on the sale itself. Confirm that the disposition is a complete termination of the passive activity (sale of the entire interest to an unrelated party); a partial sale or a gift does not fully release the losses. The suspended loss carryforward is a valuable asset that reduces the tax on a future sale, and it must be tracked accurately.

### Group Activities To Optimize Participation

A taxpayer can elect to group multiple activities into a single activity for purposes of the material participation and PAL rules. Grouping can help a taxpayer meet the material participation tests by combining hours across related activities. For example, a taxpayer with several rental properties can group them into one activity and aggregate the participation hours to meet the 500-hour test, making the grouped activity nonpassive (if the taxpayer is a real estate professional).

Confirm whether grouping is beneficial and whether a valid grouping election has been made. Grouping is generally irrevocable once made (unless there is a material change in facts). The grouped activities must share common features (similar types of business, geographical location, interdependence). Grouping rental properties can help a real estate professional meet material participation, but it can also concentrate losses or income. Analyze the grouping decision based on the taxpayer's participation pattern and overall strategy.

## Common Traps

### Deducting Rental Losses Against Active Income Without An Exception

Rental activities are per se passive. Losses are suspended unless the active participation exception (up to 25,000 dollars, phased out) or the real estate professional exception applies. Deducting without an exception overstates the loss.

### Failing To Document Material Participation Hours

Material participation requires documented hours meeting one of the seven tests. Without a time log, the IRS may reclassify the activity as passive, suspending the losses.

### Overlooking The Active Participation Exception

Many small landlords qualify for active participation (a low standard) and can deduct up to 25,000 dollars of rental losses, subject to the AGI phaseout. Missing this exception forgoes a current deduction.

### Assuming The Real Estate Professional Exception Is Easy To Meet

The 750-hour and more-than-half tests are demanding and require real estate to be the primary occupation. A taxpayer with a full-time job generally cannot qualify. Confirm the tests carefully.

### Forgetting To Track Suspended Losses For Release On Sale

Suspended passive losses carry forward and are released on full disposition. Failing to track them loses the deduction on sale. The carryforward is a valuable asset.

### Grouping Activities Without Considering The Irrevocability

Grouping elections are generally irrevocable. Grouping without analyzing the long-term effect can lock in an unfavorable classification. Analyze before grouping.

### Treating A Partial Sale As Releasing Suspended Losses

Only a complete termination of the passive activity (full disposition to an unrelated party) releases suspended losses. A partial sale or a related-party transfer does not fully release them.

- [ ] Has material participation been documented for each business activity using one of the seven tests, with a time log or other evidence?
- [ ] Have rental activities been treated as per se passive under the default rule, with exceptions (active participation or real estate professional) analyzed?
- [ ] Has the active participation exception (up to 25,000 dollars) been applied for qualifying landlords, with the AGI phaseout computed?
- [ ] Has the real estate professional exception been analyzed against the 750-hour and more-than-half tests before applying it?
- [ ] Have suspended passive losses been tracked by activity and year for carryforward and release on full disposition?
- [ ] Has any grouping election been analyzed for its effect on material participation and its irrevocability, before being made?
