# Classification Tests

Worker classification is decided by applying a legal test to the facts of the relationship, not by choosing the label that suits the business. The complication is that there is no single universal test. Federal tax law uses a common-law control analysis, the Department of Labor and many courts apply an economic-reality test, several states impose a stricter ABC test for employment and unemployment purposes, and specific statutes have their own definitions. A worker can be a contractor under one test and an employee under another, and the agent who applies only the federal tax test will miss exposure that arises under labor, unemployment, or state law.

Agents tend to grab one familiar test and treat its outcome as the answer. They may rely on the IRS three-category factors as if they were a checklist with a fixed score, or assume that an independent-contractor agreement satisfies every test. They often overlook that state ABC tests can be far harder to pass, that the burden of proof can shift, and that different tests apply to different benefits and protections. The harm is misclassification that survives a shallow review but fails under the test that actually governs the dispute.

This skill covers how to select and apply the correct classification tests for U.S. worker status, including the federal common-law factors, the economic-reality approach, and state ABC tests, and how to reconcile divergent outcomes. It does not cover the penalties that flow from misclassification or the Section 530 safe harbor, which are separate skills. Tests and their application evolve through case law and statute; confirm the current standard for the relevant jurisdiction and agency. Classification is a high-risk determination; this is not legal or tax advice, and contested or borderline cases must be escalated to a qualified professional.

## Core Rules

### Identify Which Test Governs Before Applying Any Test

The first step is to determine which body of law the classification question arises under, because each applies its own test. Federal employment tax uses the common-law control standard. Federal wage-and-hour and many employment-law claims use an economic-reality test focused on economic dependence. State unemployment and, in some states, employment and workers' compensation law use an ABC test that presumes employment unless all three prongs are met. Specific contexts, such as federal benefits or immigration, may impose additional definitions.

The agent should not begin a factor analysis until the governing law is identified. A worker who is plausibly a contractor under the IRS common-law factors may fail an ABC test because the work is within the usual course of the hiring entity's business. Map the purpose of the classification question first: is it for tax withholding, wage-and-hour exposure, unemployment registration, workers' compensation, or a state labor claim? Each purpose can require a different test, and the analysis must be run for each that is in play.

### Apply The IRS Common-Law Factors As A Whole

For federal tax purposes, classification turns on the common-law control test, organized by the IRS into three categories of evidence: behavioral control, financial control, and the type of relationship. Behavioral control examines whether the business directs how, when, and where work is done, provides training, and sets procedures. Financial control examines the worker's investment, unreimbursed expenses, opportunity for profit or loss, services available to the market, and method of payment. The relationship category examines written contracts, benefits, permanency, and whether the services are a key aspect of the business.

No single factor is decisive, and the factors are not scored numerically. The agent should gather evidence across all three categories and weigh the relationship as a whole, with the right to control carrying more weight than how control is actually exercised. The presence of a contract, invoices, or a contractor title is only one piece of evidence. Where the factors point in different directions, the case is genuinely uncertain and should be flagged for professional review or an official determination rather than resolved by picking the convenient factors.

### Apply The Economic-Reality Test For Wage And Employment Claims

The economic-reality test, used by the Department of Labor and many courts, asks whether the worker is economically dependent on the employer or is genuinely in business for themselves. It examines factors such as the degree of control, the worker's opportunity for profit or loss, the investment in equipment or materials, whether the work requires special skill, the permanency and duration of the relationship, and the extent to which the work is an integral part of the employer's business.

This test is broader and more worker-protective than a narrow control analysis. A worker with limited skill, no independent clientele, no investment, and a long-term exclusive relationship is likely an employee under economic reality even if the business does not micromanage daily tasks. The agent should not assume that low behavioral control equals contractor status under this test, because economic dependence can establish employment without day-to-day direction. Run this test separately whenever wage-and-hour, overtime, or employment-law exposure is on the table.

### Apply The ABC Test Where State Law Requires It

Several states use an ABC test for unemployment and employment purposes that presumes a worker is an employee unless the hiring entity proves all three of the following: the worker is free from control and direction, the work is performed outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business, and the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade or business. Failure on any one prong makes the worker an employee under that test.

The ABC test is structurally different because it shifts the burden to the employer and treats employment as the default. The second prong is the most common point of failure: if the worker performs work that is part of the hiring entity's usual course of business, the test is not met regardless of autonomy. The agent must identify which states use ABC tests and for which purposes, because a classification that passes the IRS factors can fail the ABC test in the same state. Some states also provide statutory exemptions for specific occupations or industries, which must be checked against the current statute.

### Reconcile Divergent Outcomes Explicitly

Because the tests differ, a single worker can be classified differently across regimes. A worker may be a contractor for federal tax purposes and an employee for state unemployment, or vice versa. The agent should not paper over the divergence by selecting one outcome as universal. Instead, state the classification under each applicable test and the consequences that follow from each.

This reconciliation matters because the obligations differ by regime. Federal tax classification drives withholding and employment tax deposits, state unemployment classification drives SUTA registration and contributions, and wage-and-hour classification drives overtime and minimum wage exposure. A clean contractor determination under one test does not neutralize the others. The agent should present a per-regime result and flag the highest-exposure outcome for escalation.

### Gather Evidence Consistent With The Controlling Test

The evidence needed depends on the test. Control-based tests call for documentation of direction, scheduling, training, tools, and procedures. Economic-reality and ABC tests additionally call for evidence of the worker's independent business, other clients, advertising, business licenses, insurance, investment, and whether the work is part of the hiring entity's core operations. A contract alone is rarely sufficient under any test.

The agent should build an evidence file that maps to the controlling test's factors, not a generic contractor file. Include the statement of work, actual communications about direction, proof of other clients, the worker's business registrations, and the basis for any claimed exemption. Where the relationship has changed over time, document the evolution, because a relationship that began as arm's-length can drift into employee-like integration. Evidence should be contemporaneous and dated, not reconstructed after a dispute arises.

### Treat Statutory And Special-Status Workers Separately

Some workers have classification set by statute regardless of the common-law facts. Statutory employees are independent contractors under common law but are treated as employees for FICA and possibly FUTA purposes, requiring withholding in defined circumstances. Statutory nonemployees, such as direct sellers and certain real-estate agents, are excluded from employee status by statute. Corporate officers are generally statutory employees for FICA purposes unless a narrow exception applies.

The agent should not run a factor analysis on a worker whose status is fixed by statute. Identify whether the worker falls into a statutory category first, then apply the common-law or state tests only if no statute controls. Misapplying a factor test to a statutory employee can lead to failing to withhold FICA that is required by law, while misclassifying a statutory nonemployee as an employee creates unnecessary withholding and reporting. The statutory categories are narrow and defined by specific criteria, so confirm the fit rather than assuming it.

## Common Traps

### Applying Only The Federal Tax Test

The IRS common-law factors answer the federal tax question, not the wage-and-hour, unemployment, or state-law questions. A worker can pass the IRS factors and fail an ABC or economic-reality test in the same jurisdiction.

### Treating The Three IRS Categories As A Scored Checklist

The behavioral, financial, and relationship categories are weighed as a whole, with the right to control weighted most heavily. Counting factors like a quiz produces a false sense of certainty in genuinely mixed cases.

### Assuming An Independent-Contractor Agreement Settles The Question

A contract is one piece of evidence under every test. If actual practice shows control, integration, or economic dependence, the agreement's label does not control the legal outcome.

### Ignoring The ABC Test's Burden Shift

ABC tests presume employment and place the burden on the employer to prove all prongs. Approaching an ABC jurisdiction with a control-first mindset misses that the usual-course-of-business prong commonly defeats classification as a contractor.

### Overlooking Statutory Employees And Nonemployees

Statutory employees require FICA withholding despite being common-law contractors, and statutory nonemployees are excluded from employee status by law. Running a factor test on these workers instead of applying the statute produces the wrong result.

### Conflating Different Tests Into One Verdict

Selecting the most favorable test and presenting it as the classification ignores divergent outcomes. Each regime's classification and its consequences must be stated separately.

### Failing To Re-Test As The Relationship Changes

A relationship that starts as a clean contractor arrangement can become employee-like through increased direction, exclusivity, or integration. A one-time classification is not durable without periodic re-testing.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the governing body of law been identified for each purpose of the classification question, rather than a single test assumed to apply universally?
- [ ] Are the IRS common-law factors weighed as a whole with the right to control weighted most heavily, rather than scored numerically?
- [ ] Has the economic-reality test been run separately for any wage-and-hour or employment-law exposure, recognizing it is broader than behavioral control?
- [ ] In ABC-test states, is employment treated as the default with the burden on the employer, and has the usual-course-of-business prong been specifically examined?
- [ ] Where tests diverge, is the classification stated per regime with the consequences and the highest-exposure outcome flagged?
- [ ] Does the evidence file map to the controlling test's factors, including proof of independent business, other clients, and contemporaneous direction records?
- [ ] Have statutory employees, statutory nonemployees, corporate officers, and other special-status categories been checked before applying a factor test?
- [ ] Has the classification been documented with a review date for relationships that may drift toward employee status over time?
- [ ] Have current state ABC tests, exemptions, and the relevant agency standards been confirmed for the applicable jurisdiction and period?
- [ ] Has the agent escalated borderline, multi-jurisdiction, or contested classifications to a qualified tax professional or employment counsel, recognizing this is not legal or tax advice?
