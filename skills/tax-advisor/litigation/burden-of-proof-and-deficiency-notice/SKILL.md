# Burden Of Proof And Deficiency Notice

The burden of proof determines who must produce evidence and who wins if the evidence is evenly balanced, and in tax litigation this single procedural rule can decide the case before the merits are even reached. The judgment problem is that the default burden allocation in tax cases is generally unfavorable to the taxpayer, the notice of deficiency carries a presumption of correctness that the taxpayer must overcome, and the statutory burden-shifting provision (section 7491) has strict prerequisites that are easy to miss. A taxpayer who litigates without understanding who bears the burden on each issue, and what evidence is required to meet or shift it, can lose a winnable case by failing to produce the right proof.

Agents tend to assume that because the IRS made the assessment, the IRS must prove it, or they overestimate the reach of section 7491 burden-shifting. They may not recognize that the notice of deficiency is presumptively correct and that the taxpayer bears the burden of disproving it, that the burden-shifting statute requires credible evidence plus cooperation and recordkeeping prerequisites, that the burden on fraud is uniquely on the IRS, or that the burden on penalties has its own allocation. The harm is an evidentiary strategy aimed at the wrong party's obligation, a failure to introduce the evidence needed to shift the burden, or a loss on a case where the merits were favorable but the proof was insufficient.

This skill covers the burden of proof in federal tax litigation, the presumptive correctness of the deficiency notice, the section 7491 burden-shifting rules, and the evidentiary planning that flows from the burden allocation. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; burden-of-proof analysis depends on the specific issues, the procedural posture, and the evidentiary record, and qualified tax litigation counsel must be consulted for specific situations. Evidentiary and procedural rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Presumptive Correctness Of The Notice Of Deficiency

A notice of deficiency is the IRS's determination that the taxpayer owes additional tax, and in the Tax Court it carries a presumption of correctness. This means the IRS's determination is presumed correct, and the taxpayer bears the burden of proving it wrong by introducing evidence that contradicts the determination. The presumption does not mean the IRS's number is conclusively right, but it does mean that if the taxpayer produces no contrary evidence, the deficiency stands. The presumption is particularly powerful where the IRS reconstructs income from bank deposits or net worth and the taxpayer has no records to rebut the reconstruction.

Analyze the deficiency notice to understand what the IRS determined and what the taxpayer must rebut. Identify the specific adjustments and the factual basis the IRS asserted (if any), because the taxpayer's evidentiary burden is to produce contrary evidence on each. A taxpayer who simply denies the deficiency without producing records or affirmative evidence will lose under the presumption. The first strategic question is always what evidence is needed to overcome the presumption for each adjustment.

### Determine The Default Burden Allocation For Each Issue

The default rule in Tax Court is that the taxpayer bears the burden of proof on most factual issues, including whether the IRS's determination of deficiency is wrong. This means the taxpayer must come forward with evidence supporting their position, and if the evidence is evenly balanced, the taxpayer loses because they did not meet their burden. For deductions, the taxpayer bears the burden of proving entitlement to the deduction and the amount. For the characterization of income or transactions, the taxpayer generally bears the burden of proving the correct treatment.

Map the burden allocation for each disputed issue under the default rule, and build the evidentiary strategy around the party who bears the burden. For issues where the taxpayer bears the burden, the strategy is to introduce affirmative evidence (records, testimony, expert opinions) that meets the standard. Assuming the IRS must disprove the taxpayer's position is a fundamental error that leads to insufficient proof and a loss.

### Apply Section 7491 Burden-Shifting Only When Prerequisites Are Met

Section 7491(a) can shift the burden of proof on factual issues from the taxpayer to the IRS, but only if several prerequisites are met. The taxpayer must introduce credible evidence with respect to the factual issue, must have substantially complied with the substantiation and recordkeeping requirements, must have cooperated with the IRS's reasonable requests for information, and (for entities other than individuals) must meet certain net worth requirements. If all prerequisites are met, the burden shifts to the IRS on that factual issue. The shift does not apply to the burden of production on penalties in the same way, and it never applies to fraud.

Evaluate section 7491 for each factual issue and determine whether the prerequisites can be met. The credible-evidence requirement means the taxpayer must still come forward with evidence; it is not an automatic shift. The cooperation and recordkeeping requirements mean a taxpayer who was uncooperative during the audit or who kept inadequate records cannot benefit. Build the strategy to satisfy the prerequisites where possible, but do not assume the burden has shifted until each element is confirmed.

### Recognize The IRS's Unique Burden On Fraud And Certain Penalties

The burden allocation changes for fraud and certain penalties. For the civil fraud penalty under section 6663, the IRS bears the burden of proving fraud by clear and convincing evidence, and this burden never shifts to the taxpayer under section 7491. For accuracy-related penalties, the IRS generally has the initial burden of production (it must come forward with evidence that the penalty is appropriate), but once it meets that threshold the burden of persuasion can rest on the taxpayer. The burden on penalties is a distinct analysis from the burden on the underlying tax.

Analyze the penalty burden separately from the tax burden. Where fraud is asserted, the defense can focus on the IRS's failure to meet its clear-and-convincing burden, which is a high standard. Where accuracy-related penalties are asserted, assess whether the IRS has met its burden of production and what the taxpayer must do to meet any shifted burden of persuasion. Conflating the tax burden with the penalty burden leads to a defense that misses the penalty-specific opportunities.

### Plan The Evidentiary Record Around The Burden Allocation

The burden allocation drives the evidentiary plan. For each issue where the taxpayer bears the burden, identify the specific evidence needed (documents, witness testimony, expert reports), the standard of proof, and the potential weaknesses. For issues where the IRS bears the burden (fraud), the strategy may be to highlight the gaps in the IRS's proof rather than to introduce extensive affirmative evidence. The evidentiary record should be built deliberately to meet the burden on each issue, with a margin for the risks of exclusion, credibility challenges, and evidentiary rulings.

Build an evidence map that ties each disputed issue to the specific exhibits, witnesses, and legal standards that will meet the burden. Identify the weakest links in the evidentiary chain and develop backup evidence or fallback arguments. A common failure is to assume that the existence of records is enough, without confirming that the records are admissible, that a witness can authenticate them, and that they actually address the disputed element. The evidentiary plan must be litigation-tested, not just audit-grade.

### Distinguish Substantiation Requirements From The General Burden Of Proof

Certain tax positions have statutory substantiation requirements that are stricter than the general burden of proof. Charitable contributions of $250 or more require a contemporaneous written acknowledgment; vehicle expenses require adequate records or sufficient evidence correlating to the mileage; travel and entertainment require records of amount, time, place, and business purpose. Meeting these specific substantiation requirements is a precondition to claiming the deduction, and failure to meet them can result in disallowance regardless of the general burden of proof or section 7491.

Audit the evidentiary record against the specific substantiation requirements for each disputed deduction, not just against the general burden. A taxpayer who has general records but lacks the specific contemporaneous acknowledgment for a $250 charitable contribution will lose the deduction even if they would otherwise meet the burden of proof. The substantiation requirements are non-negotiable thresholds that must be cleared independently of the burden allocation.

### Address The Deficiency Notice's Sufficiency And The Determination Basis

The sufficiency of the deficiency notice itself can be a litigation issue. A notice of deficiency must contain the information necessary to inform the taxpayer of the basis for the deficiency, and in some cases a notice that is wholly without foundation (a "naked" assessment with no factual basis) can be challenged. However, the bar for challenging the notice's sufficiency is high, and the IRS's determination is generally upheld if it provides enough information for the taxpayer to understand and contest the adjustment. The challenge is most viable where the IRS made no determination at all or where the notice is arbitrary.

Evaluate whether the deficiency notice has a sufficiency vulnerability, but do not over-rely on this defense. The presumption of correctness is strong, and challenges to the notice's sufficiency succeed only in narrow circumstances. The primary strategy should be to rebut the determination with evidence, with the sufficiency challenge as a secondary argument where the facts support it.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The IRS Must Prove Its Own Assessment

The symptom is believing that because the IRS asserted the deficiency, the IRS must prove it. The trap is that the notice of deficiency is presumptively correct and the taxpayer bears the burden of rebutting it, so passivity results in the deficiency standing by default. The direction is to treat the notice as presumptively correct and to build an affirmative evidentiary case to rebut it.

### Overestimating Section 7491 Burden-Shifting

The symptom is assuming the burden has shifted to the IRS because the taxpayer filed a Tax Court petition. The trap is that section 7491 requires credible evidence, cooperation, recordkeeping, and (for entities) net worth compliance, and missing any prerequisite means no shift, leaving the burden on the taxpayer. The direction is to evaluate each prerequisite for each issue and not to assume a shift until all are confirmed.

### Conflating The Tax Burden With The Penalty Burden

The symptom is building one evidentiary strategy for the whole case. The trap is that the burden on fraud is on the IRS by clear and convincing evidence, the burden on accuracy-related penalties has its own allocation, and the burden on the underlying tax is separate, so a single strategy misses the penalty-specific opportunities. The direction is to analyze the burden separately for the tax and for each penalty.

### Failing To Meet Specific Substantiation Requirements

The symptom is relying on general records to prove deductions with statutory substantiation rules. The trap is that the contemporaneous acknowledgment for $250 charitable contributions, the mileage log for vehicle expenses, and similar requirements are non-negotiable thresholds, and general records do not satisfy them, resulting in disallowance regardless of the burden of proof. The direction is to audit the evidence against the specific substantiation requirements for each deduction.

### Building An Audit-Grade Rather Than Litigation-Grade Evidentiary Record

The symptom is assuming the records that survived the audit will win at trial. The trap is that litigation requires admissible, authenticated evidence with witnesses to sponsor it, and audit-grade records may be excluded or unauthenticated, causing the taxpayer to fail the burden despite having the underlying documents. The direction is to build a litigation-tested evidence map with authentication, witnesses, and fallback evidence for each issue.

### Ignoring The IRS's Clear-And-Convincing Burden On Fraud

The symptom is treating a fraud defense like a negligence defense. The trap is that the IRS bears the burden of proving fraud by clear and convincing evidence, which is a high standard, and a defense that focuses on the gaps in the IRS's proof rather than on building affirmative evidence may be more effective. The direction is to exploit the IRS's high burden by highlighting evidentiary gaps in the fraud case.

### Over-Relying On A Sufficiency Challenge To The Notice

The symptom is betting the case on the argument that the deficiency notice is insufficient. The trap is that the presumption of correctness is strong and sufficiency challenges succeed only in narrow circumstances, so an over-reliance on this defense leaves the merits unaddressed and likely lost. The direction is to make the sufficiency challenge a secondary argument and to build the primary strategy around rebutting the determination with evidence.
