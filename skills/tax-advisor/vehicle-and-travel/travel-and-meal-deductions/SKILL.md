# Travel and Meal Deductions

Travel and meal deductions are governed by a strict distinction between travel (being away from the tax home overnight in pursuit of business) and local transportation (getting to and from a regular workplace), and the two follow different substantiation and limitation rules. The judgment problem is that the boundary is factual, not formal: a trip qualifies as travel only if it requires sleep or rest away from the tax home, is primarily for business, and is not a personal vacation dressed up with a business meeting. Meals, whether incurred during travel or as business entertainment, are generally limited to 50 percent of cost, and the temporary 100 percent deduction for restaurant meals in 2021 and 2022 has expired, so applying the old rule overstates the deduction.

Agents frequently conflate travel with commuting, treat a same-day out-of-town trip as deductible travel when it lacks the overnight requirement, or deduct meals without the contemporaneous substantiation (who, what, when, where, why, and how much) that Section 274 demands. They deduct a spouse's or companion's travel costs without meeting the strict bona fide business employee test, and they fail to allocate mixed-purpose trips where personal days are combined with business days. The harm is a disallowed deduction on audit, penalties for lack of substantiation, and a mischaracterization that cascades into incorrect per diem calculations and meal limits.

This skill covers business travel deductions, the 50 percent meal limitation, per diem versus actual substantiation, and the nondeductibility of companion travel, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the deductibility of any specific trip depends on its facts, the tax home determination, and the substantiation maintained, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Dollar amounts are 2024 figures and change annually.

## Core Rules

### Establish That The Trip Requires An Overnight Stay Away From The Tax Home

Deductible travel requires that the taxpayer be away from the tax home (the regular or principal place of business, or the regular place of abode in a realistic living situation) substantially longer than an ordinary day and need sleep or rest to meet the demands of the work. The sleep-or-rest rule is the bright line: a same-day trip, even one across the country, is local transportation, not travel, unless sleep or rest away from home is required. The tax home is generally the primary place of business, not the personal residence, so a taxpayer who works in multiple cities must determine which is the principal place before any trip's deductibility can be assessed.

Confirm the tax home first, because a taxpayer with no regular place of business (an itinerant worker) may have no tax home and thus no deductible travel at all. Then confirm the trip required an overnight stay with a need for sleep or rest, which is a factual test, not just the presence of a hotel receipt. A day trip to a client three hours away that returns the same evening is local transportation (deductible as a vehicle expense if self-employed, but not as travel), not travel. Document the business purpose and the overnight requirement, because the IRS challenges trips that look like personal travel with a business meeting appended.

### Apply The Primary-Purpose Test To Mixed-Purpose Trips

If a trip combines business and personal activities, deductibility turns on the primary purpose of the trip, measured by the time and the purpose of the trip viewed as a whole. If the primary purpose is business (more than half the days are business days, and the business activities are the main reason for the trip), the transportation costs to and from the destination are fully deductible, but lodging, meals, and other costs during personal days are not. If the primary purpose is personal, none of the transportation is deductible, though direct business expenses at the destination (a conference fee, local transportation to a meeting) may still be deductible.

Count business days carefully: travel days generally count as business days, as do days with more than four hours of business activity, while personal days (sightseeing, visiting family) do not. For a seven-day trip with four business days, two personal days, and one travel day each way, the primary purpose is business, so round-trip airfare is deductible, but lodging and meals on the two personal days are not. Document the daily itinerary with business hours and activities, because the allocation is challenged when personal days are appended to a conference. Never deduct the costs of a side trip taken purely for personal reasons.

### Apply The 50 Percent Meal Limit And Recognize The Expired 100 Percent Rule

Business meals are generally limited to 50 percent of the actual cost (or 50 percent of the standard meal per diem rate) whether they are incurred during business travel, eaten with a client, or provided at the workplace for the convenience of the employer (subject to the post-TCJA rules). The temporary 100 percent deduction for food and beverages provided by a restaurant, which applied in 2021 and 2022 under the Consolidated Appropriations Act, has expired, so meals are back to the 50 percent limit for 2024 and later. Applying the expired 100 percent rule overstates the deduction.

Apply 50 percent to the full cost of the meal including tax and tip, after any employer reimbursement. For self-employed taxpayers, the meals must have a clear business purpose and the taxpayer or an employee must be present. Note that meals that are recreational or social in nature, or de minimis fringe benefits, follow different rules. Entertainment costs, even if food is served, are separately nondeductible under the TCJA (see the entertainment deduction limits skill). Confirm the meal is a deductible business meal (ordinary, necessary, business conducted, taxpayer present) and apply 50 percent, not 100 percent, for current years.

### Substantiate With The Five Elements Who What When Where Why And How Much

Section 274(d) requires adequate records or sufficient evidence corroborating the taxpayer's own statement for travel, meals, and entertainment, documenting the amount, time and date, place, business purpose, and business relationship of the persons involved. A credit card receipt alone is insufficient because it lacks the business purpose and the identities of the attendees; a diary or log with the five elements, paired with the receipt, satisfies the rule. The Cohan rule (reasonable estimate) does not apply to travel, meals, and entertainment, so unsubstantiated amounts are disallowed in full.

Require documentation of who (the taxpayer and the business guests), what (the business discussed or the meal), when (date and time), where (location), why (the specific business purpose), and how much (the cost, with a receipt for lodging and for meals over 75 dollars). A contemporaneous record made at or near the time of the event is far stronger than a later reconstruction. For per diem, the time, place, and business purpose are still required even though the meal amount is the government rate. Flag any client whose documentation is incomplete, because the deduction is fully disallowed for the missing elements, not reduced by a percentage.

### Use Per Diem To Simplify But Know Its Limits

Per diem allows a taxpayer to deduct a set government daily rate for lodging, meals, and incidentals (M&IE) instead of actual costs, simplifying substantiation. For self-employed taxpayers and employees, the meals-and-incidental-expense per diem can be used, but the lodging per diem generally cannot (lodging must be actual for self-employed; employers can use lodging per diem for reimbursed employees under an accountable plan). The per diem rate is the federal CONUS rate for domestic travel or the foreign rate for international travel, and the high-low substantiation method provides a simplified single rate for high-cost and other localities.

Apply per diem correctly: it covers meals and incidentals, and the 50 percent limit applies to the M&IE portion (so only 50 percent of the meal per diem is deductible for a self-employed taxpayer). Per diem does not eliminate the need to document time, place, and business purpose, only the meal amount. For lodging, self-employed taxpayers must use actual costs with receipts. Do not use per diem for a trip of fewer than the applicable threshold without checking the partial-day rules. Per diem is a simplification, not a license to skip substantiation of the trip's business nature.

### Apply The Spouse And Companion Nondeductibility Rule Strictly

Costs of a spouse, dependent, or other companion who accompanies the taxpayer on a business trip are not deductible unless the companion is an employee of the taxpayer, the travel is for a bona fide business purpose, and the expenses would otherwise be deductible by the companion. The mere fact that the spouse attends business dinners or provides informal support is insufficient; the spouse must be a bona fide employee performing necessary work. This rule also applies to lodging (a double room is deductible only up to the single-room rate if the spouse is not a qualifying employee) and to meals and transportation.

Scrutinize any trip where a spouse or family member travels along. Unless the companion is a W-2 employee with a real business role, deduct the single rate for lodging (the additional cost of the double room is a nondeductible personal expense) and exclude the companion's airfare and meals entirely. A common error is deducting the full double-occupancy lodging because the taxpayer would have stayed alone anyway; the rule allocates the excess to personal. Document the companion's employee status and bona fide business role, or exclude their costs.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Same-Day Out-Of-Town Trip As Deductible Travel

Travel requires an overnight stay with a need for sleep or rest. A same-day trip, however far, is local transportation, not travel, so lodging and per diem do not apply. Applying travel rules to a day trip misstates the deduction.

### Applying The Expired 100 Percent Restaurant Meal Deduction

The 100 percent deduction for restaurant meals applied only in 2021 and 2022 and has expired. Meals are back to the 50 percent limit for 2024 and later. Using the old rule overstates the deduction by the extra 50 percent.

### Deducting Personal Days On A Mixed-Purpose Trip

If the trip's primary purpose is business, transportation is deductible, but lodging, meals, and costs on personal days are not. Deducting the full trip as if every day were business overstates the deduction and ignores the day-count allocation.

### Substantiating Only With A Credit Card Receipt

A receipt shows the amount but not the business purpose or the attendees. Section 274(d) requires all five elements, and the Cohan estimate does not apply. A receipt without a contemporaneous log is disallowed.

### Deducting A Spouse's Travel Without The Bona Fide Employee Test

A companion's costs are deductible only if the companion is an employee with a real business role. Attending dinners or providing moral support is insufficient. Deducting the double-room rate or the spouse's airfare without meeting the test is a personal expense.

### Using Lodging Per Diem For A Self-Employed Taxpayer

Self-employed taxpayers cannot use the lodging per diem and must substantiate actual lodging costs with receipts. Only the meals-and-incidental per diem is available to them. Applying lodging per diem to a self-employed filer overstates or misstates the deduction.

### Counting Personal Vacation Days As Business Days

A day counts as a business day only if more than four hours of business activity occur. Padding the business-day count to make a personal trip look primarily business triggers disallowance of transportation and the personal-day costs.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the trip been confirmed as deductible travel requiring an overnight stay with a need for sleep or rest away from the tax home, rather than local transportation or commuting?
- [ ] Has the tax home been established (principal place of business or regular abode), recognizing that an itinerant worker with no regular place may have no deductible travel?
- [ ] For a mixed-purpose trip, has the primary-purpose test been applied (more than half business days), with transportation deductible if primarily business but lodging and meals on personal days excluded?
- [ ] Has the 50 percent meal limit been applied to all business meals, with recognition that the temporary 100 percent restaurant meal rule expired after 2022 and no longer applies for 2024 and later?
- [ ] Has substantiation been confirmed for all five elements (amount, date, place, business purpose, business relationship of attendees), recognizing that a receipt alone is insufficient and the Cohan estimate does not apply?
- [ ] If per diem is used, has it been limited to meals and incidentals for self-employed taxpayers (lodging per diem not allowed), with the 50 percent limit applied to the M&IE portion, and with time, place, and business purpose still documented?
- [ ] Have any spouse or companion travel costs been excluded unless the companion is a bona fide employee with a necessary business role, with lodging limited to the single rate for the personal-use excess?
- [ ] Has the trip been checked for primarily personal character, in which case transportation is nondeductible even if some business is conducted at the destination?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal travel and meal deduction information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
