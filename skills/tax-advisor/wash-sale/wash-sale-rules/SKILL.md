# Wash Sale Rules

The wash sale rule disallows a capital loss when a taxpayer sells a security at a loss and acquires substantially identical securities within a 30-day window before or after the sale. The rule exists to prevent taxpayers from claiming a tax loss while maintaining the same economic position. It is one of the most commonly encountered and commonly misunderstood rules in investment taxation, because it applies automatically based on the facts, regardless of the taxpayer's intent, and it can be triggered by transactions the taxpayer does not even realize are connected.

Agents commonly miss the 61-day window structure (30 days before plus the sale day plus 30 days after), the meaning of substantially identical, the automatic basis and holding period adjustments to replacement shares, the application across multiple accounts and spouses, and the special rule that makes wash sale losses in an IRA permanently disallowed. The harm is a taxpayer who harvests a loss thinking it is deductible, only to find the loss disallowed and the basis adjustment buried in a replacement position, or worse, permanently lost if the replacement was in an IRA.

This skill covers the wash sale rules under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; wash sale rules have nuances and the substantially identical determination can be fact-specific, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The 61-Day Window Correctly

The wash sale period spans 61 days: the date of the sale that produced the loss, plus the 30 days before and the 30 days after. If substantially identical securities are acquired on any day within this window, the loss on the sale is disallowed. The window is symmetric around the sale date, meaning a purchase 30 days before the sale can trigger a wash sale just as a purchase 30 days after can.

The most common trigger is repurchasing the same security shortly after selling at a loss, but a purchase before the sale (for example, buying more shares on a dip and then selling the original loss shares) also triggers the rule. Count the days carefully, including weekends and holidays, and use the trade dates for all transactions. A taxpayer who sells on December 15 and repurchases on January 14 is within the 31-day window and has a wash sale. Do not assume that crossing a calendar month or year boundary avoids the rule.

### Determine Whether Securities Are Substantially Identical

The wash sale rule applies only to acquisitions of substantially identical securities. For stocks and bonds, substantially identical generally means the same security of the same issuer. Stock of one corporation is not substantially identical to stock of another corporation, even if they are in the same industry. However, the determination is more nuanced for other instruments. Options and warrants on a stock may be substantially identical to the stock itself in certain circumstances. Convertible bonds and the underlying stock may be substantially identical if the conversion is immediate and certain.

For mutual funds and exchange-traded funds, the substantially identical question is more complex. Two different S&P 500 index funds are generally not considered substantially identical even though they track the same index, because they are issued by different companies and hold different portfolios. However, two share classes of the same fund, or the same fund held directly and through a different wrapper, may be substantially identical. The substantially identical determination should be made conservatively; when in doubt, treat similar instruments as potentially triggering and seek guidance. Do not assume that "different ticker" means "not substantially identical."

### Adjust The Basis And Holding Period Of Replacement Shares

When a wash sale occurs, the disallowed loss is not lost forever (except in the IRA case); it is added to the basis of the replacement shares. The replacement shares' basis equals their purchase price plus the disallowed loss. The holding period of the replacement shares is also adjusted to include the holding period of the original shares sold at a loss, meaning the original holding period tacks onto the replacement.

These adjustments preserve the economic loss by embedding it in the replacement position, to be recognized when the replacement is eventually sold. The basis adjustment increases the replacement basis (so less gain or more loss on eventual sale), and the holding period tacking can help establish long-term status sooner. However, the adjustments complicate recordkeeping, because the replacement shares' reported basis and holding period on the broker's 1099-B may not reflect the wash sale adjustment unless the broker tracked it. Compute the adjusted basis and holding period manually when a wash sale has occurred.

### Account For Wash Sales Across Accounts And Spouses

Wash sales are not limited to transactions within a single brokerage account. A purchase of substantially identical securities in a different account (for example, a spouse's account or a second brokerage account) can trigger a wash sale on a loss sale in the first account. Brokers report wash sales only within their own accounts for covered securities, so a wash sale across accounts will not appear on any single 1099-B and must be detected and computed manually.

The across-account application is a common trap for taxpayers who harvest losses in one account and repurchase in another, thinking they have avoided the wash sale. The rule also applies to purchases by a spouse if the spouses file jointly, and in certain cases to purchases by controlled entities. When reviewing a loss harvesting strategy, map all accounts (including spouse accounts and IRA accounts) for substantially identical purchases within the window. Do not rely on broker reporting to catch cross-account wash sales.

### Recognize The Permanent Loss In IRAs

A special and harsh rule applies when the replacement shares are acquired in an IRA. If a taxpayer sells a security at a loss in a taxable account and acquires substantially identical securities in an IRA within the wash sale window, the loss is permanently disallowed. Unlike the normal wash sale rule, the disallowed loss is not added to the basis of the IRA shares, because IRA basis does not work that way for traditional or Roth IRAs. The loss is simply gone.

This trap is devastating because the taxpayer loses the deduction entirely with no basis adjustment to recover it later. A taxpayer who harvests a loss in a brokerage account and has an automatic dividend reinvestment in an IRA purchasing the same security within the window triggers a permanent wash sale loss. Review all IRA accounts (including automatic reinvestment programs) for substantially identical purchases whenever a loss is harvested in a taxable account. The IRA wash sale rule applies to both traditional and Roth IRAs.

### Report Wash Sales On Form 8949 And Schedule D

Wash sales are reported on Form 8949 with adjustment code W, and the disallowed loss amount is shown as an adjustment that reduces the deductible loss. The gain or loss column shows the original computed loss, and the adjustment column shows the disallowed amount, so that the net amount transferred to Schedule D reflects only the allowed loss. For wash sales within the same account tracked by the broker, the 1099-B will typically show the adjustment; for cross-account or noncovered wash sales, the taxpayer must compute and report the adjustment manually.

Do not omit wash sale adjustments from the return, even if the broker did not report them. The IRS may detect unreported wash sales through account reporting or examination, and the adjustment plus interest and penalties can be costly. Reconcile the 1099-B wash sale reporting with the taxpayer's own transaction records and report all wash sales correctly.

### Plan Around The Wash Sale Window For Loss Harvesting

Loss harvesting at year-end is the most common context for wash sale issues, because taxpayers sell losers to realize losses before December 31 and may want to re-establish the position. To avoid a wash sale, the taxpayer must wait more than 30 days before repurchasing substantially identical securities, or purchase a different (not substantially identical) security as a replacement during the window.

Common strategies include waiting 31 days before repurchasing, buying a similar but not substantially identical ETF or fund, or doubling up on the position before selling (though the doubling up must itself avoid the window). Each strategy has tradeoffs: waiting 31 days exposes the taxpayer to being out of the market, and substitute securities may not track the original perfectly. Confirm the wash sale analysis before executing a harvest, and document the replacement strategy. The wash sale rule is not avoided by intent; it is avoided by the facts of the transactions.

## Common Traps

### Repurchasing Within 30 Days After A Loss Sale

The classic wash sale trigger is buying back the same security within 30 days after selling at a loss. The loss is disallowed and added to the replacement basis. Wait more than 30 days or buy a different security.

### Buying More Shares Within 30 Days Before Selling At A Loss

A purchase before the sale also triggers a wash sale. A taxpayer who averages down and then sells the original loss shares has a wash sale on the overlapping portion.

### Treating Different Funds As Automatically Not Substantially Identical

Two funds tracking the same index from different providers are generally not substantially identical, but two share classes of the same fund may be. Do not assume "different ticker" means safe.

### Missing Wash Sales Across Accounts Or Spouses

Brokers report wash sales only within their own accounts. A repurchase in a spouse's account or a second brokerage account is not reported by either broker and must be detected manually.

### Permanently Losing The Deduction Via An IRA Repurchase

If replacement shares are acquired in an IRA, the disallowed loss is permanently lost with no basis recovery. This is the harshest wash sale outcome and must be checked for all IRA accounts.

### Forgetting Automatic Dividend Reinvestments

An automatic dividend reinvestment that purchases the same security within the window triggers a wash sale. These reinvestments are easy to overlook because they are automated.

### Ignoring The Holding Period Tack

The replacement shares' holding period includes the original shares' holding period. This can help establish long-term status but complicates the holding period analysis.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the 61-day window (30 days before, sale day, 30 days after) been applied using trade dates for the loss sale and any replacement purchase?
- [ ] Has the substantially identical determination been made carefully, recognizing that same-issuer stock is identical, options may be identical to the stock, and different funds tracking the same index may not be?
- [ ] Has the disallowed loss been added to the basis of the replacement shares, and has the holding period of the original shares been tacked onto the replacement?
- [ ] Have all accounts been checked for substantially identical purchases within the window, including spouse accounts and accounts at other brokers, not just the account of the loss sale?
- [ ] Have all IRA accounts (traditional and Roth) been checked for substantially identical purchases, recognizing that a wash sale into an IRA permanently loses the deduction?
- [ ] Have automatic dividend reinvestment programs been reviewed for purchases within the window that could trigger a wash sale?
- [ ] Is the wash sale reported on Form 8949 with adjustment code W, with the disallowed amount shown as an adjustment, including wash sales not reported on the 1099-B?
- [ ] For loss harvesting strategies, has the replacement plan been confirmed to avoid the wash sale window (wait 31 days or buy a not substantially identical security)?
