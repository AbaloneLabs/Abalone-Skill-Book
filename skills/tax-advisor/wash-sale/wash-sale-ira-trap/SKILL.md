# The Wash Sale IRA Trap

The wash sale rule has a special, devastating application when the replacement securities are acquired in an IRA. Under normal wash sale rules, a disallowed loss is added to the basis of the replacement shares, preserving the economic loss to be recognized later. But when the replacement shares are in an IRA, the disallowed loss is permanently lost with no basis adjustment and no future recovery. The deduction vanishes. This trap is easy to trigger accidentally, because IRA accounts often have automatic dividend reinvestment programs that purchase securities without the taxpayer's active involvement, and a single automated reinvestment within the wash sale window can wipe out a large harvested loss.

Agents commonly miss that the IRA wash sale rule applies to both traditional and Roth IRAs, that it applies to purchases by the taxpayer's own IRA as well as a spouse's IRA, that automatic reinvestments are the most common trigger, and that the permanent loss has no cure once triggered. The harm is a taxpayer who carefully harvests a loss in a brokerage account, only to have an unnoticed IRA reinvestment permanently disallow the deduction, with no basis recovery and no way to undo the result.

This skill covers the wash sale IRA trap under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the rule is harsh and the interaction with IRA basis rules is complex, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Permanent Disallowance Rule

When a taxpayer sells a security at a loss in a taxable account and acquires substantially identical securities in an IRA within the 61-day wash sale window, the loss is permanently disallowed. Unlike the normal wash sale rule, the disallowed loss is not added to the basis of the IRA shares. Traditional IRA and Roth IRA basis do not increase for wash sale adjustments, so there is no mechanism to preserve or recover the disallowed loss. The deduction is simply gone.

This is the harshest outcome in the wash sale regime. A taxpayer who harvests a 50,000 dollar loss in a brokerage account and has a 500 dollar automatic reinvestment in an IRA purchasing the same security within the window permanently loses the deduction on the shares that are washed. The amount disallowed depends on the size of the replacement purchase relative to the loss sale, but even a small IRA purchase can disallow a portion of a large loss. The rule applies automatically based on the facts, regardless of the taxpayer's intent or awareness.

### Check Both Traditional And Roth IRAs

The IRA wash sale trap applies to acquisitions in both traditional IRAs and Roth IRAs. Neither type of IRA provides a basis adjustment for wash sale disallowed losses, so the permanent disallowance applies regardless of which IRA type holds the replacement shares. The rule also applies to acquisitions in a spouse's IRA if the spouses file jointly, because the wash sale attribution rules extend to spouses.

When reviewing a loss harvest in a taxable account, check every IRA account owned by the taxpayer and the taxpayer's spouse for substantially identical purchases within the window. This includes traditional IRAs, Roth IRAs, SEP IRAs, SIMPLE IRAs, and any other IRA-type account. Do not assume that only the taxable account matters; the IRA accounts are where the permanent trap lies. A single overlooked IRA reinvestment can disallow the loss.

### Identify Automatic Dividend Reinvestments As The Primary Trigger

The most common way taxpayers fall into the IRA wash sale trap is through automatic dividend reinvestment programs. Many IRA accounts are set up to automatically reinvest dividends and capital gains distributions back into the same fund that paid them. If a taxpayer sells that fund at a loss in a taxable account, and an IRA reinvestment purchases the same fund within the wash sale window, the loss is permanently disallowed.

Automatic reinvestments are dangerous because they occur without the taxpayer's active involvement or awareness. A taxpayer may harvest a loss in December, not realize that an IRA reinvestment is scheduled for mid-December, and permanently lose the deduction. Before harvesting any loss, review all IRA accounts for automatic reinvestment programs on the same or substantially identical securities, and either turn off the reinvestment during the wash sale window or redirect it to a different security. The reinvestment does not need to be large to trigger the rule; any purchase of substantially identical securities within the window applies.

### Understand The Attribution Across Accounts And Spouses

The wash sale rule attributes purchases by related parties to the taxpayer. For spouses filing jointly, a purchase of substantially identical securities in one spouse's IRA can trigger a wash sale on a loss sale in the other spouse's taxable account. The rule looks at the economic reality of maintaining the position, not the account ownership in isolation.

When planning loss harvesting, map the full IRA footprint of both spouses. This includes each spouse's traditional IRA, Roth IRA, and any employer-sponsored plans that may hold the same securities (though employer plans like 401(k)s are generally not subject to the same wash sale attribution, the analysis should confirm the account type). A wash sale triggered by a spouse's IRA reinvestment is just as permanent as one triggered by the taxpayer's own IRA. Do not limit the review to the taxpayer's own accounts.

### Compute The Disallowed Amount

The amount of loss disallowed by an IRA wash sale depends on the size of the replacement purchase relative to the loss sale. If the replacement purchase in the IRA is smaller than the loss sale, only a portion of the loss (corresponding to the number of replacement shares) is disallowed, and the remainder is allowed. If the replacement purchase is equal to or larger than the loss sale, the entire loss is disallowed.

For example, if a taxpayer sells 1,000 shares at a loss and the IRA reinvestment purchases 100 shares of the same security, the loss on 100 shares is permanently disallowed and the loss on 900 shares is allowed. Compute the disallowed amount by matching the replacement shares to the sold shares on a per-share or per-lot basis. The disallowed loss is not added to any basis; it is simply lost. Document the computation because the IRA purchase will not appear on the taxable account's 1099-B and the IRS may not automatically detect it, but the taxpayer is still required to report it correctly.

### Prevent The Trap Before Harvesting

The only reliable way to avoid the IRA wash sale trap is to prevent the IRA purchase from occurring within the window. Before harvesting a loss in a taxable account, take these steps: identify all IRA accounts (taxpayer and spouse), check for automatic reinvestment programs on the same or substantially identical securities, and either suspend the reinvestment for the duration of the wash sale window or redirect it to a different security.

If suspending reinvestment, turn it off before the loss sale and leave it off until more than 30 days after the sale. If redirecting, ensure the alternative security is not substantially identical. Do not assume that a reinvestment scheduled for later in the month is safe; the window is 30 days after the sale, so a reinvestment up to 30 days later triggers the rule. The prevention must be proactive, because once the IRA purchase occurs, the loss is permanently lost and cannot be recovered by unwinding the transaction.

### Report The Disallowed Loss Correctly

When an IRA wash sale has occurred, the disallowed loss must be reported on Form 8949 with adjustment code W, just like any other wash sale. The disallowed amount is shown as an adjustment that reduces the deductible loss. The difference is that there is no corresponding basis increase in any account, so the loss is truly gone rather than deferred.

The IRA wash sale will not appear on the taxable account's 1099-B, because the broker has no visibility into the IRA purchase. The taxpayer must compute and report the adjustment manually. Failing to report the IRA wash sale adjustment understates gain or overstates the deductible loss, creating examination exposure. Even though the result is unfavorable to the taxpayer, the adjustment must be reported correctly.

## Common Traps

### Overlooking Automatic IRA Reinvestments

The most common trigger is an automatic dividend reinvestment in an IRA purchasing the same security within the wash sale window. These automated transactions are easy to miss.

### Assuming Roth IRAs Are Exempt

The permanent disallowance rule applies to both traditional and Roth IRAs. Neither provides a basis adjustment, so the loss is permanently lost in either case.

### Forgetting A Spouse's IRA

A purchase in a spouse's IRA triggers the wash sale for jointly filing spouses. The review must cover both spouses' IRA accounts, not just the taxpayer's.

### Believing The Loss Can Be Recovered

Unlike normal wash sales, the IRA wash sale loss is permanently disallowed with no basis adjustment. There is no recovery mechanism. Once triggered, the deduction is gone.

### Not Turning Off Reinvestment Before Harvesting

Failing to suspend or redirect IRA reinvestments before a loss sale is the root cause of most IRA wash sale traps. The prevention must be proactive and timed to the wash sale window.

### Failing To Report The Adjustment

The IRA wash sale will not appear on the 1099-B. The taxpayer must compute and report the disallowed amount manually on Form 8949, even though it is unfavorable.

- [ ] Is it understood that an IRA wash sale permanently disallows the loss with no basis adjustment and no recovery, unlike a normal wash sale?
- [ ] Has the wash sale window (30 days before through 30 days after the loss sale) been applied to all IRA purchases, including scheduled reinvestments?

- [ ] Has the disallowed loss been reported on Form 8949 with adjustment code W, even though it will not appear on the 1099-B and the result is unfavorable?
