# Wash Sale Replacement Securities

The wash sale rule turns on whether the taxpayer acquired substantially identical replacement securities within the 61-day window. The entire analysis therefore depends on what counts as substantially identical, and this is where most planning and most errors occur. A taxpayer who sells an S&P 500 index fund at a loss and buys a different S&P 500 index fund has not necessarily avoided a wash sale, while a taxpayer who sells one technology stock and buys a different technology stock has almost certainly avoided one. The substantially identical standard is not intuitive, and applying it carelessly creates either disallowed losses (too aggressive) or missed market exposure (too conservative).

Agents commonly miss that the substantially identical standard is stricter than "same sector" but broader than "same ticker," that options and futures can be substantially identical to their underlying, that different share classes of the same fund are identical, and that the IRS has provided limited guidance leaving many ETF and fund comparisons in a gray zone. The harm is a taxpayer whose harvested loss is disallowed because the replacement was too similar, or a taxpayer who sits out of the market for 31 days unnecessarily because they were overly cautious about a safe replacement.

This skill covers the determination of wash sale replacement securities under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the substantially identical standard is fact-specific and the IRS guidance is limited, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Substantially Identical Standard

The wash sale rule applies only to acquisitions of substantially identical securities. The term substantially identical is not precisely defined in the statute, but it means the securities are essentially the same for investment purposes. For stocks and bonds of corporations, substantially identical means the same security of the same issuer. Stock of Corporation A is not substantially identical to stock of Corporation B, even if they are direct competitors in the same industry with similar business models. A bond is not substantially identical to stock of the same issuer.

The standard is narrower than "similar" or "in the same asset class." Selling Apple at a loss and buying Microsoft is not a wash sale, because they are different issuers. However, the standard is broader than "identical ticker." Different share classes of the same mutual fund, or the same fund held directly and through a different account, are substantially identical. The determination must be made on the specific instruments involved, not on a general category. When the instruments are issued by the same entity and represent the same underlying economic interest, they are likely substantially identical.

### Treat Options And Futures As Potentially Identical To The Underlying

Options on a stock may be substantially identical to the stock itself, particularly deep in-the-money call options that are economically equivalent to holding the stock. The IRS has indicated that whether an option is substantially identical depends on the facts, but a deep in-the-money call is more likely to be treated as substantially identical than an out-of-the-money call. Put options can also create wash sale issues if they establish a short position equivalent to selling the stock.

Futures contracts and other derivatives may be substantially identical to the underlying in certain cases, though the analysis is complex. The safe approach is to assume that any derivative that closely tracks or is economically equivalent to the sold security may trigger a wash sale. When planning a replacement, avoid options and futures on the same underlying as the loss sale, or confirm through analysis that the specific instrument is not substantially identical. Do not assume that an option is automatically safe because it has a different name.

### Distinguish Same-Fund Share Classes From Different Funds

Different share classes of the same mutual fund (for example, Investor shares and Admiral shares of the same Vanguard fund) are substantially identical because they represent the same underlying portfolio. Selling one share class at a loss and buying another share class of the same fund triggers a wash sale. The same applies to institutional shares versus retail shares of the same fund.

However, two different funds that track the same index are generally not substantially identical, even if their performance is very similar. A Vanguard S&P 500 ETF and a State Street S&P 500 ETF are different funds with different portfolios and different issuers, and the consensus view is that they are not substantially identical. The IRS has not issued definitive guidance on ETF-to-ETF substantially identical determinations, so the standard practice is to treat funds from different providers as not substantially identical, even if they track the same index. This is a judgment area; document the reasoning and recognize the residual uncertainty.

### Evaluate ETF And Fund Swaps For Replacement Strategy

A common loss harvesting replacement strategy is to sell a losing fund and buy a similar but not substantially identical fund to maintain market exposure during the wash sale window. Selling a total stock market fund and buying an S&P 500 fund, or selling a large-cap growth fund and buying a large-cap blend fund, are typical swaps. The question is whether the replacement is substantially identical to the original.

The safer the swap (more different the funds), the lower the wash sale risk but the higher the tracking error during the replacement period. Selling a total market fund and buying a small-cap value fund maintains equity exposure but with very different characteristics. Selling one S&P 500 ETF and buying another S&P 500 ETF from a different provider is the riskiest common swap, because the substantially identical question is unresolved. The conservative approach is to swap to a fund with a different methodology, different index, or different provider, and to document why the replacement is not substantially identical. Avoid swapping between funds that are clearly the same product under different wrappers.

### Avoid Constructive Ownership Through Related Transactions

The wash sale rule can be triggered by acquisitions through related parties or arrangements that effectively maintain the taxpayer's position. Purchases by a spouse filing jointly, by a corporation controlled by the taxpayer, or through certain trust arrangements may be attributed to the taxpayer for wash sale purposes. The rule looks at economic substance, not just form.

When planning a replacement, consider all accounts and entities under the taxpayer's control or attribution. A repurchase in a spouse's IRA is as much a wash sale trigger as a repurchase in the taxpayer's own account. Automatic dividend reinvestments in any account that purchase the same security within the window also trigger wash sales. Map the full footprint of accounts and reinvestment programs before concluding a replacement is safe.

### Document The Replacement Analysis

Because the substantially identical standard involves judgment and the IRS guidance is limited, document the reasoning behind any replacement that is claimed not to trigger a wash sale. The documentation should identify the sold security, the replacement security, the basis for concluding they are not substantially identical (different issuer, different index methodology, different portfolio composition), and the dates of the transactions.

Documentation does not guarantee the position will be sustained on examination, but it demonstrates that the determination was made thoughtfully rather than casually. For swaps between similar funds, the documentation is especially important because it is the primary support for the position. Absent documentation, the IRS may impose a wash sale adjustment and the taxpayer will have difficulty defending the replacement.

### Plan The Return To The Original Position

If the taxpayer intends to return to the original security after the wash sale window expires, plan the timing carefully. The taxpayer must wait more than 30 days after the loss sale before repurchasing the original security, or the repurchase triggers a wash sale. If a replacement security was purchased, selling the replacement and buying back the original must also avoid creating a new wash sale or other tax consequence.

A common pattern is to sell the original at a loss, buy a replacement for 31 days, sell the replacement, and buy back the original. Each of these transactions has tax consequences (the replacement sale may produce its own gain or loss) and wash sale implications (the replacement sale and original repurchase must be outside the window). Model the full sequence before executing, and confirm each step avoids wash sale treatment.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Different Tickers Mean Not Substantially Identical

Different share classes of the same fund have different tickers but are substantially identical. The ticker is not the test; the underlying economic interest is.

### Treating Same-Sector Stocks As Substantially Identical

Selling one bank stock and buying another bank stock is not a wash sale. Same sector or same industry is not the standard; same issuer is.

### Overlooking Options As Substantially Identical

Deep in-the-money call options on a stock may be substantially identical to the stock. Selling a stock at a loss and buying a deep in-the-money call on the same stock can trigger a wash sale.

### Swapping Between Same-Index ETFs From Different Providers

The substantially identical question for two S&P 500 ETFs from different providers is unresolved. Treating this swap as definitely safe overstates the certainty. Use a more different replacement to reduce risk.

### Forgetting Spouse And Related-Entity Purchases

A repurchase by a spouse or controlled entity is attributed to the taxpayer. The wash sale rule applies across related parties, not just the taxpayer's own account.

### Ignoring Automatic Reinvestments During The Window

An automatic dividend reinvestment that buys the same security within the window triggers a wash sale. These automated transactions are easy to overlook.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the substantially identical standard been applied based on the specific instruments, recognizing same-issuer stock is identical, same-fund share classes are identical, and same-sector different-issuer stock is not?
- [ ] For options, futures, or derivatives used as replacements, has the potential for substantial identity with the underlying been analyzed, especially for deep in-the-money calls?
- [ ] For fund swaps, has the replacement been chosen to be genuinely different (different provider, methodology, or index), with the reasoning documented?
- [ ] Have same-fund share classes and different wrappers of the same fund been identified as substantially identical and avoided as replacements?
- [ ] Have all related accounts and entities (spouse, controlled corporations, trusts) been checked for purchases that would be attributed to the taxpayer?
- [ ] Have automatic dividend reinvestment programs in all accounts been reviewed for purchases within the wash sale window?
- [ ] Is the replacement analysis documented with the sold security, the replacement, the basis for non-identity, and the transaction dates?
- [ ] If returning to the original position, has the full sequence (sell original, buy replacement, sell replacement, buy back original) been modeled to avoid wash sales at each step?
