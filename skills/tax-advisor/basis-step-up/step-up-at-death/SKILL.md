# Step-Up At Death

Basis step-up at death is one of the most valuable and most misunderstood provisions in tax law. When a capital asset passes from a decedent to a beneficiary, the beneficiary generally receives a basis equal to the asset's fair market value on the decedent's date of death, rather than the decedent's adjusted basis. This can wipe out all the accumulated gain on an appreciated asset, so that a beneficiary who later sells pays no income tax on any appreciation that occurred during the decedent's life. An agent who discusses gifting low-basis assets without weighing the step-up forfeited at death is giving incomplete advice, because the same asset gifted during life carries over the low basis and preserves all the gain.

Agents miss the step-up analysis because they treat it as a flat rule that always applies, when in fact it depends on the character of the asset, how it passes, the valuation date elected, and whether the asset is income-producing or personal. They also conflate step-up with estate tax reduction, when the two systems move in opposite directions: step-up helps income tax but the asset remains in the taxable estate. The harm is twofold. An agent may recommend gifting an appreciated asset to remove it from the estate, inadvertently saddling the donee with carryover basis and a large capital gains bill. Or an agent may assume every inherited asset is stepped up, missing the assets that take carryover basis or that receive only a partial adjustment.

This skill applies before advising on holding versus gifting appreciated assets, before estimating the basis a beneficiary will receive, and before choosing a valuation date. It is not legal or tax advice. Basis rules interact with estate tax, valuation, and the character of the asset, and certain rules such as the alternate valuation date have strict conditions. Always confirm current law and escalate to a qualified tax professional, CPA, or estate tax attorney before finalizing any basis-sensitive decision.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Step-Up From Carryover Basis By Transfer Type

The fundamental distinction is between assets transferred at death and assets transferred by lifetime gift. Assets acquired from a decedent generally receive a fair market value basis as of the date of death under the stepped-up basis rules, which eliminates all pre-death appreciation for income tax purposes. Assets received by lifetime gift take the donor's adjusted basis, known as carryover basis, which preserves all the gain the donor had.

Before advising on any transfer of an appreciated asset, determine whether it will pass at death or by gift. For an asset with a $50,000 basis and a $500,000 value, a death transfer gives the beneficiary a $500,000 basis and no taxable gain on a later sale at that price. The same asset gifted during life gives the donee a $50,000 basis, so a sale at $500,000 triggers gain of $450,000, taxed at the beneficiary's capital gains rate. For highly appreciated assets, the step-up can be worth more than the estate tax savings of an early gift, especially when the estate is below the exemption threshold.

### Confirm Which Assets Qualify For Step-Up

Step-up applies to capital assets, but not everything a beneficiary receives is eligible. Appreciated stocks, bonds, mutual funds, real estate used as a personal residence, and tangible personal property generally receive a full step-up to date-of-death fair market value. However, certain assets receive no step-up or special treatment, including retirement accounts such as traditional IRAs and 401(k)s, which remain ordinary income assets and are taxed to the beneficiary on distribution regardless of basis.

Categorize each asset before estimating the step-up benefit. Income in respect of a decedent, which includes retirement accounts, unpaid wages, and certain receivables, carries no step-up and is taxed as ordinary income to the recipient. Assets with built-in loss may actually receive a step-down to fair market value if the date-of-death value is below basis. Savings bonds accumulate interest that is income in respect of a decedent, not stepped-up capital gain. Do not assume every asset in the estate gets a basis reset; the character of the asset determines whether step-up, step-down, or no adjustment applies.

### Choose Between Date Of Death And Alternate Valuation Date

The executor generally may elect to value estate assets either at the date of death or at the alternate valuation date, which is six months after death. The date-of-death value is the default and establishes basis for the beneficiary. The alternate valuation date can be elected only if the election is made on a timely filed estate tax return and if it reduces both the gross estate and the estate tax liability, meaning it cannot be used selectively to increase basis on some assets while reducing value on others.

Evaluate the election based on market movement. If assets declined in value during the six months after death, the alternate date lowers both estate tax and the beneficiary's basis. If assets rose, the alternate date increases both. The election is all-or-nothing for the assets to which it applies, with special rules for assets sold within the six-month window, which are valued at the sale price. Confirm whether the estate is even required to file an estate tax return, because the alternate valuation election is only available on a filed return, and small estates below the filing threshold may not make the election.

### Quantify Step-Up Value Against Estate Tax Cost

Step-up is an income tax benefit, but the asset that receives it remains in the gross estate for estate tax purposes. The two effects must be weighed together. For an estate below the exemption, the step-up is essentially free, because there is no estate tax and the beneficiary gets a reset basis. For a taxable estate, the inclusion of the asset creates estate tax at up to 40 percent, while the step-up saves capital gains tax at the beneficiary's rate.

Run the numbers for taxable estates. Compare the estate tax cost of including the asset against the income tax savings from the step-up, discounted by the probability and timing of the beneficiary's sale. If the beneficiary will hold the asset indefinitely, the step-up provides no near-term benefit while the estate tax is due in cash within nine months. If the beneficiary will sell soon, the step-up can be worth more than the estate tax cost. The answer depends on rates, timing, and the beneficiary's intent, not on a blanket preference for step-up.

### Preserve Step-Up Through Proper Titling And Transfer

Step-up depends on the asset being included in the decedent's gross estate. Assets held in joint tenancy with right of survivorship receive only a partial step-up, generally limited to the decedent's share, unless the asset is community property. Assets held in a revocable trust receive a full step-up because they are included in the gross estate, which is a key advantage of the revocable trust. Assets transferred to an irrevocable trust during life may be removed from the estate and lose the step-up entirely.

Review titling before relying on step-up. A jointly held brokerage account between spouses in a non-community-property state steps up only the decedent's half. An asset gifted to an irrevocable trust years before death is outside the estate and keeps its carryover basis. Confirm that assets meant to receive step-up are titled in a way that keeps them in the gross estate, such as individual name, revocable trust, or qualifying community property, and flag any asset that has been removed from the estate and therefore forfeited its step-up.

### Apply Special Rules For Joint Tenancy And Community Property

Joint tenancy and community property create different step-up outcomes that agents frequently confuse. In a common law state, when one joint tenant dies, only the decedent's fractional share of the asset receives a step-up, so a jointly owned asset between spouses steps up half. In a community property state, the entire asset, meaning both spouses' halves, can receive a step-up at the first death, which is a significant advantage and the subject of a separate analysis.

Identify the marital property regime before stating the step-up. For joint tenancy between spouses in a common law state, confirm the decedent's contribution to determine the stepped-up portion, typically half for spouses. For property held as tenants in common, only the decedent's stated percentage steps up. For community property, the full asset may step up at the first death. Getting the regime wrong produces a materially incorrect basis figure and can mislead the beneficiary about the gain on a future sale.

### Document Basis With Date-Of-Death Valuations

The beneficiary's stepped-up basis must be substantiated with evidence of the date-of-death fair market value. For publicly traded securities, this is the average of the high and low prices on the date of death. For real estate, a qualified appraisal or dated comparable sales are needed. For closely held business interests, a qualified appraisal is essential. Without documentation, the beneficiary cannot prove the basis years later when selling.

Establish valuation contemporaneously. Obtain date-of-death statements for all securities, and commission appraisals for real estate and business interests promptly after death while facts are fresh. Retain these records permanently, as the basis carries forward until the asset is sold, which may be decades later. An undocumented step-up is effectively no step-up if it cannot be proven on audit, and the burden falls on the taxpayer.

## Common Traps

### Assuming All Inherited Assets Receive Step-Up

Retirement accounts, unpaid wages, savings bond interest, and other income in respect of a decedent receive no step-up and are taxed as ordinary income. Treating an inherited IRA as a stepped-up capital asset is a costly error that understates the tax due on distribution.

### Gifting Appreciated Assets Without Weighing The Lost Step-Up

Recommending a lifetime gift of a low-basis asset to remove it from the estate saddles the donee with carryover basis and preserves all the gain. For highly appreciated assets in a non-taxable estate, holding until death usually produces a better after-tax result.

### Confusing Step-Up With Estate Tax Reduction

Step-up is an income tax benefit that requires the asset to remain in the taxable estate. An agent who treats step-up as free ignores that a taxable estate pays up to 40 percent on the inclusion while the income tax savings depend on whether the beneficiary ever sells.

### Misapplying The Alternate Valuation Date

The alternate date election is all-or-nothing, available only on a filed estate tax return, and only if it reduces both gross estate value and estate tax. It cannot be used selectively to raise basis on some assets while lowering estate value on others.

### Getting The Joint Tenancy Fraction Wrong

In a common law state, only the decedent's share of jointly held property steps up, typically half for spouses. Assuming a full step-up on jointly held property overstates basis and understates the gain on a later sale.

### Forgetting That Irrevocable Trust Gifts Lose Step-Up

Assets transferred to an irrevocable trust during life are removed from the gross estate and therefore forfeit the step-up. An agent who recommends irrevocable trust funding for estate tax purposes must account for the basis cost.

### Failing To Document Date-Of-Death Value

A stepped-up basis that cannot be proven is worthless on audit. Without contemporaneous valuations, appraisals, and retained records, the beneficiary may default to the original low basis and pay tax on gain that should have been eliminated.
