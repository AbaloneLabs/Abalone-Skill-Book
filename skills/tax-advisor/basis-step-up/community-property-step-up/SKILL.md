# Community Property Step-Up

Community property law creates a basis advantage that common law states do not offer. When one spouse dies in a community property state, both halves of the community property, meaning the decedent's half and the surviving spouse's half, can receive a step-up to fair market value. In a common law state, only the decedent's half of jointly held property steps up, leaving the surviving spouse's half at its original basis. This difference can be worth hundreds of thousands of dollars in capital gains tax, and an agent who applies common law logic to a community property client, or vice versa, will produce a materially wrong basis estimate.

Agents miss this because they assume joint ownership between spouses behaves the same everywhere. They apply the half-step-up rule universally, not realizing that in community property states the entire asset resets. They also fail to distinguish community property from separate property, which receives only the decedent's-half step-up, and they mishandle property that has changed character over time through commingling, transmutation, or relocation between states. The harm is an incorrect basis that either overstates the step-up, leading to underpayment and penalties, or understates it, causing the surviving spouse to overpay tax unnecessarily.

This skill applies before stating the basis a surviving spouse will receive, before advising on holding versus selling assets after a first death, and before characterizing property as community or separate. It is not legal or tax advice. Community property rules are state-specific, characterization rules are technical, and the interaction with federal tax law is nuanced. Always confirm the applicable state law and escalate to a qualified tax professional, CPA, or estate tax attorney before finalizing any basis determination.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Marital Property Regime First

The single most important step is to determine whether the client lives in, or acquired property in, a community property state. The community property states generally include Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, and Wisconsin, plus Alaska if an elective community property agreement was made. In these states, property acquired during marriage is presumptively community property, and both spouses' halves can step up at the first death. In common law states, only the decedent's interest steps up.

Identify the regime before any basis calculation. Do not assume the state of current residence governs all property, because property retains the character of the state where it was acquired, and couples who moved from a community property state to a common law state may still hold community property. Conversely, couples who moved into a community property state may hold separate property acquired before the move. The governing law is the law of the couple's domicile when the property was acquired, and quasi-community property rules can complicate relocations. Map each asset to its character before computing step-up.

### Apply The Full Step-Up To Community Property At First Death

The core advantage of community property is that at the first spouse's death, both the decedent's one-half interest and the surviving spouse's one-half interest in community property receive a basis step-up to fair market value. This means 100 percent of the community asset is stepped up, not just half. For a brokerage account worth $1,000,000 with a $200,000 total basis, the surviving spouse's basis becomes $1,000,000, eliminating all $800,000 of gain.

Quantify the advantage explicitly. Compare the community property outcome against the common law outcome for the same asset. In a common law state with joint tenancy, only the decedent's half, worth $500,000, would step up, leaving the surviving spouse with a blended basis of $600,000 and $400,000 of remaining gain. In a community property state, the full $1,000,000 steps up, eliminating all gain. This difference can change whether the surviving spouse should sell immediately or hold, and it can justify restructuring ownership to confirm community property character before death.

### Distinguish Community Property From Separate Property

Not all property owned by spouses in a community property state is community property. Separate property, which includes property owned before marriage, property acquired by gift or inheritance during marriage, and property traceable to those sources, receives only the decedent's-half step-up at death, not the full step-up. Misclassifying separate property as community property overstates the basis and creates audit risk.

Trace each asset's source. Property bought with community funds during marriage is community. Property owned before marriage, or received by gift or inheritance designated to one spouse, is separate. Property purchased with separate funds remains separate, but commingling, such as depositing separate funds into a joint account, can transmute the character and destroy the separate property treatment. Review account histories, deeds, and gift or inheritance documentation to confirm character. When character is unclear, the presumption favors community property in most community property states, but the burden and consequences vary, so ambiguous assets need careful review.

### Handle Transmutation And Commingling Carefully

Property can change character during marriage through transmutation, which is a deliberate act to convert community to separate or vice versa, or through commingling, which is the unintentional mixing of funds that can blur character. A separately held account into which community earnings are deposited may become community property. A community asset titled in one spouse's name alone remains community property despite the title. These transformations directly affect whether the full or half step-up applies.

Trace the transactions that could change character. Deposits of community earnings into a separate account, use of community funds to improve separate property, and formal transmutation agreements all alter the character analysis. In some community property states, transmutation requires a written agreement signed by the spouse whose interest is adversely affected. Do not assume that the title or the original source controls, because the movement of funds and any agreements can override both. An asset that looks separate on paper may be community in substance, or vice versa.

### Address Relocation Between Community And Common Law States

Couples who move between community property and common law states create particularly tricky characterization problems. Community property acquired in a community property state generally retains its community character after a move to a common law state, which means the full step-up may still be available at death even though the couple now lives in a common law state. Conversely, separate property acquired before moving to a community property state generally remains separate.

Identify the domicile and acquisition location for each significant asset. Property acquired while domiciled in a community property state is presumptively community and may retain that character after relocation. Quasi-community property rules in some community property states can recharacterize certain separate property brought into the state. The result is that a couple's property may be a mix of community, separate, and quasi-community, each with different step-up consequences. Do not apply a single regime to all assets; characterize each one based on when, where, and how it was acquired.

### Consider Spousal Property Agreements To Optimize Character

Spouses in a community property state can enter into agreements to convert separate property to community property, or vice versa, which can be used to maximize the step-up at the first death. Converting a separately held appreciated asset to community property before death can make the entire asset eligible for the full step-up rather than only the decedent's half. This is a planning technique, not a default rule, and it requires a valid agreement.

Evaluate whether conversion is worthwhile. For a separately held asset with large appreciation, converting to community property can double the step-up benefit, but it also changes the surviving spouse's rights, creditor exposure, and estate inclusion. The agreement must meet state law formalities, typically in writing and signed by both spouses. Do not recommend conversion without analyzing the non-tax consequences, because community property classification affects management rights, liability, and disposition during life, not just basis at death.

### Document Character And Valuation For Substantiation

The full community property step-up must be substantiated with evidence of both the community property character and the date-of-death fair market value. The surviving spouse bears the burden of proving that the asset was community property and that the full step-up applies. Without documentation, the IRS may challenge the character and limit the step-up to the decedent's half.

Establish the record contemporaneously. Maintain deeds, account opening documents, and statements that reflect community property titling or character. For assets acquired during marriage with community funds, keep the source-of-funds documentation. Obtain date-of-death valuations for all significant assets, including securities statements and appraisals for real estate and business interests. Retain these records permanently, as the basis carries forward until sale. A full step-up that cannot be proven may be reduced to a half step-up, forfeiting half the benefit.

## Common Traps

### Applying Common Law Half Step-Up To Community Property

In a community property state, both halves step up at the first death, not just the decedent's half. Applying the common law rule understates the surviving spouse's basis and causes unnecessary capital gains tax on a later sale.

### Assuming Joint Title Means Community Property

Title does not determine character in a community property state. Community property can be held in one spouse's name, and joint tenancy does not automatically make property community. Relying on title rather than source-of-funds analysis mischaracterizes the asset.

### Misclassifying Separate Property As Community

Property owned before marriage, or acquired by gift or inheritance, is separate and receives only the decedent's-half step-up. Treating it as community overstates basis and creates audit exposure and potential penalties.

### Ignoring Commingling And Transmutation

Depositing community earnings into a separate account or using community funds to improve separate property can change character. Failing to trace these transactions produces a character determination that does not match the legal reality.

### Overlooking Retained Community Character After Relocation

Community property acquired in a community property state can retain its character after a move to a common law state, preserving the full step-up. Assuming the current common law state governs forfeits the advantage.

### Forgetting Quasi-Community Property Rules

Some community property states recharacterize property brought in from a common law state as quasi-community property, affecting both management rights and step-up. Ignoring these rules misstates the basis available at death.

### Failing To Document Community Character For Substantiation

The full step-up must be proven with evidence of community property character and date-of-death value. Without records, the IRS may limit the step-up to the decedent's half, halving the benefit.
