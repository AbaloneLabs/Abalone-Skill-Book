# QTIP And Step-Up

A Qualified Terminable Interest Property trust, or QTIP, lets the first spouse defer estate tax by claiming the marital deduction while keeping the assets under the deceased spouse's control and directing where they go at the surviving spouse's death. The marital deduction eliminates tax at the first death, but it is only a deferral, because the QTIP assets are pulled into the surviving spouse's gross estate and taxed at the second death. The interaction with basis step-up is subtle and often mishandled, because QTIP assets receive a step-up at the second death, not the first, and the timing and amount of that step-up depends on how the trust is structured and valued.

Agents miss the QTIP and step-up relationship because they treat the marital deduction as a free benefit rather than a deferral that recreates the tax later, often at a larger amount if the assets appreciate. They also conflate the estate tax treatment with the income tax basis treatment, assuming the step-up happens at the first death when it actually happens at the second. The harm is a plan that defers tax into a higher-rate second estate, or that fails to capture the step-up because the QTIP assets were not properly includible in the surviving spouse's estate. A QTIP that is efficient for estate tax may be inefficient for basis, and vice versa.

This skill applies before recommending a QTIP trust, before analyzing the tax outcome across both spouses' deaths, and before advising on the basis consequences of QTIP assets. It is not legal or tax advice. QTIP rules, the marital deduction, and basis step-up interact in technical ways, and the second-death consequences depend on trust terms and valuation. Always confirm current law and escalate to a qualified tax professional, CPA, or estate tax attorney before finalizing any QTIP-based plan.

## Core Rules

### Understand That QTIP Defers Estate Tax, It Does Not Eliminate It

The QTIP election allows the executor to claim the marital deduction for property passing to the trust, which removes that property from tax at the first spouse's death. But the deferral is temporary. The QTIP assets are included in the surviving spouse's gross estate at the second death and taxed then, along with any appreciation during the survivor's lifetime. The marital deduction is a timing tool, not a permanent exclusion.

Model both estates before recommending a QTIP. Estimate the first-death estate tax with and without the QTIP election, and then estimate the second-death estate tax including the QTIP assets plus appreciation. If the surviving spouse's estate will be well below the exemption, deferral may effectively eliminate the tax, because the assets pass through the second estate sheltered. If the surviving spouse's estate will be taxable, the QTIP defers tax into an estate that may pay 40 percent on the assets plus all intervening growth. The decision depends on the projected size of both estates, the assets' growth rate, and whether the surviving spouse has other assets that will fill the exemption.

### Apply The Step-Up At The Second Death, Not The First

Because QTIP assets are included in the surviving spouse's gross estate, they generally receive a basis step-up to fair market value at the surviving spouse's death, not at the first spouse's death. This is the key basis mechanic that agents get wrong. The assets do not step up when the first spouse dies and the QTIP is funded, because the marital deduction removes them from the first estate. They step up when the surviving spouse dies and the assets are included in the second estate.

Clarify the timing before estimating basis. For an asset funded into a QTIP at the first death with a $200,000 basis and a $1,000,000 value, the basis remains $200,000 during the surviving spouse's life, because no step-up occurred at the first death. If the asset appreciates to $1,500,000 by the surviving spouse's death and is included in the survivor's estate, the beneficiaries receive a $1,500,000 basis, eliminating all gain. The step-up is powerful but delayed, and it only materializes if the asset is actually includible in the surviving spouse's estate, which depends on proper QTIP structure and the election having been made.

### Confirm QTIP Requirements For The Marital Deduction And Step-Up

For property to qualify for the marital deduction as QTIP, and therefore to be includible in the surviving spouse's estate for step-up, the trust must meet strict requirements. The surviving spouse must be entitled to all income from the property for life, payable at least annually, and no person other than the surviving spouse may receive principal during the survivor's life. The executor must make the QTIP election on the estate tax return.

Verify the trust terms before relying on either the deduction or the step-up. Income must be distributed to the surviving spouse at least yearly, and the spouse must have a qualifying income interest for life. Principal cannot be distributed to anyone else during the survivor's life. If these requirements are not met, the property does not qualify as QTIP, the marital deduction is denied, and the property is taxed at the first death, which also means it is not in the surviving spouse's estate and does not receive the second-death step-up. A defective QTIP fails on both the estate tax deferral and the basis step-up, so the qualification analysis is essential to both.

### Evaluate Whether Deferral Helps Or Hurts The Overall Tax Picture

The QTIP deferral is not always beneficial. If the first spouse's estate is below the exemption, there may be no tax to defer, and the QTIP election could push assets into a taxable second estate unnecessarily. Conversely, if the first estate is taxable and the second estate will be sheltered by the exemption, the QTIP can move assets from a taxable first estate to a non-taxable second estate, eliminating the tax entirely.

Run the comparison for both scenarios. If both estates are below the exemption, the QTIP may be unnecessary, and direct ownership or other marital trust forms may produce the same or better results with less complexity. If the first estate is taxable and the second is not, the QTIP is highly valuable. If both estates are taxable, the QTIP defers tax but the assets appreciate untaxed during the survivor's life, potentially increasing the second-death tax. The correct choice depends on the relative sizes of the two estates, the growth rate of the assets, and whether the surviving spouse can manage the assets to preserve value.

### Coordinate QTIP With Portability As An Alternative

The portable exemption allows a surviving spouse to use the deceased spouse's unused exemption amount, which can reduce or eliminate the need for a QTIP in some cases. If the first spouse's estate is below the exemption, the unused exemption can pass to the survivor, sheltering more of the survivor's estate without requiring a QTIP trust. Portability is not automatic and must be elected on a timely filed estate tax return.

Compare QTIP against portability before defaulting to either. Portability is simpler and avoids trust administration, but it does not provide the control, asset protection, or remarriage protection that a QTIP offers, and the portable exemption does not grow with appreciation the way trust assets can. A QTIP also ensures the assets pass to designated remainder beneficiaries rather than to the survivor's later choices. Portability and QTIP are not mutually exclusive and can be used together, but the choice of which to rely on depends on the estate sizes, the desire for control, and the risk of the surviving spouse remarrying or dissipating assets.

### Handle The Non-Citizen Spouse Case With A QDOT

A special rule applies when the surviving spouse is not a U.S. citizen. The unlimited marital deduction is generally not available for transfers to a non-citizen spouse, because the asset could leave the U.S. tax system. Instead, a Qualified Domestic Trust, or QDOT, is required to obtain marital deduction deferral. A QDOT is similar to a QTIP in deferring estate tax, but it has additional requirements designed to ensure the tax is collected if the assets leave U.S. jurisdiction.

Identify citizenship status early. If the surviving spouse is not a citizen, a standard QTIP will not provide the marital deduction, and a QDOT must be used instead. A QDOT must have at least one U.S. trustee, must meet security requirements such as a bond or letter of credit or holding assets in a U.S. institution, and must ensure that no principal is distributed without estate tax being paid on the distribution. The QDOT still allows deferral of estate tax and the assets remain includible in the surviving spouse's estate for step-up purposes, but the administrative requirements are stricter. Do not recommend a marital trust for a non-citizen spouse without confirming QDOT compliance.

### Plan For The Second-Death Valuation And Liquidity

At the surviving spouse's death, the QTIP assets are valued at their date-of-death fair market value for both estate tax and basis step-up purposes. This valuation drives both the estate tax owed on the second estate and the basis the remainder beneficiaries receive. Because the QTIP assets may have appreciated significantly during the survivor's life, the second-death estate tax can be substantial, and the estate must have liquidity to pay it.

Coordinate valuation and liquidity for the second death. Obtain a current valuation of the QTIP assets as part of planning, and project the estate tax that will be due when the assets are included in the survivor's estate. Ensure that liquidity sources, such as life insurance owned outside the estate, are in place to pay the second-death tax without forcing sale of the QTIP assets. The remainder beneficiaries receive the stepped-up basis, which helps if they sell, but the estate tax is due in cash regardless. A QTIP plan that ignores second-death liquidity can leave the beneficiaries with a stepped-up but encumbered asset.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Marital Deduction As Permanent Exclusion

The QTIP marital deduction defers estate tax to the second death, it does not eliminate it. Assets plus appreciation are taxed in the surviving spouse's estate, often at a higher total amount than if taxed at the first death.

### Assuming Step-Up Occurs At The First Death

QTIP assets step up at the surviving spouse's death, not the first spouse's death, because they are included in the survivor's estate. Expecting a first-death step-up overstates basis during the survivor's life.

### Using A QTIP When Both Estates Are Below Exemption

If both estates are below the exemption, a QTIP may add complexity without tax benefit. Direct ownership or portability may achieve the same result with less administration and cost.

### Failing To Meet QTIP Income And Principal Requirements

The surviving spouse must receive all income annually, and no one else can receive principal during the survivor's life. A trust that violates these terms fails QTIP qualification, losing both the marital deduction and the second-death step-up.

### Overlooking Portability As A Simpler Alternative

Portability can transfer unused exemption without a trust, which may be simpler and sufficient for modest estates. Defaulting to a QTIP without comparing portability adds unnecessary complexity.

### Ignoring QDOT Requirements For A Non-Citizen Spouse

A standard QTIP does not provide the marital deduction for a non-citizen spouse. A QDOT with U.S. trustee and security requirements is mandatory, and missing this leaves the transfer taxable at the first death.

### Forgetting Second-Death Liquidity For Appreciated QTIP Assets

QTIP assets included in the survivor's estate may have appreciated greatly, creating a large second-death estate tax with no corresponding liquidity. The estate must have cash or insurance to pay the tax without selling trust assets.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the QTIP been modeled as a deferral of estate tax to the second death rather than as a permanent exclusion?
- [ ] Has the basis step-up been correctly placed at the surviving spouse's death, with the understanding that no step-up occurs at the first death?
- [ ] Have the QTIP trust terms been verified for the annual income distribution requirement and the restriction on principal distributions to non-spouse beneficiaries?
- [ ] Has the overall tax picture been compared across both estates, accounting for appreciation and whether each estate is taxable?
- [ ] Has portability been evaluated as a simpler alternative or supplement to the QTIP, including the requirement to elect it on a timely filed estate tax return?
- [ ] Has the surviving spouse's citizenship been confirmed, with a QDOT used if the spouse is not a U.S. citizen?
- [ ] Has the second-death valuation of QTIP assets been projected, with liquidity planning for the resulting estate tax?
- [ ] Has the agent avoided recommending a QTIP without confirming whether both estates are below the exemption and whether the complexity is justified?
- [ ] Has the agent avoided assuming that QTIP assets receive a basis step-up at the first spouse's death?
