# Qualified Business Income Deduction

The Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction under Section 199A is one of the largest available deductions for owners of pass-through entities, yet it is also one of the most complex. The deduction can be up to 20 percent of qualified business income, but the actual amount depends on a layered set of rules: whether the business is a specified service trade or business (SSTB) whose deduction phases out at higher incomes, whether the W-2 wage and unadjusted basis limitations cap the deduction, whether multiple businesses can be aggregated, and how the owner's overall taxable income interacts with the thresholds. An agent who applies a flat 20 percent to business income without checking these layers will frequently overstate the deduction.

Agents tend to miss the SSTB classification, the taxable income threshold tests that determine whether the limitations even apply, the wage and basis limitation computations, and the separate rules for qualified REIT dividends and publicly traded partnerships. The harm is an overstated deduction that fails on examination, or an understated deduction where a taxpayer leaves a significant benefit unclaimed because the limitations were applied when they did not need to be.

This skill covers the Section 199A QBI deduction for U.S. federal income tax purposes. It is not tax advice; the QBI rules are detailed and subject to regulatory guidance that evolves, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether The Income Qualifies As Qualified Business Income

Qualified business income is the net amount of qualified items of income, gain, deduction, and loss from an active trade or business conducted in the United States. It is effectively the ordinary business income of a pass-through entity that flows to the owner. Several categories of income are excluded from QBI: capital gains and losses, dividend income, interest income not allocable to a trade or business, income from outside the United States, and income from a business conducted as an employee rather than as an owner.

A critical exclusion is income earned as an employee. A person who provides services through an entity but is classified as an employee for tax purposes does not generate QBI from those services. This distinction matters for professionals who attempt to convert employee status to independent contractor status to access the deduction. The substance of the relationship, not the label, controls. Also excluded is any reasonable compensation paid to an S corporation shareholder-employee and guaranteed payments to partners for services, because these are treated as wages or partner payments rather than QBI. Confirm that the income has been correctly characterized as QBI before applying the 20 percent computation.

### Classify The Business As SSTB Or Non-SSTB

The specified service trade or business (SSTB) classification is the single most consequential determination for higher-income taxpayers. An SSTB is a trade or business involving the performance of services in the fields of health, law, accounting, actuarial science, performing arts, consulting, athletics, financial services, brokerage services, or any trade or business where the principal asset is the reputation or skill of one or more of its employees. Engineering and architecture are specifically excluded from the SSTB definition and are treated as non-SSTB businesses.

For an SSTB, the QBI deduction phases out and is fully eliminated once the owner's taxable income exceeds the upper end of the phase-in range. A high-income doctor, lawyer, consultant, or accountant may receive no QBI deduction at all. For a non-SSTB, the deduction is not eliminated by the SSTB phase-out, but it may still be limited by the W-2 wage and unadjusted basis limitations. The classification must be made carefully: consulting is broadly defined and can capture many service businesses, while the reputation-or-skill clause was narrowed by final regulations to mean cases where the business derives income from selling access to the person's reputation, not merely from having a good reputation. Do not classify a business as non-SSTB without checking the specific service field definitions.

### Apply The Taxable Income Threshold Tests

The SSTB phase-out and the W-2 wage and basis limitations apply only to taxpayers whose taxable income exceeds a threshold amount. For taxpayers below the threshold, the deduction is simply 20 percent of the lesser of QBI or overall taxable income, with no SSTB restriction and no wage or basis limitation. The threshold is indexed for inflation and approximately doubles for married filing jointly filers compared to single filers.

For taxpayers within the phase-in range (between the threshold and the upper limit), the limitations phase in gradually. For SSTBs, the deductible QBI is reduced linearly from full to zero across the phase-in range. For non-SSTBs, the wage and basis limitation phases in as a fraction of the applicable limitation. The phase-in computation requires prorating the limitation based on the ratio of taxable income above the threshold to the total phase-in range width. This is a multi-step computation that must be performed for each qualified business separately (unless aggregated). Always determine where the taxpayer's taxable income falls relative to the thresholds before deciding whether the limitations even apply.

### Compute The W-2 Wage And Unadjusted Basis Limitations

For non-SSTB taxpayers above the threshold (and for the phased-in portion of those in the range), the deductible QBI for each business is limited to the greater of 50 percent of the business's W-2 wages or 25 percent of W-2 wages plus 2.5 percent of the unadjusted basis immediately after acquisition (UBIA) of qualified property. The W-2 wages are the total wages paid by the business subject to wage withholding. The qualified property is tangible property subject to depreciation that the business owns and uses in its operations.

This limitation means that a business with no employees and no depreciable property may have a QBI deduction of zero for an owner above the threshold, even if the business is profitable. A consulting firm structured as a non-SSTB with no W-2 employees and no property would face this limitation. By contrast, a capital-intensive business with significant depreciable assets and payroll can support a larger deduction. The limitation is computed per business and then summed, unless aggregation is elected. Compute the wage and basis components for each business before determining the per-business deduction cap.

### Evaluate Aggregation Of Multiple Businesses

A taxpayer who owns multiple qualified businesses may elect to aggregate them for QBI purposes. Aggregation allows the W-2 wages and qualified property of one business to support the deduction for another, which can increase the overall deduction when one business is wage-heavy and another is income-heavy. Aggregation is an annual election made on the return and cannot be made retroactively without amending.

To aggregate, the businesses must meet specific requirements: the same person or group must own a majority interest in each business, the businesses must be related through common control, and the businesses must offer coordinated products or services or share facilities or centralized business elements. The aggregation requirements are factual and must be documented. Aggregation is not available for SSTBs (they cannot be aggregated with non-SSTBs to circumvent the SSTB limitation). Evaluate whether aggregation is beneficial by comparing the combined wage and basis limitation to the sum of the per-business limitations.

### Separate The Overall Taxable Income Limit

Even after computing the per-business or aggregated deduction, there is an overall cap: the total QBI deduction cannot exceed 20 percent of the taxpayer's overall taxable income minus net capital gain. Net capital gain for this purpose includes both long-term capital gains and qualified dividends. This means a taxpayer whose income is dominated by capital gains and dividends may have a very low QBI deduction because the taxable income minus net capital gain figure is small.

Compute both the sum of the per-business deductions and the overall taxable income limit, and take the lesser. Do not present the per-business computation as the final deduction without checking the overall limit. The overall limit is particularly relevant for taxpayers with significant investment income alongside business income.

### Include Qualified REIT Dividends And Publicly Traded Partnership Income

In addition to QBI from active businesses, the 20 percent deduction applies to qualified REIT dividends and qualified publicly traded partnership (PTP) income. These amounts are not subject to the SSTB restriction, the W-2 wage limitation, or the unadjusted basis limitation. They are simply eligible for a 20 percent deduction subject to the overall taxable income cap.

This means a taxpayer with significant REIT or PTP income receives a 20 percent deduction on those amounts regardless of income level, unlike SSTB business income. When computing the total QBI deduction, include the REIT and PTP components alongside the business QBI components. Note that not all REIT dividends qualify; the qualification depends on whether the dividend is a capital gain distribution or an ordinary dividend, and special rules apply to REITs held in retirement accounts.

## Common Traps

### Applying A Flat 20 Percent Without Checking Limitations

The 20 percent figure is the maximum, not the default. SSTB status, the wage and basis limitations, and the overall taxable income cap can all reduce the deduction below 20 percent. Quoting 20 percent without checking these layers overstates the benefit.

### Misclassifying A Service Business As Non-SSTB

The SSTB fields are defined broadly, and consulting in particular can capture many businesses. Misclassifying an SSTB as non-SSTB to avoid the phase-out is a high-risk position. The reputation-or-skill clause was narrowed by regulation but still applies in limited cases. Classify based on the regulatory definitions, not on the taxpayer's preference.

### Applying Limitations When Taxable Income Is Below The Threshold

Taxpayers below the taxable income threshold are not subject to the SSTB phase-out or the wage and basis limitations. Applying these limitations to a below-threshold taxpayer understates the deduction. Always check taxable income relative to the threshold first.

### Forgetting The Overall Taxable Income Minus Net Capital Gain Cap

The total QBI deduction is capped at 20 percent of taxable income minus net capital gain. A taxpayer with large capital gains may find the cap severely limits the deduction even if the per-business computation looks favorable. Always check the overall cap.

### Overlooking Aggregation As A Planning Tool

Aggregation can significantly increase the deduction for taxpayers with multiple related businesses. Failing to evaluate aggregation leaves deduction on the table. Conversely, aggregating without meeting the ownership, control, and coordination requirements creates an unsupported position.

### Excluding Reasonable Compensation And Guaranteed Payments Incorrectly

S corporation reasonable compensation and partner guaranteed payments are not QBI. They must be excluded from the QBI computation. However, the remaining business income after these payments is QBI. Confirm the wage and guaranteed payment amounts are properly separated from QBI.

### Treating Employee Income As QBI

Income earned as an employee is never QBI, regardless of how the relationship is labeled. A professional who is an employee cannot generate QBI from those services. Confirm the worker classification before claiming the deduction.

- [ ] Has the income been confirmed as qualified business income, excluding capital gains, investment income, foreign income, employee wages, reasonable compensation, and guaranteed payments?
- [ ] Has the business been classified as SSTB or non-SSTB based on the specific service field definitions, with consulting and the reputation-or-skill clause evaluated carefully?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer's taxable income been compared to the threshold and phase-in range to determine whether the SSTB phase-out and wage and basis limitations apply at all?
- [ ] For non-SSTB taxpayers above the threshold, has the W-2 wage and unadjusted basis limitation been computed for each business as the greater of 50 percent of wages or 25 percent of wages plus 2.5 percent of qualified property?
- [ ] For SSTB taxpayers in the phase-in range, has the linear phase-out been computed to prorate the deductible QBI from full to zero?
- [ ] Has aggregation been evaluated for taxpayers with multiple related businesses, checking the ownership, control, and coordination requirements and comparing aggregated versus separate limitations?
- [ ] Has the overall taxable income minus net capital gain cap been applied, taking the lesser of the per-business sum and 20 percent of the cap?
- [ ] Have qualified REIT dividends and publicly traded partnership income been included as separate components eligible for the 20 percent deduction without wage or SSTB limitations?
- [ ] Has the worker classification been confirmed to ensure employee income is not treated as QBI?
