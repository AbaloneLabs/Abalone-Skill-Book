# Doubt as to Collectibility

An offer in compromise based on doubt as to collectibility is the most common type of OIC, and it is available when the taxpayer's reasonable collection potential (RCP) is less than the total tax debt, so the IRS doubts it can collect the full amount within the collection statute. The judgment problem is that the OIC is not a right but a discretionary remedy, and the IRS imposes strict eligibility and compliance prerequisites, a rigorous RCP analysis, and ongoing compliance conditions after acceptance. An agent who files an OIC when the prerequisites are unmet (such as missing tax returns or unpaid estimated taxes) wastes the taxpayer's time and the filing fee, because the IRS will return the offer without consideration. An agent who files an offer below the RCP invites a rejection, because the IRS will not accept less than it believes it can collect. The OIC also has procedural traps: a prior OIC rejection may bar a new offer for a period, the taxpayer must remain in compliance for five years after acceptance (or the OIC is reinstated), and the taxpayer must agree to waive the collection statute and to surrender certain refunds.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the eligibility prerequisites (all returns filed, all estimated taxes paid, no open bankruptcy, and the required filing fee and initial payment) must be met before the OIC is filed, or the IRS returns it. Second, the offer amount must equal or exceed the RCP, because the IRS will not accept less. Third, the OIC tolls the collection statute (the 10-year period is extended by the time the OIC is pending plus 30 days), so a rejected OIC extends the collection period. Fourth, the taxpayer must remain in compliance (filing returns and paying taxes) for five years after acceptance, or the OIC is reinstated and the original debt is restored. Fifth, the taxpayer must surrender any refund due for the year the OIC is accepted, and the refund is applied to the tax debt. Sixth, a prior OIC that was accepted bars a new OIC for the same tax years, and a prior OIC that was withdrawn or rejected may affect a new offer.

This skill covers the offer in compromise based on doubt as to collectibility under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Eligibility And Compliance Prerequisites

Before filing an OIC, the taxpayer must meet the eligibility and compliance prerequisites. The taxpayer must have filed all required tax returns (for all years, not just the years with the debt), must have paid all required estimated tax payments for the current year, must not be in an open bankruptcy proceeding, and must submit the required filing fee (USD 205, subject to adjustment) and the initial payment (20 percent of the offer amount for a lump-sum offer, or the first monthly payment for an installment offer). Low-income taxpayers may qualify for a fee waiver and the initial payment waiver, based on the IRS's guidelines. If any prerequisite is unmet, the IRS returns the OIC without consideration.

Confirm the eligibility and compliance prerequisites. Verify that the taxpayer has filed all required tax returns (obtain the IRS account transcript to confirm), and prepare and file any missing returns before the OIC. Verify that the taxpayer has paid the required estimated taxes for the current year, and advise the taxpayer to stay current. Confirm that the taxpayer is not in an open bankruptcy. Prepare the Form 656 (the OIC) and the Form 433-A (OIC) or 433-B (OIC), and submit the filing fee and the initial payment. If the taxpayer qualifies for the low-income waiver (based on the income guidelines), apply for the waiver. The prerequisites are strict, and a failure to meet any of them results in the return of the OIC, so the agent should verify each prerequisite before filing.

### Set The Offer Amount At Or Above The RCP

The offer amount must equal or exceed the RCP, because the IRS will not accept less than it believes it can collect. The RCP is the sum of the net realizable equity of the taxpayer's assets plus the future income component, as computed on the Form 433. If the offer is below the RCP, the IRS rejects it (or counteroffers at the RCP). The taxpayer should set the offer at the RCP, unless there are special circumstances (such as economic hardship or public policy) that justify a lower offer under the effective tax administration grounds.

Set the offer amount at or above the RCP. Compute the RCP (the NRE of the assets plus the future income component), and set the offer at that amount. If the taxpayer cannot afford the RCP as a lump sum, consider an installment offer (which uses a 24-month multiplier for the future income, roughly doubling the future income component), but the total offer must still equal or exceed the RCP. If the taxpayer believes the RCP is too high (due to economic hardship or other special circumstances), consider an effective tax administration offer (which allows a lower offer based on hardship or public policy), but this is a separate ground. The offer amount is the key to acceptance, so it should be computed carefully and set at the RCP. A below-RCP offer is a common trap, because the taxpayer may hope for a discount, but the IRS will reject it.

### Understand The Effect On The Collection Statute

The filing of an OIC tolls the collection statute of limitations (the 10-year period under Section 6502) during the time the OIC is pending plus 30 days. This means the collection period is extended by the time the IRS considers the OIC (which can be several months to over a year) plus 30 days. The tolling is a significant consequence, because it extends the IRS's collection window, and the taxpayer should be advised of this before filing. If the OIC is rejected, the collection statute resumes, but with the extended period.

Understand the effect on the collection statute. The filing of the OIC tolls the 10-year collection period during the pendency plus 30 days, so the collection window is extended. The taxpayer should be advised of this, because the tolling is a cost of filing the OIC, and the taxpayer should weigh the benefit of the OIC against the extended collection period. If the OIC is accepted, the collection statute is generally moot (the debt is compromised), but if the OIC is rejected or reinstated, the extended period applies. The agent should compute the new collection statute expiration date (CSED) after the OIC, and the taxpayer should be advised of the extended exposure. The tolling is often overlooked, but it is a material consequence of filing the OIC.

### Maintain Compliance For Five Years After Acceptance

After an OIC is accepted, the taxpayer must remain in compliance (filing all returns on time and paying all taxes on time) for five years, or the OIC is reinstated and the original debt is restored. The five-year compliance period is a condition of the OIC, and a failure to comply (such as a late return or an unpaid tax) triggers the reinstatement. The taxpayer must also surrender any refund due for the year the OIC is accepted, and the refund is applied to the tax debt. The compliance conditions are strict, and the taxpayer should be advised of them before accepting the OIC.

Maintain compliance for five years after acceptance. Advise the taxpayer that they must file all returns on time and pay all taxes on time for five years after the OIC acceptance, or the OIC is reinstated. The taxpayer should set up a system to ensure compliance (such as estimated tax payments for self-employed taxpayers, or withholding adjustments for employees). The taxpayer must also surrender the refund for the acceptance year, so the taxpayer should be advised not to expect a refund for that year. The compliance conditions are a significant obligation, and a failure to comply can restore the original debt (which may have grown with interest and penalties), so the taxpayer should take the conditions seriously. The agent should monitor the compliance during the five-year period, especially for the first year, when the risk of non-compliance is highest.

### Evaluate The Effect Of A Prior OIC

A prior OIC may affect a new offer. If the prior OIC was accepted, the taxpayer cannot file a new OIC for the same tax years (the accepted OIC resolved those years). If the prior OIC was withdrawn, the taxpayer can file a new OIC, but the prior withdrawal may affect the IRS's view of the taxpayer's seriousness. If the prior OIC was rejected, the taxpayer can file a new OIC, but the taxpayer should first appeal the rejection (within 30 days) or wait for a change in circumstances (such as a decrease in income or an increase in expenses) that lowers the RCP. A prior OIC that was reinstated (due to non-compliance) restores the original debt, and the taxpayer can file a new OIC, but the IRS may view the reinstatement negatively.

Evaluate the effect of a prior OIC. Determine the status of any prior OIC (accepted, withdrawn, rejected, reinstated), and advise the taxpayer of the effect. If the prior OIC was accepted for the same years, a new OIC is not available. If the prior OIC was rejected, consider appealing the rejection (within 30 days) or filing a new OIC with a changed RCP. If the prior OIC was reinstated, the original debt is restored, and the taxpayer can file a new OIC, but the reinstatement may affect the IRS's view. The agent should obtain the IRS's records of the prior OIC, and the taxpayer should be advised of the procedural history before filing a new offer. A prior OIC is a common complication, because the taxpayer may not disclose it, and the IRS will discover it during the review.

### Prepare The Form 656 And The Form 433

The OIC is filed on Form 656 (the offer) and Form 433-A (OIC) or 433-B (OIC) (the Collection Information Statement). The Form 656 identifies the taxpayer, the tax years, the offer amount, the payment terms, and the legal grounds (doubt as to collectibility). The Form 433 provides the asset values, the income, the expenses, and the RCP computation. The forms must be complete and accurate, and the supporting documentation (the bank statements, the pay stubs, the appraisals) must be attached.

Prepare the Form 656 and the Form 433. Complete the Form 656 with the taxpayer's information, the tax years, the offer amount, the payment terms (lump-sum or installment), and the legal grounds (doubt as to collectibility). Complete the Form 433-A (OIC) or 433-B (OIC) with the asset values, the income, the expenses, and the RCP computation. Attach the supporting documentation (the bank statements for the last three months, the pay stubs, the appraisals, the vehicle valuations, the mortgage and loan statements). The forms are the basis for the OIC, so they must be complete and accurate. The IRS will verify the items, so the taxpayer should be prepared to substantiate each item. An incomplete or inaccurate Form 656 or Form 433 delays the review or leads to a rejection, so the agent should review the forms carefully before submission.

## Common Traps

### Filing The OIC With Missing Returns Or Unpaid Estimates

The symptom is a taxpayer who files an OIC with two missing tax returns. The trap is that the IRS returns the OIC without consideration, because the eligibility prerequisites are unmet. The direction is to file all missing returns and pay the estimated taxes before filing the OIC.

### Filing An Offer Below The RCP

The symptom is a taxpayer who offers USD 5,000 when the RCP is USD 20,000. The trap is that the IRS rejects the offer (or counteroffers at the RCP), because the offer is below the collectible amount. The direction is to set the offer at or above the RCP.

### Overlooking The Collection Statute Tolling

The symptom is a taxpayer who files an OIC without realizing it extends the collection statute. The trap is that the tolling extends the 10-year collection period by the pendency plus 30 days, so the taxpayer's exposure increases. The direction is to advise the taxpayer of the tolling and to compute the new CSED.

### Failing To Maintain Compliance After Acceptance

The symptom is a taxpayer whose OIC is accepted, but who files a late return in year two. The trap is that the non-compliance reinstates the OIC, restoring the original debt with interest and penalties. The direction is to advise the taxpayer of the five-year compliance condition and to monitor the compliance.

### Re-Filing For The Same Years After A Prior Acceptance

The symptom is a taxpayer who files a new OIC for years that were resolved by a prior accepted OIC. The trap is that the prior acceptance bars a new OIC for the same years. The direction is to confirm the status of any prior OIC and to advise the taxpayer of the bar.

### Not Appealing A Rejection Within 30 Days

The symptom is a taxpayer whose OIC is rejected, and who waits more than 30 days to respond. The trap is that the appeal deadline is 30 days, so the rejection becomes final. The direction is to appeal the rejection within 30 days or to file a new OIC with a changed RCP.
