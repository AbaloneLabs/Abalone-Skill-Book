# Reasonable Collection Potential

Reasonable collection potential (RCP) is the cornerstone of an offer in compromise based on doubt as to collectibility, and it is the amount the IRS believes it can collect from the taxpayer over the remaining collection statute (generally 10 years from assessment). The RCP is the sum of the net realizable equity (NRE) of the taxpayer's assets plus the future income component, less allowable living expenses. The judgment problem is that the RCP computation is mechanical but fact-intensive, and a miscalculation of the asset values, the future income, or the allowable expenses produces an offer that is either too high (the taxpayer pays more than necessary or the offer is rejected as unaffordable) or too low (the IRS rejects the offer as not in the government's best interest). The RCP also interacts with the dissipated assets rule (assets the taxpayer transferred or spent may be included in the RCP), the allowable living expense standards (the IRS's national and local standards), and the future income multiplier (which depends on whether the taxpayer pays the offer in a lump sum or in installments).

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the net realizable equity is the fair market value of the asset less any encumbrances and less a 20-percent (for real estate) or 15-percent (for personal property) quick-sale discount, so the NRE is often much less than the taxpayer's perceived equity. Second, the future income component is the taxpayer's monthly disposable income (income less allowable expenses) multiplied by a set number of months (12 for a lump-sum offer, 24 for a short-term installment offer), so the multiplier roughly doubles the future income for an installment offer. Third, the allowable living expenses are capped by the IRS's national and local standards, and expenses exceeding the standards are generally not allowed (except for certain conditional expenses). Fourth, dissipated assets (assets the taxpayer transferred for less than fair value or spent on non-necessary items) may be included in the RCP, increasing the offer amount. Fifth, retirement accounts are generally excluded from the RCP if the taxpayer is not of retirement age, but they may be included if the taxpayer can withdraw without penalty.

This skill covers the computation of reasonable collection potential for an offer in compromise under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Compute The Net Realizable Equity Of Assets

The net realizable equity (NRE) of an asset is the amount the IRS could realize from a forced sale of the asset. The NRE is computed as the fair market value (FMV) of the asset, less any encumbrances (such as mortgages or liens), and less a quick-sale discount. The quick-sale discount is 20 percent for real estate and 15 percent for personal property (vehicles, bank accounts, and other assets), reflecting the fact that a forced sale typically realizes less than a fair market sale. The NRE is the amount included in the RCP, not the FMV or the equity. For assets with no NRE (such as a vehicle with a loan balance exceeding the FMV), the asset contributes zero to the RCP.

Compute the NRE for each asset. Determine the FMV of the asset (using appraisals for real estate, Kelly Blue Book or similar for vehicles, and the account balance for bank accounts). Subtract the encumbrances (the mortgage balance, the vehicle loan balance). Apply the quick-sale discount (20 percent for real estate, 15 percent for personal property) to the equity (FMV less encumbrances). The result is the NRE. For a house with an FMV of USD 400,000 and a mortgage of USD 250,000, the equity is USD 150,000, and the NRE is USD 120,000 (USD 150,000 less 20 percent). For a vehicle with an FMV of USD 20,000 and a loan of USD 18,000, the equity is USD 2,000, and the NRE is USD 1,700 (USD 2,000 less 15 percent). The NRE is often much less than the taxpayer's perceived equity, so the agent should compute it carefully and explain it to the taxpayer.

### Compute The Future Income Component

The future income component is the taxpayer's projected ability to pay from future income over the remaining collection period. It is computed as the monthly disposable income (the taxpayer's monthly income less the allowable monthly living expenses) multiplied by a set number of months. The multiplier depends on the payment terms: 12 months for a lump-sum offer (paid within 5 months of acceptance), 24 months for a short-term periodic payment offer (paid within 6 to 24 months), and the remaining months on the collection statute for a deferred payment offer (paid beyond 24 months). The monthly disposable income is based on the taxpayer's current income and expenses, as documented on the Collection Information Statement (Form 433-A for individuals, Form 433-B for businesses).

Compute the future income component. Determine the taxpayer's monthly income (from wages, self-employment, pensions, and other sources) and the allowable monthly living expenses (based on the IRS standards and the taxpayer's actual necessary expenses). Subtract the expenses from the income to get the monthly disposable income. Multiply by the applicable multiplier (12 for a lump-sum, 24 for a short-term installment, the remaining collection months for a deferred payment). For a taxpayer with a monthly disposable income of USD 500 and a lump-sum offer, the future income component is USD 6,000 (USD 500 times 12). For a short-term installment offer, it is USD 12,000 (USD 500 times 24). The multiplier roughly doubles the future income for an installment offer, so the payment terms significantly affect the offer amount. The agent should evaluate the payment terms to minimize the offer, subject to the taxpayer's ability to pay.

### Apply The Allowable Living Expense Standards

The allowable living expenses are the expenses the IRS allows the taxpayer to deduct from their income to compute the monthly disposable income. The expenses are governed by the IRS's financial standards, which include the national standards (for food, clothing, and similar items, a flat monthly amount based on household size), the local standards (for housing and utilities, based on the county of residence), and the transportation standards (for vehicle ownership and operating costs, based on the region and the number of vehicles). Expenses exceeding the standards are generally not allowed, except for certain conditional expenses (such as taxes, court-ordered payments, and certain medical expenses, which are allowed in full). The taxpayer's actual necessary expenses that exceed the standards (such as high medical expenses) may be allowed with documentation.

Apply the allowable living expense standards. Use the IRS's national standards for food, clothing, and similar items (the flat monthly amount based on the household size). Use the local standards for housing and utilities (the amount based on the county). Use the transportation standards for vehicle ownership and operating costs. Allow the taxpayer's actual necessary expenses that exceed the standards only with documentation (such as high medical expenses, mandatory child support, or court-ordered payments). Do not allow expenses that exceed the standards without a justification, because the IRS will disallow them and increase the RCP. The standards are updated annually, so the agent should use the current standards. The allowable living expenses significantly affect the monthly disposable income and the future income component, so they should be applied carefully and documented.

### Include Dissipated Assets In The RCP

Dissipated assets are assets that the taxpayer transferred for less than fair value or spent on non-necessary items within a certain period (generally three years for most assets, but the IRS has discretion). The IRS may include the value of the dissipated assets in the RCP, increasing the offer amount, because the taxpayer is treated as if they still had the assets. The dissipated asset rule prevents a taxpayer from reducing the RCP by transferring or spending assets before filing the offer. The inclusion of dissipated assets is discretionary, and the IRS evaluates the intent (whether the transfer was to avoid collection) and the timing.

Include dissipated assets in the RCP. Identify any assets the taxpayer transferred for less than fair value or spent on non-necessary items within the relevant period (generally three years). Determine the value of the dissipated assets, and include the value in the RCP, as if the taxpayer still had the assets. The IRS evaluates the intent and the timing, so a legitimate transfer (such as a transfer at fair market value or a transfer for a necessary expense) may not be included. The dissipated asset rule is a common trap, because a taxpayer may have spent assets on a vacation or a luxury item, and the IRS includes the value in the RCP. The agent should review the taxpayer's financial history for dissipated assets and should advise the taxpayer that the dissipation may increase the offer amount.

### Address Retirement Accounts And Special Assets

Retirement accounts (such as 401(k) plans and IRAs) are generally excluded from the RCP if the taxpayer is not of retirement age and cannot withdraw without penalty, because the IRS does not expect the taxpayer to liquidate a retirement account to pay the tax. However, if the taxpayer is of retirement age or can withdraw without penalty, the retirement account may be included in the RCP (less the applicable early withdrawal penalty and the quick-sale discount). Other special assets (such as business assets, life insurance cash value, and inheritances) are evaluated based on their liquidity and the taxpayer's ability to access them.

Address the retirement accounts and the special assets. For retirement accounts, determine whether the taxpayer is of retirement age or can withdraw without penalty. If not, the retirement account is generally excluded from the RCP. If so, the retirement account is included (less the penalty and the discount). For business assets, determine the NRE and the effect on the business's operations (the IRS may exclude essential business assets to preserve the business as an ongoing source of collection). For life insurance cash value, determine the amount and the taxpayer's ability to borrow against it. For inheritances, determine the value and the timing. The special assets are evaluated case-by-case, so the agent should document the analysis and the IRS's position.

### Document The RCP On The Collection Information Statement

The RCP is documented on the Collection Information Statement (Form 433-A for individuals, Form 433-B for businesses), which provides the asset values, the income, the expenses, and the computation. The Form 433 must be complete and accurate, because the IRS uses it to verify the RCP and to evaluate the offer. The documentation should include the supporting evidence (the appraisals, the bank statements, the pay stubs, the expense documentation), and the taxpayer should be prepared to substantiate each item.

Document the RCP on the Form 433. Complete the Form 433-A (OIC) or 433-B (OIC) with the asset values, the income, the expenses, and the RCP computation. Attach the supporting evidence (the appraisals for real estate, the Kelly Blue Book for vehicles, the bank statements, the pay stubs, the expense documentation). The Form 433 is the basis for the RCP, so it must be complete and accurate. The IRS will verify the items, so the taxpayer should be prepared to substantiate each item. A Form 433 with errors or omissions can delay the offer or lead to a rejection, so the agent should review the Form 433 carefully before submission. The documentation is the key to a successful offer, so it should be thorough and well-organized.

## Common Traps

### Using Fair Market Value Instead Of Net Realizable Equity

The symptom is an agent who includes the FMV of the house in the RCP. The trap is that the RCP uses the NRE (FMV less encumbrances less the quick-sale discount), so the FMV overstates the RCP. The direction is to compute the NRE with the 20-percent (real estate) or 15-percent (personal property) quick-sale discount.

### Using The Wrong Future Income Multiplier

The symptom is an agent who uses the 12-month multiplier for an installment offer. The trap is that the multiplier is 12 for a lump-sum offer and 24 for a short-term installment offer, so the wrong multiplier understates the future income. The direction is to apply the correct multiplier based on the payment terms.

### Exceeding The Allowable Living Expense Standards

The symptom is an agent who allows the taxpayer's actual housing expense that exceeds the local standard. The trap is that the IRS caps the expenses at the standards (except for conditional expenses), so the excess is disallowed and the RCP increases. The direction is to apply the national, local, and transportation standards and to allow excess only with documentation.

### Overlooking Dissipated Assets

The symptom is a taxpayer who spent USD 50,000 on a vacation two years ago, and the agent does not include it in the RCP. The trap is that the IRS may include the dissipated assets in the RCP, increasing the offer amount. The direction is to review the financial history for dissipated assets and to include them where applicable.

### Including Retirement Accounts That Should Be Excluded

The symptom is an agent who includes a 401(k) in the RCP for a 45-year-old taxpayer. The trap is that retirement accounts are generally excluded if the taxpayer cannot withdraw without penalty, so the inclusion overstates the RCP. The direction is to exclude retirement accounts for taxpayers below retirement age who cannot withdraw without penalty.

### Filing An Incomplete Form 433

The symptom is a Form 433 with missing asset values or undocumented expenses. The trap is that the IRS uses the Form 433 to verify the RCP, so the omissions delay the offer or lead to a rejection. The direction is to complete the Form 433 with the supporting evidence and to substantiate each item.
