# Effective Tax Administration

An offer in compromise based on effective tax administration (ETA) is the rarest and most discretionary type of OIC, available when collection of the full tax debt would create an economic hardship or would be detrimental to public policy or equity, even though the tax is fully collectible (the RCP equals or exceeds the debt). The judgment problem is that the ETA offer requires the taxpayer to show that collection would be unfair or inequitable despite the taxpayer's ability to pay, and the IRS applies a high standard. The ETA offer has two grounds: economic hardship (collection would prevent the taxpayer from meeting basic living expenses) and public policy (collection would undermine public confidence in the fair and equitable administration of the tax laws) or compelling equity. An agent who files an ETA offer without the required hardship or policy showing wastes the taxpayer's time, because the IRS will reject it. The ETA offer also requires the taxpayer to first pursue the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC (if applicable), and the IRS evaluates the ETA offer under a separate, stricter analysis.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the ETA offer is available only when the RCP equals or exceeds the debt (the tax is collectible), so if the RCP is below the debt, the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC is the correct ground. Second, the economic hardship ground requires that collection would leave the taxpayer unable to meet basic living expenses (such as a fixed-income retiree whose only asset is a modest retirement account), and the hardship must be sustained, not temporary. Third, the public policy ground is extremely narrow, and the IRS applies it only in exceptional cases (such as where the tax arose from a terrorist attack or a natural disaster, or where the taxpayer was the victim of a crime). Fourth, the ETA offer requires a showing that the taxpayer has pursued all other remedies (such as the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC and the installment agreement), and the IRS evaluates the taxpayer's compliance history. Fifth, the ETA offer, if accepted, resolves the debt, but the five-year compliance condition applies (like the other OIC types). Sixth, the special circumstances must be documented thoroughly, because the IRS scrutinizes the ETA offers more than the other types.

This skill covers the offer in compromise based on effective tax administration under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish ETA From Doubt As To Collectibility

The ETA offer is available when the tax is collectible (the RCP equals or exceeds the debt) but collection would create an economic hardship or would be against public policy or equity. The doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC is available when the RCP is below the debt, so the IRS doubts it can collect the full amount. The two grounds are mutually exclusive for the same debt: if the RCP is below the debt, the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC is the correct ground, and the ETA offer is not available. The ETA offer is the remedy for a taxpayer who can pay (the RCP is high enough) but for whom collection would be unfair or inequitable.

Distinguish the ETA offer from the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC. Compute the RCP, and compare it to the debt. If the RCP is below the debt, the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC is the correct ground, and the ETA offer is not available. If the RCP equals or exceeds the debt, the ETA offer may be available, if the taxpayer can show economic hardship or public policy grounds. The agent should not file an ETA offer when the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC is available, because the IRS will redirect the taxpayer to the collectibility ground. The distinction is the first step, because the ETA offer is a narrower remedy, and the IRS applies a higher standard.

### Apply The Economic Hardship Ground

The economic hardship ground for an ETA offer requires that collection of the full tax debt would leave the taxpayer unable to meet basic living expenses. The hardship must be sustained, not temporary, and it must prevent the taxpayer from paying for food, housing, clothing, transportation, medical care, and other necessities. The IRS evaluates the hardship based on the taxpayer's income, expenses, assets, and special circumstances (such as age, disability, or dependents). A common example is a fixed-income retiree whose only asset is a modest retirement account that, if liquidated, would leave the retiree unable to meet basic living expenses for the remainder of their life. The hardship ground is the more common of the two ETA grounds, but it still requires a strong showing.

Apply the economic hardship ground. Determine whether collection of the full debt would leave the taxpayer unable to meet basic living expenses, based on the taxpayer's income, expenses, assets, and special circumstances. Document the hardship with the financial records (the income, the expenses, the assets) and the special circumstances (such as a medical condition, a disability, or a dependent's needs). The hardship must be sustained, so a temporary hardship (such as a short-term job loss) does not qualify. The IRS evaluates the hardship based on the national and local standards, but it may allow a higher expense level if the special circumstances justify it. The economic hardship ground is the more accessible ETA ground, but it requires a thorough documentation of the taxpayer's financial situation and the special circumstances.

### Apply The Public Policy Or Compelling Equity Ground

The public policy or compelling equity ground for an ETA offer requires that collection of the full tax debt would undermine public confidence in the fair and equitable administration of the tax laws, or that compelling equity factors make collection inequitable. This ground is extremely narrow, and the IRS applies it only in exceptional cases. Examples include a tax debt that arose from a terrorist attack, a natural disaster, or a serious illness (where the taxpayer was a victim), or a tax debt that arose from an error by the IRS or a third party (where the taxpayer acted in good faith). The public policy ground is not available for general financial hardship (which is the economic hardship ground) or for the taxpayer's disagreement with the tax law.

Apply the public policy or compelling equity ground. Determine whether collection would undermine public confidence or whether compelling equity factors make collection inequitable, based on the exceptional circumstances. Document the circumstances (such as the terrorist attack, the natural disaster, the serious illness, or the third-party error) with the supporting evidence (the medical records, the disaster relief documentation, the correspondence with the IRS or the third party). The public policy ground is the rarest ETA ground, and the IRS applies a very high standard, so the agent should not file under this ground unless the circumstances are truly exceptional. The compelling equity ground is slightly broader, but it still requires a strong showing that collection would be inequitable (such as where the taxpayer was the victim of a crime or a fraud).

### Pursue Other Remedies First

The ETA offer requires the taxpayer to pursue all other remedies first, including the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC (if applicable) and the installment agreement. The IRS evaluates the ETA offer only after the other remedies are shown to be inadequate. The taxpayer should document the pursuit of the other remedies (such as a prior OIC rejection or an installment agreement that does not resolve the debt), and the ETA offer should explain why the other remedies are inadequate.

Pursue the other remedies first. If the RCP is below the debt, file the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC first, and pursue it to a conclusion. If the RCP equals or exceeds the debt, evaluate the installment agreement (which allows the taxpayer to pay the debt over time, up to the collection statute). If the installment agreement does not resolve the hardship (such as where the payment would still leave the taxpayer unable to meet basic living expenses), document the inadequacy and file the ETA offer. The IRS evaluates the ETA offer in light of the other remedies, so the taxpayer should show that the other remedies were pursued and were inadequate. A failure to pursue the other remedies may result in the IRS redirecting the taxpayer, so the agent should document the pursuit.

### Document The Special Circumstances Thoroughly

The ETA offer requires a thorough documentation of the special circumstances (the economic hardship or the public policy factors), because the IRS scrutinizes the ETA offers more than the other types. The documentation should include the financial records (the income, the expenses, the assets), the special circumstances (the medical condition, the disability, the disaster, the third-party error), and the narrative explaining why collection would be unfair or inequitable. The documentation should be specific and well-organized, because the IRS's decision turns on the strength of the showing.

Document the special circumstances thoroughly. For the economic hardship ground, gather the financial records (the income statements, the expense schedules, the asset valuations) and the special circumstances (the medical records, the disability documentation, the dependent's needs). For the public policy ground, gather the exceptional circumstances (the disaster relief documentation, the crime victim documentation, the third-party error correspondence). Prepare a narrative that explains why collection would create a hardship or would undermine public confidence, and why the other remedies are inadequate. The documentation is the key to the ETA offer, because the IRS's decision is discretionary and based on the strength of the showing. A poorly documented ETA offer is likely to be rejected, so the agent should invest in the documentation.

### Maintain Compliance After Acceptance

Like the other OIC types, the ETA offer, if accepted, requires the taxpayer to remain in compliance (filing returns and paying taxes on time) for five years, or the OIC is reinstated. The taxpayer must also surrender any refund due for the year the OIC is accepted. The compliance conditions are the same as for the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC, and the taxpayer should be advised of them before accepting the ETA offer.

Maintain compliance after acceptance. Advise the taxpayer of the five-year compliance condition and the refund surrender, and monitor the compliance during the period. The compliance condition is especially important for an ETA offer based on economic hardship, because the taxpayer's financial situation may be fragile, and a non-compliance event (such as a late return) could reinstate the original debt. The agent should help the taxpayer set up a compliance system (such as estimated tax payments or withholding adjustments) to ensure the five-year condition is met. The reinstatement of an ETA offer restores the original debt with interest and penalties, which could be devastating for a taxpayer who was granted the hardship relief, so the compliance condition must be taken seriously.

## Common Traps

### Filing An ETA Offer When The RCP Is Below The Debt

The symptom is a taxpayer whose RCP is below the debt, and the agent files an ETA offer. The trap is that the ETA offer is available only when the RCP equals or exceeds the debt, so the correct ground is doubt as to collectibility. The direction is to compute the RCP and to file the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC when the RCP is below the debt.

### Claiming Economic Hardship For A Temporary Situation

The symptom is a taxpayer who claims hardship based on a temporary job loss. The trap is that the economic hardship ground requires a sustained hardship, not a temporary one, so the claim is denied. The direction is to document a sustained hardship that prevents the taxpayer from meeting basic living expenses.

### Invoking Public Policy For General Financial Difficulty

The symptom is a taxpayer who claims public policy based on general financial difficulty. The trap is that the public policy ground is for exceptional circumstances (disasters, crimes, third-party errors), not for general financial difficulty, so the claim is denied. The direction is to reserve the public policy ground for exceptional circumstances.

### Not Pursuing Other Remedies First

The symptom is a taxpayer who files an ETA offer without first pursuing the installment agreement or the doubt-as-to-collectibility OIC. The trap is that the IRS evaluates the ETA offer in light of the other remedies, so the failure to pursue them may result in a redirection. The direction is to document the pursuit of the other remedies and their inadequacy.

### Under-Documenting The Special Circumstances

The symptom is an ETA offer with a vague narrative and no supporting evidence. The trap is that the IRS scrutinizes the ETA offers, so the under-documented offer is rejected. The direction is to document the special circumstances thoroughly with the financial records and the supporting evidence.

### Overlooking The Compliance Condition After Acceptance

The symptom is a taxpayer whose ETA offer is accepted, but who files a late return in year three. The trap is that the non-compliance reinstates the OIC, restoring the original debt, which is devastating for a hardship taxpayer. The direction is to advise the taxpayer of the five-year condition and to monitor the compliance.
