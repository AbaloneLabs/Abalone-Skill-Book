# Country of Origin and FTA Claims

Country of origin is the determination of where a good comes from for customs purposes, and it is one of the most consequential and most disputed judgments in trade. Origin drives the applicability of additional tariffs such as Section 301 measures, the eligibility for preferential duty treatment under free-trade agreements and preference programs, the country-of-origin marking requirements, and the treatment under trade-remedy laws. There is no single universal origin rule; instead there are non-preferential origin rules, used for marking and trade-remedy purposes, and separate preferential origin rules, written into each agreement, used to claim reduced or zero duty. An agent who treats origin as simply the country the goods shipped from, or who assumes one agreement's rule equals another's, will produce origin claims that fail and that can expose the importer to denied preferences, back duties, and false-certification penalties.

Agents commonly miss several things about origin determination and preference claims. They miss that preferential and non-preferential origin are different legal tests and can yield different answers for the same good, so a product may be non-preferentially of one country for Section 301 purposes yet qualify for preference under a free-trade agreement after processing elsewhere. They miss that substantial transformation, the historical non-preferential test, is highly fact-specific and that for many agreements the test is instead a product-specific rule based on a tariff shift, a regional value content threshold, or a specified process. They miss that origin and classification interact, because the product-specific rule is tied to the tariff classification of the finished good, and that claiming a preference requires more than meeting the rule of origin; it requires a valid certification, recordkeeping, and a good-faith basis. The harm is an importer who claims zero duty for years, then loses the claim on audit and owes the full duty plus interest and penalties, having built pricing and supply-chain decisions on a preference that never applied.

This skill covers country-of-origin determination and free-trade-agreement preference claims, including the distinction between preferential and non-preferential origin, the wholly-obtained substantial-transformation tariff-shift and regional-value-content rules, certification and substantiation, and how origin and classification interact. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax or legal advice; customs rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time. A qualified customs broker or customs attorney must be consulted for any specific situation, and where stakes are material a binding ruling should be requested.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Preferential From Non-Preferential Origin First

The first judgment is which origin question is being asked, because the answer uses a different rule. Non-preferential origin determines the country of origin for purposes that do not involve a trade agreement, including country-of-origin marking, the applicability of Section 301 and other additional tariffs, and trade-remedy actions. Preferential origin determines whether a good qualifies for reduced or zero duty under a specific agreement or program, and it is governed by the rule of origin written into that agreement. A good can have one non-preferential origin and qualify, or fail to qualify, for preference under several different agreements, each with its own rule.

Never conflate the two. For example, a good may be non-preferentially of a country subject to Section 301 tariffs and yet, after processing in a free-trade-agreement country, qualify for preferential treatment if it meets that agreement's rule of origin. Conversely, a good may be non-preferentially of the agreement country and still fail the preference rule because its non-originating inputs did not undergo the required tariff shift. Identify which origin question is on the table before applying any rule, because applying a preferential rule to a non-preferential question, or vice versa, produces a wrong and indefensible answer that can misstate both the duty and the additional-tariff exposure.

### Apply The Non-Preferential Tests Correctly

Non-preferential origin generally turns on one of two approaches. The first is wholly obtained, which applies to goods grown, mined, or born in a single country with no foreign input, such as minerals extracted from that country's soil or crops harvested there. The second is substantial transformation, the test used when foreign materials are processed in a country, which asks whether the processing resulted in a new and different article of commerce having a distinctive name, character, or use. Substantial transformation is a facts-and-circumstances judgment that customs authorities examine closely, and it has been narrowed in certain statutory contexts, such as for government procurement, where a specific standard applies.

Apply the correct non-preferential test and document the facts. For wholly obtained goods, establish the source. For processed goods, analyze the transformation, considering whether the processing created a new article with a new name, character, and use, or whether it was merely a minor operation such as simple assembly, packaging, or dilution that does not confer origin. Simple operations generally do not confer origin, and the line between substantial and minor processing is often the heart of a dispute. Record the inputs, the processing steps, the resulting article, and the reasoning, because a non-preferential origin claim with no analysis is indefensible, especially where additional-tariff exposure turns on it.

### Use The Product-Specific Rules For Preferential Origin

Modern free-trade agreements generally do not rely on a freestanding substantial-transformation test for preferential origin. Instead they provide product-specific rules of origin tied to the tariff classification of the good, typically requiring that non-originating inputs undergo a specified tariff shift, that the good meet a regional value content (RVC) threshold, or that a specified manufacturing process occur in the region. The rule must be looked up for the specific tariff classification of the finished good, and it differs by chapter and by product.

Apply the product-specific rule methodically. Classify the finished good, locate its rule in the agreement's origin rules annex, and test the inputs against that rule. For a tariff-shift rule, determine the classification of each non-originating input and confirm that it shifts to a different heading or subheading as required. For an RVC rule, compute the regional value content under the transaction-value or net-cost method, as the agreement allows, and confirm the threshold is met. Some goods have alternative rules, and some require both a tariff shift and an RVC. Do not assume a single generic rule applies across products; the rule is specific to the classification, and a wrong lookup defeats the claim.

### Trace Inputs And Compute Regional Value Content Accurately

Preferential origin claims depend on the origin status of every input, which requires tracing inputs through the supply chain and computing any RVC correctly. An input is originating if it is wholly obtained in the region or if it satisfies the product-specific rule for that input; otherwise it is non-originating and must undergo the required tariff shift in the finished good. The RVC computation depends on the method allowed by the agreement, and the inputs and costs included differ between the transaction-value method and the net-cost method.

Trace each input and document its origin status, because a single mischaracterized input can flip the claim. For the RVC, apply the formula the agreement prescribes, using the correct value basis, and exclude or include the costs the agreement specifies, such as freight, royalties, and certain overhead. The net-cost method, often required for automotive goods and certain other products, involves a specific cost build-up and excludes specified costs, and errors in the build-up are common. Where the agreement allows a choice of method, select the method that the facts support and document the choice. An RVC claim built on a wrong value basis or with excluded costs miscounted is not defensible.

### Recognize How Origin And Classification Interact

Origin and classification are not independent analyses; they are coupled, because the product-specific preferential rule is written against the tariff classification of the finished good, and the tariff-shift test is expressed as a change between the classification of an input and the classification of the finished good. A change in the classification of the finished good changes the rule that applies, and a change in the classification of an input can change whether a tariff shift is satisfied. This coupling runs in both directions and is the reason origin and classification must be analyzed together.

Classify the finished good first, then locate its rule, then classify each non-originating input to test the tariff shift. For example, if a finished good is reclassified into a different heading during a review, the origin rule that applies may change entirely, and a claim that previously qualified may fail. Conversely, an input reclassified into a different subheading may now satisfy a tariff shift it previously failed. Do not treat a preference claim as stable when the underlying classifications are uncertain, and re-test the claim whenever a classification changes, because an origin analysis built on a stale classification is a hidden point of failure.

### Maintain The Certification And Recordkeeping For The Claim

Claiming a preference requires a valid certification of origin, and the form and content of that certification depend on the agreement. Under some agreements the certification can be completed by the importer, exporter, or producer, and it must contain specified data elements rather than a single prescribed form. Under other programs the supporting evidence and the certification requirements differ. The importer must have a valid certification in hand, or the required data elements, at the time the preference is claimed, and must retain it for the recordkeeping period.

Confirm the certification meets the agreement's requirements before claiming the preference, not after. Verify that the required data elements are present, that the certifier is the proper party, and that the certification covers the goods and the period claimed. Retain the certification and all supporting origin documentation for the recordkeeping period, which can be several years. A preference claim without a valid certification, or with a certification completed by the wrong party, is invalid and the preference is recoverable with interest, even if the good actually qualified.

### Establish A Good-Faith Basis And Consider A Binding Ruling

A preference claim is a representation to customs, and the importer must have a good-faith basis for it, supported by the rule of origin analysis and the documentation. Claiming a preference without the analysis, or claiming it based on the country of shipment rather than the rule, is a false claim that can carry penalties, and in cases of knowing falsity, criminal exposure. Because origin is fact-intensive and the rules are agreement-specific, customs authorities offer binding origin rulings that provide prospective certainty for goods imported in volume or near the boundary of a rule.

Build the good-faith basis before claiming, and recommend a binding ruling where the duty exposure is material or the origin is recurring and uncertain. The ruling request must present the facts completely, including the inputs, the processing, the classifications, and the applicable rule, and incomplete or misleading facts can invalidate the ruling. Treat a preference claim as a legal conclusion that must be supported, not as a default that can be assumed, because the consequences of a false claim are severe and the recovery period is long.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Preferential And Non-Preferential Origin

The symptom is one origin answer used for both marking and preference. The trap is that the two regimes use different rules and can yield different answers for the same good, so using a preferential rule to answer a non-preferential question such as Section 301 applicability is wrong. The direction is to identify which origin question is asked and apply the matching rule.

### Treating The Country Of Shipment As The Country Of Origin

The symptom is an origin or preference claim based on the export country. The trap is that origin depends on where the good was obtained or last substantially transformed, not on the logistics path, so claiming from the shipment country without analyzing inputs and processing fails. The direction is to trace the inputs and the processing to determine the true origin.

### Applying A Generic Substantial-Transformation Test To Preferential Claims

The symptom is a preference claim justified by substantial transformation alone. The trap is that modern agreements use product-specific tariff-shift and RVC rules, not a freestanding substantial-transformation test, for preferential origin, so the claim is unsupported. The direction is to look up the rule for the finished good's classification and apply it.

### Misreading The Product-Specific Rule For The Classification

The symptom is a rule applied from a similar product or a prior agreement version. The trap is that the product-specific rule is tied to the tariff classification of the finished good and differs by product, so a wrong lookup defeats the claim. The direction is to classify the finished good first, then locate and apply its specific rule.

### Letting Origin And Classification Drift Apart

The symptom is a preference claim kept after the finished good or an input was reclassified. The trap is that the origin rule is written against the classification, so a classification change can change the rule or whether a tariff shift is satisfied, leaving a stale claim. The direction is to re-test the origin claim whenever a classification changes.

### Computing Regional Value Content On The Wrong Basis

The symptom is an RVC figure that uses the wrong method or includes costs that should be excluded. The trap is that RVC can be computed under the transaction-value or net-cost method with different formulas and costs, and a miscounted input flips the result. The direction is to apply the agreement's prescribed formula and value basis and to document each input's origin status.

### Claiming A Preference Without A Valid Certification

The symptom is a preference claimed before or without the certification. The trap is that a claim requires a valid certification with the specified data elements completed by the proper party, so an incomplete or wrongly completed certification makes the preference recoverable with interest even if the good qualified. The direction is to confirm the certification meets the requirements before claiming and to retain it for the recordkeeping period.

### Overclaiming Preference Based On Country Alone

The symptom is preference claimed for all goods from a beneficiary country without per-item analysis. The trap is that the duty savings create an incentive to overclaim, and customs authorities audit precisely because of this, so a claim without the rule analysis is a false claim. The direction is to build a good-faith basis for each claim and not to claim where the analysis is missing or uncertain.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the correct origin regime been identified, distinguishing non-preferential origin (marking, Section 301, trade remedies) from preferential origin under a specific agreement or program?
- [ ] For non-preferential origin, has the wholly-obtained or substantial-transformation test been applied, with the inputs, processing, and reasoning documented, and minor operations excluded from conferring origin?
- [ ] For preferential origin, has the product-specific rule of origin been located for the finished good's tariff classification and applied, rather than assuming a generic substantial-transformation test?
- [ ] Has each input been traced and its origin status determined, and where a regional value content rule applies, has the RVC been computed under the agreement's prescribed method and value basis?
- [ ] Has the interaction between origin and classification been recognized, so that the claim is re-tested whenever the finished good or an input is reclassified?
- [ ] Has a valid certification of origin with the required data elements, completed by the proper party, been obtained and retained for the recordkeeping period before the preference is claimed?
- [ ] Has a good-faith basis been established for each preference claim, with the rule analysis, input tracing, RVC computation, and supporting documentation, and has overclaiming based on country alone been avoided?
- [ ] Has a binding origin ruling been considered and recommended for material or recurring origin questions where the preference savings or additional-tariff exposure is significant?
- [ ] Has the agent recognized that origin claims are representations to customs with penalty and potential criminal exposure for false claims, and avoided presenting an unsupported claim as certain?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general educational customs information, not personalized tax or legal advice, and recommended consulting a qualified customs broker or customs attorney for the specific situation?
