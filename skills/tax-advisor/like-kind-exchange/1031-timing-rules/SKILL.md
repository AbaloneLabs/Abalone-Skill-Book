# Section 1031 Timing Rules

The timing rules of a Section 1031 like-kind exchange are among the strictest in the tax code. The taxpayer has 45 days to identify replacement property and 180 days to acquire it, both measured from the closing of the relinquished property. These deadlines are absolute and cannot be extended for weekends, holidays, or even natural disasters in most cases. An agent who treats these deadlines as flexible, or who fails to account for the interaction with the tax return due date, will cause the exchange to fail and trigger immediate taxation of the full gain.

Agents commonly miss that the 45-day and 180-day periods run concurrently (not consecutively), that the 180-day period may be cut short by the tax return due date, that the identification must follow specific rules (Three-Property, 200%, or 95%), and that extensions of time are rarely granted. The harm is a taxpayer who misses a deadline by a day and loses the entire deferral.

This skill covers Section 1031 timing rules under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; 1031 exchange timing is strict and requires professional coordination, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Measure The 45-Day Identification Period

The taxpayer must identify the replacement property in writing within 45 calendar days of the closing of the relinquished property. Day one is the day after closing. The 45-day period includes weekends and holidays; there is no extension if the 45th day falls on a weekend or holiday. The identification must be in writing, signed by the taxpayer, and delivered to the qualified intermediary or another party to the exchange (not the taxpayer or a related party) by midnight of the 45th day.

Count the 45 days carefully, starting the day after closing. Mark the deadline on the calendar and build in a buffer. The identification must be received (not just postmarked) by the 45th day. The written identification must unambiguously describe the property (legal description, street address, or other specific identification). For multiple properties, follow one of the three identification rules. Missing the 45-day deadline by even one day collapses the exchange; there is no relief for a late identification. Treat the deadline as absolute.

### Measure The 180-Day Acquisition Period

The taxpayer must acquire the replacement property within 180 calendar days of the closing of the relinquished property or by the due date of the taxpayer's tax return for the year of the sale (including extensions), whichever is earlier. Day one is the day after closing. The 180-day period includes weekends and holidays. The replacement property must be substantially the same property as identified within the 45-day period.

Count the 180 days carefully, but first check the tax return due date limit. The 180-day period is cut short if the taxpayer's tax return is due earlier. For a calendar-year taxpayer, the return is due April 15 of the following year (or October 15 with an extension). If the relinquished property sold late in the year, the return due date may be before the 180-day mark. The taxpayer must file an extension to get the full 180 days. Failing to file an extension can cut the acquisition period short. Mark both deadlines and use the earlier one.

### Apply The Three Identification Rules

The taxpayer must identify replacement property following one of three rules. The Three-Property Rule allows identification of up to three properties of any value, regardless of the fair market value of the relinquished property. The 200% Rule allows identification of any number of properties, provided their total fair market value does not exceed 200% of the relinquished property's value. The 95% Rule allows identification of any number of properties of any value, provided the taxpayer acquires at least 95% of the total identified value.

Determine which identification rule applies. The Three-Property Rule is the most common and the simplest, allowing up to three properties of any value. The 200% Rule is used when the taxpayer wants to identify more than three properties but keep the total value under 200% of the relinquished property. The 95% Rule is rarely used because it requires acquiring almost all the identified value. Choose the rule that fits the taxpayer's needs and document the identification accordingly. The taxpayer can switch rules before the 45-day deadline but must comply with one rule by the deadline.

### Handle The Interaction With The Tax Return Due Date

The 180-day acquisition period is limited by the due date of the taxpayer's tax return for the year of the sale (including extensions). If the return is due before the 180th day, the acquisition period is cut short. The taxpayer must file an extension to get the full 180 days. For a calendar-year taxpayer who sells relinquished property in November, the return is due April 15 (about 165 days later), which is before the 180-day mark; without an extension, the acquisition period is cut to about 165 days.

Identify the tax return due date and compare it to the 180-day mark. If the return due date is earlier, advise the taxpayer to file an extension. Filing an extension extends the return due date to October 15, which is always after the 180-day mark, preserving the full 180 days. Do not assume the 180 days are always available; for late-year sales, the extension is essential. Build the extension into the exchange plan from the outset.

### Manage The Identification Before Acquisition

The taxpayer must acquire property that was identified within the 45-day period. The taxpayer cannot identify one property and acquire a different one. However, the taxpayer can identify multiple properties (under the Three-Property or 200% Rule) and acquire any one or more of them. The acquisition must be of the identified property, but the taxpayer is not required to acquire all identified properties.

Ensure the acquired property matches an identification. If the taxpayer identified three properties and acquires one, the exchange is valid for the one acquired. If the taxpayer acquires a property that was not identified, that acquisition does not qualify and is treated as a separate taxable purchase. The identification must be accurate and specific to allow the acquisition. Review the identification before the acquisition to confirm the match.

### Understand Extensions Of Time Are Rarely Granted

The IRS may grant an extension of time for the identification or acquisition deadlines only in very limited circumstances, such as a Presidentially declared disaster (hurricane, flood, wildfire) affecting the taxpayer or the property. Routine delays, financing issues, or personal circumstances do not qualify for an extension. The extension is not automatic even for disasters; the taxpayer must request it.

Do not rely on an extension of time. Treat the 45-day and 180-day deadlines as absolute. If a disaster affects the exchange, consult immediately about requesting an extension, but do not assume it will be granted. The safest approach is to meet the deadlines strictly. Planning should assume no extensions and build in buffers for identification and acquisition.

## Common Traps

### Counting The 45 Or 180 Days Incorrectly

Day one is the day after closing, not the closing day itself. Counting from the closing day adds a day of error and can cause a late identification or acquisition.

### Forgetting The Return Due Date Cuts The 180-Day Period

For late-year sales, the return due date may be before the 180-day mark. The taxpayer must file an extension to get the full 180 days. Failing to extend cuts the period short.

### Missing The 45-Day Deadline By A Day

The 45-day deadline is absolute. Missing it by one day collapses the exchange. There is no relief for weekends, holidays, or minor delays.

### Identifying Property That Cannot Be Acquired

The taxpayer must acquire identified property. Identifying property that is not available, overpriced, or cannot close in time wastes an identification slot and risks the exchange.

### Switching Identification Rules Improperly

The taxpayer must comply with one identification rule by the 45-day deadline. Switching rules after the deadline or failing to comply with any rule collapses the exchange.

### Assuming Extensions Of Time Are Available

Extensions of time are rarely granted, only for Presidentially declared disasters. Routine delays do not qualify. Do not rely on an extension; meet the deadlines strictly.

- [ ] Has the 180-day acquisition period been counted correctly and compared to the tax return due date (with extension), using the earlier date?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer been advised to file a tax return extension if the return due date would cut the 180-day period short?
- [ ] Has the identification followed one of the three rules (Three-Property, 200%, or 95%), with the rule documented?
- [ ] Has the acquired property been confirmed to match an identification made within the 45-day period?
- [ ] Has the agent confirmed that extensions of time are not available for routine delays and that the deadlines are treated as absolute?
