# Section 1031 Exchange Requirements

A Section 1031 like-kind exchange allows a taxpayer to defer the gain on the sale of investment or business real property by reinvesting the proceeds in replacement property of like-kind. Done correctly, the exchange can defer tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars of gain, preserving capital for reinvestment. Done incorrectly, a single missed requirement can collapse the entire exchange and trigger immediate taxation of the full gain. The requirements are strict and unforgiving, and an agent who treats a 1031 exchange as a simple swap without understanding the rules will expose the taxpayer to full taxation.

Agents commonly miss that only real property qualifies after the TCJA (not personal property), that the property must be held for investment or productive use in a trade or business (not primarily for resale), that the exchange must use a qualified intermediary (the taxpayer cannot touch the cash), and that the replacement property must be identified and acquired within strict deadlines. The harm is a taxpayer who believes the gain is deferred but faces full taxation plus penalties.

This skill covers Section 1031 like-kind exchange requirements under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; 1031 exchanges are complex and require professional structuring, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Real Property And Like-Kind Qualification

After the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, Section 1031 applies only to real property, not personal property. Real property includes land, buildings, and inherently permanent structures, and certain intangible assets associated with real property. Personal property (equipment, vehicles, artwork, collectibles) no longer qualifies. The relinquished property and the replacement property must both be real property held for investment or productive use in a trade or business.

Confirm both the relinquished and replacement properties are real property. For mixed-use property, separate the real property from the personal property; only the real property portion qualifies. The like-kind standard is broad for real property: virtually any investment or business real property is like-kind to any other (an apartment building is like-kind to raw land, a retail building is like-kind to an office building). Do not require the properties to be identical; the like-kind standard is met if both are real property held for the qualifying purpose. The narrow issue is the holding purpose, not the property type.

### Verify The Holding Purpose Is Investment Or Business Use

The property must be held for investment or for productive use in a trade or business. Property held primarily for sale (dealer property, inventory, fix-and-flip) does not qualify. A primary residence does not qualify. The holding purpose is a facts-and-circumstances test, and the IRS scrutinizes exchanges involving property held for a short time or property that looks like dealer activity.

Verify the holding purpose through the taxpayer's history and intent. Investment property held for rental or appreciation qualifies. Business-use property (real property used in an active trade or business) qualifies. Property held primarily for resale does not qualify. A property held briefly and then exchanged may be challenged as lacking investment intent. Document the investment purpose (rental history, lease agreements, holding period, business use) to support the exchange. A taxpayer who buys a property intending to immediately exchange it may fail the holding purpose test.

### Use A Qualified Intermediary For A Deferred Exchange

In a deferred exchange (the most common structure), the taxpayer cannot receive the cash proceeds from the sale of the relinquished property. The proceeds must be held by a qualified intermediary (QI), an independent party who facilitates the exchange. The QI sells the relinquished property, holds the proceeds, and purchases the replacement property for the taxpayer. If the taxpayer receives the cash (actual or constructive receipt), the exchange fails and the gain is taxed.

Engage a qualified intermediary before the relinquished property closes. The QI must not be the taxpayer's agent, attorney, accountant, or related party (within the preceding two years). The QI holds the proceeds in a secured account. The taxpayer cannot have access to or control over the funds. If the taxpayer receives the cash, even briefly, the exchange fails. The QI structure is essential for a deferred exchange; without it, the cash receipt triggers taxation.

### Identify Replacement Property Within 45 Days

The taxpayer must identify the replacement property in writing within 45 days of the sale of the relinquished property. The 45-day period is strict and cannot be extended, even for weekends or holidays. The identification must be in writing, signed by the taxpayer, and delivered to the QI or another party to the exchange (not the taxpayer or a related party). The identification must unambiguously describe the property (legal description or street address).

Ensure the identification is made within 45 calendar days of the closing of the relinquished property. The identification must follow one of the three permitted rules: the Three-Property Rule (identify up to three properties of any value), the 200% Rule (identify any number of properties whose total value does not exceed 200% of the relinquished property value), or the 95% Rule (identify any number of properties but acquire 95% of the total identified value). The most common is the Three-Property Rule. Missing the 45-day deadline or failing to identify properly collapses the exchange.

### Acquire Replacement Property Within 180 Days

The taxpayer must acquire the replacement property within 180 days of the sale of the relinquished property or by the due date of the taxpayer's tax return for the year of the sale (including extensions), whichever is earlier. The 180-day period is strict and cannot be extended. The replacement property must be substantially the same as the identified property (cannot identify one property and acquire a different one).

Ensure the replacement property is acquired within 180 calendar days. Note that the return due date limit can be earlier than 180 days if the taxpayer does not file an extension; the taxpayer must file an extension to get the full 180 days. The acquisition must be of the identified property. If the taxpayer identifies three properties but acquires only one, the exchange is valid for the one acquired. Failing to acquire within 180 days collapses the exchange for the unacquired property.

### Reinvest All Proceeds And Match Or Exceed The Value And Debt

To defer all the gain, the taxpayer must reinvest all the net proceeds from the relinquished property and acquire replacement property of equal or greater value, with equal or greater debt (or additional cash to offset lower debt). If the taxpayer receives any cash (boot), that portion is taxed. If the replacement property has less debt than the relinquished property and the taxpayer does not add cash, the debt relief is taxed as boot.

Compute the value and debt matching. The replacement property value must be at least the relinquished property value to defer all gain. The replacement property debt must be at least the relinquished property debt (or the taxpayer adds cash equal to the debt reduction) to avoid boot from debt relief. Any cash received by the taxpayer (boot) is taxed. Any debt reduction (the replacement property has less debt than the relinquished property, without offsetting cash) is taxed. To defer all gain, match or exceed both value and debt. Present the boot computation so the taxpayer understands what portion, if any, is taxable.

### Report The Exchange On Form 8824

The exchange is reported on Form 8824 (Like-Kind Exchanges). The form reports the relinquished property, the replacement property, the realized gain, the deferred gain, and the basis of the replacement property. The basis of the replacement property is the cost minus the deferred gain (a carryover basis that preserves the deferred gain for a future sale). The form must be filed for the year of the exchange.

Ensure Form 8824 is filed for the year of the exchange. The form computes the deferred gain and the new basis. The new basis is reduced by the deferred gain, so the replacement property carries the deferred gain forward. When the replacement property is eventually sold (in a taxable transaction), the deferred gain is recognized. Track the basis reduction so the taxpayer understands the deferred gain is not eliminated but postponed. Do not treat the exchange as eliminating the gain; it is deferred.

## Common Traps

### Exchanging Personal Property After The TCJA

After 2017, only real property qualifies for Section 1031. Exchanging equipment, vehicles, or other personal property does not qualify and triggers immediate taxation.

### Failing The Holding Purpose Test

Property held primarily for sale (dealer property, fix-and-flip) or used as a primary residence does not qualify. The holding purpose must be investment or business use.

### The Taxpayer Touching The Cash

In a deferred exchange, the taxpayer cannot receive the proceeds. The QI must hold the funds. Actual or constructive receipt of cash fails the exchange and triggers taxation.

### Missing The 45-Day Identification Deadline

The identification deadline is strict and cannot be extended. Missing it collapses the exchange. The identification must be in writing and properly delivered.

### Missing The 180-Day Acquisition Deadline

The acquisition deadline is strict and may be earlier than 180 days if the return is not extended. Failing to acquire within the deadline collapses the exchange.

### Receiving Boot Without Understanding The Tax

Any cash received or debt relief is taxed as boot. The taxpayer must reinvest all proceeds and match or exceed value and debt to defer all gain.

### Treating The Exchange As Eliminating The Gain

The gain is deferred, not eliminated. The replacement property basis is reduced by the deferred gain. The gain is recognized when the replacement property is sold.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have both the relinquished and replacement properties been confirmed as real property (not personal property) held for investment or productive use in a trade or business?
- [ ] Has the holding purpose been verified and documented (investment or business use, not primarily for sale)?
- [ ] Has a qualified intermediary been engaged to hold the proceeds, with the taxpayer not receiving the cash?
- [ ] Has the replacement property been identified in writing within 45 days of the relinquished property sale, following the Three-Property, 200%, or 95% Rule?
- [ ] Has the replacement property been acquired within 180 days or by the extended return due date, whichever is earlier?
- [ ] Has the boot computation been performed, ensuring all proceeds are reinvested and value and debt are matched or exceeded to defer all gain?
- [ ] Is the exchange reported on Form 8824 with the deferred gain and the carryover basis of the replacement property computed?
- [ ] Is the deferred gain tracked so the taxpayer understands the gain is postponed, not eliminated, and will be recognized on a future taxable sale?
