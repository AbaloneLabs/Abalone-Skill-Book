# Treaty Residency Determination

Under U.S. tax treaties, treaty benefits are available only to residents of a treaty country. When a person is a resident of both the U.S. and another treaty country (a dual resident), the treaty's tie-breaker rules in Article 4 determine the residency. The tie-breaker rules consider the permanent home, the center of vital interests, the habitual abode, and the citizenship. The determination is critical because residency determines which country has the primary right to tax, and an agent who applies the wrong residency will misallocate the taxing rights and misstate the tax.

Agents commonly miss that the tie-breaker rules apply to dual residents, that the rules are sequential (permanent home first, then center of vital interests, then habitual abode, then citizenship), that a U.S. citizen or green card holder can be a treaty resident of another country (which may affect their U.S. tax via the savings clause), and that the determination requires a detailed factual analysis. The harm is a taxpayer who claims the wrong residency and faces denied benefits or double taxation.

This skill covers treaty residency determination under U.S. tax treaties. It is not tax advice; the tie-breaker rules are fact-intensive and depend on the specific treaty, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Dual Residency Conflict

A dual residency conflict arises when a person is a resident of both the U.S. and another treaty country under their respective domestic tax laws. Under U.S. domestic law, a person is a resident if they are a U.S. citizen, a U.S. green card holder, or they meet the substantial presence test. Under the other country's domestic law, the person may also be a resident (based on their rules). When both countries claim the person as a resident, the treaty's tie-breaker rules in Article 4 determine the residency for treaty purposes.

Identify the dual residency conflict. Determine the person's residency under U.S. domestic law (citizen, green card holder, or substantial presence). Determine the person's residency under the other country's domestic law. If both countries claim the person as a resident, a dual residency conflict exists, and the treaty's tie-breaker rules apply. For example, a U.S. citizen living in the U.K. may be a U.S. resident (citizen) and a U.K. resident (under U.K. domestic law), creating a dual residency conflict. The tie-breaker rules determine the treaty residency. Identify the conflict before applying the tie-breaker rules.

### Apply The Permanent Home Test

The first tie-breaker rule is the permanent home test. The person is a resident of the country where they have a permanent home available to them. A permanent home is a place where the person lives habitually and that is available to them at all times (not just a temporary lodging). If the person has a permanent home in only one country, they are a resident of that country. If the person has permanent homes in both countries (or neither), the test is inconclusive, and the next rule applies.

A permanent home is a place where the person lives habitually (a house, an apartment, a long-term rental) and that is available at all times. A hotel room or a short-term rental is not a permanent home. If the person has a permanent home in only one country, they are a resident of that country. If the person has permanent homes in both countries (for example, a house in the U.S. and an apartment in the U.K.), the test is inconclusive, and the next rule (center of vital interests) applies. If the person has no permanent home in either country, the test is inconclusive, and the next rule (habitual abode) applies.

### Apply The Center Of Vital Interests Test

If the permanent home test is inconclusive (the person has permanent homes in both countries or neither), the next tie-breaker rule is the center of vital interests test. The person is a resident of the country with which their personal and economic relations are closer. The center of vital interests considers the person's family and social ties, their political and cultural activities, their business activities, and the location of their assets. The determination is based on the overall facts and circumstances.

Apply the center of vital interests test. Determine where the person's personal and economic relations are closer. Personal relations include family (spouse, children, parents), social ties (friends, community), and cultural and political activities (memberships, affiliations). Economic relations include business activities (where the person works, where their business is located) and the location of assets (bank accounts, investments, real estate). The center of vital interests is the country where the overall ties are closer. For a person with a house in the U.S. and an apartment in the U.K., if their family, job, and bank accounts are in the U.K., the center of vital interests is the U.K., and they are a U.K. resident for treaty purposes. The determination is fact-intensive; document the analysis.

### Apply The Habitual Abode Test

If the center of vital interests test is inconclusive (the ties are equally close in both countries), the next tie-breaker rule is the habitual abode test. The person is a resident of the country where they habitually live. The habitual abode is where the person spends most of their time, considering the frequency and duration of their stays in each country.

Apply the habitual abode test. Determine where the person habitually lives. The habitual abode is where the person spends most of their time, considering the frequency and duration of their stays. Track the days of presence in each country. If the person spends more time in one country (for example, 7 months in the U.K. and 5 months in the U.S.), the habitual abode is the U.K., and they are a U.K. resident. If the time is evenly split, the test is inconclusive, and the next rule (citizenship) applies.

### Apply The Citizenship Test

If the habitual abode test is inconclusive (the time is evenly split), the next tie-breaker rule is the citizenship test. The person is a resident of the country of which they are a citizen. If the person is a citizen of both countries (or neither), the test is inconclusive, and the competent authorities of the two countries must resolve the residency by mutual agreement.

Apply the citizenship test. Determine the person's citizenship. If the person is a citizen of only one country, they are a resident of that country. If the person is a citizen of both countries (or neither), the test is inconclusive, and the competent authorities must resolve the residency by mutual agreement under the treaty's Article 25 (mutual agreement procedure). The citizenship test is the last tie-breaker rule; if it is inconclusive, the residency is unresolved until the competent authorities agree.

### Handle The Interaction With The Savings Clause For U.S. Citizens And Green Card Holders

A U.S. citizen or green card holder is a U.S. resident under U.S. domestic law. If they are also a resident of a treaty country, the tie-breaker rules may determine that they are a treaty resident of the other country. However, the savings clause in most U.S. treaties preserves the U.S. right to tax its citizens and green card holders as if the treaty did not exist. This means the tie-breaker determination may allow the person to claim certain treaty benefits (those not overridden by the savings clause), but the U.S. still taxes them as a citizen or green card holder.

Handle the interaction with the savings clause. A U.S. citizen or green card holder who is a dual resident may be determined to be a treaty resident of another country under the tie-breaker rules. This determination allows the person to claim certain treaty benefits (for example, the treaty residency can be used to claim the foreign earned income exclusion or certain treaty exemptions not overridden by the savings clause). However, the savings clause preserves the U.S. right to tax the person as a citizen or green card holder (the U.S. taxes their worldwide income). The person must still file U.S. tax returns and report worldwide income, but they may use the treaty residency to claim benefits or to resolve double taxation. The interaction is complex; a U.S. citizen or green card holder claiming treaty residency should disclose the position on Form 8833 and consult a tax professional.

## Common Traps

### Overlooking The Dual Residency Conflict

A dual residency conflict arises when both countries claim the person as a resident. Identify the conflict before applying the tie-breaker rules.

### Applying The Tie-Breaker Rules Out Of Order

The tie-breaker rules are sequential: permanent home, center of vital interests, habitual abode, citizenship. Apply them in order; do not skip to a later rule if an earlier rule is conclusive.

### Treating A Temporary Lodging As A Permanent Home

A permanent home requires habitual living and availability at all times. A hotel room or a short-term rental is not a permanent home.

### Failing To Document The Center Of Vital Interests Analysis

The center of vital interests test is fact-intensive. Document the family, social, business, and asset ties to each country to support the determination.

### Ignoring The Savings Clause For U.S. Citizens And Green Card Holders

A U.S. citizen or green card holder who is a treaty resident of another country is still subject to the savings clause. The U.S. taxes them as a citizen or green card holder, regardless of the tie-breaker determination.

### Not Disclosing The Treaty Residency Position On Form 8833

A taxpayer who claims treaty residency under the tie-breaker rules must disclose the position on Form 8833. Failure to disclose triggers a penalty.

### Assuming The Tie-Breaker Rules Are The Same In All Treaties

The tie-breaker rules vary slightly by treaty (some use citizenship, some use nationality, some have different wording). Read the specific treaty's Article 4.
