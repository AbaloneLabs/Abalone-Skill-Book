# Permanent Establishment

Under U.S. tax treaties, a foreign entity is generally taxable on its business profits in the U.S. only if it has a permanent establishment (PE) in the U.S. A PE is a fixed place of business (an office, a factory, a warehouse) or a dependent agent that habitually concludes contracts. Without a PE, the foreign entity's business profits are not taxable in the U.S. (though other income, such as investment income, may be subject to withholding). Determining whether a PE exists is a critical threshold question, and an agent who overlooks the PE analysis will misstate the U.S. tax exposure.

Agents commonly miss that the PE threshold is required for business profits taxation, that a fixed place of business and a dependent agent are alternative PE tests, that certain activities (preparatory, auxiliary) are exempt, and that construction projects and services have time thresholds. The harm is a foreign entity that either fails to file and pay U.S. tax (if a PE exists) or overpays (if no PE exists but the entity files unnecessarily).

This skill covers permanent establishment under U.S. tax treaties. It is not tax advice; PE analysis is fact-intensive and depends on the specific treaty, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The PE As The Taxability Threshold

Under U.S. tax treaties, a foreign entity's business profits are taxable in the U.S. only if the entity has a PE in the U.S. The PE is the threshold for business profits taxation. Without a PE, the business profits are taxable only in the entity's country of residence. The PE concept prevents the U.S. from taxing foreign entities that have only limited or preparatory contact with the U.S. The PE threshold does not apply to other types of income (investment income, which may be subject to withholding regardless of PE).

Understand the PE as the taxability threshold. For business profits, the U.S. can tax a foreign entity only if it has a PE in the U.S. Without a PE, the business profits are not taxable in the U.S. (they are taxable in the entity's country of residence). The PE concept is in the treaty's business profits article. The PE threshold does not apply to investment income (dividends, interest, royalties), which may be subject to withholding regardless of PE. Determine whether the foreign entity has a PE before assessing the U.S. tax on business profits. If no PE, the business profits are not U.S.-taxable. If a PE exists, the business profits attributable to the PE are U.S.-taxable.

### Apply The Fixed Place Of Business Test

A PE includes a fixed place of business through which the entity's business is wholly or partly carried on. A fixed place of business requires a physical location (an office, a factory, a workshop, a warehouse, a mine, an oil well) that is fixed (established for a sufficient duration) and that is at the disposal of the entity. The entity must have the right to use the location for its business. A location that is used only briefly or that is not at the entity's disposal is not a PE.

Apply the fixed place of business test. Determine whether the entity has a physical location in the U.S. that is fixed (established for a sufficient duration, not just a temporary visit) and that is at the entity's disposal (the entity has the right to use it for its business). Common fixed places of business include offices, factories, workshops, warehouses (if used for storage and delivery, which may be exempt), mines, and oil wells. A hotel room used briefly for meetings is not a fixed place of business. A server or hosting arrangement may or may not be a fixed place of business (depending on the treaty and the facts). Track the physical locations and the duration of use.

### Apply The Dependent Agent Test

A PE also includes a dependent agent who habitually concludes contracts in the U.S. on behalf of the entity. A dependent agent is a person (other than an independent agent) who has the authority to conclude contracts in the name of the entity and who habitually exercises that authority. A dependent agent can be an employee or a commission agent. An independent agent (a broker, a general commission agent) acting in the ordinary course of business is not a PE. The distinction between a dependent agent and an independent agent is based on the economic independence of the agent.

Apply the dependent agent test. Determine whether the entity has a person in the U.S. who habitually concludes contracts on its behalf. A dependent agent has the authority to conclude contracts in the entity's name and habitually exercises that authority. This can be an employee (a sales manager who signs contracts) or a commission agent (a dependent sales representative). An independent agent (a broker, a general commission agent) who acts for multiple principals in the ordinary course of business is not a PE. The distinction is based on the economic independence of the agent (does the agent act for multiple principals, bear their own risk, and operate independently?). A dependent agent (who acts solely for the entity, bears no risk, and is economically dependent) creates a PE if they habitually conclude contracts. Track the agents and their authority.

### Handle The Preparatory And Auxiliary Exemptions

Certain activities at a fixed place of business are exempt from PE status if they are preparatory or auxiliary. The exempt activities include: using facilities solely for storage, display, or delivery of goods; maintaining a stock of goods solely for storage, display, or delivery; maintaining a stock of goods solely for processing by another; maintaining a fixed place of business solely for purchasing goods or collecting information; and maintaining a fixed place of business solely for preparatory or auxiliary activities (advertising, research). The exemption applies only if the activity is preparatory or auxiliary to the core business.

Handle the preparatory and auxiliary exemptions. Determine whether the activity at the fixed place of business is preparatory or auxiliary. Exempt activities include storage, display, or delivery of goods (if solely for those purposes), purchasing goods, collecting information, and preparatory or auxiliary activities (advertising, research). The exemption does not apply if the activity is part of the core business (for example, a warehouse that also processes orders and makes sales is not auxiliary). The exemption also does not apply if the overall activity is not preparatory or auxiliary (a sales office that concludes contracts is core, not auxiliary). Analyze the activity to determine if the exemption applies. The exemption is narrowly construed; if the activity contributes significantly to the core business, it is not auxiliary.

### Apply The Construction Project And Service PE Thresholds

A construction project or a drilling rig creates a PE if it lasts more than a specified threshold (typically 12 months for construction projects, though the threshold varies by treaty). Services performed in the U.S. create a PE if they are performed for more than a specified threshold (typically 6 months within any 12-month period, though the threshold varies by treaty). The thresholds are in the treaty and must be tracked carefully.

Apply the construction project and service PE thresholds. For a construction project, a PE is created if the project lasts more than the treaty threshold (typically 12 months). Track the duration of the project, including all phases. For services, a PE is created if the services are performed in the U.S. for more than the treaty threshold (typically 6 months within any 12-month period). Track the days of service performance. The thresholds vary by treaty; read the specific treaty. A project or service that exceeds the threshold creates a PE, and the business profits attributable to the PE are U.S.-taxable. A project or service that does not exceed the threshold does not create a PE. Track the duration carefully.

### Attribute Profits To The PE

If a PE exists, the U.S. taxes the business profits attributable to the PE. The attribution follows the arm's length principle (the authorized OECD approach or the U.S. equivalent): the PE is treated as a separate enterprise, and the profits are computed based on the functions performed, the assets used, and the risks assumed by the PE. The attribution requires a functional and factual analysis of the PE's activities.

Attribute profits to the PE. If a PE exists, compute the business profits attributable to the PE using the arm's length principle. Treat the PE as a separate enterprise, and compute the profits based on the functions performed, the assets used, and the risks assumed by the PE. The attribution requires a detailed analysis of the PE's activities (sales, manufacturing, services) and the allocation of income and expenses to the PE. The profits attributable to the PE are reported on Form 1120-F (for foreign corporations) and are subject to U.S. corporate tax. Track the attribution analysis and the reporting. The profits not attributable to the PE are not U.S.-taxable (they remain taxable in the entity's country of residence).

## Common Traps

### Taxing Business Profits Without A PE

Under the treaty, business profits are taxable in the U.S. only if a PE exists. Without a PE, the profits are not U.S.-taxable. Do not tax business profits without confirming a PE.

### Overlooking The Dependent Agent PE

A dependent agent who habitually concludes contracts creates a PE, even without a fixed place of business. Track the agents and their authority.

### Treating Core Business Activities As Preparatory Or Auxiliary

The preparatory and auxiliary exemption applies only to activities that are preparatory or auxiliary to the core business. A sales office or a warehouse that processes orders is core, not auxiliary.

### Missing The Construction Project Or Service Threshold

Construction projects and services have time thresholds (typically 12 months and 6 months, respectively). Exceeding the threshold creates a PE. Track the duration carefully.

### Failing To Attribute Profits Correctly

If a PE exists, only the profits attributable to the PE are U.S.-taxable. The attribution follows the arm's length principle. Do not tax all of the entity's U.S.-related profits.

### Confusing Independent And Dependent Agents

An independent agent (a broker acting for multiple principals) does not create a PE. A dependent agent (acting solely for the entity) does. Assess the economic independence.

### Assuming The PE Threshold Applies To All Income Types

The PE threshold applies to business profits. Investment income (dividends, interest, royalties) may be subject to withholding regardless of PE. Do not apply the PE threshold to investment income.
