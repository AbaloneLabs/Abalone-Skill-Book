# Installment Versus OIC Versus Currently Not Collectible

The three primary collection alternatives—installment agreement, offer in compromise, and currently-not-collectible status—are often presented as a menu, but they serve fundamentally different financial situations and carry different costs, terms, and consequences. The judgment problem is that each alternative is optimized for a specific financial profile, and choosing the wrong one locks the taxpayer into an unaffordable payment plan, an offer that will be rejected, or a deferral that lets interest and penalties accrue unnecessarily. The correct choice is determined by the taxpayer's ability to pay, the reasonable collection potential, and the collection statute timeline, not by preference or by which option sounds most favorable.

Agents tend to default to the installment agreement because it is the most common and the easiest to obtain, without analyzing whether an offer would settle the debt for less or whether CNC would suspend collection entirely. They may not recognize the differences among guaranteed, streamlined, and partial-pay installment agreements, the long-term cost of an installment that does not fully pay the debt, the future-income component that drives the offer amount, or the fact that CNC is not forgiveness and has ongoing review. The harm is a multi-year payment plan that pays far more than an offer would have, a rejected offer that wasted time and money, or a CNC status that accrues penalties while the taxpayer could have been paying.

This skill covers the comparative analysis of installment agreements, offers in compromise, and currently-not-collectible status, and the financial-condition matching that should drive the choice in U.S. federal collection practice. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the choice among collection alternatives depends on the taxpayer's specific financial condition, the amount owed, and the collection statute, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations. Collection rules and thresholds are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time.

## Core Rules

### Match The Alternative To The Taxpayer's Ability To Pay

The foundational principle is that the alternative must match the financial condition. An installment agreement is appropriate when the taxpayer can pay the full liability (plus accrued interest and penalties) within the collection statute through monthly payments. An offer in compromise is appropriate when the taxpayer cannot pay the full liability but can pay a reduced amount equal to the reasonable collection potential (RCP). Currently-not-collectible status is appropriate when the taxpayer cannot pay anything without economic hardship. These categories are mutually exclusive based on the financial picture, and the correct alternative is the one that fits the taxpayer's actual ability to pay.

Determine the taxpayer's ability to pay before selecting an alternative, using the Collection Information Statement and the Collection Financial Standards. Calculate the monthly disposable income (income minus allowed expenses) and the net realizable equity in assets. If disposable income and asset equity can cover the liability within the collection statute, an installment is appropriate. If they cover a portion but not the full liability, an offer is appropriate. If they cover nothing without hardship, CNC is appropriate. The financial analysis, not the preference, drives the choice.

### Evaluate The Installment Agreement Types And Their Requirements

Installment agreements come in several types with different requirements and consequences. A guaranteed installment agreement is available for individual income tax liabilities under $10,000, with returns filed and no installment in the prior five years, and the IRS must accept it if the tax will be paid within three years. A streamlined installment agreement is available for liabilities up to higher thresholds (commonly $50,000 or $25,000 depending on the type and year), requires minimal financial disclosure, and must pay the liability within a set period (commonly 72 months or the collection statute). A partial-pay installment agreement (PPIA) is used when the taxpayer cannot pay the full liability within the collection statute, and the payments continue until the statute expires, leaving a balance that is then uncollectible. A non-streamlined installment requires full financial disclosure and IRS negotiation.

Select the installment type based on the liability amount and the taxpayer's payment ability. For small liabilities, a guaranteed or streamlined installment avoids extensive financial disclosure. For larger liabilities or where the full amount cannot be paid within the statute, a PPIA may be appropriate, though it requires financial disclosure and the balance remains until the statute expires. Understand that a PPIA, like an offer, results in less than full payment, but through a different mechanism (statute expiration rather than a compromised amount).

### Calculate The Offer In Compromise Viability Using The RCP Formula

An offer in compromise is viable only if the offered amount equals or exceeds the reasonable collection potential. The RCP is the net realizable equity in assets (asset value minus encumbrances and the applicable discount) plus the future income component (monthly disposable income multiplied by 12 for a lump-sum offer or 24 for a periodic-payment offer). If the RCP is less than the liability, an offer at the RCP amount has a strong chance of acceptance; if the RCP equals or exceeds the liability, an offer will be rejected because the IRS expects to collect the full amount.

Calculate the RCP before submitting an offer to determine viability and amount. The calculation requires accurate asset valuations using the IRS conventions, correct treatment of encumbrances, and a realistic monthly disposable income based on allowed expenses. An offer below the RCP wastes the non-refundable payment and the processing time; an offer above the RCP overpays. The RCP calculation is the offer's foundation, and it should be done with precision before submission. If the RCP equals or exceeds the liability, an installment or CNC is the better path, not an offer.

### Understand That Currently-Not-Collectible Is Deferral Not Forgiveness

CNC status suspends active collection when the taxpayer has no ability to pay without economic hardship, but it is not forgiveness. The liability remains on the books, interest and certain penalties continue to accrue, the collection statute continues to run, the IRS reviews the taxpayer's financial condition periodically (often annually), and tax refunds are offset against the debt. CNC is the appropriate alternative when the taxpayer's income is consumed by basic living expenses and there is no asset equity, making any payment a hardship.

Present CNC when hardship is genuine and documented, but be clear with the taxpayer that it is temporary and conditional. CNC is preferable to an unaffordable installment for a hardship taxpayer, because it suspends collection rather than creating a default-prone payment obligation. However, CNC has limitations: the debt grows with interest, the taxpayer must respond to IRS financial reviews, and the CNC can be reversed if the financial condition improves. CNC is a deferral strategy, not a resolution strategy, and it should be paired with a plan for the liability's eventual resolution (often the collection statute expiration).

### Compare The Total Cost And Term Of Each Alternative

The three alternatives have very different total costs and terms. An installment agreement pays the full liability plus interest and penalties over the term, which can be the highest total cost if the term is long. An offer in compromise pays a compromised amount (the RCP) that is less than the full liability, but the process has upfront costs (the non-refundable application payment and the processing fee) and requires compliance during the offer term. CNC pays nothing during the deferral, but the liability accrues interest and penalties, and the total cost depends on whether the statute expires (zero further payment) or the financial condition improves (payments resume).

Build a total-cost comparison for the alternatives available to the taxpayer, accounting for payments, interest accrual, penalties, and the collection statute. An installment that pays the full liability over 72 months may cost more than an offer that compromises the debt, even though the offer requires an upfront payment. A CNC that runs to statute expiration may cost the least in payments but leaves the taxpayer under periodic review. The total-cost comparison, not the monthly payment alone, should inform the choice.

### Factor In The Compliance And Future-Income Conditions

Each alternative has conditions that extend beyond the payment term. An installment agreement requires ongoing filing and payment compliance; default (missing payments or failing to file) terminates the agreement and restarts collection. An offer in compromise requires five years of compliance after acceptance (filing and paying on time); a default during that period reinstates the full liability. CNC requires responding to financial reviews and maintaining the hardship condition. The future-income component of the RCP effectively commits the taxpayer's disposable income for the offer's future-income period.

Evaluate the compliance and future-income conditions against the taxpayer's ability to maintain them. A taxpayer who is likely to default on an installment (due to income instability) may be better served by an offer or CNC. An offer's five-year compliance requirement is a significant commitment that, if broken, reinstates the full liability. CNC's review requirement means the taxpayer must continue to document hardship. The conditions are part of the cost and risk of each alternative, and they should be assessed realistically.

### Consider The Collection Statute Expiration Date In The Choice

The collection statute expiration date (CSED)—generally 10 years from assessment—is a critical factor in the choice. An installment agreement that extends beyond the CSED effectively pays less than the full liability (a PPIA), because the unpaid balance becomes uncollectible at expiration. An offer's RCP future-income component is limited by the CSED, because the IRS can only collect within the remaining statute. CNC allows the statute to run, and if the hardship continues to the CSED, the balance is uncollectible. The CSED affects the total cost and the viability of each alternative.

Identify the CSED early and factor it into the choice. If the CSED is near (for example, two years remaining), a PPIA or CNC may result in significant reduction through statute expiration, making them preferable to an offer that requires an upfront payment. If the CSED is far (for example, nine years remaining), the future-income component of an offer will be large, and an installment may be comparable. The CSED is a structural feature of collection, and ignoring it leads to choices that overpay or that miss the statute-expiration benefit.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To An Installment Without Comparing Total Cost

The symptom is requesting an installment agreement because it is familiar. The trap is that an installment may pay the full liability plus interest over a long term, costing far more than an offer that compromises the debt, and it may be unaffordable, leading to default. The direction is to compare the total cost of all viable alternatives before choosing.

### Submitting An Offer When The RCP Equals Or Exceeds The Liability

The symptom is filing an offer hoping for a reduction when the financial analysis shows the taxpayer can pay in full. The trap is that the offer will be rejected because the RCP meets or exceeds the liability, wasting the non-refundable payment and the processing time. The direction is to calculate the RCP first and to pursue an offer only when it is below the liability.

### Treating CNC As Debt Forgiveness

The symptom is believing CNC means the debt is gone. The trap is that CNC only suspends collection; the liability remains, interest and penalties accrue, the IRS reviews the financial condition periodically, and refunds are offset, so the taxpayer may face a larger debt later. The direction is to present CNC as temporary deferral and to pair it with a plan for the liability's eventual resolution.

### Choosing A PPIA Without Understanding The Statute Interaction

The symptom is entering a partial-pay installment without tracking the CSED. The trap is that the PPIA's benefit (the unpaid balance becoming uncollectible at expiration) depends on the statute running, and if the taxpayer does not understand this, they may overpay or miss the expiration benefit. The direction is to identify the CSED and to structure the PPIA to maximize the statute-expiration reduction.

### Ignoring The Five-Year Compliance Condition After An Offer

The symptom is accepting an offer without planning for the five-year compliance requirement. The trap is that a default during the five years (missing a filing or payment) reinstates the full liability, undoing the compromise and exposing the taxpayer to the original debt plus new accruals. The direction is to assess the taxpayer's ability to maintain five-year compliance and to treat the condition as part of the offer's cost and risk.

### Overlooking The Future-Income Component's Impact On The Offer Amount

The symptom is calculating the offer based only on asset equity. The trap is that the future-income component (disposable income times 12 or 24) often dominates the RCP, and omitting it understates the required offer, leading to rejection. The direction is to include the future-income component in the RCP calculation using the correct multiplier.

### Underestimating The Default Risk Of An Unaffordable Installment

The symptom is agreeing to an installment payment the taxpayer can barely afford. The trap is that income instability or unexpected expenses will cause a default, terminating the agreement and restarting collection, often in a worse position. The direction is to set the installment at a sustainable level or to choose an alternative (offer or CNC) that matches the financial condition.
