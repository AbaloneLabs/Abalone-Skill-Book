# Audit Type And Scope Assessment

An audit notice is the first fork in the road of a controversy, and the single most important early judgment is to correctly classify what kind of examination has begun and how wide it can reach. The judgment problem is that the notice itself often understates the real exposure: a benign-looking correspondence letter can escalate into a full field examination, and a limited-scope review of one schedule can be expanded to other items and other years if the agent's initial findings raise concerns. Misreading the audit type and scope leads to either over-disclosure (volunteering information that expands the audit) or under-response (treating a serious field exam like a simple letter), and both errors are costly.

Agents tend to focus on the item being questioned and miss the procedural signals that reveal the audit's true character. They may not recognize that a Revenue Agent's involvement signals an office or field exam rather than a correspondence exam, that an Information Document Request (IDR) carries different weight than a casual request, or that certain audit types (such as a National Research Program or a Tax Compliance Assurance Process review for large businesses) have distinct scopes and escalation paths. The harm is a response strategy mismatched to the real risk, premature admissions, or a missed opportunity to contain the audit to its original scope.

This skill covers how to classify an audit by type, assess its scope and expansion potential, and read the procedural signals in U.S. federal examination practice (with state analogues noted where relevant). It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; audit classification depends on the specific notice, the taxpayer's history, and the examiner's authority, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations. Audit procedures and thresholds are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Audit Type From The Notice And The Examiner

The first step is to determine which of the four main examination types is underway. A correspondence audit (typically conducted by the IRS Campus/Wage and Income Verification unit) is handled by mail, usually questions a single item such as earned income credit or an unreported Form W-2, and is the least invasive. An office examination is conducted in an IRS office by a Tax Compliance Officer and typically covers individual returns with itemized deductions, small business schedules, or non-complex issues. A field examination is conducted at the taxpayer's place of business or representative's office by a Revenue Agent, and is reserved for business returns, complex individual returns, and higher-stakes matters; it is the most invasive and signals the most serious non-correspondence exposure. For large businesses, a Compliance Assurance Process (CAP) or Large Case examination involves dedicated teams and coordinated issue development.

Read the notice carefully: it identifies the examiner's title (Tax Compliance Officer versus Revenue Agent), the type of return and years under examination, and the issues initially selected. A Revenue Agent's involvement is a strong signal of a field exam and greater exposure, even if the initial letter reads narrowly. Confirm the examiner's authority level, because a Tax Compliance Officer cannot expand the audit the way a Revenue Agent can.

### Determine Whether The Audit Is Limited-Scope Or Expanding

Examinations are initially scoped to specific items, but scope can expand. A limited-scope examination focuses on one or a few issues; an expanded-scope examination covers additional items or years. The examiner may expand scope when initial findings suggest noncompliance elsewhere, when the taxpayer's records raise questions, or when a related-return examination (such as a partner's return during a partnership audit) reveals connected issues. For field exams, the agent typically develops an examination plan that lists the issues; deviations or additions to that plan signal expansion.

Ask explicitly whether the examination is limited to the stated items, and document the agent's representation. If the agent says the audit is limited to, for example, vehicle expenses, treat that as the working boundary but recognize it is not a guarantee. Scope is contained through disciplined responses that answer the question asked without volunteering adjacent information, and through a clear understanding that the agent retains authority to expand when warranted.

### Assess The Expansion Risk From The Items And The Taxpayer's Profile

Certain items and profiles carry inherently higher expansion risk. Cash-intensive businesses, large or unusual deductions, related-party transactions, pass-through entity interests, cryptocurrency activity, foreign accounts (FBAR/FinCEN 114), and prior audit history all increase the likelihood that an initially narrow audit will widen. A return with a Schedule C loss, heavy meal and entertainment deductions, or a large charitable contribution relative to income invites follow-on questions. For business returns, the presence of intercompany transactions, transfer pricing, or shareholder loans signals that a single-issue exam may grow.

Map the return's risk profile before responding. If the audited item sits adjacent to a higher-risk item (for example, an audit of travel expenses on a return that also claims a large home-office deduction), the expansion risk is elevated and the response strategy should be conservative. Expansion risk assessment is not paranoia; it is the basis for deciding how much to disclose, whether to engage representation, and whether to prepare records for items beyond the initial scope.

### Identify Whether A Related Return Or Entity Is Implicated

An examination of one taxpayer frequently implicates related returns. A partnership or S corporation audit affects the partners' or shareholders' individual returns; a married taxpayer's exam may implicate the spouse's separate filings; a trust audit may reach the beneficiary. The BBA centralized partnership audit regime, in particular, changes how partnership adjustments flow through and who bears the tax. State examinations often follow federal changes, and a federal audit will trigger state notices once the federal adjustment is assessed.

Identify all returns and entities connected to the audited return before the examination progresses. Failing to see the related-return dimension leads to a settlement on one return that creates a worse outcome on another (for example, conceding a partnership-level adjustment without considering the partners' individual exposure). Coordinate the response across all affected taxpayers and anticipate the downstream state consequences of any federal adjustment.

### Read The Procedural Signals Beyond The Stated Scope

The procedural posture reveals as much as the stated scope. An IDR (Form 4564) is a formal document request with deadlines and consequences for non-response; a summons (Form 2030 series) signals compulsion and a more serious stage. A request to interview the taxpayer directly, rather than through a representative, suggests the agent is developing facts that may support penalties or referral. A second examiner or a specialist (such as an Engineer or an International Examiner) joining signals complexity and potential expansion. The issuance of a formal examination report or a Revenue Agent's Report (RAR) signals that the agent has reached tentative conclusions.

Track the procedural milestones and what each one means. A summons is not the same as an IDR, and an interview request is not the same as a document request. Each signal should adjust the response posture, the level of representation, and the documentation discipline. Ignoring these signals causes the response to lag the actual severity of the examination.

### Recognize Audit Types That Have Special Rules

Several audit types have special procedural rules that change the strategy. The Tax Compliance Assurance Process (CAP) for large businesses is a real-time, pre-filing review that resolves issues before the return is filed, fundamentally different from a post-filing exam. The National Research Program (NRP) selects returns for statistical study and is unusually thorough. Employment tax examinations (Form 941/940) carry reclassification and trust fund recovery penalty exposure. Partnership audits under the BBA regime have a centralized default that can impose tax at the entity level unless an election out is made. Estate and gift tax examinations focus on valuation, which is a specialized evidentiary field.

Confirm whether the audit falls into a special category, because the standard correspondence/office/field taxonomy does not capture the procedural differences. A CAP review, an NRP exam, and a BBA partnership audit each demand a different playbook, and treating them as ordinary exams produces strategic errors.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Field Exam Like A Correspondence Audit

The symptom is a relaxed, mail-it-in response to what looks like a simple letter. The trap is that a Revenue Agent field examination, even when the initial issue reads narrowly, carries far greater expansion and penalty potential than a campus correspondence audit, and a casual response can concede ground or invite expansion. The direction is to read the examiner's title and the notice's procedural signals, and to calibrate the response to the most serious plausible interpretation.

### Volunteering Adjacent Information That Expands Scope

The symptom is a desire to be helpful by providing complete records beyond what was asked. The trap is that an examination's scope is contained by answering the question asked, and unsolicited records revealing other issues give the agent a basis to expand. The direction is to provide responsive documents tied to the specific items under examination, and to let the agent formally request anything beyond that.

### Assuming The Stated Scope Is Fixed

The symptom is treating the agent's statement that "we are only looking at vehicle expenses" as a guarantee. The trap is that scope is not contractually fixed; the agent retains authority to expand when findings warrant, and a finding of poor records or a suspicious pattern can broaden the exam. The direction is to treat the stated scope as the working boundary while preparing internally for the plausible expansion path.

### Missing The Related-Return Dimension

The symptom is focusing solely on the audited return. The trap is that partnership, S corporation, trust, and married-filing situations implicate related returns, and a settlement or concession on one return can create a worse outcome on another, while federal changes trigger state adjustments. The direction is to map all connected returns and entities before responding, and to coordinate the strategy across them.

### Underestimating Cash-Intensive Or Foreign-Account Exposure

The symptom is treating a cash-intensive business or a return with foreign accounts as a routine exam. The trap is that these profiles carry elevated expansion, penalty (including FBAR civil and potential criminal referral), and specialist-involvement risk that an ordinary response will not address. The direction is to flag cash intensity and foreign accounts as high-expansion-risk and to escalate the representation and documentation posture accordingly.

### Ignoring A Summons As Just Another Request

The symptom is treating a summons like an IDR with a flexible deadline. The trap is that a summons is a compulsory legal process with enforcement consequences, and non-response can lead to enforcement proceedings and adverse inferences. The direction is to recognize a summons as an escalation signal, to meet the deadline, and to consider whether to quash or negotiate its scope.

### Overlooking State Follow-On From Federal Changes

The symptom is resolving a federal audit without considering the state. The trap is that federal adjustments are reported to states and trigger state assessments, interest, and penalties, so a federal-only settlement can leave state exposure unresolved. The direction is to model the state consequences of any federal adjustment and to coordinate the federal and state resolutions.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the audit type been classified (correspondence, office, field, or special program such as CAP, NRP, or BBA partnership) from the notice and the examiner's title?
- [ ] Has the initial scope been identified, and has the examiner's representation about limited scope been documented while recognizing it is not a fixed guarantee?
- [ ] Has the return's expansion-risk profile been assessed, including cash intensity, large or unusual deductions, related-party transactions, cryptocurrency, and foreign accounts?
- [ ] Have all related returns and entities (partners, shareholders, spouses, trusts, and downstream state returns) been mapped before responding?
- [ ] Have the procedural signals (IDR versus summons, interview requests, specialist involvement, RAR issuance) been read and used to calibrate the response posture?
- [ ] Has the audit been checked against special-program rules (BBA centralized partnership, CAP pre-filing, employment tax reclassification, estate/gift valuation)?
- [ ] Has a decision been made about whether to engage qualified representation, calibrated to the most serious plausible interpretation of the audit type?
- [ ] Has the response been scoped to answer the question asked without volunteering adjacent information that could expand the examination?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal examination information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
