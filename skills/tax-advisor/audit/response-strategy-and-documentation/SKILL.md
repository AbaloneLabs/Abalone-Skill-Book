# Response Strategy And Documentation

An audit response is not merely gathering receipts; it is a structured evidentiary and strategic exercise in which every document, deadline, and statement either supports the return's positions or creates new exposure. The judgment problem is that the response simultaneously must substantiate the contested items, must avoid volunteering information that expands the audit, and must create a contemporaneous record that will be evaluated later by Appeals, by counsel, or by a court. A response that is thorough in the wrong way can broaden the examination, while a response that is too thin can convert a defensible position into a sustained adjustment.

Agents frequently default to "give them everything" out of a desire to cooperate, or to "give them as little as possible" out of fear, without a principled framework for what to disclose. They miss that the burden of proof, the contemporaneous-documentation standard, and the privilege implications all shape what should be produced and how. The harm is an audit record that is either incriminating, incomplete, or both, and a missed opportunity to resolve the dispute at the lowest level through disciplined substantiation.

This skill covers how to structure an audit response, organize substantiation, manage deadlines, and build a defensible documentation trail in U.S. federal examination practice. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; response strategy depends on the specific audit, the items at issue, and the taxpayer's exposure, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations. Procedural rules and deadlines are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time.

## Core Rules

### Substantiate Each Contested Item With The Required Evidentiary Standard

Every contested item has a substantiation standard, and meeting it is the core of a successful response. Deductions require records showing the amount, time and place (for travel and meals), business purpose, and business relationship; charitable contributions of $250 or more require a contemporaneous written acknowledgment from the donee; vehicle expenses require either a contemporaneous mileage log or, under limited circumstances, documentary and circumstantial evidence; meals and entertainment require documentation of the business purpose and attendees. The Cohan rule allows estimates when records are incomplete, but it is a fallback that gives the examiner discretion to allow a reduced amount, not a substitute for records.

For each contested item, identify the specific evidentiary standard and assemble records that meet it. A bank statement alone rarely substantiates a business expense; a receipt, invoice, or cancelled check with a business-purpose notation is stronger. For items requiring contemporaneous documentation (mileage logs, $250+ charitable acknowledgments), reconstructing records after the fact is weaker than contemporaneous records and may not satisfy the statutory standard. Build the response around the strongest evidence for each item, and do not rely on the Cohan estimate unless records are genuinely unavailable.

### Answer The Question Asked Without Volunteering Adjacent Information

The discipline of a contained response is to provide documents responsive to the specific request and no more. An IDR asks for specific items; producing a complete set of books when the IDR asks for travel receipts is over-disclosure that invites the agent to examine unrelated areas. The corollary is that each document produced should be reviewed for what else it reveals, because a bank statement substantiating one expense may also reveal an unreported account or transaction.

For each document considered for production, ask whether it is responsive to the request, whether it substantiates the contested item, and whether it reveals information beyond the audit's scope. If a document is responsive but contains non-responsive sensitive content, consider redaction (with disclosure of the redaction) or negotiation of a narrower production. Volunteering information to appear cooperative is a common and avoidable source of audit expansion.

### Manage Deadlines And Extensions Deliberately

Audit deadlines are real, and missing them has consequences, but extensions are routinely granted and are often strategic. An IDR typically sets a response date; a failure to respond can lead to a summons and, ultimately, to the agent proposing the adjustment based on available information. Requesting an extension is normal and expected when more time is needed to gather records, but each extension should be requested before the deadline expires and should be documented in writing.

Track every deadline in a calendar and confirm extension requests in writing (email is usually sufficient). Do not let a deadline pass silently, because a missed deadline shifts leverage to the examiner and can result in an adverse determination based on incomplete information. Conversely, do not request extensions indefinitely; the agent's patience has limits, and a pattern of delay can trigger a summons or a delinquent-return penalty referral.

### Separate Privileged From Non-Privileged Communications

Tax return preparation communications are generally not privileged at the federal level, but a limited federal privilege under IRC section 7525 extends confidentiality to certain tax-advice communications between a taxpayer and a federally authorized tax practitioner, subject to important limits: it does not apply in criminal matters, it does not cover corporate tax shelter promotion, and it is narrower than attorney-client privilege. The broader attorney-client privilege protects communications with an attorney for legal advice, and the work-product doctrine protects materials prepared in anticipation of litigation.

Before producing documents, screen for privileged communications and segregate them. Do not inadvertently waive privilege by mixing legal advice with business records in a production. When a document is responsive but privileged, assert the privilege on a privilege log rather than producing it. The key decision is whether the taxpayer's representative is functioning as a return preparer (no broad privilege), a tax practitioner under section 7525 (limited privilege, no criminal protection), or an attorney (broader privilege); this determines what communications are protected and how the documentation should be structured from the outset.

### Maintain A Contemporaneous Record Of The Examination Itself

The audit process should generate its own defensible record. Keep a log of all contacts with the examiner (date, who, what was discussed), retain copies of all correspondence and submissions, and document the examiner's representations about scope and procedure. If the examiner makes a statement that bounds the audit (for example, "we are not examining year two"), record it contemporaneously. If the agent conducts an interview, prepare a memorandum of the interview while memory is fresh.

This contemporaneous record supports a later Appeals conference or litigation by establishing what was agreed, what was reserved, and how the audit unfolded. It also disciplines the response, because the act of recording forces precision. A dispute that reaches Appeals or court will be evaluated partly on the audit record, and a taxpayer who can show disciplined, documented engagement is in a stronger position than one whose interactions are undocumented.

### Organize Substantiation To Make The Agent's Job Easy And The Adjustment Hard

Examiners are time-constrained, and a well-organized submission that maps each contested item to its substantiation makes it easy to allow the item and hard to propose an adjustment. Provide a summary schedule tying each dollar amount to supporting documents, with tabs or exhibits keyed to the schedule. A disorganized shoebox of receipts invites the agent to disallow items for lack of clear substantiation, even when the underlying evidence exists.

Structure the response as a narrative plus a document map: a cover explanation of each item, a schedule of amounts, and indexed exhibits. Highlight the strongest evidence and address known weaknesses proactively rather than letting the agent discover them. An organized, candid submission that confronts its weaknesses often resolves favorably, while a disorganized or evasive one invites sustained adjustments and penalties.

### Decide Whether To Produce The Taxpayer For Interview

The examiner may request to interview the taxpayer directly. Producing the taxpayer can build credibility and resolve factual questions, but it also exposes the taxpayer to questions that can support penalties or referral, and statements made can be used later. The alternative is to have the representative answer factual questions after consulting the taxpayer, which is slower but controlled.

Weigh the interview request against the audit's risk profile. In a low-stakes, factually simple correspondence audit, a taxpayer interview may be efficient. In a field exam with penalty or expansion potential, a direct interview is riskier, and the representative should manage the factual development, preparing the taxpayer thoroughly if an interview occurs and considering whether to have counsel present. Never send a taxpayer into an interview unprepared, because unguarded answers can be the most damaging evidence in the file.

## Common Traps

### Producing A Complete Set Of Books To Substantiate One Item

The symptom is handing over the general ledger and bank statements to prove travel expenses. The trap is that over-production reveals unrelated issues and gives the agent a roadmap to expand the audit. The direction is to produce the specific records substantiating the requested items, indexed and explained, and to require a formal request for anything broader.

### Relying On Bank Statements Alone For Expense Substantiation

The symptom is producing cancelled checks or bank statements to prove business expenses. The trap is that a payment proves a transfer of funds but not the business purpose, and the substantiation standard requires purpose, time, place, and relationship. The direction is to pair financial records with receipts, invoices, and business-purpose notations that meet the evidentiary standard for each item.

### Treating Reconstructed Records As Equivalent To Contemporaneous Ones

The symptom is recreating a mileage log or charitable acknowledgment after the audit begins. The trap is that statutory contemporaneous-documentation rules (notably the $250 charitable acknowledgment and the mileage log) may not be satisfied by reconstruction, and the agent can disallow the item. The direction is to use contemporaneous records where they exist, to reconstruct only as a fallback, and to understand that reconstruction is weaker evidence.

### Letting Deadlines Pass Without A Written Extension

The symptom is assuming the agent will be flexible about a missed IDR deadline. The trap is that a missed deadline can trigger a summons or an adverse determination based on available information, shifting leverage to the examiner. The direction is to track every deadline, request extensions in writing before they expire, and confirm each extension.

### Inadvertently Waiving Privilege Through Mixed Productions

The symptom is producing a binder that interleaves legal advice with business records. The trap is that commingling can waive privilege over the legal communications and expose strategy to the examiner. The direction is to screen for privileged communications, segregate them, and assert privilege on a log rather than producing them mixed with non-privileged records.

### Sending The Taxpayer Into An Interview Unprepared

The symptom is letting the taxpayer answer the agent's questions directly to "keep it simple." The trap is that unguarded answers can establish facts supporting penalties or referral and become the strongest evidence against the taxpayer. The direction is to prepare the taxpayer thoroughly for any interview, to have representation present, and to consider counsel where penalty or criminal exposure exists.

### Conflating Return-Preparer Communications With Privileged Advice

The symptom is assuming all communications with a CPA are privileged. The trap is that return-preparation communications are generally not privileged, and the section 7525 privilege is limited and does not apply in criminal matters. The direction is to distinguish preparer communications from tax-advice and attorney communications, and to route sensitive matters through counsel where privilege matters.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has each contested item been matched to its specific evidentiary standard (records showing amount, time, place, purpose, and relationship; $250 charitable acknowledgment; mileage log) and substantiated with the strongest available evidence?
- [ ] Has the response been scoped to answer each request without volunteering adjacent information, with each document reviewed for what else it reveals?
- [ ] Have all deadlines been tracked, with extensions requested in writing before they expire and confirmed?
- [ ] Have privileged communications been screened and segregated, with the preparer-versus-practitioner-versus-attorney distinction applied to determine protection?
- [ ] Has a contemporaneous record of the examination been maintained, including a contact log, copies of all submissions, and memoranda of any interviews or examiner representations?
- [ ] Has the substantiation been organized into a summary schedule with indexed exhibits that maps each dollar amount to its supporting evidence?
- [ ] Has a deliberate decision been made about whether to produce the taxpayer for interview, calibrated to penalty and expansion risk, with preparation if an interview occurs?
- [ ] Has the Cohan estimate rule been used only as a fallback where records are genuinely unavailable, with the understanding that it gives the examiner discretion?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal examination information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
