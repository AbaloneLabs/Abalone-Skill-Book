# Capital Gains Tax Rates

Capital gains tax rates in the U.S. federal system are not a single number. They are a layered structure that depends on the asset type, the holding period, the taxpayer's taxable income, and surtaxes that apply on top of the base rate. An agent who quotes "15% capital gains" without checking these layers is likely giving an incomplete and potentially misleading estimate. The difference between a careful rate analysis and a casual one can be thousands of dollars per transaction and can drive poor decisions about when and whether to sell.

Agents commonly miss that the preferential long-term rates (0%, 15%, 20%) are income-tested, that certain assets are excluded from these rates entirely, that the 3.8% net investment income tax stacks on top, and that state taxes may not recognize the federal preferential rates at all. The harm is a taxpayer who sells based on an underestimated rate and faces a much larger bill, or who overpays estimated taxes based on an overestimated rate and ties up cash unnecessarily.

This skill covers the structure and application of U.S. federal capital gains tax rates. It is not tax advice; rates and thresholds change annually, state treatment varies, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Income-Tested Long-Term Rate Structure

Long-term capital gains on most assets are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on the taxpayer's taxable income. These breakpoints are indexed for inflation and change each year, so any rate analysis must use the thresholds for the specific tax year of the sale. The 0% rate applies to taxpayers whose taxable income falls below a threshold, the 15% rate applies through a wide middle band, and the 20% rate applies only to higher-income taxpayers above the top breakpoint.

The critical point is that the rate is determined by taxable income including the gain, not by the gain in isolation. A taxpayer with modest ordinary income who realizes a large gain may have part of that gain taxed at 0%, part at 15%, and potentially part at 20% if the gain is large enough to cross multiple thresholds. Model the gain as stacked on top of ordinary income to see the layered rate. Do not apply a single rate to the entire gain unless the entire gain clearly falls within one bracket.

### Stack The Net Investment Income Tax On Top

The net investment income tax (NIIT) of 3.8% applies to the lesser of net investment income or the excess of modified adjusted gross income over statutory thresholds (for example, 200,000 dollars for single filers and 250,000 dollars for married filing jointly, unindexed for inflation). Capital gains are investment income subject to this tax. The NIIT stacks on top of the capital gains rate, so a taxpayer in the 20% long-term bracket with NIIT pays 23.8% federal on the gain.

The NIIT is not withheld by brokers and is paid through estimated taxes or at filing. Taxpayers who sell appreciated assets often underpay because they budget only the base capital gains rate. Always include the NIIT in the federal cost estimate for taxpayers whose income exceeds the thresholds. Note that the thresholds are based on modified AGI, which includes the gain itself, so realizing a gain can push a taxpayer into NIIT exposure even if their wage income is below the threshold.

### Identify Assets Excluded From Preferential Rates

Several asset categories do not receive the standard 0/15/20% long-term treatment even when held more than one year. Collectibles, including art, rugs, antiques, metals, gems, stamps, coins, and alcoholic beverages, are taxed at a maximum long-term rate of 28%. Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain from depreciation on real property is taxed at a maximum of 25%. Certain small business stock gains under Section 1202 may be partially or fully excluded. Depreciation recapture under Section 1245 is generally taxed as ordinary income regardless of holding period.

When estimating tax on a diversified sale, categorize each asset by its applicable rate class before computing. A blended estimate that assumes all long-term gains are 15% will be wrong for any portfolio containing real estate, collectibles, or recapture property. Identify these assets explicitly and apply the correct rate to each portion.

### Compute The Effective Blended Rate For A Portfolio Sale

For a portfolio with multiple assets, the total federal tax is the sum of each portion taxed at its applicable rate. A sale of 100,000 dollars of standard stock held long-term, 50,000 dollars of collectibles held long-term, and 30,000 dollars of stock held short-term produces three separately rated components. The effective rate is the total tax divided by the total gain, which is useful for planning but must not be presented as the rate on any single asset.

Present both the per-asset rates and the blended effective rate so the taxpayer understands where the tax is concentrated. This is especially important for rebalancing decisions, where selling high-basis assets first may produce a lower immediate tax cost than selling low-basis winners.

### Account For Short-Term Gains At Ordinary Rates

Short-term capital gains, on assets held one year or less, are taxed at the same ordinary income rates as wages and interest, with a top federal rate of 37%. There is no preferential short-term rate. The full ordinary bracket structure applies, and the NIIT may also apply. For active traders, short-term treatment is the norm, and the tax cost of frequent trading can be substantially higher than a buy-and-hold approach, independent of investment performance.

When evaluating a trading strategy or a proposed sale, separate short-term from long-term clearly. A strategy that generates short-term gains requires significantly higher pre-tax returns to match an after-tax outcome dominated by long-term gains. Quantify this drag rather than ignoring it.

### Consider State-Level Capital Gains Treatment

State taxation of capital gains varies widely and can add a substantial layer on top of federal tax. Some states tax capital gains at the same rate as ordinary income with no preference, some offer partial exclusions or deductions, and some have no state income tax at all. A federal rate analysis that ignores state tax understates the total cost of a sale for residents of high-tax states.

Identify the taxpayer's state of residence and any state where the gain may be sourced, and apply the state-specific treatment. Note that states may have their own definitions of long-term, their own rate schedules, and their own treatment of capital loss carryforwards that differ from federal. Do not assume state conformity.

### Separate The Rate Question From The Timing Question

The applicable rate is a function of the facts at the time of sale: asset type, holding period, and taxable income. Tax planning can change these facts (holding longer, harvesting losses, spreading gains across years, donating appreciated stock), but the rate itself is determined mechanically. When advising, separate the descriptive question (what rate applies to this sale as proposed) from the prescriptive question (what could be done to change the rate). Conflating them leads to confused analysis.

For example, stating "the rate is 15%" is descriptive; stating "if you wait two weeks the rate is still 15% but if you harvest a loss you can reduce the taxable gain" is prescriptive. Be clear which mode the analysis is in.

## Common Traps

### Quoting A Single 15% Rate For All Long-Term Gains

The 15% rate is the most commonly cited but applies only to the middle income band and excludes collectibles, recapture property, and other special assets. Quoting 15% without checking income, asset type, and NIIT produces an incomplete estimate.

### Forgetting The NIIT Stacks On Top

The 3.8% net investment income tax is separate from the capital gains rate and applies to higher-income taxpayers. Omitting it understates the federal cost by a meaningful margin and can cause underpayment.

### Applying Ordinary Rates To Long-Term Gains

Some agents default to ordinary income rates for all investment sales, which overstates tax on long-term gains and can lead to over-withholding or unnecessary loss harvesting. Confirm the holding period before applying the rate.

### Ignoring That The Rate Depends On Total Taxable Income

The 0/15/20% breakpoints are based on taxable income including the gain. A large gain can push a taxpayer from the 15% band into the 20% band, or from 0% into 15%. The gain affects its own rate.

### Assuming State Conformity With Federal Rates

Many states do not offer a preferential capital gains rate. A taxpayer who budgets only the federal 15% may face an additional 9% or 13% state tax. Always check the specific state treatment.

### Treating Depreciation Recapture As Standard Long-Term Gain

Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is capped at 25%, and Section 1245 recapture is ordinary income. Real estate and equipment sales require recapture analysis before any preferential rate is applied.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the correct tax year's 0/15/20% breakpoints been used, and has the gain been modeled as stacked on ordinary income to identify any bracket straddling?
- [ ] Has the 3.8% net investment income tax been added to the base rate for taxpayers whose modified AGI exceeds the statutory thresholds?
- [ ] Have collectibles (28% max), unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (25% max), Section 1245 recapture (ordinary), and QSBS exclusions been identified and rated separately from standard long-term gains?
- [ ] Have short-term gains been separated and taxed at ordinary rates rather than lumped with long-term gains?
- [ ] Has the state of residence and any sourcing state been identified, and has the state-specific capital gains treatment been applied?
- [ ] Has a blended effective rate been computed for portfolio sales, with per-asset rates shown separately?
- [ ] Has the descriptive rate analysis (what applies now) been separated from prescriptive planning (what could change the rate)?
- [ ] Are the rate conclusions documented with the tax year, income level, asset types, and applicable rates so they can be verified against current law?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal rate information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific transaction?
