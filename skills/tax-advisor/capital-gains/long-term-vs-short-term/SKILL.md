# Long-Term Versus Short-Term Capital Gains

The single most consequential fact about a capital gain is how long the taxpayer held the asset before selling. A holding period that crosses the one-year threshold can cut the federal tax rate roughly in half compared to ordinary income rates, while a sale one day too early is taxed at full ordinary rates. This binary cliff means that holding-period determination is not a minor clerical step; it is the decision that governs which rate schedule applies and can change the tax on a single transaction by tens of thousands of dollars.

Agents tend to treat holding period as a simple date subtraction and miss the rules that govern the holding-period clock: trade date versus settlement date, the day counting convention, inherited property special rules, gift basis carryover, wash sale restarts, reinvestment plans, stock splits, and merger exchanges. The harm is misclassifying a gain as long-term when it is short-term (understating tax and creating exposure on examination) or advising a client to delay a sale into long-term territory when the actual holding period has not yet been satisfied.

This skill covers the determination of long-term versus short-term capital gains for U.S. federal income tax purposes. It is not tax advice; capital gains rules have many exceptions, state treatment often differs from federal, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The More-Than-One-Year Threshold

A capital gain or loss is long-term if the asset was held more than one year before sale, and short-term if held one year or less. The line is strict and unforgiving. An asset bought on January 2 and sold on January 2 of the following year is held exactly one year, which is short-term. To be long-term, the holding period must exceed one full year, meaning the sale must occur on January 3 or later.

The rate differential is the core reason this matters. Short-term capital gains are taxed at the same ordinary income rates as wages, interest, and business income, which can reach 37% at the top federal bracket. Long-term capital gains for most assets qualify for preferential rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on taxable income, which is a dramatic reduction. For high-income taxpayers, the additional 3.8% net investment income tax may apply to both categories, narrowing but not eliminating the gap. Never present the rate difference as a footnote; quantify it for the specific transaction.

### Count The Holding Period From The Correct Starting Date

The holding period begins the day after the acquisition date, not the acquisition date itself. This off-by-one convention is the most common source of holding-period errors. For a purchase on March 15, day one of the holding period is March 16, and the one-year mark is reached at the close of March 15 of the following year, meaning a sale on March 16 or later qualifies as long-term.

Use the trade date, not the settlement date, for both acquisition and disposition. For most securities, the trade date is the date the binding contract is formed, which controls for tax purposes regardless of when cash or shares actually settle. A common error is using settlement dates from a brokerage statement and concluding a holding period is satisfied when the trade date holding period is not. Confirm whether the broker's reported holding period uses trade dates and flag any that appear to use settlement dates.

### Handle Special Acquisition And Disposition Rules

Several situations reset, extend, or carry over the holding period, and each must be checked before classifying a gain. Inherited property is automatically treated as long-term regardless of actual holding time, meaning a beneficiary who sells inherited stock the day after receiving it reports a long-term gain or loss. Property received as a gift generally carries over the donor's holding period, so the recipient steps into the donor's shoes for holding-period purposes, though basis rules differ for gain versus loss.

Wash sale transactions restart the holding period for the replacement shares, meaning a loss disallowed under wash sale rules causes the replacement position's holding period to begin anew from the replacement purchase date. Reinvested dividends in a mutual fund or DRIP acquire their own holding period separate from the original shares. Stock received through exercise of an incentive stock option may have a holding period that depends on both the exercise date and the special ISO holding rules. Nontaxable mergers and reorganizations generally tack the old holding period onto the new shares, but taxable exchanges do not. Each of these must be verified against the transaction facts.

### Net Gains And Losses In The Correct Order

Capital gains and losses are netted within categories before applying rates. Short-term gains and losses are netted against each other, and long-term gains and losses are netted against each other. If the result is a net gain in both categories, short-term net gain is taxed at ordinary rates and long-term net gain is taxed at preferential rates. If one category nets to a loss, it offsets the other category's gain before rates are applied.

The sequencing matters because it determines how much gain is taxed at each rate. A large short-term gain offset by a large long-term loss results in netting that can eliminate the long-term preferential rate entirely, leaving a net short-term gain taxed at ordinary rates. Conversely, a net long-term gain with net short-term loss preserves the preferential rate on the long-term portion. Do not assume a taxpayer with mostly long-term sales pays only preferential rates; the netting may recharacterize the outcome. An annual capital loss of up to 3,000 dollars can offset ordinary income, with the remainder carried forward indefinitely, preserving its character as short-term or long-term.

### Check Whether The Asset Qualifies For Preferential Rates

Not all long-term gains qualify for the 0/15/20% preferential rates. Collectibles, including art, antiques, coins, precious metals, and certain other tangible collectibles, are taxed at a maximum long-term rate of 28% rather than 20%. Depreciation recapture on real property (unrecaptured Section 1250 gain) is taxed at a maximum of 25%. Qualified small business stock held more than five years may qualify for partial or full exclusion under Section 1202. These carve-outs mean that simply confirming a holding period exceeds one year does not confirm the preferential rate applies; the asset type must also be verified.

When analyzing a portfolio sale, categorize assets by rate class before estimating tax. Mixing collectibles, recapture property, and standard securities into one long-term pool produces an inaccurate tax estimate. Identify the specific rate that applies to each asset and compute the blended effective rate rather than applying a single 15% assumption.

### Consider The Impact Of Taxable Income On The Rate

The 0%, 15%, and 20% long-term rates are tied to taxable income thresholds that change annually with inflation. A taxpayer with low taxable income may qualify for the 0% rate on long-term gains, which creates planning opportunities such as harvesting gains in a low-income year. Conversely, a taxpayer near a threshold may find that realizing a large gain pushes part of the gain into the next bracket. The thresholds apply to overall taxable income, not to the gain alone, so ordinary income and the gain interact to determine the bracket.

Do not quote a single long-term rate without checking where the taxpayer's taxable income, including the gain, falls relative to the breakpoints. A gain that straddles a threshold is taxed partly at one rate and partly at the next. Model the gain as layered onto existing income to see the marginal effect.

### Verify The Characterization Before Year-End Planning

Year-end tax harvesting strategies often hinge on converting short-term gains to long-term by waiting a few extra days. Before advising such a strategy, confirm the exact acquisition date, the trade date convention, any holding-period resets from wash sales or option transactions, and whether the position is subject to special rules. A recommendation to hold until a specific date is only sound if the underlying holding-period math is correct to the day.

Also confirm that waiting does not introduce other risks, such as market exposure, wash sale complications from harvesting a loss and repurchasing, or interaction with estimated tax payment deadlines. The holding-period determination is necessary but not sufficient for sound year-end planning.

## Common Traps

### Using Settlement Date Instead Of Trade Date

Brokerage statements often emphasize settlement dates, but tax law uses the trade date for determining when a sale occurs. Using settlement dates can shift a holding period by a few days and incorrectly classify a gain as long-term when it is short-term. This trap is common with mutual funds and with the T+1 or T+2 settlement cycles.

### Off-By-One In Day Counting

Counting the acquisition date as day one of the holding period, rather than the day after, is the classic off-by-one error. An asset held for exactly 365 days is short-term, not long-term. This single-day error can convert a 20% gain into a 37% gain.

### Assuming All Long-Term Gains Get 15%

Collectibles at 28%, unrecaptured Section 1250 gain at 25%, and qualified small business stock exclusions all deviate from the standard 15% or 20% rate. Treating every long-term gain as 15% produces materially wrong tax estimates for portfolios containing these assets.

### Ignoring Netting Across Categories

A taxpayer may have many long-term sales but also short-term losses that net against the long-term gains, changing the character of the net result. Estimating tax by looking only at the long-term sales in isolation ignores the required netting and overstates the benefit of preferential rates.

### Overlooking Holding-Period Resets

Wash sales, certain option transactions, and short sales can restart a holding period. A position that appears held more than one year may have had its clock reset by a disallowed loss or a constructive sale, making it short-term. Always check for events that restart the holding period.

### Treating Inherited Property As Short-Term

Inherited property is automatically long-term regardless of how quickly the beneficiary sells it. The opposite trap is assuming a gift carries the recipient's own holding period; gifts carry over the donor's holding period, so a recently gifted asset held for years by the donor may already be long-term.

### Forgetting The Net Investment Income Tax Interaction

The 3.8% net investment income tax applies to investment income of higher-income taxpayers regardless of whether the gain is short-term or long-term. Quoting only the capital gains rate understates the total federal cost of a sale for affected taxpayers.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the holding period been counted from the day after acquisition, using trade dates for both purchase and sale, and does it exceed one year for any gain claimed as long-term?
- [ ] Have special acquisition rules been checked, including inherited property (automatic long-term), gifts (carryover holding period), wash sale replacement shares (restart), and nontaxable mergers (tack)?
- [ ] Have short-term and long-term gains and losses been netted within categories and across categories before determining the rate, rather than rated in isolation?
- [ ] Has the asset type been verified to confirm it qualifies for the 0/15/20% preferential rate, with collectibles, recapture property, and QSBS identified separately?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer's taxable income, including the gain, been checked against the annual breakpoints to determine the actual long-term rate or rates that apply?
- [ ] Has the net investment income tax of 3.8% been considered for higher-income taxpayers when estimating total federal tax on the sale?
- [ ] For year-end planning, has the exact date the holding period is satisfied been confirmed, along with any holding-period resets or constructive sale triggers?
- [ ] Has the holding-period conclusion been documented with acquisition date, sale date, day count, and any special rules applied, so it can be verified?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal capital gains information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
