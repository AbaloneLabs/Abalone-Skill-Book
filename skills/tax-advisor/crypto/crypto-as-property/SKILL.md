# Cryptocurrency As Property

For U.S. federal tax purposes, cryptocurrency is treated as property, not as currency. This classification is the foundational rule from which all crypto tax consequences flow, and it means that almost every transaction involving crypto can be a taxable event. Selling crypto for fiat, trading one crypto for another, using crypto to purchase goods or services, and receiving crypto as compensation all trigger gain or loss recognition based on the difference between the crypto's fair market value and its basis. An agent who treats crypto like currency (where only foreign currency gain or loss is recognized on exchange) will dramatically understate the tax consequences of crypto activity.

Agents commonly miss that crypto-to-crypto trades are taxable (not like-kind), that using crypto to buy a coffee creates a taxable gain, that the basis tracking burden is substantial, and that the reporting requirements are strict and evolving. The harm is a taxpayer who has years of unreported crypto transactions, accumulating penalties and interest, or who mischaracterizes the nature of their crypto activity and underreports income.

This skill covers the treatment of cryptocurrency as property under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; crypto tax rules are evolving, reporting requirements are strict, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Treat Crypto As Property For All Tax Purposes

The IRS has consistently held that virtual currency is treated as property for federal tax purposes. General tax principles applicable to property transactions apply to crypto. This means that gain or loss is recognized when the property is sold, exchanged, or disposed of, and the character of the gain or loss (capital or ordinary, long-term or short-term) depends on the holding period and the taxpayer's status as an investor or trader. Crypto is not treated as a foreign currency, so the special foreign currency rules do not apply.

The property classification means that every disposal of crypto is a potential taxable event. A taxpayer who buys Bitcoin, holds it, and does nothing else has no taxable event (no sale or exchange). But the moment they sell, trade, spend, or otherwise dispose of the crypto, gain or loss is computed based on the fair market value received (in fiat, goods, services, or other crypto) versus the basis. Confirm the property classification is applied consistently across all the taxpayer's crypto activity, and do not default to a currency framework.

### Recognize Taxable Events On Sale, Exchange, And Use

A taxable event occurs when crypto is sold for fiat currency, traded for another crypto, used to purchase goods or services, or otherwise disposed of. Each of these triggers gain or loss recognition. The gain or loss is the difference between the fair market value of what was received (measured in U.S. dollars at the time of the transaction) and the adjusted basis of the crypto disposed of. The most commonly overlooked taxable events are crypto-to-crypto trades (trading Bitcoin for Ethereum is taxable, even though no fiat was involved) and purchases of goods or services (buying a computer with Bitcoin triggers gain on the Bitcoin).

Catalog every type of transaction the taxpayer engaged in and identify which are taxable. Sales for fiat, crypto-to-crypto trades, purchases of goods or services, and payments to contractors or vendors in crypto are all taxable events. Receiving crypto as compensation, mining rewards, staking rewards, and airdrops are taxable as income at receipt. Gifting crypto, donating crypto to charity, and transferring crypto between the taxpayer's own wallets are generally not taxable events (though each has specific rules). Do not assume a transaction is non-taxable without confirming against the specific rule.

### Determine Capital Versus Ordinary Character

The character of crypto gain or loss depends on how the crypto was acquired and the taxpayer's holding period. Crypto purchased as an investment and held for more than one year produces long-term capital gain or loss on disposal. Crypto held one year or less produces short-term capital gain or loss, taxed at ordinary rates. Crypto received as compensation, mining income, or staking income is ordinary income at receipt, and the basis is the fair market value at receipt; subsequent disposal produces capital gain or loss based on the holding period from receipt.

Determine the character for each transaction based on the acquisition method and holding period. A miner who receives crypto as income and immediately sells it has ordinary income and little or no capital gain. An investor who buys and holds for two years and then sells has long-term capital gain. Track the acquisition method and holding period for each lot of crypto, because the character affects the rate and the netting. Do not assume all crypto gains are capital or all are ordinary; the character depends on the facts.

### Track Basis At The Lot Level

Crypto basis must be tracked at the lot level, similar to securities. Each acquisition of crypto (purchase, mining reward, staking reward, airdrop, compensation) creates a separate lot with its own basis (the fair market value at acquisition) and its own holding period (from the acquisition date). When the taxpayer disposes of crypto, the specific lots disposed of must be identified to compute gain or loss.

The basis tracking burden for active crypto users can be substantial, because they may have hundreds or thousands of transactions across multiple wallets and exchanges. Specific identification of lots is permitted if the taxpayer can identify which specific units are disposed of, similar to securities. If specific identification is not made, FIFO is the default. Crypto tax software can help aggregate transactions across platforms, but the output must be verified for accuracy, especially for transactions between the taxpayer's own wallets (which are transfers, not taxable events). Do not accept an unverified software output; reconcile it against the wallet and exchange records.

### Apply The Reporting Requirements

Taxpayers must report crypto transactions on their tax returns. Capital gains and losses from crypto are reported on Form 8949 and Schedule D, with each sale or exchange listed separately. Ordinary income from mining, staking, or compensation is reported on the appropriate income form (Schedule C for a business, Schedule 1 for other income). The tax return also includes a question about whether the taxpayer received, sold, exchanged, or otherwise disposed of any digital asset during the year, which must be answered truthfully.

Failing to report crypto transactions, or answering the digital asset question incorrectly, creates examination and penalty exposure. The IRS has increased enforcement of crypto reporting, including matching data from exchanges (which report on Form 1099-DA in some cases) and pursuing unreported activity. Review the taxpayer's crypto activity comprehensively and ensure all taxable events are reported. If prior years have unreported crypto activity, consider the voluntary disclosure or amendment options with a qualified professional.

### Distinguish Investment Use From Business Use

A taxpayer who mines, stakes, or trades crypto as a trade or business may deduct related expenses on Schedule C and may be subject to self-employment tax on the net income. A taxpayer who holds crypto as an investment reports gains and losses on Form 8949 and Schedule D, with related expenses subject to the investment expense rules (currently suspended for miscellaneous itemized deductions). The distinction affects the deduction of expenses and the application of self-employment tax.

Determine whether the crypto activity rises to the level of a trade or business. Mining with significant operations, professional trading with trader tax status, or operating a crypto-related service business may qualify. Casual investing, occasional mining, or hobby-level activity generally does not. The classification should be based on the facts: the time and effort, the expectation of profit, the continuity and regularity, and the businesslike manner. Do not assume all crypto activity is investment; significant operations may be a business with different tax consequences.

### Handle Hard Forks, Airdrops, And New Tokens

Hard forks and airdrops create complex tax questions. When a hard fork occurs and new cryptocurrency is created (such as Bitcoin Cash from Bitcoin), the IRS has taken the position that the new currency is taxable income to the holder if they have dominion and control over it, at the fair market value at that time. Airdrops (distributions of new tokens to existing holders) are similarly taxable as income at receipt if the taxpayer has dominion and control.

Confirm the treatment of any hard fork or airdrop the taxpayer received. The fair market value at the time dominion and control is established is the income amount and the basis. If the taxpayer received new tokens but could not access them (no dominion and control), the income is not recognized until access is established. Track these events carefully, because they are easy to miss and create income that must be reported. The rules continue to evolve, so confirm current guidance for the specific event.

## Common Traps

### Treating Crypto-To-Crypto Trades As Non-Taxable

Trading one crypto for another is a taxable event, even though no fiat was involved. Each trade triggers gain or loss based on the fair market value of the crypto received versus the basis of the crypto given.

### Forgetting That Purchasing Goods Or Services With Crypto Is Taxable

Using crypto to buy a coffee, a computer, or a service triggers gain or loss on the crypto. The transaction is both a disposal of property and a purchase, with the tax consequence on the disposal.

### Assuming Crypto Is Foreign Currency

Crypto is property, not foreign currency. The foreign currency rules (including the limited gain recognition) do not apply. Apply property tax principles.

### Failing To Track Basis Across Multiple Wallets And Exchanges

Active crypto users transact across many platforms. Basis must be tracked at the lot level across all of them. Unverified software outputs or incomplete records produce incorrect gain or loss.

### Overlooking Mining, Staking, And Airdrop Income

Mining and staking rewards and airdrops are taxable as income at receipt, at fair market value. The basis is the fair market value, and later disposal produces capital gain or loss.

### Ignoring The Digital Asset Question On The Return

The tax return asks about digital asset transactions. Answering falsely or failing to report creates penalty exposure. Review the question and answer accurately based on the year's activity.

### Treating All Crypto Activity As Investment

Significant mining, trading, or staking operations may be a trade or business, with Schedule C deductions and self-employment tax. Misclassifying business activity as investment loses deductions or misapplies rules.
