# Small Business Corporate Rates

There is no special reduced federal corporate income tax rate for small businesses. A small C corporation pays the same flat 21% federal rate as a Fortune 500 company, regardless of size. The judgment problem is that owners and agents often assume small businesses get a rate break, either by analogy to individual graduated brackets or to the pre-TCJA graduated corporate schedule (15% on the first $50,000 of income), and they make entity-choice decisions based on a rate that does not exist. The real rate story for small C corporations is not the headline 21% but the double-tax burden on distributed earnings, the PSC 35% trap for service businesses, and the accumulated earnings pressure that punishes retention.

Agents frequently miss that a small C corporation faces double taxation (corporate 21% plus shareholder dividend tax) that often makes it more expensive than an S corporation or LLC for a business that distributes profits, that a service-business C corporation is likely a PSC taxed at 35%, and that retaining earnings to defer the second layer triggers the accumulated earnings tax. The deeper blind spot is treating entity choice as a one-time setup decision when small business dynamics (growing profits, owner distributions, hiring, reinvestment) change which structure is efficient year to year. A small C corporation that was sensible at formation can become tax-inefficient as the business matures and begins distributing.

This skill covers the absence of a federal small-business corporate rate, the PSC trap, double-taxation analysis, accumulated earnings pressure, and state-level preferences. It is not tax advice; entity choice depends on facts that change annually, state rules vary, and a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Confirm There Is No Federal Small Business Corporate Rate Break

Under current federal law, all C corporations pay a flat 21% regardless of taxable income. There is no reduced rate for the first $50,000, no graduated bracket for small corporations, and no size-based preference. The pre-TCJA graduated schedule (15% on the first $50,000, 25% up to $75,000, 34% up to $10 million, with a 5% surtax and 3% phaseout) was repealed effective for tax years beginning after 2017. A small C corporation with $80,000 of taxable income pays 21% ($16,800), not the $13,750 it would have paid under the old 15%/25% brackets.

Do not let clients or prior-era training import the graduated brackets into current analysis. The only federal rate distinction among C corporations is the personal service corporation 35% rate. When a small business owner asks "what rate does my C corp pay," the answer is 21% federal, full stop, unless it is a PSC. Any planning premised on a lower small-business corporate rate is built on a repealed provision.

### Test Personal Service Corporation Status Before Assuming 21%

A small C corporation whose principal activity is performing services in a qualifying field (health, law, engineering, architecture, accounting, actuarial science, performing arts, or consulting) and whose stock is substantially held by employee-service-providers is a personal service corporation taxed at a flat 35%. This is the single most consequential rate distinction for small C corporations. A small accounting firm, medical practice, law firm, or consulting company structured as a C corporation does not get 21%; it gets 35%.

The 35% PSC rate, combined with the shareholder-level dividend tax, means a service-business C corporation that distributes earnings faces a combined federal burden approaching 50% before state tax. This usually makes C corporation status tax-inefficient for service businesses that distribute profits. Before recommending or retaining C corporation status for a service business, confirm PSC status and model the 35% rate. The PSC rules are mechanical: principal activity in a qualifying field plus substantially all stock held by performing employees. If both are met, the 35% applies regardless of income level.

### Model The Double Tax On Distributed Small Business Earnings

A small C corporation that distributes earnings to its owners triggers two layers of federal tax: 21% at the corporate level, and the shareholder-level qualified dividend rate (0%, 15%, or 20%) plus the 3.8% net investment income tax for higher earners. For an owner in the top bracket, the combined federal burden on a dollar distributed as a qualified dividend is approximately 39.8% (21% corporate plus 23.8% shareholder). This exceeds the 37% top individual ordinary rate and exceeds the effective rate of an S corporation distribution, which is taxed only once at the owner level.

For a small business that distributes most of its profit to owners, the C corporation structure is usually more expensive than a pass-through (S corporation or LLC taxed as partnership). The C corporation rate advantage (21% versus 37%) only helps when earnings are retained and reinvested, because the second layer is deferred. The entity-choice analysis must model the distribution pattern: a business that retains earnings for growth may benefit from the 21% rate, while a business that distributes profits usually bears a higher combined rate as a C corporation.

### Evaluate Accumulated Earnings Pressure On Retained Profits

Because the 21% rate is only advantageous when earnings are retained (deferring the dividend tax), small C corporations are incentivized to accumulate cash. But the accumulated earnings tax (Section 531) imposes a 20% penalty tax on earnings retained beyond the reasonable needs of the business, specifically to prevent this deferral. The reasonable accumulation allowance is generally $250,000 ($150,000 for PSCs) unless the corporation can demonstrate a specific business need for more (expansion, working capital, debt retirement, product development, bona fide business investments).

A small C corporation that accumulates $800,000 of cash to avoid distributing and triggering the dividend tax may face the 20% accumulated earnings tax on the excess over the reasonable needs. The penalty is asserted by the IRS, not automatic, but it materially changes the rate math: 21% corporate plus 20% accumulated earnings tax on the retained excess is effectively 41% on those earnings, before the eventual dividend tax. Document the business purpose for retained earnings to defend against the penalty, and recognize that the accumulated earnings tax undermines the rate advantage of retention for closely-held C corporations.

### Consider S Corporation Status To Avoid Double Taxation

For most small businesses that distribute earnings to owners, an S corporation avoids the double tax because income flows through to shareholders and is taxed once at the owner level. An S corporation also offers self-employment tax savings: only reasonable compensation paid as wages is subject to payroll tax, while the pass-through income beyond reasonable compensation is not subject to self-employment tax. This can save thousands of dollars annually for profitable service businesses.

S corporation status has constraints: a maximum of 100 shareholders, one class of stock, U.S. resident individual shareholders (with limited exceptions), and an active election (Form 2553) filed timely. A small C corporation that wants S status must confirm eligibility and file the election; a late election may be possible with reasonable-cause relief. The S corporation analysis must weigh the double-tax avoidance and SE tax savings against the loss of the 21% retention advantage, fringe-benefit differences, and eligibility constraints. There is no universal answer, but for a distributing small business, S status is frequently more efficient.

### Layer State Corporate Taxes And State Small Business Preferences

While there is no federal small-business corporate rate break, some states offer preferences that affect the state layer. States vary widely: some have no corporate income tax (Nevada, South Dakota, Wyoming, Ohio uses a commercial activity tax, Texas uses a franchise tax), some tax at rates from 3% to 9% or higher, and some offer credits, exemptions, or reduced rates for small businesses or specific industries. State apportionment also affects the effective state rate based on where sales, property, and payroll are located.

Do not assume state conformity to federal treatment. A state may not recognize an S corporation election (forcing state-level corporate tax), may impose a franchise or gross receipts tax on top of income tax, or may have its own minimum tax that applies regardless of profitability. A small C corporation or S corporation operating in multiple states must apportion income to each state and apply each state's rules. The state layer can add 3% to 9% or more to the effective rate and can change the entity-choice calculus, especially in high-tax states.

### Revisit Entity Choice As The Business Matures

Entity choice is not a one-time decision. A small C corporation that made sense at formation (when profits were retained for growth) may become inefficient when the business begins distributing. Conversely, a business that initially elected S status may benefit from C corporation status if it begins retaining substantial earnings for reinvestment at the 21% rate, or if it needs to attract outside investors who prefer C corporation stock. Changes in owner compensation, fringe benefits, ownership structure, and profitability all affect which entity is efficient.

Schedule a periodic entity review, particularly when the business crosses thresholds: beginning distributions, adding owners, taking investment, expanding states, or changing the profit-retention pattern. Do not treat the original entity election as permanent. An S corporation can revoke to C status, and a C corporation can elect S status if eligible, but each change has tax consequences (built-in gains tax for C-to-S, for example) that must be modeled before acting.

## Common Traps

### Assuming A Reduced Small Business Corporate Rate Exists

There is no federal reduced rate for small C corporations. The pre-TCJA graduated brackets are repealed. Quoting a 15% rate on the first $50,000 is using a provision that no longer exists.

### Missing The Personal Service Corporation 35% Trap

A service-business C corporation (health, law, accounting, engineering, consulting) is likely a PSC taxed at 35%, not 21%. Failing to test PSC status understates the rate by 14 percentage points and produces a large underpayment.

### Ignoring Double Taxation When Comparing To Pass-Throughs

A small C corporation that distributes earnings bears 21% corporate plus up to 23.8% shareholder, a combined federal burden near 40%. Comparing only the 21% to pass-through rates makes C status look cheaper than it is for distributing businesses.

### Retaining Earnings Without Managing Accumulated Earnings Tax Risk

Retaining cash to defer the dividend tax triggers the 20% accumulated earnings penalty tax on accumulations beyond reasonable business needs (generally $250,000, $150,000 for PSCs). The retention advantage is not free; it carries penalty-tax risk.

### Treating Entity Choice As Permanent

A structure that was efficient at formation may become inefficient as the business matures, distributes, or changes ownership. Failing to revisit entity choice periodically locks the business into a structure that no longer fits.

### Assuming State Conformity To Federal Entity Elections

Some states do not recognize S corporation elections, impose franchise or gross receipts taxes, or have minimum taxes regardless of profitability. Federal entity classification does not settle state obligations.

### Forgetting Small C Corps Face Double Taxation

The most common trap is presenting the 21% corporate rate as the total cost without the shareholder dividend layer. For a distributing small business, the combined burden approaches 40% and usually exceeds pass-through alternatives. The 21% headline is only the first layer; the second layer on dividends must be included in any rate comparison to S corporations or LLCs.
