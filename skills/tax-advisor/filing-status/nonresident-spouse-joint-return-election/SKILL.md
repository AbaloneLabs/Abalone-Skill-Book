# Nonresident Spouse Joint Return Election

A U.S. citizen or resident married to a nonresident alien faces a structural choice that most married couples never encounter: whether to make an election under Section 6013(g) or 6013(h) to treat the nonresident spouse as a U.S. resident for tax purposes, enabling a joint return. This election is not a formality. It converts a spouse who would otherwise be taxed only on U.S.-source income into a resident taxed on worldwide income, and it is irrevocable for the year made and subject to strict revocation rules thereafter. The judgment problem is that the election can produce a lower combined tax rate by unlocking joint brackets and the larger standard deduction, while simultaneously pulling the nonresident spouse's foreign income, foreign accounts, and foreign reporting obligations into the U.S. tax system in ways that can be costly and complex.

Agents tend to evaluate this election by comparing only the federal income tax on U.S.-source income under joint versus separate filing, and they miss the downstream consequences: worldwide income inclusion, the potential loss of foreign income exclusions available only to nonresident aliens, FATCA and FBAR reporting on the nonresident spouse's foreign accounts, the net investment income tax, and the difficulty of revoking the election once made. The harm is a couple who elects jointly, saves a modest amount on U.S. tax, and then faces a much larger compliance burden and tax on foreign income they did not realize would be swept in, or conversely a couple who files separately and forfeits joint-bracket savings because no one ran the worldwide-income comparison.

This skill covers the Section 6013(g) and 6013(h) election to treat a nonresident alien spouse as a resident, the worldwide income and reporting consequences, the interaction with foreign tax credits and treaty positions, and the revocation rules. It is not tax advice; the election depends on immigration status, treaty positions, and the specifics of foreign income and accounts, and a qualified tax professional with international expertise must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether The Spouse Is Actually A Nonresident Alien

The election is available only when one spouse is a U.S. citizen or resident alien and the other is a nonresident alien. The threshold question is therefore the residency status of the non-citizen spouse. Under the substantial presence test, a person is a resident alien if they are physically present in the United States for at least 31 days during the current year and a weighted total of 183 days over the current and prior two years. A person who meets this test is already a resident alien and does not need the election; they can file jointly as a matter of course.

The election matters when the non-citizen spouse does not meet the substantial presence test and is not a green-card holder, making them a nonresident alien for tax purposes. Confirm residency status before considering the election, because applying it to someone who is already a resident is unnecessary, and failing to confirm nonresident status can lead to an invalid election. Also check whether a tax treaty contains a residency tie-breaker clause that could override the default classification, as this affects whether the election is even available.

### Quantify The Worldwide Income Consequence Before Electing

The single most important consequence of the election is that the nonresident spouse becomes a resident for tax purposes and is taxed on worldwide income, not just U.S.-source income. This means foreign wages, foreign rental income, foreign business profits, foreign investment income, and foreign capital gains that would otherwise be outside U.S. taxing jurisdiction become reportable and taxable on the joint return.

Before recommending the election, identify and quantify all of the nonresident spouse's worldwide income. A couple where the nonresident spouse has minimal or no foreign income may benefit cleanly from the election, because the joint brackets and standard deduction apply with little added tax. A couple where the nonresident spouse has substantial foreign income may find that the election increases total tax significantly, even after foreign tax credits, because the U.S. rates on that income may exceed what the foreign country already collected or because the income was not previously taxed at all. Model the worldwide income scenario before concluding the election helps.

### Evaluate The Foreign Tax Credit And Treaty Interaction

When the election brings foreign income onto the U.S. return, foreign taxes paid on that income may be creditable through the foreign tax credit. This can offset much of the double taxation, but the foreign tax credit has limitations: it is calculated on a source-by-source and category-by-category basis, it is subject to an overall limitation, and excess credits carry forward rather than refund. A country with a higher tax rate than the U.S. may generate excess credits that are stranded; a country with a lower rate may leave a residual U.S. tax.

Also assess whether making the election forfeits treaty benefits. A nonresident alien may be entitled to reduced withholding rates on U.S.-source income under a tax treaty. Once the election makes them a resident, some treaty benefits as a nonresident are no longer available, though the individual may then claim treaty benefits as a U.S. resident under a different article. The treaty analysis is complex and country-specific, and getting it wrong can mean losing a beneficial rate or claiming a benefit that does not apply.

### Assess The Reporting And Compliance Burden

The election does not only add tax; it adds reporting. Once the nonresident spouse is treated as a resident, their foreign financial accounts trigger FBAR reporting if the aggregate value exceeds the threshold, and FATCA reporting (Form 8938) if the specified foreign asset thresholds are met. Foreign trusts, foreign corporations, and foreign partnerships in which the spouse has an interest may trigger additional reporting forms. These filings carry steep penalties for noncompliance, often far exceeding any tax owed.

Factor the compliance cost into the decision. A couple with a simple income profile may find the election beneficial, but a couple with the nonresident spouse's foreign accounts, foreign business interests, or foreign trust involvement may face thousands of dollars in additional preparation costs and significant penalty exposure if any reporting is missed. The election should not be made without a full inventory of the reporting obligations it triggers.

### Understand That The Election Is Revocable Only Under Limited Conditions

The election can be revoked in any subsequent year, but once revoked, it generally cannot be made again without IRS consent. This means the election is not a year-by-year toggle that a couple can switch on and off to optimize each year. A couple who elects jointly, finds the worldwide income burden too high, and revokes, may be permanently locked out of the election.

Treat the election as a significant long-term commitment, not a one-year optimization. Model not just the current year but the likely future years, including expected changes in the nonresident spouse's income, residency trajectory toward a green card or citizenship, and any planned moves. If the nonresident spouse is on a path to becoming a resident alien through the substantial presence test or a green card, the election may be a reasonable bridge; if they intend to remain a nonresident indefinitely, the long-term worldwide income cost may outweigh the short-term bracket benefit.

### Compare Against Married Filing Separately As The Default Alternative

If the election is not made, the U.S. citizen or resident spouse files as married filing separately, and the nonresident spouse files a Form 1040-NR or may not need to file at all if their U.S.-source income is below thresholds or fully withheld. Married filing separately has compressed brackets and a halved standard deduction, but it keeps the nonresident spouse's foreign income entirely outside the U.S. return.

The comparison must be honest about both sides. The joint return with the election may lower the effective rate on U.S.-source income but add tax and reporting on worldwide income. The separate filing keeps worldwide income out but applies punitive MFS brackets to the resident spouse. Run both scenarios with the actual income figures, including foreign tax credits, and present the after-tax, after-compliance-cost difference.

## Common Traps

### Electing Without Quantifying Worldwide Income

The election sweeps in the nonresident spouse's worldwide income. Recommending it based only on U.S.-source income, without identifying foreign wages, foreign investments, and foreign business income, produces a result that can be far worse than separate filing.

### Forgetting That Revocation Is Largely Irrevocable

Once the election is revoked, it generally cannot be remade without IRS consent. Treating the election as a reversible annual choice leads to a couple being locked out of joint filing after a single revocation.

### Overlooking FBAR And FATCA Reporting Triggered By The Election

The election makes the nonresident spouse a resident for reporting purposes, triggering FBAR and Form 8938 obligations on their foreign accounts. Missing these filings carries penalties that can dwarf any tax savings from the election.

### Ignoring Treaty Position Changes

A nonresident alien may lose treaty benefits they held as a nonresident, or may gain different benefits as a resident. Failing to analyze the treaty before and after the election can mean forfeiting a reduced withholding rate or claiming a benefit that no longer applies.

### Assuming The Election Is Always Beneficial For Mixed-Status Couples

The joint brackets and larger standard deduction are attractive, but for a nonresident spouse with substantial foreign income, the worldwide inclusion and added compliance can make the election a net loss. Always run the comparison with actual figures.

### Confusing Residency Status Before Applying The Election

The election is for nonresident alien spouses. A spouse who already meets the substantial presence test or holds a green card is already a resident and does not need the election. Confirm nonresident status first.
