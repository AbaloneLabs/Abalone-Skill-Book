# Dependent Qualification

Dependency is not a courtesy label; it is a set of statutory tests that gate the Child Tax Credit, the Credit for Other Dependents, Head of Household status, the Earned Income Tax Credit, dependent-care benefits, and the ability to deduct medical expenses paid for another person. The judgment problem is that the two dependency categories, qualifying child and qualifying relative, share some tests and diverge on others, and the wrong category produces the wrong downstream benefits. A qualifying child can be any age if permanently and totally disabled, while a qualifying relative has a gross-income limit; a qualifying child triggers the Child Tax Credit, while a qualifying relative generally triggers only the $500 Credit for Other Dependents.

Agents frequently apply a single "support" mental model to all dependents and miss that qualifying children require residency and age tests while qualifying relatives require a gross-income test and a relationship-or-member-of-household test. They misapply the tiebreaker rules when divorced or separated parents both claim the same child, they forget that a noncustodial parent can claim a child as a dependent only if the custodial parent releases the claim via Form 8332, and they fail to recognize that supporting a parent in a separate home can qualify the taxpayer for Head of Household under a special rule. The harm is disallowed credits, amended returns, and in cases involving the Earned Income Tax Credit, potential recapture and future-benefit bans.

This skill covers the qualifying child and qualifying relative tests, the tiebreaker rules, custodial and noncustodial parent rules, multiple support agreements, and the dependent-parent Head of Household rule, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; dependency determinations depend on facts that must be verified (residency days, support dollar amounts, relationship), and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Run The Qualifying Child Tests As A Conjunction

A qualifying child must satisfy all of: relationship (child, sibling, step-sibling, or a descendant of any of these; foster children placed by an authorized agency count), age (under 19 at year-end, or under 24 if a full-time student, or any age if permanently and totally disabled), residency (lived with the taxpayer for more than half the year), support (the child did not provide more than half of their own support), and joint return (the child did not file a joint return for the year, or filed only to claim a refund). All five must be met; failing any one disqualifies.

The residency test is the most commonly misapplied. "More than half the year" is counted in nights for children of separated parents, and temporary absences for school, vacation, medical care, or military service count as time lived with the taxpayer. The support test is about the child's contribution to their own support, not the taxpayer's contribution; a college student with a scholarship covering tuition still passes the support test if the scholarship is excluded from support, but a student who works and pays their own rent may fail it. Confirm each test with specific facts rather than assuming the relationship alone is enough.

### Run The Qualifying Relative Tests As A Conjunction

A qualifying relative must satisfy all of: not a qualifying child of anyone, relationship or member of household (lived with the taxpayer all year as a member of the household, or is one of the listed relatives who need not live with the taxpayer), gross income (the person's gross income for the year is under a dollar limit, 2024 approximately $5,050), and support (the taxpayer provided more than half of the person's total support). All four must be met.

The gross-income test is the one that most often surprises agents. A parent with a pension and Social Security that pushes gross income above the limit cannot be a qualifying relative regardless of how much support the taxpayer provides. The relationship-or-member-of-household test distinguishes relatives who need not live with the taxpayer (parent, grandparent, child, sibling, and certain others) from unrelated persons who must live with the taxpayer all year as a member of the household; a friend or partner who lived in the home for 11 months fails the test. The support test for a qualifying relative requires the taxpayer to provide over half of total support, a dollar calculation that must be documented.

### Apply The Tiebreaker Rules When More Than One Person Could Claim

When a child meets the qualifying child tests for more than one taxpayer, the tiebreaker rules determine who claims. If only one is a parent, that parent claims. If both are parents, the child is claimed by the parent with whom the child lived the longest during the year; if residence is equal, the parent with the higher adjusted gross income claims. If neither claimant is a parent, the claimant with the highest AGI claims.

The tiebreaker rules are mandatory, not a choice, but they can be overridden by an agreement only in the custodial-versus-noncustodial parent context (see below). Agents often let the higher-income parent claim by default, but the rule is residency first, then AGI. Document the residency count and the AGI comparison. Note that a child who is a qualifying child of a nonparent (such as a grandparent) cannot be claimed by a noncustodial parent under the release rule; the release rule applies only between parents.

### Handle Custodial And Noncustodial Parent Rules Through Form 8332

For divorced or separated parents, the custodial parent (the parent with whom the child lived the greater number of nights) generally claims the child as a dependent and for the Child Tax Credit and Earned Income Tax Credit. The noncustodial parent can claim the dependency exemption and the Child Tax Credit only if the custodial parent signs Form 8332 (or a substantially similar written declaration) releasing the claim. The release does not transfer the Earned Income Tax Credit, the Head of Household status, the dependent-care credit, or the ability to deduct medical expenses paid for the child; those stay with the custodial parent.

This is a high-frequency error area. A divorce decree saying "the father claims the child in even years" is not by itself sufficient; the custodial parent must sign Form 8332 or a conforming declaration that contains the required specific language. Confirm the form is signed for the tax year, because the release can be for one year, for multiple specified years, or for all future years. If the noncustodial parent claims without a valid release, the claim is disallowed even if the decree allocated the benefit.

### Use Multiple Support Agreements When No One Provides Over Half

When no single person provides more than half of a person's support but a group together does, a multiple support agreement allows one member of the group to claim the person as a qualifying relative, provided that person provided more than 10% of the support, more than half of the person's support came from the group, and each other group member who provided more than 10% signs Form 2120 waiving their claim. The person claimed must otherwise meet the qualifying relative tests.

Multiple support agreements are common for siblings sharing the support of an elderly parent. The claiming sibling must keep the signed Form 2120 forms from the others and must have provided more than 10% themselves. The agreement does not work for a qualifying child (the support test for a qualifying child is different), and it does not transfer Head of Household status unless the claimed person is a parent and the other HoH tests are met. Document the support computation and the waivers.

### Verify The Dependent Parent Head Of Household Rule

A taxpayer can claim Head of Household based on a dependent parent even if the parent does not live with the taxpayer, which is an exception to the general residency requirement. The taxpayer must be able to claim the parent as a dependent (qualifying relative), must pay more than half the cost of maintaining the parent's main home (which can be the parent's own home, a care facility that is the parent's main home, or the taxpayer's home), and the home must be the parent's main home for the year.

This is a narrow but valuable rule that agents miss. Confirm the parent qualifies as a dependent (gross income under the limit, over-half support by the taxpayer), then confirm the cost-of-maintaining-the-home test. The cost of maintaining the home includes rent, mortgage interest, property taxes, insurance, repairs, utilities, and food consumed in the home; it does not include the parent's own medical care, clothing, or education. Compute the home cost separately from the support computation, because they are different tests that can reach different results.

### Distinguish Which Credit Follows Which Dependent

A qualifying child under 17 at year-end triggers the Child Tax Credit (up to $2,000 per child for 2024, subject to phaseout). A qualifying child 17 or older, or a qualifying relative, triggers the $500 Credit for Other Dependents. The Earned Income Tax Credit requires a qualifying child meeting its own slightly different tests. Do not assume every dependent produces the same benefit.

Map each dependent to the credit they generate. A 19-year-old full-time student who is a qualifying child produces the $500 Credit for Other Dependents (not the Child Tax Credit, because of the age-17 cutoff) but may still support the Earned Income Tax Credit. A qualifying relative parent produces only the $500 credit. Present the dependent-to-credit mapping so the taxpayer understands which benefits attach to which person.

## Common Traps

### Using Only The Support Test For All Dependents

Qualifying children require residency, age, and relationship tests; qualifying relatives require a gross-income test. Applying only "did I support them" misses the other mandatory tests.

### Letting The Noncustodial Parent Claim Without Form 8332

A divorce decree allocating the dependency exemption is not enough; the custodial parent must sign Form 8332 or a conforming declaration. Without it, the noncustodial parent's claim fails.

### Forgetting That The Release Does Not Transfer All Benefits

Form 8332 transfers the dependency exemption and the Child Tax Credit, but not the Earned Income Tax Credit, Head of Household status, the dependent-care credit, or medical-expense deductions. Those remain with the custodial parent.

### Missing The Gross-Income Limit For Qualifying Relatives

A qualifying relative's gross income must be under a dollar limit (2024 approximately $5,050). A parent with a pension and Social Security above the limit cannot be claimed regardless of support.

### Misapplying The Tiebreaker As "Higher Income Wins"

The tiebreaker rule is residency first, then AGI for parents. Defaulting to the higher-income parent without counting residency nights is wrong.

### Overlooking The Dependent Parent Head Of Household Exception

A supported parent living in their own home can qualify the taxpayer for Head of Household if the taxpayer pays over half the cost of maintaining that home. Missing this forces an unnecessary Single filing.

### Treating A Scholarships As Support The Child Provided

Certain scholarships are excluded from the support computation. A student whose tuition is covered by a scholarship may still pass the support test, but the computation must be done correctly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] For each claimed qualifying child, have the relationship, age (under 19, or under 24 full-time student, or any age if disabled), residency (more than half the year, counting nights and temporary absences), support (child did not provide over half their own support), and joint return tests all been documented?
- [ ] For each claimed qualifying relative, have the not-a-qualifying-child, relationship-or-member-of-household, gross income (under the 2024 limit of approximately $5,050), and over-half-support tests all been documented?
- [ ] Where more than one person could claim a child, have the tiebreaker rules (parent over nonparent, then longer residency, then higher AGI) been applied rather than a default to higher income?
- [ ] For a noncustodial parent claiming a child, is a signed Form 8332 or conforming declaration on file for the specific tax year?
- [ ] Has it been confirmed that the Form 8332 release does not transfer the Earned Income Tax Credit, Head of Household status, dependent-care credit, or medical-expense deduction to the noncustodial parent?
- [ ] Where no single person provides over half of a relative's support, has a multiple support agreement (Form 2120 waivers from each over-10% contributor) been documented?
- [ ] For Head of Household based on a dependent parent, has the cost-of-maintaining-the-parent's-home test been computed separately from the support test?
- [ ] Has each dependent been mapped to the correct credit (Child Tax Credit for qualifying children under 17, Credit for Other Dependents for others, EITC for qualifying children meeting its tests)?
- [ ] Are the gross-income limit, support computations, and residency counts supported by specific figures rather than estimates?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal dependency information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
