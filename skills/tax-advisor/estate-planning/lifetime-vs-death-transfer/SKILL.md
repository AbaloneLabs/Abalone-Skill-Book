# Lifetime Versus Death Transfer

The choice between transferring wealth during life and transferring it at death is one of the most consequential decisions in estate planning, because the same asset can carry dramatically different tax outcomes depending on when and how it moves. A lifetime gift generally carries over the donor's basis, while an asset passing at death generally receives a basis step-up to fair market value. That single difference can outweigh gift tax savings, exemption use, and annual exclusion math. An agent that treats gifting as automatically good because it "removes appreciation from the estate" is ignoring basis, and one that treats death-time transfer as automatically good because of step-up is ignoring estate tax exposure, creditor protection, and control.

Agents frequently miss this trade-off because they focus on a single variable. They optimize the estate tax side and forget the income tax basis side, or they optimize basis step-up and forget that a taxable estate may still owe estate tax at 40 percent. They also tend to use outdated exemption figures, ignore the scheduled sunset of the higher exemption, and apply annual exclusion logic to assets that are illiquid, hard to value, or carry built-in gain. The harm is real: a poorly timed gift can lock in a low basis, create a taxable gift, trigger a gift tax return filing obligation, and still fail to reduce estate tax because of the three-year clawback rule.

This skill applies before recommending lifetime gifts, death-time transfers, exemption use, or any timing-of-transfer analysis. It is not legal or tax advice. Estate tax thresholds, exemption amounts, and basis rules change over time, and the scheduled 2026 sunset of the elevated exemption makes timing especially sensitive. Always confirm current law and escalate to a qualified tax professional, CPA, or estate tax attorney before finalizing any transfer plan.

## Core Rules

### Separate Estate Tax From Income Tax Basis

The most important rule is that estate tax and income tax basis are two different systems that move in opposite directions. A lifetime gift removes the asset and its future appreciation from the gross estate, which can reduce estate tax, but the donee receives the donor's carryover basis, which can create a large income tax bill on a later sale. An asset passing at death is included in the gross estate, which can increase estate tax, but the beneficiary receives a basis stepped up to the date-of-death fair market value, which can eliminate income tax on all pre-death appreciation.

Quantify both sides before recommending timing. Estimate the estate tax marginal rate, currently 40 percent above the exemption, against the capital gains rate the beneficiary would pay on built-in gain, which for high earners can approach 23.8 percent federal plus state tax. If an asset has low basis and high appreciation, the step-up at death may be worth more than the estate tax savings of an early gift. If an asset has high basis and is expected to appreciate greatly, an early gift may shelter future appreciation from the estate. Do not collapse the two systems into one number.

### Use The Correct Exemption And Annual Exclusion Figures

Transfer tax planning depends on thresholds that change annually and that are scheduled to change dramatically. The lifetime gift and estate tax exemption is indexed for inflation and was scheduled to sunset at the end of 2025 to roughly the pre-2018 level adjusted for inflation, absent legislative action. Using a stale figure can cause an agent to recommend using exemption that does not exist, or to leave exemption unused that will disappear.

Track these moving pieces separately. The annual gift tax exclusion, recently $18,000 per donor per donee for 2024 and $19,000 for 2025, allows gifting without using lifetime exemption and without filing a gift tax return. The lifetime exemption is unified across gifts and the estate, so every dollar of exemption used in life reduces the shelter available at death. Confirm the exact exemption and exclusion for the year of the planned transfer, and confirm whether any sunset has occurred, before recommending exemption use.

### Apply The Three-Year Estate Tax Clawback

A common planning assumption is that a gift removes the asset from the estate immediately. For most gifts this is true, but for certain transfers the gross estate includes the transferred amount if the donor dies within three years. The most important example is gifts of life insurance where the donor retains incidents of ownership, and gifts made shortly before death in some contexts. The three-year rule can pull a transferred life insurance policy back into the estate, defeating the planning intent.

Before treating a transfer as estate-tax-reducing, confirm whether any three-year clawback applies. If the donor is elderly or in poor health, a transfer made within the lookback window may provide no estate tax benefit at all. For life insurance specifically, consider whether the policy should be transferred to an irrevocable life insurance trust more than three years before anticipated death, and document the transfer and the change of ownership carefully.

### Evaluate Carryover Basis Versus Step-Up In Each Direction

When an agent recommends a lifetime gift, the donee takes the donor's adjusted basis and the donor's holding period. This is carryover basis. When an agent recommends holding the asset until death, the beneficiary generally takes a basis equal to the date-of-death fair market value, which is the step-up. The direction of the trade-off depends on the asset's embedded gain relative to estate tax exposure.

Build a small comparison. For an asset with a $100,000 basis and a $1,000,000 value, a lifetime gift gives the donee a $100,000 basis, so a later sale triggers gain on $900,000. At death, the beneficiary takes a $1,000,000 basis and owes no income tax on the appreciation. If the estate is below the exemption, the step-up is essentially free. If the estate is taxable, weigh the estate tax on the $1,000,000 inclusion against the income tax the beneficiary would save. The correct answer depends on rates, the beneficiary's tax bracket, and whether the beneficiary intends to sell or hold.

### Account For Gift Tax Returns And Filing Triggers

A transfer that uses lifetime exemption, or that exceeds the annual exclusion, generally requires a gift tax return on Form 709 even when no gift tax is owed. Agents often treat exemption use as invisible because no cash tax is paid, but the return is a legal filing that starts the statute of limitations for basis adjustments to the donee's basis. A gift tax return that is never filed can leave the donee with an unprovable basis years later.

Confirm whether Form 709 is required for each planned gift. Gifts above the annual exclusion, gifts of future interests, gifts to trusts, gifts of hard-to-value assets, and any gift where the donor wants the donee's basis increased by gift tax paid all typically require the return. Note the annual April 15 deadline and the availability of an automatic extension. Do not tell a client that exemption use has no filing consequence.

### Consider Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax

When wealth moves more than one generation down, the generation-skipping transfer tax, or GST, can apply in addition to gift and estate tax. GST is a flat tax at the highest estate tax rate, currently 40 percent, and has its own exemption that is unified with the estate and gift exemption in amount but tracked separately. A direct gift to a grandchild, or to a trust for grandchildren, can trigger GST if exemption is not allocated.

Before recommending multi-generational transfers, identify the transferees and whether they are skip persons. Allocate GST exemption deliberately on the gift tax return, and understand the difference between direct skips, taxable distributions, and taxable terminations. Failing to allocate GST exemption to a transfer to a dynasty trust can permanently expose the trust assets to GST on later distributions.

### Preserve Control, Access, And Asset Protection

Timing of transfer is not only a tax question. A lifetime gift of a completed transfer means the donor gives up control and access to the asset. A transfer to an irrevocable trust may provide creditor protection but locks the donor out unless a carefully drafted trust retains limited powers. A death-time transfer through a will or revocable trust keeps the donor in full control until death but provides no asset protection during life.

Match the transfer vehicle to the donor's need for control and liquidity. A donor who may need the asset for retirement should not gift it outright, even if the tax math favors gifting. Consider retained interest trusts, such as a grantor retained annuity trust or qualified personal residence trust, which can transfer appreciation while retaining income or use for a term, but which carry the risk that the donor must outlive the term for the transfer to work.

## Common Traps

### Recommending Gifts To Remove Appreciation Without Checking Basis

This is the most common and most expensive trap. The agent calculates estate tax savings from removing a high-growth asset and stops there, never computing the income tax cost of carryover basis. For assets with large embedded gain, the lost step-up can exceed the estate tax saved, especially when the beneficiary is in a high capital gains bracket plus state tax.

### Using Stale Or Soon-To-Sunset Exemption Figures

The exemption is large but temporary. An agent who quotes last year's figure, or who assumes the elevated exemption continues indefinitely, may recommend using exemption that no longer exists or may miss the urgency of using exemption before a sunset. Always confirm the figure for the exact transfer year and the sunset status.

### Treating Annual Exclusion Gifts As Automatically Risk-Free

Annual exclusion gifts are powerful, but they fail when the gift is of a future interest, when it is made to a trust without Crummey withdrawal rights, or when the donee is a trust rather than an individual. A gift that does not qualify as a present interest does not use the annual exclusion and instead consumes lifetime exemption.

### Ignoring The Three-Year Clawback On Life Insurance

Transferring an insurance policy within three years of death pulls the death benefit back into the gross estate. An agent who recommends a last-minute policy transfer to remove it from the estate may achieve nothing and may have created administrative complexity. The transfer must occur more than three years before death, or the policy should be acquired by an irrevocable trust from the start.

### Forgetting Form 709 Filing And Adequate Disclosure

Exemption use without a filed Form 709 leaves the donee's basis adjustment unprovable and can start no statute of limitations on valuation. This trap is invisible until the donee sells the asset years later and cannot establish basis. Adequate disclosure on the return is what starts the limitations clock for valuation challenges.

### Applying Gift Logic To Illiquid Or Hard-To-Value Assets

Gifting interests in closely held businesses, real estate, or family limited partnerships requires valuation discounts and exposes the donor to valuation scrutiny on audit. The math that works for marketable securities does not transfer cleanly to illiquid assets, and a bad valuation can create both gift tax exposure and basis problems.

### Assuming Death-Time Transfer Always Wins Because Of Step-Up

Step-up is valuable, but a taxable estate pays 40 percent on the inclusion, and the step-up only helps if the beneficiary actually sells. If the beneficiary holds the asset indefinitely, the step-up provides no current benefit while the estate tax is due in cash within nine months of death. Weigh liquidity, not just basis.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have estate tax exposure and income tax basis been modeled as two separate systems rather than collapsed into a single number?
- [ ] Is the correct lifetime exemption and annual exclusion figure confirmed for the exact year of the planned transfer, including any sunset status?
- [ ] Has the three-year clawback been checked for any life insurance transfer or pre-death gift?
- [ ] Has carryover basis versus step-up been compared quantitatively for each asset, using the beneficiary's likely capital gains rate and intent to sell or hold?
- [ ] Has Form 709 filing been identified for any gift exceeding the annual exclusion or using lifetime exemption, with the adequate-disclosure requirement noted?
- [ ] Has generation-skipping transfer tax exposure and exemption allocation been considered for any multi-generational transfer?
- [ ] Has the donor's need for control, access, liquidity, and asset protection been weighed against the tax benefit of a completed lifetime transfer?
- [ ] Have illiquid or hard-to-value assets been flagged for valuation discount and audit risk rather than treated like marketable securities?
- [ ] Has the agent avoided recommending a transfer based on a single variable such as estate tax savings or step-up alone?
- [ ] Has the agent confirmed that this analysis is not tax advice and that final timing and structure must be reviewed with a qualified tax professional, CPA, or estate tax attorney?
