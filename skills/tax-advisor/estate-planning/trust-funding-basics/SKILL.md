# Trust Funding Basics

A trust is only as effective as the assets that have been legally transferred into it. An unfunded trust is a empty container, and the most common failure in estate planning is not a drafting error in the trust document but the client's failure to retitle assets, update beneficiary designations, and complete the funding steps after signing. An agent who reviews an estate plan by reading only the trust document is looking at the wrong artifact. The operative question is whether the trust actually owns or controls the assets it is supposed to govern, and that is determined by account titles, deeds, and beneficiary designation forms held by third parties.

Agents miss trust funding failures because the trust document reads as if everything is handled. The trust says it governs the residence, the brokerage account, and the life insurance, but unless the deed was recorded, the brokerage account was retitled, and the beneficiary form was updated, none of that is true. The harm is severe: assets meant to avoid probate go through probate, assets meant for a trust beneficiary pass by operation of law to the wrong person, and the entire estate plan can be defeated by a single stale beneficiary designation on a retirement account or life insurance policy.

This skill applies before declaring an estate plan complete, before reviewing whether a trust is properly funded, and before advising on retitling, beneficiary designations, or pour-over wills. It is not legal or tax advice. Funding a trust can trigger transfer taxes, reassessment of property, lender issues, and changes to creditor protection, and retirement account rules are especially restrictive. Always confirm current procedures with the institution and escalate to a qualified estate planning attorney or tax professional before finalizing any retitling or beneficiary change.

## Core Rules

### Verify Funding By Title And Designation, Not By Trust Language

The trust document describes intent, but title and beneficiary designations determine control. An asset is in a revocable trust only if the account is retitled to the trust or the trustee, or if the deed names the trust as grantee. A life insurance policy or retirement account reaches a trust only if the trust is named as beneficiary on the institution's form. Reading the trust and concluding the assets are funded is the core error.

For each asset the trust is meant to govern, confirm the controlling document. For real estate, obtain a recorded deed transferring title to the trust. For bank and brokerage accounts, obtain written confirmation from the institution that the account is titled in the name of the trust or trustee. For life insurance and annuities, obtain a copy of the current beneficiary designation form. For retirement accounts, confirm whether a trust can even be named as beneficiary and what form governs. Do not rely on the client's belief that funding was done years ago.

### Distinguish Probate Assets From Non-Probate Assets

Funding strategy depends on whether an asset passes through probate or by operation of law. Probate assets, such as individually titled real estate and personal property, pass under the will and require court involvement if not retitled to the trust. Non-probate assets, such as life insurance, retirement accounts, and payable-on-death accounts, pass by beneficiary designation regardless of what the will or trust says.

Map every asset into one of three categories: assets titled in the trust, assets passing by beneficiary designation, and assets that remain individually titled and therefore probate assets. The goal of funding is to move individually titled probate assets into the trust and to align beneficiary designations with the trust where intended. An asset that passes by beneficiary designation to a named person cannot be redirected by the trust unless the trust is the beneficiary, so a will or trust provision cannot override a stale beneficiary form.

### Update Beneficiary Designations Consistently With The Plan

Beneficiary designations are the most overlooked funding step and the most common source of misdirected assets. Life insurance, retirement accounts, annuities, and payable-on-death accounts pass to whoever is named on the form, even if that name is an ex-spouse from decades ago. A trust-based plan often intends for these assets to pour into the trust, but that only happens if the trust is named as beneficiary on the current form.

Review every beneficiary designation for consistency with the overall plan. Decide deliberately whether retirement accounts should name the trust, name individuals outright, or name individuals as primary with the trust as contingent. Naming a trust as beneficiary of a retirement account triggers complex required minimum distribution rules and can accelerate taxation, so this decision needs specific analysis, not a default. Confirm both primary and contingent beneficiaries, and confirm the forms are the ones currently on file with the institution, not drafts.

### Use A Pour-Over Will As A Safety Net, Not A Funding Plan

A pour-over will directs that any probate asset not titled in the trust at death be poured into the trust. It is a backstop for assets that were missed, acquired after funding, or never retitled. It is not a substitute for funding, because assets that pass through the pour-over will still go through probate, which defeats a primary purpose of a revocable trust.

Confirm that a pour-over will exists as a safety net, but do not let its presence substitute for actual funding. Identify any asset that would rely on the pour-over will and treat it as an unfunded asset that needs retitling. The pour-over will also requires probate court involvement, filing fees, and possible delays, so an estate that depends heavily on it has lost much of the benefit of the trust.

### Handle Real Estate Funding Through Properly Recorded Deeds

Real estate is funded into a trust by executing and recording a deed transferring title to the trustee. This is not automatic and not accomplished by mentioning the property in the trust. Each parcel requires its own deed, proper legal description, notarization, and recording in the county where the property is located. Out-of-state property requires a deed recorded in that state, or the property will face ancillary probate.

Before declaring real estate funded, confirm the recorded deed for each parcel. Watch for title insurance implications, as transferring property to a trust may affect coverage and may require an endorsement. In some states, transferring real estate can trigger property tax reassessment, transfer taxes, or due-on-sale clause concerns, though federal protections under the Garn-St. Germain Act generally prevent acceleration for transfers to a revocable trust. Confirm state-specific consequences before recording.

### Account For Retirement Account Restrictions

Retirement accounts, including IRAs, 401(k)s, and other qualified plans, cannot simply be retitled into a trust during life. They are funded into a trust only by beneficiary designation at death, and the rules for a trust beneficiary are strict and unforgiving. The trust must generally be valid under state law, be irrevocable or become irrevocable at death, and have identifiable individual beneficiaries, to qualify as a see-through trust for required minimum distribution purposes.

Do not assume a trust can be named as retirement beneficiary without analysis. If the trust qualifies as a see-through trust, the required minimum distribution can be stretched over the oldest individual beneficiary's life expectancy under the rules applicable after the SECURE Act, generally a 10-year rule for most non-spouse beneficiaries. If the trust does not qualify, the entire retirement account may have to be distributed quickly, accelerating income tax. This is a specialized analysis that requires the plan documents and trust terms.

### Track Funding As An Ongoing Process, Not A One-Time Event

Funding is not complete when the trust is signed. New assets acquired later, accounts opened subsequently, and beneficiary designations changed by institutions all create ongoing funding risk. A client who funds the trust perfectly at signing but then opens a new brokerage account in individual name ten years later has created an unfunded probate asset.

Establish a process for ongoing funding. When new accounts are opened, title them in the trust from the start. When new real estate is acquired, deed it to the trust. Review beneficiary designations periodically, especially after major life events such as marriage, divorce, birth, or death. Treat funding as a living checklist that is revisited at each life change and at each plan review, not as a box that was checked once.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Signed Trust Document As Proof Of Funding

The trust document proves the trust exists and what it says, but it proves nothing about which assets it controls. Agents and clients both make this error, concluding the plan is complete because the trust is signed. Until titles and designations are confirmed, the trust may govern nothing.

### Leaving Beneficiary Designations Stale Or Inconsistent

A beneficiary form naming an ex-spouse or a deceased parent overrides the entire trust and will. This trap is invisible until death, when the asset passes by operation of law to the named person regardless of the estate plan. Stale designations are the single most common cause of assets passing to the wrong recipient.

### Assuming A Pour-Over Will Avoids Probate

The pour-over will ensures missed assets reach the trust eventually, but it routes them through probate first. An agent who relies on the pour-over will as the funding mechanism has conceded the probate avoidance benefit of the trust and accepted court costs and delays.

### Naming A Trust As Retirement Beneficiary Without See-Through Analysis

Naming a trust as beneficiary of an IRA or 401(k) without confirming it qualifies as a see-through trust can force rapid distribution of the entire account, creating a large income tax bill and destroying the tax deferral. This is a high-cost error made by treating retirement accounts like other assets.

### Ignoring Out-Of-State Real Estate And Ancillary Probate

Property in another state that is not deeded to the trust will require ancillary probate in that state, duplicating court costs and delays. Funding must cover every parcel in every state, with a deed recorded in each relevant county.

### Overlooking Transfer Tax And Reassessment On Real Estate

While transfers to a revocable trust are often exempt from transfer tax and reassessment, this is state-specific and not universal. Assuming no consequence can lead to unexpected property tax increases or transfer tax bills at recording.

### Forgetting That Funding Is Ongoing

A plan funded perfectly at signing decays as new assets accumulate in individual name. Agents who treat funding as a one-time event miss the assets acquired later that fall outside the trust and into probate.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has each asset the trust is meant to govern been confirmed by recorded deed, account title, or beneficiary designation form rather than by trust language alone?
- [ ] Has every asset been categorized as trust-titled, beneficiary-designated, or individually titled probate asset?
- [ ] Have all beneficiary designations on life insurance, retirement accounts, annuities, and payable-on-death accounts been reviewed for consistency with the plan, including primary and contingent beneficiaries?
- [ ] Has a pour-over will been confirmed as a safety net without being treated as the primary funding mechanism?
- [ ] Has each real estate parcel been confirmed with a recorded deed, including out-of-state property that requires ancillary recording?
- [ ] Has any plan to name a trust as retirement account beneficiary been analyzed for see-through trust qualification and required minimum distribution consequences?
- [ ] Has an ongoing funding process been established for new assets acquired after the initial funding?
- [ ] Have state-specific transfer tax, property tax reassessment, and title insurance implications of real estate transfers been checked?
- [ ] Has the agent avoided declaring the estate plan complete based on the signed trust document alone?
- [ ] Has the agent confirmed that this analysis is not legal or tax advice and that retitling and beneficiary changes must be reviewed with a qualified estate planning attorney or tax professional?
