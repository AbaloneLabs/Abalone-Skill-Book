# NIIT The 3.8 Percent Net Investment Income Tax

The Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) under IRC Section 1411 imposes an additional 3.8% tax on the lesser of a taxpayer's net investment income or the amount by which their modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) exceeds statutory thresholds. The thresholds are fixed by statute and not indexed for inflation: $200,000 for single filers, $250,000 for married filing jointly, $125,000 for married filing separately, and $250,000 for a qualifying surviving spouse or head of household. Because the dollar thresholds are frozen, more taxpayers cross them each year through nominal income growth, making this a tax that silently reaches deeper into the middle and upper-middle class over time.

The judgment problem is recognizing when NIIT is in play, computing it correctly on the lesser-of basis, and not confusing it with the ordinary long-term capital gains rate structure. Agents frequently apply 3.8% to all investment income when it only applies to the portion of investment income that sits above the MAGI threshold, or conversely assume the threshold protects income it does not. The harm is either overstating tax due (causing unnecessary panic and over-withholding) or understating it (triggering an underpayment penalty and an unexpected bill). The most common silent failure is treating wages and self-employment income as subject to NIIT when they are generally excluded, while missing that passive activity income, rental income, and certain trust distributions are included.

This skill applies to NIIT analysis, Section 1411 surtax computation, MAGI threshold testing, and investment income tax planning for individuals, estates, and trusts. It is not tax advice; NIIT rules have detailed exceptions for active traders, real estate professionals, qualified retirement plan distributions, and nonresident aliens, and outcomes depend on facts that must be verified. Consult a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) before relying on any conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Apply The 3.8% To The Lesser Of NII Or Excess MAGI

The NIIT formula is not 3.8% of all investment income. It is 3.8% of the lesser of (a) net investment income, or (b) the excess of MAGI over the filing-status threshold. For a single filer with $150,000 of net investment income and MAGI of $230,000, the excess over the $200,000 threshold is only $30,000, so NIIT applies to $30,000, not $150,000. The tax is $1,140, not $5,700.

Always compute both quantities and take the smaller. This lesser-of mechanic is the single most misunderstood element of NIIT. A taxpayer with very high investment income but MAGI only slightly above the threshold owes little NIIT, while a taxpayer with modest investment income and very high MAGI owes NIIT on all of their investment income. The interaction means the effective exposure depends on the shape of both income and MAGI, not on either alone.

### Use The Correct MAGI Threshold By Filing Status

The thresholds are statutory and fixed: $200,000 single, $250,000 married filing jointly, $125,000 married filing separately, and $250,000 qualifying surviving spouse or head of household. These amounts have not changed since the tax took effect in 2013 and are not adjusted for inflation. Confirm the filing status before testing the threshold, because a couple near $250,000 of joint MAGI could face very different exposure if they file separately.

Note that the head-of-household threshold is $200,000, not $250,000. The $250,000 figure applies to qualifying surviving spouse. Misapplying the head-of-household threshold as $250,000 understates NIIT by applying the surtax to $50,000 too little income. Always restate the threshold from the statute for the specific filing status in the analysis, and flag that these are frozen amounts that will capture more income over time.

### Identify What Counts As Net Investment Income

Net investment income (NII) generally includes interest, dividends, capital gains (both short and long term), rental and royalty income, non-qualified annuities, passive activity income, and income from trading financial instruments or commodities. It is reduced by properly allocable deductions, such as investment interest expense, investment advisory and brokerage fees, and state and local taxes allocable to investment income (subject to the SALT cap). Gross investment income is not the base; deductions matter.

Excluded from NII are wages, self-employment income, distributions from qualified retirement plans (401(k), 403(b), IRA, pension), Social Security benefits, tax-exempt interest, and active trade or business income for a material participant. The exclusions are the trap source: agents often add back IRA distributions or Social Security to NII when they do not belong there. Conversely, rental income from a passive real estate activity is included unless the taxpayer qualifies as a real estate professional with material participation.

### Compute MAGI Correctly With The Section 1411 Adjustments

MAGI for NIIT purposes starts with adjusted gross income (AGI) and then adds back certain items, most notably the foreign earned income exclusion under IRC Section 911. Unlike MAGI for other purposes (such as the Roth IRA contribution limits), the NIIT MAGI generally does not add back student loan interest, tuition deductions, or IRA contribution deductions, because those have already reduced AGI and Section 1411 does not require adding them back. The key add-back is foreign earned income.

Do not conflate NIIT MAGI with the MAGI used for Affordable Care Act premium tax credits or Roth IRA limits. Each MAGI has its own definition. Using the wrong MAGI definition produces an incorrect threshold test. State the MAGI definition explicitly as the Section 1411 version (AGI plus the foreign earned income exclusion, less certain related deductions) before comparing to the threshold.

### Distinguish NIIT From The Ordinary Capital Gains Rate

NIIT is a separate surtax layered on top of the regular income tax. A long-term capital gain taxed at the 15% or 20% preferential rate is also subject to the additional 3.8% NIIT if it falls in the NII base, bringing the top effective rate on long-term gains to 23.8% (20% + 3.8%) for high earners. The 0% long-term capital gains bracket does not eliminate NIIT; if the taxpayer is over the MAGI threshold, the 3.8% still applies even though the base capital gains rate is 0%.

Clarify the stacking effect when discussing effective rates. A taxpayer in the 0% long-term capital gains bracket but above the $200,000 MAGI threshold still owes 3.8% on those gains. Conversely, a taxpayer below the threshold owes no NIIT even on large gains. The interaction between the capital gains rate structure and NIIT is not additive in an intuitive way; compute both layers separately and then combine.

### Recognize The Special Rules For Estates And Trusts

Estates and trusts are subject to NIIT, but at a much lower threshold that is adjusted for inflation annually (approximately $14,000 to $15,000 of undistributed net investment income in recent years). This means a trust can owe NIIT on a small amount of income that an individual would not. However, income that is distributed to beneficiaries is generally taxed to the beneficiary, not the trust, and the trust's distribution deduction can shift the NII out of the trust.

For trust planning, the distribution timing matters: distributing income before year-end can move it out of the trust's NIIT base and into the beneficiary's, where the much higher individual threshold may apply. But this must be weighed against the beneficiary's own MAGI and whether the distribution pushes them over their threshold. Trust NIIT is a distinct analysis from individual NIIT and should not be treated as a simple extension of the individual rules.

### Evaluate Strategies To Manage NIIT Exposure

Because NIIT depends on the lesser of NII and excess MAGI, strategies fall into two categories: reducing NII and reducing MAGI. Reducing NII includes harvesting capital losses to offset gains, holding tax-exempt bonds, and maximizing deductible investment expenses. Reducing MAGI includes deferring income, increasing above-the-line deductions, contributing to qualified retirement plans and HSAs, and in some cases accelerating deductions.

The most effective lever is often timing: deferring a large capital gain from a year where the taxpayer is well over the threshold to a year where MAGI is lower can eliminate or reduce NIIT. Charitable contributions of appreciated stock can reduce both the gain in NII and MAGI simultaneously. Each strategy has tradeoffs (deferral changes market exposure; charitable giving reduces wealth) and should be modeled on the actual lesser-of computation, not applied generically.

### Document The Lesser-Of Computation And Source Figures

Because NIIT hinges on two computed quantities (NII and excess MAGI), the analysis should show both numbers explicitly, the threshold used, and the lesser amount that is taxed at 3.8%. A conclusion that simply states "NIIT is $X" without showing the lesser-of math is not verifiable and is prone to error. Show the filing status, the threshold, MAGI, the excess, NII, and the lesser of the two.

Retain the source documents: brokerage statements for gains and dividends, Schedule E for rental and passive income, Form 8606 for any basis items, and the prior-year return for MAGI benchmarking. NIIT is reported on Form 8959, which ties to the Form 1040. The computation should be reproducible from the source figures so that a reviewer or preparer can verify each input.

## Common Traps

### Applying 3.8% To All Investment Income Regardless Of MAGI

The trap is multiplying total investment income by 3.8% without applying the lesser-of rule. This overstates the tax for anyone whose MAGI is only modestly above the threshold. Always compute the excess of MAGI over the threshold and take the lesser.

### Using The Wrong MAGI Definition

NIIT MAGI differs from Roth MAGI and ACA MAGI. The trap is borrowing a MAGI figure computed for another purpose and testing it against the NIIT threshold. State that the MAGI is the Section 1411 version (AGI plus foreign earned income exclusion).

### Treating The Thresholds As Inflation-Adjusted

The NIIT thresholds are frozen by statute and have not changed since 2013. The trap is assuming they rise with inflation like brackets. Restate the fixed amounts each time.

### Including Wages Or Retirement Distributions In NII

Wages, self-employment income, and qualified plan distributions are excluded from NII. The trap is adding them to the NII base, inflating the tax. Confirm each income source against the inclusion list.

### Missing That Passive Rental And Business Income Is Included

Passive activity income, including rental income from a non-materially-participated activity, is part of NII. The trap is assuming rental income is exempt. Only real estate professionals with material participation can exclude it.

### Forgetting The 0% Capital Gains Bracket Still Triggers NIIT

A taxpayer in the 0% long-term capital gains bracket but above the MAGI threshold still owes 3.8% NIIT. The trap is telling the taxpayer the gains are tax-free. NIIT is a separate layer from the capital gains rate.

### Applying Individual Thresholds To Trusts And Estates

Trusts face NIIT at an inflation-adjusted threshold around $14,000-$15,000, far below individual thresholds. The trap is analyzing a trust as if it were an individual. Use the trust-specific threshold and consider distribution planning.

### Overlooking That NIIT Stacks On Top Of The 20% Capital Gains Rate

The top effective rate on long-term gains for high earners is 23.8% (20% + 3.8%), not 20%. The trap is quoting 20% as the top rate. Layer the surtax explicitly when discussing high-earner capital gains.
