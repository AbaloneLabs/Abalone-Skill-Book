# QEF and MTM Elections

The default Section 1291 PFIC regime is punitive (ordinary income tax plus interest charges on deferred gains). A U.S. shareholder can avoid the default regime by making a Qualified Electing Fund (QEF) election under Section 1295 or a mark-to-market (MTM) election under Section 1296. The QEF election converts the PFIC to a pass-through, taxing the shareholder annually on their share of the PFIC's income. The MTM election (available only for marketable PFIC shares) taxes the shareholder annually on the change in fair market value. Both elections must be made timely and have specific requirements. An agent who applies the elections without understanding the timing, eligibility, and computation will misapply the benefit.

Agents commonly miss that the QEF election requires annual PFIC information from the corporation, that the MTM election requires marketable shares, that the elections must be made in the first year of holding, and that a "purging" election may be needed for existing holdings. The harm is a taxpayer who makes an invalid election or misses the timing and remains in the default regime.

This skill covers the QEF and MTM elections for PFICs under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the elections are complex and have specific requirements, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The QEF Election Mechanics

The QEF election under Section 1295 allows a U.S. shareholder to elect to be taxed annually on their share of the PFIC's ordinary earnings and net capital gain, as if the PFIC were a partnership and the shareholder were a partner. The QEF election converts the PFIC to a pass-through regime: the shareholder reports their share of the PFIC's income annually, regardless of whether distributions are made. On a future sale of the PFIC shares, the gain is treated as capital gain (not subject to the default regime), and the income previously reported under the QEF election increases the shareholder's basis.

Apply the QEF election mechanics. The shareholder elects to be taxed annually on their share of the PFIC's ordinary earnings and net capital gain. The income is reported on Form 8621 and included in the shareholder's gross income, regardless of distributions. The character of the income follows the PFIC's income (ordinary earnings as ordinary income, capital gains as capital gains). On a future sale, the gain is capital gain (not subject to the default regime), and the basis is increased by the income previously reported under the QEF election. The QEF election avoids the default regime but requires the shareholder to pay tax annually on undistributed PFIC income (a cash flow consideration). The QEF election must be made annually and requires PFIC information from the corporation.

### Obtain The Annual PFIC Information Statement For The QEF Election

The QEF election requires the shareholder to obtain an annual PFIC information statement from the corporation (Form 8621 Schedule A or similar information). The statement provides the shareholder's share of the PFIC's ordinary earnings and net capital gain for the year. Without the information statement, the shareholder cannot make a valid QEF election. Many foreign mutual funds do not provide the information, which can make the QEF election unavailable.

Obtain the annual PFIC information statement. The QEF election requires the shareholder to have the PFIC's annual information (ordinary earnings and net capital gain attributable to the shareholder). The information is provided by the corporation (often on Form 8621 Schedule A or a similar statement). If the corporation does not provide the information, the shareholder cannot make a valid QEF election. This is a common problem with foreign mutual funds, which often do not provide U.S.-compliant PFIC information. If the information is unavailable, the shareholder must use the default regime or the MTM election (if the shares are marketable). Confirm the availability of the PFIC information before relying on the QEF election.

### Understand The MTM Election Mechanics

The mark-to-market (MTM) election under Section 1296 allows a U.S. shareholder to elect to be taxed annually on the change in the fair market value of the PFIC shares. The MTM election is available only if the PFIC shares are marketable (regularly traded on an established securities market). Under the MTM election, the shareholder includes the annual increase in fair market value as ordinary income and deducts the annual decrease as an ordinary loss (subject to limitations). On a future sale, the gain or loss is ordinary (not capital), and the basis is adjusted for the annual MTM income or loss.

Apply the MTM election mechanics. The shareholder elects to mark the PFIC shares to market annually. The annual increase in fair market value is included as ordinary income; the annual decrease is deducted as an ordinary loss (limited to prior MTM income on those shares). On a future sale, the gain or loss is ordinary (not capital), and the basis is adjusted for the MTM income and loss. The MTM election avoids the default regime and does not require PFIC information from the corporation (only the fair market value, which is available for marketable shares). The MTM election is available only for marketable PFIC shares (regularly traded on an established securities market). For non-marketable shares, the MTM election is not available, and the shareholder must use the default regime or the QEF election.

### Determine The Eligibility For The MTM Election

The MTM election is available only if the PFIC shares are marketable. Marketable means the shares are regularly traded on an established securities market (such as a major stock exchange). Shares traded on foreign exchanges may qualify if the exchange is established and the shares are regularly traded. Shares in privately held foreign corporations or in foreign mutual funds not traded on an established market do not qualify for the MTM election.

Determine the eligibility for the MTM election. The shares must be marketable (regularly traded on an established securities market). Confirm the shares are traded on an established market and meet the regularity requirement. For shares in a foreign mutual fund, check whether the fund is traded on an established market. Many foreign mutual funds are not traded on an established market (they are purchased and redeemed through the fund company, not traded on an exchange), and the MTM election is not available. For shares in a publicly traded foreign corporation, the MTM election is generally available. Confirm the marketability before relying on the MTM election.

### Make The Election Timely In The First Year Of Holding

The QEF election and the MTM election must be made timely, generally in the first tax year in which the shareholder holds the PFIC shares. The election is made on Form 8621, attached to the shareholder's return for the first year. If the election is not made in the first year, the default regime applies for that year and prior years, and a "purging" election (a deemed sale followed by a QEF or MTM election) may be needed to start the QEF or MTM regime for future years.

Make the election timely. The QEF or MTM election must be made on Form 8621 for the first tax year the shareholder holds the PFIC shares. If the election is not made in the first year, the default regime applies for the years before the election, and the shareholder may need to make a purging election to start the QEF or MTM regime. The purging election treats the shares as sold and reacquired on the first day of the election year, triggering the default regime on the deemed gain, and then starting the QEF or MTM regime for future years. The purging election can be costly (it triggers the default regime on the accrued gain), but it may be preferable to remaining in the default regime. Advise the shareholder to make the election in the first year to avoid the need for a purging election.

### Handle The Purging Election For Existing Holdings

For a shareholder who has held PFIC shares for years without making a QEF or MTM election, a purging election may be needed to start the QEF or MTM regime. The purging election (also called a "deemed sale election") treats the shares as sold and reacquired on the first day of the election year. The deemed sale triggers the default regime on the accrued gain (ordinary income, allocation, and interest charge). After the purging election, the QEF or MTM regime applies to the shares for future years. The purging election is costly but may be preferable to remaining in the default regime indefinitely.

Handle the purging election for existing holdings. If the shareholder has held PFIC shares without an election, the default regime applies to the accrued gain. A purging election (deemed sale on the first day of the election year) triggers the default regime on the accrued gain, but then starts the QEF or MTM regime for future years. The cost of the purging election is the default regime tax on the accrued gain (ordinary income, allocation, and interest charge). The benefit is that future gains are not subject to the default regime. Model the cost and benefit of the purging election. For a shareholder with a large accrued gain, the purging election may be very costly, but remaining in the default regime may be worse if the gain continues to grow. Advise the shareholder to consult a tax professional about the purging election.

## Common Traps

### Making A QEF Election Without The PFIC Information Statement

The QEF election requires the annual PFIC information statement from the corporation. Without the information, the election is invalid. Many foreign mutual funds do not provide the information.

### Making An MTM Election On Non-Marketable Shares

The MTM election is available only for marketable PFIC shares (regularly traded on an established securities market). Shares in privately held corporations or non-traded mutual funds do not qualify.

### Failing To Make The Election In The First Year

The QEF or MTM election must be made in the first year of holding. Failing to do so requires a purging election (deemed sale) to start the regime, which triggers the default regime on the accrued gain.

### Forgetting The Annual Reporting Under QEF Or MTM

Under the QEF election, the shareholder reports annual PFIC income. Under the MTM election, the shareholder reports the annual fair market value change. The reporting is required each year on Form 8621.

### Treating MTM Gains As Capital Gains

Under the MTM election, the annual gain and the sale gain are ordinary income (not capital gains). The MTM election converts the PFIC to an ordinary income asset, which is a trade-off for avoiding the default regime.

### Overlooking The Cash Flow Impact Of The QEF Election

The QEF election requires the shareholder to pay tax annually on undistributed PFIC income. If the PFIC does not distribute cash, the shareholder must pay tax from other sources. Consider the cash flow impact.

### Assuming The Purging Election Is Cost-Free

The purging election triggers the default regime on the accrued gain (ordinary income, allocation, and interest charge). It can be costly. Model the cost before proceeding.
