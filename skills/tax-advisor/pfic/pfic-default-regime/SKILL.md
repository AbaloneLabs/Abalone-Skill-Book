# PFIC Default Regime

The Section 1291 default regime is the punitive tax treatment that applies to a U.S. shareholder of a PFIC who has not made a QEF or mark-to-market election. Under the default regime, gains and "excess distributions" on PFIC shares are taxed as ordinary income (not capital gains), allocated ratably over the holding period, and subject to an interest charge on the deferred tax. The result can be a tax liability that far exceeds the actual economic gain, plus interest. An agent who treats a PFIC sale as a normal capital gain, without applying the default regime, will understate the tax by a wide margin.

Agents commonly miss that the default regime taxes gains and excess distributions as ordinary income, that the gain is allocated over the holding period (not all in the sale year), that an interest charge applies to the deferred tax, and that the regime applies unless an election is made. The harm is a taxpayer who sells a PFIC expecting capital gains treatment and faces ordinary income tax plus interest.

This skill covers the Section 1291 default PFIC regime under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the regime is complex and punitive, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Default Regime Triggers

The Section 1291 default regime is triggered by two events: a sale or other disposition of PFIC shares (recognizing a gain), or an "excess distribution" from the PFIC. An excess distribution is a distribution that exceeds 125% of the average annual distributions over the prior three years (or the holding period if shorter). The default regime applies to the gain on the sale and to the excess portion of the distribution. Both are taxed as ordinary income, allocated over the holding period, and subject to the interest charge.

Identify the default regime triggers. A sale or disposition of PFIC shares that produces a gain triggers the regime. A distribution that exceeds 125% of the average annual distributions over the prior three years (or the holding period) is an excess distribution, and the excess portion triggers the regime. For a shareholder who has held the PFIC for years with no distributions and then receives a large distribution, the entire distribution may be an excess distribution. For a shareholder who sells the PFIC for a gain, the entire gain triggers the regime. Identify the trigger and apply the default regime to the gain or the excess distribution.

### Allocate The Gain Or Excess Distribution Over The Holding Period

Under the default regime, the gain or excess distribution is allocated ratably over the shareholder's holding period for the PFIC shares. The allocation is to each day in the holding period. The portion allocated to each prior year is taxed as ordinary income in that year (as if the shareholder had received it then), and the portion allocated to the current year is taxed as ordinary income in the current year. The allocation creates deferred tax for the prior years.

Allocate the gain or excess distribution over the holding period. For a gain on a sale, allocate the gain to each day in the holding period (from acquisition to sale). For an excess distribution, allocate the excess portion to each day in the holding period (from acquisition to the distribution date). The portion allocated to each prior year is taxed as ordinary income in that year. The portion allocated to the current year is taxed as ordinary income in the current year. The allocation to prior years creates deferred tax, which is subject to the interest charge. The allocation can span many years, creating substantial deferred tax and interest.

### Compute The Interest Charge On Deferred Tax

The deferred tax (the tax on the portions allocated to prior years) is subject to an interest charge. The interest charge is computed from the due date of the prior year's return to the due date of the current year's return, for each prior year. The interest rate is the underpayment rate (the IRS underpayment interest rate), compounded daily. The interest charge can be substantial, especially for long holding periods.

Compute the interest charge. For each prior year with an allocated portion, compute the tax on that portion (at the ordinary income rate for that year). The interest charge is computed on that tax from the due date of the prior year's return to the due date of the current year's return. The interest rate is the IRS underpayment rate, compounded daily. Sum the interest charges for all prior years. The interest charge is added to the current-year tax liability. For a PFIC held for 10 years, the interest charge can be a significant portion of the gain. The interest charge makes the default regime punitive and is the reason many taxpayers seek to make a QEF or mark-to-market election.

### Treat The Gain Or Excess Distribution As Ordinary Income

Under the default regime, the gain on a PFIC sale and the excess distribution are taxed as ordinary income, not as capital gains. This means the preferential long-term capital gains rate (0/15/20%) does not apply. The gain and excess distribution are taxed at the shareholder's ordinary income rate (up to 37%). The ordinary income treatment is a significant penalty, especially for long-term holdings that would otherwise qualify for the lower capital gains rate.

Apply the ordinary income treatment. The gain on a PFIC sale and the excess distribution are taxed at the shareholder's ordinary income rate (up to 37%), not the capital gains rate. The preferential rate does not apply. For a shareholder in the 37% bracket, a 100,000 dollar gain on a PFIC sale is taxed at 37,000 dollars (plus the interest charge), compared to 20,000 dollars at the long-term capital gains rate. The ordinary income treatment, combined with the interest charge, can result in an effective tax rate exceeding 50% on the gain. Present the ordinary income treatment and the interest charge so the shareholder understands the punitive nature of the default regime.

### Report The Default Regime On Form 8621

The default regime is reported on Form 8621 (Information Return by a Shareholder of a Passive Foreign Investment Company or Qualified Electing Fund). The form reports the PFIC, the gain or excess distribution, the allocation over the holding period, the deferred tax, and the interest charge. The form is attached to the shareholder's Form 1040. The computation is complex and must be done for each PFIC.

Ensure Form 8621 is filed with the default regime computation. The form reports the PFIC, the triggering event (sale or excess distribution), the gain or excess distribution amount, the allocation over the holding period, the deferred tax for each prior year, and the interest charge. The total tax (current-year portion plus deferred tax plus interest charge) is added to the shareholder's tax liability. The form must be filed for each PFIC. The computation is detailed and requires the holding period, the gain or excess distribution amount, and the prior-year tax rates. Reconcile the reporting with the brokerage statements and the PFIC records.

### Consider The QEF Or Mark-To-Market Election As Alternatives

The default regime is punitive, but the shareholder can avoid it by making a QEF election (which converts the PFIC to a pass-through regime, taxing the shareholder annually on their share of the PFIC's income) or a mark-to-market election (if the shares are marketable, taxing the shareholder annually on the change in fair market value). These elections must be made timely (generally in the first year the shareholder holds the PFIC) and have their own requirements. The elections are discussed in separate skills.

Consider the QEF or mark-to-market election as alternatives to the default regime. The QEF election converts the PFIC to a pass-through, taxing the shareholder annually on their share of the PFIC's income (avoiding the default regime on a future sale). The mark-to-market election (available only for marketable PFIC shares) taxes the shareholder annually on the change in fair market value (also avoiding the default regime on a future sale). Both elections must be made timely and have their own complexities. For a shareholder who has not made an election, the default regime applies. Advise the shareholder to consult a tax professional about making an election for future years or for newly acquired PFICs.

## Common Traps

### Treating A PFIC Sale As A Capital Gain

Under the default regime, the gain is taxed as ordinary income, not as a capital gain. The preferential rate does not apply. Apply the ordinary income treatment.

### Failing To Allocate The Gain Over The Holding Period

The gain is allocated ratably over the holding period, with the prior-year portions taxed in those years. Failing to allocate understates the deferred tax and the interest charge.

### Forgetting The Interest Charge

The deferred tax is subject to an interest charge from the due date of each prior year's return. The interest charge can be substantial for long holding periods.

### Overlooking Excess Distributions

An excess distribution (exceeding 125% of the average annual distributions over the prior three years) triggers the default regime. The excess portion is taxed as ordinary income with the allocation and interest charge.

### Not Filing Form 8621

The default regime is reported on Form 8621. Failure to file triggers penalties and extends the statute of limitations for the entire return.

### Assuming The Default Regime Is The Only Option

The shareholder can avoid the default regime by making a QEF election or a mark-to-market election. The elections must be made timely. Advise the shareholder about the alternatives.

### Underestimating The Total Tax

The ordinary income tax plus the interest charge can result in an effective tax rate exceeding 50% on the gain. Present the total tax so the shareholder understands the punitive nature.
