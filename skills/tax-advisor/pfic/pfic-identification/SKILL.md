# PFIC Identification

A Passive Foreign Investment Company (PFIC) is a foreign corporation that meets either an income test or an asset test for passive activity. PFIC status triggers punitive U.S. tax rules for U.S. shareholders, including the default Section 1291 regime with interest charges on deferred gains and the requirement for annual reporting on Form 8621. The first and most critical step is identifying whether a foreign corporation is a PFIC, because the entire punitive regime depends on this determination. An agent who overlooks PFIC status, or who assumes a foreign entity is not a PFIC, will expose the taxpayer to significant tax and penalties.

Agents commonly miss that the income test and asset test are alternative (either one makes it a PFIC), that foreign mutual funds are almost always PFICs, that the tests are applied annually, and that the shareholder must affirmatively determine PFIC status. The harm is a taxpayer who holds a PFIC for years without reporting and faces massive tax and penalties on a sale.

This skill covers PFIC identification under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; PFIC rules are complex and depend on the foreign corporation's activities, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Income Test

A foreign corporation is a PFIC if it meets the income test: at least 75% of its gross income for the tax year is passive income. Passive income includes dividends, interest, royalties, rents, annuities, gains from commodities and foreign currency transactions, and income from notional principal contracts. Income that is derived in the active conduct of a banking, insurance, or securities business by certain financial institutions may be excluded from passive income under specific rules.

Apply the income test. Determine the foreign corporation's gross income for the tax year. Classify the income as passive or active. Passive income includes dividends, interest, royalties, rents (other than rents from active business), annuities, commodity and currency gains, and notional principal contract income. If passive income is at least 75% of gross income, the income test is met, and the corporation is a PFIC for that year. The test is applied annually; a corporation can be a PFIC in one year and not in another. Obtain the corporation's financial information to apply the test.

### Apply The Asset Test

A foreign corporation is a PFIC if it meets the asset test: at least 50% of its assets (by value, averaged over the tax year) produce passive income or are held for the production of passive income. The assets are valued at fair market value (or adjusted basis for certain assets). Assets that produce active income (business assets, inventory, active business real estate) are not passive assets. Cash is generally a passive asset.

Apply the asset test. Determine the foreign corporation's assets by value, averaged over the tax year. Classify the assets as passive or active. Passive assets are assets that produce passive income (stocks, bonds, cash, passive real estate) or are held for the production of passive income. Active assets are assets used in an active trade or business (business equipment, inventory, active business real estate). If passive assets are at least 50% of the total assets by value, the asset test is met, and the corporation is a PFIC for that year. The test is applied annually. Obtain the corporation's balance sheet to apply the test.

### Recognize Common PFIC Scenarios

Certain foreign corporations are almost always PFICs. Foreign mutual funds (foreign investment funds that hold a portfolio of securities) are almost always PFICs because their income and assets are predominantly passive. Foreign holding companies (foreign corporations that hold investments rather than operating a business) are often PFICs. Foreign insurance companies with passive investment portfolios may be PFICs. U.S. taxpayers holding these entities through foreign brokerage accounts should assume PFIC status unless determined otherwise.

Recognize the common PFIC scenarios. Foreign mutual funds are almost always PFICs (their income and assets are passive). Foreign holding companies that hold investments are often PFICs. Foreign insurance companies with passive portfolios may be PFICs. A U.S. taxpayer holding a foreign mutual fund (for example, a U.S. expat with a foreign brokerage account holding non-U.S. mutual funds) should assume the fund is a PFIC and apply the PFIC rules. The PFIC status does not depend on the shareholder's knowledge; the shareholder must determine the status and comply with the rules. Identify all foreign corporations in the taxpayer's portfolio and assess PFIC status.

### Determine PFIC Status Annually

PFIC status is determined annually, based on the foreign corporation's income and assets for each tax year. A corporation can be a PFIC in one year and not in another. The shareholder must determine the PFIC status for each year they hold the shares. Once a corporation is a PFIC for a year, the punitive rules apply to the shareholder for that year, and the PFIC rules continue to apply in future years even if the corporation ceases to be a PFIC (the once-a-PFIC-always-a-PFIC rule, subject to certain exceptions).

Determine the PFIC status annually. For each year the shareholder holds the shares, assess whether the corporation meets the income test or the asset test. A corporation can fluctuate between PFIC and non-PFIC status. However, once a corporation is a PFIC, the PFIC rules generally continue to apply to the shareholder's shares even if the corporation ceases to be a PFIC (the once-a-PFIC-always-a-PFIC rule), unless a qualifying electing fund (QEF) election or mark-to-market election is in place. Track the PFIC status over the holding period. The shareholder must affirmatively determine the status each year.

### Identify The U.S. Shareholder's Reporting Obligation

A U.S. shareholder who holds shares of a PFIC must report the PFIC on Form 8621 (Information Return by a Shareholder of a Passive Foreign Investment Company or Qualified Electing Fund). The reporting is required for each PFIC, and the form is attached to the shareholder's Form 1040. The reporting is required even if no distribution or sale occurred during the year (for certain shareholders). Failure to report can trigger significant penalties.

Identify the U.S. shareholder's reporting obligation. A U.S. shareholder holding PFIC shares must file Form 8621 for each PFIC. The reporting is required for each tax year in which the shareholder has PFIC shares. The form reports the PFIC, the shareholder's interest, any distributions, any sales, and the applicable PFIC regime (default, QEF, or mark-to-market). Failure to file Form 8621 can trigger penalties and can extend the statute of limitations for the entire return. Identify all PFICs in the shareholder's portfolio and ensure Form 8621 is filed for each.

### Handle The Once-A-PFIC-Always-A-PFIC Rule

Once a corporation is a PFIC for any year during the shareholder's holding period, the PFIC rules continue to apply to the shareholder's shares for all subsequent years, even if the corporation ceases to be a PFIC. This is the once-a-PFIC-always-a-PFIC rule. The rule ensures that the punitive regime cannot be escaped by the corporation changing its activities. The rule does not apply if the shareholder has made a QEF election or a mark-to-market election.

Handle the once-a-PFIC-always-a-PFIC rule. Once the corporation is a PFIC for any year during the shareholder's holding period, the PFIC rules apply to the shares for all subsequent years, regardless of the corporation's current status. The shareholder cannot escape the PFIC regime by holding the shares until the corporation becomes a non-PFIC. The only ways out are to make a QEF election (which converts the PFIC to a pass-through regime), make a mark-to-market election (if the shares are marketable), or sell the shares (triggering the default regime on the gain). Track the PFIC status and the applicable regime over the holding period.

## Common Traps

### Overlooking Foreign Mutual Funds As PFICs

Foreign mutual funds are almost always PFICs. A U.S. taxpayer holding a foreign mutual fund should assume PFIC status and apply the rules. Overlooking the status leads to years of unreported PFIC holding.

### Applying Only One Test

The income test and asset test are alternative. Either test makes the corporation a PFIC. A corporation can fail the income test but meet the asset test (or vice versa). Apply both tests.

### Assuming PFIC Status Is Permanent Or One-Time

PFIC status is determined annually. A corporation can be a PFIC in one year and not in another. However, the once-a-PFIC-always-a-PFIC rule continues the regime for the shareholder.

### Failing To File Form 8621

A U.S. shareholder holding PFIC shares must file Form 8621 for each PFIC. Failure to file triggers penalties and extends the statute of limitations for the entire return.

### Treating PFIC Distributions As Qualified Dividends

PFIC distributions under the default regime are taxed as ordinary income with interest charges, not as qualified dividends. The punitive regime applies unless a QEF or mark-to-market election is made.

### Ignoring The Shareholder's Affirmative Duty To Determine Status

The shareholder must affirmatively determine the PFIC status. The status does not depend on the shareholder's knowledge. A shareholder who fails to determine the status is still subject to the PFIC rules.

### Overlooking PFICs In Foreign Brokerage And Retirement Accounts

PFICs can be held in foreign brokerage accounts and foreign retirement accounts. A U.S. expat with a foreign retirement account holding foreign mutual funds may have PFICs. Review all foreign accounts.
