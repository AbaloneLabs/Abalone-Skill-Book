# Year-End Checklist And Deadlines

Year-end tax planning is governed by two different clocks, and confusing them is the most expensive mistake in the field. The first clock is December 31: certain moves must physically happen within the tax year to count for that year — a charitable gift must be postmarked or charged, a Roth conversion must be executed, a required minimum distribution must be received, a tax-loss harvest must settle. Once January 1 arrives, these doors are closed and no amount of paperwork can reopen them. The second clock is the filing deadline (April 15 for individuals, plus extensions): certain moves — an IRA contribution, an HSA contribution, certain elections — can still be made for the prior year even into the following spring. The judgment problem is knowing which clock each move runs on, and sequencing the December 31 moves so that none are missed in the year-end rush.

The blind spots cluster around two failures. The first is treating soft-deadline moves as if they were urgent and hard-deadline moves as if they were flexible — spending December energy on an IRA contribution that could wait until April while letting a Roth conversion or RMD lapse. The second is misunderstanding what "done by December 31" actually requires: a check written but not mailed, a trade placed but not settled, a gift promised but not delivered, a conversion initiated but not completed — each can fail to count for the year depending on the rule. Agents also miss the cascading dependencies: a year-end Roth conversion must be completed before December 31, but the decision depends on knowing the year's income, which depends on bonuses and distributions that may not be finalized until late December, compressing the decision window.

This skill covers the time-sensitive checklist of year-end moves, the distinction between December 31 hard deadlines and filing-deadline soft deadlines, and the sequencing of moves so that irreversible deadlines are met first. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; deadlines and rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, RMD ages and contribution limits are indexed or legislated, and a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) must be consulted for any actual situation. TCJA provisions sunset after 2025, and amounts such as the standard deduction and contribution limits are indexed annually.

## Core Rules

### Separate Hard-Deadline Moves From Soft-Deadline Moves

The foundational discipline is to classify every planned move by its deadline. Hard-deadline moves must be completed by December 31 (the tax year) and cannot be cured afterward: charitable contributions (by delivery date), Roth IRA conversions, required minimum distributions, tax-loss harvesting trades, most elective deferrals into a workplace retirement plan, gifts using the annual exclusion, and most entity-level elections. Soft-deadline moves can be completed by the filing deadline (typically April 15) and still count for the prior year: traditional and Roth IRA contributions, HSA contributions, and certain SEP contributions (the filing-deadline-plus-extension rule for self-employed).

Build two lists. The December 31 list is urgent and irreversible; the April 15 list is flexible. The classic error is inverting the urgency. An IRA contribution for the prior year can be made as late as April 15 (and you can even file the return early and fund it after), so it should never crowd out a Roth conversion that must close by December 31. Sequence the hard list first, then use the soft list to mop up remaining capacity in January through April. Confirm the exact rule for each move, because some "obvious" deadlines are subtler than they appear (e.g., a Solo 401(k) employee deferral must be made by December 31, but the employer profit-sharing portion can be made by the extended filing deadline).

### Treat December 31 Delivery Rules Literally

For a hard-deadline move, what counts is the legal completion, not the taxpayer's intent. A charitable contribution is deductible in the year delivered: a check mailed by December 31 counts (postmark rule for mailed gifts), a credit-card charge by December 31 counts (even if paid off in January), but a check handed over or postmarked January 2 does not. A stock gift to charity is effective on the date the brokerage transfers the shares, not the date the donor clicked "initiate," and brokerages can take days to process year-end transfers, so initiating in the last week is risky.

For each December 31 move, identify the controlling completion event and build in a buffer. A Roth conversion is complete when the funds move, which for a trustee-to-trustee transfer can take several business days — initiate it well before the last week. A tax-loss harvest requires the trade to settle within the year; under T+1 settlement a December 30 trade settles in the year, but a December 31 trade may settle in January (the trade date generally controls for recognition, but confirm the broker's reporting and the wash-sale window). Required minimum distributions must be received by December 31 (the "received," not "requested," date). Do not rely on best-case processing times at year-end, when brokerages and custodians are overloaded.

### Sequence Moves By Irreversibility And Information Dependency

Not all December 31 moves are equal; sequence them by how irreversible they are and how much they depend on still-unknown information. Start with moves that are both irreversible and time-sensitive and that do not depend on uncertain income: RMDs (missing one triggers a 25% excise tax, reducible for good faith but still punitive), charitable gifts that have already been decided, and any fixed elections. Defer moves that depend on final income figures — a Roth conversion sized to fill a bracket, a charitable gift sized to get under an IRMAA threshold — until late December when wage and distribution data is clearer, but leave enough time for processing.

Recognize that late-December information dependency creates a compressed window. The taxpayer's marginal bracket for the year may not be knowable until the final pay stub and any December bonuses arrive in the third or fourth week of December. A conversion or gain-realization decision that depends on that bracket must then be executed in a few business days, when custodians are slow. The mitigation is to model the range of outcomes in November, pre-stage the paperwork, and execute as soon as the bracket is clear — not to wait until December 30 and discover the conversion cannot settle in time.

### Apply The RMD Rules By The Correct Age And Deadline

Required minimum distributions from traditional retirement accounts must begin by a specific age and continue annually by December 31. Under the SECURE Act and SECURE 2.0, the RMD age rose to 73 (for those born 1951-1959) and will rise to 75 (for those born 1960 or later). The first RMD can be delayed to April 1 of the following year (the "required beginning date"), but every subsequent RMD is due by December 31 — and taking the first one late means taking two in one year, which can spike income into a higher bracket and trigger IRMAA and NIIT.

Treat the RMD as the single most urgent year-end item because the penalty for a shortfall is severe (25%, reducible to 10% for timely correction). Confirm the account owner's birth year to set the RMD age, confirm whether this is the first RMD (April 1 grace) or a recurring one (December 31), and confirm the distribution has been received, not merely requested, by the deadline. Also check inherited IRAs, which have their own (often stricter) 10-year or annual distribution rules under the SECURE Act, and which are a frequent source of missed-deadline penalties.

### Lock In Charitable Substantiation Before Year-End Closes

A charitable deduction is only as good as its substantiation, and substantiation that is missing or late cannot be cured after the return is filed. Cash gifts of $250 or more require a contemporaneous written acknowledgment from the charity (stating whether goods or services were provided) obtained by the filing deadline or the extended due date, whichever is earlier. Non-cash gifts over $500 require Form 8283, and non-cash gifts over $5,000 require a qualified appraisal. "Contemporaneous" means obtained before the return is filed, but the gift itself must be delivered by December 31 to count for that year.

For year-end charitable planning, confirm both the delivery (postmark or transfer date by December 31) and the substantiation path before the year closes. A donor-advised fund contribution made by December 31 secures the current-year deduction even if the grants to operating charities happen later, which is a common bunching tool — but the contribution to the DAF itself must clear by December 31. For appreciated-stock gifts, the transfer date (not the gift date) controls, and brokerages need lead time, so initiate stock gifts by mid-December.

### Distinguish Estimated Tax Timing From Year-End Moves

Estimated tax payments have their own quarterly deadlines (April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15 of the following year for individuals), which straddle the calendar year. The January 15 payment is for the prior tax year and is the last chance to avoid an underpayment penalty through the safe-harbor rules. Year-end withholding is special: withholding is treated as paid evenly throughout the year, so a large year-end withholding from a bonus or IRA distribution can cure an underpayment for the whole year, which a January estimated payment cannot do retroactively.

If the taxpayer is underpaid for the year, consider increasing December withholding (e.g., from a year-end bonus, or by voluntarily increasing W-4 withholding) rather than making a January estimated payment, because withholding's even-ratability can eliminate a penalty that a late estimated payment cannot. Confirm the safe harbor (generally 90% of current-year tax or 100%/110% of prior-year tax) and whether the taxpayer qualifies for the annualized-income method if income was back-loaded. Do not assume a January estimated payment fixes a prior-year underpayment the way December withholding can.

### Confirm Elections That Must Be Made On A Timely Filed Return

Several valuable elections are made by attaching a statement or checking a box on a timely filed return (including extensions), not by a December 31 action — but they still have a deadline. Examples include the Section 179 expense election, the de minimis safe harbor for tangible property, the mark-to-market election for traders, and certain entity elections. These are "soft" in the sense that they run to the filing deadline, but they are "hard" in the sense that a late or missed election often cannot be cured without IRS consent.

For each election the plan depends on, confirm the filing mechanism and the deadline. Some elections are automatic if the form is filed; others require a private letter ruling to fix if missed. Do not treat an election as safely in the future merely because it is not a December 31 move; calendar it against the filing deadline and confirm the supporting documentation (e.g., a cost-segregation study for a Section 179 or bonus-depreciation claim) is ready in time to file.

## Common Traps

### Letting A Soft-Deadline Move Crowd Out A Hard One

The symptom is spending December on an IRA or HSA contribution that could wait until April. The trap is inverting urgency — the IRA can be funded through April 15, but a Roth conversion or RMD cannot be made up after December 31. Prioritize the hard list first.

### Treating "Initiated" As "Completed" For A December 31 Move

The symptom is clicking "submit" on a conversion or stock gift on December 30 and assuming it counts. The trap is that processing takes business days, and the controlling event (transfer, settlement, receipt) may occur in January. Build a processing buffer and initiate by mid-December for slow custodians.

### Missing An RMD And Incurring The Excise Tax

The symptom is forgetting a recurring RMD or misreading the first-RMD grace period. The trap is the 25% (potentially 10%) excise tax on the shortfall, plus the income-spike risk of doubling up in one year. Treat RMDs as the most urgent item and confirm receipt by December 31.

### Assuming Withholding And Estimated Payments Are Equivalent For Penalty Cure

The symptom is planning a January estimated payment to fix a current-year underpayment. The trap is that withholding is ratably applied across the year and can cure a full-year underpayment, while a late estimated payment cannot. Use December withholding to cure underpayment when possible.

### Overlooking Inherited-IRA Distribution Deadlines

The symptom is focusing only on the owner's own RMD. The trap is that inherited IRAs under the SECURE Act often require annual or 10-year-window distributions with stricter rules and their own penalties. Check inherited accounts separately.

### Failing To Secure Charitable Substantiation Before Filing

The symptom is deducting a year-end gift without the $250 written acknowledgment or the Form 8283/appraisal. The trap is that missing or late substantiation disallows the deduction regardless of whether the gift was validly made. Obtain acknowledgments contemporaneously.

### Treating A Late Election As Easily Curable

The symptom is assuming a missed Section 179 or mark-to-market election can be added later. The trap is that many elections are time-sensitive and a late one may require a private letter ruling. Calendar every election against its filing deadline, not just the December 31 date.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has every planned move been classified as a December 31 hard deadline or a filing-deadline soft deadline, with the hard list sequenced first?
- [ ] For each December 31 move, has the controlling completion event (postmark, transfer, settlement, receipt) been identified with a processing buffer built in?
- [ ] Has the RMD age (73 or 75 depending on birth year) been confirmed, and has receipt — not just request — by December 31 been verified, including for inherited IRAs?
- [ ] Has the first-RMD April 1 grace period been evaluated against the risk of doubling income in one year (bracket, IRMAA, NIIT)?
- [ ] Have Roth conversions been initiated early enough to settle by December 31, with the decision window protected against late-December information dependency?
- [ ] Has charitable substantiation (written acknowledgment for $250+ gifts, Form 8283 for non-cash over $500, appraisal for over $5,000) been confirmed obtainable before filing?
- [ ] Has the estimated-tax versus withholding distinction been applied, using December withholding to cure a full-year underpayment where a January estimated payment would not?
- [ ] Have all return-filed elections been calendared against the filing deadline with supporting documentation ready, recognizing that late elections may be irremediable?
- [ ] Has the plan confirmed that soft-deadline contributions (IRA, HSA, SEP employer portion) are not consuming December attention needed for irreversible moves?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general educational guidance, not personalized tax advice, that deadlines and amounts are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time (RMD ages and limits are legislated, standard deduction and contribution limits indexed annually), and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
