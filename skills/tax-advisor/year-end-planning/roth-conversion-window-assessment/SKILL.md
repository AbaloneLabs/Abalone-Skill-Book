# Roth Conversion Window Assessment

A Roth conversion — moving pre-tax money from a traditional IRA into a Roth IRA and paying tax on the converted amount as ordinary income — is one of the most powerful and most easily misjudged moves in year-end planning. The core logic is simple: convert when the current marginal rate is lower than the expected future marginal rate on the same dollars, so that tax is paid once at the cheaper rate and all future growth escapes tax entirely. The judgment problem is that "current versus future rate" is a comparison between a known present and an unknowable future, and the conversion is permanent. Since 2018, conversions cannot be undone by recharacterization, so a conversion sized on a wrong assumption locks in the mistake for life. The decision therefore demands a careful assessment of the year-end window: where the taxpayer's income sits relative to bracket ceilings and AGI thresholds, what the future rate path looks like, and what collateral damage the conversion's added income inflicts on IRMAA surcharges, ACA subsidies, and NIIT.

The agent blind spots on conversion assessment are concentrated in three areas. First, agents size the conversion to the ordinary bracket ceiling while ignoring that the conversion income ripples through several other means-tested systems — IRMAA (Medicare premium surcharges based on two-year-prior MAGI), ACA premium subsidies (which phase out on a cliff based on current-year household income), and NIIT (3.8% above the MAGI threshold) — each of which can add effective marginal cost that erases the conversion's benefit. Second, agents recommend paying the conversion tax from inside the traditional IRA, which both reduces the net amount moved to Roth and, for pre-59.5 taxpayers, can trigger an early-distribution penalty on the withholding. Third, agents overlook the pro-rata aggregation rule, which forces all traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRA balances to be combined when determining how much of any distribution (including a conversion) is taxable, so a taxpayer with a large pre-tax balance and a small after-tax basis converts mostly taxable money regardless of which account the dollars come from. The harm prevented is a conversion that looks rate-advantaged on the bracket alone but is a net loss once collateral costs and aggregation are accounted for.

This skill covers the assessment of whether the year-end window is optimal for a Roth conversion, including current-versus-future rate comparison, bracket-filling against thresholds, the 5-year rule, the IRMAA and ACA and NIIT interactions, paying the tax from outside versus inside the IRA, and the pro-rata aggregation rule. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change frequently, and dollar amounts are indexed annually. A qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation. All figures are illustrative for framing and must be verified for the applicable tax year, and TCJA-era provisions are scheduled to sunset, which can change the future-rate comparison.

## Core Rules

### Convert Only When Current Rate Is Below Expected Future Rate

The foundational test is that a conversion pays off only if the rate paid on conversion is lower than the rate that would otherwise apply to those dollars at withdrawal. A taxpayer in the 24% bracket now who expects to withdraw in the 32% bracket later (due to large required minimum distributions, a working spouse, or rising rates) wins by converting at 24%. The same taxpayer who expects to withdraw in the 12% bracket later (due to lower retirement income) loses by converting at 24%. The comparison must use the all-in marginal rate in each period, including NIIT and state tax, not the headline bracket.

Build the comparison against a realistic future-rate projection. Estimate the taxpayer's retirement taxable income once RMDs begin (currently age 73, rising to 75 for those born 1960 or later), including Social Security taxation, pension income, and any other pre-tax distributions. If RMDs alone will push the taxpayer into a higher bracket than the current conversion year, the window is favorable. If the taxpayer will be in a lower bracket in retirement (common for those with modest pre-tax balances or large taxable accounts), conversion is usually unfavorable. The comparison is the whole decision, so invest in getting the future projection as realistic as possible and stress-test it across scenarios.

### Size The Conversion To Fill, Not Overflow, The Target Threshold

Conversion sizing is the mechanical heart of the move. The goal is to convert up to the top of a target bracket or threshold — commonly the top of the 24% bracket, or the top of the 0% capital-gains bracket, or just below an IRMAA tier — without spilling over. Spilling one dollar into the 32% bracket taxes that dollar at 32% instead of 24%, an 8-point jump that can erase the benefit of all the conversion dollars below it. The conversion must be sized against the full year's income, not in isolation, because every other dollar of income consumes bracket space.

Model the conversion against projected taxable income including wages, investment income, other distributions, and the conversion itself. For 2024 (illustrative), the 24% bracket ceiling for MFJ is roughly $383,900 of taxable income, so a couple with $150,000 of other taxable income could convert up to about $233,900 at 24% or below before hitting 32%. But size conservatively below the ceiling to leave a margin for late-year income surprises — a December bonus, an unexpected dividend, a year-end gain — that can consume the planned room. Re-check the actual bracket in December before executing, and never convert a round number; convert the precise figure the model produces. Also coordinate with capital-gains realization, because the conversion's ordinary income pushes up the line that determines the capital-gains bracket, potentially shifting gains from 0% to 15%.

### Account For IRMAA As A Conversion Cost

The Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount (IRMAA) adds surcharges to Medicare Part B and Part D premiums based on modified AGI from two years prior. Because IRMAA tiers are income thresholds, a conversion that pushes MAGI across a tier creates a discontinuous cost — a jump of several hundred to over a thousand dollars per person per year in premiums — that is effectively a marginal tax on the conversion dollars that crossed the line. A conversion sized only to the ordinary bracket can land in an IRMAA tier whose surcharge wipes out the rate advantage.

Map the IRMAA tiers for the conversion year and identify whether the conversion crosses one. Because IRMAA looks back two years, a conversion in 2025 affects Medicare premiums in 2027, so the cost is deferred but real. For a married couple both on Medicare, the surcharge applies to both, doubling the cost. When the conversion would cross a tier, size it to land just below the tier boundary rather than straddling it, unless the amount above the tier is large enough that the bracket savings clearly exceed the surcharge. IRMAA is often the binding constraint on conversion size for taxpayers in their 60s, tighter than the ordinary bracket itself.

### Protect ACA Subsidies And Other Current-Year Cliffs

For taxpayers under 65 buying health insurance on the ACA marketplace, premium tax credits and cost-sharing subsidies phase out on a cliff based on current-year household income (historically at 400% of the federal poverty line, though temporary expansions have softened this). A conversion that pushes income over the cliff can eliminate thousands of dollars of subsidies, a cost that dwarfs the conversion's rate benefit. Unlike IRMAA, the ACA cliff is a current-year effect, so the conversion and the subsidy loss hit in the same year.

Check whether the taxpayer is on an ACA plan and where their income sits relative to the cliff before recommending a conversion. If a conversion would cross the cliff, either defer it, size it to stay below, or accept the subsidy loss only if the long-term conversion benefit clearly exceeds it. The same discipline applies to other current-year AGI-cliff provisions — the NIIT threshold, student-loan-interest phaseouts, the Section 469 rental exception, Child Tax Credit phaseouts — each of which can add effective marginal cost to the conversion income. Enumerate every cliff the conversion income could cross and include the cost in the marginal-rate calculation.

### Honor The 5-Year Rule For Earnings And Early Distributions

The Roth IRA has two distinct 5-year clocks. The first governs qualified distributions of earnings: earnings are tax-free only after the Roth has been open for at least 5 years and the owner is 59.5, disabled, or a first-time homebuyer. Each conversion starts its own 5-year clock for purposes of the 10% early-distribution penalty on converted amounts distributed before 59.5 — a converted amount withdrawn within 5 years of the conversion and before age 59.5 triggers the penalty, even though the conversion itself was taxed. This matters for younger converters or those who might need to tap the converted funds.

Confirm the 5-year implications before converting. For a taxpayer over 59.5 with an established 5-year-old Roth, the clocks are satisfied and distributions are clean. For a younger taxpayer, or one opening a first Roth via the conversion, the converted principal can be withdrawn penalty-free but the earnings cannot, and the converted amount itself is subject to the 5-year penalty clock if withdrawn early. The 5-year rule does not usually defeat a conversion for a long-horizon taxpayer, but it constrains liquidity and must be disclosed so the taxpayer does not assume the converted funds are freely accessible.

### Pay The Conversion Tax From Outside The IRA

The conversion tax is owed on the full converted amount as ordinary income, and the question is where the cash to pay it comes from. Paying from outside the IRA — from a taxable account, cash savings, or current cash flow — is almost always superior, because the entire converted amount lands in the Roth and compounds tax-free. Paying the tax from inside the IRA by withholding reduces the net amount converted, so less money enjoys the Roth's future tax-free growth, and for a taxpayer under 59.5 the amount withheld for tax is itself an early distribution that can trigger the 10% penalty.

Recommend paying from outside whenever feasible. Illustratively, converting $100,000 at 24% creates a $24,000 tax bill; paying it from outside means $100,000 grows tax-free in the Roth, while paying it from inside means only $76,000 moves to Roth and the $24,000 withholding may carry a penalty. If the taxpayer lacks outside funds, the conversion may still be worth doing but the math is weaker because less capital is rescued into the Roth. Never recommend withholding from the conversion to pay the tax without flagging the early-distribution penalty risk for pre-59.5 taxpayers.

### Apply The Pro-Rata Aggregation Rule Across All Traditional IRAs

The pro-rata rule is the most overlooked constraint on conversions, and it can make a conversion far more taxable than expected. When a taxpayer has any pre-tax money in a traditional IRA (including a SEP or SIMPLE IRA, and including a rollover from a prior 401(k)), every distribution — including a conversion — is treated as coming proportionally from the pre-tax and after-tax portions across all traditional IRAs combined. A taxpayer with $90,000 pre-tax and $10,000 after-tax basis who converts $10,000 converts 90% taxable money ($9,000) and 10% tax-free ($1,000), regardless of which account the dollars are pulled from.

Compute the taxable fraction using Form 8606 logic before recommending a conversion. The aggregation means a taxpayer cannot selectively convert only after-tax basis; the basis is diluted by the pre-tax balance. A taxpayer whose only pre-tax retirement money is in a 401(k) (which is not aggregated with IRAs) can sidestep the rule by rolling the IRA pre-tax balance into the 401(k) first, leaving only after-tax basis in the IRA to convert cleanly — but this depends on the 401(k) plan accepting inbound rollovers. The backdoor Roth strategy (nondeductible contribution followed by conversion) works cleanly only when the IRA balance is near zero; with a large pre-tax IRA, the pro-rata rule taxes most of the conversion. Always check the total IRA balance and basis before assessing a conversion window.

### Sequence The Conversion Before The December 31 Deadline

A Roth conversion must be completed within the tax year to count for that year, and "completed" means the funds have actually moved, not merely that paperwork was initiated. Unlike an IRA contribution (which can be made for the prior year through April 15), a conversion has no such grace period — December 31 is a hard cutoff. Trustee-to-trustee transfers and same-trustee conversions take processing time, and custodians are overloaded at year-end, so a conversion initiated in the last week of December may not settle until January, pushing it into the next tax year.

Initiate the conversion early enough to settle by December 31, building a buffer for processing. Recognize the late-December information dependency: the conversion size depends on knowing the year's final income (a December bonus, a year-end distribution), which may not be clear until late in the month, compressing the window. Pre-stage the paperwork in November, model the bracket range, and execute as soon as the bracket is clear. Because conversions are irrevocable since 2018, there is no recharacterization safety net, so getting the year and the size right on the first execution is essential.

## Common Traps

### Sizing The Conversion To The Bracket And Ignoring IRMAA

The symptom is converting up to the 24% bracket ceiling. The trap is that the conversion income crosses an IRMAA tier, adding Medicare premium surcharges for both spouses that erase the rate benefit. The direction is to map the IRMAA tiers and size the conversion to land below the binding one.

### Crossing The ACA Subsidy Cliff With Conversion Income

The symptom is recommending a conversion for a pre-65 taxpayer on an ACA plan. The trap is that the added income eliminates premium subsidies worth thousands, a current-year cost that dwarfs the conversion benefit. The direction is to check the ACA cliff and defer or downsize the conversion when exposed.

### Paying The Conversion Tax From Inside The IRA

The symptom is withholding from the conversion to cover the tax bill. The trap is that less money reaches the Roth, and for pre-59.5 taxpayers the withholding is an early distribution that carries a 10% penalty. The direction is to pay the tax from outside funds whenever feasible.

### Ignoring The Pro-Rata Rule Across SEP And Traditional Balances

The symptom is assuming a conversion of after-tax basis is tax-free. The trap is that the pro-rata aggregation rule taxes the conversion proportionally across all traditional IRA balances, so a large pre-tax balance makes most of the conversion taxable. The direction is to compute the Form 8606 fraction and consider rolling pre-tax money into a 401(k) first.

### Converting In A Year When The Future Rate Is Actually Lower

The symptom is converting because the current bracket "seems low." The trap is that the taxpayer's retirement bracket will be lower still (modest pre-tax balance, large taxable account, low expenses), so conversion pays tax now that would never have been owed at that rate. The direction is to project the realistic future withdrawal rate before converting.

### Assuming A Conversion Can Be Undone

The symptom is treating a conversion as reversible if the rate assumption proves wrong. The trap is that recharacterization of conversions was repealed for tax years beginning after 2017, so a conversion is permanent and irrevocable. The direction is to treat the conversion as a one-way move and size it conservatively with partial execution to hedge.

### Overlooking The 5-Year Clock On Converted Amounts

The symptom is assuming converted funds are freely accessible. The trap is that converted principal withdrawn within 5 years and before 59.5 triggers the 10% early-distribution penalty, and earnings require a separate 5-year clock. The direction is to confirm the 5-year implications and flag the liquidity constraint for younger converters.
