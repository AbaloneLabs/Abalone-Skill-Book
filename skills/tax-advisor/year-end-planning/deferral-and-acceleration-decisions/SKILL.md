# Deferral And Acceleration Decisions

The default instinct in year-end tax planning is reflexive deferral: push income out, pull deductions in. That instinct is often right, but it is a heuristic, not a rule. The real judgment problem is that every deferral or acceleration decision is a trade between this year's marginal rate and next year's marginal rate, weighted by the probability that those rates hold, discounted for the time value of money, and bounded by timing rules that determine whether the move is even possible. A bonus deferred into January, a deduction pulled into December, a capital gain realized now versus later, a Roth conversion sized against the bracket ceiling — each is the same underlying question: which year's marginal dollar is cheaper, and how confident are you in that comparison?

The blind spots are severe. Agents apply average rates instead of marginal rates, ignore the standard deduction (which makes the first dollars of deduction worth zero), assume the current rate structure is permanent when the TCJA brackets are scheduled to sunset after 2025, and forget that income cannot always be deferred because of constructive receipt. They also treat income and deduction timing as symmetric when they are not, and they overlook threshold taxes — the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax, the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax, IRMAA Medicare surcharges — that create cliffs where one extra dollar of income costs far more than the headline bracket. The harm is a plan that looks conservative but actually raises lifetime tax, or a move that is disallowed because the taxpayer had no real control over the timing.

This skill covers the decision logic for deferring or accelerating income and deductions across a two-year horizon, including marginal rate comparison, expected rate changes, the standard deduction interaction, constructive receipt, and threshold-tax cliffs. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; outcomes depend on facts that must be verified, and a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) must be consulted for any actual situation. All rules are U.S. federal, jurisdiction-dependent, and subject to change — TCJA provisions sunset after 2025, statutory amounts are indexed annually, and bracket thresholds shift each year.

## Core Rules

### Decide On Marginal Rate, Not Average Or Headline Rate

The deferral decision turns entirely on the marginal rate in each year — the rate on the next dollar — not the average (effective) rate and not the top bracket the taxpayer touches. Income should be moved toward the year with the lower marginal rate. A taxpayer at the top of the 24% bracket this year who expects to be in the 22% bracket next year should defer income, because the deferred dollar is taxed at 22% instead of 24%. The same taxpayer who expects a large one-time gain next year pushing them into the 32% bracket should accelerate income into the cheaper 24% year.

Compute the marginal rate after accounting for the standard deduction and the bracket structure. For 2024 the standard deduction is $29,200 for Married Filing Jointly, so the first $29,200 of income for an MFJ couple with no other deductions is taxed at 0%. A couple with $100,000 of taxable income is in the 22% bracket, but a $30,000 bonus pushes the last dollars into 24%. The marginal rate on the accelerated or deferred dollars is what matters, and it must be computed for both years before deciding direction. Never quote the average rate as the basis for the move.

### Factor Expected Statutory Rate Changes Into The Comparison

Bracket arbitrage is not only about the taxpayer's own income fluctuating; it is also about the law changing. The TCJA individual rates (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%) are scheduled to sunset after 2025 and revert to the pre-TCJA structure (10%, 15%, 25%, 28%, 33%, 35%, 39.6%), with the 12% bracket becoming 15%, the 22% becoming 25%, and the 24% becoming 28%. If a sunset is expected, accelerating ordinary income or capital gains into 2025 can lock in the lower TCJA rate before the higher 2026 rate applies.

Do not treat the sunset as certain. Congress has extended expiring provisions before, and the political outcome is unknowable in advance. The disciplined approach is to model both scenarios — sunset and extension — and prefer moves that are robust under both, or to size any rate-change bet so that being wrong is tolerable. A taxpayer who accelerates a large gain into 2025 betting on a 2026 increase, only to see the brackets extended, has paid tax early for no benefit. Flag rate-change assumptions explicitly and avoid presenting them as facts.

### Treat Income And Deduction Timing As Asymmetric Decisions

Deferring income and accelerating deductions are not mirror images. Income deferral is usually subject to timing control (when the taxpayer can choose to receive income), while deduction acceleration is usually subject to payment rules (cash-method taxpayers deduct when paid, subject to the economic-benefit and related-party rules). Accelerating a deduction by prepaying is often easy and low-risk; deferring income is often impossible because the income has already been earned or the right to receive it is fixed.

Separate the two analyses. For deductions, confirm the payment is made (for cash-method taxpayers, charging a deductible expense to a credit card by December 31 counts as paid in that year even if the card is paid off later) and that it is not subject to a deferral or capitalization rule (prepaid interest, prepaid rent beyond 12 months, related-party accruals). For income, confirm the taxpayer actually controls the timing. A bonus already earned but unpaid may be subject to constructive receipt and cannot be deferred merely by asking the employer to wait. The two sides of the move have different rules and different feasibility, so analyze them independently.

### Respect Constructive Receipt And Timing Control

Income can only be deferred if the taxpayer does not yet have an unrestricted right to receive it. The constructive receipt doctrine treats income as received when it is made available without substantial limitation, even if the taxpayer chooses not to take it. A bonus that is credited or made available in December cannot be deferred to January by leaving it on the books; it is taxed in December. Similarly, a taxpayer who has the option to take a distribution now and waits is still taxed now.

Confirm who controls the timing before recommending deferral. Self-employed taxpayers invoicing in late December can defer by billing in January (for cash-method taxpayers, income is recognized when received). Employees generally cannot defer salary or an already-earned bonus. Deferred-compensation arrangements under Section 409A have strict timing and anti-acceleration rules, and a misstep can trigger immediate taxation plus a 20% penalty. The feasibility of deferral is a threshold question; if the taxpayer lacks control, the decision is moot.

### Account For The Standard Deduction And Bracket Filling

A deduction accelerated into a year when the taxpayer claims the standard deduction produces no current benefit. With the TCJA's higher standard deduction (roughly $29,200 MFJ, $14,600 single for 2024), many taxpayers no longer itemize, so accelerating itemizable deductions like charitable gifts or state tax prepayments into a standard-deduction year wastes the move. The deduction only saves tax in a year the taxpayer itemizes.

Map whether each year is an itemizing year or a standard year before accelerating deductions. If the taxpayer alternates (itemizing one year, standard the next), deductions should be concentrated into the itemizing year — but that is a bunching strategy, which is a separate multi-year decision. For the two-year deferral question, the rule is simple: a deduction is worth its marginal rate only in a year the taxpayer itemizes, and worth zero in a standard-deduction year. Never count a deduction at the marginal rate without first confirming it clears the standard-deduction hurdle.

### Watch Threshold Taxes That Create Rate Cliffs

The headline bracket understates the true marginal rate because several taxes trigger at income thresholds. The 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax applies to investment income above $200,000 single / $250,000 MFJ of modified AGI. The 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax applies to wages and self-employment income above the same thresholds. IRMAA Medicare premium surcharges rise in tiers based on two-year-prior MAGI. Each of these adds to the effective marginal rate at the margin, sometimes pushing the real rate well above the stated bracket.

Compute the all-in marginal rate including threshold taxes for both years. A taxpayer at the 24% bracket who accelerates $10,000 of investment income may also trigger the 3.8% NIIT and cross an IRMAA tier, making the true marginal cost closer to 28% plus a multi-hundred-dollar Medicare premium increase. Conversely, deferring income to stay below a threshold can be worth far more than the bracket difference alone. The deferral or acceleration decision must use the all-in marginal rate, not the bracket rate, or it will systematically misjudge the trade.

### Weigh Time Value, Risk, And Liquidity

Deferring income is not free money; it is a loan from the government that must eventually be repaid, and it carries risk. The time value of deferral is modest at current rates (deferring $10,000 of 24% tax for one year saves only the interest on $2,400). The risk is that future rates rise, future income rises and pushes the deferred dollar into a higher bracket, or rules change to capture the deferred income. Acceleration has the opposite profile: certain current cost, uncertain future benefit.

Do not recommend deferral without acknowledging it is a bet on future rates being no higher. For taxpayers who expect rates to rise (TCJA sunset, career income growth, a planned Roth conversion or large sale next year), acceleration may dominate deferral. For taxpayers near retirement or in a temporarily low-income year, acceleration into the cheap year is often the better move. Frame every recommendation as conditional on the rate assumptions, and prefer moves that hedge rather than bet heavily on one rate outcome.

## Common Traps

### Deferring Income Reflexively Without A Rate Comparison

The symptom is recommending "push the bonus to January" by default. The trap is treating deferral as automatically correct when it is only correct if next year's marginal rate is lower. Compare the all-in marginal rates first; if next year is higher (rising income, TCJA sunset, a planned conversion), deferral raises lifetime tax.

### Applying Average Or Headline Rate Instead Of Marginal Rate

The symptom is quoting the taxpayer's effective rate or top bracket as the cost of the moved dollars. The trap is that the moved dollars are taxed at the marginal rate on the next dollar, which may be 0% (standard deduction), 12%, 22%, or 24% depending on where income lands. Always compute the marginal rate on the specific dollars being moved, in both years.

### Accelerating Deductions Into A Standard-Deduction Year

The symptom is prepaying charitable gifts or property tax in December to capture a deduction. The trap is that if the taxpayer claims the standard deduction, the accelerated deduction saves nothing. Confirm the year is an itemizing year before accelerating, or the move is wasted.

### Assuming Income Can Be Deferred When Constructive Receipt Applies

The symptom is telling an employee to defer an already-earned bonus or a contractor to delay invoicing income they have a right to. The trap is constructive receipt: income made available without restriction is taxed when available, not when taken. Confirm the taxpayer actually controls timing before recommending deferral.

### Ignoring Threshold Taxes That Distort The True Marginal Rate

The symptom is deciding the move on the 22% or 24% bracket alone. The trap is that NIIT (3.8%), Additional Medicare (0.9%), and IRMAA tiers add to the marginal cost at thresholds, so the real rate can be several points higher. Compute the all-in marginal rate including threshold taxes for both years.

### Treating The Current Rate Structure As Permanent

The symptom is assuming 2025's brackets continue into 2026. The trap is that the TCJA rates are scheduled to sunset after 2025, which can flip the deferral decision entirely. Model the rate-change possibility and avoid betting the entire plan on one legislative outcome.

### Overlooking State Tax And Payment-Timing Rules

The symptom is planning only at the federal level or assuming a prepayment always counts. The trap is that state conformity, the SALT cap, and rules against prepaid interest or beyond-12-month prepayments can eliminate the expected benefit. Check state interaction and the specific payment rule before counting a deduction.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the marginal rate (not average or headline rate) been computed for both the current and next year on the specific dollars being moved, after the standard deduction?
- [ ] Has the all-in marginal rate including NIIT (3.8%), Additional Medicare (0.9%), and any IRMAA tier been used, rather than the bracket rate alone?
- [ ] Has the standard-deduction interaction been checked, so deductions are only accelerated into years the taxpayer itemizes?
- [ ] Has constructive receipt been evaluated to confirm the taxpayer actually controls the timing of any income being deferred?
- [ ] Has the TCJA sunset (scheduled after 2025) and any expected rate change been modeled as a scenario rather than assumed away?
- [ ] Have income deferral and deduction acceleration been analyzed separately, since they have different feasibility and payment rules?
- [ ] Has the deferral been framed as a conditional bet on future rates, with acceleration considered when next year's rate is expected to be higher?
- [ ] Have state tax conformity, the SALT cap, and payment-timing rules (credit-card payment date, 12-month prepayment limit) been verified?
- [ ] Has the time value of deferral been acknowledged as modest, so the recommendation is not oversold as a large saving?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general educational guidance, not personalized tax advice, that rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
