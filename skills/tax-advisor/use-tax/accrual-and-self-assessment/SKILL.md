# Accrual And Self Assessment

Use-tax self-assessment is the legal mechanism by which a business becomes its own tax collector, and the accrual is the controlled process that turns a scattered legal duty into a periodic, defensible remittance. The judgment problem is that the obligation is transactional — it attaches to each purchase where sales tax was not fully collected — but the compliance is periodic, so the business must reconstruct taxable spend from the accounts-payable ledger, compute the tax, accrue it, and report it on the right return at the right rate. The decisions are layered: which vendor invoices to flag, which purchases are taxable versus exempt, which rate to apply when the seller charged tax below the home-jurisdiction rate, how to reconcile what was accrued against what was reported, and what to do when prior periods turn out to have been under-reported or never reported at all. Self-assessment is unforgiving because the duty exists whether or not the business measures it, and an unmeasured duty accrues interest and penalty from the date it was originally due.

Agents tend to treat accrual as a bookkeeping exercise and miss that it is, at its core, a self-assessment of legal tax liability that the business is attesting to under penalty. They forget that a seller charging tax at a rate below the home-jurisdiction rate creates a complementary or supplementary use-tax gap that the buyer must self-assess for the difference, not ignore. They conflate the accrual estimate with the reported tax and never reconcile the two, so under-accruals quietly compound. They also fail to recognize that discovering prior unreported use tax is not the end of the matter — interest runs from each original due date, and the choice between quietly amending, a formal lookback, and a voluntary disclosure agreement materially changes the penalty and interest outcome. The harm prevented is both accumulated back-tax exposure with compounding interest and the overpayment that occurs when a business self-assesses use tax on purchases that were already fully taxed or genuinely exempt.

This skill covers the self-assessment duty and the accrual mechanics for businesses — accounts-payable review, flagging non-taxed and under-taxed vendor purchases, period-end accrual, complementary and supplementary use tax on rate differences, reconciliation of accrued versus reported tax, and lookback and voluntary disclosure for prior unreported use tax — under U.S. state and local tax principles. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; accrual methods, complementary-tax rules, lookback periods, and voluntary disclosure terms are jurisdiction-dependent and change frequently. A qualified state-and-local tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Treat Self-Assessment As A Legal Attestation, Not A Bookkeeping Estimate

The use-tax accrual is the business's periodic attestation that it has measured and is remitting the tax it legally owes on purchases where sales tax was not fully collected, and that attestation is made under the penalties that apply to a false return. This framing changes how the process must be built: the accrual is not a rough reserve the business can set and forget, but a measured liability that must be supportable transaction by transaction. The duty attaches at the moment of purchase, so even if the business never accrues or reports, the liability exists and grows with interest from the original due date.

Build the accrual so that each component — the taxable base, the rate, the jurisdiction of first use, and any exemption — is traceable to evidence, because an auditor will reconstruct the same base from the ledger and compare it to what was reported. A defensible self-assessment is one where the business can show how it identified taxable purchases, how it computed the tax, and how it reconciled the accrual to the return. Treat the period-end accrual as a signed representation of tax owed, and apply to it the same care the business would apply to signing its income-tax return. The bookkeeping is the vehicle; the legal attestation is the substance.

### Drive The Accrual From A Systematic Accounts-Payable Review, Not Memory

A defensible accrual begins with a systematic review of the accounts-payable ledger to surface purchases where sales tax was not charged or was charged below the home-jurisdiction rate. The reliable method is to flag vendors known not to collect tax — typically out-of-state and online suppliers — and to review their invoices each period, because these are the purchases where the self-assessment duty is concentrated. Relying on memory or an annual sample lets the majority of taxable spend pass unreviewed, which is the most common cause of material under-reporting found in business audits.

Establish a vendor-master flag for non-collecting and under-collecting vendors so their invoices route to use-tax review before posting, and supplement the vendor flag with an invoice-level check for the tax charged, because even registered vendors occasionally fail to collect on a taxable line. For each flagged invoice, confirm whether the purchase is taxable, identify the jurisdiction of first use, and apply the correct combined rate. The accounts-payable review is the intake stage of self-assessment, and its thoroughness determines whether the accrual captures the real liability or a fraction of it. Where volume makes manual review impractical, tax-determination software can automate the flag and the rate, but the exempt-versus-taxable judgment still requires review.

### Compute Complementary And Supplementary Use Tax On Rate Differences

A seller that charged sales tax at a rate below the buyer's home-jurisdiction rate does not eliminate the use-tax duty; it creates a partial gap that the buyer must self-assess as complementary or supplementary use tax equal to the difference between the home rate and the rate collected. This is distinct from the full use tax owed when no tax was collected at all, and it is the most commonly missed component of business self-assessment because the invoice shows some tax and the buyer assumes the matter is closed. The complementary tax is computed on the same taxable base as the original sale, using the home-jurisdiction's combined rate.

Identify under-taxed purchases during the accounts-payable review by comparing the rate charged to the destination's combined rate, and accrue the difference. For example, if a vendor charged 4 percent on a 10,000 dollar equipment purchase delivered to an 8 percent jurisdiction, the buyer owes a 4 percent complementary use tax of roughly 400 dollars, not zero and not a fresh 8 percent. Retain proof of the tax the seller collected, because the credit for tax paid is allowed only to the extent documented, and an undocumented rate is treated as zero. Treating a partially taxed invoice as fully taxed is the signature self-assessment error, and the complementary tax is the correction.

### Reconcile Accrued Tax Against Reported Tax Every Period

The accrual is an estimate or a transaction-level computation that feeds a liability, and the return is the actual remittance, so the two must be reconciled every filing period to confirm that what was accrued is what was reported and paid. A residual accrual balance that never clears signals either tax accrued but not moved to the return or over-accrual that was never corrected, and a negative balance signals tax remitted without a matching accrual. Unreconciled differences are not noise; they are evidence that the self-assessment chain is broken somewhere between measurement and remittance.

Require a reconciliation workpaper each period that ties the accrual rollforward to the return total and to cash remitted, and investigate every residual until it is explained and cleared. If the reconciliation reveals under-accrual, the under-reported tax must be addressed through an amended or supplemental return for the affected period, because the duty was originally due then and interest runs from that date. If it reveals over-accrual, document the basis and pursue the state's credit or refund procedure rather than silently netting it against future periods. Periodic reconciliation is what converts an accrual from a hopeful estimate into a controlled self-assessment, and it is the single strongest evidence of a compliant process on audit.

### Apply The Correct Rate For Each Jurisdiction Of First Use

Use tax is owed to the jurisdiction where the property is first stored, used, or consumed, and the rate is that jurisdiction's combined state-plus-local rate, not a flat statewide figure. A business operating across multiple locations must accrue use tax by destination, because local-option, transit, and special-district components can differ materially even within one state. Applying a single statewide rate to all purchases understates the liability for higher-rate localities and produces a reconciliation that never ties to the true per-jurisdiction base.

Determine the first-use jurisdiction for each flagged purchase — typically the delivery or installation address — and look up that location's combined rate, including all local components. A purchase delivered to a facility in a 9 percent jurisdiction is taxed at 9 percent even if the business's headquarters sits in a 6 percent jurisdiction, and the complementary tax on an under-taxed invoice is measured against the 9 percent destination rate. Recheck rates periodically because local rates change, and document the rate source and effective date so the self-assessment is defensible. The destination rate is the legal measure of the duty, and a statewide approximation is a systematic understatement.

### Distinguish Taxable From Exempt Purchases Before Accruing

Not every untaxed purchase creates a use-tax duty, because exemptions that apply to sales tax apply symmetrically to use tax, including resale, manufacturing, agriculture, and government exemptions, provided the buyer holds a valid certificate and uses the property in the exempt manner. The accounts-payable review must therefore apply an exempt-versus-taxable judgment to each flagged purchase, rather than accruing use tax on every invoice from a non-collecting vendor. Accruing tax on a genuinely exempt purchase is an overpayment that ties up cash and distorts the reconciliation, while skipping the judgment and taxing everything is not a conservative shortcut but an incorrect self-assessment.

Confirm each claimed exemption against the jurisdiction's exemption schedule and the buyer's certificate, and document the basis so the exemption survives audit. A manufacturer buying production machinery may be exempt, while the same manufacturer's office computers are taxable, and both may appear on invoices from non-collecting vendors. Apply the exemption only to the property and use it genuinely covers, split mixed invoices, and accrue use tax on the remainder. The exemption judgment is what keeps the accrual accurate rather than uniformly over- or under-stated, and it must be made deliberately, not by default.

### Remediate Prior Unreported Use Tax Through Lookback Or Voluntary Disclosure

When a review reveals use tax that was never reported or was under-reported in prior periods, the liability does not disappear; it accrues interest from each original due date, and the statute of limitations may still be open for the most recent periods. The business faces a remediation choice: file amended or supplemental returns for the open years, conduct a formal lookback review that quantifies and remits the exposure, or enter a voluntary disclosure agreement (VDA) with the state, which typically limits the lookback period and may waive penalties and sometimes a portion of interest. The choice materially changes the financial outcome, because a VDA can cap the lookback to a defined number of years while a quiet amendment addresses only the periods the business chooses to correct.

Quantify the exposure period by period before choosing a path, because the open statute years, the magnitude of the under-reporting, and whether the business was already registered in the state all affect which remedy is available and advantageous. A VDA is often the right vehicle for material, multi-year exposure in a state where the business has nexus but limited prior registration, because it brings the liability current under defined and often lenient terms; a simple amended return may suffice for a small, isolated error in an already-filed period. Never bury a prior-period correction silently into the current period's accrual, because that fails to stop interest running from the original due date and can itself be treated as a misstatement. Remediation is where self-assessment meets enforcement risk, and the structure of the correction determines whether the exposure is contained or compounded.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Partially Taxed Invoice As Fully Taxed

The symptom is a buyer who sees some sales tax on an invoice and records no use tax. The trap is that when the seller charged below the home-jurisdiction rate, a complementary or supplementary use tax is owed for the difference, and ignoring it leaves a gap that interest accrues on. The direction is to compare the rate charged to the destination's combined rate during the accounts-payable review and accrue the difference as complementary use tax.

### Accruing Use Tax But Never Reconciling It To The Return

The symptom is an accrual account with a balance that drifts period after period. The trap is assuming a lingering balance is benign, when it signals tax accrued but not reported or over-accrual that was never corrected, either of which is an audit finding. The direction is to reconcile the accrual to the return every period and investigate every residual until it is explained and cleared.

### Flagging Only Large Purchases And Letting Small Ones Accumulate

The symptom is an accrual that captures a few big equipment buys but ignores many small online supply orders. The trap is that aggregated small purchases from non-collecting vendors produce a material liability that an auditor reconstructs from the ledger, so selective flagging understates the duty. The direction is to flag all non-collecting and under-collecting vendors in the vendor master and review their invoices systematically regardless of dollar size.

### Using A Flat Statewide Rate Across All Locations

The symptom is a multi-site business that accrues use tax at one statewide percentage. The trap is omitting local-option, transit, and special-district components that differ by location, which understates the liability for higher-rate jurisdictions and breaks the per-jurisdiction reconciliation. The direction is to determine the first-use jurisdiction for each purchase and apply that location's combined rate, rechecking it as local rates change.

### Taxing Every Untaxed Purchase Without An Exemption Judgment

The symptom is a business that accrues use tax on every invoice from non-collecting vendors, including genuinely exempt purchases. The trap is treating taxation as the safe default, when accruing tax on an exempt purchase is an overpayment that distorts the accrual and ties up cash, and skipping the judgment is an incorrect self-assessment. The direction is to apply an exempt-versus-taxable judgment to each flagged purchase, confirm any exemption against the schedule and certificate, and accrue only on the taxable remainder.

### Burying A Prior-Period Correction Into The Current Accrual

The symptom is a discovered prior-period underpayment folded into the current period's accrual without an amended return. The trap is that this fails to stop interest running from the original due date and can itself be treated as a misstatement, while a proper amended return or voluntary disclosure would have contained the exposure. The direction is to remediate prior unreported use tax through amended returns, a formal lookback, or a voluntary disclosure agreement, never by silently netting it forward.

### Assuming Discovery Of Past Under-Reporting Ends The Matter

The symptom is a business that finds prior unreported use tax and simply begins accruing going forward. The trap is that the historical liability still exists, accrues interest from each original due date, and remains exposed for the open statute years, so forward-only accrual does not resolve it. The direction is to quantify the exposure period by period and choose a remediation path — amended return, lookback, or VDA — that addresses the historical duty on defined terms.
