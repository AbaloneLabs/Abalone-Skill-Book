# Expatriation Reporting

Expatriation triggers a set of reporting requirements that extend beyond the exit tax. A covered expatriate must file Form 8854 (Initial and Annual Expatriation Statement) for the year of expatriation and potentially for subsequent years (if they have deferred compensation or grantor trust items subject to ongoing rules). A non-covered expatriate must also file Form 8854 to certify compliance and confirm non-covered status. Additionally, the Section 2801 inheritance tax applies to gifts and bequests received from covered expatriates. An agent who treats expatriation as a one-time event, without the ongoing reporting and the inheritance tax, will miss critical obligations.

Agents commonly miss that Form 8854 is required for all expatriating persons (covered and non-covered), that the form has ongoing filing requirements for deferred compensation and grantor trusts, and that the Section 2801 inheritance tax applies to recipients of covered expatriate gifts and bequests. The harm is a person who fails to file required forms and faces penalties, or a recipient who fails to pay the inheritance tax.

This skill covers expatriation reporting under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the reporting requirements are detailed, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### File Form 8854 For All Expatriating Persons

Form 8854 (Initial and Annual Expatriation Statement) is required for all expatriating U.S. citizens and long-term residents, whether they are covered expatriates or not. For a covered expatriate, the form reports the covered status, the exit tax computation, and the special rules for deferred compensation and grantor trusts. For a non-covered expatriate, the form certifies tax compliance for the 5 preceding years and confirms non-covered status. Failure to file Form 8854 can trigger a 10,000 dollar penalty and can result in the IRS presuming covered expatriate status.

File Form 8854 for all expatriating persons. For a covered expatriate, the form reports the covered status (the net worth test, the tax liability test, the certification test), the exit tax computation (the mark-to-market, the exclusion, the special rules), and the deferred compensation and grantor trust elections. For a non-covered expatriate, the form certifies compliance with all U.S. federal tax obligations for the 5 preceding years and confirms that the person does not meet any of the covered expatriate tests. The form is due by the tax return due date (April 15, or October 15 with an extension) for the year of expatriation. Failure to file triggers a 10,000 dollar penalty and can result in presumed covered status. File the form for every expatriating citizen and LTR.

### Handle The Ongoing Form 8854 Filing For Deferred Compensation

A covered expatriate who has deferred compensation items or grantor trust assets subject to the ongoing rules must file Form 8854 annually for the years in which they receive distributions. The ongoing filing reports the distributions and the withholding (for non-eligible deferred compensation subject to the 30% withholding) or the mark-to-market election results (for eligible deferred compensation). The ongoing filing continues until the deferred compensation is fully distributed.

Handle the ongoing Form 8854 filing. A covered expatriate with deferred compensation items (pensions, 401(k) plans, IRAs, stock options) or grantor trust assets subject to the special rules must file Form 8854 annually for the years in which they receive distributions. The ongoing filing reports the distributions and the applicable tax treatment (the 30% withholding for non-eligible deferred compensation, or the mark-to-market results for eligible deferred compensation). The ongoing filing continues until the deferred compensation or grantor trust assets are fully distributed. Track the distributions and the filing requirement. Failure to file the ongoing Form 8854 can trigger penalties and can affect the withholding obligations of the payer.

### Apply The Section 2801 Inheritance Tax On Gifts And Bequests

Section 2801 imposes a tax on U.S. citizens and residents who receive gifts or bequests from covered expatriates. The tax is computed at the highest gift or estate tax rate (40%) on the value of the gift or bequest, with an annual exclusion (the annual gift tax exclusion, 18,000 dollars per recipient for 2024). The tax is paid by the recipient (not the covered expatriate) and is reported on Form 708. The Section 2801 tax applies to gifts and bequests received after the expatriation date.

Apply the Section 2801 inheritance tax. A U.S. citizen or resident who receives a gift or bequest from a covered expatriate is subject to the tax, computed at the highest gift or estate tax rate (40%) on the value, with the annual exclusion (18,000 dollars per recipient for 2024). The tax is paid by the recipient and is reported on Form 708. The tax applies to gifts and bequests received after the covered expatriate's expatriation date. For example, a U.S. child who receives a 100,000 dollar gift from a covered expatriate parent pays a 40% tax on the amount above the annual exclusion (100,000 minus 18,000 = 82,000, taxed at 40% = 32,800 dollars). The recipient must file Form 708 and pay the tax. Track the gifts and bequests from covered expatriates.

### Handle The Coordination With The Gift And Estate Tax

The Section 2801 inheritance tax is separate from the U.S. gift and estate tax. The covered expatriate is not subject to the U.S. gift or estate tax on the gift or bequest (they have expatriated and are no longer a U.S. person for transfer tax purposes). However, the recipient is subject to the Section 2801 tax. The coordination is important: the covered expatriate does not pay the gift tax, but the recipient pays the Section 2801 tax at the same rate. The recipient cannot use the unified credit (the lifetime gift and estate tax exemption) to offset the Section 2801 tax.

Handle the coordination with the gift and estate tax. The covered expatriate is not subject to the U.S. gift or estate tax on the gift or bequest (they are no longer a U.S. person for transfer tax purposes). The recipient is subject to the Section 2801 tax, computed at the highest gift or estate tax rate (40%) on the value above the annual exclusion. The recipient cannot use the unified credit (the lifetime exemption) to offset the Section 2801 tax; the tax is in addition to any gift or estate tax the recipient might owe. The coordination is important for estate planning: a covered expatriate who wants to transfer wealth to U.S. recipients should consider the Section 2801 tax burden on the recipients. Track the coordination and advise the covered expatriate and the recipients accordingly.

### Report The Expatriation On Form 1040

A covered expatriate files a Form 1040 for the year of expatriation, covering the period from January 1 to the expatriation date (a short-period return). The Form 1040 reports the income, the exit tax (from Form 8854), and any other tax for the year. After expatriation, the covered expatriate files Form 1040-NR (for nonresident aliens) for any U.S. source income, if they remain subject to U.S. tax on U.S. source income. The transition from Form 1040 to Form 1040-NR occurs at the expatriation date.

Report the expatriation on Form 1040. A covered expatriate files a Form 1040 for the short period from January 1 to the expatriation date. The return reports the income for the period, the exit tax (from Form 8854), and any other tax. After the expatriation date, the covered expatriate is a nonresident alien for U.S. tax purposes and files Form 1040-NR for any U.S. source income (subject to withholding or treaty benefits). The dual-status return (part-year resident, part-year nonresident) may be required for the year of expatriation. Track the filing requirements and the transition from Form 1040 to Form 1040-NR. The covered expatriate must also continue to file FBAR and Form 8938 if they have foreign accounts or assets that trigger the reporting (the reporting obligations may continue after expatriation for certain assets).

### Maintain Records And Track The Statute Of Limitations

The expatriation reporting and the exit tax create records that must be maintained for the statute of limitations period. The Form 8854, the exit tax computation, the asset inventory, and the basis documentation must be retained. The statute of limitations for the exit tax return is generally 3 years from the filing date, but the IRS can extend the period if there is a substantial understatement or fraud. The ongoing Form 8854 filings (for deferred compensation) also create records that must be maintained.

Maintain records and track the statute of limitations. Retain the Form 8854, the exit tax computation, the asset inventory, the basis documentation, and any related records for the statute of limitations period (generally 3 years, but longer for substantial understatements or fraud). The ongoing Form 8854 filings (for deferred compensation and grantor trusts) create additional records that must be retained. The Section 2801 inheritance tax records (Form 708) must also be retained by the recipients. Track the statute of limitations for each filing. The expatriation is a significant event that creates multi-year reporting and record-keeping obligations; maintain the records carefully.

## Common Traps

### Failing To File Form 8854 For Non-Covered Expatriates

Form 8854 is required for all expatriating persons, including non-covered expatriates (to certify compliance and confirm non-covered status). Failure to file triggers a 10,000 dollar penalty and can result in presumed covered status.

### Overlooking The Ongoing Form 8854 Filing

A covered expatriate with deferred compensation or grantor trust items must file Form 8854 annually for distribution years. The ongoing filing continues until the items are fully distributed.

### Ignoring The Section 2801 Inheritance Tax

Gifts and bequests from covered expatriates to U.S. recipients are subject to the Section 2801 tax (40% on the value above the annual exclusion), paid by the recipient. Recipients must file Form 708.

### Treating Expatriation As A One-Time Event

Expatriation creates ongoing reporting (Form 8854 for deferred compensation) and tax obligations (Section 2801 for recipients). Do not treat it as a single filing.

### Filing The Wrong Return After Expatriation

After expatriation, the covered expatriate files Form 1040-NR for U.S. source income, not Form 1040. The dual-status return may be required for the year of expatriation.

### Forgetting Continued FBAR And Form 8938 Reporting

The covered expatriate may continue to have FBAR and Form 8938 reporting obligations after expatriation for certain foreign accounts and assets. Do not assume all reporting ends at expatriation.

### Overlooking The Records And Statute Of Limitations

The expatriation records (Form 8854, exit tax computation, asset inventory) must be retained for the statute of limitations period. Track the filing dates and the retention.
