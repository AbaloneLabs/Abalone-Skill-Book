# Exit Tax Calculation

The Section 877A exit tax is the mark-to-market regime that applies to covered expatriates. Under the exit tax, a covered expatriate is treated as if they sold all of their worldwide assets at fair market value on the day before expatriation, and the unrealized gain is taxed as a capital gain (subject to an exclusion amount). The exit tax can result in a substantial tax liability, especially for covered expatriates with significant appreciated assets. An agent who treats the exit tax as a simple capital gains tax, without understanding the mark-to-market, the exclusion, and the special rules for deferred compensation and grantor trusts, will miscompute the liability.

Agents commonly miss that the exit tax is a mark-to-market on all worldwide assets, that there is an exclusion amount (about 866,000 dollars for 2024, adjusted for inflation), that deferred compensation and grantor trusts have special rules, and that the alternative valuation date may apply. The harm is a covered expatriate whose exit tax is miscomputed and who faces an unexpected liability or an overpayment.

This skill covers the exit tax calculation under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the calculation is complex and depends on the assets and the elections, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Mark-To-Market Regime

Under the Section 877A exit tax, a covered expatriate is treated as if they sold all of their worldwide assets at fair market value on the day before expatriation. The unrealized gain on each asset is computed (fair market value minus adjusted basis) and is taxed as a capital gain. The mark-to-market applies to all assets except those subject to special rules (deferred compensation, grantor trusts, specified tax deferred accounts). The mark-to-market creates a deemed sale, and the gain is taxed in the year of expatriation.

Apply the mark-to-market regime. List all of the covered expatriate's worldwide assets (real estate, stocks, bonds, mutual funds, business interests, personal property, and any other assets). For each asset, compute the unrealized gain (fair market value on the day before expatriation minus the adjusted basis). The gain is taxed as a capital gain (long-term or short-term based on the holding period). The mark-to-market applies to all assets except those subject to special rules (deferred compensation items, grantor trust assets, specified tax deferred accounts, and eligible deferred compensation). The total gain is the sum of the gains on all assets. The mark-to-market creates a deemed sale on the day before expatriation, and the gain is taxed in the year of expatriation. Obtain a complete asset inventory and the basis for each asset.

### Apply The Exclusion Amount

The exit tax provides an exclusion amount that reduces the taxable gain. For 2024, the exclusion amount is 866,000 dollars (adjusted for inflation annually). The exclusion is applied to the total mark-to-market gain (the sum of the gains on all assets). The exclusion is allocated pro rata to the assets based on their relative gains. After applying the exclusion, the remaining gain is taxed as a capital gain. The exclusion amount is per person (each covered expatriate gets the full exclusion).

Apply the exclusion amount. Compute the total mark-to-market gain (the sum of the gains on all assets). Subtract the exclusion amount (866,000 dollars for 2024, adjusted for inflation). The remaining gain is taxed as a capital gain. The exclusion is allocated pro rata to the assets based on their relative gains (for basis adjustment purposes). For a covered expatriate with a total gain of 1,500,000 dollars, the exclusion reduces the taxable gain to 634,000 dollars (1,500,000 minus 866,000). The 634,000 dollars is taxed as a capital gain (at the 0/15/20% rate, plus the 3.8% NIIT if applicable). The exclusion amount is per person, so a married couple where both spouses are covered expatriates each gets the full exclusion. Track the exclusion and the pro rata allocation.

### Handle The Special Rules For Deferred Compensation

Deferred compensation items (pensions, 401(k) plans, IRAs, stock options, RSUs, and other deferred compensation) are subject to special rules under the exit tax, rather than the mark-to-market. For non-eligible deferred compensation (such as foreign pensions), the exit tax applies a withholding rate of 30% on the present value of the deferred compensation (the payer withholds 30% on distributions). For eligible deferred compensation (such as a 401(k) or IRA), the covered expatriate can elect to have the mark-to-market apply or to have the 30% withholding apply on distributions. The election is made on Form 8854.

Handle the special rules for deferred compensation. Identify the deferred compensation items (pensions, 401(k) plans, IRAs, stock options, RSUs, foreign pensions). For non-eligible deferred compensation (foreign pensions, non-qualifying plans), the exit tax applies a 30% withholding rate on the present value (the payer withholds 30% on distributions, with no treaty reduction). For eligible deferred compensation (qualifying 401(k) plans, IRAs), the covered expatriate can elect either the mark-to-market (taxing the gain now) or the 30% withholding on distributions (deferring the tax but losing treaty benefits on the distributions). The election is made on Form 8854. Model both options. The deferred compensation rules are complex; the covered expatriate should consult a tax professional to optimize the election.

### Handle The Special Rules For Grantor Trusts

Grantor trust assets (assets in a trust where the covered expatriate is treated as the owner for U.S. tax purposes) are subject to special rules under the exit tax. The covered expatriate is treated as receiving a distribution of the grantor trust assets equal to their fair market value on the day before expatriation, and the distribution is taxed under the trust distribution rules (generally as a capital gain to the extent of the trust's undistributed net income). The grantor trust rules prevent the covered expatriate from avoiding the exit tax on assets held in a grantor trust.

Handle the special rules for grantor trusts. Identify the grantor trust assets (assets in a trust where the covered expatriate is the grantor for U.S. tax purposes). The covered expatriate is treated as receiving a distribution of the grantor trust assets equal to their fair market value on the day before expatriation. The distribution is taxed under the trust distribution rules (generally as a capital gain to the extent of the trust's undistributed net income). The grantor trust rules ensure that the exit tax applies to assets held in a grantor trust, preventing the covered expatriate from avoiding the tax by transferring assets to a trust before expatriating. Track the grantor trust assets and the deemed distribution.

### Apply The Alternative Valuation Date

The covered expatriate can elect to use the alternative valuation date (the date 6 months after the expatriation date, or the date of distribution if earlier) instead of the day-before-expatriation date for valuing the assets. The alternative valuation date is generally used if the assets have declined in value between the expatriation date and the 6-month date. The election is made on Form 8854 and applies to all assets (not on an asset-by-asset basis).

Apply the alternative valuation date. The covered expatriate can elect to value the assets at the alternative valuation date (6 months after expatriation, or the distribution date if earlier) instead of the day before expatriation. The election is beneficial if the assets have declined in value, reducing the mark-to-market gain. The election is made on Form 8854 and applies to all assets (the covered expatriate cannot cherry-pick assets). Model both valuation dates to determine which is more favorable. The alternative valuation date is generally used only in declining markets; in rising markets, the day-before-expatriation date is more favorable. Document the election and the valuation.

### Report The Exit Tax On Form 8854

The exit tax is reported on Form 8854 (Initial and Annual Expatriation Statement). The form reports the covered expatriate status, the mark-to-market computation (the assets, the gains, the exclusion), the special rules for deferred compensation and grantor trusts, the alternative valuation election (if applicable), and the total exit tax liability. The form is filed with the covered expatriate's Form 1040 for the year of expatriation. The exit tax is due by the normal tax return due date (April 15, or October 15 with an extension).

Ensure Form 8854 is filed with the exit tax computation. The form reports the mark-to-market on all assets, the exclusion amount, the special rules for deferred compensation and grantor trusts, the alternative valuation election, and the total exit tax liability. The exit tax is added to the covered expatriate's tax liability for the year of expatriation and is due by the return due date. The form is detailed and requires a complete asset inventory, the basis for each asset, the fair market value, and the special rule computations. Failure to file Form 8854 or to pay the exit tax triggers penalties and interest. Reconcile the exit tax with the asset records and the basis documentation.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Exit Tax As A Simple Capital Gains Tax

The exit tax is a mark-to-market on all worldwide assets, with an exclusion and special rules for deferred compensation and grantor trusts. Treating it as a simple capital gains tax understates or misstates the liability.

### Forgetting The Exclusion Amount

The exclusion amount (866,000 dollars for 2024) reduces the taxable gain. Failing to apply the exclusion overstates the exit tax. The exclusion is per person.

### Overlooking The Special Rules For Deferred Compensation

Deferred compensation (pensions, 401(k), IRAs, stock options) has special rules (30% withholding or mark-to-market election). Applying the standard mark-to-market to deferred compensation misstates the tax.

### Ignoring The Grantor Trust Rules

Grantor trust assets are subject to a deemed distribution on the day before expatriation. Ignoring the grantor trust rules allows the covered expatriate to avoid the exit tax on trust assets.

### Failing To Elect The Alternative Valuation Date When Beneficial

If the assets have declined in value, the alternative valuation date (6 months after expatriation) can reduce the exit tax. Model both dates and elect if beneficial.

### Not Reporting On Form 8854

The exit tax is reported on Form 8854. Failure to file triggers penalties and can result in the IRS presuming covered expatriate status.

### Overlooking The Worldwide Scope

The mark-to-market applies to all worldwide assets, not just U.S. assets. Foreign real estate, foreign investments, and foreign business interests are included. Obtain a complete worldwide asset inventory.
