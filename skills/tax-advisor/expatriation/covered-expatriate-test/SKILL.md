# Covered Expatriate Test

When a U.S. citizen renounces their citizenship or a long-term resident (LTR) terminates their U.S. residency, they may be subject to the exit tax under Section 877A. The exit tax applies only to "covered expatriates." A covered expatriate is a person who meets one of three tests: the net worth test (net worth of 2 million dollars or more), the average annual net income tax liability test (average annual liability above a threshold, 201,000 dollars for 2024, adjusted for inflation), or the certification test (failure to certify compliance with U.S. tax obligations for the prior 5 years). An agent who does not apply the covered expatriate tests, or who applies them incorrectly, will misstate whether the exit tax applies.

Agents commonly miss that the tests are alternative (any one makes the person a covered expatriate), that the net worth test includes worldwide assets, that the certification test requires 5 years of tax compliance, and that exceptions exist for dual citizens and minors. The harm is a person who renounces without knowing they are a covered expatriate and faces the exit tax, or who overpays because they did not claim an exception.

This skill covers the covered expatriate test under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the determination is fact-intensive, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Net Worth Test

The net worth test is met if the person's net worth is 2 million dollars or more on the date of expatriation or the date of termination of residency. Net worth is the fair market value of all worldwide assets minus all liabilities. The assets include all types: real estate, investments, retirement accounts, business interests, personal property, and any other assets. The liabilities include all debts: mortgages, loans, credit card debt, and other obligations. The net worth is computed as of the expatriation date.

Apply the net worth test. Compute the person's net worth as of the expatriation date. Total the fair market value of all worldwide assets (real estate, investments, retirement accounts, business interests, personal property, and any other assets). Subtract all liabilities (mortgages, loans, credit card debt, and other obligations). If the net worth is 2 million dollars or more, the net worth test is met, and the person is a covered expatriate. The 2 million dollar threshold is fixed (not adjusted for inflation). The worldwide scope means that assets outside the U.S. are included. Obtain a complete asset and liability inventory to apply the test. Document the net worth computation.

### Apply The Average Annual Net Income Tax Liability Test

The average annual net income tax liability test is met if the person's average annual U.S. federal net income tax liability for the 5 tax years preceding expatriation exceeds a threshold. For 2024, the threshold is 201,000 dollars (adjusted for inflation). The average is computed over the 5 years. If the average exceeds the threshold, the test is met. The tax liability is the net federal income tax liability (after credits) for each year.

Apply the average annual net income tax liability test. Compute the person's U.S. federal net income tax liability for each of the 5 tax years preceding expatriation. Average the 5 years. If the average exceeds the threshold (201,000 dollars for 2024, adjusted for inflation), the test is met, and the person is a covered expatriate. The tax liability is the net federal income tax liability (after credits, including the foreign tax credit) for each year. A person with high income or significant capital gains may exceed the threshold. Obtain the 5 years of tax returns to apply the test. Document the average computation.

### Apply The Certification Test

The certification test is met if the person fails to certify under penalties of perjury that they have complied with all U.S. federal tax obligations for the 5 tax years preceding expatriation. The certification covers all tax obligations, including filing returns, reporting foreign accounts (FBAR, Form 8938), and paying all tax due. If the person cannot certify compliance (for example, they failed to file returns, failed to report foreign accounts, or have unpaid tax), the certification test is met, and the person is a covered expatriate.

Apply the certification test. Determine whether the person can certify compliance with all U.S. federal tax obligations for the 5 years preceding expatriation. The certification covers filing all required returns (Form 1040, Form 8938, FBAR, and any other required forms), reporting all foreign accounts and assets, and paying all tax due. If the person has any non-compliance (unfiled returns, unreported accounts, unpaid tax), they cannot certify, and the certification test is met. A person who is non-compliant should consider remedying the non-compliance (filing the missing returns, reporting the accounts, paying the tax) before expatriating, to be able to certify compliance. The certification is made on Form 8854 (Initial and Annual Expatriation Statement).

### Apply The Dual Citizen And Minor Exceptions

There are exceptions to the covered expatriate status for certain dual citizens and certain minors. The dual citizen exception applies to a person who became a U.S. citizen at birth and is also a citizen of another country, who remains a citizen of that country, who has not been a U.S. resident for at least 10 years (under the substantial presence test) before expatriation, and who would not be subject to U.S. tax as a resident after expatriation. The minor exception applies to a person who expatriated before age 18.5. These exceptions relieve the person from the net worth and average annual tax liability tests (but not the certification test).

Apply the dual citizen and minor exceptions. For the dual citizen exception: the person became a U.S. citizen at birth, is also a citizen of another country (and remains so), has not been a U.S. resident for at least 10 of the 15 years preceding expatriation (under the substantial presence test), and would not be subject to U.S. tax as a resident after expatriation. For the minor exception: the person expatriated before age 18.5. If either exception applies, the person is not a covered expatriate under the net worth and average annual tax liability tests (but the certification test still applies; the person must certify tax compliance). Document the exception and the eligibility.

### Determine Long-Term Resident Status

The covered expatriate rules apply to U.S. citizens and to long-term residents (LTRs). An LTR is a green card holder who was a U.S. resident for at least 8 of the 15 years preceding termination of residency. When an LTR terminates their residency (by abandoning the green card), they may be a covered expatriate subject to the exit tax. The LTR definition is critical; a green card holder who held the card for fewer than 8 years is not an LTR and is not subject to the exit tax.

Determine long-term resident status. An LTR is a green card holder who was a U.S. resident for at least 8 of the 15 years preceding termination. Count the years of green card status (the years in which the person held the green card and was a U.S. resident). If the count is at least 8 years, the person is an LTR. When the LTR terminates their residency (abandons the green card), the covered expatriate tests apply. A green card holder with fewer than 8 years is not an LTR and is not subject to the exit tax. Track the years of green card status. The LTR definition includes years of actual residence, not just years of card holding; a green card holder who was a treaty resident of another country (under the tie-breaker rules) may not count those years.

### Report On Form 8854

A covered expatriate must file Form 8854 (Initial and Annual Expatriation Statement) for the year of expatriation and for subsequent years if they have deferred compensation or other specified items. The form reports the covered expatriate status, the net worth, the tax liability, the certification, and the exit tax computation (if applicable). The form is also required for non-covered expatriates (to certify compliance and confirm non-covered status). Failure to file Form 8854 can trigger penalties.

Ensure Form 8854 is filed. A covered expatriate files Form 8854 for the year of expatriation, reporting the covered status, the net worth, the tax liability, the certification, and the exit tax computation. A non-covered expatriate also files Form 8854 to certify compliance and confirm non-covered status. The form is required for all expatriating citizens and LTRs. Failure to file Form 8854 can trigger a 10,000 dollar penalty and can result in the person being treated as a covered expatriate (the IRS can presume covered status). File the form for the year of expatriation and for subsequent years if there are deferred compensation items or other specified items subject to the ongoing rules.

## Common Traps

### Overlooking The Net Worth Test's Worldwide Scope

The net worth test includes all worldwide assets, not just U.S. assets. A person with significant foreign assets may meet the test even if their U.S. assets are modest.

### Miscomputing The Average Annual Tax Liability

The average annual tax liability test uses the net federal income tax liability (after credits) for the 5 preceding years. Using gross tax or omitting credits misstates the average.

### Failing The Certification Test Due To Non-Compliance

The certification test requires 5 years of full tax compliance, including FBAR and Form 8938 reporting. Unreported foreign accounts or unfiled returns fail the certification. Remedy non-compliance before expatriating.

### Overlooking The Dual Citizen And Minor Exceptions

The dual citizen and minor exceptions can relieve the net worth and tax liability tests. Check the eligibility before concluding covered expatriate status.

### Miscounting The Years For Long-Term Resident Status

An LTR must have been a U.S. resident for at least 8 of the 15 preceding years. Count the years of green card status and actual residence. Treaty-resident years may not count.

### Not Filing Form 8854

Form 8854 is required for all expatriating citizens and LTRs, covered or not. Failure to file triggers a 10,000 dollar penalty and can result in presumed covered status.

### Assuming The Exit Tax Applies Only To The Wealthy

The certification test can make a person a covered expatriate regardless of net worth or income. A non-compliant person with modest assets can be a covered expatriate.
