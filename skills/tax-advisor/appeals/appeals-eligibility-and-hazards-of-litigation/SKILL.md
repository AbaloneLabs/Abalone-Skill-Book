# Appeals Eligibility And Hazards Of Litigation

IRS Independent Appeals is the primary administrative settlement forum between examination and court, and the threshold judgment is whether a dispute is eligible for Appeals and what settlement value the hazards of litigation create. The judgment problem is that Appeals is not a court and not a continuation of the audit; it is a separate, independent organization whose settlement authority is anchored to the litigation hazards—the probability that the IRS would lose if the case went to trial. Misjudging eligibility means either missing the Appeals window entirely or pursuing a conference that Appeals will reject, and misjudging the hazards of litigation means settling for far more or far less than the case is worth.

Agents tend to treat Appeals as a guaranteed second look or, conversely, as a rubber stamp for the examiner. They miss that Appeals has specific eligibility requirements (a written protest, a disputed amount that is not too small, a matter that is not excluded), that certain issues are not Appeals-stipendable (such as matters designated for litigation or constitutional challenges), and that the settlement range is driven by an objective assessment of litigation risk rather than by sympathy or fairness. The harm is a protest that is rejected for procedural reasons, a settlement that concedes a strong position, or a failure to quantify the hazards that would have justified a far better outcome.

This skill covers Appeals eligibility, the protest requirements, the small-case and simplified procedures, and the role of the hazards of litigation in driving Appeals settlement authority in U.S. federal practice. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; Appeals eligibility and settlement depend on the specific dispute, the amounts, and the procedural posture, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations. Appeals procedures and thresholds are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Procedural Prerequisites For Appeals Eligibility

A matter reaches Appeals after the examiner issues a proposed adjustment and the taxpayer files a timely written protest (or requests a small-case conference). The protest must be in writing, must be signed under penalties of perjury, must identify the taxpayer and the disputed adjustments, must state the facts and law supporting the taxpayer's position, and must be filed within the time stated in the examiner's letter (commonly 30 days for a 30-day letter). For certain small disputes, the taxpayer can request a small-case (formerly SFR-equivalent and Appeals small-case) conference with an informal written statement rather than a formal protest. Missing the protest deadline or failing to meet the formal requirements can forfeit Appeals.

Confirm each procedural prerequisite before relying on Appeals. Verify the type of letter issued (a 30-day letter versus a notice of deficiency), the protest deadline, the signature and perjury requirements, and whether the dispute qualifies for small-case treatment. A protest that is late, unsigned, or substantively deficient will be returned or treated as a waiver, and the taxpayer may be left with the statutory notice of deficiency and the Tax Court as the only path. The procedural gateway is unforgiving, and it must be cleared first.

### Determine Whether The Issue Is Excluded From Appeals

Not every disputed issue is eligible for Appeals. Matters designated for litigation by Chief Counsel, constitutional challenges to the tax law, issues that have been specifically excluded by management, and certain collection due process matters have different or restricted paths. Some issues are subject to mandatory referral or technical advice. Additionally, Appeals will not consider issues that have already been litigated to a final decision between the same parties, or issues that are moot.

Screen the disputed issues for exclusions before investing in a protest. If the issue is designated for litigation or is a constitutional challenge, Appeals is not the forum and the strategy must shift. Identifying an exclusion early prevents wasted effort and allows the taxpayer to pursue the correct path, whether that is litigation, a technical advice request, or another procedure.

### Quantify The Hazards Of Litigation As The Settlement Driver

The core of Appeals settlement is the hazards of litigation: the probability, expressed as a percentage, that the IRS would lose if the case were tried. Appeals Officers are authorized to settle based on the litigating hazards, meaning that if the taxpayer has a 60 percent chance of winning at trial, Appeals may concede 60 percent of the adjustment. The hazards assessment is objective and evidence-based, drawing on the facts, the legal authorities, the burden of proof, and the credibility of the parties. A taxpayer who cannot articulate the hazards will settle on the examiner's terms.

Develop an explicit hazards-of-litigation analysis for each disputed issue. List the strongest authorities for both sides, assess the burden of proof, evaluate the factual record, and assign a realistic probability of success. Present this analysis to Appeals as the basis for settlement, because Appeals is institutionally designed to respond to a well-supported hazards argument. A taxpayer who says "this is unfair" gets nowhere; a taxpayer who says "we have a 70 percent probability under Revenue Ruling X and the burden is on the IRS" engages Appeals on its own terms.

### Identify Whether Small-Case Or Simplified Procedures Apply

For smaller disputes, simplified procedures reduce the burden of the Appeals process. A small-case request is available for individual income tax disputes where the proposed additional tax is below a threshold (commonly $25,000 for all tax periods combined), allowing an informal conference without a formal written protest. The Tax Court also has a small tax case procedure (S case) for deficiencies under $50,000 for individuals and $10,000 for entities in certain matters, which is informal and whose decision is not precedential. These procedures trade formality and precedential value for speed and lower cost.

Determine whether the dispute qualifies for small-case or simplified treatment and weigh the tradeoffs. Small-case procedures are efficient for low-stakes disputes but waive the right to a precedential decision and to normal appellate review. For a matter where setting precedent matters or where the amount is near the threshold, the regular procedure may be preferable even though it is more burdensome. The choice should be deliberate, not default.

### Assess The Effect Of Penalties And Interest On The Settlement Range

The settlement range is not just the tax; it includes penalties and accrued interest, which can substantially increase the total. Appeals can settle penalties on the same hazards basis as tax, and the accuracy-related or fraud penalties may be the most negotiable part of the adjustment because the hazards on penalty elements (especially willfulness) often favor the taxpayer. Interest is generally not negotiable on a hazards basis, but the timing of the settlement affects how much interest accrues.

Model the full settlement range including tax, penalties, and interest, and identify which components are most susceptible to hazards-based concession. A settlement that concedes the tax but removes a 20 percent accuracy-related penalty can be a strong outcome, and the penalty concession may be driven by hazards that are easier to establish than the underlying tax position. Do not treat the adjustment as a single number; decompose it and negotiate the components where the hazards are strongest.

### Recognize The Limits Of Appeals Authority And When It Runs Out

Appeals settlement authority is broad but not unlimited. Appeals Officers operate within delegations of authority that cap the concession they can make without higher approval, and certain large or novel issues may require technical or management consultation that slows the process. Appeals also does not have authority over matters that require a statutory change, regulatory action, or a published guidance process. Finally, the Appeals process has a finite lifespan: if settlement fails, Appeals issues a notice of deficiency (or a statutory notice of deficiency for collection matters) and the taxpayer must petition the Tax Court within 90 days or pay and sue for refund.

Understand where Appeals authority ends and what the fallback is. If the hazards analysis supports a settlement beyond the Appeals Officer's delegation, the process will require escalation and time. If settlement fails entirely, the taxpayer must be prepared to litigate, and the 90-day window for petitioning the Tax Court after a notice of deficiency is a hard deadline that cannot be missed. Appeals is a settlement forum, not a permanent shelter, and the strategy must account for the litigation fallback.

## Common Traps

### Missing The Protest Deadline Or Formal Requirements

The symptom is assuming the 30-day letter's deadline is flexible. The trap is that a late or deficient protest forfeits Appeals and leaves the taxpayer with only the notice of deficiency and the Tax Court, narrowing the options and increasing the stakes. The direction is to calendar the protest deadline, confirm the formal requirements (written, signed, perjury statement, facts and law), and file before the deadline expires.

### Treating Appeals As A Sympathy Forum

The symptom is arguing to the Appeals Officer that the adjustment is unfair or burdensome. The trap is that Appeals settles on hazards of litigation, not on fairness or hardship, and a sympathy argument does not move the settlement range. The direction is to present an objective hazards-of-litigation analysis with authorities and probabilities, which is the currency Appeals uses.

### Failing To Quantify The Litigation Hazards

The symptom is asserting that the taxpayer has a strong case without assigning a probability. The trap is that without a quantified hazards assessment, Appeals defaults to its own view of the risk, which is often more favorable to the IRS, and the taxpayer settles for more than the hazards justify. The direction is to develop an explicit, evidence-based probability for each issue and present it as the settlement basis.

### Pursuing An Excluded Or Ineligible Issue Through Appeals

The symptom is protesting a constitutional challenge or a designated-litigation matter to Appeals. The trap is that Appeals will reject the issue, wasting the protest period and potentially missing the deadline for the correct forum. The direction is to screen issues for exclusions before protesting and to route ineligible issues to the correct procedure.

### Overlooking Penalty Hazards As The Most Negotiable Component

The symptom is focusing the Appeals negotiation on the tax and treating penalties as fixed. The trap is that penalty elements, especially willfulness and reasonable cause, often carry hazards favorable to the taxpayer, and failing to argue penalty hazards leaves the most negotiable component on the table. The direction is to decompose the adjustment and argue the hazards of each penalty component.

### Forgetting The 90-Day Tax Court Window If Appeals Fails

The symptom is letting the Appeals process run without preparing for the litigation fallback. The trap is that if Appeals fails, the notice of deficiency starts a 90-day clock to petition the Tax Court (150 days if abroad), and missing it forecloses prepayment review and forces a refund suit. The direction is to prepare the Tax Court petition in parallel and calendar the 90-day deadline before the notice issues.

### Defaulting To Small-Case Procedure Without Weighing Precedent

The symptom is choosing small-case treatment because it is easier. The trap is that small-case decisions are non-precedential and have limited appellate review, which can matter if the issue is recurring or if the taxpayer wants to establish a principle. The direction is to weigh the efficiency of small-case against the loss of precedential value and appellate rights before electing it.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have the procedural prerequisites for Appeals been confirmed, including the letter type, the protest deadline, the written-and-signed requirement, and the perjury statement?
- [ ] Has the disputed issue been screened for Appeals exclusions (designated litigation, constitutional challenges, previously litigated matters)?
- [ ] Has an explicit hazards-of-litigation analysis been developed for each issue, with authorities, burden of proof, and a quantified probability of success?
- [ ] Has the eligibility for small-case or simplified procedures been assessed, with the tradeoff of efficiency versus precedential value weighed?
- [ ] Has the full settlement range (tax, penalties, and interest) been modeled, with the most negotiable components identified based on hazards?
- [ ] Has the limit of Appeals authority and the fallback path (notice of deficiency and the 90-day Tax Court window) been understood and prepared for?
- [ ] Has the protest been structured to present the hazards analysis objectively rather than as a sympathy or fairness argument?
- [ ] Has the 90-day Tax Court petition deadline been calendared so that the litigation fallback is not missed if Appeals fails?
