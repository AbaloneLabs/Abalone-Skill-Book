# Settlement And Concession Strategy

A tax controversy settlement is not a single negotiation about a single number; it is a portfolio decision about which issues to concede, which to contest, and how to package the concessions to extract the maximum value on the contested issues. The judgment problem is that every issue is connected: conceding a weak issue builds credibility and settlement momentum that can be spent on a strong issue, but conceding too much or too early collapses the leverage needed to win the issues that matter. The art is in the sequencing and packaging—deciding what to give up in order to buy the best outcome on what is worth fighting for.

Agents tend to negotiate issue by issue without a portfolio view, or to adopt an all-or-nothing posture that concedes nothing and therefore settles nothing. They miss that Appeals settlement is driven by the aggregate hazards across all issues, that credibility is a tradable asset, that the order of concessions shapes the government's perception of the case, and that a settlement is a package deal whose value exceeds the sum of its parts. The harm is a settlement that concedes the strong issues to protect the weak ones, a failure to use credibility strategically, or an impasse that forces expensive litigation on a case that could have settled favorably.

This skill covers concession-and-contest strategy, settlement packaging, credibility management, and the negotiation dynamics of tax controversy settlement in U.S. federal practice. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; settlement strategy depends on the specific facts, the hazards, and the taxpayer's risk tolerance, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations. Settlement practices are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time.

## Core Rules

### Develop An Issue-By-Issue Hazards Assessment Before Negotiating

The foundation of settlement strategy is a hazards assessment for each disputed issue, independent of the others. For each adjustment, list the taxpayer's strongest authorities, the government's strongest authorities, the burden of proof, the quality of the factual record, and a realistic probability of success if litigated. Some issues will be strong (high probability of taxpayer victory), some will be weak (low probability), and some will be marginal (roughly even). This assessment is the inventory from which the settlement is built.

Complete the issue-by-issue hazards assessment before any negotiation, because the strategy depends on knowing which issues are worth fighting for and which are liabilities. A taxpayer who negotiates without this map will overvalue weak issues and undervalue strong ones, and will be unable to package concessions intelligently. The hazards assessment is not a guess; it is a disciplined evaluation that should be defensible if challenged, and it should be revisited as the record develops.

### Classify Each Issue As Concede, Contest, Or Marginal

Once the hazards are assessed, classify each issue into one of three categories. Concede the issues where the hazards strongly favor the government and the taxpayer has no realistic path to victory; contesting these burns credibility and settlement capital for no return. Contest the issues where the hazards favor the taxpayer or are genuinely in play; these are where the settlement value is created. Hold the marginal issues as negotiation currency—issues that can be conceded in exchange for concessions on the contested issues, or that can be litigated if the settlement fails.

Apply the classification rigorously and resist the urge to contest everything. A taxpayer who contests clearly losing positions signals weakness and lack of judgment, and the government discounts the entire case. Conversely, a taxpayer who concedes the losers and fights the winners signals credibility and focuses the negotiation on the issues that matter. The classification is the strategic backbone, and it should drive every offer and counteroffer.

### Use Concessions On Weak Issues To Build Credibility And Leverage

Credibility is the most valuable asset in settlement negotiation, and it is built by conceding positions that the government expects to win. When a taxpayer concedes a weak issue early and candidly, the government takes the taxpayer's arguments on the strong issues more seriously, because the taxpayer has demonstrated judgment and good faith. This credibility dividend translates directly into better settlement terms on the contested issues, because the Appeals Officer or examiner recalibrates the overall hazards in the taxpayer's favor.

Plan the concessions as investments in credibility, not as losses. Concede the weakest issues first, frame the concession as a recognition of the government's stronger position, and pivot immediately to the strong issues where the credibility will be spent. Do not concede grudgingly or with caveats that undermine the good-faith signal. A clean, early concession on a loser buys more settlement value than fighting it and winning nothing.

### Package The Settlement As A Whole Rather Than Issue By Issue

Appeals settlement is fundamentally a package deal. The settlement value is the aggregate of the hazards across all issues, weighted by the dollar amounts, and the government evaluates the package as a whole. A settlement that concedes the weak issues and wins the strong issues can produce a net result far better than litigating everything, because litigation is binary (win or lose each issue) while settlement can capture the expected value across the portfolio. The package should be presented as a coherent whole with a single bottom-line number, supported by the issue-by-issue hazards.

Structure the settlement offer as a package with a clear aggregate figure and a hazards rationale for each component. Avoid negotiating each issue to a separate conclusion before addressing the others, because that forecloses the ability to trade across issues. The government is often more flexible on a package than on individual issues, because the package lets it accept concessions on some issues in exchange for giving ground on others. Present the package, explain the hazards logic, and negotiate the aggregate.

### Identify The Issues Worth Litigating And Protect Them

Not every issue should be settled, and protecting the right to litigate the strongest issues is a strategic imperative. Some issues have precedent value, some involve amounts too large to settle on unfavorable terms, and some have hazards so favorable that settlement would leave significant value on the table. For these issues, the settlement must preserve the litigation option, either by excluding them from the package or by setting a floor below which the taxpayer will litigate rather than settle.

Identify the litigation-worthy issues in advance and set a reservation price for each—the worst settlement outcome the taxpayer will accept before choosing litigation. Protect these issues in the negotiation by making clear that they are non-negotiable below the reservation price, and be prepared to litigate if the government will not meet it. Conflating settle-able issues with litigate-able issues leads to settling the strong cases on weak terms.

### Manage The Timing And Sequencing Of Offers

The order in which offers and concessions are made shapes the negotiation's trajectory. Opening with a maximalist position on every issue, including the losers, anchors the negotiation high but costs credibility. Opening with a concession on the losers and a strong position on the winners signals seriousness and focuses the negotiation. Subsequent offers should be paced to demonstrate movement without collapsing too quickly, because rapid capitulation signals that the opening position was insincere and invites the government to hold firm.

Sequence the offers to tell a coherent story: concede the losers early, hold the winners firmly, and use the marginal issues as the negotiation moves. Each offer should reflect a deliberate recalibration, not panic or fatigue. The timing of the final offer matters too: an offer made too early may leave value on the table, while one made too late may force litigation. Pace the negotiation toward a resolution that captures the package value before the deadlines force a binary choice.

### Evaluate The Total Cost Of Settlement Versus Litigation

The settlement decision is not purely about the tax dollars; it includes the cost, time, and risk of litigation, the accrual of interest during the dispute, the disruption to the taxpayer, and the precedential consequences. Litigation is expensive (attorney fees, expert witnesses, discovery), slow (often years), and uncertain (the hazards are probabilities, not certainties). Settlement captures the expected value immediately, stops the interest clock, and provides finality. A settlement that is slightly worse than the expected litigation outcome may be preferable when the cost, time, and risk of litigation are included.

Quantify the full cost of litigation—including fees, interest, management time, and the risk discount—and compare it to the settlement value. A taxpayer who ignores litigation costs will reject favorable settlements in pursuit of a marginally better litigation outcome that, net of costs, is worse. Conversely, a taxpayer who overweights litigation costs will settle too cheaply. The comparison must be honest and comprehensive, and it should inform the reservation price.

## Common Traps

### Contesting Every Issue Including The Clear Losers

The symptom is refusing to concede any adjustment out of a desire to fight everything. The trap is that contesting clear losers burns credibility, signals lack of judgment, and causes the government to discount the taxpayer's position on the strong issues, leading to a worse overall settlement. The direction is to concede the losers early and candidly to build credibility for the contested issues.

### Settling The Strong Issues To Protect The Weak Ones

The symptom is conceding the high-hazards issues to hold ground on the low-hazards issues. The trap is that this inverts the value: the strong issues are where the settlement value is created, and giving them up to protect losers produces a net loss. The direction is to classify issues by hazards and protect the strong ones while conceding the weak.

### Negotiating Issue By Issue Without A Package View

The symptom is resolving each adjustment separately before moving to the next. The trap is that issue-by-issue negotiation forecloses cross-issue trading and prevents the package deal that captures the aggregate hazards value. The direction is to negotiate the settlement as a package with a single bottom line, using the issue-by-issue hazards as support.

### Failing To Set A Reservation Price And Settling Too Cheaply

The symptom is accepting the government's final offer without a pre-set floor. The trap is that without a reservation price based on the litigation alternative, the taxpayer has no objective basis to reject a bad offer and may settle for less than the case is worth. The direction is to set a reservation price for each litigate-able issue based on the expected litigation outcome net of costs.

### Overlooking Interest Accrual In The Settlement Calculus

The symptom is comparing the settlement tax to the litigated tax without accounting for interest. The trap is that interest accrues during the dispute and can substantially increase the liability, so a settlement that stops the interest clock may be worth more than it appears, while prolonged litigation compounds the cost. The direction is to model interest accrual in the settlement-versus-litigation comparison.

### Collapsing Too Quickly And Signaling Insincerity

The symptom is moving rapidly from a maximalist opening to a near-final offer. The trap is that rapid capitulation signals the opening was insincere and invites the government to hold firm for more, eroding the settlement value. The direction is to pace offers deliberately, with each movement reflecting a recalibration, to preserve credibility and negotiating room.

### Forgetting That Marginal Issues Are Negotiation Currency

The symptom is either conceding marginal issues for free or litigating them to the bitter end. The trap is that marginal issues are the currency of trade—they can be conceded to extract value on stronger issues or held as fallbacks—and mishandling them wastes a strategic asset. The direction is to classify marginal issues explicitly and deploy them as trade material in the package negotiation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has an issue-by-issue hazards assessment been completed for every disputed adjustment before negotiation, with authorities, burden of proof, and a quantified probability?
- [ ] Has each issue been classified as concede, contest, or marginal, with the weak issues identified for early concession?
- [ ] Has a credibility strategy been developed that concedes the losers early and candidly to build leverage for the contested issues?
- [ ] Has the settlement been structured as a package with a single aggregate bottom line, rather than negotiated issue by issue?
- [ ] Have the litigation-worthy issues been identified with reservation prices below which the taxpayer will litigate rather than settle?
- [ ] Has the offer sequencing been planned to tell a coherent story, with deliberate pacing that avoids signaling insincerity?
- [ ] Has the full cost of litigation (fees, interest, time, risk discount) been quantified and compared to the settlement value to set an honest reservation price?
- [ ] Have the marginal issues been reserved as negotiation currency rather than conceded for free or litigated wastefully?
