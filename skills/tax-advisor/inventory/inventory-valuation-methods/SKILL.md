# Inventory Valuation Methods

The inventory valuation method is a foundational accounting election that determines which costs flow to cost of goods sold (COGS) and which remain on the balance sheet, and in a period of changing prices the choice materially shifts taxable income between years. The judgment problem is that the method is not freely changeable year to year, that LIFO carries a conformity requirement that reaches the financial statements, and that the choice interacts with the lower-of-cost-or-market rule and with the small-business exemption that may excuse a taxpayer from keeping formal inventory altogether. An agent who picks FIFO by default, or who treats the method as a minor bookkeeping detail, misses a significant tax-planning lever and may create a conformity violation.

Agents frequently default to FIFO without quantifying the LIFO benefit in a rising-price environment, apply specific identification where it is not genuinely available (because the goods are fungible), or fail to recognize that the lower-of-cost-or-market write-down must be applied consistently and is not a free year-end adjustment. They misunderstand the TCJA small-business exemption, which allows certain taxpayers under the gross receipts threshold to treat inventory as non-incidental materials and supplies or to use a simplified method, conflating it with a license to ignore inventory. The harm is an inconsistent method that the IRS challenges, a conformity violation on LIFO, or a missed deduction opportunity.

This skill covers FIFO, LIFO, specific identification, lower of cost or market (or net realizable value), and the small-business inventory exemption, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the method choice depends on the taxpayer's facts, the price environment, and the elections made, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Dollar thresholds are 2024 figures and are indexed annually.

## Core Rules

### Choose Among FIFO LIFO And Specific Identification Deliberately

The principal inventory costing methods are first-in first-out (FIFO), last-in first-out (LIFO), and specific identification. FIFO assumes the earliest goods purchased are the first sold, so ending inventory reflects the most recent (usually higher) costs and COGS reflects older (usually lower) costs; in a rising-price environment, FIFO produces higher taxable income. LIFO assumes the most recent goods purchased are the first sold, so COGS reflects the most recent (higher) costs and ending inventory reflects older (lower) costs; in a rising-price environment, LIFO produces lower taxable income and a tax deferral (the LIFO reserve). Specific identification tracks the actual cost of each identifiable item and is generally available only for goods that are not fungible, such as automobiles, jewelry, or art, where each unit is distinct.

Select the method based on the price environment, the taxpayer's cash-flow preference, and the conformity constraints. In a rising-price environment, LIFO can defer significant tax for a taxpayer with growing inventory, but it requires the LIFO conformity rule (see below) and adds complexity (index calculations, pooling). FIFO is simpler and matches the physical flow for most goods, but it produces higher income in inflation. Specific identification is unavailable for fungible goods (a retailer cannot pick which identical shirts were sold), so do not apply it where the goods are interchangeable. Confirm the method is permissible for the taxpayer's inventory type before electing.

### Apply The LIFO Conformity Requirement And Form 970

If a taxpayer elects LIFO for tax purposes, the taxpayer must also use LIFO for financial reporting purposes (the LIFO conformity rule). This means the financial statements presented to shareholders, partners, beneficiaries, or creditors must reflect LIFO, not FIFO, for the same inventory. The conformity rule is strict and a violation can cause the loss of the LIFO election. The election is made on Form 970 (Application to Use LIFO Inventory Method) for the first year, and the LIFO pools and indexing method must be specified.

Confirm the taxpayer can comply with LIFO conformity before electing, because the financial statements are affected and lenders or investors may require FIFO presentation (which would violate conformity). The conformity rule has limited exceptions (certain internal reports and certain disclosures), but the external financial statements must use LIFO. Track the LIFO reserve (the difference between FIFO and LIFO inventory) because it grows in a rising-price environment and represents a deferred tax liability that reverses when inventory is liquidated. A LIFO liquidation (drawing down inventory below prior-year layers) can trigger a large income spike as old, low-cost layers are released into COGS, so manage inventory levels to avoid unintended liquidations.

### Apply Lower Of Cost Or Market Or Net Realizable Value Consistently

Regardless of the costing method (FIFO, LIFO, specific identification), inventory must be valued at the lower of cost or market (LCM), or under the TCJA simplification, lower of cost or net realizable value (NRV) for eligible taxpayers. Market generally means replacement cost, with a ceiling (NRV) and a floor (NRV minus a normal profit margin). If market (or NRV) is below cost, the inventory is written down to the lower value, and the write-down increases COGS (or reduces ending inventory), increasing the current deduction. The method must be applied consistently from year to year.

Apply the lower-of-cost-or-market (or NRV) test at year-end to each item (or category, depending on the method), and write down inventory that has declined in value, such as obsolete, damaged, or slow-moving goods. The write-down is a legitimate deduction, but it must be supported by evidence of the decline (replacement cost, obsolescence, market conditions), not by a desire to reduce income. Once an item is written down, it establishes a new cost basis for that item, and subsequent recovery is recognized as income in the later year (the item cannot simply be marked back up without recognition). Apply the test consistently each year, because inconsistent application is a method change requiring Form 3115.

### Evaluate The Small-Business Gross Receipts Exemption

Under the TCJA, a taxpayer with average annual gross receipts of 29 million dollars or less (for the three prior tax years, 2024 threshold, indexed) is exempt from Section 471 if the taxpayer's principal business activity is not tax-exempt. Such a taxpayer is not required to keep inventories or use the accrual method for inventory, and may treat inventory as non-incidental materials and supplies (deducted in the year sold or paid, depending on the method) or use a simplified method that conforms to the taxpayer's accounting method. This exemption can simplify recordkeeping and allow cash-method treatment of inventory for qualifying small businesses.

Confirm the taxpayer's average gross receipts against the 29 million dollar threshold (2024) before applying the exemption, and recognize that the exemption is elective in the sense that the taxpayer can still keep formal inventory if preferred. The exemption does not eliminate the need to track inventory for operational purposes, and it interacts with Section 263A (UNICAP) and the overall accounting method. A taxpayer near the threshold must monitor gross receipts, because crossing the threshold in a future year requires a method change to resume formal inventory. Do not treat the exemption as a license to ignore inventory valuation; the chosen treatment must still clearly reflect income and be applied consistently.

### Recognize How The Method Choice Affects COGS And Taxable Income

In a period of rising prices, FIFO produces lower COGS (older, cheaper costs flow to COGS) and higher taxable income, while LIFO produces higher COGS (newer, more expensive costs flow to COGS) and lower taxable income. The difference is the LIFO reserve, which accumulates over time and represents a tax deferral. The choice is not neutral: over the life of the business, total income is the same (all costs eventually flow through), but the timing differs, and timing has real cash-flow value. The method also affects the balance sheet (LIFO inventory is understated relative to current cost) and financial ratios.

Model the COGS and income difference before electing. For a taxpayer with 1,000,000 dollars of inventory and a 200,000 dollar LIFO reserve, LIFO defers tax on 200,000 dollars of income compared to FIFO, a significant cash-flow benefit at a 21 percent corporate rate (roughly 42,000 dollars deferred). However, the deferral reverses on LIFO liquidation, so the taxpayer must manage inventory levels. The method choice is long-term because changing it later requires a Form 3115 method change (with a Section 481(a) adjustment), and LIFO cannot be adopted and abandoned casually. Confirm the multi-year impact and the conformity burden before committing.

### Maintain The Method Consistently And Change Only Via Form 3115

Once an inventory method is adopted, it must be applied consistently from year to year. A change in the method (such as from FIFO to LIFO, or a change in the LIFO pooling or indexing method, or a change in the treatment of the small-business exemption) is a change in accounting method that requires Form 3115 and a Section 481(a) adjustment to prevent duplication or omission of income. An error in applying the existing method (such as a math mistake) is corrected as an error, not a method change, but a change in the underlying approach is a method change.

Distinguish between an error correction (amended return or adjustment within the same method) and a method change (Form 3115 with a Section 481(a) adjustment). See the dedicated skill on inventory method changes for the detailed rules. Do not change the method informally or apply it inconsistently, because the IRS treats inconsistent application as an unauthorized method change subject to adjustment and potential penalties. Document the method adopted and apply it consistently, and if a change is desired, file Form 3115 and compute the adjustment.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To FIFO Without Quantifying The LIFO Benefit

In a rising-price environment, LIFO can defer significant tax. Defaulting to FIFO because it is simpler forfeits the deferral. Model the LIFO reserve and the tax difference before defaulting.

### Applying Specific Identification To Fungible Goods

Specific identification requires the goods to be individually identifiable (cars, jewelry, art). Applying it to fungible goods (identical retail items) is not permitted, because the taxpayer cannot genuinely track which unit was sold.

### Violating The LIFO Conformity Rule

LIFO for tax requires LIFO for financial reporting. Presenting FIFO financial statements to shareholders or creditors while using LIFO for tax violates conformity and can cause loss of the LIFO election.

### Triggering A LIFO Liquidation Income Spike

Drawing inventory below prior-year LIFO layers releases old, low-cost inventory into COGS, creating a large income spike. Failing to manage inventory levels to avoid unintended liquidations causes a surprise tax bill.

### Misunderstanding The Small-Business Exemption As Ignoring Inventory

The TCJA exemption allows simplified treatment for qualifying small businesses, but it does not eliminate inventory tracking or the requirement to clearly reflect income. Treating it as a license to ignore inventory misstates income.

### Inconsistent Application Of Lower Of Cost Or Market

The LCM or NRV write-down must be applied consistently each year. Applying it only in years when it reduces income, or marking inventory back up inconsistently, is an unauthorized method change.

### Changing The Method Without Form 3115

A change in inventory method (FIFO to LIFO, pooling change, exemption treatment) requires Form 3115 and a Section 481(a) adjustment. Changing the method informally or inconsistently is an unauthorized method change subject to adjustment.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the inventory costing method (FIFO, LIFO, or specific identification) been selected deliberately based on the price environment, cash-flow preference, and conformity constraints, rather than by default?
- [ ] If LIFO is elected, has Form 970 been filed, the LIFO pools and indexing method specified, and the LIFO conformity requirement (LIFO for both tax and financial reporting) been confirmed as satisfiable?
- [ ] Has the lower-of-cost-or-market (or net realizable value) test been applied consistently at year-end to write down obsolete, damaged, or declined-value inventory, with the write-down supported by evidence?
- [ ] Has the small-business gross receipts exemption (average annual gross receipts of 29 million dollars or less for 2024) been evaluated, recognizing it allows simplified treatment but does not eliminate inventory tracking or the clear-reflection-of-income requirement?
- [ ] Has the COGS and taxable income difference between methods been modeled (such as the LIFO reserve and the deferred tax), recognizing the choice is long-term and affects cash flow?
- [ ] Has the method been applied consistently from year to year, with any change handled as an accounting method change via Form 3115 with a Section 481(a) adjustment rather than an informal switch?
- [ ] For LIFO, has the risk of LIFO liquidation (releasing old low-cost layers into COGS) been managed by monitoring inventory levels, and has the LIFO reserve been tracked as a deferred tax liability?
- [ ] Has specific identification been confirmed as available only for non-fungible, individually identifiable goods, and not applied to interchangeable retail inventory?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal inventory valuation information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
