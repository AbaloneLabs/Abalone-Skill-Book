# COGS Calculation

Cost of goods sold (COGS) is the mechanism by which a business deducts the cost of inventory only when the inventory is sold, matching the deduction to the related revenue. The judgment problem is that COGS is not simply purchases minus what is left: it must include the direct costs and, for producers and certain resellers, the indirect costs that Section 263A (UNICAP) requires to be capitalized, and it must exclude costs that are currently deductible. An agent who treats COGS as purchases alone, or who deducts indirect production costs currently when UNICAP requires capitalization, misstates income and creates an audit exposure.

Agents frequently omit the beginning and ending inventory reconciliation, deduct indirect costs (such as factory overhead or storage) currently when they must be capitalized under UNICAP, or fail to apply the small-business exemption that excuses many taxpayers from the full UNICAP burden. They confuse the producer rules (which capitalize a broad set of direct and indirect costs) with the reseller rules (which capitalize a narrower set), and they mishandle freight-in, storage, and handling. The harm is an understated COGS (overstated income) when required capitalization is missed, or an overstated COGS (understated income) when deductible costs are improperly capitalized, and in either case an audit adjustment.

This skill covers the COGS formula, the Section 263A UNICAP capitalization rules for producers and resellers, the small-business exemption, and the classification of includible versus deductible costs, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; COGS depends on the taxpayer's method, activities, and costs, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Dollar thresholds are 2024 figures and are indexed annually.

## Core Rules

### Apply The COGS Formula With Beginning And Ending Inventory

The COGS formula is beginning inventory plus purchases plus additional costs (such as freight-in and, for producers, direct and indirect production costs) minus ending inventory. Beginning inventory is the prior year's ending inventory, and ending inventory is determined by a physical count (or a permissible estimation method) valued under the taxpayer's inventory method (FIFO, LIFO, specific identification, at lower of cost or market). The formula ensures that the cost of unsold inventory is deferred (on the balance sheet) and recognized only when sold.

Reconcile beginning inventory to the prior year's ending inventory, because a mismatch (such as a prior-year adjustment not carried forward) understates or overstates COGS. Confirm the ending inventory is supported by a physical count or a permissible method, and valued under the elected method. A common error is using purchases alone as the deduction, ignoring the inventory reconciliation, which misstates income whenever inventory levels change. If inventory grew during the year, purchases overstate COGS (because some goods remain unsold); if inventory shrank, purchases understate COGS. Run the full formula, not a purchases shortcut.

### Capitalize Direct And Indirect Costs Under Section 263A UNICAP

Section 263A (the Uniform Capitalization Rules, UNICAP) requires producers of property and resellers of property to capitalize the direct costs and an allocable share of the indirect costs of producing or acquiring the property, into the inventory basis (and thus into COGS when sold), rather than deducting them currently. For producers, the capitalized indirect costs are broad and include factory overhead such as depreciation of production equipment, utilities for the factory, production supervisory salaries, quality control, and certain administrative costs allocable to production. For resellers, the capitalized indirect costs are narrower and include primarily the costs of purchasing, handling, and storage (such as inbound freight, inspecting, and warehousing of goods on hand), but exclude marketing and general administrative costs.

Identify whether the taxpayer is a producer (manufactures, builds, constructs, or grows property) or a reseller (buys and sells finished goods), because the capitalization scope differs. For a producer, the UNICAP capitalization is extensive and captures most production-related overhead; deducting factory overhead currently when UNICAP requires capitalization understates COGS and overstates income, and the IRS adjusts this on audit. For a reseller, the capitalization is narrower but still includes purchasing, handling, and storage costs that must be allocated to ending inventory rather than deducted. Apply the appropriate cost classification and allocation method (a reasonable allocation such as the simplified production or resale method is available), and capitalize the required costs.

### Apply The Small-Business Exemption From UNICAP

A taxpayer with average annual gross receipts of 29 million dollars or less (for the three prior tax years, 2024 threshold, indexed) is exempt from the Section 263A UNICAP rules for resale property (and, separately, for producers of property that are not required to use UNICAP under the small-producer rules, which historically had a 1 million dollar threshold, though the reseller exemption at 29 million dollars is the principal relief). This exemption allows qualifying small resellers to deduct purchasing, handling, and storage costs currently rather than capitalizing them into inventory, simplifying the COGS calculation significantly.

Confirm the taxpayer's average gross receipts against the 29 million dollar threshold (2024) before applying the exemption. For a qualifying small reseller, the COGS calculation is simplified because fewer indirect costs must be capitalized, and the taxpayer can deduct the purchasing and handling costs currently. A taxpayer near the threshold must monitor gross receipts, because crossing the threshold requires capitalization to begin (a method change). The exemption does not apply to producers in the same way (producers have a separate, smaller threshold historically), so confirm the taxpayer's activity type before relying on the exemption. Do not assume UNICAP never applies; verify the threshold and the activity.

### Classify Freight-In And Storage Costs Correctly

Freight-in (the cost of shipping goods to the taxpayer's place of business) is a direct cost of acquiring inventory and must be capitalized into inventory (and thus into COGS when sold) under both the general inventory rules and UNICAP, regardless of the small-business exemption for other indirect costs. Freight-out (the cost of shipping goods to customers) is a selling expense and is currently deductible, not part of COGS. Storage costs differ between producers and resellers: for producers, storage of raw materials and work in process is capitalized under UNICAP; for resellers, storage of goods on hand is capitalized under UNICAP unless the small-business exemption applies, while storage of finished goods awaiting sale may follow the reseller rules.

Distinguish freight-in (capitalized) from freight-out (deducted), because misclassifying freight-out as part of COGS overstates COGS and understates income. For storage, apply the producer or reseller rule based on the activity, and apply the small-business exemption for resellers where available. A producer who deducts storage of raw materials currently when UNICAP requires capitalization understates COGS and overstates income. Confirm the cost classification for each freight and storage line item, because the rules are specific and the audit exposure is real.

### Identify What Costs Are Currently Deductible Outside COGS

Costs that are not directly or indirectly attributable to producing or acquiring inventory are currently deductible and are not part of COGS. These include selling expenses (marketing, sales commissions, advertising, freight-out), general and administrative expenses (officer compensation, office rent, accounting, legal), research and experimental expenditures (subject to the post-TCJA Section 174 capitalization rules), and interest (generally deductible under Section 163, though production-period interest may be capitalized for producers). The distinction matters because currently deductible costs reduce income immediately, while capitalized costs reduce income only when the inventory is sold.

Classify each cost as either capitalized into inventory (direct and required indirect costs under UNICAP) or currently deductible (selling, G&A, and other non-inventory costs). A common error is capitalizing general administrative costs into inventory under an overbroad reading of UNICAP, which overstates COGS and understates income; UNICAP captures production-related and acquisition-related indirect costs, not all overhead. Conversely, deducting a required UNICAP indirect cost currently understates COGS and overstates income. Apply the classification consistently, and note that the post-TCJA Section 174 rules require capitalization and amortization of research and experimental expenditures, which is separate from the UNICAP inventory capitalization.

### Reconcile COGS To The Tax Return And The Books

COGS is reported on the tax return (Schedule C for sole proprietors, Form 1125-A for corporations and partnerships) with the beginning inventory, purchases, cost of labor and other costs, and ending inventory lines. The COGS must reconcile to the book inventory and the physical count, and any book-to-tax differences (such as UNICAP adjustments or different costing methods) must be documented. A reconciliation that does not tie is a red flag on audit and often indicates a missing inventory adjustment or an inconsistent method.

Reconcile the tax COGS to the book COGS and the physical count, and document any adjustments (UNICAP add-backs, method differences, lower-of-cost-or-market write-downs). The beginning inventory on the return must equal the prior year's ending inventory, and the ending inventory must be supported by the count and the valuation method. If the books and the tax return differ, the difference must be explained and supported, because an unexplained variance is an audit target. Maintain the reconciliation as part of the return workpapers.

## Common Traps

### Using Purchases Alone Without The Inventory Reconciliation

COGS requires beginning inventory plus purchases plus costs minus ending inventory. Using purchases alone ignores the inventory change and misstates income whenever inventory levels move.

### Deducting UNICAP Indirect Costs Currently

Section 263A requires capitalization of direct and indirect production (and reseller acquisition) costs into inventory. Deducting them currently understates COGS and overstates income, and the IRS adjusts this.

### Missing The Small-Business UNICAP Exemption

Resellers under the 29 million dollar gross receipts threshold (2024) are exempt from UNICAP for resale property. Failing to apply the exemption over-capitalizes costs and understates current deductions.

### Misclassifying Freight-In And Freight-Out

Freight-in (to the taxpayer) is capitalized into inventory; freight-out (to customers) is a selling expense deducted currently. Treating freight-out as COGS overstates COGS and understates income.

### Capitalizing General Administrative Costs Under An Overbroad UNICAP Reading

UNICAP captures production-related and acquisition-related indirect costs, not all overhead. Capitalizing general G&A into inventory overstates COGS and understates income.

### Failing To Reconcile Beginning Inventory To Prior Year Ending

Beginning inventory must equal the prior year's ending inventory. A mismatch from an un-carried-forward adjustment misstates COGS and is an audit red flag.

### Confusing Producer And Reseller UNICAP Scope

Producers capitalize a broad set of production overhead; resellers capitalize a narrower set of acquisition and handling costs. Applying the wrong scope misstates capitalization.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has COGS been computed using the full formula (beginning inventory plus purchases plus additional costs minus ending inventory), reconciling beginning inventory to the prior year's ending inventory?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer been classified as a producer or a reseller for Section 263A UNICAP purposes, and have the direct costs and the allocable indirect costs (production overhead for producers, purchasing and handling for resellers) been capitalized into inventory?
- [ ] Has the small-business UNICAP exemption (average annual gross receipts of 29 million dollars or less for 2024 for resellers) been evaluated and applied where the taxpayer qualifies?
- [ ] Have freight-in costs been capitalized into inventory and freight-out costs been deducted as selling expenses, with the distinction applied correctly?
- [ ] Have storage costs been classified correctly (capitalized for producers' raw materials and work in process; capitalized for resellers' goods on hand unless the small-business exemption applies)?
- [ ] Have selling, general, and administrative expenses been currently deducted rather than capitalized into inventory, recognizing UNICAP captures only production-related and acquisition-related indirect costs?
- [ ] Has the COGS on the tax return (Schedule C or Form 1125-A) been reconciled to the book inventory and the physical count, with any book-to-tax adjustments (UNICAP, method differences, write-downs) documented?
- [ ] Has the ending inventory been valued under the elected method (FIFO, LIFO, specific identification, lower of cost or market) and supported by a physical count or permissible estimation method?
