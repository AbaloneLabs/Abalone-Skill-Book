# Depreciation Recapture Basics

Depreciation recapture is the mechanism that converts what looks like a capital gain on the sale of depreciable business property back into ordinary income, to the extent the gain is attributable to depreciation previously claimed (or allowable). The judgment problem is that the character of the gain, and thus the tax rate, depends on the asset class and the depreciation history, not on the holding period alone. An agent who treats the sale of business equipment or a rental building as a straightforward capital gain, without separating the recapture portion, understates the tax and mischaracterizes the income. The recapture rules are asset-specific: Section 1245 (personal property) recaptures all depreciation as ordinary income; Section 1250 (real property) recaptures only accelerated depreciation as ordinary, with the straight-line depreciation generally taxed as unrecaptured Section 1250 gain at a maximum 25 percent rate.

Agents frequently conflate the two sections, apply the 25 percent cap to equipment (which should be full ordinary recapture), or forget that depreciation allowed or allowable, not just claimed, is recaptured. They miss the Section 1231 netting that determines whether the excess gain is capital or ordinary, and they mishandle home office depreciation, which is subject to recapture even though the home is a personal residence. The harm is a misstated tax liability, an incorrect Form 4797, and a surprise ordinary-income tax bill on what the seller expected to be a capital gain.

This skill covers Section 1245 and Section 1250 depreciation recapture, Section 1231 netting, unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, home office recapture, and installment sale recapture, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; recapture depends on the asset, the depreciation history, and the transaction structure, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply Section 1245 Full Ordinary Recapture To Personal Property

Section 1245 property is depreciable personal property, including machinery, equipment, vehicles, furniture, fixtures, computers, software, and certain intangible assets subject to amortization. On the sale of Section 1245 property, the gain is recaptured as ordinary income to the extent of the depreciation allowed or allowable, regardless of the depreciation method used and regardless of the holding period. The recapture is full, not capped: every dollar of gain attributable to depreciation is ordinary income, taxed at ordinary rates up to 37 percent. Only the gain in excess of the total depreciation, if any, is Section 1231 gain (potentially long-term capital gain).

Compute the recapture as the lesser of the total depreciation (allowed or allowable) or the total gain. For equipment bought for 100,000 dollars, depreciated by 60,000 dollars (adjusted basis 40,000 dollars), and sold for 90,000 dollars, the 50,000 dollar gain is fully recaptured as ordinary income because the depreciation (60,000 dollars) exceeds the gain. If sold for 150,000 dollars, the 110,000 dollar gain is 60,000 dollars ordinary recapture plus 50,000 dollars Section 1231 gain. The recapture is limited to the gain, so a sale at a loss produces no recapture (the loss is Section 1231). Confirm the asset is Section 1245 (personal property), not Section 1250 (real property), because the recapture rules differ sharply.

### Apply Section 1250 Limited Recapture To Real Property

Section 1250 property is depreciable real property, including commercial buildings, residential rental buildings, and structural components. Under current law, where buildings are depreciated straight-line under MACRS, there is generally no ordinary recapture of the straight-line depreciation. Instead, the depreciation is taxed as unrecaptured Section 1250 gain at a maximum federal rate of 25 percent (the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain rate), provided the property was held more than one year. Ordinary income recapture under Section 1250 applies only to accelerated depreciation in excess of straight-line, which is rare for post-1986 MACRS property but can arise for pre-1987 property or for non-MACRS methods.

Compute the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain as the lesser of the depreciation (allowed or allowable) or the gain, taxed at a maximum 25 percent. For a rental building bought for 300,000 dollars (excluding land), depreciated by 80,000 dollars, and sold for 400,000 dollars, the 180,000 dollar gain consists of 80,000 dollars unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (at up to 25 percent) and 100,000 dollars Section 1231 capital gain (at the long-term capital gains rate). Do not apply full ordinary recapture (Section 1245 rules) to a building; the cap at 25 percent for unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is the key distinction. Confirm the depreciation method, because accelerated depreciation on pre-1987 property can trigger ordinary recapture of the excess.

### Net Section 1231 Gains And Losses For Character Determination

Section 1231 property includes depreciable business property and real property used in a trade or business, held more than one year. After applying Section 1245 or Section 1250 recapture to the depreciation portion, the remaining gain (or any loss) is Section 1231 gain or loss. Section 1231 gains and losses for the year are netted together: if the net result is a gain, the net gain is treated as long-term capital gain (favorable); if the net result is a loss, the net loss is treated as ordinary loss (favorable, because it offsets ordinary income without the capital loss limitations). This netting is mandatory and determines the character.

Net all Section 1231 transactions for the year before characterizing the result. A sale producing 50,000 dollars of Section 1231 gain and another producing 20,000 dollars of Section 1231 loss nets to 30,000 dollars of long-term capital gain. If the net were a loss, it would be ordinary. Apply the five-year lookback rule: any net Section 1231 gain in the current year is recharacterized as ordinary income to the extent of ordinary Section 1231 losses deducted in the prior five years. This lookback prevents taxpayers from alternating gains and losses to get the best of both characters, so check the prior five years before concluding the current gain is capital.

### Handle Home Office Depreciation Recapture Specifically

A taxpayer who claims a home office deduction depreciates the business-use portion of the home, and that depreciation is subject to recapture when the home is sold, even though the home is a personal residence. The home office depreciation allowed or allowable after May 6, 1997 is recaptured as unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (at up to 25 percent), separate from the Section 121 exclusion on the sale of a principal residence. The Section 121 exclusion (up to 250,000 dollars single, 500,000 dollars married filing jointly) does not apply to the depreciation recapture portion; that portion is taxed at the 25 percent rate regardless of the exclusion.

Track the cumulative home office depreciation claimed, because it is taxed at 25 percent on sale even if the rest of the gain is excluded under Section 121. A taxpayer who excluded the full gain without recapturing the depreciation understates the tax. Confirm whether the home office was a separate structure (different rules) or within the dwelling unit, and apply the recapture to the depreciation after May 6, 1997. The gain attributable to the home office is not separately excluded; only the depreciation recapture is carved out and taxed.

### Apply Recapture In Installment Sales Of Depreciable Property

In an installment sale (where the seller receives payments over more than one year), depreciation recapture must be reported in the year of sale, not spread with the installment payments. Section 1245 and the ordinary recapture portion of Section 1250 are reported as ordinary income in full in the year of sale, regardless of how much cash was received. The remaining gain (Section 1231 or unrecaptured Section 1250 gain) is reported under the installment method as payments are received. This front-loads the ordinary recapture and spreads the capital portion.

Compute the recapture portion (full ordinary under Section 1245, or the accelerated portion under Section 1250) and report it in the year of sale, then apply the installment method to the remaining gain. For an equipment sale with 60,000 dollars of Section 1245 recapture and 50,000 dollars of Section 1231 gain, paid over three years, the 60,000 dollars is ordinary income in year one, and the 50,000 dollars is reported proportionally as payments arrive. Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (the 25 percent portion) is generally eligible for the installment method, but the ordinary recapture portion is not. Confirm the installment reporting on Form 6252 and the recapture on Form 4797, because front-loading the recapture can create a cash mismatch (tax due before all cash is received).

### Report Recapture On Form 4797 With The Correct Characterization

Depreciation recapture is reported on Form 4797 (Sale of Business Property), not Schedule D. Section 1245 recapture goes on Form 4797 Part III, which computes the ordinary income recapture and the Section 1231 excess gain. Section 1250 recapture and unrecaptured Section 1250 gain are reported on Form 4797 Part III as well, with the unrecaptured portion tracked for the 25 percent rate on the Schedule D worksheet. The ordinary recapture flows to Form 4797 Part II as ordinary income; the Section 1231 gain flows to Part I for netting.

Ensure each asset sale is reported on the correct part of Form 4797 with the recapture computed and the character separated. Do not report the full gain on Schedule D as capital gain; the recapture portion is ordinary income on Form 4797 and is taxed at ordinary rates (up to 37 percent), significantly higher than the long-term capital gains rate. Reconcile the Form 4797 reporting with the depreciation schedule and the asset list, and use the Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain Worksheet to compute the 25 percent portion for real property.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Building Sale As Full Capital Gain

A rental or commercial building sale includes unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (the depreciation) taxed at up to 25 percent, separate from the Section 1231 capital gain. Reporting the full gain on Schedule D understates the tax on the depreciation portion.

### Applying The 25 Percent Cap To Equipment

Section 1245 (personal property) recaptures all depreciation as ordinary income, with no 25 percent cap. The 25 percent cap applies only to unrecaptured Section 1250 gain on real property. Applying the cap to equipment understates the ordinary recapture.

### Recapturing Only Depreciation Claimed, Not Allowable

Recapture is based on depreciation allowed or allowable. A taxpayer who failed to claim depreciation still faces recapture on the amount they should have claimed, so the basis must be reduced by allowable depreciation even if not deducted.

### Ignoring The Section 1231 Netting And Lookback

The Section 1231 excess gain is netted with other Section 1231 transactions, and the five-year lookback recharacterizes current net gain as ordinary to the extent of prior ordinary losses. Treating the excess as automatic capital gain without netting misstates the character.

### Forgetting Home Office Depreciation Recapture

Home office depreciation after May 6, 1997 is recaptured at 25 percent on sale of the residence, separate from the Section 121 exclusion. Excluding the full gain without recapturing the depreciation understates the tax.

### Spreading Ordinary Recapture Over An Installment Sale

Section 1245 ordinary recapture (and the ordinary portion of Section 1250) is reported in full in the year of sale, not spread with installment payments. Spreading it understates the current-year ordinary income and overstates future-year gain.

### Reporting Recapture On Schedule D Instead Of Form 4797

Recapture is reported on Form 4797, with the ordinary portion in Part II and the Section 1231 gain in Part I. Reporting on Schedule D misses the ordinary recapture and understates tax.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has each asset been classified as Section 1245 (personal property, full ordinary recapture) or Section 1250 (real property, limited recapture with unrecaptured Section 1250 gain at up to 25 percent)?
- [ ] For Section 1245 property, has the ordinary recapture been computed as the lesser of total depreciation (allowed or allowable) or total gain, recognizing the full depreciation is recaptured as ordinary income to the extent of gain?
- [ ] For Section 1250 property, has the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain been computed as the lesser of depreciation or gain and taxed at the maximum 25 percent rate, with any ordinary recapture limited to accelerated depreciation in excess of straight-line?
- [ ] Has the Section 1231 excess gain (above recapture) been netted with other Section 1231 transactions for the year, and has the five-year lookback rule been applied to recharacterize net gain as ordinary to the extent of prior ordinary losses?
- [ ] For a principal residence with a home office, has the depreciation after May 6, 1997 been recaptured at 25 percent, separate from and not excluded by the Section 121 exclusion?
- [ ] In an installment sale, has the Section 1245 ordinary recapture (and ordinary Section 1250 recapture) been reported in full in the year of sale, with only the Section 1231 or unrecaptured Section 1250 gain spread under the installment method?
- [ ] Is the recapture reported on Form 4797 Part III with the ordinary portion flowing to Part II and the Section 1231 gain to Part I, and not on Schedule D as capital gain?
- [ ] Has the recapture analysis been reconciled with the depreciation schedule, the asset list, and the gain computation, and has the interaction with Section 179 and bonus depreciation (which increase recapture) been considered?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal depreciation recapture information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
