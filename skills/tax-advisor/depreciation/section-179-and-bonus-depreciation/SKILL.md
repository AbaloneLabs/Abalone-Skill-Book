# Section 179 and Bonus Depreciation

Section 179 expensing and bonus depreciation (additional first-year depreciation) are the two principal vehicles for accelerating the depreciation of business property into the year of acquisition, but they operate by different rules and are chosen for different reasons. The judgment problem is that they are not interchangeable: Section 179 is an affirmative election with an annual dollar limit, a phaseout threshold, a business income limitation that prevents it from creating or increasing a loss, and per-asset selection; bonus depreciation is automatic (applied unless elected out) by class, can create or increase a loss, applies to used property (post-TCJA), and has a percentage that is phasing down. An agent who treats them as equivalent, or who applies one without considering the other, misstates the deduction and forfeits optimization.

Agents frequently apply Section 179 to the maximum without checking the business income limitation, apply bonus depreciation to property categories that are excluded (such as structures and certain intangibles), or fail to recognize that bonus depreciation can create a loss while Section 179 cannot. They forget that the bonus percentage is declining (60 percent for 2024, 40 percent for 2025, 20 percent for 2026, and zero for 2027 unless extended), so applying a stale percentage overstates the deduction. The harm is an overstated current deduction, an audit adjustment, and a missed opportunity to sequence the two provisions to maximize after-tax benefit across years.

This skill covers Section 179 expensing and bonus depreciation, their limits and exclusions, the business income limitation, and the choice and sequencing between them, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the application depends on the property, the taxpayer's income, and the elections made, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Dollar amounts and percentages are 2024 figures and change annually.

## Core Rules

### Apply Section 179 With Its Annual Limit, Phaseout, And Business Income Ceiling

Section 179 allows a taxpayer to elect to expense the cost of qualifying Section 1245 tangible personal property (and certain other property such as qualified real property improvements) in the year placed in service, up to an annual dollar limit. For 2024 the maximum deduction is approximately 1,220,000 dollars, and the phaseout begins when total Section 179 property placed in service during the year exceeds approximately 3,050,000 dollars, reducing the limit dollar for dollar (so the limit reaches zero at approximately 4,270,000 dollars of placements). The deduction is also limited to the aggregate net taxable income from the taxpayer's active trades or businesses; Section 179 cannot create or increase a net loss, and any amount disallowed by the income limit carries forward to future years.

Confirm the annual limit and the phaseout threshold for the tax year, because both are indexed for inflation and change annually. Then confirm the business income limitation: if the taxpayer has a net operating loss before the Section 179 deduction, or if the Section 179 amount would push the business into a loss, the excess is not deductible currently and carries forward. This is the key distinction from bonus depreciation, which can create a loss. Track the carryforward of disallowed Section 179, because it is not lost but is deducted in future years subject to the same income limit. The election is made per asset, so the taxpayer can selectively expense some assets and depreciate others.

### Apply Bonus Depreciation With Its Declining Percentage And Its Automatic Application

Bonus depreciation (additional first-year depreciation under Section 168(k)) allows a percentage of the basis of qualified property to be deducted in the first year, on top of the regular MACRS depreciation on the remainder. The percentage is declining under the TCJA schedule: 60 percent for property placed in service in 2024, 40 percent for 2025, 20 percent for 2026, and zero for 2027 (unless extended by legislation). Unlike Section 179, bonus depreciation applies automatically to all qualified property in a class unless the taxpayer affirmatively elects out by class, and it can create or increase a net operating loss.

Apply the correct percentage for the placed-in-service year, because the declining schedule is a frequent source of error. Confirm the property is qualified: tangible personal property with a recovery period of 20 years or less, computer software, water utility property, and qualified improvement property, acquired by purchase (not self-constructed, with special rules for the construction start date) after September 27, 2017. Post-TCJA, used property qualifies if it is the taxpayer's first use of the property. The election out is made by asset class on Form 4562 and is irrevocable without consent, so model the multi-year impact before electing out. Because bonus can create a loss, it is often preferable to Section 179 for a taxpayer who wants the maximum current deduction regardless of income.

### Distinguish The Property Each Provision Covers

Section 179 applies to tangible personal property that is Section 1245 property, plus qualified real property (qualified improvement property, and certain leasehold, restaurant, and retail improvements historically). It does not apply to land, buildings (other than qualified real property improvements), intangible assets other than off-the-shelf computer software, and property used outside the United States or in certain other excluded categories. Bonus depreciation applies to a broader set: tangible personal property with a recovery period of 20 years or less, qualified improvement property, water utility property, and computer software, including used property acquired by purchase.

Confirm the property qualifies under the specific provision before applying it. A building itself does not qualify for either Section 179 or bonus depreciation, but qualified improvement property (interior improvements to nonresidential real property after the building is placed in service) qualifies for both. Land never qualifies. Intangible assets such as goodwill and going concern generally do not qualify for either, though acquired intangibles amortize over 15 years separately. Misclassifying property as qualified overstates the deduction; for example, treating a roof or structural component as qualified improvement property when it is a structural improvement that does not qualify.

### Sequence Section 179 Before Bonus Depreciation And Model The Interaction

When both provisions apply to the same property, the taxpayer claims Section 179 first, then bonus depreciation on the remaining basis, then regular MACRS depreciation on the remainder. The sequencing matters because Section 179 is elective and limited, while bonus is automatic. A common optimization is to use Section 179 up to the income limit (since it cannot create a loss), then let bonus depreciation apply to the rest (since it can create a loss that may be carried back or forward as a net operating loss).

Model the combined effect. For a taxpayer with 500,000 dollars of qualified property and 300,000 dollars of business income, claiming 300,000 dollars of Section 179 (limited to income) plus 60 percent bonus on the remaining 200,000 dollars (120,000 dollars) produces a 420,000 dollar deduction, with the remaining 80,000 dollars depreciated under MACRS. If the taxpayer instead claimed no Section 179 and let bonus apply to all 500,000 dollars (300,000 dollars bonus), the deduction is smaller in the current year but creates a loss that carries forward. The choice depends on whether the taxpayer wants to preserve income, create an NOL, or maximize the current deduction, so run both scenarios.

### Recognize The Listed Property And Luxury Auto Interactions

Both Section 179 and bonus depreciation are subject to the listed property rules (requiring more than 50 percent business use) and the passenger automobile annual dollar limitations (the luxury car caps). For a passenger automobile, the first-year cap for 2024 is approximately 12,220 dollars without bonus and approximately 20,400 dollars with bonus depreciation. Section 179 on a passenger automobile is also subject to the cap, so the large Section 179 limit does not translate to a large car deduction. Heavy SUVs and trucks over 6,000 pounds gross vehicle weight are subject to a higher Section 179 limit (approximately 30,500 dollars for 2024 for SUVs over 6,000 and under 14,000 pounds) and are not subject to the passenger automobile caps in the same way.

Apply the listed property 50 percent business-use test before claiming Section 179 or bonus on vehicles, computers, and other listed property, and apply the luxury auto caps to passenger automobiles. For a heavy SUV used 100 percent for business, Section 179 up to the SUV limit plus bonus on the remainder can produce a very large first-year deduction, which is why heavy vehicles favor the actual expense method. Confirm the gross vehicle weight rating and the business-use percentage, because a vehicle used 40 percent for business triggers mandatory ADS and no Section 179 or bonus.

### Document The Elections And Track The Carryforwards

Section 179 is elected on Form 4562 Part I by listing each asset and the amount expensed; the election is made per asset and is irrevocable without consent. Bonus depreciation is applied by default, and the election out is made by class on Form 4562. The disallowed Section 179 amount (from the income limitation) carries forward and must be tracked, because it is deducted in future years subject to the limit and the annual cap. The basis of each asset is reduced by the Section 179 and bonus amounts, which affects the depreciation in future years and the gain on disposition.

Maintain a schedule that tracks, for each asset, the original cost, the Section 179 amount, the bonus amount, the MACRS basis, the accumulated depreciation, and the adjusted basis, plus any Section 179 carryforward. The elections are irrevocable, so the documentation must support the choice made. On audit, the IRS verifies that the property qualified, that the limits and phaseouts were applied, and that the income limitation was respected, so the schedule is the primary defense.

## Common Traps

### Applying Section 179 Beyond The Business Income Limitation

Section 179 cannot create or increase a loss. Applying it when the taxpayer has insufficient business income overstates the deduction; the excess carries forward rather than being currently deductible.

### Using A Stale Bonus Depreciation Percentage

The bonus percentage declines: 60 percent for 2024, 40 percent for 2025, 20 percent for 2026. Applying 100 percent or the prior 80 percent overstates the deduction. Confirm the percentage for the placed-in-service year.

### Applying Bonus Depreciation To Excluded Property

Bonus does not apply to land, buildings (other than qualified improvement property), and certain intangibles. Treating a structure or goodwill as qualified property overstates the deduction.

### Treating Section 179 And Bonus As Interchangeable

Section 179 is elective, limited, income-capped, and per-asset; bonus is automatic, loss-creating, and by-class. Choosing the wrong one forfeits optimization, especially around the income limitation and NOL creation.

### Ignoring The Luxury Auto Caps For Vehicles

Passenger automobiles are subject to annual dollar limits regardless of Section 179 or bonus. Expensing a 60,000 dollar car under Section 179 is capped at approximately 12,220 dollars (or 20,400 with bonus) for 2024.

### Forgetting The Section 179 Carryforward

The amount disallowed by the income limitation carries forward to future years. Failing to track it loses the deduction in the year it becomes usable and misstates the return.

### Electing Out Of Bonus Without Modeling The Multi-Year Effect

The election out of bonus is by class and irrevocable. Electing out to smooth income without modeling the lost deduction and the alternative of creating an NOL can forfeit significant benefit.
