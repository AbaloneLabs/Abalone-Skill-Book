# MACRS Basics

MACRS (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System) is the mandatory depreciation system for most tangible property placed in service after 1986, and its rules determine the recovery period, the convention, and the depreciation method that govern how an asset's cost is recovered over time. The judgment problem is that these three variables are not freely chosen: the recovery period is fixed by asset class, the convention is dictated by the timing and concentration of acquisitions, and the method is generally prescribed by statute, with only narrow elections. An agent who treats MACRS as a menu of optional accelerations, or who applies the wrong recovery period or convention, misstates depreciation in every year of the asset's life and creates a recapture distortion on disposition.

Agents frequently default to straight-line over an assumed life without checking the asset class, apply the half-year convention when the mid-quarter convention is actually required by concentrated purchases, or fail to recognize when the Alternative Depreciation System (ADS) is mandatory rather than optional. They conflate MACRS with the elective Section 179 and bonus depreciation provisions, which sit on top of MACRS but do not replace its classification framework. The harm is a depreciation schedule that does not tie to the tax return, an audit adjustment, and downstream errors in basis, gain, and recapture calculations.

This skill covers the MACRS depreciation system, the General Depreciation System (GDS) versus the Alternative Depreciation System (ADS), recovery periods, conventions, and the Section 168 election, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; depreciation depends on the asset, the placed-in-service date, and the elections made, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Asset Into The Correct Recovery Period

The recovery period under MACRS is determined by the asset's class, not by the taxpayer's preference or the useful life. Personal property (tangible property other than real property) is assigned to a 3-, 5-, 7-, 10-, 15-, or 20-year class based on the Asset Depreciation Range and the class life in Rev. Proc. 87-56 and its successors. Common examples: automobiles, light trucks, and computers are 5-year property; office furniture and fixtures are 7-year property; equipment and machinery vary by class life; certain land improvements are 15-year property; and certain farm buildings and utility property are 20-year property. Real property is 27.5-year residential rental or 39-year nonresidential (commercial) real property under GDS.

Confirm the recovery period from the asset class before computing depreciation, because the wrong period misstates every year's deduction. A computer is 5-year property, not 3-year; office furniture is 7-year, not 5-year. For real property, distinguish residential rental (27.5 years) from nonresidential commercial (39 years), and note that a mixed-use building may require cost segregation to allocate components to shorter personal-property recovery periods. Use cost segregation only where defensible, because the IRS scrutinizes aggressive allocations of building components to short recovery periods. Document the asset class and the recovery period for each asset on the depreciation schedule.

### Apply The Correct Convention Based On Timing And Concentration

The convention determines how much depreciation is allowed in the placed-in-service year and the disposition year. For personal property, the half-year convention allows a half-year of depreciation in the first and last years regardless of when in the year the asset was placed in service. The mid-quarter convention is required if more than 40 percent of the total personal property placed in service during the year is placed in service during the last three months; it then applies to all personal property for the year, treating each asset as placed in service at the midpoint of its quarter. For real property, the mid-month convention always applies, treating the asset as placed in service at the midpoint of the month.

Compute the 40 percent threshold by aggregating all personal property placed in service during the year and comparing the last-three-month total to the full-year total. If the last quarter exceeds 40 percent, mid-quarter applies to everything, which generally reduces the first-year deduction for assets placed in service earlier in the year. This is a common trap when a taxpayer buys most of its equipment in October through December; the mid-quarter convention is mandatory and cannot be elected out. For real property, mid-month always applies, so a building placed in service in June gets 6.5 months of depreciation in the first year. Confirm the convention for each asset, because the wrong convention misstates the first-year and disposition-year depreciation.

### Choose Between GDS And ADS With The Statutory Defaults

The General Depreciation System (GDS) is the default and generally uses the 200 percent declining balance method for 3-, 5-, 7-, and 10-year property and the 150 percent declining balance for 15- and 20-year property, switching to straight-line when optimal, with the recovery periods described above. The Alternative Depreciation System (ADS) uses straight-line over longer recovery periods (the class life for personal property, 30 years for residential rental, 40 years for nonresidential real) and is generally less favorable. A taxpayer may elect ADS for any asset, but ADS is mandatory for certain property, including listed property used 50 percent or less for business, property used predominantly outside the United States, tax-exempt-use property, and property financed by tax-exempt bonds.

Apply GDS by default and elect ADS only where required or where the longer straight-line recovery is strategically preferred (for example, to smooth income or to conform to book depreciation). When ADS is mandatory, such as for a vehicle used 40 percent for business (below the 50 percent threshold for listed property), apply ADS straight-line and recognize that no bonus depreciation or Section 179 is available. Confirm whether the asset triggers mandatory ADS before defaulting to GDS. The election to use ADS is made per asset class and is filed with the return, and once made it is generally binding for the life of the asset.

### Recognize When The Straight-Line Election Or Section 168 Election Applies

Under GDS, a taxpayer may elect the straight-line method over the GDS recovery period instead of the declining balance method, which is useful when the taxpayer wants steady deductions or expects higher future marginal rates. This is an election under Section 168 and is made per asset class. The election is generally irrevocable for the asset once made. Additionally, the taxpayer may elect out of bonus depreciation (which applies automatically to qualified property) by class, which is relevant when the taxpayer prefers to spread depreciation rather than front-load it.

Evaluate whether straight-line over GDS or an election out of bonus depreciation is preferable based on the taxpayer's current and projected income and marginal rates. A startup with losses might prefer to elect out of bonus depreciation to preserve depreciation for profitable years (subject to the net operating loss rules), while a profitable business might want the maximum current deduction. The Section 168 elections are asset-class-specific and irrevocable, so model the multi-year impact before electing. Document the election on the return, because an election not properly made is treated as not made, and a missed election cannot be cured retroactively without a method change.

### Apply The Listed Property And Luxury Auto Limits For Passenger Automobiles

Passenger automobiles are 5-year property under MACRS but are also listed property subject to the annual dollar limitation on depreciation (the luxury car caps), regardless of the depreciation method, Section 179 election, or bonus depreciation claimed. The annual caps are adjusted for inflation; for 2024 the first-year cap is approximately 12,220 dollars (or approximately 20,400 dollars with bonus depreciation). The caps extend the recovery period beyond five years because the full depreciation is not reached within the standard MACRS schedule.

Apply the annual dollar limitation to any passenger automobile, and track the unrecovered basis that carries forward beyond the five-year MACRS schedule. A 60,000 dollar car used 100 percent for business cannot be fully depreciated in five years because the annual caps hold each year's deduction below the straight-line amount. Confirm the business-use percentage is above 50 percent to avoid mandatory ADS, and apply the cap after the percentage. Note that heavy SUVs and trucks over 6,000 pounds gross vehicle weight are not subject to the passenger automobile caps in the same way and may qualify for higher Section 179 deductions, so classify the vehicle correctly before applying the caps.

### Coordinate MACRS With Section 179 And Bonus Depreciation

MACRS provides the baseline depreciation system, but Section 179 expensing and bonus depreciation (additional first-year depreciation) sit on top of it and allow much larger first-year deductions. Section 179 allows immediate expensing of up to approximately 1,220,000 dollars (2024) of qualifying property, subject to a phaseout and a business income limitation. Bonus depreciation allows an additional percentage (60 percent for 2024, declining) of the basis of qualified property in the first year. Both reduce the basis subject to MACRS depreciation in subsequent years.

Apply Section 179 and bonus depreciation before computing the regular MACRS depreciation on the remaining basis, and recognize that these elections interact with the luxury auto caps and the listed property rules. The order of application matters: Section 179 first, then bonus depreciation, then MACRS on the remainder. Do not double-count by claiming MACRS on the full basis after expensing a portion under Section 179 or bonus. See the dedicated skills on Section 179 and bonus depreciation for the detailed rules, but understand that MACRS is the underlying framework that these provisions modify.

## Common Traps

### Using The Wrong Recovery Period Based On Assumed Useful Life

The recovery period is fixed by asset class, not by the taxpayer's view of useful life. A computer is 5-year property regardless of how long it is used. Defaulting to a different period misstates depreciation every year.

### Missing The Mandatory Mid-Quarter Convention

If more than 40 percent of personal property is placed in service in the last three months, mid-quarter applies to all personal property for the year. Missing this and using half-year overstates the first-year depreciation for early-year assets and understates it for late-year assets.

### Defaulting To GDS When ADS Is Mandatory

ADS is required for listed property used 50 percent or less for business, property used outside the United States, tax-exempt-use property, and certain other categories. Applying GDS to such property overstates depreciation and triggers audit adjustment.

### Conflating MACRS With Section 179 And Bonus Depreciation

Section 179 and bonus depreciation are elections on top of MACRS, not replacements for it. Claiming MACRS on the full basis after expensing part under Section 179 double-counts and overstates depreciation.

### Applying Straight-Line Without The Section 168 Election

Straight-line under GDS requires an affirmative election under Section 168, made per asset class. Using straight-line without the election is not allowed, and the default declining balance applies.

### Ignoring The Luxury Auto Caps For Passenger Automobiles

Passenger automobiles are subject to annual dollar limits regardless of method or election. Depreciating a car on the straight 5-year MACRS schedule without the caps overstates the deduction and ignores the basis that carries forward.

### Failing To Use Cost Segregation Where Defensible

A commercial building depreciated entirely over 39 years may have components (fixtures, specialized wiring) that qualify for shorter personal-property recovery periods. Failing to segregate where defensible understates current depreciation, though aggressive segregation is audit-sensitive.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has each asset been classified into the correct MACRS recovery period (3, 5, 7, 10, 15, or 20-year personal property, or 27.5-year residential or 39-year commercial real property) based on its asset class?
- [ ] Has the correct convention been applied, including the mandatory mid-quarter convention if more than 40 percent of personal property was placed in service in the last three months, and the mid-month convention for real property?
- [ ] Has GDS been applied as the default, with ADS used only where elected or where mandatory (listed property at 50 percent or less business use, foreign-use, tax-exempt-use, tax-exempt-bond-financed property)?
- [ ] Has the depreciation method (200 percent declining balance for 3-10-year property, 150 percent for 15-20-year, straight-line for real property) been applied, with any straight-line election under Section 168 properly made per asset class?
- [ ] For passenger automobiles, have the annual luxury auto dollar limitations been applied after the business-use percentage, with the unrecovered basis tracked for carryforward beyond the five-year MACRS schedule?
- [ ] Has MACRS been coordinated with Section 179 and bonus depreciation, applying those elections first and computing MACRS only on the remaining basis, with no double-counting?
- [ ] Has the placed-in-service date been confirmed, since it determines the first-year convention and the year depreciation begins, distinct from the purchase or payment date?
- [ ] Has the depreciation schedule been reconciled to the tax return (Form 4562) and the asset list, with the recovery period, convention, and method documented for each asset?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal MACRS depreciation information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
