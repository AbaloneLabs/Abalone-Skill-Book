# CFC Shareholder Rules

A controlled foreign corporation (CFC) is a foreign corporation that is more than 50% owned (by vote or value) by U.S. shareholders. CFC status triggers the anti-deferral regimes (Subpart F and GILTI) and the reporting requirements (Form 5471). Determining whether a foreign corporation is a CFC requires applying the ownership tests, the constructive ownership and attribution rules, and the U.S. shareholder definition. An agent who misapplies the ownership tests or overlooks constructive ownership will misclassify the corporation and miss the anti-deferral and reporting requirements.

Agents commonly miss that CFC status requires more than 50% U.S. shareholder ownership (by vote or value), that a U.S. shareholder is a 10% owner (by vote, and for certain purposes by value), that constructive ownership attributes ownership from related parties, and that Form 5471 has multiple filer categories with different requirements. The harm is a U.S. shareholder who fails to report the CFC income or the Form 5471 and faces penalties.

This skill covers CFC shareholder rules under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the ownership and attribution rules are complex, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The CFC Ownership Test

A foreign corporation is a CFC if more than 50% of its stock (by vote or by value) is owned by U.S. shareholders. A U.S. shareholder is a U.S. person who owns 10% or more of the total combined voting power of the corporation's stock (directly, indirectly, or constructively). The 50% test is applied to the U.S. shareholders' combined ownership. If the U.S. shareholders collectively own more than 50% by vote or by value, the corporation is a CFC.

Apply the CFC ownership test. Identify the U.S. shareholders (U.S. persons owning 10% or more of the voting stock, directly, indirectly, or constructively). Sum the U.S. shareholders' ownership by vote and by value. If the combined ownership exceeds 50% by vote or by value, the corporation is a CFC. The vote test and the value test are alternative; either one makes it a CFC. For example, if U.S. shareholders own 45% of the vote but 55% of the value, the corporation is a CFC (the value test is met). Track the ownership by both vote and value. The CFC status is determined annually and can change from year to year.

### Apply The U.S. Shareholder 10% Test

A U.S. shareholder is a U.S. person who owns 10% or more of the total combined voting power of the CFC's stock. The 10% test is applied to voting power (not value) for the Subpart F and GILTI purposes. However, for the CFC determination itself (the 50% test), the value test also applies (a U.S. shareholder for the value test includes U.S. persons owning 10% or more of the value). The 10% test is applied to direct, indirect, and constructive ownership.

Apply the U.S. shareholder 10% test. A U.S. shareholder is a U.S. person owning 10% or more of the voting stock (directly, indirectly, or constructively). For the CFC determination (the 50% test), both the vote and value tests apply, and the 10% threshold is applied to each. A U.S. person owning less than 10% is not a U.S. shareholder and is not counted toward the 50% test (and is not subject to Subpart F or GILTI). The 10% test is critical: a foreign corporation with 100 U.S. shareholders each owning 0.5% (total 50% U.S. ownership) is not a CFC, because no individual U.S. person owns 10% or more. Track the ownership percentages and apply the 10% test.

### Apply Constructive Ownership And Attribution Rules

Ownership is not limited to direct ownership. Constructive ownership attributes ownership from related parties under the attribution rules of Section 318 and the related rules. A U.S. person is treated as owning the stock owned by their spouse, children, and certain other family members (family attribution). A U.S. person is treated as owning the stock owned by a partnership, estate, or trust in proportion to their interest (entity attribution). A U.S. corporation is treated as owning the stock owned by another corporation in the same chain (corporate attribution, subject to certain limitations).

Apply the constructive ownership and attribution rules. A U.S. person is treated as owning stock owned by related parties. Family attribution: a U.S. person owns stock owned by their spouse, children, parents, and certain other family members. Entity attribution: a U.S. person owns stock owned by a partnership, estate, or trust in proportion to their interest. Corporate attribution: a U.S. corporation owns stock owned by a related corporation (subject to limitations). The attribution can cause a U.S. person to be a U.S. shareholder even if they do not directly own 10% or more. For example, a U.S. person who owns 6% directly and whose foreign spouse owns 6% is treated as owning 12% (through family attribution) and is a U.S. shareholder. Trace the ownership through the attribution rules.

### Identify The Form 5471 Filer Categories

Form 5471 has multiple filer categories, each with different reporting requirements. Category 1 filers are U.S. shareholders of a CFC (replaced by Category 4 and Category 5 under current rules). Category 2 filers are U.S. citizens or residents who are officers or director of a foreign corporation (related to acquisitions). Category 3 filers are U.S. persons who acquire stock in a foreign corporation. Category 4 filers are U.S. shareholders of a CFC who are in control of the CFC (own more than 50%). Category 5 filers are U.S. shareholders of a CFC who own 10% or more (but are not necessarily in control). Each category has specific schedules and reporting.

Identify the Form 5471 filer category. Category 4 filers are U.S. shareholders who own more than 50% of the CFC (the controlling shareholders). Category 5 filers are U.S. shareholders who own 10% or more of a CFC (the non-controlling U.S. shareholders). Category 3 filers are U.S. persons who acquire stock in a foreign corporation that becomes a CFC or who acquire sufficient stock to trigger reporting. Category 2 filers are related to acquisitions of foreign corporation stock by U.S. persons. Each category requires different schedules and information. Determine the filer category based on the ownership and the transaction. Failure to file the correct category can trigger penalties and incomplete reporting.

### Report The CFC Income And Ownership On Form 5471

Form 5471 reports the CFC's financial information, the U.S. shareholder's ownership, and the anti-deferral inclusions (Subpart F and GILTI). The form includes schedules for the CFC's income statement, balance sheet, the U.S. shareholders' ownership, the Subpart F income, and the GILTI computation. The form is required for each CFC and each U.S. shareholder in a filer category. The form is attached to the U.S. shareholder's tax return.

Ensure Form 5471 is filed for each CFC and each U.S. shareholder in a filer category. The form reports the CFC's financial information, the ownership, the Subpart F income, and the GILTI computation. The form is detailed and requires the CFC's income statement, balance sheet, and the ownership information. Failure to file Form 5471 can trigger significant penalties (10,000 dollars or more per form, per year) and extend the statute of limitations for the entire return. The form is required for Category 4 and Category 5 filers (U.S. shareholders of a CFC). Track the filing requirement and ensure the form is complete and timely.

### Handle The Coordination With Subpart F And GILTI

The CFC shareholder rules are the gateway to the anti-deferral regimes. Once a foreign corporation is a CFC and the U.S. shareholder is identified, the Subpart F and GILTI inclusions apply. The Subpart F inclusion (the U.S. shareholder's share of the CFC's Subpart F income) and the GILTI inclusion (the U.S. shareholder's share of the CFC's income above the 10% QBAI return) are computed and reported on the U.S. shareholder's tax return, based on the CFC ownership. The coordination ensures the CFC income is subject to current U.S. tax under the correct regime.

Coordinate with Subpart F and GILTI. Once the CFC and the U.S. shareholder are identified, the Subpart F and GILTI inclusions apply. The Subpart F inclusion is the U.S. shareholder's share of the CFC's Subpart F income (FPHCI, FBCSI, FBCSVI). The GILTI inclusion is the U.S. shareholder's share of the CFC's tested income above the 10% QBAI return. The inclusions are reported on the U.S. shareholder's tax return (as deemed dividends or GILTI inclusions). The CFC shareholder rules determine who is subject to the inclusions and at what percentage. Track the ownership and the inclusions to ensure the correct U.S. tax is computed.

## Common Traps

### Applying Only The Vote Test For CFC Status

CFC status can be triggered by either the vote test or the value test. A foreign corporation with 45% U.S. vote but 55% U.S. value is a CFC. Apply both tests.

### Overlooking Constructive Ownership And Attribution

Ownership includes constructive ownership from related parties (family, entity, corporate attribution). A U.S. person can be a U.S. shareholder through attribution even without direct 10% ownership.

### Counting Sub-10% U.S. Owners Toward The 50% Test

Only U.S. shareholders (10% or more) are counted toward the 50% test. A foreign corporation with 100 U.S. persons each owning 0.5% is not a CFC, because no individual owns 10% or more.

### Failing To File The Correct Form 5471 Category

Form 5471 has multiple filer categories (Category 2, 3, 4, 5). Each has different requirements. Filing the wrong category results in incomplete reporting and potential penalties.

### Not Filing Form 5471 At All

Form 5471 is required for U.S. shareholders of a CFC. Failure to file triggers significant penalties (10,000 dollars or more per form) and extends the statute of limitations for the entire return.

### Forgetting The CFC Status Can Change Annually

CFC status is determined annually based on the ownership. A foreign corporation can be a CFC in one year and not in another. Reassess the status each year.

### Overlooking The Coordination With Subpart F And GILTI

The CFC shareholder rules are the gateway to Subpart F and GILTI. Once the CFC and the U.S. shareholder are identified, the anti-deferral inclusions apply. Coordinate the regimes.
