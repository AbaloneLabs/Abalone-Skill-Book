# Subpart F Income

Subpart F of the U.S. tax code requires U.S. shareholders of a controlled foreign corporation (CFC) to include certain types of CFC income in their U.S. taxable income currently, even if the income is not distributed. This anti-deferral regime prevents U.S. taxpayers from accumulating passive or mobile income in a foreign corporation to defer U.S. tax. Subpart F income includes foreign personal holding company income (dividends, interest, royalties), foreign base company sales income, and foreign base company services income. An agent who overlooks Subpart F, or who treats the CFC income as deferred until distributed, will understate the U.S. tax liability.

Agents commonly miss that Subpart F applies to U.S. shareholders of a CFC (not all shareholders), that the inclusion is current (not deferred), that the income categories have specific definitions, and that the inclusion is a deemed dividend reported on Form 5471. The harm is a U.S. shareholder who does not report Subpart F income and faces penalties and back taxes.

This skill covers Subpart F income under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the rules are complex and depend on the CFC's activities and ownership, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Subpart F Income Categories

Subpart F income includes several categories of income earned by a CFC. Foreign personal holding company income (FPHCI) includes dividends, interest, royalties, rents, annuities, and gains from the sale of property producing these types of income. Foreign base company sales income (FBCSI) is income from the sale of property purchased from or sold to a related party, where the property is manufactured and sold for use outside the CFC's country. Foreign base company services income (FBCSVI) is income from services performed for or on behalf of a related party, outside the CFC's country.

Identify the Subpart F income categories. FPHCI is passive income (dividends, interest, royalties, rents, annuities, gains). FBCSI is sales income from related-party purchases or sales where the property is used outside the CFC's country (a base company structure where the CFC acts as a middleman). FBCSVI is services income performed for a related party outside the CFC's country. For each category, determine whether the CFC's income qualifies. The categories target income that is easily moved to a low-tax jurisdiction (passive income and mobile sales and services income). Active business income (manufacturing in the CFC's country, selling to unrelated parties) is generally not Subpart F income. Review the CFC's income by category.

### Apply The Inclusion Rule For U.S. Shareholders

Subpart F requires U.S. shareholders of a CFC to include their share of the CFC's Subpart F income in their U.S. taxable income currently, as a deemed dividend. A U.S. shareholder is a U.S. person who owns 10% or more of the CFC's voting stock (the 10% threshold, applied to direct, indirect, and constructive ownership). The inclusion is the U.S. shareholder's pro rata share of the CFC's Subpart F income, based on their ownership percentage. The inclusion is reported as a deemed dividend, regardless of whether the CFC distributes cash.

Apply the inclusion rule. Identify the U.S. shareholders (U.S. persons owning 10% or more of the voting stock, directly, indirectly, or constructively). Compute each U.S. shareholder's pro rata share of the CFC's Subpart F income (based on ownership percentage). The U.S. shareholder includes this amount in their U.S. taxable income as a deemed dividend, in the year the CFC earns the income, regardless of distributions. The inclusion increases the shareholder's basis in the CFC stock (preventing double taxation when the income is eventually distributed). The inclusion is current, not deferred. A U.S. shareholder who fails to include Subpart F income understates their U.S. tax.

### Apply The De Minimis And Full Inclusion Rules

Subpart F income is subject to a de minimis rule and a full inclusion rule. Under the de minimis rule, if the CFC's Subpart F income is less than 5% of gross income (and less than 1 million dollars), no Subpart F income is included. Under the full inclusion rule, if the CFC's Subpart F income is 70% or more of gross income, the entire gross income (minus certain deductions) is treated as Subpart F income. Between the de minimis and full inclusion thresholds, only the actual Subpart F income is included.

Apply the de minimis and full inclusion rules. If the CFC's Subpart F income is less than 5% of gross income (and less than 1 million dollars), no Subpart F income is included (the de minimis rule). If the Subpart F income is 70% or more of gross income, the entire gross income is treated as Subpart F income (the full inclusion rule). Between 5% and 70%, only the actual Subpart F income is included. Compute the percentage and apply the correct rule. The de minimis rule provides relief for CFCs with minimal Subpart F income. The full inclusion rule prevents CFCs from using a small amount of active income to avoid the Subpart F regime.

### Handle The Foreign Personal Holding Company Income Look-Through Rule

The FPHCI look-through rule provides relief for certain related-party dividends, interest, rents, and royalties. If a CFC receives dividends, interest, rents, or royalties from a related party (another CFC or a U.S. parent), and the related party's income is active (not Subpart F), the recipient CFC's income may not be FPHCI under the look-through rule. The look-through rule prevents the same income from being taxed as Subpart F twice (once at the payor and once at the recipient).

Apply the FPHCI look-through rule. For related-party dividends, interest, rents, and royalties received by the CFC, determine whether the payor's income that generated the payment is active (not Subpart F). If the payor's income is active, the recipient CFC's payment may not be FPHCI under the look-through rule. The look-through rule applies for a limited period (currently through 2025, subject to extension). Track the applicability of the look-through rule. The rule is important for CFC groups where one CFC pays another; without the look-through rule, the payment would be FPHCI and subject to Subpart F inclusion.

### Report The Subpart F Inclusion On Form 5471

The Subpart F inclusion is reported on Form 5471 (Information Return of U.S. Persons With Respect to Certain Foreign Corporations). The form reports the CFC, the U.S. shareholder's ownership, the CFC's income (including Subpart F income by category), and the U.S. shareholder's inclusion. The inclusion is then reported on the shareholder's tax return (as a deemed dividend on Schedule B, Part III, or as appropriate for the shareholder's entity type). The form is required for each CFC.

Ensure Form 5471 is filed for each CFC. The form reports the CFC's financial information, the Subpart F income by category, and the U.S. shareholder's inclusion. The inclusion is reported on the shareholder's tax return. Failure to file Form 5471 can trigger significant penalties (10,000 dollars or more per form) and extend the statute of limitations for the entire return. The form is required for Category 4 and Category 5 filers (U.S. shareholders of a CFC). Track the filing requirement and ensure the form is complete and timely.

### Coordinate With The Foreign Tax Credit And GILTI

The Subpart F inclusion is a deemed dividend and may generate a foreign tax credit for the foreign taxes paid by the CFC on the Subpart F income. The FTC is computed under the rules for in lieu of taxes and deemed paid taxes (for corporate shareholders). Additionally, Subpart F income is excluded from GILTI (global intangible low-taxed income), which is a separate anti-deferral regime. The coordination between Subpart F, the FTC, and GILTI is critical for computing the total U.S. tax on the CFC's income.

Coordinate with the FTC and GILTI. The Subpart F inclusion may generate a foreign tax credit for the foreign taxes paid by the CFC on the Subpart F income (the deemed paid FTC for corporate shareholders, or the in lieu of taxes credit). The FTC is computed in the passive or general category, depending on the Subpart F income type. Additionally, Subpart F income is excluded from GILTI (the income is either Subpart F or GILTI, not both). The coordination ensures the CFC's income is taxed once under the correct regime and that the foreign taxes are credited appropriately. Track the interaction to avoid double counting or missed credits.

## Common Traps

### Treating CFC Income As Deferred Until Distributed

Subpart F requires current inclusion of the U.S. shareholder's share of Subpart F income, regardless of distributions. Treating the income as deferred understates the U.S. tax.

### Overlooking The 10% U.S. Shareholder Threshold

Subpart F applies to U.S. shareholders who own 10% or more of the CFC's voting stock (directly, indirectly, or constructively). Shareholders below 10% are not subject to Subpart F.

### Failing To Identify All Subpart F Income Categories

Subpart F income includes FPHCI, FBCSI, and FBCSVI. Each category has specific definitions. Overlooking a category understates the inclusion.

### Ignoring The De Minimis And Full Inclusion Rules

The de minimis rule (Subpart F below 5% of gross income) provides relief. The full inclusion rule (Subpart F at 70% or more) treats all income as Subpart F. Apply the correct rule.

### Not Filing Form 5471

The Subpart F inclusion is reported on Form 5471. Failure to file triggers significant penalties (10,000 dollars or more per form) and extends the statute of limitations for the entire return.

### Forgetting The Basis Increase For The Inclusion

The Subpart F inclusion increases the U.S. shareholder's basis in the CFC stock, preventing double taxation when the income is distributed. Track the basis increase.

### Overlooking The Interaction With GILTI

Subpart F income is excluded from GILTI. The income is either Subpart F or GILTI, not both. Coordinate the two regimes to avoid double counting.
