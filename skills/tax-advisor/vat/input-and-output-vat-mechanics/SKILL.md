# Input And Output VAT Mechanics

The central judgment problem in VAT accounting is that output VAT and input VAT look like mirror images of each other but are governed by completely different rules, and getting either side wrong distorts the net VAT payable in ways that are easy to miss until an audit. Output VAT is charged on supplies a business makes and is largely a function of correctly classifying the supply and its rate; input VAT is VAT a business pays on its purchases and is recoverable only when a specific set of conditions are met. The two are netted on the VAT return, but recoverability is not automatic, and a business that assumes all input VAT is reclaimable will overstate its refund, while one that misses recoverable input VAT permanently loses cash.

Agents tend to treat input VAT recovery as the simple inverse of charging output VAT — if tax appears on an invoice, reclaim it — and skip the conditions that gate recovery: a valid VAT invoice, business purpose, direct and indirect linkage to taxable supplies, and the partial exemption machinery where the business makes exempt supplies. They forget that input VAT on certain categories (entertainment, passenger cars, business assets given away) is blocked by statute in many jurisdictions, that recovery may need to be apportioned over time for capital goods, and that the netting position (a payment due versus a refund due) triggers different administrative processes and evidentiary burdens. The harm this prevents is a return that misstates net VAT, a refund claim that is rejected for documentation defects, or input VAT that is permanently lost because it was claimed in the wrong period or outside the time limit.

This skill covers the accounting mechanics of output VAT, input VAT, netting, and the recovery and refund process, primarily under EU and EU-style VAT systems. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; VAT recovery rules, blocked-input categories, partial exemption methods, refund procedures, and time limits are jurisdiction-dependent — they vary by EU member state and by country — and change over time. A qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Separate The Output VAT Calculation From The Input VAT Recovery Logic

Output VAT and input VAT must be analyzed with different mental models. Output VAT is computed by identifying each supply, its taxable amount, its place of supply, and the applicable rate (standard, reduced, zero, or exempt), then charging tax accordingly. Input VAT recovery is a separate permission-based process: the business asks whether each item of input VAT it paid meets the recovery conditions, and only the qualifying portion is reclaimable. Conflating the two leads to errors such as assuming that because tax was charged correctly on a sale, the corresponding purchase tax is automatically reclaimable.

For example, a consultancy charging standard-rated services to business clients computes output VAT on its fees, but the input VAT on its staff entertainment is not recoverable in many member states even though it relates to the business, because entertainment is a blocked category. Run the output side as a classification-and-rating exercise and the input side as a conditions-and-evidence exercise, and only net them at the return level. This separation also makes it far easier to spot the partial exemption cases, where exempt outputs poison the recovery of related input VAT.

### Gate Input VAT Recovery On The Four Core Conditions

Before reclaiming any input VAT, confirm four conditions that most VAT systems require in some form: the business must be VAT-registered, the purchase must be for the purpose of the business's economic activity, the goods or services must relate to taxable (not exempt) supplies, and a valid supporting document (typically a VAT invoice meeting the jurisdiction's requirements) must exist. If any condition fails, recovery is wholly or partly denied. These conditions are cumulative, not alternative.

Consider a business that buys a laptop used partly for the business and partly privately — the private portion fails the business-purpose condition and must be apportioned. Or a bank (making mostly exempt financial supplies) that pays VAT on consultancy — the input VAT may be largely irrecoverable because it relates to exempt supplies, regardless of how valid the invoice is. Always test each input VAT line against all four conditions, and where a purchase serves mixed purposes, apply an apportionment rather than claiming 100% or 0% by default.

### Apply Partial Exemption When The Business Makes Both Taxable And Exempt Supplies

When a business makes taxable and exempt supplies in the same period, it is a "mixed" or "partially exempt" business, and input VAT must be split into directly attributable and residual pools, then apportioned. Input VAT directly attributable to taxable supplies is fully recoverable; input VAT directly attributable to exempt supplies is not recoverable; input VAT that cannot be directly attributed (residual, or overhead input VAT) is apportioned using a method that reflects the use of the purchases in making taxable versus exempt supplies.

The standard apportionment is pro-rata based on the ratio of taxable turnover to total turnover, but member states permit or require alternative methods (transaction counts, floor space, time, sector-based methods) where the pro-rata distorts. For example, a training provider selling both standard-rated courses and exempt educational services would recover residual input VAT in proportion to taxable turnover, and may need a special method if turnover-based pro-rata is misleading. Partial exemption is the single most error-prone area of input VAT mechanics because the residual pool and the recovery percentage must be recomputed each period and often annualized and adjusted later, so never treat partial exemption as a one-time setup.

### Respect Blocked-Input Categories And Per-Capital-Goods Rules

Many VAT systems block recovery of input VAT on specific categories regardless of business purpose. Common blocked categories include passenger cars available for private use, business entertainment and hospitality, and goods or services given away as gifts beyond a de minimis value. Conversely, capital goods (such as buildings, aircraft, ships, and high-value equipment) often have extended adjustment periods — typically several years for immovable property and shorter for other capital items — during which recovery can be clawed back or adjusted if the use of the asset changes.

For example, input VAT on a company car used by an employee with private use is commonly irrecoverable in several member states, while input VAT on a commercial van used only for business is recoverable. A business that buys a building and recovers input VAT, then later changes its use toward exempt activities, must adjust (claw back) part of the recovered VAT over the capital goods adjustment period. Always identify blocked categories before claiming, and flag capital goods purchases for ongoing adjustment rather than treating recovery as final.

### Net Input VAT Against Output VAT Correctly On The Return

The VAT return nets the two flows: total output VAT charged (plus any VAT due under reverse charge or on acquisitions) minus total recoverable input VAT for the period. If output exceeds input, the business pays the net VAT to the authority; if input exceeds output, the business is owed a refund (subject to the refund process). The netting must use only recoverable input VAT — not gross input VAT paid — and must respect the period in which the right to deduct arises, which in many systems is the period the valid invoice is held, not necessarily the period of supply.

For example, if a business has high start-up input VAT (equipment, fit-out) before it begins making taxable sales, it will show a refund position on early returns. Ensure the return distinguishes domestic output VAT, reverse-charge output VAT, intra-EU acquisitions, and cross-border inputs that may need reclaiming through a separate refund mechanism, because netting cross-border input VAT against domestic output VAT is often not permitted and requires a different process.

### Treat The Refund Process As Evidence-Dependent, Not Automatic

When a business is in a net refund position, the refund is not automatically paid. Tax authorities routinely scrutinize refund returns more heavily than payment returns, may request the underlying invoices and evidence of business purpose, and may apply timing or de minimis rules before releasing cash. Persistent or large refund positions can trigger audits or requests for explanation, and some systems carry forward a refund against future liabilities unless the business affirmatively claims repayment.

For example, an exporter that is almost always in refund (because its sales are zero-rated but it incurs domestic input VAT) should expect periodic documentation requests and must keep robust invoice records. Understand the member state's refund timing rules, whether refunds are paid by a set deadline or only on request, and whether a carry-forward election exists. Never present a refund as a guaranteed cash event; it is contingent on the authority accepting the recovery claims.

### Observe Time Limits And The Correct Period For Claiming Input VAT

Input VAT recovery is time-limited. Most systems require the claim in the period the valid invoice is held (or by a deadline after the period), and impose an absolute time limit — commonly several years, varying by member state — after which input VAT cannot be reclaimed. Late claims, missed periods, and invoices discovered after the deadline can permanently forfeit the recovery. Some systems allow a late claim under specific conditions, but relying on that is risky.

For example, a business that finds a valid supplier invoice from two years prior must check whether the member state's time limit and late-claim rules still permit recovery, and if so, whether it corrects via an amended return or a separate claim. Build a process to capture input VAT in the correct period and to review for missed invoices before the absolute deadline, because forfeited input VAT is a permanent cash loss that no later netting can recover.

## Common Traps

### Assuming All Input VAT On A Valid Invoice Is Recoverable

The business has a proper VAT invoice, so it reclaims the full amount. The trap is that a valid invoice is necessary but not sufficient — the purchase must also be for business purpose and relate to taxable supplies, and must not fall in a blocked category. Test all recovery conditions, not just invoice validity.

### Claiming Input VAT Linked To Exempt Supplies

A bank or insurer reclaims input VAT on overheads the same way a trader would. The trap is that input VAT relating to exempt supplies is not recoverable, and for mixed businesses the residual must be apportioned, which often drastically reduces recovery. Apply partial exemption before netting.

### Forgetting Apportionment On Mixed-Use Purchases

A laptop or phone is bought for an employee who uses it partly privately, and 100% of the input VAT is claimed. The trap is that the private-use portion fails the business-purpose condition and must be apportioned, with periodic adjustments in some systems. Apportion mixed-use purchases and document the method.

### Treating Blocked Entertainment Or Car Input VAT As Recoverable

The business reclaims VAT on client dinners or a director's car because they seem business-related. The trap is that entertainment and passenger cars are commonly blocked-input categories regardless of business nexus. Identify blocked categories before claiming and exclude them.

### Netting Foreign Input VAT Against Domestic Output VAT

A business pays VAT in another member state and nets it against its domestic return. The trap is that cross-border input VAT usually cannot be netted domestically and must be reclaimed through the relevant refund mechanism (such as the EU cross-border refund directive or an equivalent process). Route foreign input VAT to the correct refund channel.

### Ignoring Capital Goods Adjustment Periods

A business recovers input VAT on a building and treats it as final. The trap is that capital goods have extended adjustment periods, and any later change in use toward exempt activity triggers a claw-back over the adjustment window. Flag capital goods for ongoing adjustment.

### Assuming A Refund Position Guarantees Prompt Cash

The return shows a refund, so the business books the cash immediately. The trap is that authorities scrutinize refund returns, request evidence, and may delay or carry forward. Treat refunds as contingent on authority acceptance and track the actual release.

### Claiming Input VAT In The Wrong Period Or After The Time Limit

Input VAT is claimed in a later period or after the absolute deadline, and recovery is denied. The trap is that recovery is period-specific and time-limited; a missed deadline can permanently forfeit the VAT. Capture input VAT in the correct period and review for missed invoices before the deadline.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has output VAT been computed by classifying each supply (rate, taxable amount, place of supply) separately from the input VAT recovery analysis, rather than treating them as mirror images?
- [ ] Has every input VAT line been tested against the four core recovery conditions (registration, business purpose, relation to taxable supplies, valid invoice) before being claimed?
- [ ] Where the business makes exempt supplies, has partial exemption been applied — separating directly attributable, exempt-linked, and residual input VAT and apportioning the residual?
- [ ] Have blocked-input categories (entertainment, passenger cars, gifts) been identified and excluded from recovery regardless of business purpose?
- [ ] Have capital goods purchases been flagged for adjustment over the relevant period, rather than treated as final one-time recovery?
- [ ] Has mixed-use expenditure been apportioned and documented, rather than claimed at 0% or 100% by default?
- [ ] Has the VAT return netted only recoverable input VAT against output VAT, with cross-border input VAT routed to the correct refund mechanism rather than netted domestically?
- [ ] Has input VAT been claimed in the correct period, and have missed invoices been reviewed against the member state's time limit and late-claim rules?
- [ ] Has the refund position been treated as evidence-dependent and contingent on authority acceptance, rather than as guaranteed prompt cash?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general VAT information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
