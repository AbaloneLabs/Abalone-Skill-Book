# Intangible Drilling Costs and Depletion

Oil and gas taxation is built around two provisions that exist to encourage domestic drilling: the intangible drilling cost (IDC) deduction under Section 263(c) and the depletion allowance under Section 611. The IDC election lets an operator or investor deduct the otherwise capitalizable costs of preparing a well for production (labor, fuel, materials, supplies) in the year paid or incurred, rather than recovering them through depreciation. The depletion allowance lets the owner of an economic interest in a producing well recover the capital cost of the mineral resource, with percentage depletion offering a statutory percentage of gross income from the property (15 percent for oil and gas) subject to limits, and cost depletion offering recovery based on the units sold. These provisions are the economic engine of oil and gas investing, but they are also the most frequently misapplied, because the IDC election is binding, the depletion choice must be made annually, and the limitations interact in ways that can convert a paper deduction into a suspended or disallowed benefit.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the IDC election is a one-time election per taxpayer per well (or per partnership) and is binding for the life of the well, so a taxpayer who elects to expense IDCs cannot later amortize them, and the choice affects basis, recapture, and the alternative minimum tax preference. Second, percentage depletion is computed independently for each property and is limited to 100 percent of the net taxable income from the property (65 percent of net income for independent producers and royalty owners before the 100-percent limit), and a taxpayer with losses on other properties cannot use them to increase the depletion on a profitable property. Third, IDCs create an alternative minimum tax preference item for individuals, and although the AMT has been substantially narrowed, the interaction must still be checked. Fourth, the at-risk rules under Section 465 and the passive activity rules under Section 469 determine whether the IDC and depletion deductions are currently usable, and a working interest held by a limited partner can be trapped by passive rules despite the working-interest exception.

This skill covers intangible drilling costs and depletion under U.S. federal oil and gas rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The IDC Election And Its Binding Nature

Section 263(c) authorizes the deduction of intangible drilling and development costs (IDCs) that are otherwise capitalizable, provided the taxpayer makes an election under regulations prescribed by the Secretary. The election allows the operator or investor to deduct IDCs as paid (cash method) or incurred (accrual method) in the year the well is drilled, rather than capitalizing them into the basis of the well and recovering them through depreciation over the well's productive life. IDCs include the costs of labor, fuel, repairs, hauling, supplies, and drilling rig time, but exclude the cost of the drilling equipment itself and the casing and tubing that have a salvage value, which are capitalized and depreciated. The election is made by claiming the deduction on the return for the first taxable year in which the costs are paid or incurred, with a statement attached.

Treat the IDC election as binding and irrevocable for the well. Once a taxpayer elects to expense IDCs for a property, the same method must be used for all IDCs for that property in the current and all future years, and a taxpayer who elected to expense cannot later switch to amortization. The election is made at the taxpayer level for direct interests and at the partnership level for partnership interests, so an investor in an oil and gas partnership is bound by the partnership's election. Confirm whether the election has been made for each interest before reporting any IDC deduction, because the election status determines basis, the eventual gain on sale, and any recapture.

### Choose Between Percentage And Cost Depletion Annually

Section 611 allows the owner of an economic interest in a mineral property to recover the capital cost of the mineral through depletion, computed either as cost depletion (based on the units of the mineral sold relative to the remaining recoverable units) or as percentage depletion (a statutory percentage of the gross income from the property). For oil and gas, the percentage depletion rate is 15 percent for independent producers and royalty owners (small producers), subject to a daily production barrel limit (1,000 barrels per day per taxpayer) and a net-income limitation. The taxpayer must compute both methods each year and claim the larger, so cost depletion is used when the property is in its early high-production years and percentage depletion when the basis is largely recovered.

Compute both depletion methods for each property annually and claim the larger. Cost depletion requires the taxpayer's basis in the mineral, the total recoverable units (reserves), and the units sold in the year, and it cannot reduce the basis below zero. Percentage depletion is 15 percent of the property's gross income, limited to 100 percent of the taxable income from the property (computed without the depletion deduction), and further limited to 65 percent of the taxpayer's net taxable income from all sources before the depletion deduction, with any excess carried forward. Do not assume percentage depletion always wins, because the net-income limits can reduce it below cost depletion, especially for a taxpayer with low overall income or high deductions.

### Apply The Percentage Depletion Net-Income Limits

The percentage depletion allowance for oil and gas is subject to two net-income limitations that agents frequently misapply. The first is the 100-percent-of-taxable-income-from-the-property limit, which caps percentage depletion at the net taxable income from that specific property (gross income from the property minus all allowable deductions attributable to the property, including operating costs and depreciation but excluding the depletion deduction itself). The second is the 65-percent-of-net-income limit, which caps the total percentage depletion from all properties at 65 percent of the taxpayer's taxable income from all sources (computed before the depletion allowance and before any net operating loss deduction), with any excess carried forward indefinitely until there is sufficient net income to absorb it.

Apply the limits in order and track the carryforward. Compute the 100-percent property-level limit first for each property, then aggregate and apply the 65-percent overall limit, and carry forward any excess. A taxpayer with a loss on one property cannot use that loss to increase the depletion on a profitable property, because the property-level limit is computed separately. The 65-percent limit is a frequent source of suspended depletion, especially for investors whose oil and gas income is a large share of total income, and the carryforward must be tracked so that it is claimed in a later year when net income allows.

### Account For The AMT Preference And IDC Recapture

IDCs expensed by an individual create an alternative minimum tax preference item equal to the excess of the IDC deduction over the amount that would have been deductible had the costs been amortized over ten years (for non-integrated oil companies) or over the useful life of the well. While the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act suspended the AMT for corporations and narrowed the individual AMT, the IDC preference remains a consideration for individuals with large IDC deductions, and the basis of the AMT computation must be checked. The AMT preference is reduced for independent producers and royalty owners to the extent the IDCs do not exceed 65 percent of the net income from the property.

Track the IDC basis and the potential recapture. When a property is sold, the gain attributable to the expensed IDCs is generally ordinary income under Section 1254 recapture to the extent of the IDCs previously deducted (and the depletion claimed), with any excess gain as Section 1231 gain. An investor who expensed large IDCs and then sells the interest may face significant ordinary recapture that wipes out the benefit of the lower capital rate, so the recapture must be modeled before any sale. Keep records of the IDCs deducted and the depletion claimed for each property so the Section 1254 recapture is computed correctly.

### Apply The At-Risk And Passive Activity Rules

The deductibility of IDCs and losses from an oil and gas interest depends on the at-risk rules under Section 465 and the passive activity loss rules under Section 469. The at-risk rules limit loss deductions to the amount the taxpayer has at risk (cash and property contributed, plus recourse debt and certain nonrecourse financing qualified by real property), so an investor who funded the interest with nonrecourse debt that is not qualified nonrecourse financing cannot deduct losses beyond the at-risk amount. The passive activity rules limit the deduction of passive losses to passive income, unless the taxpayer materially participates or qualifies for an exception.

Apply the working-interest exception to the passive loss rules for oil and gas. A working interest (operating interest) in an oil and gas property held by a non-corporate taxpayer is treated as non-passive if the taxpayer's interest was held directly or through an entity that does not limit liability, regardless of material participation, so working-interest losses are deductible against any income. However, the exception does not apply to a working interest held through a limited partnership interest or an LLC that limits liability, and it does not apply to royalty interests (which are passive unless the taxpayer materially participates). Confirm the form of the interest and the liability protection before claiming the exception, because a working interest held through a limiting entity is passive and the losses are suspended.

### Distinguish The Tax Treatment Of Operators, Working-Interest Owners, And Royalty Owners

The tax treatment of oil and gas income and deductions depends on the character of the owner's interest. An operator (the company actually drilling and producing) deducts operating expenses and IDCs under its elected method and claims depletion on its economic interest. A working-interest owner bears a share of the drilling and operating costs and receives a share of the production, and is entitled to IDC deductions and depletion on its share, with the working-interest passive exception available if the interest is held without liability limitation. A royalty owner receives a share of production free of the costs of production and has no IDC deductions but claims depletion (typically percentage depletion at 15 percent for small royalty owners) and reports the royalty income on Schedule E.

Classify the owner's interest before reporting any income or deduction. A common error is treating a royalty owner as entitled to IDC deductions, or treating a working-interest owner as a passive investor regardless of form. The interest type determines which deductions are available, whether the passive exception applies, and which schedule the income is reported on. Obtain the operating agreement, the division order, or the lease to confirm the interest character, because the payor's 1099 may not reflect the economic substance.

## Common Traps

### Treating The IDC Election As A Year-By-Year Choice

The symptom is a taxpayer who switches between expensing and amortizing IDCs from year to year. The trap is that the IDC election is binding for the life of the well, so a switch is not permitted and creates an inconsistent position that the IRS will challenge. The direction is to confirm the election status for each interest and to apply it consistently, documenting the election made.

### Claiming Percentage Depletion Without The Net-Income Limits

The symptom is a taxpayer who claims the full 15 percent of gross income as depletion regardless of net income. The trap is that the 100-percent property-level limit and the 65-percent overall limit can reduce the depletion, and ignoring them overstates the deduction and creates a carryforward that is lost if not tracked. The direction is to compute both limits and to track any suspended percentage depletion for future years.

### Applying The Working-Interest Passive Exception To A Limited Partnership Interest

The symptom is an investor in an oil and gas limited partnership who claims working-interest losses as non-passive. The trap is that the working-interest exception requires the interest be held without liability limitation, so a limited partnership interest or an LLC interest with liability protection is passive and the losses are suspended. The direction is to confirm the entity form and liability protection before claiming the exception.

### Forgetting Section 1254 Recapture On Sale

The symptom is an investor who sells an oil and gas interest and reports the entire gain as capital. The trap is that gain attributable to prior IDC deductions and depletion is ordinary income under Section 1254 recapture, so the capital treatment overstates the benefit and understates the tax. The direction is to compute the Section 1254 recapture from the IDC and depletion records before reporting the sale.

### Counting Nonrecourse Debt As At-Risk Without Qualification

The symptom is an investor who deducts losses funded by nonrecourse debt. The trap is that only recourse debt and qualified nonrecourse financing count as at-risk, so non-qualified nonrecourse debt does not increase the at-risk amount and the losses are suspended. The direction is to confirm the debt is recourse or qualified nonrecourse financing before counting it as at-risk.

### Reporting Royalty Income As Working-Interest Income

The symptom is a royalty owner who reports royalty income on Schedule F or as self-employment income. The trap is that royalty income is reported on Schedule E and is generally not subject to self-employment tax, and the misclassification overstates SE tax and may misstate the depletion and QBI computations. The direction is to classify the interest as royalty or working interest before choosing the schedule.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the IDC election status been confirmed for each interest, recognizing that the election is binding for the life of the well and made at the operator or partnership level?
- [ ] Has both cost depletion and percentage depletion been computed for each property annually, with the larger claimed?
- [ ] Has the 100-percent-of-taxable-income-from-the-property limit and the 65-percent-of-net-income limit been applied to percentage depletion, with any excess carried forward and tracked?
- [ ] Has the alternative minimum tax preference for expensed IDCs been considered for individual investors, including the reduction for independent producers and royalty owners?
- [ ] Has the Section 1254 recapture potential been modeled from the IDC and depletion records before any sale of an oil and gas interest?
- [ ] Has the at-risk amount been confirmed to include only cash, property, recourse debt, and qualified nonrecourse financing, with non-qualified nonrecourse debt excluded?
- [ ] Has the working-interest passive exception been confirmed to apply only where the interest is held without liability limitation, with limited partnership and LLC interests treated as passive?
- [ ] Has each owner's interest been classified as operator, working interest, or royalty before reporting income and deductions, with the correct schedule used?
