# Working Interest vs Royalty

The distinction between a working interest and a royalty interest in oil and gas is the single most consequential classification for the owner's federal tax treatment, yet it is frequently misunderstood because the two interests can look similar on the surface. A working interest (also called an operating interest) is the interest that bears the cost of drilling, developing, and operating the well and receives the production after royalties and other burdens are paid. A royalty interest is the right to receive a share of production free of the costs of production, typically created by the mineral owner's lease to the operator. The classification determines whether the owner has intangible drilling cost deductions (working interest yes, royalty no), whether the income is subject to self-employment tax (working interest generally yes, royalty generally no), whether the passive activity loss exception applies (working interest yes if held without liability limitation, royalty no), and which depletion method and rate apply.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the form of the interest (the lease, the operating agreement, or the division order) controls the classification, not the label the payor uses on the 1099, and an interest that is called a royalty but bears production costs is a working interest. Second, the working-interest passive exception under Section 469 requires the interest be held directly or through an entity that does not limit liability, so a working interest held through a limited partnership or a limiting LLC is passive and the losses are suspended, defeating the main reason investors hold working interests. Third, royalty income is generally not self-employment income and is reported on Schedule E, but a working-interest owner who actively operates the well may have self-employment income reportable on Schedule F or Schedule C, and the SE tax and the qualified business income deduction follow. Fourth, an overriding royalty interest (created out of the working interest rather than the mineral owner's share) is still a royalty for tax purposes if it is free of production costs, but the depletion basis differs.

This skill covers the classification and tax treatment of working interests and royalty interests under U.S. federal oil and gas rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Interest From The Legal Form, Not The 1099 Label

The classification of an oil and gas interest as a working interest or a royalty interest turns on whether the owner bears a share of the costs of production. A working interest owner is obligated to pay a share of the drilling, development, and operating costs and receives the net production after royalties and burdens. A royalty interest owner (including an overriding royalty) is entitled to a share of production free of production costs and has no obligation to fund drilling or operations. The classification is determined by the lease, the operating agreement, the deed, or the division order that created the interest, and it is not controlled by the label the payor places on the Form 1099.

Read the instrument that created the interest before classifying. A common error is treating an interest as a royalty because the 1099 reports royalty income, when the owner actually bears costs and is a working-interest owner, or vice versa. The instrument states whether the owner's share is before or after costs, whether the owner has voting or operating rights, and whether the owner is subject to cash calls for drilling and operations. Confirm the cost-bearing obligation, because that is the dispositive factor, and document the classification so it is defensible on examination.

### Determine Self-Employment Tax Exposure By Interest Type

Self-employment tax under the Self-Employment Contributions Act applies to net earnings from a trade or business, and the classification of the oil and gas interest determines whether the income is subject to SE tax. A working-interest owner who is engaged in the trade or business of producing oil and gas and who actively participates in operations generally has net earnings from self-employment subject to SE tax, reportable on Schedule F (as a farmer if part of a farming operation) or Schedule C. A royalty interest owner who merely receives a share of production free of cost and does not participate in operations is generally not engaged in a trade or business, and the royalty income is reported on Schedule E and is not subject to SE tax.

Determine whether the working-interest owner's activity rises to a trade or business. An active operator who makes decisions, supervises drilling, and manages production is clearly in a trade or business with SE tax. A passive working-interest owner who merely invests and does not participate may still have trade-or-business income if the interest is operated as a business by the partnership or operator, but the SE tax analysis depends on the owner's role and the entity form. A royalty owner is almost never subject to SE tax unless the royalty is part of a larger operating business. Do not apply SE tax to royalty income reflexively, and do not omit it from active working-interest income, because both errors are common and costly.

### Apply The Working-Interest Passive Activity Exception

The passive activity loss rules under Section 469 generally suspend losses from activities in which the taxpayer does not materially participate, but Section 469(c)(3) provides an exception for a working interest in any oil or gas property which the taxpayer holds directly or through an entity which does not limit the liability of the taxpayer with respect to such property. A qualifying working interest is treated as non-passive regardless of material participation, so working-interest losses are deductible against any income, which is the principal tax advantage of direct working-interest ownership. Royalty interests do not qualify for the exception and are passive unless the taxpayer materially participates.

Confirm the liability-limitation condition before claiming the exception. The exception requires that the working interest be held directly or through a general partnership, a sole proprietorship, or another entity that does not limit the owner's liability. A working interest held through a limited partnership interest, or through an LLC treated as a partnership where the member's liability is limited by state law, does not qualify for the exception and is passive, so the losses are suspended against passive income only. This is the most common error in oil and gas investing: an investor buys a working interest through a limiting entity expecting non-passive loss treatment and finds the losses suspended. Confirm the entity form and the state-law liability protection before claiming the exception.

### Apply Depletion By Interest Type

Depletion is available to the owner of an economic interest in a mineral property, which includes both working-interest owners and royalty owners, but the method and the basis differ. A working-interest owner has a basis in the mineral (through lease bonus, development costs, and the cost of the mineral rights) and can claim cost depletion or percentage depletion on its share of production. A royalty owner generally has a low or zero basis in the royalty (unless the royalty was purchased), so cost depletion is often minimal and percentage depletion at 15 percent (for small producers) is the primary depletion method. An overriding royalty owner has a basis in the overriding royalty if it was purchased, and claims depletion accordingly.

Compute depletion for each interest based on its character. For a working interest, the depletion basis includes the leasehold cost, the lease bonus, and the share of the mineral cost, and both cost and percentage depletion are computed with the larger claimed. For a royalty, the depletion basis is generally the cost of acquiring the royalty, and percentage depletion at 15 percent subject to the net-income limits is typically the larger deduction. Do not allow IDC deductions to a royalty owner, because royalties bear no production costs and have no IDCs, and do not deny depletion to a working-interest owner by treating the interest as a pure royalty.

### Evaluate The At-Risk Consequences For Working-Interest Losses

Working-interest losses are subject to the at-risk rules under Section 465, which limit loss deductions to the amount the taxpayer has at risk in the activity. The at-risk amount includes cash and property contributed, recourse debt for which the taxpayer is personally liable, and qualified nonrecourse financing (typically financing secured by real property and borrowed from a government or a commercial lender). Nonrecourse debt that is not qualified nonrecourse financing does not increase the at-risk amount, so a working-interest owner who funded the interest with non-qualified nonrecourse debt cannot deduct losses beyond the cash and recourse amounts at risk.

Confirm the at-risk amount before deducting working-interest losses. Many oil and gas programs are structured with nonrecourse financing or with recourse limited to the property, which means the investors' at-risk amount is limited to their cash contributions. An investor who expects to deduct losses equal to the full investment plus the debt-funded drilling may find the debt-funded portion suspended under the at-risk rules, even if the working-interest passive exception makes the losses non-passive. The at-risk and passive rules operate independently, so a loss must clear both to be currently deductible.

### Recognize Overriding Royalty And Net Profits Interests

Beyond the basic working interest and royalty, oil and gas interests include overriding royalty interests (ORRIs), net profits interests (NPIs), and production payments, each with distinct tax treatment. An ORRI is created out of the working interest (rather than the mineral owner's share) and is free of production costs, so it is treated as a royalty for depletion and SE tax purposes, with depletion based on the cost of acquiring the ORRI. An NPI is a share of the net profits from production after costs, and its classification depends on whether the NPI holder bears costs; a typical NPI that does not require the holder to fund costs is treated as a royalty, while one that requires cost contributions may be a working interest.

Classify non-standard interests carefully. An ORRI is generally a royalty, but the depletion basis is the acquisition cost and the owner has no IDC deductions. An NPI that is carved out of production free of cost is a royalty, while an NRI or similar interest that requires cost contributions is a working interest with IDC deductions and SE tax. A production payment (a right to a specified amount of production or proceeds) has its own rules and may be treated as a loan, a sale, or a retained interest depending on its structure. Read the instrument and classify based on the cost-bearing obligation, not the label.

## Common Traps

### Classifying An Interest From The 1099 Label Instead Of The Lease

The symptom is an owner whose interest is classified based on the 1099 box. The trap is that the payor may use the wrong box, and the tax character follows the legal form, so a cost-bearing interest reported as royalty is still a working interest. The direction is to read the lease, operating agreement, or division order and classify based on the cost-bearing obligation.

### Claiming The Working-Interest Passive Exception Through A Limiting Entity

The symptom is an investor in an oil and gas limited partnership or LLC who claims working-interest losses as non-passive. The trap is that the exception requires the interest be held without liability limitation, so a limited partnership or limiting LLC interest is passive and the losses are suspended. The direction is to confirm the entity form and liability protection before claiming the exception.

### Applying Self-Employment Tax To Royalty Income

The symptom is a royalty owner who reports royalty income on Schedule C or F with SE tax. The trap is that royalty income is generally reported on Schedule E and is not subject to SE tax unless it is part of an operating business, so the misclassification overstates tax. The direction is to report royalty income on Schedule E and to reserve SE tax for active working-interest operators.

### Denying Depletion To A Working-Interest Owner Or Allowing IDCs To A Royalty Owner

The symptom is a return that treats a working interest as a pure royalty (no depletion basis) or a royalty as entitled to IDC deductions. The trap is that both interest types can claim depletion based on their economic interest, but only working interests bear costs and have IDCs, so the misclassification misstates both deductions. The direction is to apply depletion to both interest types based on their basis and to allow IDCs only to cost-bearing working interests.

### Counting Nonrecourse Debt As At-Risk

The symptom is a working-interest owner who deducts losses funded by nonrecourse debt. The trap is that only recourse debt and qualified nonrecourse financing count as at-risk, so non-qualified nonrecourse debt does not increase the at-risk amount and the losses are suspended. The direction is to confirm the debt is recourse or qualified nonrecourse financing before counting it as at-risk.

### Treating An Overriding Royalty As A Working Interest

The symptom is an ORRI owner who claims IDC deductions or SE-tax treatment. The trap is that an ORRI is free of production costs and is a royalty for tax purposes, so the owner has no IDCs and no SE tax, with depletion based on acquisition cost. The direction is to classify the ORRI as a royalty and to apply royalty treatment.
