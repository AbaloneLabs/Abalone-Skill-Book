# Partnership and Tax Shelter

Oil and gas partnerships have historically been a vehicle for both legitimate drilling investments and abusive tax shelters, and the line between the two is the central judgment problem. A legitimate oil and gas partnership drills real wells, incurs real costs, and generates income and deductions that reflect the economics of production; an abusive shelter manufactures deductions through inflated appraisals, circular financing, or non-economic fee structures designed to generate tax losses far exceeding the investor's economic investment. The tax rules respond with the at-risk rules under Section 465, the passive activity rules under Section 469, the economic-substance doctrine under Section 7701(o), the hobby-loss rules under Section 183, and the partnership-level audit regime under the Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015, each of which can disallow losses, recharacterize the transaction, or impose penalties. An agent who evaluates an oil and gas partnership only by its projected deductions, without examining the economics, the financing, and the at-risk and passive consequences, will either recommend an abusive shelter or fail to protect a legitimate investment.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the at-risk rules limit loss deductions to the amount the partner has at risk, and oil and gas programs are often structured with nonrecourse debt or with recourse limited to the property, so the at-risk amount is far less than the projected deductions and the excess losses are suspended. Second, the passive activity rules suspend losses from interests in which the partner does not materially participate, and although the working-interest exception can make a direct working interest non-passive, the exception does not apply through a limiting entity, so most limited partners are passive. Third, the economic-substance doctrine and the related penalty under Section 6662 can disallow and penalize a transaction that lacks a genuine business purpose and economic substance apart from tax reduction, and the IRS actively audits oil and gas partnerships for inflated costs and circular financing. Fourth, the partnership audit regime means that partnership-level adjustments are collected at the partnership level, which changes how a partner should evaluate the risk of an audit.

This skill covers oil and gas partnerships and tax-shelter risk under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate The Economic Substance Before Recommending A Partnership

The economic-substance doctrine under Section 7701(o) requires that a transaction have a meaningful change in the taxpayer's economic position apart from federal income tax effects and a substantial non-tax purpose for the transaction's form. For an oil and gas partnership, this means the partnership must actually drill wells, incur real costs, and pursue a genuine profit from production, not merely generate tax losses through paper transactions. Inflated drilling costs, circular financing (where the same money moves among related parties to create the appearance of investment), and fee structures that extract the investor's cash as promoter fees are the hallmarks of a shelter that fails economic substance.

Evaluate the partnership's economics before recommending it. Compare the projected deductions to the investor's actual cash investment; a program that generates deductions several times the cash invested, funded by nonrecourse debt or by fees returned to the promoter, is a shelter. Confirm that the drilling costs are reasonable by comparing to industry norms, that the wells are real and drilled, and that the partnership expects to produce and sell oil or gas at a profit. A partnership whose only realistic return is the tax deduction fails economic substance and is likely to be disallowed and penalized. Decline to support a partnership that exhibits the shelter pattern, because the accuracy-related penalty under Section 6662 and the promoter penalty under Section 6700 can exceed the tax saved.

### Apply The At-Risk Rules To The Partner's Investment

The at-risk rules under Section 465 limit the deduction of losses from certain activities, including oil and gas, to the amount the taxpayer has at risk in the activity. The at-risk amount includes cash and the adjusted basis of property contributed, amounts borrowed for which the taxpayer is personally liable (recourse debt), and qualified nonrecourse financing (typically financing secured by real property and borrowed from a federal, state, or local government or from a commercial lender and for which no one is personally liable). Nonrecourse debt that is not qualified nonrecourse financing does not increase the at-risk amount, so an investor who funded the partnership interest with non-qualified nonrecourse debt cannot deduct losses beyond the cash and recourse amounts at risk.

Compute the at-risk amount before projecting the deductible loss. Many oil and gas programs are structured so that the investor's cash is the only at-risk amount, with the drilling funded by nonrecourse debt or by the operator's assumption of cost obligations, which means the at-risk amount is the cash and the excess losses are suspended until the partner generates more at-risk amounts or has income from the activity. An investor who expects to deduct losses equal to the full projected drilling cost may find that only the cash portion is deductible currently, with the rest suspended. The at-risk rules operate independently of the passive rules, so a loss must clear both to be currently deductible.

### Apply The Passive Activity Rules And The Working-Interest Exception

The passive activity loss rules under Section 469 suspend losses from activities in which the taxpayer does not materially participate, offset only against passive income, and release on full disposition of the interest. For an oil and gas partnership, a limited partner is generally passive unless the partner satisfies one of the modified material-participation tests (such as over 500 hours or substantially all of the participation), and limited partners rarely meet these tests. The working-interest exception under Section 469(c)(3) treats a working interest in oil and gas as non-passive if held directly or through an entity that does not limit liability, but the exception does not apply to a working interest held through a limited partnership or a limiting LLC, so most partnership interests are passive.

Determine the partner's participation and the entity form before projecting the loss. A direct working interest held without liability limitation is non-passive regardless of participation, but a partnership interest (limited or a limiting LLC) is passive unless the partner materially participates. Confirm whether the partnership interest qualifies for the working-interest exception (it generally does not if the entity limits liability), and if not, apply the material-participation tests to determine whether the losses are currently deductible or suspended. The passive rules can suspend losses even after the at-risk rules are satisfied, so both must be checked.

### Apply The Material-Participation Tests For Non-Corporate Partners

For a partner whose interest is passive by default (a limited partner or a member of a limiting LLC), the partner can still achieve non-passive treatment by materially participating in the activity under the seven regulatory tests. The most common are the 500-hour test (over 500 hours during the year), the substantially-all test, the 100-hour test (over 100 hours and more than anyone else), and the regular-continuous-substantial test based on facts and circumstances. Limited partners are subject to modified tests that generally require the partner to participate for more than 500 hours or to materially participate for at least five of the prior ten years.

Evaluate whether the partner's participation meets a material-participation test before claiming non-passive treatment. Most passive investors in oil and gas partnerships do not materially participate, so their losses are suspended. A partner who genuinely spends over 500 hours in the partnership's operations (such as an active operator-partner) may meet the test, but the hours must be documented contemporaneously and must be operational, not investor-level. Do not assume that reviewing statements or attending annual meetings constitutes material participation, because investor-level work does not count. The material-participation determination is fact-intensive and must be documented to survive examination.

### Recognize The Partnership-Level Audit Regime

The Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015 (BBA) partnership audit regime, codified under Sections 6221 through 6241, generally assesses and collects any partnership-level tax adjustment at the partnership level rather than separately against the partners, unless the partnership makes an election under Section 6226 to push the adjustment out to the reviewed-year partners. This changes the risk analysis for a partner, because an audit adjustment to the partnership's deductions (such as disallowed IDCs or recharacterized income) is collected from the partnership in the adjustment year, which can burden the current-year partners who may not be the same as the reviewed-year partners.

Understand the partnership's audit posture before investing. A partnership that has made aggressive deductions in prior years carries audit risk that may be borne by the current partners under the BBA regime, so a prospective partner should understand the partnership's filing positions and whether a push-out election is available. For a partnership facing an audit, evaluate whether the push-out election under Section 6226 (which moves the tax to the reviewed-year partners) is beneficial based on the partners' rates and the partnership's liquidity. The BBA regime makes partnership-level diligence and the partnership agreement's tax-allocation provisions more important, because the agreement determines who bears an adjustment.

### Recognize And Decline Abusive Shelter Patterns

Oil and gas has been a recurring vehicle for abusive tax shelters, and the IRS publishes guidance identifying the patterns. The hallmarks include deductions that exceed the investor's cash investment (funded by nonrecourse debt or by fees returned to the promoter), inflated drilling cost estimates far above industry norms, circular or back-to-back financing among related parties, fee structures that extract the investor's cash as promoter commissions before any drilling, and marketing that emphasizes the tax deduction rather than the economics of production. Syndicated conservation easements and listed transactions involving oil and gas are on the IRS abusive-promotion list and carry severe penalties.

Recognize the abusive pattern and decline to support it. A legitimate oil and gas investment has reasonable drilling costs, real wells, a genuine expectation of production and profit, and deductions that reflect actual economic outlays. A shelter has deductions that exceed the investment, inflated costs, and a return that depends on the tax deduction. If a partnership exhibits the shelter pattern, the deductions are likely to be disallowed under the at-risk rules, the passive rules, the economic-substance doctrine, or Section 183, and the investor and the promoter face accuracy-related and promoter penalties. The agent's role is to identify the pattern, explain the risk, and decline to recommend or prepare a return that claims the shelter deductions.

## Common Traps

### Recommending A Partnership Based Only On Projected Deductions

The symptom is an investor who chooses an oil and gas partnership based on the projected tax deduction. The trap is that the at-risk and passive rules may suspend most of the deductions, and the economic-substance doctrine may disallow the transaction, so the projected deduction is illusory. The direction is to evaluate the economics, the financing, and the at-risk and passive consequences before projecting any deductible loss.

### Counting Nonrecourse Debt As At-Risk

The symptom is an investor who deducts losses funded by nonrecourse partnership debt. The trap is that only recourse debt and qualified nonrecourse financing count as at-risk, so non-qualified nonrecourse debt does not increase the at-risk amount and the losses are suspended. The direction is to confirm the debt is recourse or qualified nonrecourse financing before counting it as at-risk.

### Assuming The Working-Interest Exception Applies Through A Partnership

The symptom is a limited partner who claims working-interest losses as non-passive. The trap is that the working-interest exception requires the interest be held without liability limitation, so a limited partnership or limiting LLC interest is passive and the losses are suspended. The direction is to apply the material-participation tests to partnership interests and to confirm the entity form before claiming the exception.

### Overlooking Inflated Drilling Costs And Promoter Fees

The symptom is a partnership whose drilling cost estimate is far above industry norms or whose fee structure extracts the investor's cash as promoter commissions. The trap is that the inflated costs fail economic substance and are disallowed, with accuracy-related penalties, and the investor loses both the deduction and the cash. The direction is to compare the drilling costs to industry norms and to scrutinize the fee structure for shelter patterns.

### Ignoring The Partnership-Level Audit Risk Under The BBA Regime

The symptom is a partner who ignores prior-year partnership filing positions. The trap is that the BBA regime collects partnership-level adjustments at the partnership level in the adjustment year, so a current partner may bear the tax from a reviewed-year adjustment. The direction is to understand the partnership's audit posture and the partnership agreement's tax-allocation provisions before investing or during an audit.

### Treating Investor-Level Activity As Material Participation

The symptom is a partner who logs time reviewing statements and attending meetings and claims material participation. The trap is that investor-level work does not count toward material participation, so the hours are insufficient and the losses are suspended. The direction is to count only operational hours and to document them contemporaneously.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the partnership been evaluated for economic substance, including a comparison of projected deductions to the investor's cash investment, the reasonableness of drilling costs, and the fee structure?
- [ ] Has the at-risk amount been computed to include only cash, property, recourse debt, and qualified nonrecourse financing, with non-qualified nonrecourse debt excluded?
- [ ] Has the passive activity status been determined, with the working-interest exception confirmed to apply only where the interest is held without liability limitation?
- [ ] Has the partner's material participation been evaluated under the seven tests (or the modified limited-partner tests), with only operational hours counted and documented contemporaneously?
- [ ] Has the partnership-level audit risk under the BBA regime been considered, including the partnership's filing positions and the availability of a push-out election?
- [ ] Has the partnership been screened for abusive shelter patterns (deductions exceeding investment, inflated costs, circular financing, promoter fees), with abusive transactions declined?
- [ ] Has the investor been advised that suspended at-risk and passive losses may be carried forward and released only when the conditions are met or the interest is fully disposed of?
- [ ] Has the agent declined to support or prepare a return claiming shelter deductions that exhibit the abusive pattern?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal oil and gas partnership and tax-shelter information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
