# Physical Presence Test

The physical presence test is one of two tests a U.S. citizen or resident alien can meet to qualify for the foreign earned income exclusion. It requires the taxpayer to be physically present in a foreign country for at least 330 full days during any 12-month period. Unlike the bona fide residence test (which is facts-and-circumstances), the physical presence test is an objective day count. It is often the easier test for taxpayers who move abroad mid-year or who are on temporary assignments. An agent who miscounts the days, or who does not understand the 12-month period and the full-day rule, will misapply the qualification.

Agents commonly miss that the test requires 330 full days (not partial days), that the 12-month period can start on any day and does not need to align with the tax year, that travel days over international waters do not count, and that the same days can be used for multiple tax years under certain conditions. The harm is a taxpayer who miscounts and claims the exclusion without qualifying.

This skill covers the physical presence test under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the test is an objective day count that must be verified, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Count 330 Full Days In A Foreign Country

The physical presence test requires the taxpayer to be physically present in a foreign country (or countries) for at least 330 full days during any 12-month period. A full day is a 24-hour period from midnight to midnight in the foreign country. Partial days (days of arrival or departure) do not count as full days. The 330 days must be in foreign countries; days in the U.S. or in international waters do not count. The test is an objective count, not a facts-and-circumstances determination.

Count the 330 full days carefully. A full day is a 24-hour period from midnight to midnight in the foreign country. The day of arrival in the foreign country (a partial day) does not count. The day of departure (a partial day) does not count. Days in the U.S. or traveling over international waters do not count. Track each day of presence in a foreign country. The taxpayer needs 330 full days in a 12-month period. Build in a buffer for travel and U.S. visits. The count is strict; a taxpayer with 329 full days does not qualify. Document the travel itinerary and the day count.

### Understand The 12-Month Period

The 12-month period can start on any day and does not need to align with the tax year. The taxpayer chooses the 12-month period that maximizes the qualifying days. For a taxpayer who moves abroad mid-year, the 12-month period can span two tax years (for example, July 1, 2024, to June 30, 2025). The same 12-month period can be used to qualify for the exclusion in both tax years, but the exclusion is prorated for each tax year based on the qualifying days in that year.

Determine the 12-month period. The period can start on any day. For a taxpayer who moves abroad on July 1, 2024, a 12-month period from July 1, 2024, to June 30, 2025, may capture 330 full days. This period qualifies the taxpayer for the exclusion in both 2024 (prorated for the qualifying days in 2024) and 2025 (prorated for the qualifying days in 2025). The taxpayer chooses the period that maximizes the benefit. The same days cannot be used in two different 12-month periods (once a 12-month period is chosen, it is locked in for those days). Track the 12-month period and the proration for each tax year.

### Handle Partial Year Qualifications

For a taxpayer who qualifies for only part of the tax year (the 12-month period does not cover the full tax year), the exclusion is prorated based on the number of qualifying days in that tax year. The proration fraction is the number of qualifying days in the tax year divided by 365 (or 366 in a leap year). The prorated exclusion is the maximum exclusion multiplied by the fraction.

Compute the proration for partial year qualifications. For a taxpayer who moves abroad on July 1, 2024, and uses a 12-month period from July 1, 2024, to June 30, 2025, the qualifying days in 2024 are the days from July 1 to December 31 (about 184 days, assuming all are full days in a foreign country). The proration fraction for 2024 is 184/365. The prorated exclusion for 2024 is the maximum exclusion (126,500 dollars for 2024) multiplied by 184/365. For 2025, the qualifying days are January 1 to June 30 (about 181 days), and the proration fraction is 181/365. The proration applies to both the FEIE and the housing exclusion. Track the qualifying days and the proration for each tax year.

### Exclude Travel Days And U.S. Days

Days spent traveling over international waters do not count toward the 330 days. The day of departure from the U.S. and the day of arrival in the U.S. do not count as full days in a foreign country (they are partial days). Days in the U.S. for any reason (business, vacation, medical) do not count. The taxpayer must be physically present in a foreign country for the day to count.

Exclude the non-qualifying days. Days traveling over international waters (the day of a long flight from the U.S. to Asia) do not count. Days in the U.S. do not count. Partial days (arrival and departure days) do not count. Only full days (midnight to midnight) in a foreign country count. Track each day and exclude the non-qualifying days from the count. A taxpayer who travels frequently to the U.S. may struggle to reach 330 full days. Build the travel schedule into the count and confirm 330 full days are achievable.

### Handle Overlapping 12-Month Periods And Multiple Tax Years

The same days cannot be used in two different 12-month periods. Once a 12-month period is chosen for a set of days, those days cannot be used in another 12-month period for a subsequent tax year. However, a taxpayer can choose a new 12-month period for the next tax year, as long as it does not overlap with the prior period. This allows the taxpayer to qualify in consecutive years without double-using days.

Manage the overlapping periods. For a taxpayer who uses a 12-month period from July 1, 2024, to June 30, 2025, for the 2024 tax year, the next 12-month period for the 2025 tax year must start after June 30, 2025 (it cannot overlap with the prior period). The taxpayer can choose a new 12-month period (for example, July 1, 2025, to June 30, 2026) for 2025, but the qualifying days in 2025 from the first period (January 1 to June 30, 2025) cannot be reused. Track the periods to avoid double-using days. The rule ensures that each day is counted in only one 12-month period.

### Distinguish The Physical Presence Test From The Bona Fide Residence Test

The physical presence test is an objective day count (330 full days in a 12-month period). The bona fide residence test is a facts-and-circumstances determination of genuine residence for an entire tax year. A taxpayer can meet either test. The physical presence test is often easier for taxpayers who move abroad mid-year or who are on temporary assignments (which do not establish bona fide residence). The bona fide residence test is often easier for taxpayers who have established indefinite residence and who want flexibility for U.S. visits.

Determine which test is more appropriate. The physical presence test is objective and does not require an assessment of intent or ties, but it requires strict day counting and limits U.S. visits. The bona fide residence test is facts-and-circumstances and allows more flexibility for U.S. visits, but it requires an entire tax year and genuine residence. For a taxpayer on a temporary assignment, the physical presence test is the only option (temporary assignments do not establish bona fide residence). For a taxpayer who has moved indefinitely, the bona fide residence test may be easier. Model both tests and choose the one that fits the taxpayer's situation.

## Common Traps

### Counting Partial Days As Full Days

Only full days (midnight to midnight) in a foreign country count. Arrival and departure days are partial days and do not count. Track each day carefully.

### Including Travel Days Over International Waters

Days traveling over international waters do not count. The day of a long flight from the U.S. to Asia does not count toward the 330 days.

### Using The Same Days In Two 12-Month Periods

Once a 12-month period is chosen for a set of days, those days cannot be used in another 12-month period. Track the periods to avoid double-using days.

### Failing To Prorate For Partial Year Qualifications

For a partial year, the exclusion is prorated based on the qualifying days in the tax year. Failing to prorate overstates the exclusion.

### Miscounting U.S. Days

Days in the U.S. for any reason (business, vacation, medical) do not count. Track all U.S. days and exclude them from the count.

### Assuming The 12-Month Period Must Align With The Tax Year

The 12-month period can start on any day. For a taxpayer who moves abroad mid-year, a period spanning two tax years may capture more qualifying days.

### Overlooking The Buffer For Travel

The taxpayer needs 330 full days in a 12-month period (365 or 366 days). This leaves only 35 or 36 non-qualifying days for U.S. visits and travel. Build in a buffer.
