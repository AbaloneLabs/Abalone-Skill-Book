# Foreign Earned Income Exclusion

The foreign earned income exclusion (FEIE) under Section 911 allows a U.S. citizen or resident alien living and working abroad to exclude a significant amount of foreign earned income from U.S. federal income tax. For 2024, the maximum exclusion is 126,500 dollars per qualifying individual. An additional foreign housing exclusion may apply for housing costs above a base amount. The FEIE is one of the most valuable benefits for expatriates, but it requires meeting either the bona fide residence test or the physical presence test, and the mechanics interact with the foreign tax credit in ways that can trap the unwary. An agent who applies the exclusion without verifying the qualification tests or understanding the interactions will misstate the tax benefit.

Agents commonly miss that the exclusion applies only to earned income (not investment income), that the housing exclusion has specific cost limits, that taking the exclusion disqualifies the foreign earned income from the foreign tax credit, and that the exclusion is elected on a per-individual basis. The harm is an expatriate who claims the exclusion without qualifying, or who double-counts foreign income, and faces an IRS adjustment.

This skill covers the foreign earned income exclusion under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the exclusion rules are complex and depend on the individual's circumstances, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Eligibility And The Tax Home Requirement

The FEIE is available to U.S. citizens who are residents of a foreign country, and to resident aliens of the U.S. who are citizens or nationals of a country with which the U.S. has a tax treaty, provided they meet the tax home requirement. The tax home must be in a foreign country. The tax home is the general area of the taxpayer's main place of business or employment, regardless of where the family home is maintained. A taxpayer whose tax home is in the U.S. (for example, an itinerant worker whose abode is in the U.S.) cannot claim the exclusion.

Confirm the eligibility and the tax home requirement. The taxpayer must be a U.S. citizen or a qualifying resident alien, and the tax home must be in a foreign country. The tax home is determined by where the taxpayer's main place of work is located, not where they live. A taxpayer who works abroad but whose abode (family, bank accounts, voter registration) is in the U.S. may not have a foreign tax home. Verify the tax home is in a foreign country. If the tax home is in the U.S., the exclusion is not available. Document the foreign employment and the tax home location.

### Apply The Bona Fide Residence Or Physical Presence Test

To claim the FEIE, the taxpayer must meet either the bona fide residence test or the physical presence test. The bona fide residence test requires the U.S. citizen (or qualifying resident alien) to be a bona fide resident of a foreign country for an uninterrupted period that includes an entire tax year. The physical presence test requires the taxpayer to be physically present in a foreign country for at least 330 full days during any 12-month period. Both tests require the tax home to be in a foreign country.

Determine which test the taxpayer meets. The bona fide residence test requires a full tax year of residence in a foreign country, with the intent to remain (not just a temporary assignment). The physical presence test requires 330 full days of physical presence in a foreign country during any 12-month period (the 12-month period can start on any day and does not need to align with the tax year). A taxpayer who does not meet either test cannot claim the exclusion. For a partial year (the taxpayer moves abroad mid-year), the physical presence test is often easier to meet, using a 12-month period that spans the tax year. Track the days of presence and the residency status.

### Compute The Excludable Foreign Earned Income

The FEIE applies to foreign earned income, which includes wages, salaries, professional fees, and other compensation for personal services performed in a foreign country. The exclusion does not apply to investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains), pension distributions, or income from U.S. government employment. The maximum exclusion for 2024 is 126,500 dollars per qualifying individual. For a partial year (the taxpayer qualifies for only part of the year), the exclusion is prorated based on the number of qualifying days.

Compute the excludable foreign earned income. Identify the foreign earned income (wages for services performed abroad). Apply the maximum exclusion (126,500 dollars for 2024) per individual. For a partial year, prorate based on the number of qualifying days over 365. The exclusion is per individual, so a married couple where both spouses work abroad can each exclude up to 126,500 dollars. The exclusion does not apply to investment income, pensions, or U.S. government pay. Compute the excludable amount and reduce the taxable income accordingly. Document the foreign wages and the exclusion computation.

### Apply The Foreign Housing Exclusion Or Deduction

In addition to the FEIE, a taxpayer may claim a foreign housing exclusion (for employees) or deduction (for self-employed individuals) for reasonable housing expenses in a foreign country that exceed a base amount. The base amount is 16% of the maximum FEIE (about 20,240 dollars for 2024). The housing exclusion is the housing expenses above the base amount, subject to a cap of 30% of the maximum FEIE (about 37,950 dollars for 2024). The cap may be higher in high-cost localities (the IRS publishes a list of high-cost cities with higher caps).

Compute the foreign housing exclusion or deduction. Identify the reasonable housing expenses (rent, utilities, insurance, occupancy taxes, but not mortgage principal, furniture, or domestic help). Subtract the base amount (16% of the maximum FEIE). The remaining amount, up to the cap (30% of the maximum FEIE, or higher in high-cost localities), is the housing exclusion. The housing exclusion is for employees; self-employed individuals take a housing deduction. The housing exclusion reduces the foreign earned income eligible for the FEIE (the total of FEIE and housing exclusion cannot exceed the total foreign earned income). Document the housing expenses and the locality cap.

### Understand The Interaction With The Foreign Tax Credit

The FEIE and the foreign tax credit (FTC) interact in a critical way: foreign earned income that is excluded under the FEIE cannot also generate a foreign tax credit. If the taxpayer excludes income under the FEIE, the foreign taxes paid on that excluded income cannot be claimed as a foreign tax credit. The taxpayer must choose: either exclude the income under the FEIE, or claim the FTC on the income (but not both). This interaction affects the optimal strategy.

Analyze the FEIE-FTC interaction. If the foreign country has a higher tax rate than the U.S., the FTC may be more valuable (the taxpayer can credit the foreign taxes and reduce U.S. tax dollar for dollar). If the foreign country has a lower tax rate, the FEIE may be more valuable (the taxpayer excludes the income entirely). The taxpayer can choose to take the FEIE on some income and the FTC on other income, but cannot take both on the same income. For a taxpayer in a high-tax country, the FTC may be better than the FEIE. Model both options. The election to take the FEIE is binding for future years unless revoked (revocation triggers a 5-year wait to re-elect).

### Elect The Exclusion On Form 2555

The FEIE is elected on Form 2555 (Foreign Earned Income), attached to Form 1040. The form reports the tax home, the qualification test (bona fide residence or physical presence), the foreign earned income, and the exclusion amount. The housing exclusion is also reported on Form 2555. The election is made by filing Form 2555 for the first year the taxpayer wants to claim the exclusion. Once elected, the exclusion applies automatically in future years unless revoked.

Ensure Form 2555 is filed for the year the exclusion is claimed. The form computes the exclusion and the housing exclusion. The election is made by filing the form; there is no separate election statement. Once elected, the exclusion applies in future years unless the taxpayer revokes it. Revocation (by failing to claim the exclusion or by affirmatively revoking) triggers a 5-year period during which the taxpayer cannot re-elect the exclusion without IRS consent. Track the election status. The FEIE election is a significant decision that affects future years.

## Common Traps

### Claiming The Exclusion Without Meeting The Qualification Tests

The taxpayer must meet either the bona fide residence test or the physical presence test. Failing both disqualifies the exclusion. Track the days of presence and the residency status.

### Excluding Investment Income Or Pensions

The FEIE applies only to earned income (wages, salaries, fees). Investment income, pensions, and U.S. government pay are not excludable. Confirm the income type.

### Double-Counting Foreign Income For FEIE And FTC

Foreign earned income excluded under the FEIE cannot generate a foreign tax credit. The taxpayer must choose the FEIE or the FTC for each income item, but not both.

### Forgetting The Housing Exclusion Base And Cap

The housing exclusion has a base amount (16% of the FEIE) and a cap (30% of the FEIE, or higher in high-cost localities). Expenses below the base or above the cap are not excludable.

### Ignoring The Revocation Rule

Once elected, the FEIE applies in future years unless revoked. Revocation triggers a 5-year wait to re-elect without IRS consent. The election is a significant multi-year decision.

### Overlooking The Tax Home Requirement

The tax home must be in a foreign country. A taxpayer whose abode is in the U.S. may not have a foreign tax home, even if they work abroad. Verify the tax home location.

### Failing To Prorate For A Partial Year

For a partial year, the exclusion is prorated based on the number of qualifying days. Failing to prorate overstates the exclusion.
