# Bona Fide Residence Test

The bona fide residence test is one of two tests a U.S. citizen (or qualifying resident alien) can meet to qualify for the foreign earned income exclusion. It requires the taxpayer to be a bona fide resident of a foreign country for an uninterrupted period that includes an entire tax year. Unlike the physical presence test (which counts days), the bona fide residence test is a facts-and-circumstances determination of whether the taxpayer has genuinely established residence in a foreign country. An agent who treats the test as a simple day-count or who overlooks the intent and ties analysis will misapply the qualification.

Agents commonly miss that the test requires an entire tax year (January 1 to December 31 for a calendar-year taxpayer), that intent matters (a temporary assignment does not establish bona fide residence), that the taxpayer can visit the U.S. without breaking residence, and that the determination is facts-and-circumstances. The harm is a taxpayer who claims the exclusion without genuinely establishing residence and faces an IRS denial.

This skill covers the bona fide residence test under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the test is a facts-and-circumstances determination, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Entire Tax Year Requirement

The bona fide residence test requires the taxpayer to be a bona fide resident of a foreign country for an uninterrupted period that includes an entire tax year. For a calendar-year taxpayer, this means bona fide residence from January 1 to December 31 of the same year. A taxpayer who moves abroad mid-year cannot meet the bona fide residence test for that year (they would need to rely on the physical presence test for the first year). The entire tax year requirement is strict; partial years do not qualify.

Confirm the entire tax year requirement. The taxpayer must be a bona fide resident for a full tax year (January 1 to December 31 for a calendar-year taxpayer). A taxpayer who moves abroad on July 1, 2024, cannot meet the bona fide residence test for 2024 (they were not a resident for the full year). They would need to meet the physical presence test for 2024 (330 days in a 12-month period) and could meet the bona fide residence test for 2025 (a full year of residence). Track the residency period and confirm it includes an entire tax year.

### Determine Bona Fide Residence By Facts And Circumstances

Bona fide residence is a facts-and-circumstances determination. The IRS looks at whether the taxpayer has genuinely established residence in a foreign country, as opposed to being there temporarily. Factors include the intention of the taxpayer, the purpose of the trip, the length and nature of the stay, the nature and location of the taxpayer's residence, the establishment of ties (bank accounts, driver's license, voter registration, family location), and the taxpayer's actions. A taxpayer who intends to return to the U.S. after a temporary assignment is not a bona fide resident.

Assess the bona fide residence by the facts and circumstances. The key question is whether the taxpayer has established a genuine residence in the foreign country, with the intent to remain for an indefinite (not temporary) period. Factors include: intention (does the taxpayer intend to stay indefinitely or return after a set period?), purpose (is the assignment open-ended or for a fixed term?), ties (has the taxpayer established bank accounts, a driver's license, community ties in the foreign country?), and family (is the family with the taxpayer or in the U.S.?). A taxpayer on a 2-year assignment with a clear intent to return is likely not a bona fide resident. A taxpayer who has moved indefinitely, established ties, and has no fixed return date is likely a bona fide resident. Document the intention and the ties.

### Distinguish Temporary Assignment From Bona Fide Residence

A temporary assignment does not establish bona fide residence. If the taxpayer is abroad for a temporary period (for example, a 2-year or 3-year employment contract with a fixed end date), the IRS generally treats them as not a bona fide resident, even if they are physically present for the entire tax year. The distinction is the intent: a temporary assignment implies an intent to return, while bona fide residence implies an intent to remain indefinitely.

Distinguish temporary assignment from bona fide residence. A taxpayer on a fixed-term assignment (2 years, 3 years) with an intent to return is generally not a bona fide resident. The IRS views a fixed-term assignment as temporary, regardless of the length. A taxpayer who has moved indefinitely, with no fixed return date, and who has established ties, is more likely a bona fide resident. The line is not always clear; a taxpayer on a 5-year assignment who renews and stays for 10 years may transition from temporary to bona fide. Document the employment terms and the intention. If the assignment is clearly temporary, rely on the physical presence test instead.

### Allow U.S. Visits Without Breaking Residence

A bona fide resident can visit the U.S. without breaking the foreign residence, provided the visits are temporary and the taxpayer does not establish a U.S. residence. Brief visits for business, vacation, or family events do not break the bona fide residence, as long as the taxpayer clearly intends to return to the foreign country. However, extended U.S. stays or actions that suggest a U.S. residence (enrolling children in U.S. schools, taking a U.S. job) can break the residence.

Track the U.S. visits. Brief visits (a few weeks) for business, vacation, or family events do not break the bona fide residence, as long as the taxpayer returns to the foreign country and maintains the foreign residence. Extended U.S. stays (several months) or actions suggesting a U.S. residence can break the residence. The key is the intention: the taxpayer must intend to return to the foreign country as their residence. Document the visits and the intention. Unlike the physical presence test (which strictly counts days), the bona fide residence test allows more flexibility for U.S. visits, but the visits must be genuinely temporary.

### Apply The Test To Qualifying Resident Aliens

The bona fide residence test is available to U.S. citizens and to qualifying resident aliens. A resident alien of the U.S. must be a citizen or national of a country with which the U.S. has an income tax treaty, and must be a bona fide resident of a foreign country for the entire tax year. A resident alien who is not a citizen or national of a treaty country cannot use the bona fide residence test (but may use the physical presence test).

Confirm the eligibility for resident aliens. A resident alien must be a citizen or national of a treaty country to use the bona fide residence test. Verify the treaty status of the alien's home country. A resident alien who is not from a treaty country cannot use the bona fide residence test, even if they meet the residence requirement; they must rely on the physical presence test. U.S. citizens can use either test regardless of the foreign country. Document the citizenship and treaty status.

### Handle Waiver Of Time Requirements

In limited cases, the IRS may waive the time requirements for the bona fide residence test if the taxpayer had to leave the foreign country due to war, civil unrest, or other adverse conditions. The waiver allows the taxpayer to claim the exclusion without meeting the full-year requirement. The IRS publishes a list of countries for which the waiver applies, and the taxpayer must demonstrate they left due to the adverse conditions.

Check for a waiver of time requirements. If the taxpayer had to leave the foreign country due to war, civil unrest, or other adverse conditions (listed by the IRS), the time requirement may be waived. The taxpayer must demonstrate they left due to the adverse conditions and would otherwise have met the requirement. Document the adverse conditions and the departure. The waiver is limited and does not apply to routine departures. Consult the IRS list of waiver countries for the applicable year.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Test As A Day Count

The bona fide residence test is a facts-and-circumstances determination, not a day count. The physical presence test counts days; the bona fide residence test assesses intent and ties. Do not apply a day count.

### Claiming Residence For A Partial Tax Year

The test requires an entire tax year (January 1 to December 31 for a calendar-year taxpayer). A partial year does not qualify. Use the physical presence test for the first year.

### Treating A Temporary Assignment As Bona Fide Residence

A temporary assignment (fixed-term contract with an intent to return) does not establish bona fide residence. The IRS views fixed-term assignments as temporary.

### Breaking Residence With Extended U.S. Stays

Brief U.S. visits do not break residence, but extended stays or actions suggesting a U.S. residence can. Track the visits and the intention to return.

### Overlooking The Treaty Requirement For Resident Aliens

A resident alien must be a citizen or national of a treaty country to use the bona fide residence test. Non-treaty-country resident aliens must use the physical presence test.

### Assuming The Waiver Applies To Routine Departures

The time requirement waiver applies only to war, civil unrest, or adverse conditions listed by the IRS. Routine departures do not qualify.
