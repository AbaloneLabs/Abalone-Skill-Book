# Separation of Liability

Separation of liability under Section 6015(c) is the relief category that allocates the understatement (or the underpayment) between the spouses as if they had filed separate returns, so the requesting spouse is liable only for their share. The judgment problem is that the allocation requires identifying which items of income, deduction, and credit are attributable to each spouse, and the allocation interacts with the actual-knowledge bar (if the requesting spouse had actual knowledge of an erroneous item, that item is allocated to them) and the fraudulent-transfer bar (if the requesting spouse transferred assets fraudulently, the relief is barred). An agent who misallocates the items, or who overlooks the actual-knowledge or the fraudulent-transfer bar, produces an incorrect allocation that either overstates or understates the requesting spouse's liability. The allocation also interacts with the divorce decree (which may require one spouse to indemnify the other, but which does not bind the IRS), the community property laws, and the deceased spouse rules.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the allocation is made as if the spouses had filed separate returns, so the income and the deductions are allocated based on their source, not based on a 50/50 split. Second, an item of which the requesting spouse had actual knowledge is allocated to the requesting spouse, even if the item is attributable to the non-requesting spouse, so the actual knowledge overrides the source allocation. Third, the fraudulent-transfer bar applies if the requesting spouse transferred assets with the intent to avoid collection, and it bars the entire relief, not just the allocation. Fourth, a divorce decree that requires one spouse to pay the tax does not bind the IRS, so the requesting spouse may still seek c-relief even if the decree says otherwise. Fifth, the c-relief allocation applies to the understatement and the underpayment, but it does not apply to the penalties and the interest, which are allocated separately. Sixth, the deceased spouse rules apply if the non-requesting spouse is deceased, and the requesting spouse may still seek c-relief, but the IRS's ability to collect from the deceased spouse's estate may be limited.

This skill covers the separation of liability allocation under Section 6015(c) under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Allocate The Items As If Separate Returns Were Filed

The c-relief allocation is made as if the spouses had filed separate returns, so the income, the deductions, and the credits are allocated based on their source. Income earned by the requesting spouse is allocated to the requesting spouse, and income earned by the non-requesting spouse is allocated to the non-requesting spouse. Deductions related to the requesting spouse's income (such as business expenses) are allocated to the requesting spouse, and deductions related to the non-requesting spouse's income are allocated to the non-requesting spouse. Shared deductions (such as the standard deduction or the itemized deductions) are allocated based on the source of the funds or on a 50/50 basis if the source is unclear. The allocation is not a 50/50 split of the total liability; it is a source-based allocation.

Allocate the items as if separate returns were filed. Identify each item of income, deduction, and credit on the joint return, and determine the source. Income is allocated to the spouse who earned it (the W-2 income to the employee, the business income to the business owner, the investment income to the owner of the assets). Deductions are allocated to the spouse whose income they relate to (the business expenses to the business owner, the investment expenses to the investor). Shared deductions are allocated based on the source of the funds or on a 50/50 basis. Compute the requesting spouse's share of the understatement or the underpayment based on the source allocation. The source-based allocation can significantly reduce the requesting spouse's liability if most of the understatement is attributable to the non-requesting spouse's items, so the allocation should be done carefully.

### Apply The Actual-Knowledge Override

Under Section 6015(c), if the requesting spouse had actual knowledge of an erroneous item that gave rise to the understatement, that item is allocated to the requesting spouse, even if the item is attributable to the non-requesting spouse. The actual-knowledge override prevents a requesting spouse from escaping liability for an item they knew about. The actual knowledge must be of the specific erroneous item (such as the unreported income or the overstated deduction), and it must exist at the time of signing the return. The IRS bears the burden of proving actual knowledge by a preponderance of the evidence.

Apply the actual-knowledge override. For each erroneous item attributable to the non-requesting spouse, determine whether the requesting spouse had actual knowledge of the item at the time of signing the return. If so, the item is allocated to the requesting spouse, increasing their share of the liability. The actual knowledge must be of the specific erroneous item, not just of the general financial situation. The IRS bears the burden of proof, so the requesting spouse does not need to prove a negative. The actual-knowledge override is a significant exception to the source allocation, so the agent should analyze each erroneous item for actual knowledge. A requesting spouse who had actual knowledge of some items but not others is allocated only the items they knew about, so the allocation is item-by-item.

### Evaluate The Fraudulent-Transfer Bar

The c-relief election is barred if the requesting spouse made a fraudulent transfer of assets with the intent to avoid the collection of tax, or if the requesting spouse entered into a fraudulent scheme with the non-requesting spouse to evade tax. The fraudulent-transfer bar applies to the entire relief, not just to the allocation, so a requesting spouse who made a fraudulent transfer cannot elect c-relief. The bar is intended to prevent a requesting spouse from transferring assets to the non-requesting spouse (or to a third party) to avoid collection and then seeking c-relief. The fraudulent intent is the key element, and it is evaluated based on the circumstances of the transfer (such as a transfer to a related party, a transfer below fair market value, or a transfer after the tax liability arose).

Evaluate the fraudulent-transfer bar. Determine whether the requesting spouse transferred assets with the intent to avoid collection, based on the circumstances of the transfer. A transfer to a related party (such as a family member), a transfer below fair market value, or a transfer after the tax liability arose may indicate fraudulent intent. If the fraudulent intent is established, the c-relief election is barred. The bar is severe, because it eliminates the entire relief, so the agent should evaluate the transfer history carefully. A legitimate transfer (such as a transfer in a divorce settlement at fair market value) does not trigger the bar, so the agent should distinguish the legitimate transfers from the fraudulent ones.

### Address The Effect Of A Divorce Decree

A divorce decree that requires one spouse to pay the tax or to indemnify the other spouse does not bind the IRS. The IRS is not a party to the divorce, and the decree is a matter of state law, so the IRS can still collect the tax from either spouse (subject to the innocent spouse relief). A requesting spouse may seek c-relief even if the divorce decree says they are responsible for the tax, because the c-relief is a federal tax relief, not a state-law obligation. However, the requesting spouse's indemnification right (under the divorce decree) may allow them to recover from the non-requesting spouse after the IRS collects, but this is a separate matter.

Address the effect of the divorce decree. Determine whether the divorce decree allocates the tax liability or requires indemnification, and advise the requesting spouse that the decree does not bind the IRS. The requesting spouse may seek c-relief regardless of the decree, because the c-relief is a federal relief. If the IRS collects from the requesting spouse despite the decree, the requesting spouse may have a state-law claim against the non-requesting spouse for indemnification, but this is a separate matter. The agent should not assume that the divorce decree resolves the tax liability, because it does not bind the IRS, and the requesting spouse should be advised to seek c-relief if eligible. The divorce decree is relevant to the requesting spouse's financial situation (such as the division of assets), but it does not determine the federal tax relief.

### Allocate The Penalties And The Interest

The c-relief allocation applies to the understatement and the underpayment, but the penalties and the interest are allocated separately. The penalties (such as the failure-to-pay penalty and the accuracy-related penalty) are allocated based on the requesting spouse's share of the liability, and the interest is allocated based on the requesting spouse's share. However, some penalties (such as the failure-to-file penalty) may apply to the entire return, and the allocation may differ. The agent should allocate the penalties and the interest based on the applicable rules, and the requesting spouse should be advised of the separate allocation.

Allocate the penalties and the interest. Determine the penalties and the interest on the joint return, and allocate them based on the requesting spouse's share of the liability. The failure-to-pay penalty and the accuracy-related penalty are generally allocated based on the share, but the failure-to-file penalty may apply to the entire return. The interest is allocated based on the share. The separate allocation is important because the penalties and the interest can be significant, and the requesting spouse's total liability includes their share of the tax, the penalties, and the interest. The agent should compute the total allocation (tax, penalties, interest) to advise the requesting spouse of their total exposure.

### Address The Deceased Spouse And The Estate

If the non-requesting spouse is deceased, the requesting spouse may still seek c-relief (the relief is available to a widowed spouse). The IRS's ability to collect from the deceased spouse's estate depends on the estate's assets and the state's probate laws, and the collection may be limited. The requesting spouse's c-relief allocation is based on the source allocation, as if separate returns were filed, and the deceased spouse's share is collectible from the estate (subject to the probate laws). The agent should coordinate the c-relief with the probate proceedings, because the estate's assets may be available to satisfy the deceased spouse's share.

Address the deceased spouse and the estate. If the non-requesting spouse is deceased, confirm that the requesting spouse may seek c-relief as a widowed spouse. Allocate the liability based on the source, and determine the deceased spouse's share. The IRS's ability to collect from the estate depends on the estate's assets and the probate laws, so the agent should coordinate with the probate proceedings. The requesting spouse's c-relief allocation is based on the source, so the deceased spouse's share is collectible from the estate (if the estate has assets). The deceased spouse rules are a specialized issue, so the agent should seek qualified tax counsel for cases involving a deceased non-requesting spouse.

## Common Traps

### Using A 50/50 Split Instead Of A Source-Based Allocation

The symptom is an agent who allocates the liability 50/50 between the spouses. The trap is that the allocation is source-based (as if separate returns were filed), so a 50/50 split is incorrect. The direction is to allocate each item based on its source (the income to the earner, the deductions to the related spouse).

### Overlooking The Actual-Knowledge Override

The symptom is an agent who allocates an erroneous item to the non-requesting spouse without checking the requesting spouse's actual knowledge. The trap is that the actual-knowledge override allocates the item to the requesting spouse if they had actual knowledge. The direction is to analyze each erroneous item for actual knowledge and to allocate accordingly.

### Missing The Fraudulent-Transfer Bar

The symptom is a requesting spouse who transferred assets to a family member before filing for c-relief, and the agent does not evaluate the bar. The trap is that a fraudulent transfer bars the entire c-relief election. The direction is to evaluate the transfer history for fraudulent intent and to advise the requesting spouse of the bar.

### Assuming The Divorce Decree Binds The IRS

The symptom is an agent who tells the requesting spouse they cannot seek c-relief because the divorce decree says they are responsible. The trap is that the divorce decree does not bind the IRS, so the requesting spouse may still seek c-relief. The direction is to advise the requesting spouse that the decree is a state-law matter and that the c-relief is a federal relief.

### Failing To Allocate The Penalties And The Interest

The symptom is an agent who allocates only the tax and ignores the penalties and the interest. The trap is that the penalties and the interest are allocated separately, and they can be significant. The direction is to allocate the tax, the penalties, and the interest based on the applicable rules.

### Overlooking The Deceased Spouse Rules

The symptom is an agent who does not consider c-relief for a widowed spouse because the non-requesting spouse is deceased. The trap is that c-relief is available to a widowed spouse, and the deceased spouse's share may be collectible from the estate. The direction is to confirm the widowed spouse's eligibility and to coordinate with the probate proceedings.
