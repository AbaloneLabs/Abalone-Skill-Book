# Relief Categories and Eligibility

Innocent spouse relief under Section 6015 allows a spouse to be relieved of joint and several liability for an understatement or an underpayment of tax on a jointly filed return. The statute provides three distinct categories of relief, each with its own eligibility requirements, scope, and procedural rules: innocent spouse relief (Section 6015(b)), which applies to understatements due to erroneous items of the other spouse; separation of liability (Section 6015(c)), which allocates the understatement or underpayment between the spouses based on their respective contributions; and equitable relief (Section 6015(f)), which is a discretionary relief for items that do not qualify under the other two categories, including underpayments (where the tax was reported but not paid). The judgment problem is that the three categories have different eligibility requirements and different outcomes, and an agent who files under the wrong category, or who misses the procedural deadlines, forfeits the relief. The categories also interact with the marital status (whether the spouses are still married, separated, or divorced), the knowledge of the erroneous items, and the economic hardship of the requesting spouse.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the three categories are not interchangeable: innocent spouse relief (b-relief) applies to understatements, separation of liability (c-relief) applies to understatements and underpayments but requires separation or divorce (or abandonment), and equitable relief (f-relief) applies to both understatements and underpayments and is available to married spouses. Second, the knowledge requirement differs: b-relief requires that the requesting spouse did not know and had no reason to know of the erroneous item, while c-relief requires that the requesting spouse did not have actual knowledge of the erroneous item. Third, the election of c-relief is barred if the requesting spouse made a fraudulent transfer or entered into a fraudulent scheme with the non-requesting spouse. Fourth, the deadline for b-relief and c-relief is the later of the expiration of the collection statute or one year from the date of collection activity, while f-relief has no explicit deadline but is subject to laches and the IRS's discretion. Fifth, the relief applies to the requesting spouse only, and the non-requesting spouse remains liable for the full amount.

This skill covers the three categories of innocent spouse relief and the eligibility requirements under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Three Relief Categories And Their Scope

Section 6015 provides three categories of relief. Innocent spouse relief under Section 6015(b) applies to an understatement of tax (where the tax reported on the joint return was less than the correct tax, due to an erroneous item of the non-requesting spouse). The requesting spouse must establish that they did not know and had no reason to know of the erroneous item, that it would be inequitable to hold them liable, and that the relief is sought within the collection statute. Separation of liability under Section 6015(c) allocates the understatement (or the underpayment) between the spouses based on their respective contributions, and it is available to a spouse who is separated, divorced, widowed, or abandoned, or who has not been a member of the same household for the 12 months preceding the election. Equitable relief under Section 6015(f) is a discretionary relief for items that do not qualify under (b) or (c), including underpayments (where the tax was correctly reported but not paid), and it is available based on a multi-factor test.

Identify the relief category based on the nature of the liability and the marital status. For an understatement (the tax was understated due to an erroneous item), consider b-relief (if the requesting spouse had no knowledge) or c-relief (if separated or divorced). For an underpayment (the tax was reported but not paid, often because the non-requesting spouse spent the withheld taxes), consider f-relief (equitable relief), because b-relief and c-relief do not apply to underpayments. For a spouse who is still married and living with the non-requesting spouse, consider b-relief or f-relief, because c-relief requires separation or divorce. The category selection drives the eligibility analysis and the outcome, so it must be made carefully. The agent should not assume that all relief is "innocent spouse relief" under (b), because the three categories have different requirements.

### Apply The Eligibility Requirements For Innocent Spouse Relief (B-Relief)

Innocent spouse relief under Section 6015(b) requires the following: a joint return was filed; there was an understatement of tax attributable to an erroneous item of the non-requesting spouse; the requesting spouse did not know and had no reason to know of the erroneous item when they signed the return; taking into account all the facts and circumstances, it would be inequitable to hold the requesting spouse liable; and the election is made within the collection statute. The "reason to know" standard is objective, based on whether a reasonable person in the requesting spouse's position would have known. The inequitable determination considers whether the requesting spouse significantly benefited from the erroneous item.

Apply the eligibility requirements for b-relief. Confirm that a joint return was filed and that the understatement is attributable to an erroneous item of the non-requesting spouse (such as unreported income or an overstated deduction). Evaluate whether the requesting spouse knew or had reason to know of the erroneous item, based on the facts (the requesting spouse's involvement in the financial affairs, the presence of the erroneous item in the records, the lifestyle of the spouses). Evaluate the inequitable factor, considering whether the requesting spouse significantly benefited from the erroneous item. File the election on Form 8857 within the collection statute. The b-relief analysis is fact-intensive, and the requesting spouse bears the burden of proof, so the documentation should support each requirement.

### Apply The Eligibility Requirements For Separation Of Liability (C-Relief)

Separation of liability under Section 6015(c) allocates the understatement (or the underpayment) between the spouses based on their respective contributions, as if they had filed separate returns. The requesting spouse must be separated, divorced, widowed, or abandoned, or not a member of the same household as the non-requesting spouse for the 12 months preceding the election. The requesting spouse must not have actual knowledge of the erroneous item that gave rise to the understatement. The c-relief election is barred if the requesting spouse made a fraudulent transfer of assets to avoid collection or entered into a fraudulent scheme with the non-requesting spouse. The allocation is made based on the source of the income and the deductions, so the requesting spouse is liable only for their share.

Apply the eligibility requirements for c-relief. Confirm the marital status (separated, divorced, widowed, abandoned, or not in the same household for 12 months), and confirm that the requesting spouse does not have actual knowledge of the erroneous item. The "actual knowledge" standard is subjective, based on whether the requesting spouse actually knew of the erroneous item (a lower standard than the "reason to know" of b-relief). Evaluate the fraudulent transfer and the fraudulent scheme bars. Allocate the liability based on the respective contributions of the spouses, as if they had filed separate returns. File the election on Form 8857 within the collection statute. The c-relief analysis is valuable for a separated or divorced spouse, because it allocates the liability rather than requiring the full b-relief showing.

### Apply The Eligibility Requirements For Equitable Relief (F-Relief)

Equitable relief under Section 6015(f) is a discretionary relief for items that do not qualify under (b) or (c), including underpayments (where the tax was reported but not paid). The IRS evaluates equitable relief under a multi-factor test, detailed in Revenue Procedure 2013-34. The factors include marital status (separated or divorced is favorable), economic hardship (if the collection would leave the requesting spouse unable to meet basic living expenses), knowledge or reason to know (less knowledge is favorable), compliance with tax laws (current compliance is favorable), and whether the non-requesting spouse abused or controlled the requesting spouse. The IRS first applies a streamlined threshold (for liabilities of USD 500,000 or less and certain other conditions), and if the threshold is not met, it applies the full multi-factor test.

Apply the eligibility requirements for f-relief. Determine whether the liability is an understatement or an underpayment (f-relief is the only category for underpayments). Evaluate the multi-factor test, with emphasis on economic hardship (the requesting spouse's ability to meet basic living expenses), knowledge or reason to know, marital status, compliance, and abuse or control. File the request on Form 8857. The f-relief is discretionary, so the IRS weighs the factors, and the requesting spouse should document the factors thoroughly. The streamlined threshold (for liabilities of USD 500,000 or less) provides a faster path, but the full multi-factor test applies if the threshold is not met. The f-relief is the safety net for items that do not qualify under (b) or (c), and it is particularly important for underpayments.

### Meet The Election And Timing Requirements

The election for b-relief and c-relief must be made on Form 8857 within the later of two years from the date of the first collection activity against the requesting spouse or the expiration of the collection statute of limitations. The two-year deadline was reinstated after a temporary suspension, and it is a hard deadline. For f-relief, there is no explicit statutory deadline, but the IRS applies a practical deadline based on laches and the collection statute, so the request should be filed promptly. The election is irrevocable once made, unless the IRS consents to a withdrawal. The non-requesting spouse is notified of the election and has the right to participate in the proceeding.

Meet the election and timing requirements. File Form 8857 within the two-year deadline for b-relief and c-relief (the later of two years from the first collection activity or the collection statute expiration). For f-relief, file promptly, because the IRS applies a practical deadline. The election is irrevocable, so the requesting spouse should be certain before filing. The non-requesting spouse is notified, which may have personal and financial implications (such as in a divorce), so the requesting spouse should be advised of this consequence. The two-year deadline is a common trap, because the requesting spouse may wait too long after the collection activity begins, so the deadline should be monitored.

### Evaluate The Interaction With Community Property And State Law

Innocent spouse relief interacts with community property laws in community property states. In a community property state, the income and the assets may be treated as community property, and the tax liability may be allocated differently. The IRS has guidance (Revenue Ruling 2000-306) on the treatment of community property for innocent spouse purposes, and the relief may be affected by the state's community property laws. The interaction is complex, because the state law determines the property rights, while the federal law determines the tax relief.

Evaluate the interaction with the community property laws. If the spouses reside in a community property state, determine how the state law allocates the income and the assets, and how this affects the innocent spouse relief. The IRS guidance provides that the relief is available in community property states, but the allocation and the factors may differ. The agent should consult the state's community property laws and the IRS guidance, and the requesting spouse should be advised of the interaction. The community property interaction is a specialized issue, so the agent should seek qualified tax counsel for community property cases.

## Common Traps

### Filing Under The Wrong Relief Category

The symptom is a spouse who files for b-relief for an underpayment (where the tax was reported but not paid). The trap is that b-relief applies to understatements, not underpayments, so the relief is denied. The direction is to identify the nature of the liability (understatement or underpayment) and to file under the correct category (f-relief for underpayments).

### Missing The Two-Year Deadline For B-Relief And C-Relief

The symptom is a spouse who files Form 8857 more than two years after the first collection activity. The trap is that the two-year deadline (the later of two years from the first collection activity or the collection statute expiration) is a hard deadline, so the relief is barred. The direction is to file Form 8857 within the two-year deadline.

### Confusing "Reason To Know" With "Actual Knowledge"

The symptom is an agent who applies the "actual knowledge" standard to b-relief. The trap is that b-relief requires that the requesting spouse did not know and had no reason to know (an objective standard), while c-relief requires no actual knowledge (a subjective standard). The direction is to apply the correct knowledge standard for each category.

### Overlooking Equitable Relief For Underpayments

The symptom is a spouse whose liability is an underpayment (the tax was reported but the non-requesting spouse spent the withheld taxes), and the agent does not consider f-relief. The trap is that b-relief and c-relief do not apply to underpayments, so f-relief is the only option. The direction is to consider f-relief for underpayments and to document the multi-factor test.

### Failing To Consider The Fraudulent Transfer Bar For C-Relief

The symptom is a separated spouse who transferred assets to the non-requesting spouse before filing for c-relief. The trap is that a fraudulent transfer (to avoid collection) bars the c-relief election. The direction is to evaluate the fraudulent transfer bar and to advise the requesting spouse of the consequence.

### Assuming Relief Applies To Both Spouses

The symptom is an agent who believes the relief eliminates the liability for both spouses. The trap is that the relief applies to the requesting spouse only, and the non-requesting spouse remains liable for the full amount. The direction is to advise the requesting spouse that the relief is personal and does not affect the non-requesting spouse's liability.
