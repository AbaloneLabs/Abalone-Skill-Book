# Casualty And Disaster Loss Deductions

A casualty loss is the damage, destruction, or loss of property from a sudden, unexpected, or unusual event such as a storm, fire, flood, earthquake, or accident. The deduction for personal casualty losses has been sharply restricted since 2018, and the current rule is narrow: a personal casualty loss is deductible only if the loss is attributable to a federally declared disaster. The judgment problem is that agents often apply pre-2018 rules that allowed all personal casualty losses, or they conflate personal and business casualty losses, which have different rules. A taxpayer who suffers a non-disaster personal loss and claims a deduction files an incorrect return, and a business owner who fails to distinguish a business casualty loss from a personal one misses a deduction that is still available.

The common failures are deducting personal casualty losses that are not tied to a federally declared disaster, miscalculating the loss by using post-event rather than pre-event fair market value or by failing to reduce the loss by insurance reimbursements, applying the per-event and AGI thresholds incorrectly, and overlooking the special rules and exclusions available for qualified disaster relief. The harm is a disallowed deduction with penalties, or a foregone deduction that the taxpayer was entitled to claim.

This skill covers the qualification of casualty and disaster losses, the personal-versus-business distinction, the federally declared disaster requirement, the loss computation and thresholds, and qualified disaster relief. It is not tax advice; casualty loss rules depend on the nature of the event, the property, and disaster declarations, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Loss Qualifies As A Casualty Under The Sudden-Unexpected Test

A casualty loss requires a sudden, unexpected, and unusual event. Sudden means the event is swift, not gradual. Unexpected means it is unanticipated. Unusual means it is not a day-to-day occurrence. Storms, fires, floods, earthquakes, hurricanes, tornadoes, and accidents generally qualify. Gradual damage from progressive deterioration, termite damage, slow leaks, and ordinary wear and tear do not qualify, because they lack suddenness. A loss from a disease affecting property, such as a tree disease, generally does not qualify.

Before claiming a casualty loss, confirm the event meets the sudden-unexpected-unusual test. A common error is deducting damage that accumulated over time as a casualty, when it is actually maintenance or a non-deductible personal loss. Document the event, its date, and its suddenness.

### Apply The Federally Declared Disaster Requirement For Personal Losses

Under current law, a personal casualty loss is deductible as an itemized deduction only if the loss is attributable to a federally declared disaster, meaning the president has declared a major disaster under the Stafford Act. A personal casualty loss from a non-disaster event, such as a house fire not tied to a declared disaster, is not deductible. This is the single most important rule change from the pre-2018 regime, and applying the old rule that allowed all personal casualty losses is a frequent error.

Verify that the event is a federally declared disaster by checking the FEMA declaration. If the event is not a declared disaster, the personal casualty loss is not deductible, regardless of how devastating it was to the taxpayer. Business casualty losses are not subject to this restriction and remain deductible under the general casualty rules.

### Distinguish Personal-Use From Business Property Losses

The rules differ by property type. Personal-use property casualty losses, such as a damaged personal residence, are deductible only for federally declared disasters and are subject to the per-event floor and AGI threshold. Business or income-producing property casualty losses are not subject to the federally declared disaster restriction and are not subject to the personal-loss AGI threshold; they are deducted as a business loss, often as a casualty loss on the appropriate business schedule or as a theft loss.

Classify the property correctly. A rental property is business property; a personal residence is personal-use property. A vehicle used in business may have a business portion subject to business casualty rules. Misclassifying property leads to applying the wrong rules, thresholds, and restrictions.

### Compute The Loss Using The Lesser Of Basis Or Decline In Value

The deductible casualty loss is the lesser of the property's adjusted basis or the decline in fair market value caused by the casualty, reduced by any insurance or other reimbursement. The basis is generally the original cost plus improvements minus depreciation. The decline in value is the difference between the fair market value immediately before and immediately after the event. Using the higher of the two would overstate the loss; the rule uses the lesser to prevent a deduction exceeding the taxpayer's investment.

Obtain a credible appraisal or repair estimate for the decline in value, and confirm the adjusted basis. Reduce the loss by insurance proceeds received or expected, even if not yet collected, because anticipated reimbursement reduces the deductible loss. If insurance produces a gain because the reimbursement exceeds the basis, special gain recognition and deferral rules apply.

### Apply The Per-Event Floor And The AGI Threshold For Personal Losses

For personal casualty losses that qualify (federally declared disasters), each casualty loss is reduced by 100 dollars per event, and the total of all personal casualty losses is further reduced by 10 percent of adjusted gross income. Only the amount exceeding both thresholds is deductible as an itemized deduction. These thresholds mean that small losses and losses for higher-income taxpayers often produce no deduction.

Compute the thresholds in order: reduce each event by 100 dollars, sum the results, then reduce the total by 10 percent of AGI. Because the AGI threshold is large, a casualty loss must be substantial to produce a deductible amount, especially for taxpayers with higher income. Confirm that the resulting deduction is claimed as an itemized deduction, which means it provides no benefit if the taxpayer takes the standard deduction.

### Consider Qualified Disaster Relief Exclusions And Special Rules

Certain qualified disaster relief payments, such as payments from a government agency to reimburse reasonable and necessary personal, family, living, or funeral expenses resulting from a qualified disaster, are excluded from income under Section 139. This exclusion is separate from the casualty loss deduction and can affect the computation, because a reimbursement that is excluded from income may still reduce the deductible loss. Additionally, special rules in specific disaster relief legislation may allow an election to deduct the loss in the prior year, accelerating the benefit.

Check whether the disaster qualifies for Section 139 relief and whether any special legislation permits a prior-year election. These provisions can materially change the timing and amount of the benefit, and missing them forfeits relief that Congress intended.

## Common Traps

### Deducting Personal Casualty Losses From Non-Disaster Events

Current law requires a federally declared disaster for personal casualty losses. Claiming a deduction for a house fire, theft, or storm not tied to a declaration is an error.

### Treating Gradual Damage As A Casualty

Termite damage, slow leaks, progressive deterioration, and ordinary wear lack suddenness and do not qualify. Deducting them as casualty losses is incorrect.

### Using Post-Event Value Instead Of The Decline In Value

The loss is the decline in fair market value, measured before and after the event, not simply the post-event value. Using the wrong measure overstates or understates the loss.

### Failing To Reduce The Loss By Insurance Reimbursements

Insurance or other reimbursements, including expected amounts, reduce the deductible loss. Omitting the reduction overstates the deduction.

### Applying The Federally Declared Disaster Rule To Business Property

Business casualty losses are not restricted to declared disasters. Treating a business loss under the personal-loss restriction forecloses a legitimate deduction.

### Overlooking The Prior-Year Election And Section 139 Relief

Special disaster legislation may allow a prior-year deduction, and Section 139 may exclude certain relief payments from income. Missing these provisions forfeits intended relief.

### Forgetting The AGI Threshold Eliminates Small Losses

The 10 percent AGI threshold means many personal casualty losses produce no deduction, especially for higher-income taxpayers. Claiming the full loss without applying the threshold overstates the deduction.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the event been confirmed as sudden, unexpected, and unusual, excluding gradual damage and progressive deterioration?
- [ ] For personal-use property, has the loss been confirmed as attributable to a federally declared disaster under the Stafford Act?
- [ ] Has the property been classified as personal-use or business, with the appropriate rules applied to each?
- [ ] Has the loss been computed as the lesser of adjusted basis or decline in fair market value, reduced by insurance and other reimbursements?
- [ ] For personal losses, have the 100 dollar per-event floor and the 10 percent AGI threshold been applied in order, with only the excess claimed as an itemized deduction?
- [ ] Have qualified disaster relief exclusions under Section 139 and any special prior-year election rules been checked?
- [ ] Has the interaction with insurance gain recognition and deferral been considered where reimbursement exceeds basis?
