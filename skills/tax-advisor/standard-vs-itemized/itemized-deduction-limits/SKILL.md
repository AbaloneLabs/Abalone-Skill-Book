# Itemized Deduction Limits

Each category of itemized deduction has its own limit, floor, or suspension, and applying the wrong limit is one of the most common return errors. The judgment problem is that the limits interact and several were changed by the TCJA: the SALT cap ($10,000) restricts state and local taxes, the medical floor is 7.5% of AGI, charitable contributions are capped at 60% (cash) or 30%/20% (property) of AGI with five-year carryforward, mortgage interest is deductible only on acquisition debt up to $750,000 for loans originated after December 15, 2017, the 2% miscellaneous floor is suspended through 2025, and the Pease limitation that reduced total itemized deductions for high earners is also suspended through 2025.

Agents frequently apply the pre-TCJA rules or mix up the limits across categories. They deduct the full property tax ignoring the $10,000 SALT cap, they apply a 50% cash charitable limit instead of the current 60%, they deduct mortgage interest on a cash-out refinance above the acquisition-debt limit, and they forget that miscellaneous itemized deductions (unreimbursed employee expenses, tax prep fees) are suspended through 2025. The harm is an overstated deduction that the IRS disallows on examination, often with accuracy-related penalties.

This skill covers the category-by-category limits on itemized deductions under U.S. federal tax law, including the SALT cap, the medical floor, the charitable percentage limits, the mortgage interest debt limits, the suspended miscellaneous floor, and the suspended Pease limitation. It is not tax advice; the limits involve fact-specific determinations (debt tracing, contribution characterization, AGI computation) that must be verified, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Dollar amounts and suspensions are current through 2025 and are subject to change after that.

## Core Rules

### Apply The SALT Cap At $10,000 ($5,000 MFS)

The deduction for state and local taxes (SALT), including property taxes plus either state and local income taxes or state and local sales taxes (taxpayer's choice), is capped at $10,000 ($5,000 for Married Filing Separately) through 2025. The cap applies to the combined total of property taxes and the chosen income-or-sales taxes, not to each separately. Foreign real property taxes and foreign income taxes are not subject to the cap but are deductible (subject to the foreign tax credit election).

The cap is the single biggest change to itemizing under the TCJA, and it must be applied before comparing itemized to the standard deduction. A taxpayer with $12,000 of property tax and $9,000 of state income tax has $21,000 of SALT but deducts only $10,000. Confirm which taxes qualify (real property tax, personal property tax, state and local income or sales tax) and apply the combined cap. Note that the choice between income tax and sales tax is made per year; a taxpayer in a no-income-tax state deducts sales tax, and the optional sales tax tables can be supplemented with actual major purchases. For pass-through entity owners, the entity-level pass-through entity tax (PTET) is not subject to the SALT cap, which is a key workaround.

### Apply The 7.5% AGI Floor To Medical Expenses

Medical expenses are deductible only to the extent they exceed 7.5% of AGI. Only unreimbursed medical expenses for the taxpayer, spouse, and dependents qualify, including doctor and hospital bills, prescription drugs, dental and vision care, medical insurance premiums (including Medicare premiums and long-term care premiums subject to age limits), and medical transportation (including mileage at the medical rate, approximately 21 cents per mile for 2024). The floor is computed on AGI, so lowering AGI (through retirement contributions, above-the-line deductions) increases the medical deduction.

The 7.5% floor is where most medical deductions are lost. A taxpayer with $80,000 AGI can deduct only medical expenses above $6,000 (7.5% of $80,000); $6,000 of medical expenses yields no deduction. Confirm AGI first, then subtract the floor, then deduct the excess. Also confirm that each expense qualifies (cosmetic surgery generally does not, over-the-counter drugs without a prescription do not, but insulin does). Long-term care premiums are subject to age-based sub-limits (2024 approximately $470 under 40, $880 40-49, $1,760 50-59, $4,710 60-69, $5,880 70+), which further limits the deduction.

### Apply The Charitable Contribution Percentage Limits With Carryforward

Cash contributions to public charities are deductible up to 60% of AGI (temporarily 100% in 2020-2021, now back to 60%). Contributions of appreciated long-term capital gain property to public charities are limited to 30% of AGI (at fair market value, with a 20% limit and basis-only deduction for certain capital gain property to private foundations). Contributions to most private foundations are limited to 30% of AGI (cash) or 20% (capital gain property). Excess contributions carry forward for up to five years, subject to the same limits in the carryforward year.

The trap is applying the wrong percentage. A $100,000 cash gift to a public charity is deductible up to 60% of AGI, but a $100,000 gift of appreciated stock to the same charity is limited to 30% of AGI. Confirm the type of charity (public charity, private foundation, donor-advised fund) and the type of contribution (cash, property, capital gain property) before applying the limit. Also confirm substantiation: contributions of $250 or more require a contemporaneous written acknowledgment from the charity, and non-cash contributions over $500 require Form 8283, with a qualified appraisal for non-cash contributions over $5,000. A deduction without the required substantiation is disallowed regardless of merit.

### Apply The Mortgage Interest Acquisition Debt Limit

Home mortgage interest is deductible on acquisition debt (debt used to buy, build, or substantially improve the residence) up to $750,000 for loans originated after December 15, 2017 ($1,000,000 for loans originated on or before that date, grandfathered). Interest on home equity debt used for purposes other than buying, building, or improving the residence is not deductible through 2025 (the pre-TCJA $100,000 home equity debt deduction is suspended). The limit applies per residence, and to the taxpayer's primary and one secondary residence combined.

The debt-tracing requirement is the key. A cash-out refinance where the extra proceeds paid off credit cards or funded a vacation produces interest that is not deductible, because the proceeds were not used to acquire or improve the residence. Trace the use of the loan proceeds: only the portion used to acquire or substantially improve the residence counts as acquisition debt. Confirm the loan origination date (for the $750,000 vs $1,000,000 limit), the use of proceeds (acquisition versus personal), and the combined balance across the primary and secondary residence. Points paid on a purchase mortgage are generally deductible in the year paid; points on a refinance are amortized over the loan term.

### Recognize That The 2% Miscellaneous Floor Is Suspended Through 2025

Miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2% of AGI floor, including unreimbursed employee business expenses, tax preparation fees, investment expenses (other than investment interest), and safe-deposit box fees, are suspended (not deductible) through 2025 under the TCJA. This suspension is one of the most frequently missed changes, because taxpayers and some preparers still try to deduct unreimbursed employee expenses.

Confirm that any expense previously deducted as a 2% miscellaneous itemized deduction is not claimed for 2018 through 2025. An employee who pays for work tools or uniforms out of pocket gets no deduction in this period. The workaround for some is the self-employed status (Schedule C expenses are not subject to the suspension), but that requires genuine independent contractor classification. Note that the suspension expires after 2025, so unreimbursed employee expenses may be deductible again in 2026, but do not assume the suspension will lapse as scheduled without monitoring the law.

### Recognize That The Pease Limitation Is Suspended Through 2025

The Pease limitation, which reduced total itemized deductions by 3% of AGI over a threshold (capped at 80% of deductions), is suspended through 2025. This means high-income taxpayers can deduct the full amount of their itemized deductions (subject to the per-category limits) without an overall reduction. The Pease limitation is scheduled to return after 2025.

The Pease suspension interacts with the per-category limits. A high-income taxpayer with large charitable contributions and SALT (capped at $10,000) deducts the full allowed amounts without a further Pease reduction, which is more favorable than the pre-TCJA regime. Confirm that no Pease reduction is applied for 2018 through 2025, and flag that it may return after 2025, which would reduce the benefit of itemizing for high earners.

### Confirm The Casualty Loss Restriction

Personal casualty losses are deductible only if the loss is attributable to a federally declared disaster area, through 2025. Casualty losses outside a disaster declaration (a house fire not in a declared disaster, a theft) are not deductible. The deductible amount is the loss over $100 per casualty, further reduced by 10% of AGI.

Confirm that any casualty loss is in a federally declared disaster area before claiming it. The restriction eliminates most personal casualty deductions, which surprises taxpayers who suffered a non-disaster loss. The 10% AGI floor (on top of the $100 per-casualty reduction) further limits the deduction, so even a qualifying disaster loss may yield little benefit for higher-income taxpayers. Note that casualty losses on income-producing property (a rental) are not subject to the disaster-area restriction and are handled under the casualty rules for business or investment property.

## Common Traps

### Deducting Full SALT Ignoring The $10,000 Cap

The SALT cap limits the combined property and income-or-sales tax deduction to $10,000. Applying the pre-TCJA full deduction overstates the itemized total.

### Using A 50% Cash Charitable Limit Instead Of 60%

The cash contribution limit to public charities is 60% of AGI (not 50%), and appreciated property is 30%. Applying the wrong percentage understates or overstates the deduction.

### Deducting Mortgage Interest On Non-Acquisition Debt

Interest on home equity debt used for personal purposes is not deductible through 2025. Tracing the use of proceeds is required to separate acquisition debt from personal debt.

### Claiming Unreimbursed Employee Expenses Through 2025

The 2% miscellaneous floor is suspended, so unreimbursed employee expenses are not deductible for 2018-2025. Claiming them is a clear error.

### Missing The Age-Based Long-Term Care Premium Sub-Limits

LTC premiums are deductible as medical expenses only up to age-based amounts. Deducting the full premium overstates the medical deduction.

### Applying The Pease Limitation During Its Suspension

The Pease reduction of total itemized deductions is suspended through 2025. Applying it understates the deduction for high earners.

### Deducting Casualty Losses Outside A Declared Disaster

Personal casualty losses are deductible only in federally declared disaster areas through 2025. Non-disaster losses are not deductible.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the SALT deduction been capped at $10,000 ($5,000 MFS) for the combined property taxes and state and local income or sales taxes, with the PTET workaround considered for pass-through entity owners?
- [ ] Has the 7.5% AGI floor been applied to medical expenses, with only qualifying expenses included and long-term care premiums capped at the age-based sub-limits?
- [ ] Have charitable contributions been limited to 60% of AGI (cash to public charities), 30% (appreciated property to public charities, cash to private foundations), or 20% (capital gain property to private foundations), with five-year carryforward for excess?
- [ ] Is charitable substantiation in place (contemporaneous written acknowledgment for gifts of $250 or more, Form 8283 for non-cash over $500, qualified appraisal for non-cash over $5,000)?
- [ ] Has mortgage interest been limited to acquisition debt up to $750,000 (post-December 15, 2017 loans) or $1,000,000 (grandfathered loans), with debt tracing to exclude personal-use proceeds?
- [ ] Has the 2% miscellaneous floor suspension been applied, so unreimbursed employee expenses, tax preparation fees, and investment expenses are not deducted for 2018-2025?
- [ ] Has the Pease limitation suspension been applied, so no overall reduction of itemized deductions is made through 2025?
- [ ] Has any casualty loss been confirmed to be in a federally declared disaster area, with the $100 per-casualty and 10% AGI reductions applied?
- [ ] Are all category limits and suspensions flagged as TCJA provisions scheduled to change after 2025, affecting multi-year planning?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal itemized-deduction-limit information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
