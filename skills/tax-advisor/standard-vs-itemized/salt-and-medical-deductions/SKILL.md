# SALT And Medical Deductions

SALT and medical are the two itemized categories where timing and characterization strategy most change the outcome, because both are subject to AGI-based floors or caps and both can be partially controlled by the taxpayer. The judgment problem is that the SALT cap ($10,000) is a hard ceiling that defeats simple prepayment, while the medical floor (7.5% of AGI) is a threshold that can be cleared by accelerating controllable expenses into one year. A taxpayer who understands the difference between a cap and a floor can preserve a medical deduction that would otherwise be lost, and can use the pass-through entity tax (PTET) workaround to deduct state taxes beyond the SALT cap.

Agents frequently advise prepaying property tax without recognizing the SALT cap makes it useless, they choose the state income tax deduction without comparing to the sales tax option for a no-income-tax state or a high-spending year, and they miss the PTET workaround entirely for pass-through entity owners. On medical, they list only doctor bills and miss the qualifying insurance premiums, the long-term care premiums (subject to age limits), and the medical mileage, all of which can push the total over the 7.5% floor. The harm is a lost deduction and a taxpayer who itemizes with a suboptimal result.

This skill covers SALT strategies (property tax timing, income versus sales tax, PTET), the medical expense threshold, what qualifies as medical, and the bunching strategy, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the strategies depend on the taxpayer's facts (entity ownership, AGI, controllable expenses, state law) that must be verified, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Dollar amounts and the SALT cap are current through 2025 and are subject to change.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That The SALT Cap Defeats Most Property Tax Prepayment

The SALT cap of $10,000 ($5,000 MFS) applies to the combined property taxes and state and local income or sales taxes. Prepaying property tax to shift the deduction into the current year only helps if the taxpayer's combined SALT for the year is below the $10,000 cap; once the cap is reached, additional prepayment produces no additional deduction. A taxpayer already at $10,000 of SALT gets zero benefit from prepaying next year's property tax.

Always test whether the taxpayer is below the cap before recommending prepayment. If the taxpayer's state income tax alone approaches $10,000, prepaying property tax is futile. The prepayment also must comply with state law (some states discount or reject prepayment) and must be for an assessed amount, not an estimate. The PTET workaround (below) is the real tool for pass-through entity owners to deduct state taxes beyond the SALT cap, not property tax prepayment.

### Choose Between State Income Tax And State Sales Tax Deliberately

The taxpayer can deduct either state and local income taxes or state and local sales taxes (not both), plus property taxes, within the $10,000 SALT cap. The choice is made annually. Taxpayers in states with no income tax (Texas, Florida, Washington, Nevada, others) deduct sales tax by default, using the IRS optional sales tax tables plus actual major purchases (cars, boats, homebuilding materials). Taxpayers in income-tax states usually deduct income tax, but in a year with a large purchase, the sales tax deduction may exceed the income tax deduction.

Compute both options before choosing. The optional sales tax tables are based on income, family size, and state and local rates, and they can be supplemented with actual receipts for major purchases. A taxpayer who bought a $60,000 vehicle and lives in a no-income-tax state may have a sales tax deduction well above the table amount. Conversely, a taxpayer in a high-income-tax state almost always does better with the income tax deduction. Confirm the choice is made per year and that property taxes are added to whichever income-or-sales option is chosen, all subject to the combined cap.

### Use The Pass-Through Entity Tax Workaround For Entity Owners

The state-level pass-through entity tax (PTET) election allows a partnership or S corporation to pay state income tax at the entity level, which is deductible by the entity as a business expense (bypassing the $10,000 SALT cap, because the cap applies to individuals, not entities). The entity-level tax flows through to the owners as a reduction of their share of entity income, effectively restoring the state tax deduction for pass-through owners. Over 30 states have enacted a PTET, with varying mechanics.

The PTET is the single most valuable SALT strategy for pass-through entity owners, and it is frequently missed because it requires an entity-level election that the owner must request. Confirm the entity is eligible (partnership or S corporation, sometimes LLC), that the state has a PTET regime, and that the election is made at the entity level for the correct year. The deduction bypasses the SALT cap, so an owner who would be capped at $10,000 can deduct their full share of the entity's state tax. Note that the PTET does not work for C corporations or for sole proprietors (Schedule C), and the state's specific rules on credit or deduction at the owner level must be followed.

### Apply The 7.5% AGI Medical Floor And Use AGI Reduction To Help

Medical expenses are deductible only above 7.5% of AGI. The floor is computed on AGI, so reducing AGI increases the deductible medical amount. A taxpayer who contributes to a traditional IRA or a health savings account (HSA) lowers AGI, which lowers the 7.5% floor and increases the deductible medical expenses.

Always compute the floor on the final AGI, not on gross income, and look for AGI-reducing moves (retirement contributions, HSA, above-the-line deductions like the half of self-employment tax) that can push more medical expenses above the floor. For a taxpayer near the threshold, even a small AGI reduction can flip a near-zero medical deduction into a meaningful one. Confirm that each medical expense qualifies and is unreimbursed, and that the expenses are for the taxpayer, spouse, or a dependent (a person who qualifies as a dependent even if not claimed, due to the multiple-support or custodial rules, can have their expenses deducted).

### Include All Qualifying Medical Expenses, Not Just Doctor Bills

Qualifying medical expenses include far more than doctor and hospital bills: health insurance premiums (including Medicare Part B and D premiums, Medicare Advantage premiums, and employer-sponsored plan premiums paid with after-tax dollars), dental and vision care, prescription drugs (insulin qualifies; over-the-counter drugs without a prescription do not), medical transportation including mileage at the medical rate (approximately 21 cents per mile for 2024), lodging (up to $50 per night) while receiving medical care, and long-term care premiums subject to age-based sub-limits. Medical equipment (wheelchairs, hearing aids, glasses) and home modifications medically necessary (entrance ramps, grab bars) also qualify.

The common error is listing only out-of-pocket doctor bills and missing the premiums, the mileage, and the equipment. Medicare premiums are often automatically deducted from Social Security and easy to overlook, but they are a substantial qualifying expense. Long-term care premiums are capped by age (2024 approximately $470 under 40, $880 40-49, $1,760 50-59, $4,710 60-69, $5,880 70 and older), so a 75-year-old paying $12,000 of LTC premiums deducts only $5,880. Build a complete medical expense inventory, including premiums, drugs, transportation, equipment, and qualified home modifications, before applying the 7.5% floor.

### Use Bunching To Clear The Standard Deduction In Alternate Years

Because the standard deduction is all-or-nothing, a taxpayer whose itemizable expenses hover near the standard deduction can benefit from bunching: accelerating charitable contributions and, where controllable, medical expenses and elective procedures into one year to exceed the standard deduction, then taking the standard deduction in the off year. Over two years, bunching can yield more total deduction than splitting evenly.

Bunching works best with controllable expenses. Charitable contributions are the most flexible (a donor-advised fund can front-load multiple years of giving); elective medical procedures (a scheduled surgery, a full year of orthodontia) can be shifted into the bunch year; property tax prepayment helps only below the SALT cap. Model two years under both the even-split and the bunching approach, and confirm the bunched year clears the standard deduction after the SALT cap and the 7.5% medical floor. Do not bunch without checking that the bunched total, after floors and caps, exceeds the standard deduction.

### Coordinate SALT And Medical Strategy With Filing Status And State Conformity

The SALT cap and medical floor interact with filing status and state law. Married Filing Separately faces a $5,000 SALT cap and the coordination rule (if one itemizes, both must). Some states do not conform to the federal SALT cap, allowing a full state-tax deduction on the state return, and some states have their own medical floor or deduction rules. The federal and state results can diverge, and the combined result governs the strategy.

Confirm the filing status's SALT cap and itemizing coordination before finalizing the strategy. For state conformity, check whether the state follows the federal SALT cap, allows its own deduction, or decouples entirely. A taxpayer in a non-conforming state may itemize on the state return even when taking the federal standard deduction, or vice versa. The SALT and medical strategy must be modeled on both the federal and state returns, because a strategy that helps federally may not help on the state return, or the reverse.

## Common Traps

### Prepaying Property Tax When Already At The SALT Cap

Once the $10,000 SALT cap is reached, prepaying property tax produces no additional deduction. Confirm the taxpayer is below the cap before recommending prepayment.

### Defaulting To State Income Tax Without Comparing Sales Tax

In a no-income-tax state or a year with a large purchase, the sales tax deduction may exceed the income tax deduction. Compute both before choosing.

### Missing The PTET Workaround For Pass-Through Entity Owners

The PTET election deducts state tax at the entity level, bypassing the SALT cap. Owners who do not request the entity-level election lose a significant deduction.

### Listing Only Doctor Bills And Missing Premiums, Mileage, And Equipment

Health insurance premiums (including Medicare), medical mileage, and equipment are qualifying medical expenses. Omitting them understates the medical deduction.

### Applying The 7.5% Floor To Gross Income Instead Of AGI

The medical floor is 7.5% of AGI. Reducing AGI (retirement contributions, HSA) lowers the floor and increases the deductible medical amount.

### Deducting Full Long-Term Care Premiums Above The Age Limit

LTC premiums are capped by age. A 75-year-old deducts only approximately $5,880 (2024), not the full premium paid.

### Bunching Without Confirming The Bunched Year Clears The Standard Deduction

Bunching only helps if the bunched year's net itemized (after SALT cap and medical floor) exceeds the standard deduction. Bunching that falls short wastes the off-year standard deduction.

- [ ] Has the choice between state income tax and state sales tax been computed both ways, including the optional sales tax tables plus actual major purchases?
- [ ] For a pass-through entity owner, has the PTET election been evaluated and, if available in the state, requested at the entity level to bypass the SALT cap?
- [ ] Has the 7.5% medical floor been computed on AGI (not gross income), with AGI-reducing moves (retirement contributions, HSA, half-SE-tax) applied to increase the deductible amount?
- [ ] Does the medical expense inventory include health insurance premiums (including Medicare), prescription drugs, dental and vision, medical mileage at the medical rate, equipment, and medically necessary home modifications?

- [ ] Has the SALT and medical strategy been modeled on both the federal and state returns, accounting for state conformity and the state's own deduction rules?
- [ ] Has the filing status interaction (the $5,000 MFS SALT cap and the itemizing coordination rule) been checked?
