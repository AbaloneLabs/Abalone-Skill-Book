# Bonus And Supplemental Withholding

Supplemental wages, bonuses, commissions, and equity vesting income are withheld at flat federal rates that are simple to apply but routinely understate the actual tax. The flat 22 percent federal supplemental rate is below the marginal bracket for most mid-to-high earners, and the 37 percent rate applies only above a $1 million annual supplemental threshold that almost no one hits. The judgment problem is that supplemental withholding feels sufficient because taxes were withheld, yet the withheld amount is often far short of the true liability, producing a year-end shortfall. An agent who sees "taxes withheld on the bonus" and assumes the bonus is covered leaves the taxpayer with a surprise balance due.

Agents tend to miss that the flat 22 percent rate is a withholding rule, not a tax rate, so the bonus is still taxed at the taxpayer's actual marginal bracket on the return; that RSU vesting and ESPP income are supplemental wages subject to the same flat rate and the same underwithholding; and that the aggregate method (optional for the employer) withholds at the regular bracket rate and is more accurate but rarely used for large bonuses. They also miss that state supplemental rates vary widely and can themselves underwithhold, and that equity comp withholding is often capped or uses sell-to-cover that leaves a gap. The harm includes large April balances due, underpayment penalties, and the false belief that "my bonus already had taxes taken out."

This skill covers U.S. federal income tax withholding on supplemental wages under IRC section 3402 and related rules. The 22 percent and 37 percent rates, the $1 million threshold, and state rates are set by statute and regulation and must be verified for the tax year. This is not tax advice. A qualified tax professional, CPA, or equity compensation specialist must be consulted before relying on supplemental withholding planning.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish The Flat Rate From The Actual Tax Rate

The flat 22 percent federal supplemental withholding rate is a withholding mechanism, not the tax the bonus ultimately bears. On the tax return, supplemental wages are combined with regular wages and taxed at the taxpayer's actual marginal rate, which for many earners is 24 percent, 32 percent, 35 percent, or 37 percent. The difference between the 22 percent withheld and the actual marginal rate becomes part of the year-end balance due.

This is the central concept. A $10,000 bonus with 22 percent withholding has $2,200 withheld, but if the taxpayer is in the 32 percent bracket, the actual tax is $3,200, leaving a $1,000 shortfall on that bonus alone. Across multiple bonuses, commissions, or RSU vestings, the shortfall compounds. Never tell a taxpayer their bonus is "covered" because 22 percent was withheld; it is covered only if their marginal rate is 22 percent or lower.

### Apply The 22 Percent And 37 Percent Federal Thresholds

Federal supplemental withholding uses two flat rates. Supplemental wages up to $1 million in a calendar year are withheld at 22 percent. Supplemental wages exceeding $1 million in a calendar year are withheld at 37 percent on the excess. The $1 million threshold is annual and per employer, so only very highly compensated employees ever trigger the 37 percent rate.

For the vast majority of taxpayers, all supplemental wages are withheld at 22 percent. The 37 percent rate is effectively irrelevant except for executives with massive bonuses or equity events. The practical implication is that supplemental withholding is almost always below the marginal rate for anyone in the 24 percent bracket or higher, which is most professionals and above. Plan for the gap rather than relying on the flat rate.

### Understand The Aggregate Method As An Alternative

Employers may choose between two withholding methods for supplemental wages. The flat percentage method withholds 22 percent (or 37 percent above $1 million) if the supplemental pay is not combined with regular wages in the same check. The aggregate method combines the supplemental pay with the most recent regular wage payment, computes tax on the total as if annualized, subtracts the tax already withheld on the regular wages, and withholds the difference, effectively applying the employee's actual bracket rate.

The aggregate method is more accurate because it uses the real marginal rate, but employers rarely use it for large bonuses because it withholds more and reduces the net payout, which employees dislike. Some employers use aggregate for small supplemental payments and flat percentage for large ones. The employee cannot choose the method; the employer decides. Knowing which method an employer uses helps predict the shortfall, but the planning response is the same either way: estimate the gap and cover it through W-4 Step 4(c) or estimated payments.

### Treat RSU And ESPP Income As Supplemental Wages

Restricted Stock Unit (RSU) vesting and Employee Stock Purchase Plan (ESPP) qualifying dispositions generate supplemental wage income. At RSU vesting, the fair market value is ordinary compensation withheld at the flat 22 percent rate (if the employer withholds at all). At an ESPP qualifying disposition, the bargain element is supplemental wages. Both are subject to the same underwithholding dynamic as cash bonuses.

RSU withholding is especially prone to gaps because some plans sell only enough shares to cover the 22 percent federal rate (sell-to-cover), leaving state tax, Social Security, Medicare, and the bracket gap uncovered. A high earner with large RSU vestings can face a substantial April balance due even though shares were sold to cover. For taxpayers with recurring equity comp, build the expected annual vesting value into W-4 Step 4(a) or Step 4(c) so the base paycheck withholds more throughout the year, rather than relying on sell-to-cover at vesting.

### Account For State Supplemental Rates Separately

States set their own supplemental withholding rates, which vary widely. Some states have a flat supplemental rate close to the top marginal rate (more accurate), others have a low flat rate that underwithholds, and some require the aggregate method or have no special supplemental rate and apply regular withholding tables. A bonus withheld adequately for federal purposes can still underwithhold for state.

Check the specific state's supplemental rate and method. A taxpayer in a high-tax state with a low flat supplemental rate faces a state shortfall on top of the federal one. State shortfalls compound the year-end balance due and can trigger state underpayment penalties. Coordinate federal and state supplemental withholding planning together.

### Estimate And Cover The Bracket Gap Proactively

The planning response to supplemental underwithholding is to estimate the annual gap and cover it through the base W-4 or estimated payments. For recurring supplemental income (monthly commissions, quarterly RSU vestings), compute the expected annual supplemental total, multiply by the difference between the marginal rate and 22 percent, and spread that amount across the year via W-4 Step 4(c) extra withholding or quarterly estimated payments.

For one-time events (a single large bonus), the gap can be covered by a one-time estimated payment or a temporary W-4 Step 4(c) increase for a few pay periods. The ratable-withholding advantage makes the W-4 channel preferable if the event occurs mid-year, because the extra withholding is deemed paid evenly and avoids installment shortfalls. Do not wait until April to discover the gap; estimate it when the supplemental income is known.

### Reconcile Supplemental Income On The W-2 And Return

At filing, supplemental wages appear on the W-2 as part of total wages (Box 1), with total federal withholding in Box 2. The bonus is not separately labeled on the W-2 in most cases, though some employers provide a supplemental earnings statement. The tax return taxes the combined wages at the actual brackets, and the total withholding (regular plus supplemental) is credited against it.

Reconcile by confirming total withholding matches all pay stubs (regular and supplemental) and that the combined income is taxed correctly. A common error is double-counting a bonus if it appears on both a supplemental statement and the W-2. Use the W-2 as the authoritative source and retain supplemental statements to support the withholding figure if questioned.

## Common Traps

### Believing The Bonus Is Covered Because 22 Percent Was Withheld

The 22 percent is a withholding rate, not the tax rate. For anyone above the 22 percent bracket, the bonus is underwithheld and adds to the year-end balance due.

### Assuming The 37 Percent Rate Applies To Ordinary Bonuses

The 37 percent supplemental rate applies only above $1 million of supplemental wages in a year. Almost all bonuses are withheld at 22 percent, regardless of size, unless they push the annual supplemental total over $1 million.

### Relying On RSU Sell-To-Cover To Withhold Enough

Sell-to-cover typically withholds only the 22 percent federal rate, leaving state tax, payroll tax, and the bracket gap uncovered. Large recurring vestings create substantial shortfalls.

### Forgetting State Supplemental Rates Differ

State supplemental rates vary and can underwithhold independently of the federal rate. A bonus adequately withheld federally can still create a state shortfall.

### Ignoring The Bracket Gap Until April

Waiting until filing to discover the supplemental underwithholding guarantees a balance due and possible penalty. Estimate the gap when the supplemental income is known and cover it during the year.

### Double-Counting A Bonus On The Return

A bonus included in W-2 Box 1 is already on the return. Adding it again from a supplemental statement double-counts the income and overstates tax.

### Assuming The Aggregate Method Is Used

Employers rarely use the aggregate method for large bonuses because it withholds more. Default to assuming the flat 22 percent rate and plan for the gap.
