# Opportunity Zone Basics

The Opportunity Zone program, created by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, provides three tiers of tax benefits for investing capital gains in economically distressed communities through Qualified Opportunity Funds. Investors can defer tax on an eligible capital gain by reinvesting it in a Qualified Opportunity Fund, receive a basis step-up for holding the investment, and potentially exclude all appreciation on the QOF investment if held for at least 10 years. The program is powerful but complex, with strict timing and qualification rules. An agent who treats Opportunity Zones as a simple tax-deferral vehicle without understanding the structuring, timing, and risk will mislead the investor.

Agents commonly miss that only capital gains are eligible (not ordinary income), that the gain must be invested within 180 days, that the basis step-up benefits have specific holding periods, and that the 10-year exclusion requires a QOF partnership or corporation interest (not a direct real estate investment). The harm is an investor who misses a deadline, invests in a non-qualifying structure, or fails to capture the full benefit.

This skill covers Opportunity Zone basics under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the program has specific deadlines and structuring requirements, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Three Tiers Of Benefits

The Opportunity Zone program provides three tiers of tax benefits. First, deferral: an eligible capital gain can be deferred by investing in a Qualified Opportunity Fund within 180 days of the gain. The deferred gain is recognized on December 31, 2026, or earlier if the QOF investment is sold. Second, basis step-up: if the QOF investment is held for at least 5 years, the basis is increased by 10% of the deferred gain; if held for at least 7 years, an additional 5% step-up (total 15%) applies. Third, the 10-year exclusion: if the QOF investment is held for at least 10 years, the appreciation on the QOF investment itself can be excluded from tax.

Explain the three tiers to the investor. The deferral provides a current tax benefit (deferring the gain to 2026). The basis step-up (for 5-year and 7-year holds) reduces the deferred gain ultimately recognized. The 10-year exclusion is the most valuable benefit: all appreciation on the QOF investment can be excluded if held for at least 10 years. Note that the 5-year and 7-year basis step-ups had deadlines tied to the original program (the 7-year step-up required investment by December 31, 2019, to be meaningful), so for new investments, the primary benefits are deferral and the 10-year exclusion. Present the benefits realistically, noting that the step-up windows have largely closed for new investments.

### Confirm The Gain Is An Eligible Capital Gain

Only capital gains are eligible for Opportunity Zone deferral. This includes capital gains from the sale of stocks, bonds, real estate, businesses, and other capital assets. Ordinary income (wages, interest, dividends taxed as ordinary, business income) does not qualify. Section 1231 gains are eligible to the extent they are capital (after netting). The gain must be a realized capital gain, not a paper gain.

Confirm the gain is an eligible capital gain. Review the source of the gain (sale of stock, real estate, business). Ordinary income does not qualify. Section 1231 gains qualify if they are capital after netting. The investor can invest all or part of the eligible gain; partial investment is allowed. The 180-day investment period begins on the date the gain is realized (the closing date of the sale), or in some cases the due date of the return for pass-through gains. Confirm the gain character and the 180-day start date.

### Invest Within The 180-Day Period

The eligible capital gain must be invested in a Qualified Opportunity Fund within 180 days of the gain realization date. The 180-day period is strict. For direct sales, the realization date is the closing date. For pass-through gains (from a partnership, S corporation, trust, or estate), the 180-day period generally begins on the date the gain is realized by the pass-through entity, though the entity can elect to start the clock at its own realization date or allow the owner to use the entity's realization date.

Track the 180-day investment deadline carefully. For a direct sale, the clock starts at closing. For a pass-through gain, the start date may be the entity's realization date or the date the gain is allocated to the owner; confirm the applicable rule. The investor must invest in a QOF (not directly in Opportunity Zone property) within the 180 days. Missing the deadline forfeits the deferral. Build the QOF selection and investment process into the timeline from the gain realization date.

### Invest Through A Qualified Opportunity Fund

The investment must be made through a Qualified Opportunity Fund, which is an investment vehicle organized as a partnership or corporation that invests at least 90% of its assets in Qualified Opportunity Zone property. The investor cannot invest directly in Opportunity Zone property (real estate or a business in a QOZ); the investment must be in a QOF. The QOF is the required intermediary. The investor acquires an equity interest in the QOF (a partnership interest or corporate stock).

Confirm the investment is in a Qualified Opportunity Fund. The QOF must be a partnership or corporation that has self-certified as a QOF (filed Form 8996) and meets the 90% asset test. The investor acquires an equity interest in the QOF. Direct investment in Opportunity Zone real estate or a business does not qualify; the QOF structure is required. Verify the QOF's certification and compliance with the 90% asset test. The QOF, in turn, must hold Qualified Opportunity Zone property (QOZ stock, QOZ partnership interests, or QOZ business property) to meet its requirements.

### Meet The QOF Substantial Improvement And Use Requirements

For the QOF to qualify, it must hold Qualified Opportunity Zone property and meet the substantial improvement and use requirements. For real estate, the QOF (or its subsidiary QOZ business) must substantially improve the property: the QOF must invest an amount equal to the purchase price of the property (excluding land) in improvements within 30 months of acquisition. The property must be used in the active conduct of a trade or business in the Opportunity Zone. Original use must begin with the QOF, or the QOF must substantially improve the property.

Understand the QOF's requirements for its underlying investments. For real estate, the substantial improvement test requires doubling the basis (excluding land) within 30 months. The property must be used in a trade or business in the QOZ. Vacant or non-business property does not qualify. For operating businesses, the business must generate at least 50% of its gross income in the QOZ and meet the tangible property and intangible property tests. The investor should understand the QOF's compliance with these requirements, as the QOF's failure to qualify can jeopardize the investor's benefits.

### Elect The Deferral On Form 8949

The Opportunity Zone deferral is elected on Form 8949 (Sales and Other Dispositions of Capital Assets) and reported on Form 8997 (Initial and Annual Statement of Qualified Opportunity Fund Holdings). The investor reports the eligible gain on Form 8949, elects to defer it by investing in a QOF, and reduces the recognized gain by the deferred amount. Form 8997 tracks the QOF holdings. The deferred gain is recognized on December 31, 2026 (or earlier if the QOF investment is sold).

Ensure the deferral is elected on Form 8949 and Form 8997 is filed. The election is made by reporting the gain and the QOF investment on the tax return for the year of the gain. The deferred gain is tracked and recognized on December 31, 2026 (the statutory inclusion date), or earlier if the QOF investment is sold. The basis step-up (for 5-year and 7-year holds) and the 10-year exclusion (for a 10-year hold) are also reported on these forms. Track the QOF holdings and the deferred gain so the investor understands the future recognition.

## Common Traps

### Investing Ordinary Income Instead Of Capital Gains

Only capital gains qualify for Opportunity Zone deferral. Ordinary income (wages, interest, ordinary dividends) does not qualify. Confirm the gain is a capital gain.

### Missing The 180-Day Investment Deadline

The 180-day period is strict. Missing it forfeits the deferral. Track the deadline from the gain realization date and invest in a QOF in time.

### Investing Directly In Opportunity Zone Property

The investment must be through a Qualified Opportunity Fund, not directly in Opportunity Zone property. The QOF is the required intermediary.

### Assuming The Basis Step-Up Is Still Available

The 5-year and 7-year basis step-ups had deadlines tied to the original program. For new investments, the primary benefits are deferral and the 10-year exclusion.

### Failing The Substantial Improvement Test

For real estate, the QOF must substantially improve the property (double the basis excluding land within 30 months). Failing this test jeopardizes the QOF's qualification.

### Overlooking The December 31, 2026 Inclusion Date

The deferred gain is recognized on December 31, 2026 (or earlier if the QOF investment is sold). The deferral is temporary, not permanent. Plan for the recognition.

### Forgetting The 10-Year Exclusion Requires A QOF Interest

The 10-year exclusion applies to the appreciation on a QOF partnership or corporation interest. Direct real estate ownership does not qualify for the exclusion.
