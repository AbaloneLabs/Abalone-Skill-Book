# FDII and Intangible Income

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 created a set of incentives designed to encourage U.S. corporations to hold intellectual property and other intangible-producing assets in the United States rather than offshore. The central provision for purely domestic companies is foreign-derived intangible income (FDII), a category of income from property held domestically that is sold or licensed for foreign use and that receives a reduced effective tax rate through a Section 250 deduction. FDII is the domestic counterpart to the global intangible low-taxed income (GILTI) regime, which taxes U.S. shareholders on certain income of controlled foreign corporations, and the two interact through the shared Section 250 deduction, which is allocated between them under a formula. The judgment problem is that the FDII benefit turns on the foreign-derived ratio, the deduction-eligible income computation, and the substantiation of foreign use, and an overstatement of the deduction through misallocated deductions or unqualified property will be disallowed with penalties. An agent who claims the FDII deduction without computing the foreign-derived ratio and substantiating foreign use will produce an overstated benefit and an exposed return.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, FDII applies only to domestic C corporations, and the income must be from property held by the corporation that exceeds a routine return on tangible assets, so the benefit is the excess return attributed to intangibles. Second, the foreign-derived determination requires that the property be sold or licensed to a foreign person or for foreign use, and the substantiation of foreign use (the destination, the foreign person, or the foreign use) is the most contested element, because domestic sales to a U.S. distributor that exports do not qualify unless the foreign use is documented. Third, the Section 250 deduction is a single deduction shared between FDII and GILTI, and the allocation formula means that a corporation with both types of income may receive a reduced FDII deduction, so the two must be modeled together. Fourth, the deduction-eligible income (DEI) computation requires the proper allocation of deductions, and an over-allocation of deductions to foreign income inflates DEI and the FDII benefit.

This skill covers FDII, the Section 250 deduction, and the interaction with GILTI under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The FDII Mechanism And The Deemed Intangible Return

FDII is a deemed income category that gives a domestic C corporation a reduced effective tax rate on the excess return it earns from property held domestically and sold or used abroad. The mechanism works by computing a deemed intangible income (DII), which is the corporation's deduction-eligible income (DEI) less a routine return of 10 percent on its qualified business asset investment (QBAI), the tax basis of tangible property used in the trade or business. The foreign-derived portion of that deemed intangible income, based on the foreign-derived ratio, is FDII, and a Section 250 deduction equal to a percentage of FDII (37.5 percent through 2025, reduced to 21.875 percent thereafter, before considering the GILTI interaction) reduces the tax, producing an effective rate well below the corporate rate.

Compute the FDII in the correct order. First, compute DEI as the corporation's gross income from the relevant trades or businesses less the directly and indirectly allocable deductions, excluding certain categories like foreign-source income subject to high tax and Subpart F income. Second, compute QBAI as the average adjusted tax basis of tangible property used in the trades or businesses. Third, compute DII as DEI less 10 percent of QBAI. Fourth, compute the foreign-derived ratio (foreign-derived deduction-eligible income divided by DEI) and apply it to DII to get FDII. Each step depends on the prior, and an error in DEI or QBAI propagates through the computation.

### Compute Deduction-Eligible Income And The Deduction Allocation

Deduction-eligible income (DEI) is the corporation's gross income from its trades or businesses, excluding certain categories (such as foreign-derived income already taxed at a high effective rate, Subpart F income, GILTI, and certain financial-statement income), less the deductions directly and indirectly allocable to that gross income. The allocation of deductions is governed by the Section 861 and Section 250 regulations, and the taxpayer must allocate and apportion deductions between domestic and foreign categories consistently. DEI is the denominator for the foreign-derived ratio and the base for DII, so an over-allocation of deductions to non-DEI categories inflates DEI and the FDII benefit.

Apply the deduction allocation conservatively and consistently. The regulations require a reasonable method for allocating indirect deductions (such as overhead, interest, and executive compensation) between DEI and non-DEI categories and between foreign-derived and domestic DEI. An aggressive allocation that minimizes deductions against DEI inflates the benefit and is disallowed on audit. Document the allocation method, use it consistently across years, and retain the workpapers. The deduction allocation is the most technically demanding part of the FDII computation and the most common source of error.

### Determine The Foreign-Derived Ratio And Substantiate Foreign Use

The foreign-derived ratio is the fraction of DEI attributable to foreign-derived deduction-eligible income (FDDEI), which is DEI from property sold or licensed to any foreign person for foreign use or from services provided to a foreign person or with respect to foreign use. The ratio is applied to DII to determine the portion that is FDII. The determination of foreign use is the most contested element, because the property or service must be for use, consumption, or disposition outside the United States, and the recipient must be a foreign person (for property) or the service must be for foreign use (for services).

Substantiate the foreign use for each category of income. For property sold to a foreign person, document the foreign recipient and the foreign destination (such as the bill-to and ship-to addresses, customs documents, and foreign resale). For property licensed to a foreign person, document the license and the foreign use. For services, document that the service was provided to a foreign person or with respect to property located outside the United States. A common error is treating a sale to a U.S. distributor that exports as foreign-derived; the foreign use must be documented at the taxpayer's level, and the IRS requires evidence that the property or service reached the foreign end use. Maintain the substantiation contemporaneously, because reconstructed evidence is weaker.

### Apply The Section 250 Deduction And The GILTI Interaction

Section 250 provides a single deduction that applies to both FDII and GILTI, and the deduction percentage and the allocation between the two are governed by the statute. For FDII, the deduction is 37.5 percent through 2025 (reducing the effective rate to about 13.125 percent), declining to 21.875 percent thereafter. For GILTI, the deduction is 50 percent through 2025 (reducing the effective rate to about 10.5 percent), declining to 37.5 percent thereafter. However, the Section 250 deduction is limited so that the total deduction cannot exceed the corporation's taxable income (with specific ordering), and the deduction is computed on a consolidated basis for affiliated groups.

Model the FDII and GILTI together when the corporation has both. A corporation with controlled foreign corporations generating GILTI and with domestic intangible income generating FDII must allocate the Section 250 deduction between them, and the interaction can reduce the effective FDII benefit. The GILTI computation (the aggregate tested income of the CFCs less 10 percent of QBAI) and the FDII computation both draw on the same Section 250 deduction pool, so a large GILTI inclusion can crowd out the FDII deduction. Also confirm that the high-tax exception or election (if applicable) excludes certain foreign income from both GILTI and the FDII computation, because income subject to a high foreign effective tax rate is generally excluded.

### Confirm Eligible Property And Exclude Disqualified Categories

Not all income qualifies for the FDII benefit. The FDII computation excludes certain categories of income, including foreign-source income subject to a high effective foreign tax rate (generally exceeding 18.9 percent through 2025), Subpart F income, GILTI, and certain financial-services income. The property must be used in a trade or business of the corporation, and the QBAI must reflect tangible property used in those trades or businesses, excluding property that produces excluded income. An agent who includes excluded income in DEI or excluded property in QBAI overstates the benefit.

Identify and exclude the disqualified categories before computing DEI and QBAI. Foreign income subject to high tax is excluded to prevent double-benefit (the high foreign tax credit and the FDII deduction). Subpart F and GILTI are excluded because they have their own regimes. Confirm that the tangible property in QBAI is used in the trades or businesses that generate DEI, and exclude property used to produce excluded income. The exclusions are detailed and require a careful categorization of the corporation's income and assets.

### Document The FDII Computation For Each Tax Year

The FDII computation is complex and fact-intensive, and it must be documented for each tax year on Form 8993 (for FDII) and the related forms. The documentation includes the DEI computation with the deduction allocation workpapers, the QBAI computation with the asset records, the foreign-derived ratio with the foreign-use substantiation, and the Section 250 deduction with the GILTI interaction. The IRS audits FDII claims by examining the deduction allocation and the foreign-use substantiation, so the documentation must be contemporaneous and complete.

Maintain the computation workpapers and the substantiation for each year. The foreign-use documentation (the bill-to and ship-to records, the customs documents, the license agreements, and the service-location records) must support the foreign-derived ratio, and the deduction allocation must be reasonable and consistent. A corporation that claims the FDII deduction without the supporting workpapers is exposed to disallowance and penalties. The FDII benefit is valuable, but it is earned through rigorous computation and documentation, not merely by claiming the deduction.

## Common Traps

### Claiming FDII Without Substantiating Foreign Use

The symptom is a corporation that claims FDII on sales to a U.S. distributor that exports. The trap is that the foreign use must be documented at the taxpayer's level, and a sale to a U.S. party without evidence of the foreign end use does not qualify, so the FDII is disallowed. The direction is to substantiate the foreign recipient and destination for each category of income.

### Inflating DEI Through An Aggressive Deduction Allocation

The symptom is a large FDII benefit produced by allocating few deductions to DEI. The trap is that the Section 861 and Section 250 allocation regulations require a reasonable method, and an aggressive allocation inflates DEI and is disallowed. The direction is to document a reasonable, consistent allocation method and to retain the workpapers.

### Including Excluded Income In DEI Or Excluded Property In QBAI

The symptom is a DEI or QBAI that includes high-taxed foreign income, Subpart F, or GILTI. The trap is that these categories are excluded from the FDII computation, so including them overstates the benefit. The direction is to identify and exclude the disqualified categories before computing DEI and QBAI.

### Ignoring The GILTI Interaction With The Section 250 Deduction

The symptom is a corporation that claims the full FDII deduction while also having GILTI. The trap is that the Section 250 deduction is shared between FDII and GILTI, and a large GILTI inclusion can crowd out the FDII deduction. The direction is to model the FDII and GILTI together and to allocate the deduction correctly.

### Treating The FDII Deduction As Automatic

The symptom is a corporation that claims the FDII deduction without computing DEI, QBAI, and the foreign-derived ratio. The trap is that the FDII deduction requires a specific computation and substantiation, and claiming it without the computation is unsupported and disallowed. The direction is to compute the FDII on Form 8993 with documented workpapers.

### Forgetting The Reduced Deduction Percentage After 2025

The symptom is a corporation that projects the FDII benefit at the 37.5 percent deduction indefinitely. The trap is that the deduction declines to 21.875 percent after 2025, so the projected benefit overstates the long-term value. The direction is to model the benefit using the correct deduction percentage for each year.

- [ ] Has the deduction-eligible income been computed with a reasonable, consistent allocation of deductions under the Section 861 and Section 250 regulations, with workpapers retained?
