# Foreign-Derived Revenue Sourcing

The source of income, U.S. or foreign, is the foundation for nearly every international tax computation, and getting it wrong propagates errors through the foreign tax credit, the FDII deduction, the GILTI computation, and the Subpart F inclusion. Sourcing rules determine where income is taxed, and the rules differ by income type: sales of inventory depend on the passage of title and the production-location rules, services depend on where the service is performed, and royalties and licenses depend on the place of use. Layered on top of the sourcing is the allocation and apportionment of deductions under the Section 861 regulations, which determines the taxable income in each sourcing category and thus the foreign tax credit limitation, the FDII deduction-eligible income, and the tested income for GILTI. The judgment problem is that sourcing and allocation are fact-intensive, the rules have multiple elective methods, and an error in one category distorts the entire international computation. An agent who sources revenue by the ship-to address or allocates deductions by a simplistic formula, without applying the specific rules, will misstate the foreign tax credit and the FDII benefit.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, inventory sales are sourced under either the destination rule (title passes abroad) or the production-activity rule (50-50 or 863(b) methods for property produced in the U.S. and sold abroad), and the choice and the title-passage documentation determine the source, so a sale shipped from the U.S. may be foreign-source or U.S.-source depending on title. Second, services are sourced to where performed, not where the customer is, and mixed services (with elements performed in and outside the U.S.) require an allocation under the Section 861 regulations or an election to split based on time. Third, the deduction allocation under Section 861 requires a specific method for each category of deduction (interest, research, stewardship, and general), and the method affects the taxable income in each basket, which in turn affects the foreign tax credit limitation and the FDII computation. Fourth, the substantiation of foreign-derived revenue for FDII and the substantiation of foreign-source income for the foreign tax credit are related but distinct, and both require documentation of the foreign person, the foreign destination, or the foreign use.

This skill covers foreign-derived revenue sourcing and deduction allocation under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Source Inventory Sales By Title Passage Or Production Activity

The source of income from the sale of inventory depends on whether the inventory was purchased or produced by the seller and where title passes. For purchased inventory resold, the source is determined by where title passes: if title passes in the United States, the income is U.S.-source; if title passes abroad, the income is foreign-source. For inventory produced by the seller, the income is split between a production return (sourced to where production occurs) and a sales return (sourced by title passage), and the taxpayer may use the 50-50 method (half to production, half to sales) or the Section 863(b) method, which allocates based on the ratio of production assets to total assets for the production portion and title passage for the sales portion.

Document the title passage and choose the production method deliberately. The title passage is established by the contract terms (the delivery and risk-of-loss provisions), the shipping terms (FOB, CIF), and the customs documents, and the documentation must support the claimed source. For produced inventory, choose the method (50-50 or 863(b)) that is appropriate and apply it consistently, because switching methods requires IRS consent. The source of inventory income affects the foreign tax credit (foreign-source income can absorb foreign taxes) and the FDII computation (foreign-derived income is the base for the benefit), so the sourcing must be correct.

### Source Services By Where Performed, Not Where The Customer Is

Income from services is sourced to the place where the services are performed, regardless of where the customer is located or where the benefit is received. A service performed entirely in the United States is U.S.-source income, even if the customer is foreign; a service performed entirely abroad is foreign-source income, even if the customer is U.S. Mixed services, with elements performed both in and outside the United States, must be allocated, generally based on the time spent or the cost incurred in each location, unless a specific rule or election applies. Certain services (such as engineering and architectural services) have special sourcing rules, and the source rules for services have been updated in recent regulations.

Determine where each service is performed and allocate mixed services. Maintain time and cost records by location for employees and contractors performing the services, because the allocation depends on where the work occurred. A common error is sourcing a service to the customer's location, which is incorrect; the source is the performance location. For services that support the sale of inventory or intangibles, confirm whether the service is bundled (and sourced with the underlying transaction) or separate, because the treatment differs. The service sourcing affects the foreign tax credit and the FDII computation for service income.

### Source Royalties And Licenses By Place Of Use

Income from royalties and licenses is sourced to the place where the intangible is used. A royalty for the use of a patent abroad is foreign-source income; a royalty for use in the United States is U.S.-source income. The place of use is determined by where the licensee exploits the intangible, not by where the licensee is located, so a license to a U.S. company for use in its foreign operations is foreign-source to the extent of the foreign use. Rents from tangible property are sourced to where the property is located.

Determine the place of use for each royalty and license. The license agreement should specify the territory of use, and the royalty income is sourced accordingly. A common error is sourcing a royalty to the licensee's location, which is incorrect if the use is elsewhere. For bundled arrangements (such as a license with related services or goods), allocate the income among the components and source each separately. The royalty sourcing affects the foreign tax credit (foreign-source royalties can absorb foreign withholding taxes) and the FDII computation (foreign-use royalties are foreign-derived).

### Apply The Section 861 Deduction Allocation Methodically

The allocation and apportionment of deductions under the Section 861 regulations determines the taxable income in each sourcing category (U.S.-source and foreign-source, and within foreign-source, by separate baskets), which in turn determines the foreign tax credit limitation, the FDII deduction-eligible income, and the GILTI tested income. The regulations require specific methods for each deduction category. Interest is allocated based on the corporation's assets (the modified gross income method or the fair-market-value method). Research and experimental expenditures are allocated based on where the research is performed and the geographic source of income, with a special rule for research that benefits all product categories. Stewardship expenses (costs of managing investments) are allocated to the income they produce. General and administrative expenses are allocated based on gross income or a reasonable alternative.

Apply the Section 861 allocation methodically and consistently. Each deduction category has its own allocation method, and the methods interact, so the allocation must be computed in the correct order. Document the allocation method for each category, use it consistently across years, and retain the workpapers. The allocation is the most technically demanding part of international compliance, and an error distorts the foreign tax credit limitation, the FDII computation, and the GILTI tested income. For affiliated groups filing consolidated returns, the allocation is computed at the group level, which adds complexity.

### Distinguish Foreign-Derived Revenue (FDII) From Foreign-Source Income (FTC)

Foreign-derived revenue for FDII and foreign-source income for the foreign tax credit are related but distinct concepts, and conflating them produces errors. Foreign-derived revenue is income from property sold or licensed to a foreign person for foreign use, or services provided to a foreign person or for foreign use, regardless of the source of the income under the sourcing rules. Foreign-source income is income sourced to a foreign country under the sourcing rules, regardless of who the customer is. A sale of U.S.-produced inventory to a foreign person for foreign use may be both foreign-derived (for FDII) and partly foreign-source (for the FTC, under the production-activity rules), but the two determinations use different tests and produce different results.

Compute foreign-derived revenue and foreign-source income separately. For FDII, determine the foreign person and the foreign use, and the income is foreign derived regardless of its source. For the FTC, determine the source under the sourcing rules (title passage or production activity), and the foreign-source portion absorbs foreign taxes. Do not assume that foreign-derived revenue is automatically foreign-source, because a sale of U.S.-produced inventory to a foreign person may be largely U.S.-source under the production-activity rule (the production return is U.S.-source). The two computations serve different purposes and must be done independently.

### Substantiate The Foreign Person, Destination, And Use

Both the FDII foreign-derived determination and the FTC foreign-source determination require substantiation, and the substantiation differs. For FDII, the foreign-derived revenue must be supported by evidence of the foreign person (the customer or licensee) and the foreign use (the destination or place of use). For the FTC, the foreign-source income must be supported by evidence of the source (title passage for inventory, performance location for services, place of use for royalties). The substantiation includes contracts, invoices, shipping records, customs documents, and license agreements.

Maintain the substantiation contemporaneously for both computations. The FDII substantiation (foreign person and foreign use) and the FTC substantiation (source of income) may overlap but are not identical, so the records must support both. The IRS audits both the FDII foreign-derived determination and the FTC sourcing, and reconstructed documentation is weaker. Confirm each year that the foreign-derived revenue and the foreign-source income are supported by the transaction records, and reconcile the two computations so that they are internally consistent.

## Common Traps

### Sourcing Inventory By The Ship-To Address Instead Of Title Passage

The symptom is a company that sources inventory sales by where the goods are shipped. The trap is that the source depends on title passage (for purchased inventory) or the production-activity method (for produced inventory), so the ship-to address is not determinative and the source is misstated. The direction is to document title passage and apply the correct production method.

### Sourcing Services To The Customer's Location

The symptom is a company that sources services to where the customer is located. The trap is that services are sourced to where performed, so a service performed in the U.S. for a foreign customer is U.S.-source and the FTC and FDII are misstated. The direction is to source services by performance location and to allocate mixed services.

### Sourcing Royalties To The Licensee's Location

The symptom is a company that sources royalties by where the licensee is located. The trap is that royalties are sourced to the place of use, so a license to a U.S. company for foreign use is foreign-source to the extent of the foreign use. The direction is to determine the place of use from the license territory.

### Allocating Deductions By A Simplistic Formula

The symptom is a company that allocates all deductions by a single ratio (such as total revenue) across sourcing categories. The trap is that the Section 861 regulations require specific methods for each deduction category, and a simplistic formula misallocates and distorts the FTC limitation and the FDII computation. The direction is to apply the regulatory methods for interest, research, stewardship, and general expenses.

### Conflating Foreign-Derived Revenue With Foreign-Source Income

The symptom is a company that treats foreign-derived revenue as automatically foreign-source. The trap is that the two concepts use different tests, so foreign-derived revenue may be largely U.S.-source under the production-activity rule, and conflating them overstates the FTC and misstates the FDII. The direction is to compute foreign-derived revenue and foreign-source income separately.

### Claiming FDII Or FTC Without Substantiation

The symptom is a company that claims the FDII deduction or foreign tax credit without supporting documentation. The trap is that both require substantiation (foreign person and use for FDII, source for FTC), and claiming either without records is disallowed. The direction is to maintain the contracts, invoices, shipping records, and license agreements contemporaneously.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has inventory income been sourced by title passage (for purchased inventory) or the production-activity method (for produced inventory), with the method documented and applied consistently?
- [ ] Has service income been sourced to where performed, with mixed services allocated based on time or cost by location?
- [ ] Has royalty and license income been sourced to the place of use, with the license territory documented?
- [ ] Has the Section 861 deduction allocation been computed under the regulatory methods for interest, research, stewardship, and general expenses, with workpapers retained?
- [ ] Have foreign-derived revenue (for FDII) and foreign-source income (for FTC) been computed separately, recognizing that the two use different tests?
- [ ] Has the foreign person, foreign destination, and foreign use been substantiated for FDII, and has the source of income been substantiated for the FTC?
- [ ] Has the sourcing and allocation been reconciled across the foreign tax credit, FDII, and GILTI computations so the return is internally consistent?
- [ ] Has the allocation method been applied consistently across years, with any change supported and disclosed?
