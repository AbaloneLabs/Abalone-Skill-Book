# IC-DISC and Export Income

The Interest Charge Domestic International Sales Corporation (IC-DISC) is one of the last remaining export tax incentives for U.S. companies, providing a way to convert export income taxed at ordinary corporate or individual rates into dividend income taxed at qualified dividend rates for the shareholders. The structure works by allowing a separately incorporated IC-DISC to earn a commission on export sales handled by a related operating company, where the commission is deductible by the operating company and distributed as a dividend by the IC-DISC, which itself pays no tax as long as it meets the qualification tests. The judgment problem is that the IC-DISC must satisfy strict organizational, gross-receipts, and foreign-content tests, the commission must be computed under one of two regulatory methods and substantiated, and the IRS actively audits IC-DISC commissions for reasonableness, so an inflated or undocumented commission will be disallowed. An agent who treats the IC-DISC as a paperwork entity that earns whatever commission the company wants, without applying the qualification tests and the commission methodology, will produce a structure that fails on examination.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the IC-DISC must be a validly formed corporation with its own stock, bank account, and minimum capital (generally USD 2,500), and it must maintain its separate existence, because a shell with no substance will be disregarded. Second, the qualified export property and the 50-percent gross-receipts test (at least 50 percent of the DISC's gross receipts from qualified export receipts) and the 95-percent foreign-content test (at least 95 percent of the property's fair market value attributable to U.S. content and direct costs) are the gating conditions, and property with significant foreign content fails. Third, the commission is computed under either the four-percent-of-gross-receipts method or the 50-percent-of-combined-taxable-income (CTI) method, and the CTI method requires a proper allocation of expenses under Section 861 regulations that is frequently misapplied. Fourth, the commission must be paid within the tax year or treated as paid through a note that carries the Section 992 interest charge, and the timing and the interest-charge mechanics are easy to get wrong.

This skill covers IC-DISC qualification, commission computation, and export income treatment under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Establish And Maintain The IC-DISC As A Substantive Entity

An IC-DISC must be a domestic corporation that has elected IC-DISC status, has filed Form 4876-A, and satisfies the organizational and operational requirements throughout the year. The corporation must have a class of stock with a par or stated value and paid-in capital of at least USD 2,500, must maintain its own bank account and books, and must hold itself out as a corporation. The IC-DISC does not need employees or an office, but it must have some substance and must actually perform or contract for the export functions (such as billing, shipping, or customer support) that justify the commission, and it must be the party of record in the export transactions to the extent required.

Confirm the IC-DISC's substantive existence each year. A shell with no bank account, no records, and no role in the transactions is vulnerable to being disregarded. The IC-DISC should have its own board (which may overlap with the operating company), should hold occasional meetings, and should be identified as the export intermediary in the transaction documents. The commission must be documented by a written commission agreement with the operating company, computed under a specified method, and actually paid or evidenced by a note. The substance requirements are not onerous, but they must be satisfied, because a disregarded IC-DISC means the commission is disallowed and the structure produces no benefit.

### Satisfy The Gross-Receipts And Foreign-Content Tests

To qualify as an IC-DDISC, the corporation must meet the 50-percent gross-receipts test (at least 50 percent of its gross receipts must be qualified export receipts) and the 95-percent foreign-content test. Qualified export receipts generally include receipts from the sale or lease of export property for use outside the United States, and the property must be held for sale or lease by the DISC or the related supplier. Export property is property manufactured, produced, grown, or extracted in the United States, with not more than 50 percent of its fair market value attributable to articles imported into the United States, and held for use, consumption, or disposition outside the United States.

Apply the foreign-content test carefully. The 50-percent imported-content limit (and the related 95-percent U.S.-content requirement for the direct-cost portion) means that property with significant foreign components may fail. Compute the foreign content by reference to the fair market value of imported articles and the direct costs of production, and document the computation. Property produced entirely in the United States clearly qualifies; property assembled in the United States from significant foreign components requires a content analysis. Confirm that the ultimate destination is outside the United States, because property sold to a U.S. purchaser or destined for U.S. use is not export property. The qualification tests are annual and must be met each year the IC-DISC claims the benefit.

### Compute The Commission Under The Four-Percent Or CTI Method

The commission an IC-DISC can earn is computed under one of two methods, and the taxpayer can choose the method that produces the larger commission. The four-percent method computes the commission as four percent of the qualified export gross receipts (or 10 percent of export income, capped at four percent of gross receipts, for certain transactions). The 50-percent-of-combined-taxable-income (CTI) method computes the commission as 50 percent of the combined taxable income of the DISC and the related supplier from the export transactions, or four percent of export gross receipts, whichever is greater, with the CTI method generally producing a larger commission when export margins are high.

Compute both methods and claim the larger, but apply the CTI method correctly. The CTI computation requires an allocation of the operating company's expenses to the export transactions under the Section 861 regulations, which allocate deductions between domestic and foreign-source income. A common error is to under-allocate expenses to the export transactions, inflating the CTI and the commission; the IRS scrutinizes these allocations, and an inflated CTI is disallowed. Document the Section 861 allocation method, use a reasonable and consistent approach, and retain the workpapers. The four-percent method is simpler and safer but may produce a smaller commission, so the choice depends on the export margins and the reliability of the allocation.

### Apply The Section 861 Allocation Conservatively For CTI

The CTI method depends on the allocation of expenses to export income under the Section 861 regulations, and this is the most contested area of IC-DISC compliance. The combined taxable income is the gross income from the export transactions less the directly and indirectly allocable deductions, and the allocation of indirect expenses (such as overhead, executive compensation, and interest) determines the CTI. The regulations provide methods for allocating and apportioning deductions, and the taxpayer must apply them consistently. An allocation that assigns too few expenses to export income inflates the CTI and the commission, and the IRS routinely challenges aggressive allocations.

Document the Section 861 allocation method and apply it consistently across years. Use a reasonable basis for allocating indirect expenses, such as gross income, sales, or units, and retain the workpapers showing the allocation. Be conservative, because an inflated CTI produces a commission that is disallowed on audit, with interest and penalties. If the allocation is uncertain, the four-percent method may be safer. The CTI method is most valuable for exporters with high margins and clean, well-documented expense allocations, and it is riskiest for exporters with complex operations and aggressive allocations.

### Handle The Dividend Distribution And Shareholder Treatment

The IC-DISC pays no federal income tax on its commission income (it is a tax-exempt entity), and the commission is deductible by the operating company, reducing the operating company's taxable income. The IC-DISC must distribute 95 percent of its income (or an equivalent amount) to its shareholders by the end of the year or treat the undistributed amount as a note to the operating company that carries the Section 992 interest charge. The distribution to the shareholders is taxed as a qualified dividend, generally at the long-term capital gains rates (subject to the conditions for qualified dividend status), which is the core benefit of the IC-DISC.

Confirm the distribution and the qualified-dividend treatment. The distribution must be made to the IC-DISC's shareholders, who are often the same individuals or entities that own the operating company, and the structure must be coordinated so that the ownership produces the intended dividend recipient. The dividend is qualified only if the IC-DISC is a qualified U.S. corporation and the holding-period requirements are met, which is generally satisfied for IC-DISC stock held by U.S. individuals. For C-corporation shareholders, the IC-DISC benefit is different (the dividends-received deduction may apply), and the structure is less common. For pass-through entity shareholders, coordinate the IC-DISC ownership so the dividend reaches the intended individual. The undistributed portion subject to the interest charge must be computed and the interest paid, because the interest charge is the quid pro quo for the deferral.

### Document The Commission Agreement And Substantiate Each Year

The commission must be paid pursuant to a written commission agreement between the IC-DISC and the operating company, and the agreement must specify the method (four-percent or CTI), the transactions covered, and the payment terms. The commission must be computed for each year, documented in workpapers, and actually paid or evidenced by a note within the required timeframe. The IC-DISC must file Form 1120-IC-DISC annually, and the operating company must reflect the commission deduction on its return.

Maintain the documentation contemporaneously. The commission agreement, the Section 861 allocation workpapers (for the CTI method), the content analysis, and the distribution records must all be in place for each year. The IRS audits IC-DISCs by examining the commission methodology and the allocation, and reconstructed documentation is far less persuasive. Confirm each year that the qualification tests are met, that the commission is computed under the chosen method, and that the distribution or interest charge is handled. The IC-DISC is a multi-year structure, so the consistency and the documentation across years are essential.

## Common Traps

### Operating The IC-DISC As A Shell With No Substance

The symptom is an IC-DISC with no bank account, no records, and no role in the transactions. The trap is that a shell with no substance may be disregarded, disallowing the commission and eliminating the benefit. The direction is to maintain the IC-DISC as a substantive entity with its own bank account, books, board, and a documented role in the export transactions.

### Failing The Foreign-Content Test For Assembled Products

The symptom is an exporter of products assembled from significant foreign components. The trap is that the 50-percent imported-content limit (and the 95-percent U.S.-content requirement) may fail, disqualifying the property and the commission. The direction is to compute the foreign content by reference to imported articles and direct costs and to confirm the property qualifies before claiming the benefit.

### Inflating The CTI Through An Aggressive Section 861 Allocation

The symptom is a CTI commission that is large because few expenses are allocated to export income. The trap is that the IRS scrutinizes the Section 861 allocation, and an aggressive allocation inflates the CTI and produces a disallowed commission with penalties. The direction is to document a reasonable, consistent allocation method and to be conservative.

### Choosing The CTI Method Without The Allocation Workpapers

The symptom is a taxpayer who claims the CTI method commission without documented expense allocations. The trap is that the CTI method requires a Section 861 allocation, and without workpapers the commission is unsupported and disallowed. The direction is to prepare and retain the allocation workpapers or to use the four-percent method.

### Missing The 95-Percent Distribution Or The Interest Charge

The symptom is an IC-DISC that retains income beyond the 95-percent distribution requirement without paying the interest charge. The trap is that undistributed income must carry the Section 992 interest charge, and failing to compute and pay it triggers the charge plus penalties. The direction is to distribute 95 percent or to compute and pay the interest charge on the undistributed amount.

### Treating The IC-DISC Dividend As Ordinary Income

The symptom is a shareholder who reports the IC-DISC dividend as ordinary dividend income. The trap is that the IC-DISC dividend is generally a qualified dividend taxed at capital gains rates, so reporting it as ordinary overstates the tax. The direction is to confirm the qualified-dividend treatment and the holding period and to report the dividend at the qualified rate.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the IC-DISC been established and maintained as a substantive entity with its own stock, bank account, books, board, and a documented role in the export transactions?
- [ ] Has the qualified export property been confirmed to meet the 50-percent gross-receipts test and the 95-percent foreign-content (50-percent imported-content) test, with the content analysis documented?
- [ ] Has the commission been computed under both the four-percent and CTI methods, with the larger claimed?
- [ ] Has the Section 861 expense allocation for the CTI method been documented with a reasonable, consistent method and retained in workpapers?
- [ ] Has the commission agreement been executed in writing, specifying the method and the transactions covered, and has the commission been paid or evidenced by a note within the required timeframe?
- [ ] Has the 95-percent distribution requirement been met, or has the Section 992 interest charge been computed and paid on the undistributed amount?
- [ ] Has the IC-DISC dividend been confirmed as a qualified dividend for the shareholders, with the holding-period and qualified-corporation conditions met?
- [ ] Has Form 1120-IC-DISC been filed annually, with the operating company's commission deduction reflected on its return?
