# Stock Options And 83(b)

Stock options and Section 83(b) elections involve some of the most timing-sensitive and error-prone decisions in equity compensation. The difference between an incentive stock option (ISO) and a nonqualified stock option (NQSO), and the decision to file an 83(b) election on restricted stock within 30 days, can change the tax character of hundreds of thousands of dollars from ordinary income to capital gain, or trigger alternative minimum tax (AMT). The agent should not treat these as routine transactions; the deadlines are absolute and the elections are irrevocable.

Agents tend to miss that the 83(b) election deadline is 30 days from the grant or transfer date with no extensions, that ISO exercises can create a large AMT preference even when no shares are sold, that disqualifying an ISO sale collapses the favorable treatment back to ordinary income, and that the spread on an NQSO exercise is ordinary compensation at exercise regardless of a later sale. The harm includes missed elections that lock in ordinary income on later appreciation, unexpected AMT bills of tens of thousands of dollars, and misreported basis that double-taxes the bargain element.

This skill covers U.S. federal income tax treatment of ISOs, NQSOs, restricted stock, and Section 83(b) elections. AMT exemptions, exemption phaseouts, capital gains rates, and holding-period rules are amended periodically and must be verified for the tax year. This is not tax advice. A qualified tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney must be consulted before any exercise, sale, or election.

## Core Rules

### Separate ISOs From NQSOs Before Any Analysis

The tax treatment of a stock option depends entirely on whether it is an incentive stock option (ISO) under Section 422 or a nonqualified stock option (NQSO). An ISO, when exercised and held for the qualifying holding periods, can produce long-term capital gain on the entire spread. An NQSO always produces ordinary compensation income on the bargain element (spread between exercise price and FMV) at exercise, reported on the W-2.

Confirm the option type from the grant agreement and the employer's equity plan before advising. ISOs require a board-approved plan, a $100,000-per-year exercisable limit on the grant-date FMV, a $100,000 ISO grant limit per calendar year, a 10-year maximum term, and grants only to employees. NQSOs can be granted to contractors and directors and have no statutory limits. Misclassifying an NQSO as an ISO produces wrong income, wrong forms, and wrong planning.

### File The 83(b) Election Within 30 Days For Restricted Stock

A Section 83(b) election applies to property subject to a substantial risk of forfeiture, such as restricted stock or founder shares that vest over time. Filing the election within 30 days of the grant (the date of transfer) causes the taxpayer to recognize ordinary income equal to the FMV on the grant date—often near zero for an early-stage startup—rather than waiting and recognizing income at each vesting date when the value is higher. The election is irrevocable unless a mistake of fact exists.

The 30-day deadline is calendar days and includes weekends and holidays; there are no extensions. The election is mailed to the IRS service center where the taxpayer files, with a copy attached to the tax return and a copy provided to the employer. Missing the deadline means income is recognized at vesting on the then-current (often much higher) FMV, which can create a large tax bill on illiquid shares. For early-stage founders, the 83(b) is almost always the right move; for later-stage grants with substantial FMV, the analysis is different and the tax cost of electing must be weighed.

### Understand ISO Exercise Creates AMT Preference, Not Immediate Tax

Exercising an ISO does not create regular tax income if the shares are held, but the bargain element (FMV at exercise minus exercise price) is an AMT preference item on Form 6251. A large ISO exercise can push a taxpayer into AMT, creating a tax liability due in April even though no shares were sold. The AMT paid generates a minimum tax credit (MTC) usable in future years when regular tax exceeds AMT.

The AMT risk is invisible to employees who expect "no tax until sale." Model the AMT before exercising, especially in years with high income or large spreads. Strategies include exercising early in the calendar year to allow a same-year sale to wash out the preference, spreading exercises across years, or exercising when the spread is small. The $100,000 ISO exercisable limit per calendar year (based on grant-date FMV) also caps how much can be exercised with ISO treatment annually.

### Apply The ISO Qualifying Disposition Holding Periods

To get long-term capital gain treatment on the full ISO spread, the taxpayer must hold the shares for at least two years from the grant date AND one year from the exercise date before selling (a qualifying disposition). If either holding period is missed, the sale is a disqualifying disposition, and the bargain element (up to the sale proceeds) is ordinary compensation income at sale; only appreciation above the exercise-date FMV is capital gain.

Disqualifying dispositions can occur accidentally through a same-day sale at exercise or an early sale. The ordinary income portion is reported as wages (if the employer is notified) or as additional compensation on Schedule 1. Track the grant date, exercise date, and sale date for each ISO lot precisely, because a few days' difference can convert capital gain into ordinary income. Note the special rule that a disqualifying sale to the employer is treated as occurring at FMV.

### Recognize NQSO Spread As Ordinary Income At Exercise

For an NQSO, the bargain element at exercise (FMV minus exercise price) is ordinary compensation income on the exercise date, reported on the W-2 and subject to income tax, Social Security, and Medicare withholding. The FMV at exercise becomes the cost basis for any later sale, and post-exercise appreciation or decline is capital gain or loss.

Employers typically withhold on NQSO exercises using the flat 22% supplemental rate, which may be below the employee's marginal rate and create a shortfall. The 1099-B from the broker may report a basis that excludes or includes the compensation differently, so verify the basis equals the exercise-date FMV. Unlike ISOs, NQSO exercises do not create an AMT preference, simplifying the analysis but keeping the ordinary-income and withholding issues.

### Coordinate Timing Across Exercises, Sales, And Life Events

Equity decisions interact with income level, AMT exposure, residency, and life events. Exercising in a low-income year reduces both ordinary tax (for NQSOs) and AMT exposure (for ISOs). Moving states around an exercise can trigger sourcing rules that allocate the spread to the state where services were performed, not where the employee lives at exercise.

Death, disability, and separation from service can accelerate vesting or change holding periods, and some plans allow extended exercise windows while others forfeit unexercised options on termination. Coordinate any exercise with a review of the plan's termination provisions, the employee's liquidity, and the concentration risk of holding employer stock. Avoid exercising options the employee cannot afford to hold or sell, because the tax is due regardless of share liquidity.

### Document Elections, Dates, And Basis For Each Lot

Retain the 83(b) election with proof of mailing (certified mail return receipt), grant agreements, exercise confirmations, FMV determinations, and trade confirmations. For ISOs, track grant date, exercise date, and sale date for each lot to substantiate qualifying versus disqualifying treatment. For NQSOs, track exercise date and FMV to establish basis.

Because AMT credit carryforwards and capital loss carryforwards persist across years, retain records for the full period until carryforwards are exhausted plus the limitations period. Reconcile the W-2, 1099-B, and exercise statements annually to ensure compensation is not double-counted and basis is correctly reported on Form 8949.

## Common Traps

### Missing The 30-Day 83(b) Deadline

The 83(b) election must be postmarked within 30 calendar days of the grant date with no extensions. A late or missing election forces income recognition at each vesting date at then-current FMV, often a much larger and illiquid tax bill.

### Assuming ISO Exercise Is Tax-Free

An ISO exercise creates no regular tax but does create an AMT preference equal to the bargain element. Large exercises can produce AMT liabilities due in April even when no shares are sold.

### Triggering A Disqualifying Disposition By Accident

A same-day sale or a sale within one year of exercise or two years of grant disqualifies the ISO, converting the bargain element to ordinary income. Track holding periods precisely to avoid unintended disqualification.

### Treating NQSO Spread As Capital Gain

The NQSO bargain element at exercise is ordinary compensation, subject to payroll taxes, not capital gain. Mischaracterizing it understates income and payroll tax.

### Overlooking The $100,000 ISO Annual Limit

Only $100,000 of ISOs (based on grant-date FMV) can first become exercisable with ISO treatment in a calendar year; the excess is treated as NQSOs. Exercising more than the limit without analysis can misstate the tax character.

### Ignoring State Sourcing On Relocation

The spread on an option exercise is generally sourced to the states where the services were performed, not the state of residence at exercise. Moving before exercise does not always avoid the prior state's tax.

### Failing To Track AMT Credit Carryforwards

AMT paid on ISO exercises generates a minimum tax credit usable in later years. Forgetting the credit forfeits a real benefit and overstates lifetime tax.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the option type (ISO versus NQSO) been confirmed from the grant agreement and equity plan before any tax analysis?
- [ ] For restricted stock or founder shares, has the Section 83(b) election been filed within 30 calendar days of the grant date, with proof of mailing and copies to the IRS, the return, and the employer?
- [ ] For ISO exercises, has the AMT preference (bargain element) been modeled on Form 6251, and has the minimum tax credit carryforward been tracked?
- [ ] Are ISO holding periods (2 years from grant, 1 year from exercise) tracked for each lot to determine qualifying versus disqualifying disposition?
- [ ] For NQSO exercises, is the bargain element recognized as ordinary compensation at exercise with correct payroll tax withholding and basis set to the exercise-date FMV?
- [ ] Has the $100,000 ISO annual exercisable limit (based on grant-date FMV) been checked, with excess treated as NQSOs?
- [ ] Have exercises been coordinated with income level, AMT exposure, state sourcing, liquidity, and plan termination provisions?
- [ ] Are grant agreements, exercise confirmations, FMV determinations, 83(b) filings, and trade confirmations retained, with AMT and capital loss carryforwards tracked until exhausted?
- [ ] Are the W-2, 1099-B, and exercise statements reconciled annually so compensation is not double-counted and basis is correctly reported on Form 8949?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer been advised that this is general guidance and not tax advice, and that a qualified tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney must be consulted before any exercise, sale, or election?
