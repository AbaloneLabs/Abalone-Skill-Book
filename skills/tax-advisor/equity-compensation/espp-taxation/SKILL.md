# ESPP Taxation

Employee Stock Purchase Plans (ESPPs) under Section 423 offer employees the ability to buy employer stock at a discount, often with a lookback feature that can produce significant gains. The tax treatment is deceptively complex because the same sale can be partly ordinary income and partly capital gain, and the ordinary portion depends on whether the disposition is qualifying or disqualifying. Agents who apply a single rule, or who rely on the broker's reported basis, routinely misstate income and overpay or underpay tax.

Agents tend to miss that the ESPP discount is ordinary compensation income only at sale (not at purchase), that the qualifying-disposition rules require both a two-year-from-grant and one-year-from-purchase holding period, that the ordinary income portion is capped at the purchase-date discount for qualifying sales but equals the full bargain element for disqualifying sales, and that brokers frequently report the basis incorrectly because the compensation element is not reflected. The harm includes double-taxation when basis is understated, missed favorable treatment when holding periods are miscalculated, and W-2 reconciliation failures.

This skill covers U.S. federal income tax treatment of qualified Section 423 ESPPs. Discount caps, holding-period rules, and reporting requirements are amended periodically and must be verified for the tax year. Non-qualified ESPPs (non-Section 423) follow different rules and are outside the core scope. This is not tax advice. A qualified tax professional, CPA, or equity compensation specialist must be consulted before any sale or reporting decision.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Plan Is A Qualified Section 423 ESPP

Only qualified Section 423 ESPPs receive the favorable tax treatment described here. Confirm from the plan document that the plan is IRS-approved under Section 423, which requires shareholder approval, a $25,000 annual purchase limit (based on FMV at the start of each offering period, not the purchase date), no more than a 15% discount, and eligibility limited to employees. Non-qualified ESPPs are taxed like NQSOs at purchase, with the discount as ordinary income immediately.

The $25,000 limit is computed on the stock's FMV at the start of the offering period, accumulated across all overlapping offerings in a calendar year. An employee participating in multiple offerings or with a long lookback can hit the cap even if the actual purchase price is lower. Verify plan qualification and the purchase limit before applying the favorable rules.

### Identify The Discount, Lookback, And Purchase Price Mechanics

A qualified ESPP sets the purchase price as a discount (up to 15%) off the FMV on either the purchase date or the offering (grant) date, whichever is lower—the lookback feature. For example, with a 15% discount and lookback, if the grant-date FMV was $50 and the purchase-date FMV is $80, the employee pays 85% of $50, or $42.50, buying shares worth $80. The per-share discount for tax purposes is the purchase-date FMV minus the actual purchase price ($80 - $42.50 = $37.50).

The discount is not taxed at purchase for a qualified ESPP; it becomes ordinary compensation income only when the shares are sold. The purchase price paid ($42.50) is the starting basis, but the basis is adjusted upward by the ordinary income recognized at sale. Always compute the grant-date FMV, purchase-date FMV, actual purchase price, and per-share discount from the ESPP purchase confirmation before analyzing any sale.

### Apply The Qualifying Disposition Holding Periods

A sale is a qualifying disposition only if the shares are held for at least two years from the grant (offering) date AND one year from the purchase date. If both hold, the ordinary income portion is limited to the lesser of the actual discount at purchase or the gain on sale; any remaining gain is long-term capital gain, and any loss is a capital loss (the discount cannot create a loss).

For a qualifying sale, the ordinary income is the per-share discount calculated at purchase (e.g., $37.50 in the example), capped at the gain actually realized. If the stock declined between purchase and sale, the ordinary income is limited to the gain (possibly zero), and the basis is the purchase price plus that ordinary amount. Qualifying treatment favors the employee by capping ordinary income and characterizing the rest as long-term capital gain, but it requires patience through the holding periods.

### Compute Disqualifying Disposition Income Differently

If either holding period is missed, the sale is a disqualifying disposition, and the ordinary income portion equals the bargain element at purchase—the difference between the purchase-date FMV and the purchase price (the full $37.50 discount in the example)—regardless of whether the stock later declined. Any gain above the purchase-date FMV is capital gain; any loss below the purchase-date FMV is a capital loss.

The ordinary income for a disqualifying sale is typically reported as wages on the W-2 if the employer is notified of the disposition, or as additional compensation on Schedule 1 if not. Because the ordinary portion is fixed at the purchase-date discount even in a losing sale, disqualifying early sales can produce ordinary income with no cash gain to cover it. Compare the qualifying versus disqualifying result before deciding to sell early.

### Correct The Basis Reported On Form 1099-B

Brokers routinely report ESPP basis as the actual purchase price paid (e.g., $42.50), without adding back the ordinary income element. If the taxpayer reports the sale using the broker's basis, the ordinary income portion is taxed twice—once as wages and again as part of the capital gain. The taxpayer must adjust the basis on Form 8949 to the purchase price plus the ordinary income recognized.

For a qualifying sale, the adjusted basis is purchase price plus the ordinary income (discount) recognized; for a disqualifying sale, it is purchase price plus the full purchase-date discount. Use Form 8949 adjustment code B (basis incorrect) or code C as appropriate, and enter the corrected basis. Retain the ESPP purchase confirmation and the W-2 or compensation statement to substantiate the adjustment; without it, the IRS matching program may flag the discrepancy.

### Reconcile W-2 Compensation With The Sale

For disqualifying dispositions, the employer may report the ordinary bargain element as wages on the W-2 (Box 1, and Boxes 3/4 for FICA if in the same year), reducing the basis adjustment needed. For qualifying dispositions, the ordinary income is generally not on the W-2 and must be reported by the taxpayer on Schedule 1 as additional compensation. The total reported—W-2 wages plus capital gain—must reconcile to the sale proceeds.

A common failure is double-reporting: the employee includes the discount as wages (from the W-2) and also fails to adjust basis, taxing the same dollars as capital gain. Identify whether the employer reported the ordinary income and adjust basis accordingly so each dollar is taxed exactly once. Reconcile the W-2, the 1099-B, and the ESPP purchase confirmation before filing.

### Track Lots, Holding Periods, And Offering Dates Annually

Each ESPP purchase is a separate lot with its own grant date, purchase date, purchase price, and discount. Because employees often accumulate shares over many offering periods, the lots must be tracked individually to determine qualifying versus disqualifying status and the correct basis for each sale. Specific share identification or FIFO must be applied consistently.

Maintain the purchase confirmations showing the offering date, purchase date, FMVs, and purchase price for every lot. Recheck holding periods at each sale, because a sale of mixed lots can be partly qualifying and partly disqualifying. Because the $25,000 limit and discount rules can change, verify the plan terms for each offering period rather than assuming prior-year mechanics.

## Common Traps

### Relying On The Broker's Reported Basis

Brokers often report ESPP basis as the purchase price without the compensation element, causing the discount to be taxed twice. Always adjust basis on Form 8949 to include the ordinary income recognized.

### Missing The Qualifying Holding Periods By A Few Days

Qualifying treatment needs two years from grant and one year from purchase. Selling even one day early disqualifies the sale and converts the discount to ordinary income.

### Treating The Discount As Capital Gain

The ESPP discount is ordinary compensation income at sale, not capital gain, regardless of holding period. Only appreciation above the purchase-date FMV is capital gain.

### Assuming The Purchase Is The Taxable Event

For a qualified Section 423 ESPP, the discount is not taxed at purchase; it is taxed at sale. Treating purchase as the taxable event misstates the timing and the income.

### Forgetting The $25,000 Annual Purchase Limit

The limit is based on FMV at the start of the offering period, accumulated across overlapping offerings in a calendar year. Long lookbacks and multiple offerings can hit the cap unexpectedly.

### Double-Reporting Ordinary Income

When the employer reports the disqualifying-disposition bargain element on the W-2, the taxpayer must still adjust basis; failing to do so taxes the discount as both wages and capital gain.

### Applying Qualified-ESPP Rules To A Non-Qualified Plan

Non-qualified ESPPs tax the discount as ordinary income at purchase, with no holding-period benefit. Confirm the plan is Section 423 before applying the favorable rules.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the plan been confirmed as a qualified Section 423 ESPP, with the $25,000 annual purchase limit (based on grant-date FMV) and 15% discount cap verified?
- [ ] Are the grant (offering) date, purchase date, grant-date FMV, purchase-date FMV, actual purchase price, and per-share discount identified for each lot from the purchase confirmation?
- [ ] For each sale, has qualifying versus disqualifying disposition status been determined using the two-years-from-grant and one-year-from-purchase holding periods?
- [ ] Is the ordinary income portion computed correctly (capped at the purchase-date discount for qualifying sales; equal to the full bargain element for disqualifying sales)?
- [ ] Is the cost basis on Form 8949 adjusted to the purchase price plus the ordinary income recognized, with adjustment code B or C entered when the 1099-B basis is incorrect?
- [ ] Has the W-2 been checked for employer-reported ordinary income on disqualifying dispositions, with basis adjusted to avoid double-taxation?
- [ ] For qualifying dispositions, is the ordinary income reported on Schedule 1 as additional compensation if not on the W-2?
- [ ] Are ESPP lots tracked individually with consistent specific-share or FIFO identification, since a sale can be partly qualifying and partly disqualifying?
- [ ] Are purchase confirmations, W-2s, and 1099-Bs reconciled annually so that each dollar of discount is taxed exactly once?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer been advised that this is general guidance and not tax advice, and that a qualified tax professional, CPA, or equity compensation specialist must be consulted before any sale or reporting decision?
