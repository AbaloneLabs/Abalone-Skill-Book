# RSU Taxation

Restricted Stock Units (RSUs) are a form of equity compensation whose taxation is triggered by vesting, not by grant or a later election. The single most consequential fact is that the fair market value of the shares on the vesting date is ordinary compensation, subject to income and payroll taxes, and is added to basis. Agents who treat RSUs like stock options, or who assume the brokerage sale proceeds equal taxable income, produce overstated gains, double taxation, and missed withholding reconciliation.

Agents tend to miss that RSU income is supplemental wages subject to a flat 22% federal withholding rate (37% on amounts over $1 million in a year), which is almost always lower than the employee's marginal rate and creates a tax-time shortfall; that the cost basis reported on Form 1099-B is often missing or zero, leading the taxpayer to report the full sale proceeds as gain; and that the vesting date value is already taxed so only post-vest appreciation is capital gain. The harm includes paying tax twice on the same dollars, large unexpected balances due, and basis disputes with the IRS.

This skill covers U.S. federal income and employment tax treatment of RSUs granted by an employer. Tax rates, withholding rates, and reporting rules are amended periodically and must be verified for the tax year. This is not tax advice. A qualified tax professional, CPA, or equity compensation specialist must be consulted before filing or planning around RSU activity.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Ordinary Income At Vesting, Not At Grant Or Sale

The taxable event for an RSU is the vesting date, when the restriction (substantial risk of forfeiture) lapses and the shares become transferable. On that date, the fair market value (FMV) of the shares—typically the closing price on the vesting date—is ordinary wage income, reported on Form W-2 (or a standalone statement) and subject to federal income tax, Social Security, and Medicare withholding. No Section 83(b) election is available for RSUs because there is no actual stock ownership until vesting.

This means the grant date is irrelevant for taxation and a later sale is a separate capital transaction. The vesting income is compensation regardless of whether the employee sells the shares. An employee who holds vested shares and watches them decline still owes tax on the vesting-date value, which can produce a tax liability exceeding the shares' later worth. Always identify the vesting date and FMV first.

### Treat Vesting Income As Supplemental Wages And Check Withholding

RSU vesting income is supplemental wages. If the employer withholds, federal income tax is withheld at a flat 22% rate (or 37% on supplemental wages exceeding $1 million in a calendar year). This flat rate is frequently below the employee's actual marginal rate, so the employee often owes additional tax at filing.

The employee should estimate the gap between the flat withholding and their marginal rate and either increase W-4 withholding, make estimated payments, or sell additional shares to cover the difference. Payroll taxes (Social Security at 6.2% up to the wage base, Medicare at 1.45% plus 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax above thresholds) also apply. Confirm whether the employer withheld and at what rate, because some plans net-share-settle (sell shares to cover) while others require the employee to fund the tax.

### Establish The Correct Cost Basis For Post-Vesting Sales

The vesting-date FMV that was taxed as compensation becomes the cost basis for any later sale of the shares. If the employee sells immediately at vesting (a same-day or sell-to-cover sale), there is essentially no additional gain or loss because basis equals proceeds. If the employee holds the shares, any appreciation after vesting is a capital gain (short-term or long-term based on holding period from vesting), and any decline is a capital loss.

The recurring problem is Form 1099-B reporting. Brokers often report a zero or missing basis for RSU shares because they did not track the compensation already included in income. The taxpayer must adjust the basis on Schedule D / Form 8949 to the vesting-date FMV to avoid taxing the same dollars twice. Use Form 8949 adjustment code B (basis reported as zero) and enter the correct basis; retain the W-2 or vesting statement proving the taxed amount.

### Distinguish Same-Day Sale, Sell-To-Cover, And Hold Strategies

A same-day sale sells all vested shares at vesting; the compensation income is taxed, withholding occurs, and the remaining proceeds are delivered with little or no capital gain. A sell-to-cover (net settlement) sells just enough shares to cover the withholding obligation and deposits the remaining shares into the employee's brokerage account; those retained shares have a basis equal to the vesting-date FMV. Holding all shares means the employee receives the full share count but must fund the tax from other sources.

Each strategy has different cash and risk profiles. Holding concentrates wealth in employer stock and exposes the employee to post-vesting decline on already-taxed income. Selling immediately diversifies but may trigger transaction costs. Identify which strategy occurred from the brokerage and payroll records before computing the tax result, because the basis and gain calculations differ.

### Apply The $100,000 Statutory Stock Option Distinction Does Not Apply, But ISO/ESPP Interactions Do

RSUs are not statutory stock options and do not qualify for ISO or ESPP treatment, so the $100,000 ISO grant limit and ISO holding-period rules are irrelevant to RSUs. However, employees often hold RSUs alongside incentive stock options and ESPP shares, and the different basis and holding-period rules must not be blended. An RSU's holding period for long-term capital gain begins on the vesting date, not the grant date.

Do not apply ISO disqualifying-disposition logic to RSU sales, and do not assume an ESPP qualifying-disposition holding period governs RSU shares. Keep each equity type's lots separate in the brokerage account and in tax records, because commingling leads to basis errors and misreported gain. When advising on a sale, identify the equity type and the specific lot before computing the result.

### Manage Concentration, Timing, And Estimated Tax Risk

Because RSU vesting creates compensation income regardless of whether shares are sold, employees can face large tax bills from vesting events even if they hold the shares. Concentration in employer stock adds investment risk on top of tax risk: if the stock falls after vesting, the employee owes tax on the higher vesting value while holding depreciated shares.

Plan around vesting dates by forecasting annual income, estimating the withholding shortfall from the 22% flat rate, and arranging estimated payments or W-4 adjustments. Consider a Section 10b5-1 trading plan for orderly diversification if the employee is an insider. Avoid selling to cover at the last minute in a down market, which can lock in a loss while the tax on the higher vesting value remains due.

### Reconcile W-2, 1099-B, And Brokerage Records Annually

At tax time, reconcile three documents: the W-2 (which includes RSU compensation and withholding), the 1099-B (which reports gross proceeds and often a missing basis), and the brokerage vesting statements (which show the vesting-date FMV). The W-2 vesting income plus any post-vest capital gain or loss should equal the total reported on the return.

A common reconciliation failure is reporting the full 1099-B proceeds as gain because the basis field is blank, which taxes the vesting income a second time. Enter the correct basis on Form 8949 with adjustment code B and verify the total against the W-2 compensation. Retain vesting statements and trade confirmations for at least the period of limitations plus any carryforward years.

## Common Traps

### Taxing The Sale Proceeds As Full Gain

When the 1099-B reports a zero basis, reporting the full proceeds as gain double-taxes the vesting income already on the W-2. The correct basis is the vesting-date FMV, entered with a Form 8949 adjustment.

### Assuming The 22% Withholding Covers The Tax

Flat 22% supplemental withholding is often below the employee's marginal rate, especially for high earners. The gap becomes a balance due at filing unless estimated payments or W-4 adjustments close it.

### Treating RSUs Like Stock Options

RSUs have no purchase price, no 83(b) election, and no option exercise. Applying option logic (such as a bargain element or exercise spread) produces incorrect income and basis.

### Overlooking Payroll Taxes At Vesting

RSU vesting income is subject to Social Security, Medicare, and Additional Medicare Tax, not just income tax. Failing to account for these can understate the total withholding need.

### Holding Concentrated Employer Stock After Vesting

Retaining vested shares concentrates risk and leaves the employee owing tax on the vesting value even if the stock later declines. Diversification should be weighed against tax already paid.

### Blending RSU Lots With ISO Or ESPP Shares

Different equity types have different basis and holding-period rules. Commingling lots in records or in the brokerage account leads to basis errors and misreported gain.

### Missing The Same-Day Sale Basis Adjustment

A same-day sale at vesting should produce near-zero capital gain, but if the basis is reported as zero on the 1099-B, the full proceeds appear as gain unless adjusted. Always verify and correct the basis.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the vesting date identified and is the fair market value on that date treated as ordinary compensation income reported on the W-2?
- [ ] Has supplemental withholding (flat 22%, or 37% above $1 million) been confirmed, and has the gap to the employee's marginal rate been addressed via W-4 or estimated payments?
- [ ] Are Social Security, Medicare, and Additional Medicare Tax obligations at vesting accounted for?
- [ ] Is the cost basis for any post-vesting sale set to the vesting-date FMV, with a Form 8949 adjustment (code B) entered when the 1099-B reports a zero or missing basis?
- [ ] Has the strategy (same-day sale, sell-to-cover, or hold) been identified from brokerage and payroll records to compute basis and gain correctly?
- [ ] Are RSU lots kept separate from ISO and ESPP shares, with holding periods measured from the vesting date?
- [ ] Has concentration risk in employer stock been considered alongside the tax already paid at vesting?
- [ ] Are the W-2, 1099-B, and brokerage vesting statements reconciled so that vesting income is not taxed twice?
- [ ] Are vesting statements and trade confirmations retained for the period of limitations plus any carryforward years?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer been advised that this is general guidance and not tax advice, and that a qualified tax professional, CPA, or equity compensation specialist must be consulted before filing or planning?
