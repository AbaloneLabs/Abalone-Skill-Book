# Lifetime Gift Exemption

The federal lifetime gift exemption is the same unified applicable exclusion amount that shelters the estate at death; gifts exceeding the annual exclusion and not otherwise excepted consume it dollar-for-dollar. Making a large lifetime gift is therefore not "free" tax planning but a prepayment of exemption that reduces what remains at death. Agents frequently frame lifetime gifting as a pure benefit, ignoring that the exemption is finite and unified, that gifts carry over the donor's basis rather than stepping up to fair market value, and that the elevated exemption is scheduled to sunset, which changes the calculus of when and what to gift.

The harm from casual lifetime-gift reasoning is often irreversible. An agent may encourage gifting appreciated assets to "remove future growth from the estate" without recognizing that the recipient takes the donor's low carryover basis, so the income tax on a later sale can exceed the estate tax saved. Conversely, an agent may underuse exemption by gifting depreciated assets when gifting appreciated-but-basis-poor assets, or may fail to lock in the higher pre-sunset exemption before it drops. The exemption sunset, the irrevocability of completed gifts, and the interaction with the annual exclusion and prior Form 709 usage all determine whether a lifetime gift actually advances the client's goals.

This skill covers the judgment needed to evaluate large lifetime gifts, how exemption is consumed and reconciled, the basis tradeoff between gifting and bequeathing, and the sunset timing risk. It does not cover annual exclusion mechanics, gift tax return preparation, or advanced trust structures such as GRATs or IDGTs, which warrant separate analysis. Because exemption amounts, sunset dates, and basis rules are statutory, nothing here is tax advice, and a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney must be consulted before relying on any conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Treat Lifetime Gifts As Consuming Unified Exemption

Lifetime taxable gifts and the estate at death share one applicable exclusion amount. A gift above the annual exclusion that is not otherwise excepted consumes lifetime exemption, and every dollar used during life reduces the shelter available at death. This is not a separate gift allowance.

Before advising a large gift, compute remaining exemption by subtracting prior adjusted taxable gifts from the year-specific applicable exclusion amount, and confirm whether any deceased-spouse unused exclusion is available. Request and review prior Form 709 returns, because clients often forget prior large gifts to trusts or family entities that have already consumed exemption. Do not describe lifetime gifting as additional capacity on top of the estate exemption.

### Weigh Carryover Basis Against Estate Tax Savings

Gifts of appreciated assets carry over the donor's basis to the recipient, while assets passing at death generally receive a stepped-up basis to fair market value. This creates a tradeoff: gifting removes future appreciation from the taxable estate but leaves the recipient with a low basis that generates income tax on a later sale.

For any proposed gift of appreciated property, compare the estate tax savings from removing the asset and its growth against the income tax cost of the recipient's carryover basis on a future sale. High-basis or depreciated assets are better gift candidates than low-basis appreciated assets, all else equal. Do not recommend gifting appreciated low-basis assets purely to remove growth without quantifying the basis cost; the income tax on sale can exceed the transfer tax avoided.

### Factor In The Exemption Sunset As A Timing Risk

The elevated applicable exclusion amount is scheduled to sunset after 2025 to a lower baseline unless Congress acts. Gifts completed before sunset generally lock in the higher exemption even if the amount later drops, subject to anti-abuse rules, which can make timely gifting advantageous for clients whose net worth exceeds the post-sunset amount.

When evaluating whether to gift before sunset, confirm the completion of the gift before the reduction, document the exemption used, and weigh the benefit against irrevocability, basis loss, and the client's life expectancy. The sunset is a reason to accelerate analysis, not to gift blindly; clients who die sooner than expected may have been better served retaining assets for the step-up. Model both pre-sunset and post-sunset scenarios before concluding.

### Confirm Completion And Valuation Of The Gift

Exemption is consumed only by completed gifts, meaning transfers where the donor has parted with dominion and control. Incomplete gifts, such as contributions to trusts retaining too much control, may not consume exemption immediately but create uncertainty, and overvaluation inflates the exemption used.

For each gift, confirm it is complete: the donor relinquished control, retained no prohibited strings, and the transfer is effective under state law. For hard-to-value assets such as closely held business interests, real estate, or collectibles, obtain a qualified appraisal and consider whether valuation discounts are defensible, because the reported value is the exemption consumed. An inflated valuation permanently wastes exemption; an undervalued gift risks audit adjustment and gift tax.

### Coordinate With Annual Exclusion And Exceptions

Lifetime exemption is consumed only by the portion of a gift exceeding the annual exclusion and not covered by the tuition or medical exceptions. Proper use of the annual exclusion, gift splitting, and the direct-payment exceptions reduces the exemption cost of an overall gifting program.

Structure gifts to use the annual exclusion first, consider gift splitting to double the per-donee shelter, and route education and medical support through direct payments that qualify for the separate exceptions. Do not treat a large gift as consuming exemption on its full value when part could be sheltered by the annual exclusion or recharacterized as a direct payment. Coordination across the year and across recipients preserves exemption.

### Reconcile Lifetime Usage On The Estate Return

Adjusted taxable gifts from all prior years are added back to the taxable estate on Form 706 to compute tentative tax, and the unified credit for any prior gift tax paid is reconciled. Lifetime gifts do not escape the estate tax system; they are folded into the final computation.

Ensure that prior Form 709 figures are accurate and available, because the estate return reconciliation depends on them. Errors in prior gift reporting propagate into the estate tax computation, potentially causing underpayment with interest or overpayment that is hard to recover. Maintain a running cumulative adjusted taxable gifts figure for each client to support accurate reconciliation.

### Consider Income, Gift, And Estate Tax Holistically

A lifetime gift decision affects three tax systems simultaneously: gift tax at the time of transfer, income tax through carryover basis, and estate tax through exemption consumption and the addback. Optimizing one in isolation can worsen another.

Evaluate the combined outcome: gift tax cost now, income tax cost to the recipient on a future sale, and estate tax savings or cost at death. For appreciated low-basis assets, the income tax cost can dominate; for high-basis or non-appreciated assets, gifting may be clearly beneficial. Do not present a lifetime gift as advantageous based on estate tax alone without considering the income tax basis consequence.

## Common Traps

### Framing Lifetime Gifting As Additional Free Capacity

Lifetime gifts consume the same unified exemption as the estate. Describing gifting as extra capacity on top of the estate exemption misleads clients into believing they have more shelter than they do.

### Gifting Appreciated Low-Basis Assets Without Basis Analysis

Recipients take the donor's carryover basis. Gifting appreciated low-basis assets can generate income tax on a later sale that exceeds the estate tax saved, making the gift net-negative.

### Ignoring The Sunset Timing Risk

The elevated exemption is scheduled to drop after 2025. Failing to model pre- and post-sunset scenarios can cause clients to miss the chance to lock in higher exemption or to gift unnecessarily when retention would have been better.

### Overvaluing Or Undervaluing Hard-To-Value Gifts

The reported gift value is the exemption consumed. Overvaluation wastes exemption permanently; undervaluation risks audit adjustment, gift tax, and penalties.

### Treating Incomplete Or Retained-Control Transfers As Gifts

Exemption is consumed only by completed gifts. Transfers where the donor retains too much control may not be completed gifts, creating uncertainty about whether and when exemption was used.

### Overlooking Prior Form 709 Usage

Remaining exemption depends on prior adjusted taxable gifts. Forgetting earlier large gifts causes the available shelter to be overstated and can lead to unexpected gift tax on a new transfer.
