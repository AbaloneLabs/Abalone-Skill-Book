# Self-Employed Retirement Plans

Self-employed retirement plans (SEP-IRA, solo 401k, SIMPLE IRA) let a business owner shelter a large share of earnings from current tax while building retirement savings, but each plan has a different contribution formula, limit, eligibility rule, administrative burden, and deadline. The judgment problem is that the "best" plan depends on the owner's contribution goal, income level, age, employee situation, and tolerance for administration — and choosing based on the highest headline limit alone often produces a poor fit, missed deadlines, or a plan that must cover employees.

Agents frequently recommend the plan with the largest limit without checking whether the owner can actually use it (the solo 401k employee contribution is capped at earned income, and the employer contribution formula differs for self-employed vs. W-2 owners), or they miss that funding deadlines are tied to the entity's tax filing deadline including extensions. The harm is a contribution that is disallowed (excess contribution penalty), a plan set up too late to fund for the prior year, or a plan that triggers employer contributions for employees the owner did not intend to cover. A second silent failure is using the wrong contribution base — the 20% self-employed formula versus the 25% W-2 formula look similar but are mechanically different because the self-employed base must be reduced for the contribution itself.

This skill covers SEP-IRA, solo 401k, and SIMPLE IRA contribution mechanics, limits, deadlines, and selection criteria. It is not tax advice; retirement plan rules have detailed employee coverage, nondiscrimination, and top-heavy requirements, and outcomes depend on facts that must be verified. Consult a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) or financial advisor before relying on any conclusion. Limits cited are 2024 figures and are indexed annually.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish The Contribution Formulas For Self-Employed Versus W-2 Owners

The contribution formula differs depending on whether the owner is paid as a self-employed individual (Schedule C, partner) or as a W-2 employee (S corporation shareholder). For a SEP-IRA, the employer contribution is 25% of W-2 compensation for a common-law employee, but only about 20% of net self-employment earnings for a self-employed owner, because the self-employed base must be reduced by the contribution itself and the one-half SE tax deduction. The two formulas produce different dollar amounts for the same economic income.

The trap is applying the 25% W-2 formula to self-employment earnings, overstating the contribution. An agent must identify the owner's compensation type and use the correct formula. For a self-employed owner with $100,000 of net SE earnings, the SEP contribution is approximately $20,000 (about 20%), not $25,000 (25%). For an S corporation shareholder-employee with $100,000 of W-2 wages, the SEP contribution is $25,000 (25%). The solo 401k employer side has the same self-employed vs. W-2 distinction. Always state the formula and the base explicitly before computing the contribution.

### Compute Solo 401k As Employee Plus Employer Components

A solo 401k (also called an individual or one-participant 401k) allows two contributions: an employee elective deferral up to the annual limit ($23,000 for 2024, plus a $7,500 catch-up for age 50+) and an employer profit-sharing contribution up to 25% of compensation (or about 20% of net SE earnings for a self-employed owner). The combined employee-plus-employer total is capped at $69,000 for 2024 ($76,500 for age 50+). The employee deferral is made from earned income and is the most powerful component for a lower-income owner because it is a flat dollar amount, not a percentage.

The solo 401k advantage is that an owner can contribute a large dollar amount on relatively modest earnings, because the employee deferral does not depend on a percentage. For a self-employed owner with $50,000 of net earnings, the employee deferral can be up to $23,000 (limited to earned income) plus an employer contribution of about $10,000, totaling $33,000 — far more than a SEP would allow on the same earnings (about $10,000). The trap is forgetting the employee deferral is limited to earned income, or that the combined total cannot exceed $69,000. Compute both components and apply both caps.

### Apply The Annual Limits And Catch-Ups For Each Plan

Each plan has its own annual limit. For 2024: SEP-IRA employer contribution is the lesser of 25% of compensation (or ~20% of net SE earnings) or $69,000; solo 401k combined employee-plus-employer is the lesser of the computed amount or $69,000 ($76,500 with age 50+ catch-up); SIMPLE IRA employee deferral is $16,000 with a $3,500 catch-up. The $69,000 overall defined-contribution limit (Section 415) applies across all employer plus employee contributions to a participant in a defined contribution plan.

The trap is mixing limits across plans or forgetting the overall 415 cap. An owner with both a SEP and a solo 401k for the same business cannot double the limit; the combined employer contributions are capped at $69,000 per person. An agent should identify the plan, apply the specific employee and employer limits, apply the age-based catch-up, and confirm the total does not exceed the 415 cap. Note that limits are indexed annually and must be verified for the year at issue.

### Check Employee Coverage Requirements Before Choosing A Plan

This is the most consequential selection factor. A SEP-IRA must cover all employees age 21+ who worked for the employer in at least 3 of the last 5 years and earned at least $750 (2024 threshold), with an employer contribution equal to the same percentage of their compensation as the owner's. A SIMPLE IRA requires covering all employees who earned at least $5,000 in any two prior years and are expected to earn $5,000 this year. A solo 401k is only available if the owner (and spouse) are the only employees (excluding certain union and nonresident alien employees); having even one common-law employee generally disqualifies the solo 401k structure.

The trap is setting up a solo 401k or SEP without checking employee coverage, then owing unexpected employer contributions for staff. An agent must determine whether the business has common-law employees and, if so, which plans are viable. A solo 401k is ideal for a true one-person business; a SEP works with employees but requires equal-percentage contributions; a SIMPLE IRA has lower limits but simpler employee coverage. The employee situation often dictates the plan more than the contribution limit.

### Determine Setup And Funding Deadlines Tied To Filing Extensions

Deadlines differ by plan and are often tied to the entity's tax filing deadline including extensions. A SEP-IRA can be set up and funded by the extended due date of the return (October 15 for a calendar-year sole proprietor or partnership). A solo 401k must be adopted by the end of the plan year (December 31) to make contributions for that year, but the employee elective deferral portion generally must be deposited by the regular W-2 filing deadline (January 31) or a similar date, and the employer contribution can be made by the extended return due date. A SIMPLE IRA must be set up by October 1 of the plan year (for an existing business) to allow contributions for that year.

The trap is assuming all plans can be funded by the extended return due date. A solo 401k adopted after year-end cannot accept contributions for the prior year, even with an extension — the plan must exist by December 31. An agent should confirm the setup deadline for each plan before recommending one for a last-minute contribution. For a sole proprietor who wants to reduce prior-year tax in March, a SEP-IRA is often the only viable option because the solo 401k setup deadline has passed. State the setup and funding deadlines explicitly for the chosen plan.

### Evaluate The Plan Choice Against Contribution Goals And Admin Burden

The selection should weigh contribution capacity, income level, age, employee situation, and administrative tolerance. A solo 401k maximizes contributions for a low-to-moderate-income owner (because of the flat employee deferral) but requires a plan document, potentially Form 5500-EZ filing when assets exceed $250,000, and adherence to the December 31 setup deadline. A SEP-IRA is simple to set up and fund late, has no Form 5500 filing, but only allows employer contributions (no employee deferral), so it is weaker for a low-income owner. A SIMPLE IRA offers employee deferrals at a lower limit with minimal admin but requires the October 1 setup and a 25% early-distribution penalty in the first two years.

An agent should map the owner's facts to the plan features. For a high-earning solo owner who wants maximum contribution and can handle admin, a solo 401k often wins. For an owner with employees who wants simplicity, a SEP or SIMPLE is better. For a last-minute prior-year contribution, a SEP is often the only option. Do not recommend a plan based on the headline limit alone — the actual usable contribution depends on the formula, the base, and the deadlines.

### Coordinate With Roth Options And The Backdoor Considerations

Solo 401k plans can include a Roth (after-tax) employee contribution component, allowing tax-free growth on the deferral portion. SEP-IRAs and SIMPLE IRAs are traditionally pre-tax, though a SIMPLE can sometimes be funded with Roth treatment depending on the plan. An owner considering a backdoor Roth strategy should note that a SEP-IRA holding pre-tax funds creates a pro-rata issue that complicates the backdoor Roth conversion, because the IRS aggregates all traditional IRAs for the pro-rata rule.

The trap is recommending a solo 401k Roth component or a backdoor Roth without considering existing pre-tax IRA balances. An agent should ask about existing traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRA balances before advising on Roth strategies, because the pro-rata rule can make a backdoor Roth largely taxable. The Roth decision also depends on the owner's current versus expected future tax rate, which is a planning judgment beyond the mechanical contribution computation.

## Common Traps

### Applying The 25% W-2 Formula To Self-Employment Earnings

The SEP employer contribution is 25% of W-2 compensation but only about 20% of net self-employment earnings. The trap is using 25% for a self-employed owner, overstating the contribution and creating an excess.

### Forgetting The Employee Deferral Is Limited To Earned Income

The solo 401k employee deferral ($23,000 for 2024) is capped at the owner's earned income. The trap is assuming the full $23,000 is always available regardless of how low the earnings are.

### Missing Employee Coverage Requirements

A SEP requires equal-percentage employer contributions for eligible employees; a solo 401k is generally unavailable with common-law employees. The trap is choosing a plan without checking staff, triggering unexpected contributions or plan disqualification.

### Assuming All Plans Can Be Funded By The Extended Return Due Date

A solo 401k must be adopted by December 31 to accept prior-year contributions. The trap is recommending a solo 401k in March for the prior year, when only a SEP can still be set up and funded.

### Overlooking The Section 415 Overall Limit Across Plans

The combined employer plus employee contributions to a participant are capped at $69,000 for 2024. The trap is contributing to multiple plans without aggregating, exceeding the cap and triggering excess contribution penalties.

### Ignoring Pro-Rata Issues With Backdoor Roth Strategies

Pre-tax balances in a SEP or traditional IRA complicate backdoor Roth conversions under the pro-rata rule. The trap is recommending a backdoor Roth without checking existing IRA balances.

### Choosing A Plan Based Only On The Headline Limit

The largest limit is not always the best fit. The trap is recommending the solo 401k for its high limit when the owner's income, employees, or admin tolerance make a SEP or SIMPLE more appropriate.
