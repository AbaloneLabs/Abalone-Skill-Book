# Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction

The self-employed health insurance deduction allows a self-employed individual to deduct health insurance premiums as an above-the-line adjustment, effectively getting a deduction that is more valuable than an itemized medical deduction because it reduces AGI. The judgment problem is that eligibility and the deductible amount are governed by several interacting limits — net SE profit, access to employer-subsidized coverage, the LTC age-based caps, and special partner and S corporation 2% shareholder rules — and missing any one of them produces a deduction that is either disallowed or miscalculated.

Agents frequently claim the full premium without checking whether the taxpayer (or spouse) was eligible for an employer-subsidized plan, which disqualifies the deduction for any month of eligibility even if the taxpayer did not enroll. The harm is an overstated adjustment that fails on audit with back tax plus penalties. A second silent failure is the net SE profit cap: the deduction cannot exceed net SE profit from the trade or business providing the coverage, so a taxpayer with a small Schedule C profit and large premiums can only deduct up to the profit. A third is mishandling the S corporation 2% shareholder rule, where premiums paid by the corporation are reported as wages and then deducted on Schedule 1 — a two-step process that agents often collapse or reverse.

This skill covers eligibility, the employer-plan disqualifier, family coverage, LTC premium limits, the net profit cap, and the partner and S corporation 2% shareholder mechanics. It is not tax advice; the deduction has detailed rules for ACA premium tax credit interaction, Medicare premiums, and partial-year eligibility, and outcomes depend on facts that must be verified. Consult a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) before relying on any conclusion. LTC limits cited are 2024 figures and change annually.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Eligibility Based On Net SE Profit And Self-Employed Status

The deduction is available to a self-employed individual with net SE profit from a trade or business, including sole proprietors (Schedule C), general partners, and S corporation shareholders owning more than 2% of the stock. The taxpayer must have net profit from the business; if the business has a loss, the deduction is not available from that business. The deduction is computed using the net SE profit, and the actuarial value of the coverage is not relevant — only the premiums paid and the eligibility rules.

An agent should first confirm that the taxpayer has qualifying self-employment income. A partner's eligibility is based on their distributive share of partnership net earnings plus guaranteed payments; an S corporation 2% shareholder's eligibility is based on wages from the corporation. The deduction is allowed for the taxpayer, spouse, and dependents, and the coverage can be in the name of the business or the individual. Do not assume eligibility from the mere fact of being self-employed — verify net profit and the specific entity rules.

### Apply The Employer-Subsidized Plan Disqualifier For Each Month

The deduction is disallowed for any month in which the taxpayer (or spouse, if filing jointly) was eligible to participate in an employer-subsidized health plan, even if they did not enroll. "Subsidized" means the employer paid any part of the premium or reimbursed any part of the cost. This is a month-by-month test: if the spouse became eligible for employer coverage on July 1, the deduction is allowed for January through June and disallowed for July through December, regardless of enrollment.

This is the most common reason the deduction is denied on audit. An agent must ask, for each month, whether the taxpayer or spouse was eligible for any employer-subsidized plan (current employer, former employer via COBRA, or a spouse's employer). Eligibility, not enrollment, controls. A taxpayer who declines employer coverage to stay on an individual plan still loses the deduction for the months of eligibility. Document the eligibility status for each month, because a single month of eligibility can disallow a large portion of the annual premium.

### Include Premiums For Self, Spouse, And Dependents With LTC Limits

The deduction covers medical, dental, and qualified long-term care (LTC) insurance premiums for the taxpayer, spouse, and dependents. Qualified LTC premiums are deductible but subject to age-based annual caps that are indexed for inflation. For 2024, the per-person LTC caps are approximately $470 (age 40 or under), $880 (41-50), $1,760 (51-60), $4,710 (61-70), and $5,880 (71 and over). Premiums above the age cap are not deductible as LTC insurance (though excess may qualify as a medical itemized deduction).

The trap is deducting the full LTC premium without applying the age-based cap, which overstates the deduction. An agent should identify each insured person's age and apply the corresponding cap. Also confirm that the LTC policy is "qualified" under Section 7702B — non-qualified policies are not deductible. Medicare premiums (Part B, Part D, Medicare Advantage) are deductible for a self-employed individual who is 65 or older, but only to the extent they are not reimbursed and subject to the same employer-plan disqualifier.

### Apply The Net SE Profit Cap On The Deductible Amount

The deduction cannot exceed the net SE profit from the trade or business that provides the coverage basis. For a sole proprietor, this is Schedule C net profit; for a partner, it is their net earnings from the partnership (distributive share plus guaranteed payments, reduced by the one-half SE tax deduction); for an S corporation 2% shareholder, it is their wages from the corporation. If premiums exceed net SE profit, the excess is not deductible as an above-the-line adjustment (though it may qualify as an itemized medical deduction subject to the 10%-of-AGI floor).

An agent should compute the cap explicitly and compare premiums to net profit. For a taxpayer with $30,000 of Schedule C profit and $18,000 of family premiums, the full $18,000 is deductible (under the cap). For a taxpayer with $10,000 of profit and $18,000 of premiums, only $10,000 is deductible above the line; the remaining $8,000 may be deductible as an itemized medical expense if the taxpayer itemizes and exceeds the 10% AGI floor. Do not deduct premiums in excess of net profit as an above-the-line adjustment.

### Handle The S Corporation 2% Shareholder Two-Step Mechanics

For an S corporation shareholder owning more than 2% of the stock, health insurance premiums paid or reimbursed by the corporation must be reported as wages on the shareholder's W-2 (in box 1, but not boxes 3 or 5 for FICA purposes), and the shareholder then deducts the premiums as an above-the-line adjustment on Schedule 1. The corporation does not deduct the premiums as a business expense on its own return in the same way; instead, the wages are deducted, and the shareholder gets the adjustment. This two-step is required for the deduction to be valid.

The trap is treating the premiums as a corporate business expense without reporting them as wages to the shareholder, which breaks the deduction. An agent must confirm that the S corporation has added the premiums to the shareholder's W-2 wages and that the shareholder claims the adjustment on Schedule 1. If the shareholder pays the premiums personally and the corporation does not reimburse, the deduction is still available to the shareholder as long as the wage-reporting and adjustment rules are followed. The 2% ownership threshold is strict — verify the shareholder's exact ownership percentage.

### Apply The Partner Mechanics Via Guaranteed Payments Or Distribution

For a partner, health insurance premiums can be paid by the partnership as a guaranteed payment (deductible by the partnership, reported on the partner's K-1, and the partner deducts them on Schedule 1) or paid by the partner personally and deducted on Schedule 1. Either way, the partner gets the above-the-line deduction, but the partnership-level treatment must be consistent. If the partnership pays premiums as a guaranteed payment, it is included in the partner's net SE earnings for SE tax purposes; if the partner pays personally, it is not a guaranteed payment.

An agent should determine how the premiums were paid and ensure the K-1 and Schedule 1 treatment match. The partner's deduction is limited to their net earnings from the partnership, and the employer-plan disqualifier applies at the partner (and spouse) level. Do not double-count premiums that were both reimbursed by the partnership and deducted personally.

### Coordinate With The ACA Premium Tax Credit

If the taxpayer receives a premium tax credit (PTC) under the Affordable Care Act to help pay for marketplace coverage, the self-employed health insurance deduction cannot include the portion of premiums paid by the PTC. The deduction is only for the portion the taxpayer paid out of pocket. The interaction is computed on Form 8962 and Form 7206, and the order of operations matters because the deduction reduces AGI, which affects PTC eligibility, creating a circularity that the forms resolve.

The trap is deducting the full premium when part was paid by the PTC, or claiming the deduction without reconciling the PTC on Form 8962. An agent should obtain the Form 1095-A and reconcile the PTC before finalizing the health insurance deduction, because the two are interdependent. The deduction can also affect other AGI-based computations (NIIT, QBI, student loan phaseouts), so model the AGI reduction carefully.

## Common Traps

### Claiming The Full Premium Despite Employer-Plan Eligibility

The deduction is disallowed for any month the taxpayer or spouse was eligible for an employer-subsidized plan, even without enrolling. The trap is claiming the full annual premium without checking monthly eligibility, which overstates the deduction.

### Exceeding The Net SE Profit Cap

The deduction cannot exceed net SE profit from the business providing the coverage basis. The trap is deducting premiums above net profit as an above-the-line adjustment, when the excess may only be an itemized medical deduction subject to the 10% AGI floor.

### Missing The S Corporation 2% Shareholder Wage-Reporting Step

S corporation premiums must be added to the shareholder's W-2 wages and then deducted on Schedule 1. The trap is treating premiums as a corporate expense without wage reporting, which invalidates the shareholder's deduction.

### Deducting Full LTC Premiums Above Age Caps

Qualified LTC premiums are capped by the insured's age. The trap is deducting the full LTC premium without applying the indexed age-based cap, overstating the deduction.

### Double-Counting Reimbursed Partner Premiums

If the partnership pays premiums as a guaranteed payment and the partner also deducts them personally, the deduction is doubled. The trap is failing to coordinate the K-1 and Schedule 1 treatment.

### Ignoring The ACA Premium Tax Credit Interaction

The portion of premiums paid by the PTC is not deductible. The trap is deducting the full premium without reconciling the PTC on Form 8962, overstating the deduction and misstating the credit.

### Forgetting Medicare Premiums Are Deductible For Self-Employed Seniors

Medicare Part B, Part D, and Medicare Advantage premiums are deductible for a self-employed individual age 65 or older. The trap is overlooking these premiums, which are often withheld from Social Security and easy to miss.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Eligibility has been confirmed based on net SE profit from a qualifying trade or business (sole proprietor, general partner, or S corporation shareholder owning more than 2% of stock).
- [ ] The employer-subsidized plan disqualifier has been tested month by month for both the taxpayer and spouse, with the deduction disallowed for any month of eligibility regardless of enrollment.
- [ ] Premiums for self, spouse, and dependents have been included, with qualified LTC premiums capped by the insured's age (2024 caps approx. $470 under 41 to $5,880 over 70).
- [ ] The deduction has been capped at net SE profit from the business providing the coverage basis, with any excess routed to itemized medical (subject to the 10% AGI floor) rather than the above-the-line adjustment.
- [ ] For an S corporation 2% shareholder, premiums have been added to W-2 wages (box 1 only) and the shareholder deducts them on Schedule 1, with the two-step confirmed.
- [ ] For a partner, the guaranteed-payment or personal-payment treatment has been coordinated between the K-1 and Schedule 1 to avoid double-counting.
- [ ] The ACA premium tax credit has been reconciled on Form 8962, and only the out-of-pocket portion of premiums (not the PTC-paid portion) has been deducted.
- [ ] Medicare premiums (Part B, D, Advantage) have been included for self-employed individuals age 65 or older, subject to the same employer-plan disqualifier.
- [ ] The deduction has been reflected in AGI for its effect on QBI, NIIT, and other AGI-based computations.
