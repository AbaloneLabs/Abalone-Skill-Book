# Financial Aid And Ownership Impact

How a 529 plan is owned changes both who controls the money and how the financial aid formulas treat it, and the two objectives can pull in opposite directions. The judgment problem is that the ownership structure that maximizes contributor control or tax efficiency is not always the structure that minimizes the aid assessment, and the ownership structure that looks best for aid today may not survive the next change to the FAFSA rules. With the FAFSA Simplification Act now in effect, the treatment of grandparent-owned 529 distributions has shifted, and the SECURE 2.0 Act added a long-awaited escape valve that lets families roll unused 529 balances into a Roth IRA under specific conditions. An agent who reasons from pre-simplification rules, or who treats the assessment rates as fixed, will give advice that is both outdated and potentially harmful to the aid award.

The blind spots here are concentrated in three places. First, agents apply the old FAFSA rule under which grandparent 529 distributions counted as student income and devastated aid, when under FAFSA Simplification those distributions are no longer reported as income on the FAFSA, a major change that flips the grandparent-ownership calculus. Second, agents conflate the assessment rates of different account types, forgetting that parent-owned 529s and parent assets are assessed at a much lower rate than student-owned custodial accounts, which means where the money is titled can change the aid award by thousands of dollars. Third, agents either ignore the SECURE 2.0 529-to-Roth rollover or state its rules loosely, missing the 15-year account-age requirement, the 35,000 dollar lifetime cap, the annual IRA contribution limit, and the income and compensation tests that gate eligibility. The harm is a collapsed aid award, a trapped 529 balance, or a disallowed rollover.

This skill covers how account ownership affects FAFSA financial aid and the SECURE 2.0 529-to-Roth rollover, under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax or financial aid advice. FAFSA rules are actively in flux under the FAFSA Simplification Act and implementation has shifted across award years, so all assessment treatment must be verified against the current FAFSA form and guidance. The SECURE 2.0 rollover conditions are statutory but the dollar amounts and operational details should be confirmed against current IRS guidance. A qualified tax or financial aid professional must be consulted for an actual family's situation.

## Core Rules

### Treat Ownership As An Aid And Control Decision Simultaneously

Account ownership is not just a titling detail; it determines both who controls the funds and how the aid formula assesses them, and these two effects must be weighed together. The account owner of a 529 controls distributions, can change the beneficiary, and can even reclaim the funds, so ownership is a control decision first. But ownership also drives the aid assessment: a parent-owned 529 is reported as a parent asset and assessed at a favorable rate, a grandparent-owned 529 is generally not reported as an asset at all, and a custodial (UGMA/UTMA) 529 or a student-owned account is assessed at a much higher rate. The decision must balance the contributor's need for control against the aid formula's treatment of that ownership type.

Clarify both axes with the family before recommending. A grandparent who wants control should open their own 529 rather than contributing to a parent-owned plan, and under FAFSA Simplification the grandparent-owned plan's distributions no longer harm aid on the FAFSA, which makes grandparent ownership more attractive than it was. But a family applying to CSS Profile schools must remember those schools may still treat grandparent assets or distributions differently. Always pair the control question, who needs to direct the funds, with the aid question, how each school's formula treats that ownership, and note that the FAFSA and CSS Profile are not interchangeable.

### Apply The FAFSA Assessment Rates By Account Type

Under the FAFSA, different account types are assessed at different rates, and the gap is large enough to drive the ownership decision. Parent assets, including parent-owned 529 plans, are assessed at a maximum rate of 5.64 percent under the Student Aid Index methodology, meaning each dollar of parent asset reduces aid eligibility by roughly five and a half cents. Student-owned assets, including custodial UGMA and UTMA accounts, are assessed at 20 percent, meaning each dollar reduces aid by twenty cents, a punitive rate more than three times the parent rate. Grandparent-owned 529 plans are generally not reported as an asset on the FAFSA at all, so the balance does not reduce aid as an asset.

Position education savings in the most aid-favorable vehicle where control allows. For a family with savings in a child's custodial account, the 20 percent assessment is a significant drag, and strategies such as spending the custodial funds on the child's behalf or moving them to a 529 (where permitted) can reduce the assessment, though direct transfers have limits. For new savings, prefer parent-owned 529s over custodial accounts when aid matters. Always confirm the current assessment rates and the asset protection allowances, because FAFSA Simplification changed the asset protection allowance and the exact treatment continues to evolve across award years.

### Update Grandparent-Distribution Treatment To FAFSA Simplification

The most important recent change is how grandparent-owned 529 distributions are treated, and outdated advice here is actively harmful. Under the pre-simplification FAFSA, a distribution from a grandparent-owned 529 to pay for the student's education was counted as untaxed student income on the subsequent FAFSA, assessed at up to 50 percent, which could wipe out aid eligibility. Under the FAFSA Simplification Act, qualified distributions from grandparent-owned 529s are no longer reported as student income on the FAFSA, removing the aid penalty that previously made grandparent ownership dangerous.

Recompute the grandparent-ownership calculus with current rules. Because grandparent 529 balances are not reported as assets and distributions no longer count as income on the FAFSA, grandparent ownership is now far more aid-favorable than it was, which can make a grandparent-owned 529 a strong vehicle for families focused on federal aid. However, emphasize two caveats: CSS Profile institutions may still collect grandparent 529 information and treat it differently, and the FAFSA Simplification rollout has had implementation shifts across award years, so the treatment must be confirmed for the specific award year. Never repeat the old advice that grandparent distributions crush aid without verifying whether the school uses the FAFSA or the CSS Profile.

### Use The SECURE 2.0 529-To-Roth Rollover Within Its Limits

The SECURE 2.0 Act created a rollover from a 529 to a Roth IRA in the beneficiary's name, which is the main escape valve for overfunded 529s, but it is tightly constrained and the conditions must be tracked precisely. The lifetime limit is 35,000 dollars per beneficiary rolled from a 529 to a Roth IRA. The 529 must have been open for at least 15 years before the rollover, and contributions made within the last five years, plus their earnings, are not eligible to be rolled. The rollover counts against the beneficiary's annual IRA contribution limit for the year, and the beneficiary must have compensation, and their income must fall within the Roth IRA eligibility limits for that year.

Apply the rollover only after confirming every condition. For a family whose child did not need the full 529 balance, rolling up to 35,000 dollars to the child's Roth IRA can rescue the funds from tax and penalty, but only if the account is 15 years old, the recent contributions are excluded, and the child has compensation and qualifies for a Roth contribution in the rollover year. Note that the rollover is per beneficiary, so a 529 with a new beneficiary who is a sibling can be rolled for that sibling, but the 15-year clock and the 35,000 dollar cap apply to that beneficiary. Because the rollover is new and operational guidance has evolved, always verify the current IRS rules before executing, and do not present the rollover as an unrestricted escape hatch.

### Coordinate Ownership And Rollover Planning Over The Timeline

The ownership decision and the rollover option interact over the saving timeline, so plan them together rather than separately. Early on, ownership is chosen for control and aid treatment, typically parent-owned for aid favorability or grandparent-owned for control plus FAFSA asset invisibility. As the beneficiary nears and enters college, the focus shifts to spending the funds on qualified expenses to avoid penalties. If funds remain after education is complete, the SECURE 2.0 rollover, beneficiary changes to a sibling, or qualified uses like student loan repayment (up to a lifetime cap) become the exit strategies.

Sequence the plan across the timeline. For a young child, a parent-owned 529 captures aid-favorable treatment and a state deduction; a grandparent-owned 529 preserves control and FAFSA asset invisibility. For a child approaching college, confirm the ownership is still optimal and that no late ownership changes create unintended gift or tax effects. For leftover funds, evaluate the 15-year-aged rollover, a beneficiary change, or the student-loan-repayment qualified use, in that order of flexibility. Always keep multiple exit options open, because overfunding risk is real and the rollover has a 15-year waiting period that cannot be shortcut.

### Distinguish FAFSA From CSS Profile Treatment

Federal aid and institutional aid use different formulas, and advice that works for the FAFSA can fail at a CSS Profile school. The FAFSA, which governs federal grants, loans, and work-study, uses the Student Aid Index and the assessment rates described above. The CSS Profile, used by many selective private colleges for their own institutional aid, collects more information, may include home equity, non-custodial parent income, and in some cases retirement balances, and may treat grandparent 529 assets or distributions differently than the FAFSA. A move that is invisible on the FAFSA, like a grandparent-owned 529, may be visible and assessed on the CSS Profile.

Identify which schools use which methodology before finalizing ownership advice. For a family targeting only FAFSA-only schools, grandparent 529 ownership is strongly favorable under current rules. For a family targeting CSS Profile schools, the grandparent 529 may be collected and assessed, reducing or eliminating the advantage, and the family should run each school's net price calculator. Never generalize FAFSA treatment to all schools, and always frame the recommendation as dependent on the specific institutions' methodologies.

## Common Traps

### Applying Pre-Simplification Grandparent-Distribution Rules

The symptom is warning that grandparent 529 distributions will crush the student's aid. The trap is that under FAFSA Simplification those distributions are no longer reported as student income on the FAFSA, so the old penalty is gone for federal aid. Use current rules and distinguish FAFSA from CSS Profile schools.

### Parking Savings In A Custodial Account For Aid Reasons

The symptom is holding education savings in a child's UGMA or UTMA account. The trap is that student assets are assessed at 20 percent, far above the 5.64 percent parent rate, which punishes aid. Prefer parent-owned 529s when aid matters.

### Stating The SECURE 2.0 Rollover As An Unrestricted Escape Hatch

The symptom is telling a family they can freely roll any leftover 529 into a Roth IRA. The trap is the 15-year account-age requirement, the 35,000 dollar lifetime cap, the annual IRA limit, and the compensation and income tests, all of which can disqualify the rollover. Confirm every condition before recommending.

### Conflating FAFSA And CSS Profile Treatment

The symptom is giving ownership advice as if all schools use the FAFSA. The trap is that CSS Profile schools collect and assess assets, including grandparent 529s and home equity, differently, so FAFSA-favorable moves can backfire institutionally. Identify each school's methodology first.

### Ignoring The Five-Year Recent-Contribution Exclusion For Rollover

The symptom is assuming the full 529 balance is rollable once the account is 15 years old. The trap is that contributions made in the last five years, plus their earnings, are ineligible for the rollover. Subtract recent contributions before computing the rollable amount.

### Treating Assessment Rates As Fixed And Permanent

The symptom is quoting the 5.64 percent and 20 percent rates as immutable. The trap is that FAFSA Simplification changed the asset protection allowance and the formula, and rates and allowances continue to evolve across award years. Verify the current treatment for the specific award year.

### Recommending Late Ownership Changes Without Tax Awareness

The symptom is moving a 529 between owners or beneficiaries late to improve aid. The trap is that ownership or beneficiary changes can carry gift-tax or other consequences and may not even change the FAFSA treatment as intended. Weigh any late change against its tax effects and actual aid impact.

- [ ] Have the FAFSA assessment rates been applied by account type, recognizing parent-owned 529s at up to 5.64 percent versus student-owned custodial accounts at 20 percent, and grandparent-owned 529s generally not reported as assets?
- [ ] Has grandparent-distribution treatment been updated to FAFSA Simplification, confirming that qualified grandparent 529 distributions are no longer reported as student income on the FAFSA, while flagging CSS Profile differences?
- [ ] Have all SECURE 2.0 529-to-Roth rollover conditions been tracked, including the 15-year account age, the 35,000 dollar lifetime cap, the five-year recent-contribution exclusion, the annual IRA limit, and the compensation and income tests?
- [ ] Has the ownership and rollover plan been sequenced across the timeline, from early saving through college spending to leftover-fund exit strategies?
- [ ] Has the distinction between FAFSA and CSS Profile methodologies been applied, with each target school's methodology identified before finalizing ownership advice?
- [ ] Has the family been directed to each school's net price calculator for decision-relevant estimates rather than relying on generic formula assumptions?
- [ ] Have all assessment rates, FAFSA Simplification treatment, and rollover dollar limits been framed as subject to change, with the current award year and IRS guidance verified?
