# Consolidated Loss Limits

Within a consolidated group, the ability to use one member's losses to offset another member's income is the principal benefit of filing consolidated, but it is governed by a layered set of limitations that make loss utilization far more complex than simple aggregation. The judgment problem is that a consolidated loss is not a single pool; it is a collection of member-level attributes, each carrying its own limitation history, and the rules determine which losses can be absorbed in which order against which income. Agents tend to treat consolidated taxable income as a netting of all members' results and miss that losses a member generated before joining the group, or losses generated while a member had no positive separate taxable income, are restricted by the separate return limitation year (SRLY) rules.

The harm is that an agent can overstate the group's usable losses, leading to underpayment, surprise tax at year-end, and exposure to interest and penalties when the limitation surfaces on examination. The SRLY rules, the consolidated Section 382 rules on ownership changes, the loss disallowance rules on certain intercompany transactions, and the attribute basis rules all constrain loss use in ways that do not appear on a simple consolidated income statement. Losses can be permanently lost if a member leaves the group or if an ownership change trips Section 382 while the member holds unused attributes.

This skill covers federal US consolidated return loss limitation under Treas Reg 1.1502-21 and the related consolidated regulations, including SRLY and consolidated Section 382. It presumes familiarity with consolidated group eligibility. It does not cover the eligibility election itself or the intercompany transaction matching rules in detail, which are separate skills. All regulatory references reflect the current federal framework but must be verified against current law. This is structural tax analysis, not tax advice; a qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific determination.

## Core Rules

### Treat Consolidated Losses As Member-Level Attributes, Not A Single Pool

A consolidated group does not have its own net operating loss in the ordinary sense. Each member retains its separate loss attributes, and the consolidated return regulations determine how those attributes are combined, limited, and absorbed. When the group computes consolidated taxable income, the losses of all members are aggregated, but the absorption of each member's losses is tracked separately because the limitations differ. A loss generated by Member A may be fully usable while a loss generated by Member B is restricted by SRLY rules or by a Section 382 limitation specific to Member B.

The agent must maintain member-level attribute schedules, not just a consolidated total. For each member, track the loss year of origin, the character (ordinary, capital, Section 1231), the SRLY status, any applicable Section 382 limitation, and the absorption history. Without member-level tracking, the agent cannot determine how much loss is actually usable in a given year or what survives to future years.

### Apply The Separate Return Limitation Year Or SRLY Rules

The SRLY rule under Treas Reg 1.1502-21 is the central limitation on pre-acquisition and pre-joining losses. A separate return limitation year is any taxable year of a member that ends before the member became part of the group (including a short year caused by joining), and certain loss years of a member that had no positive consolidated net income attributable to it. Losses arising in a SRLY can be used to offset the consolidated taxable income of the group only to the extent of the member's separate taxable income, computed as if the member filed a separate return, for the consolidated year.

The practical effect is that a loss a subsidiary brought into the group at acquisition, or generated in a year when it had no positive income of its own, cannot freely offset the income of profitable sister members. It is limited to the subsidiary's own contribution to consolidated income. A member with large pre-acquisition losses and little current income may be unable to use those losses against the group's profits for years, or ever. The agent must identify which member losses are SRLY losses and apply the limitation, rather than assuming all member losses are freely usable.

### Determine Separate Taxable Income For Each Member Correctly

The SRLY limitation is measured against the member's "separate taxable income," a concept defined in the consolidated regulations that approximates what the member's taxable income would be on a separate return, but with adjustments for intercompany transactions and other consolidated items. Separate taxable income includes the member's own operating results, its share of intercompany items as they become taken into account, and certain other adjustments, but excludes items that are deferred or eliminated under the intercompany transaction rules.

The agent should compute separate taxable income for each SRLY-loss member to determine the absorption ceiling. A member with $500,000 of separate taxable income in the consolidated year can absorb up to $500,000 of its SRLY losses against consolidated income, even if the group has millions in consolidated taxable income. The ceiling is member-specific, which is why aggregated analysis fails.

### Apply Consolidated Section 382 On Ownership Changes

Section 382 limits a loss corporation's ability to use pre-change losses after an ownership change. In a consolidated context, the rules are modified and extended by Treas Reg 1.1502-79 and related provisions. An ownership change in a member can trigger a Section 382 limitation on that member's pre-change losses, and the limitation is computed by reference to the member's value and the long-term tax-exempt rate. The consolidated rules also address how an ownership change in the common parent or in a member affects the group.

The trap is assuming that consolidated filing immunizes members from Section 382. It does not. A member that undergoes an ownership change, whether through a stock issuance, a redemption, or an acquisition, can have its pre-change losses severely limited, and those limits apply even when the losses are used in the consolidated return. The agent must monitor ownership changes at the member level and compute Section 382 limitations for each affected member, applying the consolidated modifications.

### Track The Interaction Between SRLY And Section 382

SRLY losses and Section 382-limited losses can overlap, and the more restrictive limitation generally controls. A member's pre-acquisition losses are both SRLY losses and potentially subject to Section 382 if an ownership change occurred in connection with the acquisition. The agent must apply both limitations and determine which binds. In some cases the SRLY limitation (based on separate taxable income) is more restrictive; in others the Section 382 limitation (based on value times the long-term tax-exempt rate) is more restrictive.

The interaction requires careful sequencing. The agent should first identify all member losses, classify each as SRLY or non-SRLY, then determine whether any Section 382 ownership change limits apply, then apply the controlling limitation to each loss attribute. This is not a single computation but a layered analysis that must be documented for each member and each loss year.

### Recognize Loss Disallowance And Attribute Basis Rules

Beyond SRLY and Section 382, the consolidated regulations impose additional loss limitations. The loss disallowance rule under Treas Reg 1.1502-19 limits loss recognition on the disposition of subsidiary stock, generally preventing the group from recognizing a loss on stock of a member sold or disposed of while the member is in the group. The attribute basis rules adjust a member's attributes when it leaves the group, potentially reducing loss carryovers to prevent double benefit. These rules can eliminate losses that an agent might otherwise assume are usable.

The agent should flag any disposition of member stock, any member departure from the group, and any transaction that could trigger attribute reduction. The loss disallowance rule is particularly easy to overlook because an agent focused on the subsidiary's operations may not consider the parent's stock basis and the rule that prevents loss on intra-group stock dispositions.

### Maintain Per-Member Attribute Schedules Across Years

The consolidated loss limitation analysis is multi-year. Losses carry forward (and in some cases back) subject to the limitation rules, and the absorption in one year affects availability in future years. The agent must maintain schedules showing, for each member, the loss attributes by year of origin, character, SRLY status, Section 382 status, cumulative absorption, and remaining balance. These schedules must be reconciled to the consolidated return each year.

Without disciplined multi-year tracking, the agent cannot answer the basic question of how much loss is usable in the current year, and errors compound. A loss incorrectly treated as absorbed in one year is unavailable later; a loss incorrectly treated as available may trigger underpayment. The schedules are the analytical backbone of consolidated loss management.

## Common Traps

### Treating Consolidated Taxable Income As Simple Aggregation Of Member Results

Member losses are aggregated but absorbed subject to per-member limitations. SRLY and Section 382 can make large member losses unusable against group income.

### Assuming Pre-Acquisition Losses Are Freely Usable After Joining

SRLY losses are limited to the member's separate taxable income in the consolidated year. A subsidiary with big historic losses and little current income may use little or none of them.

### Overlooking Member-Level Section 382 Ownership Changes

Consolidated filing does not prevent Section 382 from limiting a member's pre-change losses after an ownership change. The consolidated rules modify but do not eliminate Section 382.

### Failing To Compute Separate Taxable Income For Each SRLY Member

The SRLY ceiling is member-specific. Without per-member separate taxable income, the absorption limit cannot be determined.

### Ignoring The Loss Disallowance Rule On Subsidiary Stock Dispositions

Loss on stock of a group member generally cannot be recognized while the member is in the group. This can eliminate an expected loss on a sale.

### Confusing SRLY And Section 382 Or Applying Only One

The two limitations can overlap, and the more restrictive controls. Applying only one can overstate usable losses.

### Losing Track Of Attributes When A Member Leaves The Group

Attribute basis rules can reduce a departing member's loss carryovers. Without tracking, losses may be overstated after departure.

## Self-Check

- Are consolidated losses treated as member-level attributes with per-member tracking, rather than as a single aggregated pool?
- Have all member losses been classified as SRLY or non-SRLY, with SRLY losses limited to the member's separate taxable income in the consolidated year?
- Has separate taxable income been computed for each SRLY-loss member to establish the absorption ceiling?
- Have Section 382 ownership changes been monitored at the member level, with consolidated Section 382 limitations computed for affected members?
- Has the interaction between SRLY and Section 382 been analyzed, with the more restrictive limitation applied to each loss attribute?
- Has the loss disallowance rule on subsidiary stock dispositions been checked before assuming a loss is recognizable?
- Have attribute basis rules been considered for any member departing the group, to avoid overstating carryover losses?
- Are multi-year per-member attribute schedules maintained, reconciled to the consolidated return, showing origin, character, SRLY status, Section 382 status, absorption, and remaining balance?
- Is the agent aware that usable consolidated losses may be far smaller than the sum of member losses, and has the current-year usable amount been specifically determined?
- Has the agent escalated to a qualified tax professional or CPA for any specific consolidated loss limitation determination, recognizing this analysis is not tax advice?
