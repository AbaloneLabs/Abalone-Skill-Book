# Deductibility And Substantiation

A deduction is not safe just because money left the bank account. Tax deduction review asks whether the cost is connected to the business, ordinary and necessary under the applicable rule, properly timed, properly classified, adequately documented, and not limited or disallowed. Agents often over-focus on categorizing expenses and under-focus on whether the taxpayer can prove the facts behind the deduction.

Use this skill before classifying tax deductions, preparing records for a return, reviewing expense policies, evaluating mixed-use costs, separating personal and business expenses, checking travel or home office claims, or deciding whether a cost should be deducted immediately, capitalized, depreciated, amortized, or routed for professional review. The goal is to prevent convenient expense treatment from replacing supported tax analysis.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Business Purpose

Every deduction should have a business purpose that can be explained in plain language. A receipt proves that something was purchased; it does not prove that the purchase was for the business.

Capture:

- what was purchased;
- who paid;
- who benefited;
- date incurred or paid;
- amount;
- vendor;
- business activity supported;
- client, project, location, or event if relevant;
- personal-use component;
- reimbursement status;
- related contract or policy.

If the business purpose cannot be stated, do not assume deductibility. Mark it for review.

### Apply Ordinary And Necessary As A Real Test

For U.S. federal business deductions, a common baseline is whether the expense is ordinary and necessary for the trade or business. Ordinary means common and accepted in the field; necessary means helpful and appropriate, not necessarily indispensable. Other jurisdictions and tax regimes may use different language, but the same discipline applies: connect the cost to the activity and applicable rule.

Ask:

- Is this expense common or accepted for this type of business?
- Is it helpful and appropriate for the business?
- Is it personal, capital, lavish, illegal, political, entertainment, or otherwise limited?
- Is there a specific rule for this expense category?
- Does the taxpayer have evidence beyond a bank entry?
- Does the expense create a future benefit that should be capitalized?

Do not turn the test into a slogan. Use facts.

### Substantiate The Elements Required For The Expense

The burden of proof matters. The taxpayer should be able to substantiate entries, deductions, and statements. Different expense categories require different facts.

Common evidence includes:

- receipt or invoice;
- proof of payment;
- business purpose;
- date and place;
- attendees and business relationship for meals when relevant;
- mileage log or travel itinerary;
- contract or engagement letter;
- payroll record;
- asset purchase details;
- allocation worksheet;
- reimbursement policy;
- canceled check, card statement, or electronic transfer evidence.

Do not rely on a credit card statement alone when category-specific records are needed. A card statement may prove payment but not purpose, attendees, mileage, business use, or item detail.

### Separate Personal, Mixed-Use, And Business Costs

Many risky deductions involve mixed personal and business use. Phones, vehicles, internet, home office, travel, meals, computers, subscriptions, gifts, and clothing may include both business and personal elements.

For mixed-use costs, document:

- total cost;
- business-use percentage or method;
- personal-use component;
- calculation basis;
- period covered;
- change in use over time;
- evidence supporting allocation.

Do not deduct the full cost merely because the item is sometimes used for business. If allocation rules are unclear or strict, route to tax review.

### Distinguish Expense From Asset Or Future Benefit

Some costs provide benefits beyond the current period or relate to acquiring, improving, or producing an asset. These may need capitalization, depreciation, amortization, inventory treatment, or special rules.

Review whether the cost relates to:

- equipment;
- furniture;
- vehicles;
- software implementation;
- building improvements;
- leasehold improvements;
- inventory or cost of goods sold;
- startup costs;
- loan costs;
- intangible assets;
- prepaid services;
- security deposits;
- repairs versus improvements.

Immediate deduction may be available under specific rules, elections, or thresholds, but the agent should not assume it. Capture facts needed for the professional to decide.

### Review Timing And Accounting Method

Tax timing depends on accounting method and specific rules. Cash-basis and accrual-basis taxpayers may treat the same transaction differently. Prepayments, unpaid liabilities, accrued expenses, inventory, and related-party transactions can create timing issues.

Ask:

- Was the amount paid, incurred, billed, or accrued?
- What period does the cost benefit?
- Has the service or product been received?
- Is the liability fixed and determinable?
- Is payment to a related party involved?
- Is the item subject to special timing rules?
- Is there a year-end cutoff issue?

Do not record a deduction in a year simply because it is convenient for tax planning.

### Identify Category-Specific Limits And Disallowances

Some expenses are deductible only partly, only under conditions, or not at all. The agent should flag category-specific rules rather than treating all expenses alike.

High-review categories include:

- meals;
- travel;
- entertainment;
- gifts;
- vehicle expenses;
- home office;
- business interest;
- charitable contributions;
- fines and penalties;
- lobbying and political expenses;
- insurance;
- employee benefits;
- owner compensation;
- related-party payments;
- startup costs;
- research and development;
- depreciation and amortization;
- taxes;
- legal settlements.

The correct treatment may depend on current-year law, entity type, documentation, and purpose. Use current official instructions or professional guidance before finalizing.

### Match Deductions To Gross Income And Activity

Expenses should make sense relative to the business. A deduction review should consider whether expenses align with revenue, business model, staffing, asset base, and operations.

Look for:

- large expenses with no clear revenue connection;
- high personal-use risk;
- expenses inconsistent with business activity;
- duplicate deductions;
- reimbursed expenses deducted again;
- owner personal costs in business accounts;
- expenses belonging to another entity;
- costs incurred before business start;
- costs after business closure;
- expenses paid by one entity for another.

Unusual does not mean nondeductible, but unusual should mean documented and reviewed.

## Common Traps

### Treating Bank Feed Categories As Tax Conclusions

Accounting software categories are not legal analysis. A transaction categorized as "office expense" may be a capital asset, personal item, prepaid cost, or nondeductible expense. Classification should follow evidence and tax rules.

### Assuming A Receipt Is Enough

A receipt may show date, amount, and vendor but not the business purpose. For travel, meals, vehicle use, home office, gifts, and mixed-use property, additional records may be required.

### Deducting Personal Expenses Paid From Business Accounts

Business accounts often contain owner draws, personal meals, family travel, household items, and personal subscriptions. Paying from a business account does not convert personal spending into a business deduction.

### Ignoring Reimbursements

A reimbursed cost generally should not create a duplicate deduction for the person who was reimbursed. Review whether the taxpayer paid personally, was reimbursed, billed the client, or deducted the cost elsewhere.

### Overlooking Capitalization

Agents may prefer immediate expense treatment because it is simpler. Asset purchases, improvements, inventory, startup costs, loan costs, and prepaid expenses require review before deduction timing is decided.

### Using Last Year's Rules Without Checking Current Law

Deduction percentages, thresholds, mileage rates, depreciation rules, credit interactions, and documentation expectations can change. Always verify current official rules for the year at issue.

### Failing To Flag Aggressive Or Uncertain Positions

Some positions may be supportable but uncertain. Do not bury uncertainty in the category label. Identify the facts, cite the rule source if known, state the uncertainty, and recommend qualified review.

## Self-Check

- Is the business purpose documented for each material deduction?
- Does the expense satisfy the applicable ordinary, necessary, business-related, or jurisdiction-specific deductibility test?
- Are required substantiation elements captured beyond proof of payment?
- Are personal, mixed-use, reimbursed, owner, and related-party costs separated?
- Are asset purchases, improvements, inventory, prepaids, startup costs, and long-term benefits flagged for capitalization or timing review?
- Is the deduction period consistent with accounting method, payment or accrual facts, service period, and cutoff rules?
- Are category-specific limits or disallowances checked for meals, travel, home office, vehicles, interest, gifts, legal costs, fines, benefits, and owner compensation?
- Are expenses matched to the correct entity, activity, project, and period?
- Are unusual or aggressive positions disclosed as review items rather than hidden in ordinary categories?
- Have current official rules or qualified professional guidance been checked before finalizing tax treatment?
