# EV And Charging Infrastructure Credits

Electric vehicle incentives under the Inflation Reduction Act span three distinct credits, each with its own qualification tests, dollar mechanics, and reporting obligations, and the most consequential of them, the new-vehicle Clean Vehicle Credit under Section 30D, is governed by supply-chain rules that the buyer cannot observe from the vehicle alone. The judgment problem is that a car being electric is necessary but not sufficient; the credit turns on where the vehicle and its battery were assembled, the sourcing of battery components and critical minerals, the buyer's income, the vehicle's price, and whether the seller has reported the required information. An agent who treats "it is an EV" as the whole test will overstate eligibility and expose the taxpayer to a denied credit.

Agents commonly miss several things: that 30D splits the 7,500 dollar credit into two independent 3,750 dollar halves with separate sourcing tests, that a vehicle can qualify for one half and not the other, that income limits use modified AGI and are tested in either the purchase year or the prior year, that the MSRP cap is a manufacturer's suggested retail price limit (not the negotiated price) tied to vehicle type, that used vehicles under 25E require a dealer sale and a specific price cap, that 30C for home charging has its own location and property-type rules, and that the seller must furnish a report the buyer relies on. The harm prevented is a buyer who structures a purchase expecting a credit that fails a sourcing, income, or price test discovered only at filing.

This skill covers the Section 30D Clean Vehicle Credit, the Section 25E Previously Owned Clean Vehicle Credit, and the Section 30C Alternative Fuel Vehicle Refueling Property Credit under U.S. federal income tax law for the Inflation Reduction Act era. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; energy credit rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change frequently, and many provisions have phase-down schedules and sourcing requirements that evolve. A qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Split The 30D Credit Into Two Independent Halves

The new-vehicle Clean Vehicle Credit under Section 30D is structured as two separate 3,750 dollar components, each with its own test, and a vehicle can satisfy one and fail the other. The first 3,750 dollars turns on the critical minerals requirement: a required percentage of the battery's critical minerals must be extracted or processed in the United States or a country with which the U.S. has a free trade agreement, or be recycled in North America. The second 3,750 dollars turns on the battery components requirement: a required percentage of the battery components must be manufactured or assembled in North America. The required percentages ramp up over years, so a vehicle that qualifies in one year may not in the next.

Do not assume a qualifying vehicle earns the full 7,500 dollars. Confirm which half (or both) the vehicle earns by checking the IRS list of qualifying vehicles and the seller's report, because the two tests are independent. The ramp-up of the percentage thresholds means the analysis is year-specific; verify the threshold in effect for the placed-in-service year. A vehicle meeting the minerals test but failing the components test earns only 3,750 dollars, and presenting the full amount to the buyer overstates the credit.

### Confirm Assembly, MSRP, And Income Limits For 30D

Beyond the sourcing halves, Section 30D has three additional gates. First, final assembly of the vehicle must occur in North America, confirmed through the vehicle identification number (VIN). Second, the manufacturer's suggested retail price (MSRP) must not exceed a cap that depends on the vehicle type: illustratively 80,000 dollars for vans, sport utility vehicles, and pickup trucks, and 55,000 dollars for other vehicles. The cap uses the MSRP, not the negotiated purchase price. Third, the taxpayer's modified adjusted gross income (modified AGI) must not exceed limits (illustratively 300,000 dollars for joint filers, 225,000 for head of household, and 150,000 for single filers), tested in either the year of purchase or the prior year, whichever is lower.

Run all three gates before claiming. Verify the VIN-based North American assembly, confirm the MSRP against the cap for the vehicle classification (SUV versus sedan classification matters), and confirm modified AGI against the limit using the lower of the current or prior year. The income test's two-year look allows a buyer whose income dropped to qualify, and a buyer whose income rose to still qualify using the prior year; model both years. A vehicle that passes sourcing but fails assembly, MSRP, or income earns no credit.

### Apply The Seller Reporting Requirement And The Transfer Option

For a 30D credit, the seller must furnish the buyer and the IRS a report at the time of sale containing the VIN, the amount of the credit the buyer may be eligible for, and other required information. The buyer relies on this report to claim the credit, and the seller's registration with the IRS is a prerequisite. The IRA also introduced a transfer option: the buyer may elect to transfer the credit to the dealer for an immediate price reduction, effectively monetizing the credit at the point of sale, which is especially relevant for buyers who cannot use the full nonrefundable credit.

Confirm the seller is registered and that the report is provided and accurate before relying on it. For the transfer election, weigh the immediate cash benefit against any loss of carryforward utility, and confirm the dealer's participation and the timing. The seller's report is the evidentiary backbone of the claim; without it, the credit is not available regardless of the vehicle's qualification. Note that the transferred credit becomes advance-paid and is reconciled on the return.

### Qualify The Previously Owned Clean Vehicle Credit Under 25E

The Previously Owned Clean Vehicle Credit under Section 25E provides up to 4,000 dollars (the lesser of 4,000 dollars or 30 percent of the sale price) for a qualifying used clean vehicle. The qualification gates include: the sale price must not exceed a cap (illustratively 25,000 dollars), the vehicle must be a model year at least two years earlier than the purchase year, the sale must be made by a licensed dealer (not a private party), the vehicle must meet the battery capacity and other clean-vehicle requirements, and the buyer's modified AGI must not exceed limits (illustratively 150,000 dollars for joint filers, 112,500 for head of household, and 75,000 for single filers), tested in the purchase year or prior year.

Confirm the dealer-sale requirement and the model-year offset before claiming; a private-party purchase does not qualify, and a too-new vehicle fails the two-year rule. Apply the income limit using the lower-of-two-years test. The 30-percent-of-price alternative matters for inexpensive used EVs, where 30 percent of a low sale price may be below 4,000 dollars. Note that a buyer cannot claim both 30D and 25E for the same vehicle, and the same buyer cannot claim 25E more than once every three years.

### Qualify The Refueling Property Credit Under 30C

The Alternative Fuel Vehicle Refueling Property Credit under Section 30C covers the cost of qualified alternative fuel vehicle refueling property, including home EV charging equipment. Under IRA-era law the credit is 6 percent of the cost for non-business property (capped illustratively at 1,000 dollars per item), and 30 percent of the cost for business property (capped illustratively at 100,000 dollars per item), with the property required to be placed in service in an eligible census tract (generally rural or low-income areas for non-business property). The eligible-census-tract rule is a key gate introduced by the IRA.

Confirm the property is qualified refueling property and that it is placed in service in an eligible census tract before claiming the non-business credit. Verify the cost basis includes installation and that the property is placed in service (not merely purchased) in the claim year. The census-tract eligibility must be checked using the IRS mapping tool; a charger in an ineligible tract earns no non-business credit. Coordinate 30C with any utility rebates that reduce the basis.

### Sequence The Three Credits And Avoid Conflicts

The three credits serve different transactions and do not stack on the same vehicle: 30D is for new vehicles, 25E is for used vehicles, and 30C is for charging infrastructure. A buyer of a new EV may claim 30D for the vehicle and 30C for a home charger, but cannot claim 25E for the same vehicle. The sequencing is to identify the transaction type, select the matching credit, run that credit's gates, and confirm no double-counting.

Map each expenditure to one credit. For a household buying a new EV and installing a charger, run 30D for the vehicle and 30C for the charger independently. For a used EV purchase, run 25E and confirm the dealer-sale and price-cap gates. Do not allow a vehicle purchase to be claimed under both 30D and 25E, and do not assume a charger automatically qualifies for 30C without the census-tract check.

## Common Traps

### Assuming A Full 7500 Dollar Credit For Any Qualifying EV

The symptom is a buyer expecting the full amount for any electric car. The trap is that 30D splits into two independent 3,750 dollar halves with separate minerals and components tests, so a vehicle may earn only one half. The direction is to confirm which half the vehicle earns from the seller's report and the IRS list.

### Ignoring The MSRP Cap And Vehicle Classification

The symptom is a credit claimed for a vehicle over the price limit. The trap is that the cap uses the MSRP and depends on whether the vehicle is classified as an SUV or a sedan, with different limits. The direction is to confirm the MSRP against the correct classification cap, not the negotiated price.

### Missing The Modified AGI Two-Year Look

The symptom is a buyer disqualified by a current-year income spike. The trap is that the income limit uses the lower of the purchase year or the prior year modified AGI, so a prior-year lower income can still qualify. The direction is to test both years and use the qualifying one.

### Claiming 25E For A Private-Party Used EV Purchase

The symptom is a used EV credit claimed for a purchase from an individual. The trap is that Section 25E requires a sale by a licensed dealer and a price under the cap. The direction is to confirm the dealer sale and the price and model-year gates before claiming.

### Treating The Seller's Report As Optional

The symptom is a 30D claim without documentation. The trap is that the seller must furnish a report to the buyer and the IRS, and the buyer relies on it; without it the credit is unavailable. The direction is to obtain and verify the seller's report at the time of sale.

### Claiming 30C Without The Census-Tract Eligibility Check

The symptom is a home-charger credit claimed regardless of location. The trap is that the non-business 30C credit requires the property to be in an eligible census tract under IRA rules. The direction is to verify the tract eligibility using the IRS mapping tool before claiming.

### Overlooking The Sourcing Percentage Ramp-Up Over Time

The symptom is assuming a vehicle's qualification is stable. The trap is that the critical minerals and battery components percentages increase year over year, so a vehicle that qualifies now may not later. The direction is to verify the threshold in effect for the placed-in-service year.

- [ ] Have the three credits been mapped to distinct transactions with no double-counting of the same vehicle or expenditure across 30D, 25E, and 30C?
