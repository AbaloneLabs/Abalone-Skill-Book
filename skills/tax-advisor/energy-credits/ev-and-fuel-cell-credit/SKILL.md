# EV And Fuel Cell Credit

The Clean Vehicle Credit under IRC Section 30D is a nonrefundable personal credit of up to 7,500 dollars for the purchase of a new qualified plug-in electric or fuel-cell vehicle. The judgment problem is that the headline 7,500 dollar figure is conditional and fragile. After the Inflation Reduction Act, eligibility depends on a stack of overlapping tests, final assembly in North America, a manufacturer's suggested retail price at or below 55,000 dollars for most vehicles, the buyer's modified adjusted gross income under statutory caps, and two independent battery sourcing requirements that each unlock only half the credit. A vehicle that passes one test can fail another, and the sourcing rules change annually so a car that qualified in one half of the year can lose eligibility the next.

Agents tend to miss that the 7,500 dollar credit is split into two 3,750 dollar halves with separate requirements, one tied to battery component manufacturing or assembly in North America and one tied to critical mineral extraction or processing in a free-trade-agreement country or recycling in North America. They assume a vehicle on a dealer lot automatically qualifies because it is electric. They overlook the MSRP ceiling, which is measured against the manufacturer's suggested retail price and not the negotiated purchase price, and the income limits, which are tested by modified adjusted gross income over the current or prior year. They also conflate the new-vehicle 30D credit with the Used Clean Vehicle Credit under 25E, which has a 4,000 dollar cap, its own price limit, and different rules. The harm is a taxpayer who structures a purchase expecting a 7,500 dollar credit and receives zero, or who buys a used car believing it carries the new-vehicle credit.

This skill covers the U.S. federal Clean Vehicle Credit under IRC 30D for new vehicles and the Used Clean Vehicle Credit under IRC 25E for pre-owned vehicles, as reshaped by the Inflation Reduction Act. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice. These rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, the battery sourcing percentages escalate annually and many vehicles lose eligibility as thresholds tighten, the income and price caps are inflation-adjusted, and phase-downs are built into the statute. A qualified tax professional and the current IRS qualified vehicle list must be consulted before relying on any credit estimate.

## Core Rules

### Confirm North American Final Assembly First

The first gate for the 30D credit is that the vehicle's final assembly must occur in North America, meaning the United States, Canada, or Mexico. This is determined by the vehicle identification number and is published on the IRS qualified vehicle list and the Department of Energy assembly list. A vehicle assembled entirely overseas, even an otherwise compliant electric vehicle, qualifies for zero credit. Confirm assembly location before anything else, because it is a binary pass-or-fail test that cannot be cured by price or income.

For example, a qualifying electric sedan assembled in Tennessee or Ontario passes the assembly test, while an identical model assembled overseas fails it entirely. Some manufacturers produce the same model at multiple plants, so two vehicles of the same make and model can have different eligibility depending on the plant encoded in the VIN. Always check the specific VIN against the current qualified list rather than assuming a model qualifies, and re-check close to the purchase date because the list updates as manufacturers certify vehicles.

### Apply The 55,000 Dollar MSRP Cap Correctly

For most vehicles, the manufacturer's suggested retail price must be at or below 55,000 dollars for the vehicle to qualify. For vans, the cap is 80,000 dollars, and for SUVs and pickup trucks the cap is also 80,000 dollars, reflecting their higher price points. The critical point is that the cap is measured against the MSRP, not the negotiated or dealer price, and not the price including destination charges in the way a buyer might think. The MSRP is the manufacturer's suggested retail price as reported, and a vehicle over the cap is ineligible regardless of how much the buyer negotiated off the sticker.

For example, a luxury electric sedan with an MSRP of 62,000 dollars is over the 55,000 dollar cap and qualifies for zero credit even if the buyer negotiated the price down to 50,000 dollars. An SUV with an MSRP of 79,000 dollars is under the 80,000 dollar SUV cap and passes this test. The IRS publishes which vehicles are classified as SUVs versus sedans for this purpose, and that classification can differ from the manufacturer's marketing label, so verify the classification rather than relying on the body style name. The caps are subject to inflation adjustment, so confirm the figure for the purchase year.

### Test Modified Adjusted Gross Income Against The Annual Limits

The buyer's modified adjusted gross income for the taxable year of purchase, or the preceding year, must be at or below the statutory cap. For most filers, the cap is 300,000 dollars for married filing jointly, 225,000 dollars for heads of household, and 150,000 dollars for other filers, including single filers. The buyer can use the lower of the current-year or prior-year MAGI to satisfy the test, which provides flexibility when income fluctuates, but exceeding the cap in both years disqualifies the purchase entirely.

For example, a married couple with MAGI of 280,000 dollars in the prior year and 310,000 dollars in the current year can use the prior year to qualify, because the test allows either year. A single buyer with MAGI of 160,000 dollars in both years is over the 150,000 dollar cap and gets no credit. MAGI for this purpose generally means adjusted gross income plus certain exclusions, and the buyer must reasonably project current-year MAGI at the time of purchase. Flag buyers near the threshold and model both years before assuming eligibility.

### Split The 7,500 Dollar Credit Into Two 3,750 Dollar Halves

The 7,500 dollar 30D credit is not a single amount. It is two independent 3,750 dollar components. The first 3,750 dollars requires that a minimum percentage of the battery's components be manufactured or assembled in North America, and that percentage escalates annually. The second 3,750 dollars requires that a minimum percentage of the critical minerals in the battery be extracted or processed in the United States or a country with a U.S. free trade agreement, or recycled in North America, and that percentage also escalates annually. A vehicle can qualify for one half, both halves, or neither.

For example, a vehicle may meet the component requirement for the first 3,750 dollars but fail the mineral requirement, yielding a 3,750 dollar credit rather than the full 7,500 dollars. As the sourcing percentages ramp up each year, vehicles that qualified for both halves in an earlier year may qualify for only one half, or neither, in a later year. Never assume a vehicle earns the full 7,500 dollars; check the current IRS qualified vehicle list, which shows each vehicle's eligible amount, and re-verify close to purchase because eligibility shifts mid-year as the percentages increase.

### Treat Sourcing Rules As Annually Changing And Fragile

The battery component and critical mineral percentages are not static. They increase on a statutory schedule, typically stepping up each year, which means the set of qualifying vehicles shrinks over time unless manufacturers restructure their supply chains. A vehicle eligible in January can lose a half-credit or the full credit later in the same year when a percentage threshold rises. This fragility is the single most important behavioral guard for an agent, because a credit estimate given months before purchase can be stale by the time the sale closes.

For example, the critical mineral percentage might step from 40 percent to 50 percent of battery value at a scheduled date, pushing some vehicles below the line. Treat any eligibility determination as time-stamped to the purchase date, and instruct the buyer to confirm the vehicle's eligible amount on the IRS list at the time of sale. Do not carry forward an old eligibility finding, and do not assume a manufacturer's marketing claim of credit eligibility is current. The conservative posture is to re-verify eligibility within days of the purchase and to warn the buyer that the amount can change.

### Distinguish The Used Clean Vehicle Credit Under 25E

The Used Clean Vehicle Credit under IRC Section 25E is a separate nonrefundable credit of up to 4,000 dollars for the purchase of a qualified used plug-in or fuel-cell vehicle from a licensed dealer. The sale price must be at or below 25,000 dollars, the buyer's MAGI must satisfy the same income limits as 30D, the buyer must not be the original owner, and the vehicle must be at least two model years old. The credit is the lesser of 4,000 dollars or 30 percent of the sale price, and it applies only to the first transfer of the used vehicle after August 16, 2022.

For example, a used electric car sold by a licensed dealer for 20,000 dollars to a buyer under the income cap could yield a 4,000 dollar credit, the maximum, because 30 percent of 20,000 dollars is 6,000 dollars but the credit is capped at 4,000 dollars. A private party sale does not qualify because it must be through a licensed dealer who reports the sale. A buyer cannot stack 25E on top of 30D for the same vehicle, and the prior new-vehicle 30D credit generally does not prevent a later used buyer from claiming 25E. Confirm the dealer reporting and the price and age thresholds before claiming 25E.

### Verify The Buyer Is The Original User And The Vehicle Is For Use, Not Resale

The 30D credit requires that the buyer be the original user of the vehicle, that the vehicle be acquired for use and not for resale, and that the vehicle be used predominantly in the United States. A buyer who purchases a new vehicle and immediately resells it, or who claims the credit on a vehicle placed in service by someone else, does not qualify. For leased vehicles, the lessor typically claims the credit, not the lessee, because the leasing company is the purchaser for 30D purposes.

For example, a consumer who buys a new electric vehicle for personal use and is the first to place it in service qualifies, subject to the other tests. A dealer inventory vehicle that was never sold to an end user does not generate a credit for a later buyer under 30D, because the later buyer is not the original user. For leases, the consumer may receive a benefit indirectly through a reduced lease payment, but the credit itself belongs to the lessor. Confirm who is the original user and the intended use before claiming.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Any Electric Vehicle Earns The Full 7,500 Dollars

The symptom is telling a buyer a specific model is worth 7,500 dollars without checking the current IRS qualified list. The trap is that the credit is split into two sourcing-dependent halves and many vehicles qualify for only 3,750 dollars or zero. Always confirm the per-vehicle eligible amount on the current list.

### Ignoring The MSRP Cap And Measuring Against The Negotiated Price

The symptom is claiming the credit for a vehicle the buyer negotiated below 55,000 dollars when the MSRP was over the cap. The trap is that the cap applies to the MSRP, not the deal price, so a discounted over-cap vehicle still fails. Verify the MSRP and the correct vehicle-class cap.

### Missing The MAGI Test In Both The Current And Prior Year

The symptom is a high-earning buyer assuming eligibility because current-year income dipped. The trap is that the test uses the lower of current or prior year, so a buyer over the cap in both years is disqualified even with a one-year dip. Model both years before confirming eligibility.

### Treating Annual Sourcing Changes As Stable

The symptom is relying on an eligibility determination made months before purchase. The trap is that battery component and mineral percentages escalate annually, so a vehicle can lose a half-credit or the full credit mid-cycle. Re-verify eligibility close to the purchase date.

### Conflating The New-Vehicle 30D Credit With The Used-Vehicle 25E Credit

The symptom is applying 7,500 dollar new-vehicle logic to a used car purchase. The trap is that 25E is capped at 4,000 dollars, requires a 25,000 dollar sale price ceiling, a licensed dealer, and a two-model-year-old vehicle. Classify the transaction as new or used before applying the rules.

### Claiming The Credit On A Leased Vehicle Or A Resale Purchase

The symptom is a lessee claiming the 30D credit on a leased electric vehicle. The trap is that the lessor is the purchaser for credit purposes, so the lessee cannot claim it; similarly a buyer who is not the original user cannot claim 30D. Confirm who is the original user and purchaser.

### Relying On Manufacturer Marketing Instead Of The IRS List

The symptom is trusting a dealer advertisement that a vehicle qualifies for the full credit. The trap is that marketing claims are frequently outdated or overstated relative to the IRS qualified list, which is authoritative. Cross-check every claim against the current IRS list.

### Overlooking The 25E Dealer Reporting Requirement

The symptom is claiming the used credit on a private-party sale. The trap is that 25E requires a sale through a licensed dealer who reports the transaction, so a private sale yields no credit. Confirm the seller is a licensed dealer and that the sale was reported.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has North American final assembly been confirmed for the specific vehicle identification number against the current IRS or Department of Energy list before any other test is applied?
- [ ] Has the MSRP been checked against the correct cap, 55,000 dollars for most vehicles and 80,000 dollars for vans, SUVs, and pickup trucks, using MSRP rather than the negotiated price?
- [ ] Has the buyer's modified adjusted gross income been tested against the 300,000, 225,000, or 150,000 dollar limits using the lower of the current or prior year?
- [ ] Has the 7,500 dollar credit been split into its two 3,750 dollar sourcing halves, and has the vehicle's actual eligible amount been confirmed on the current IRS qualified list rather than assumed as the full amount?
- [ ] Has the buyer been warned that battery component and critical mineral percentages escalate annually, so eligibility and the eligible amount can change between the estimate and the purchase date?
- [ ] For a used vehicle, has the transaction been evaluated under 25E with its 4,000 dollar cap, 25,000 dollar price ceiling, two-model-year age, licensed-dealer, and income requirements, rather than under 30D?
- [ ] Has it been confirmed that the buyer is the original user of a new vehicle acquired for use and not resale, and for leases, that the lessor, not the lessee, is the credit claimant?
- [ ] Has the dealer or seller reporting been verified, especially for 25E used-vehicle transactions where a licensed dealer report is required for the credit?
- [ ] Has the credit been treated as nonrefundable against the buyer's tax liability, with awareness that excess 30D does not carry forward and is simply lost?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer been advised that this is general educational guidance and not personalized tax advice, that EV credit and sourcing rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change annually with built-in phase-downs, and that a qualified tax professional and the current IRS qualified vehicle list must be consulted before relying on a credit estimate?
