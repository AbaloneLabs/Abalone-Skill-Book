# Simple Versus Complex Trusts

Within the nongrantor trust category, the Internal Revenue Code divides trusts into simple trusts and complex trusts, and this classification determines how income is taxed, whether a distribution deduction is allowed, and how Form 1041 is prepared. A simple trust must distribute all of its income annually, is prohibited from distributing principal, and makes no charitable contributions, while any trust that fails any of these requirements is a complex trust. The distinction is not cosmetic; it controls the distribution deduction, the timing of beneficiary taxation, and the trust's ability to accumulate income. Agents frequently treat the simple-versus-complex label as a drafting formality when it is actually a tax-classification test driven by the trust instrument's mandatory terms and the trust's actual conduct each year.

The harm from misclassification is reporting-driven and recurring. An agent may treat a trust as simple and distribute all income to beneficiaries annually, when the instrument permits principal distributions or accumulation, making it a complex trust whose income can be trapped at compressed trust tax brackets if not properly distributed. Conversely, an agent may treat a trust as complex and accumulate income, when the instrument mandates annual distribution of all income, creating a simple trust whose failure to distribute still taxes the income to the beneficiaries under the four-tier regime. Because nongrantor trust brackets reach the top 37 percent federal rate at roughly $14,000 of taxable income, misclassifying a trust that accumulates income can impose punitive tax year after year, while misclassifying a simple trust can misallocate tax between the trust and its beneficiaries.

This skill covers the judgment needed to classify a trust as simple or complex, apply the four-tier income distribution regime, compute distributable net income and the distribution deduction, and evaluate the 65-day election. It does not cover grantor trust classification or the detailed taxation of specific distribution types, which warrant separate analysis. Because classification tests, distribution deduction rules, and bracket thresholds are statutory and change with legislation, nothing here is tax advice, and a qualified tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney must be consulted before relying on any conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Three-Part Simple Trust Test

A trust is a simple trust for a given tax year only if it meets all three requirements: it must be required by its terms to distribute all of its income annually, it must not have any provision that permits principal distributions during the year, and it must not make any charitable contributions. If a trust fails any one of these requirements in any year, it is a complex trust for that year. The test is applied annually based on the trust instrument's terms and the trust's actual conduct, not on a one-time classification.

For each trust, read the instrument for mandatory income distribution language, any principal distribution powers, and charitable provisions, and confirm the trust's actual distributions matched the instrument's requirements for the year. A trust that is required to distribute all income but also has the power to distribute principal is complex, even if no principal was distributed. Do not classify a trust as simple based on a single year's distributions; verify all three requirements hold for each tax year, because status can change from year to year.

### Recognize That Complex Trusts Can Accumulate Or Distribute Principal

Any trust that is not a simple trust is a complex trust, which means it may accumulate income, distribute principal, make charitable contributions, or distribute less than all of its income. Complex trusts have flexibility that simple trusts lack, but that flexibility carries the risk of accumulating income at compressed trust tax brackets. The distribution deduction for a complex trust is based on the income actually distributed, subject to the distributable net income limitation.

When a trust is complex, confirm whether income was distributed or accumulated, and compute the distribution deduction based on distributions actually made within the tax year or under the 65-day rule. Evaluate whether accumulation is efficient given the compressed brackets, since a complex trust that accumulates modest income can pay the top federal rate while its beneficiaries may be in much lower brackets. Do not assume a complex trust can defer tax indefinitely; undistributed income is taxed to the trust each year at punitive rates.

### Apply The Four-Tier Income Distribution Regime

When a trust distributes amounts to beneficiaries, the distribution is characterized under a four-tier statutory ordering regime that determines what type of income the beneficiary receives. Tier one is required income distributions to beneficiaries, such as a simple trust's mandatory income distribution. Tier two is other distributions of income, such as discretionary distributions. Within each tier, the income is further ordered as ordinary income, then capital gains, then tax-exempt income, then principal, based on the trust's distributable net income composition.

For each distribution, characterize it under the four-tier regime to determine what the beneficiary reports and what deduction the trust claims, because the ordering affects whether the distribution carries out ordinary income, capital gain, or principal. Confirm that capital gains are generally not included in distributable net income unless allocated to income under the trust's accounting or a state principal and income act, so a distribution funded by capital gains may be treated as principal to the beneficiary. Do not treat every trust distribution as ordinary income to the beneficiary; the four-tier ordering and the capital gain exclusion from DNI can change the character entirely.

### Compute Distributable Net Income As The Distribution Deduction Limit

Distributable net income, or DNI, is the cap on both the amount of income taxed to beneficiaries and the distribution deduction claimed by the trust. DNI is essentially the trust's taxable income adjusted to exclude capital gains allocated to corpus, exclude items not properly allocable to income, and include tax-exempt income net of related expenses. The distribution deduction cannot exceed the lower of the income distributed or DNI, so distributing more than DNI produces no additional deduction and the excess is treated as principal.

For each trust, compute DNI before claiming the distribution deduction, and recognize that the deduction is limited to the income portion of distributions, not principal distributions. Tax-exempt income is included in DNI, so distributions funded by tax-exempt income carry that character to the beneficiary, preserving the exemption at the beneficiary level. Do not claim a distribution deduction for principal distributions or for amounts exceeding DNI; both are non-deductible to the trust and non-taxable to the beneficiary as income.

### Evaluate The 65-Day Election For Complex Trusts

A complex trust, or the final year of a simple trust that becomes complex upon termination, can elect under section 663(b) to treat distributions made within the first 65 days of the following tax year as if made on the last day of the prior year. This election allows the trustee to manage the distribution deduction after the year ends, by seeing the trust's income before deciding how much to distribute. The election is made annually on Form 1041 and is irrevocable once made.

For each complex trust, evaluate whether a 65-day election is beneficial based on the prior year's income and the relative tax brackets of the trust and beneficiaries, and confirm the distribution was actually made within the 65-day window. The election is particularly valuable when the trust accumulated high-bracket income that could be distributed to lower-bracket beneficiaries. Do not assume the election is automatic; it must be affirmatively made on the return, and distributions outside the 65-day window cannot be pulled back into the prior year.

### Confirm Required And Discretionary Distribution Obligations

The trust instrument distinguishes required distributions, which create a fixed obligation that affects simple trust status and tier one characterization, from discretionary distributions, which the trustee may or may not make. Required income distributions to a beneficiary create a tier one distribution regardless of whether the cash is actually paid, if the beneficiary has a right to demand payment. Discretionary distributions are tier two and depend on the trustee's actual exercise of discretion.

Read the instrument to identify which distributions are mandatory and which are discretionary, and confirm that required distributions were actually made or properly accounted for, because an unpaid required distribution can still be taxed to the beneficiary if the beneficiary had the right to compel payment. For discretionary trusts, document the trustee's distribution decisions to support the distribution deduction claimed. Do not treat a discretionary distribution as a required one, or vice versa, because the tier classification and the timing of beneficiary taxation differ.

### Reconcile Simple And Complex Status Year By Year

A trust's simple or complex status is determined annually, so a trust can be simple in one year and complex in another if the instrument's terms or the trust's conduct change. A trust becomes complex permanently in its final year, because the termination involves principal distribution. Status changes affect the distribution deduction, the four-tier characterization, and the beneficiaries' reporting.

For each tax year, reconfirm the simple or complex status based on that year's instrument terms and conduct, and adjust the Form 1041 preparation accordingly. A trust that was simple for years can become complex if the instrument is amended, if principal is distributed, or if a charitable contribution is made. Do not carry forward a prior year's classification without retesting; the annual test can produce a different result based on the year's facts.

## Common Traps

### Classifying A Trust As Simple When It Has Principal Distribution Powers

A trust with any power to distribute principal is complex, even if no principal was distributed in the year. The existence of the power, not its exercise, defeats simple trust status.

### Treating A Complex Trust's Accumulated Income As Deferred

Complex trusts that accumulate income pay tax on it each year at compressed brackets reaching 37 percent at roughly $14,000. Accumulation is not tax deferral; it is high-rate current taxation.

### Claiming A Distribution Deduction For Principal

The distribution deduction is limited to the income portion of distributions, capped by DNI. Principal distributions produce no deduction to the trust and no income to the beneficiary.

### Including Capital Gains In DNI Without Justification

Capital gains are generally excluded from DNI unless allocated to income under the trust's accounting or state principal and income act. Assuming capital gains flow out to beneficiaries can mischaracterize distributions and overstate the distribution deduction.

### Forgetting The 65-Day Election Must Be Affirmatively Made

The section 663(b) election to treat first-65-day distributions as prior-year distributions is made annually on Form 1041 and is irrevocable. Assuming it applies automatically can cause a missed deduction.

### Misapplying The Four-Tier Ordering To Distributions

The four-tier regime orders distributions as required income, other income, then by character within tiers. Treating every distribution as ordinary income ignores the ordering and the capital gain exclusion from DNI.

### Carrying Forward Simple Or Complex Status Without Retesting

Status is determined annually based on instrument terms and conduct. A trust simple in one year can be complex in another, and the final year is always complex.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the trust been tested against all three simple trust requirements for the year, requiring annual distribution of all income, prohibiting principal distributions, and making no charitable contributions?
- [ ] For a complex trust, has the distribution deduction been computed based on income actually distributed, with accumulated income recognized as taxed to the trust at compressed brackets?
- [ ] Has each distribution been characterized under the four-tier regime, distinguishing required income distributions, other income, and the character ordering within tiers?
- [ ] Has distributable net income been computed as the cap on both the distribution deduction and beneficiary taxation, with tax-exempt income included and principal excluded?
- [ ] Has the section 663(b) 65-day election been evaluated and affirmatively made on Form 1041 where beneficial, with distributions confirmed within the 65-day window?
- [ ] Have required distributions been distinguished from discretionary distributions, with unpaid required distributions analyzed for whether the beneficiary had the right to compel payment?
- [ ] Has capital gain treatment been confirmed, recognizing gains are generally excluded from DNI unless allocated to income under the trust's accounting or state law?
- [ ] Has simple or complex status been retested for each tax year rather than carried forward, recognizing status can change and the final year is always complex?
