# Health Insurance Deduction

Health insurance deduction is a high-stakes area where eligibility, timing, and entity interaction determine whether premiums are deductible and at what level. A wrong assumption that all premiums are deductible, or that the deduction is automatic, can produce an overstated deduction, penalties, or a missed benefit. The agent should not treat health insurance costs as a generic expense; the rules differ sharply between self-employed individuals, employees, S corporation shareholder-employees, partners, and those with subsidized marketplace coverage.

Agents tend to miss several invisible risks: the self-employed deduction has a profit-based limitation and an employer-subsidy disqualifier that interact in non-obvious ways; S corporation shareholder-employees must be paid wages to deduct premiums for them; subsidized ACA coverage can wipe out the deduction for months where a premium tax credit is available; and long-term care premiums have age-based caps that are far lower than the actual premium paid. The harm from casual handling includes disallowed deductions, incorrect basis or wage reporting, and missed savings when an available deduction is overlooked.

This skill applies to U.S. federal income tax treatment of health insurance premiums for individuals and pass-through business owners. It is not tax advice. Health insurance rules change annually with inflation-adjusted limits, ACA parameters, and tax law. A qualified tax professional, CPA, or enrolled agent must be consulted before filing or relying on any deduction.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction Conditions

The self-employed health insurance deduction (IRC section 162(l)) lets an eligible self-employed individual deduct premiums for medical, dental, and qualified long-term care insurance covering the taxpayer, spouse, dependents, and children under 27 at year-end. Eligible taxpayers include sole proprietors, partners in a partnership, LLC members taxed as partners, and S corporation shareholders owning more than 2% of the stock.

The deduction is limited to net earned income from the trade or business underlying the coverage, and it is disallowed for any month the taxpayer (or spouse or dependent) was eligible to participate in an employer-subsidized health plan, including a spouse's plan. The deduction is taken above the line on Schedule 1, so it reduces adjusted gross income even for taxpayers who do not itemize. Do not assume eligibility; verify the profit limit, the >2% shareholder test, and the employer-plan disqualifier month by month.

### Handle S Corporation Shareholder-Employee Premiums Correctly

For an S corporation with a more-than-2% shareholder-employee, health insurance premiums paid or reimbursed by the company must be reported as wages on the shareholder's Form W-2 (typically in Box 14), and the shareholder then claims the self-employed health insurance deduction on their personal return. The premium is not deductible by the S corporation as a separate insurance expense in the same way; instead it flows through wages.

A critical rule: the deduction cannot exceed the wages the S corporation pays that shareholder for the year. If wages are zero or below the premium amount, the deduction is limited accordingly. The wages must actually be paid before year-end; a later correction or bonus does not retroactively fix a missed wage reporting item. Confirm the W-2 reporting and that reasonable compensation has been addressed before claiming the deduction.

### Account For ACA Premium Tax Credit Interactions

Taxpayers who buy coverage through the ACA marketplace may receive a Premium Tax Credit (PTC) advance. Any month a taxpayer is eligible for employer-subsidized coverage, or any month they are eligible for (even if not enrolled in) subsidized marketplace coverage, the self-employed deduction is disallowed for that month. The interaction is monthly, not annual.

If the taxpayer receives advance PTC, the actual premium used for the deduction is the portion the taxpayer paid out of pocket after the subsidy, not the full premium. Reconciling Form 1095-A against Form 8962 is required before finalizing the deduction. Failing to reconcile can trigger repayment of the credit and an incorrect deduction. Always request the 1095-A and confirm the months of coverage and subsidy.

### Apply Long-Term Care Premium Age-Based Limits

Qualified long-term care insurance premiums are deductible under the self-employed health insurance deduction, but only up to age-based limits set annually by the IRS. For 2024, the limits (per person) are roughly $470 under age 40, $880 age 40-49, $1,760 age 50-59, $4,710 age 60-69, and $5,880 age 70 or older. These figures are indexed for inflation and must be confirmed for the tax year in question.

The limit applies to the eligible LTC premium only, not to non-qualified policies or to services. If the taxpayer is an employee (not self-employed), LTC premiums fall under the medical expense threshold on Schedule A, which is generally only deductible to the extent total medical expenses exceed 7.5% of adjusted gross income. Do not conflate the two regimes; the self-employed deduction is above the line and uncapped by the AGI floor, but capped by the age limit.

### Distinguish Employee Versus Self-Employed Treatment

Employees who pay premiums personally and are not self-employed generally cannot deduct health insurance premiums except as itemized medical expenses on Schedule A subject to the 7.5% AGI floor. Most employees with employer-sponsored coverage pay premiums pre-tax through a Section 125 cafeteria plan, which already provides the tax benefit and prevents a separate deduction.

The trap is double-counting or misclassifying. A part-time business owner who also has W-2 wages must determine whether they were eligible for an employer plan in any month. A worker misclassified as a contractor who should be an employee cannot claim the self-employed deduction based on the wrong classification. Confirm employment status and plan eligibility before applying either regime.

### Verify Eligible Coverage And Avoid Disqualified Expenses

Only premiums for qualified health coverage count. This includes major medical, dental, vision, and qualified LTC. It does not include Medicare supplements treated as non-qualified, non-insurance health arrangements, sharing ministry payments (not insurance), or out-of-pocket medical costs like copays and prescriptions, which belong in the medical expense bucket.

Medicare premiums (including Part B, Part D, and Medicare Advantage) can qualify for the self-employed deduction for an eligible self-employed individual or their spouse, even though Medicare is generally not deductible as a standalone item for retirees below the AGI floor. Confirm the premium is for qualified coverage and that the payer is the eligible self-employed person or their entity before claiming.

### Document The Deduction And Reconcile Annually

Maintain premium statements, 1095 forms (1095-A/B/C), W-2 entries, reimbursement records, and proof of net self-employment income supporting the profit limit. For S corporation shareholder-employees, retain the W-2 showing the premium reported as wages and the wage amount used as the deduction cap.

Because limits and ACA parameters change annually, recompute the deduction each year rather than rolling forward a prior-year figure. Confirm the long-term care age-based limit against the current IRS inflation adjustment, and confirm the >2% shareholder and employer-plan eligibility facts for each year, since life events (marriage, new job, divorce, Medicare enrollment) change eligibility.

## Common Traps

### Assuming All Health Premiums Are Deductible

Out-of-pocket premiums for a non-self-employed employee are usually not deductible because they fall under the 7.5% AGI floor on Schedule A, which most taxpayers never exceed. Treating personal premiums as automatically deductible overstates the benefit.

### Ignoring The Employer-Subsidy Disqualifier

Eligibility for a spouse's employer plan in any month kills the self-employed deduction for that month, even if the taxpayer never enrolled. Agents often check only the taxpayer's own employment and miss the spouse's coverage.

### Claiming The Deduction Without Wages For A Shareholder-Employee

An S corporation more-than-2% shareholder cannot deduct more premiums than the wages the corporation paid them. Zero wages means a limited or zero deduction, even if the company paid the premium.

### Using The Full Premium When A Subsidy Applied

When advance PTC reduced the billed premium, only the taxpayer-paid portion counts for the self-employed deduction. Using the gross premium double-counts the subsidized portion and conflicts with Form 8962 reconciliation.

### Applying The Medical Expense Floor To The Self-Employed Deduction

The self-employed deduction is above the line and is not subject to the 7.5% AGI floor. Agents sometimes incorrectly route it through Schedule A, losing the benefit or misstating AGI for other computations.

### Exceeding The Long-Term Care Age Limit

The full LTC premium is rarely fully deductible because age-based caps are well below typical premiums for older taxpayers. Deducting the full premium without applying the cap overstates the deduction.

### Treating Non-Insurance Payments As Premiums

Health care sharing ministry contributions, non-qualified supplements, and out-of-pocket medical costs are not insurance premiums and do not qualify. Confirm the policy is qualified insurance before claiming.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has eligibility for the self-employed health insurance deduction been confirmed, including self-employment profit, partner or >2% S shareholder status, and the per-month employer-subsidy disqualifier?
- [ ] For S corporation shareholder-employees, are premiums reported as W-2 wages and is the deduction capped at wages actually paid before year-end?
- [ ] Has any ACA marketplace coverage been reconciled using Form 1095-A and Form 8962, and is only the out-of-pocket premium used after subsidy?
- [ ] Are qualified long-term care premiums capped at the correct age-based IRS limit for the tax year?
- [ ] Has the correct regime been applied (above-the-line self-employed deduction versus Schedule A medical expenses subject to the 7.5% AGI floor)?
- [ ] Are only qualified insurance premiums included, excluding sharing ministry payments, non-qualified supplements, and out-of-pocket medical costs?
- [ ] Is net earned income from the underlying trade or business sufficient to support the deduction amount claimed?
- [ ] Are supporting documents retained, including 1095 forms, premium statements, W-2 entries, and reimbursement records?
- [ ] Has the deduction been recomputed for the current tax year using current limits rather than prior-year figures?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer been advised that this is general guidance and not tax advice, and that a qualified tax professional, CPA, or enrolled agent must be consulted before filing?
