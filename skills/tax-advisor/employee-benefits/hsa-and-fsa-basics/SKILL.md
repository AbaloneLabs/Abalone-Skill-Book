# HSA And FSA Basics

Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) and Flexible Spending Arrangements (FSAs) are tax-favored accounts that can deliver powerful triple-tax benefits (HSAs) or pre-tax savings (FSAs), but they are governed by strict eligibility, contribution, and timing rules that agents routinely mishandle. A single disqualifying coverage, an over-contribution, or a missed deadline can convert a benefit into taxable income plus penalties. The agent should not treat these accounts as interchangeable savings vehicles; the rules differ materially and the cost of casual handling is high.

Agents frequently miss that HSA eligibility requires enrollment in a qualifying High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) and no other disqualifying coverage (including a general-purpose FSA, Medicare, or a spouse's non-HDHP), that contribution limits are indexed annually and include a catch-up for age 55+, and that the last-month rule holds an HSA contributor to HDHP coverage through December 1 or triggers testing and income inclusion. FSAs are generally use-or-lose with a carryover or grace period option, are employer-owned, and a general-purpose FSA can disqualify HSA eligibility for the account holder and sometimes a spouse. The harm includes excise taxes on excess HSA contributions, forfeited FSA balances, and lost tax savings.

This skill covers U.S. federal tax rules for HSAs and FSAs. Contribution limits, HDHP thresholds, and penalty amounts are indexed annually and must be verified for the specific tax year. This is not tax advice. A qualified tax professional, CPA, or benefits specialist must be consulted before relying on any contribution or distribution decision.

## Core Rules

### Verify HSA Eligibility From Coverage, Not Intent

HSA eligibility for a given month requires three conditions on the first day of that month: coverage under an HDHP, no other disqualifying first-dollar coverage, and no enrollment in Medicare (Part A counts once enrolled, even if retroactive). Disqualifying coverage includes a general-purpose health FSA, an HRA that pays medical costs first, TRICARE, and being claimed as a dependent on another's return.

The most common invisible failure is a general-purpose FSA. An employee enrolled in a general-purpose FSA cannot contribute to an HSA for the period the FSA can pay medical expenses, even if the FSA balance is zero. A spouse's general-purpose FSA can also disqualify the other spouse's HSA eligibility if it can cover the other spouse's expenses. Confirm coverage month by month, including a spouse's plans, before recommending any HSA contribution.

### Apply The Correct Annual Contribution Limits And Catch-Ups

HSA contribution limits are indexed annually. For 2024, the limit for self-only HDHP coverage is $4,150 and for family HDHP coverage is $8,300, with an additional $1,000 catch-up for individuals age 55 or older. For 2025, limits rise to $4,300 self-only and $8,550 family. These figures include both employer and employee contributions combined.

The family HDHP limit is a single pool shared by all HSA-eligible family members covered under the family plan; it is not per person. A special rule allows a full year's contribution for someone eligible on the first day of the last month (December 1), but the last-month rule requires maintaining HDHP coverage through the end of the following tax year or the excess is included in income and subject to tax. Confirm the correct limit, the catch-up age test, and whether the last-month rule applies before computing contributions.

### Understand FSA Types, Use-Or-Lose, And Carryover

FSAs are employer-established and employer-owned. The most common is the health FSA, funded by employee salary reduction under a Section 125 cafeteria plan, with a 2024 contribution limit of $3,200 (rising to $3,300 for 2025). Funds are generally use-or-lose at year-end unless the employer's plan offers either a grace period (up to 2.5 additional months) or a carryover (a set dollar amount, up to $640 for 2024 and $660 for 2025, indexed).

A limited-purpose FSA (covering only dental and vision, or post-deductible expenses) does not disqualify HSA eligibility, but a general-purpose health FSA does. Dependent care FSAs are a separate account with a separate limit ($5,000 household in most cases) and cannot be used for medical expenses. Do not assume any FSA works like an HSA; FSAs cannot be invested, are not individually owned, and generally do not transfer between employers.

### Distinguish Qualified Medical Expenses And Timing

Both HSAs and health FSAs pay for qualified medical expenses under IRC section 213(d), which includes diagnosis, treatment, prevention, prescriptions, dental, vision, and many over-the-counter items. Non-qualified withdrawals (HSA) or ineligible claims (FSA) are taxable and, for HSAs taken before age 65, subject to a 20% additional tax.

A key timing rule: HSA distributions must be for expenses incurred after the HSA was established. An HSA opened late in the year cannot reimburse a January surgery. FSAs can reimburse expenses incurred during the plan year (and grace period) regardless of when funds were deposited, because the full annual election is available on day one. Track the HSA establishment date and the FSA plan year separately when evaluating reimbursements.

### Manage Excess Contributions And Corrections

Excess HSA contributions (over the limit or made while ineligible) incur a 6% excise tax each year they remain in the account. Correction requires withdrawing the excess plus earnings before the due date of the return (including extensions) to avoid the excise tax; the earnings are then taxable as other income. Failing to correct carries the 6% forward annually.

Excess can also arise from employer contributions or a mistaken last-month-rule contribution. If HDHP coverage lapses after a December 1 contribution, the testing period is violated and the prorated excess becomes income plus a 10% additional tax (instead of the usual 20%, because it is a testing-period failure). Identify excess early and correct within the deadline to minimize tax and penalty.

### Coordinate HSA And FSA With Each Other And With Spouses

A taxpayer cannot have both an HSA and a general-purpose health FSA covering the same period. The safe pairing is an HSA plus a limited-purpose FSA. Spouses must coordinate: if one spouse has family HDHP coverage and a general-purpose FSA, the other spouse may be disqualified from HSA contributions. Two HSA-eligible spouses under a family HDHP split the single family contribution limit and may allocate it between their separate HSAs in any proportion.

Also coordinate with Medicare: enrolling in any part of Medicare (including premium-free Part A) ends HSA eligibility from the first month enrolled, and retroactive Part A can create excess contributions for prior months. Anyone approaching age 65 or considering Medicare must time HSA contributions and Medicare enrollment carefully, since stopping Social Security to avoid Part A has its own consequences.

### Document Contributions, Distributions, And Eligibility

Retain Form 8889 (HSA) and Form 1040 entries, Form 5498-SA (contributions), Form 1099-SA (distributions), HDHP coverage confirmation, and receipts for qualified medical expenses. For FSAs, retain the plan document references, salary reduction elections, claim records, and carryover or grace-period confirmations.

Because eligibility can change mid-year (new job, marriage, Medicare, new FSA), recheck HSA eligibility at each life event and at year-end. An HSA contribution made when ineligible is excess even if it seemed fine at the time. Annual review prevents compounding excise taxes and forfeited benefits.

## Common Traps

### Contributing To An HSA While Covered By A General-Purpose FSA

A general-purpose health FSA disqualifies HSA eligibility for the entire period it can pay expenses, even with a zero balance. This is the single most common HSA eligibility error and creates excess contributions subject to the 6% excise tax.

### Using The Per-Person Mindset For The Family HSA Limit

The family HDHP contribution limit is a shared pool, not a per-person allowance. Treating it as per spouse or per child over-contributes and triggers the excise tax.

### Ignoring The Last-Month Rule Testing Period

Contributing a full year's amount based on December 1 eligibility locks the taxpayer into HDHP coverage through the next December 31. Lapsing coverage breaks the testing period and converts the excess to income plus a 10% additional tax.

### Forgetting Medicare Ends HSA Eligibility

Enrolling in Medicare Part A (even premium-free) stops HSA eligibility from the first month of coverage, and retroactive Part A can disqualify prior months. Delaying Medicare to keep contributing has Social Security implications that must be weighed.

### Assuming FSAs Work Like HSAs

FSAs are employer-owned, use-or-lose, non-investable, and non-portable. They cannot be rolled to a new employer or invested for growth. Treating an FSA balance as portable savings leads to forfeitures.

### Overlooking FSA Carryover And Grace Period Limits

Carryover and grace periods are optional employer plan features with indexed dollar caps. Assuming a full balance carries over, or assuming no carryover exists, both produce wrong planning. Confirm the specific plan's terms.

### Spending HSA Funds On Non-Qualified Expenses Before Age 65

Non-qualified HSA distributions before age 65 are taxable plus a 20% additional tax. After 65 they are taxable but penalty-free, which is why HSAs are sometimes used as retirement savings, but premature non-medical withdrawals are costly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has HSA eligibility been confirmed for each month, including HDHP coverage and absence of disqualifying coverage such as a general-purpose FSA, HRA, Medicare, or TRICARE?
- [ ] Has a spouse's general-purpose FSA or non-HDHP coverage been checked for its effect on the taxpayer's HSA eligibility?
- [ ] Is the HSA contribution within the correct annual limit for self-only or family HDHP coverage, including the age 55+ catch-up, and are employer contributions counted toward the limit?
- [ ] If the last-month rule was used, has the testing period requirement (HDHP coverage through the next December 31) been flagged?
- [ ] Has the FSA type (general-purpose versus limited-purpose) been confirmed, along with use-or-lose, grace period, or carryover terms and the indexed dollar caps?
- [ ] Are distributions limited to qualified medical expenses incurred after the HSA was established (or within the FSA plan year and grace period)?
- [ ] Have any excess HSA contributions been identified and corrected (withdrawn plus earnings) before the return due date to avoid the 6% excise tax?
- [ ] Has Medicare enrollment timing been coordinated with HSA contributions, including the effect of retroactive Part A?
- [ ] Are Forms 8889, 5498-SA, 1099-SA, HDHP coverage confirmation, and expense receipts retained?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer been advised that this is general guidance and not tax advice, and that a qualified tax professional, CPA, or benefits specialist must be consulted before relying on any contribution or distribution decision?
