# Estate Tax Basics

Estate tax is a transfer tax imposed on the right to transfer property at death, computed on the value of the decedent's taxable estate rather than on what each beneficiary receives. Agents frequently confuse the estate tax (an estate-level tax on the decedent's assets) with inheritance tax (a beneficiary-level tax imposed by some states), and with income tax rules that apply to beneficiaries. The gross estate for federal estate tax purposes is defined under Internal Revenue Code section 2031 and is broader than probate assets; it can include life insurance proceeds, retirement accounts, jointly held property, certain lifetime transfers, and powers of appointment. Collapsing "estate" into "probate estate" produces materially wrong conclusions about whether a federal Form 706 is due.

The harm from casual estate-tax reasoning is significant. An agent may tell a family that "no estate tax applies" because assets are under a headline exemption number, while ignoring jointly owned property with right of survivorship, life insurance owned by the decedent, retirement benefits, payable-on-death accounts, and state-level estate or inheritance taxes that kick in at far lower thresholds than the federal system. Conversely, an agent may overstate liability by double-counting assets already subject to marital or charitable deduction, or by failing to recognize that a surviving spouse's share may qualify for the unlimited marital deduction. Deadlines are unforgiving: Form 706 is generally due nine months after death, with an automatic six-month extension available on Form 4768, but any tax due must still be paid by the original nine-month date to avoid interest.

This skill covers the foundational judgment needed to evaluate whether a federal estate tax filing is required, what counts in the gross estate, how deductions and credits reduce the taxable estate, and where state-level taxes diverge from federal rules. It does not cover advanced techniques such as portability elections, generation-skipping transfer tax, qualified personal residence trusts, or valuation discounts, which warrant separate analysis. Because estate tax law is jurisdiction-specific, time-sensitive, and carries personal liability for executors, nothing here is tax advice, and a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney must be consulted before filing or relying on any conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Gross Estate From Probate Estate

The gross estate for federal estate tax is defined by statute and includes assets the decedent owned or controlled at death, regardless of whether they pass through probate. Probate assets are only those that pass under a will or by intestacy; the gross estate is broader and is the figure that drives the Form 706 calculation.

Assets commonly included in the gross estate include solely owned real and personal property, the decedent's interest in jointly held property (often the full value if the joint tenant was a spouse, or a fractional value otherwise), life insurance proceeds on the decedent's life if the decedent owned the policy or held incidents of ownership, retirement account balances, bank and brokerage accounts titled in the decedent's name, certain interests in trusts created by the decedent, and property transferred within three years of death. Do not assume that non-probate titling removes assets from the gross estate; it often only changes how they pass, not whether they are taxed.

### Apply The Federal Filing Threshold Correctly

A federal Form 706 is generally required only when the gross estate exceeds the applicable exclusion amount in the year of death, or when the executor elects portability for a surviving spouse regardless of estate size. The applicable exclusion amount is adjusted periodically and was significantly expanded by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, with the elevated amount scheduled to sunset after 2025 absent further legislation, which means the threshold can change between the planning stage and the actual death date.

Before concluding that no filing is required, sum all gross estate assets using death-date fair market value, add adjusted taxable gifts made during life (which consume lifetime exemption), and check whether any prior deceased-spouse unused exclusion (DSUE) amount is available. Even when no tax is owed, an estate may need to file Form 706 to elect portability, to establish a stepped-up basis for beneficiaries, or to satisfy state filing rules. Never rely on a single exemption number without confirming the year of death and whether portability or prior gifts change the calculation.

### Map Deductions Before Computing Taxable Estate

The taxable estate is the gross estate minus allowable deductions, and missing deductions inflates the apparent tax. The most important deductions are the marital deduction for property passing to a surviving spouse who is a U.S. citizen, the charitable deduction for property passing to qualified charities, and administrative expenses, debts, funeral costs, and casualty losses.

For the marital deduction, confirm that the recipient is a surviving U.S. citizen spouse and that the property actually passes to that spouse; property in certain trusts may require a qualified domestic trust (QDOT) if the surviving spouse is not a U.S. citizen. For the charitable deduction, verify the recipient qualifies and document the bequest. For expenses and debts, distinguish estate administration costs (deductible on the estate tax return or, alternatively, on the estate's income tax return, but not both) from income tax deductions. Choosing where to deduct is a timing and rate decision that should not be made casually.

### Account For Adjusted Taxable Gifts

Lifetime gifts that exceeded the annual exclusion and used lifetime exemption are added back to the taxable estate on Form 706 to compute the tentative tax, even though they are not themselves taxed again. This is one of the most commonly misunderstood mechanics: gifts do not escape the estate tax system, they simply prepay the exemption.

Confirm whether the decedent made gifts requiring Form 709 during life, obtain prior gift tax returns, and ensure the adjusted taxable gifts figure is accurate. The estate tax is computed on the sum of the taxable estate plus adjusted taxable gifts, then reduced by the unified credit and any prior gift tax paid. Failing to add back lifetime gifts understates the tax base; failing to credit prior gift tax paid overstates the tax owed.

### Evaluate State Estate And Inheritance Tax Separately

Federal conclusions do not control state outcomes. Several states impose an estate tax on the estate, with exemption thresholds far below the federal amount, and a smaller number impose an inheritance tax on beneficiaries based on their relationship to the decedent. Some states have both, and some have neither.

For each state where the decedent was domiciled and where real property is located, determine whether an estate or inheritance tax return is required, the applicable exemption, the filing deadline (which can differ from the federal nine-month rule), and whether the state allows a credit or deduction for federal tax paid. State rules often tax life insurance differently, treat retirement accounts differently, and use different definitions of residency and domicile. A federal "no tax" conclusion does not eliminate state obligations.

### Value Assets At Death-Date Fair Market Value

Estate tax is computed on the fair market value of assets as of the date of death, and the executor may elect the alternate valuation date (generally six months after death) if it reduces both the gross estate and the estate tax. Valuation errors are a primary source of audit adjustments and penalties.

For closely held business interests, real estate, collectibles, and non-public securities, obtain qualified appraisals and consider whether valuation discounts for lack of marketability or minority interest are defensible. For marketable securities, use the date-of-death pricing conventions specified by the regulations. Document the valuation method for every asset, because the same values generally establish basis for beneficiaries, and inconsistencies between the estate tax return and later income tax positions invite scrutiny.

### Respect Filing Deadlines And Payment Rules

Form 706 is generally due nine months after the date of death. An automatic six-month extension of time to file is available on Form 4768, but it extends only filing, not payment; tax still owed by the original due date accrues interest, and late payment can trigger penalties. Some estates can defer payment under section 6166 when a closely held business interest is a substantial part of the estate.

Track the nine-month filing deadline, the extension request, the payment deadline, and any deferral election windows separately. If the estate includes illiquid business assets, evaluate section 6166 installment deferral early, because it has strict eligibility and interest requirements and must be elected on a timely filed return. Do not assume that because no tax appears due on a quick estimate, the filing deadline can be ignored; portability and basis considerations may still require a return.

## Common Traps

### Equating Probate Estate With Gross Estate

Probate assets are only a subset of the gross estate. Life insurance, retirement accounts, payable-on-death accounts, jointly held property, and certain trust interests are outside probate but inside the gross estate. Using the probate inventory as the filing-threshold test understates the estate and can cause a missed Form 706.

### Assuming The Headline Exemption Applies Automatically

The federal applicable exclusion amount is large and indexed, but it is consumed by lifetime gifts, affected by portability from a prior spouse, and subject to legislative sunset. Quoting one number without confirming the death year, prior gift usage, and prior spouse DSUE produces a wrong filing conclusion.

### Overlooking State Estate Or Inheritance Tax

Many states have exemption thresholds far below the federal level, and inheritance taxes are imposed on beneficiaries by relationship class. A federal no-filing conclusion says nothing about state obligations, and deadlines and definitions differ by jurisdiction.

### Double-Counting Marital Or Charitable Assets

Property qualifying for the marital or charitable deduction is removed from the taxable estate. Taxing the same assets or failing to claim the deduction distorts the result. The trap is especially common with jointly held spousal property and charitable bequests that are ambiguous in the will.

### Forgetting That Lifetime Gifts Are Added Back

Lifetime gifts that used exemption are added to the taxable estate to compute tentative tax. Agents sometimes treat large lifetime transfers as fully outside the estate tax system, which understates the tax base and omits the unified credit reconciliation.

### Misvaluing Hard-To-Value Assets

Closely held business interests, real estate, and collectibles require qualified appraisals, and unsupported discounts invite audit adjustments and penalties. Using book value, assessed value, or an informal estimate is a trap because it rarely reflects fair market value and may also corrupt beneficiary basis.

### Missing The Nine-Month Deadline Or Confusing Extension With Payment

The Form 706 nine-month deadline is strict, and the Form 4768 extension extends only filing. Treating the extension as a payment extension causes interest and penalties. Portability and basis elections also generally require a timely filed return.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the gross estate been assembled from all includable assets, including life insurance, retirement accounts, jointly held property, payable-on-death accounts, and three-year look-back transfers, rather than from probate assets alone?
- [ ] Has the federal filing threshold been confirmed for the specific year of death, with adjusted taxable gifts and any prior deceased-spouse unused exclusion accounted for?
- [ ] Have marital, charitable, debt, funeral, and administrative deductions been identified and documented, with the election between estate tax and income tax deduction for expenses made deliberately?
- [ ] Have adjusted taxable gifts from prior Form 709 filings been added back to compute the tentative tax, with the unified credit and prior gift tax paid reconciled?
- [ ] Has the estate or inheritance tax exposure been evaluated separately for every state of domicile and every state where real property is located, using each state's own threshold and deadline?
- [ ] Have hard-to-value assets been supported by qualified appraisals, and has the alternate valuation date election been considered only where it reduces both gross estate and tax?
- [ ] Have the nine-month Form 706 filing deadline, the Form 4768 extension, the payment deadline, and any section 6166 deferral election been tracked as distinct obligations?
- [ ] Has portability and beneficiary stepped-up basis been considered as reasons to file even when no federal tax appears due?
- [ ] Are uncertain valuation, deduction, and jurisdictional questions escalated to a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney rather than resolved from general assumptions, since this guidance is not tax advice?
