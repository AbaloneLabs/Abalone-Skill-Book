# Estate Tax Exemption

The federal estate and gift tax system uses a single unified credit, expressed as an applicable exclusion amount, that shelters transfers both during life and at death from transfer tax. The same lifetime exemption is consumed by taxable gifts above the annual exclusion and by the taxable estate, so every dollar of exemption used during life reduces what is available at death. Agents frequently treat the exemption as a fixed, evergreen number and ignore three dynamics that determine the real available shelter: annual indexing and legislative sunset, prior lifetime gift usage, and any deceased-spouse unused exclusion (DSUE) inherited from a predeceased spouse.

The harm from misjudging the exemption is concrete. An agent may tell a client there is "plenty of room" to make a large gift based on a headline number, while the client has already consumed exemption on prior Form 709 gifts, or may inherit DSUE that materially expands the available shelter if a portability election was made on the first spouse's estate. Conversely, an agent may underestimate exposure by ignoring the scheduled sunset of the elevated exemption amounts, which could reduce the available exclusion by roughly half unless Congress acts. Because the exemption is measured against the fair market value of transferred assets and adjusted taxable gifts, valuation and prior-return accuracy directly determine how much shelter remains.

This skill covers the judgment needed to determine the available applicable exclusion amount for a given decedent or donor, how lifetime gifts consume it, how the sunset and indexing change it, and how DSUE from a prior spouse interacts with it. It does not cover the mechanics of electing portability or structuring trusts to use exemption, which warrant separate analysis. Because exemption amounts, sunset dates, and DSUE rules are statutory and change with legislation, nothing here is tax advice, and a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney must be consulted before relying on any figure or planning conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Exemption As Unified And Consumable

The federal transfer tax system is unified: one applicable exclusion amount applies across lifetime gifts and the estate at death. Taxable gifts made during life, meaning gifts above the annual exclusion and not otherwise excluded, reduce the exemption available at death dollar-for-dollar. This is not a separate gift allowance and a separate estate allowance.

To determine remaining exemption, start with the applicable exclusion amount for the relevant year, subtract adjusted taxable gifts reported on prior Form 709 returns, and account for any DSUE. Always request and review prior gift tax returns before stating how much exemption remains, because clients routinely forget large prior gifts to trusts, family entities, or family members. Do not describe the exemption as "the amount you can give tax-free each year," which conflates it with the annual exclusion.

### Use The Correct Year-Specific Amount And Track Indexing

The applicable exclusion amount is indexed for inflation and has changed materially over time, including a temporary doubling under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act that is scheduled to sunset after 2025 absent further legislation. The relevant amount for an estate is generally the amount in effect for the year of death, not the year of planning.

Confirm the exact figure for the specific year before computing available exemption, and flag the sunset risk explicitly when planning before the scheduled reduction. The post-sunset amount is projected to be roughly half of the elevated amount, so lifetime gifts made to lock in the higher exemption before sunset may be advantageous but carry their own risks (loss of basis step-up, gift tax reporting, and irrevocability). Never quote an exemption number without stating the year and noting whether it is pre- or post-sunset.

### Add Deceased-Spouse Unused Exclusion When Available

A surviving spouse may inherit the unused exemption of a predeceased spouse if the executor of the first spouse's estate elected portability on a timely filed Form 706. This DSUE increases the surviving spouse's available exclusion, sometimes substantially, and is portable only if the election was properly made.

Confirm whether Form 706 was filed for the first spouse, whether the portability election was made, and the amount of DSUE reported. DSUE is specific to the most recent deceased spouse; it is not cumulative across multiple marriages without specific rules. Do not assume portability was elected automatically; it requires an affirmative election on a timely filed Form 706, and late relief may or may not be available depending on the facts and the procedure in effect.

### Reconcile Against Prior Gift Tax Returns

The available exemption is only as accurate as the prior gift tax reporting. Adjusted taxable gifts from all prior years must be subtracted from the applicable exclusion amount to find the remaining shelter, and the unified credit for prior gift tax paid must be reconciled on the estate tax return.

Obtain every Form 709 the decedent or donor filed, confirm the cumulative adjusted taxable gifts figure, and verify that gifts were properly split, valued, and allocated. A common error is treating gifts to trusts or family entities as annual-exclusion gifts when they exceeded the exclusion or lacked a present interest, which means they silently consumed exemption. If prior returns are missing or appear inaccurate, the remaining-exemption calculation is unreliable until corrected.

### Distinguish Exemption From The Tax Rate And Unified Credit

The applicable exclusion amount is expressed as a dollar value of transfers sheltered, while the unified credit is the dollar tax credit that implements it. The estate tax rate above the exemption is a flat percentage under current law, applied to the taxable estate plus adjusted taxable gifts, reduced by the unified credit and prior gift tax paid.

Do not conflate the exemption with the marginal rate or with the tax actually owed. A large estate above the exemption can owe substantial tax even when the exemption is fully used, because the rate applies to the excess. When modeling outcomes, compute tentative tax on the full base, then subtract the unified credit and any DSUE effect, rather than simply subtracting the exemption from the estate value and applying the rate.

### Consider The Sunset As A Planning Risk, Not A Certainty

The elevated exemption amounts are scheduled to revert to a lower baseline after 2025 unless Congress extends them. This creates a timing risk: gifts made before sunset to use the higher exemption are generally protected if the exemption later drops, but the rules protecting pre-sunset gifts have anti-abuse conditions.

When advising on large gifts intended to lock in exemption, document the value, the exemption used, and the filing, because the protection of pre-sunset gifts depends on a completed gift before the reduction. Weigh the benefit of using higher exemption against the loss of basis step-up at death, the irrevocability of the gift, and the risk that the client dies sooner than expected. Do not present sunset as a reason to gift blindly; it is a factor in a larger tradeoff analysis.

### Account For State Exemption Divergence

State estate tax exemptions are set independently and are frequently far lower than the federal amount, with different thresholds, rates, and definitions. Some states also have a state gift tax or clawback rules that recapture exemption used for out-of-state assets.

For any planning conclusion, check the state of domicile and any state where real property is located, and apply that state's exemption separately. A transfer that is fully sheltered federally may still trigger state estate tax because the state exemption is lower, and the state computation may not allow DSUE. Never let a federal "fully sheltered" conclusion imply the same at the state level.

## Common Traps

### Quoting A Single Exemption Number Without A Year Or Sunset Caveat

The applicable exclusion amount changes annually and is subject to legislative sunset. Quoting one figure as if it is permanent misleads clients about available shelter and ignores the post-2025 reduction risk.

### Forgetting Prior Lifetime Gifts Consume The Same Pool

Lifetime taxable gifts and the estate share one unified exemption. Agents sometimes describe the exemption as a fresh annual or lifetime gift allowance, ignoring prior Form 709 usage that has already reduced the available shelter.

### Assuming Portability Was Automatically Elected

DSUE is available only if the first spouse's executor elected portability on a timely filed Form 706. Assuming the election exists without verifying the filed return can cause a surviving spouse to overstate available exemption.

### Conflating Exemption With Tax Owed

The exemption is the value of transfers sheltered, not the tax. Estates above the exemption still owe tax at the statutory rate on the excess, and the unified credit must be reconciled with prior gift tax paid.

### Ignoring State Exemption Gaps

State exemptions are often much lower than federal and may not allow DSUE. A federal no-tax conclusion does not prevent state estate tax, and the state thresholds can be a small fraction of the federal amount.

### Treating The Sunset As A Reason To Gift Without Tradeoff Analysis

Using elevated exemption before sunset can be beneficial, but gifts are irrevocable, forfeit basis step-up, and carry reporting obligations. Treating sunset as an automatic trigger to gift ignores the cost side of the decision.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the applicable exclusion amount been confirmed for the specific year of death or gift, with the sunset risk flagged for any pre-2026 planning?
- [ ] Has the remaining exemption been computed by subtracting adjusted taxable gifts from all prior Form 709 returns from the year-specific applicable exclusion amount?
- [ ] Has any deceased-spouse unused exclusion been verified by checking whether a timely Form 706 with a portability election was filed for the predeceased spouse?
- [ ] Has the unified credit been distinguished from the exemption value, with tentative tax computed on the full base before applying credits?
- [ ] Have prior gift tax returns been obtained and reviewed for accuracy of gift splitting, present-interest treatment, and valuation, since the remaining-exemption figure depends on them?
- [ ] Has the state estate tax exemption been checked separately for the state of domicile and states with real property, recognizing it may be far lower than federal and may not allow DSUE?
- [ ] Has the sunset been treated as a planning risk requiring tradeoff analysis, not as an automatic reason to make irrevocable gifts?
- [ ] Has the agent avoided describing the exemption as a recurring annual or lifetime gift allowance separate from the estate?
- [ ] Are uncertain exemption, DSUE, sunset, and state-law questions escalated to a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney rather than answered from general assumptions, since this guidance is not tax advice?
