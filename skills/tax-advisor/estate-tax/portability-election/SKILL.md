# Portability Election

Portability allows a surviving spouse to use the unused federal estate and gift tax exemption of a predeceased spouse, provided the executor of the first spouse's estate makes an affirmative election on a timely filed Form 706. The amount transferred is the deceased-spouse unused exclusion (DSUE), calculated as the predeceased spouse's applicable exclusion amount minus the value of taxable gifts and the taxable estate reported on that return. Portability is not automatic; it is an election, and without it the surviving spouse loses access to the first spouse's unused exemption regardless of how large it was.

The harm from mishandling portability is permanent and quantifiable. Executors of small estates routinely skip filing Form 706 because no tax is owed, not realizing that portability, and therefore potentially millions of dollars of additional exemption, is forfeited unless a return is filed. Conversely, agents may assume portability exists without verifying the election, or may mishandle the rules when a surviving spouse remarries, because DSUE from an earlier spouse is generally displaced by DSUE from a later deceased spouse. The deadline is also unforgiving: the election must be made on a Form 706 filed within the time prescribed, including extensions, and while late relief procedures exist, they have eligibility conditions and are not guaranteed.

This skill covers the judgment needed to decide whether to elect portability, how DSUE is computed and used, the remarriage and time-limitation rules, and the late-relief landscape. It does not cover credit shelter or bypass trust planning as an alternative, valuation of estate assets, or generation-skipping tax, which warrant separate analysis. Because portability elections, DSUE computations, and late-relief procedures are statutory and procedural, nothing here is tax advice, and a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney must be consulted before filing or relying on any conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Portability Requires A Form 706 Election

Portability is elected by the executor of the predeceased spouse's estate by filing a complete and properly prepared Form 706 on which the election is made. It is not triggered by the marriage, by a will provision, or by the absence of tax. Many small estates have no federal filing obligation apart from the portability election, so the only reason to file is to preserve DSUE.

Determine early whether the first spouse's estate has unused exemption worth preserving. If the predeceased spouse's assets plus adjusted taxable gifts are below their applicable exclusion amount, DSUE likely exists, and filing Form 706 to elect portability should be evaluated even when no tax is due. Do not assume that because the estate is small, no return is needed; for portability, the return is the election.

### Compute DSUE From The First Spouse's Return

DSUE equals the predeceased spouse's basic exclusion amount plus any DSUE they themselves received from an earlier spouse, minus the sum of the taxable estate and adjusted taxable gifts. The figure is reported on the first spouse's Form 706 and becomes the amount the surviving spouse may use.

Confirm the basic exclusion amount for the year of the first spouse's death, verify the taxable estate and adjusted taxable gifts figures, and document the DSUE calculation on the return. Errors in valuing estate assets or in reporting prior gifts directly reduce or distort DSUE. The surviving spouse can only use what was correctly computed and reported, so the quality of the first return governs the value of portability.

### File Within The Prescribed Time Including Extensions

The portability election must be made on a Form 706 filed within the time prescribed by section 6075, generally nine months after death, plus any timely granted extension on Form 4768. The election is made by checking the appropriate box and filing a complete return; a partial or incomplete return may not constitute a valid election.

Track the nine-month deadline and any extension as a hard constraint on portability, separate from the payment deadline. Even when no tax is owed, missing the filing window forfeits portability unless late relief applies. Calendar the deadline at the start of estate administration, because executors often deprioritize Form 706 when no tax appears due, not realizing the election is at stake.

### Apply The Remarriage And Successive-Spouse Rules

A surviving spouse who has inherited DSUE and later remarries can continue to use the first spouse's DSUE until the new spouse dies. When the new spouse dies, the surviving spouse's available DSUE generally becomes the DSUE from the most recent deceased spouse, displacing the earlier DSUE under the "last deceased spouse" rule.

When advising a surviving spouse who has remarried, identify which spouse's DSUE is currently available and how a future death of the current spouse would change it. Do not assume DSUE accumulates across spouses; the rule is generally last-in-time, and earlier DSUE can be lost on remarriage followed by the new spouse's death. Document the chain of marriages, deaths, and portability elections to avoid relying on displaced DSUE.

### Respect The Time Limitation On Using DSUE

Under current law, DSUE is available to the surviving spouse without a fixed expiration during the surviving spouse's life, but it can be displaced by a later deceased spouse and is lost at the surviving spouse's own death if unused. Some prior guidance referenced a five-year limitation that has since been removed, so the controlling rule is the current statute and regulations.

When advising a surviving spouse, confirm the current limitation rules rather than relying on outdated five-year assumptions. The practical constraint is not a clock but the remarriage and successive-spouse displacement rule, plus the fact that DSUE cannot be used after the surviving spouse's own death. Plan DSUE use accordingly, prioritizing gifts or estate planning that captures the inherited exemption before it is displaced or lost.

### Evaluate Late Portability Relief Realistically

Revenue Procedure 2017-34 and successor guidance provide a simplified procedure for late portability elections, generally allowing an executor to elect portability by filing Form 706 within a set window (commonly five years) after the due date, provided the estate is otherwise eligible and the filing is a complete return. This relief is not automatic and has conditions.

When the original deadline was missed, evaluate whether the estate qualifies for late relief under the current procedure, confirm the relief window, and ensure the late-filed return is complete and properly elects portability. Do not assume relief is always available; eligibility can depend on whether the estate was required to file, whether prior extensions were sought, and whether the procedure in effect at filing time applies. Late relief is a fallback, not a substitute for timely filing.

### Distinguish Portability From Trust-Based Planning

Portability and credit shelter (bypass) trust planning are alternative ways to use both spouses' exemptions. Portability is simpler but has limitations: it does not capture growth on the sheltered assets outside the surviving spouse's estate, it may not provide creditor protection, and it is subject to the remarriage displacement rule. Trust planning can lock in exemption and provide asset protection but requires funding and administration.

When deciding between relying on portability and using a credit shelter trust, weigh simplicity against growth, asset protection, control, and the remarriage risk. Do not treat portability as a complete replacement for trust planning in all cases, particularly where asset protection, control over distributions, or capturing asset growth outside the taxable estate matters. The choice depends on family facts, state law, and the size of the estates.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Portability Is Automatic

Portability requires an affirmative election on a timely filed Form 706. Executors who skip filing because no tax is owed forfeit the first spouse's DSUE, which can be a large and permanent loss for the surviving spouse.

### Filing An Incomplete Form 706

A partial or incomplete return may not constitute a valid portability election. The election requires a complete, properly prepared return with the DSUE computation; shortcuts taken because no tax is due can invalidate the election.

### Overlooking The Remarriage Displacement Rule

DSUE from an earlier spouse is generally displaced by DSUE from a later deceased spouse. Assuming DSUE accumulates across marriages causes a surviving spouse to rely on exemption that is no longer available after a remarriage and subsequent death.

### Relying On Outdated Five-Year Limitations

Earlier guidance imposed a five-year limit on using DSUE that has since been removed. Applying the old rule causes either unnecessary urgency or misplaced confidence; the controlling constraint is remarriage and the surviving spouse's death, not a fixed clock.

### Treating Late Relief As Guaranteed

Late portability relief under Rev. Proc. 2017-34 and successor procedures has eligibility conditions and a defined window. It is not automatic and is not a substitute for timely filing; relying on it as a default plan is risky.

### Equating Portability With Full Trust Planning

Portability transfers exemption but does not provide the asset protection, growth-sheltering, and control benefits of a funded credit shelter trust. Treating portability as a complete substitute ignores cases where trust planning is superior.
