# Professional Status Qualification Test

Real estate professional status under Section 469(c)(7) is the gateway that allows a taxpayer's rental real estate activities to be treated as non-passive, but the qualification test is strict and heavily scrutinized. The taxpayer must spend more than 50 percent of their personal service time in real property trades or businesses and more than 750 hours in real property trades or businesses in which they materially participate. Both tests are measured at the individual taxpayer level, and both must be met in the same year. The status is not self-certifying in any meaningful sense; it is a factual determination that the IRS regularly challenges, and the taxpayer bears the burden of proof. An agent who treats the 750-hour number as a low bar, without examining what counts as a real property trade or business and what counts as personal service time, will overstate the taxpayer's eligibility and expose them to disallowed losses, tax, interest, and penalties.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the 50-percent test compares real estate time to all of the taxpayer's personal service time, including a full-time non-real-estate job, so a taxpayer with substantial other employment rarely qualifies. Second, the hours must be in real property trades or businesses, which are defined to include development, redevelopment, construction, reconstruction, acquisition, conversion, rental, operation, management, leasing, or brokerage, but the taxpayer must materially participate in those trades or businesses, not merely own them. Third, the hours must be the taxpayer's own, and work performed by employees, agents, and independent contractors generally does not count. The harm is a taxpayer who claims the status based on ownership rather than services, loses the status on audit, and faces recharacterization of all rental losses as suspended passive losses.

This skill covers the real estate professional qualification test under U.S. federal tax law. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the test is fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply Both Tests In The Same Year

The taxpayer must satisfy two tests in the same tax year. The first is the more-than-50-percent test: more than 50 percent of the personal services performed by the taxpayer in trades or businesses during the year must be performed in real property trades or businesses in which the taxpayer materially participates. The second is the more-than-750-hours test: the taxpayer must perform more than 750 hours of services in real property trades or businesses in which they materially participate. Failing either test disqualifies the taxpayer for that year.

Compute both tests from the taxpayer's total personal service hours. The denominator of the 50-percent test is all personal services the taxpayer performs in all trades or businesses, including a W-2 job, a professional practice, and any other business activity. A taxpayer with a demanding non-real-estate career will struggle to clear 50 percent unless the real estate work is genuinely their dominant activity. The 750-hour test is an absolute floor. Both tests use only the taxpayer's own hours in trades or businesses where they materially participate; passive ownership of a real estate entity without material participation does not contribute. Confirm both tests with documented hours before claiming the status.

### Define Real Property Trades Or Businesses Correctly

A real property trade or business is defined under Section 469(c)(7)(C) as any real property development, redevelopment, construction, reconstruction, acquisition, conversion, rental, operation, management, leasing, or brokerage trade or business. The definition is broad, but the taxpayer must materially participate in the specific trade or business for the hours to count. Merely owning rental property through an entity is not enough; the taxpayer must perform services in the operation, management, leasing, or other qualifying functions.

Identify which of the taxpayer's activities qualify as real property trades or businesses. Rental operations, property management, leasing, and brokerage are the most common. Activities that are tangential to real estate, such as a separate consulting business or a non-real-estate investment activity, do not count. For an activity to contribute hours, the taxpayer must materially participate in it, so the hours must meet one of the material participation tests for that activity. Confirm that each activity counted toward the 750 hours is a qualifying trade or business and that the taxpayer materially participates in it.

### Exclude Hours That Do Not Count

Not all time spent on real estate counts toward the tests. Hours performed by employees of the taxpayer do not count unless the taxpayer is an employee performing services in the real estate activity. Hours performed by agents, property managers, and independent contractors do not count toward the taxpayer's total. Investor-level work, such as reviewing financial statements, organizing records, studying the market, or attending investor seminars, generally does not count unless the taxpayer is also materially participating in management or operations. Travel time to and from properties may count in limited circumstances but is often excluded or limited.

Scrutinize the hours before counting them. Count operational and management work performed personally by the taxpayer, such as repairing units, showing properties, screening tenants, collecting rent, maintaining grounds, and keeping the books. Exclude time spent by others and pure investor activities. The distinction between owner-operator work and investor work is the most common audit battleground, because taxpayers tend to log everything they do, while the IRS excludes investor-level time. A conservative log that counts only clearly qualifying operational hours is more defensible than an inflated log.

### Handle The Spousal And Entity-Level Complications

For married taxpayers filing jointly, each spouse's status is determined separately. A spouse who does not personally meet both tests cannot claim the status, even if the other spouse does. However, a spouse's hours may count toward the taxpayer's material participation in some circumstances, though not toward the taxpayer's own 750-hour professional test. For entities, the taxpayer's status is determined at the individual level; an S corporation or partnership that owns real estate does not itself qualify. The individual owner must perform the services and meet the tests.

Determine the status for each spouse individually. If both spouses work in the real estate, each must independently clear the 50-percent and 750-hour tests to claim the status for their respective interests. If only one spouse works in real estate, only that spouse can claim the status, and the rental activities owned by the other spouse remain passive unless that spouse also qualifies. For pass-through entities, attribute the real estate activity to the individual owner and apply the tests at the owner level, counting only the owner's own services in the entity's real property trades or businesses.

### Establish Contemporaneous Time Documentation

The taxpayer bears the burden of proving they met the tests, and the IRS regularly requests time logs on examination. Contemporaneous records, such as daily or weekly logs with the date, property, task, and hours, are the strongest evidence. Reconstructed logs created after an audit begins are weak and may be disregarded entirely. The log should distinguish qualifying operational work from non-qualifying investor work and from work performed by others. The documentation should cover the entire year and total more than 750 qualifying hours.

Set up a documentation system before the tax year. The taxpayer should record hours regularly, not at year-end, and the records should be specific. A log that says "worked on rentals, 20 hours" is far weaker than one that says "replaced water heater at 123 Main, 3 hours; showed unit to three applicants at 456 Oak, 2 hours." The specificity establishes credibility and withstands IRS scrutiny. Without contemporaneous documentation, even a taxpayer who genuinely spent 800 hours may lose the status, because the IRS is not required to accept uncorroborated estimates.

### Make The Election On A Timely Filed Return

To have rental activities treated as non-passive, the taxpayer must make an election under Section 469(c)(7)(A) on a timely filed return, including extensions, for the first year the status is claimed. The election is made by attaching a statement to the return. Once made, the election applies to that year and future years in which the taxpayer qualifies, and it does not need to be refiled annually, though the taxpayer must continue to meet the tests each year. A late election generally requires IRS consent, which is difficult to obtain.

Confirm the election was made timely. If the taxpayer is claiming the status for the first time, the election must be on the original or extended return for that year. If the taxpayer claimed the status in a prior year and continues to qualify, the election remains in effect, but the tests must be met annually. If the election was missed, advise the taxpayer that a late election is generally not allowed without a private letter ruling, and that claiming the status without the election risks disallowance. Do not assume a prior election exists; verify it from prior returns.

### Anticipate IRS Examination Challenges

Real estate professional status is a high-examination area. The IRS challenges both the hours claimed and the nature of the activities. Common challenges include questioning whether the taxpayer truly spent 750 hours, whether the activities were real property trades or businesses, whether the taxpayer materially participated, and whether the time logs are contemporaneous and credible. Taxpayers who claim the status with thin documentation or who have a full-time non-real-estate job face a high risk of disallowance.

Prepare the claim as if it will be examined. The documentation should be thorough, specific, and contemporaneous. The taxpayer should be able to explain how they spent their time, which activities counted, and why they qualify as real property trades or businesses. Anticipate the question of how the taxpayer meets the 50-percent test if they have other employment, and document the total personal service hours across all activities to support the ratio. A defensible claim rests on real hours in real activities, documented in real time, not on a round-number estimate produced at filing season.

## Common Traps

### Treating Ownership As Substituting For Services

The symptom is a taxpayer who owns several rentals through an LLC and claims the status based on ownership rather than personal services. The trap is that the tests measure the taxpayer's own services in real property trades or businesses, and passive ownership does not count. The direction is to count only the taxpayer's personal operational and management hours and to exclude time of agents and employees.

### Ignoring The 50-Percent Test When The Taxpayer Has A Full-Time Job

The symptom is a taxpayer with a demanding W-2 career who claims the status based on 800 real estate hours. The trap is that the 50-percent test compares real estate time to all personal service time, and a full-time job often consumes more than 50 percent, disqualifying the taxpayer. The direction is to compute the ratio against total personal service hours and to recognize that the status is rarely available to someone with substantial other employment.

### Counting Investor-Level Work As Qualifying Hours

The symptom is a taxpayer who logs time reviewing financials, studying markets, and attending seminars. The trap is that investor-level work generally does not count toward the 750 hours unless the taxpayer is also materially participating in management or operations. The direction is to count only operational and management work and to exclude pure investor activities.

### Using Reconstructed Or Year-End Time Logs

The symptom is a taxpayer who produces a time log only after receiving an audit notice. The trap is that reconstructed logs are weak evidence and may be disregarded, causing the taxpayer to lose the status even if they genuinely spent the hours. The direction is to maintain contemporaneous logs recorded weekly or monthly with specific tasks and properties.

### Assuming Both Spouses Qualify Because One Does

The symptom is a married couple where one spouse works in real estate and both claim the status. The trap is that each spouse's status is determined separately, and a non-participating spouse does not qualify. The direction is to test each spouse individually and to limit the status to the spouse who meets both tests.

### Missing The Election Deadline

The symptom is a taxpayer who realizes mid-year they qualify and claims the status without filing the election. The trap is that the election must be on a timely filed return including extensions, and a late election generally requires IRS consent. The direction is to file the election on the original or extended return for the first qualifying year.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the taxpayer been confirmed to meet both the more-than-50-percent personal services test and the more-than-750-hours test in the same year, with the 50-percent ratio computed against all personal service hours?
- [ ] Has each activity counted toward the 750 hours been confirmed as a real property trade or business in which the taxpayer materially participates?
- [ ] Have non-qualifying hours, including investor-level work, employee and agent time, and travel, been excluded from the count?
- [ ] For married taxpayers, has each spouse's status been determined separately, with the status limited to the spouse who meets both tests?
- [ ] Has a contemporaneous time-log system been established, with specific tasks, properties, and hours recorded regularly throughout the year?
- [ ] Has the Section 469(c)(7)(A) election been confirmed as filed on a timely return (including extensions) for the first qualifying year?
- [ ] Has the claim been prepared with examination in mind, documenting the total personal service hours and the nature of the activities to withstand IRS challenge?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer been informed that the status must be re-justified annually and that failing the tests in a later year reverts the rentals to passive treatment?
