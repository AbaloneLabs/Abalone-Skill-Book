# Tax Obligation Calendar

Tax compliance fails when the work starts from the form someone remembers instead of the obligations that actually apply. A taxpayer may have income tax, estimated tax, employment tax, information return, excise tax, sales or use tax, franchise tax, property tax, local registrations, foreign reporting, or industry-specific obligations. Missing a due date can create penalties even when the final tax return later shows a refund. Filing an extension may extend time to file but not time to pay. A calendar that ignores entity type, payroll status, fiscal year, state rules, and payment method is not safe.

Use this skill before building or reviewing a tax calendar, preparing a filing checklist, advising an agent to collect tax records, deciding whether estimated payments are needed, mapping employer tax deadlines, or checking whether a business has covered its recurring filing and payment obligations. The goal is to force an obligation-first review rather than a form-first guess.

## Core Rules

### Start With Taxpayer Profile And Jurisdiction

Do not choose deadlines until the taxpayer profile is clear. Different taxpayers have different filing forms, payment duties, and calendars.

Identify:

- individual, sole proprietor, partnership, corporation, S corporation, LLC, trust, estate, nonprofit, or disregarded entity;
- calendar year or fiscal year;
- resident jurisdictions;
- places where work is performed;
- states or countries where customers are located;
- employees, contractors, partners, or owners;
- payroll, sales, inventory, property, imports, exports, or regulated activities;
- prior-year filing status and elections;
- current-year business start, sale, closure, merger, conversion, or address change.

Treat "LLC" as an incomplete answer. An LLC can be taxed in different ways depending on elections, owners, and jurisdiction. The legal form and tax classification both matter.

### Separate Filing Duties From Payment Duties

Filing a form and paying tax are related but not the same obligation. A taxpayer can file on time and still pay late, or pay enough but file late. An extension often extends the filing deadline only; it usually does not postpone the payment obligation.

For each tax type, record:

- return or form due date;
- payment due date;
- deposit due date if applicable;
- extension availability;
- whether extension covers filing only or payment too;
- required payment method;
- penalty exposure for late filing, late payment, or underpayment;
- confirmation evidence for filing and payment.

Never summarize an extension as "deadline moved" without stating what moved and what did not.

### Include Estimated Tax And Pay-As-You-Go Requirements

Many taxpayers must pay tax during the year through withholding or estimated payments. Waiting for annual filing can create penalties if withholding and estimated payments are insufficient.

Review estimated payment needs for:

- self-employment income;
- business income not subject to withholding;
- partnership or S corporation pass-through income;
- capital gains;
- interest and dividends;
- rental income;
- prizes or awards;
- under-withheld wages;
- alternative minimum tax exposure;
- state and local estimated tax obligations.

Use current official worksheets, prior-year return data, safe harbor rules, and current-year projections where available. If income is volatile, build a review rhythm so estimates can be revised rather than copied blindly.

### Map Employment Tax Obligations Separately

An employer has responsibilities beyond annual income tax. Employment taxes can involve withholding, deposits, quarterly or annual returns, wage statements, unemployment taxes, state payroll obligations, and correction forms.

Check:

- employer identification number;
- employee onboarding forms;
- federal income tax withholding;
- Social Security and Medicare taxes;
- Additional Medicare Tax where applicable;
- FUTA;
- state unemployment insurance;
- local payroll taxes;
- deposit schedule;
- payroll return schedule;
- W-2 and W-3 reporting;
- corrected wage reports;
- payroll provider responsibility versus employer responsibility.

Payroll providers can process filings, but the employer still needs evidence that filings and deposits happened. Do not let outsourcing hide accountability.

### Include Information Returns And Recipient Statements

Payments to contractors, landlords, attorneys, interest recipients, dividend recipients, and other payees may require information returns. Recipient statements may have earlier or different deadlines than IRS filing, and electronic filing thresholds may change.

Review:

- nonemployee compensation;
- rent;
- royalties;
- attorney payments;
- interest;
- dividends;
- payment card or third-party network reporting;
- backup withholding;
- foreign payees;
- W-9 or W-8 collection;
- taxpayer identification number matching;
- state information return requirements.

Do not wait until January to discover missing taxpayer identification numbers. Collect and validate payee information before payment or before year-end.

### Consider State, Local, And Non-Income Taxes

Federal income tax is only part of the compliance map. A business may owe state income tax, franchise tax, gross receipts tax, sales and use tax, lodging tax, excise tax, property tax, payroll tax, business license renewals, or local registrations.

Ask:

- Where is the taxpayer legally formed?
- Where does it have employees, contractors, inventory, offices, warehouses, or remote workers?
- Where are customers located?
- Does it sell taxable goods or services?
- Does it use marketplace platforms?
- Does it import, manufacture, transport, or sell regulated products?
- Does it own business property?
- Does it operate in cities or counties with separate registrations?

For non-U.S. or multi-state situations, treat jurisdiction review as required rather than optional. Do not infer that no obligation exists because no federal form appears.

### Build A Calendar With Evidence And Owners

A tax calendar should be operational, not just a list of dates. Each obligation needs an owner, inputs, review step, filing method, payment method, and evidence.

Track:

- obligation name;
- jurisdiction;
- taxpayer or entity;
- period covered;
- due date;
- extension date if applicable;
- responsible person or provider;
- required inputs;
- review deadline;
- filing method;
- payment method;
- confirmation number or proof;
- status;
- notes for next period.

Use reminders before the due date, not on the due date. Build earlier internal cutoffs for record collection, review, authorization, and cash planning.

### Verify Current Forms And Dates

Tax forms, thresholds, payment methods, and deadlines can change. Disaster relief, weekends, legal holidays, and special rules may move due dates. Never rely solely on memory or an old calendar.

Before finalizing:

- check current official instructions;
- confirm calendar-year versus fiscal-year treatment;
- confirm weekend and holiday adjustments;
- check disaster relief notices;
- confirm electronic filing rules;
- verify deposit schedule;
- confirm state and local rules separately;
- preserve links or citations used.

If the task requires an actual filing decision, recommend review by a qualified tax professional.

## Common Traps

### Treating April 15 As The Only Date That Matters

Businesses, employers, partnerships, S corporations, contractors, and estimated taxpayers may have earlier or recurring deadlines. A calendar built around only annual individual filing misses major obligations.

### Confusing Extension To File With Extension To Pay

A common error is assuming an extension postpones payment. The agent should explicitly state whether tax must still be paid by the original due date and whether penalties or interest can apply.

### Assuming A Payroll Provider Owns The Risk

A payroll provider may file and deposit, but the employer should still monitor confirmations, deposit timing, employee classifications, wage accuracy, and state registrations. Outsourcing execution is not the same as outsourcing responsibility.

### Ignoring Information Returns Until Year-End

Form 1099 and similar reporting becomes difficult when payee data was never collected. Missing W-9 or W-8 information, incorrect taxpayer identification numbers, and unclear payment categories should be fixed during the year.

### Forgetting State And Local Nexus

Remote employees, contractors, inventory, warehouses, trade shows, customers, affiliates, and marketplace activity can create obligations beyond the home state. Do not let a federal-only view stand in for tax compliance.

### Reusing Last Year's Calendar Without Review

Deadlines, forms, thresholds, business activities, entities, payroll frequency, and filing methods can change. Last year's calendar is a starting point, not evidence that this year's obligations are complete.

### Missing Business Lifecycle Events

Starting, ending, merging, selling, converting, electing S corporation status, adding owners, hiring employees, changing address, or entering a new state can create new registrations, short-year filings, final returns, or amended calendars.

## Self-Check

- Is the taxpayer profile complete by entity type, tax classification, fiscal year, jurisdictions, activities, workers, and lifecycle events?
- Are filing, payment, deposit, extension, and confirmation duties separated for each obligation?
- Have estimated tax and pay-as-you-go requirements been reviewed for income not covered by withholding?
- Are employment tax deposits, returns, wage statements, unemployment obligations, and payroll-provider confirmations included?
- Are information returns, recipient statements, W-9 or W-8 collection, backup withholding, and state reporting considered?
- Are state, local, sales and use, excise, property, franchise, and license obligations considered instead of only federal income tax?
- Does each calendar item have an owner, inputs, internal review deadline, filing method, payment method, and evidence requirement?
- Have current official instructions, due dates, weekend or holiday adjustments, disaster relief, and electronic filing rules been checked?
- Are extension limits and late-payment risks clearly stated?
- Are uncertain filing decisions routed to a qualified tax professional rather than guessed?
