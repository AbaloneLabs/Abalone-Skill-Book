# Transfer Pricing Documentation

Transfer pricing documentation is the taxpayer's defense against IRS adjustments and penalties. Under Section 6662, a taxpayer with related-party transactions must maintain contemporaneous documentation that supports the arm's length pricing. The documentation must be prepared at the time of the transaction (or the tax return filing) and provided to the IRS within 30 days of a request. The OECD three-tier approach (master file, local file, country-by-country report) has been adopted in many jurisdictions, including the U.S., adding layers of documentation. An agent who treats documentation as an afterthought, or who prepares it only after an audit, will expose the taxpayer to penalties.

Agents commonly miss that the documentation must be contemporaneous, that it must cover the method selection and the comparability analysis, that the three-tier approach has specific content requirements, and that the penalty for inadequate documentation is 20% to 40% of the adjustment. The harm is a taxpayer who faces a transfer pricing adjustment and cannot avoid the penalty due to missing or late documentation.

This skill covers transfer pricing documentation under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the documentation requirements are detailed, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Contemporaneous Documentation Requirement

Under Section 6662, a taxpayer with related-party transactions must maintain contemporaneous documentation to support the arm's length pricing and to qualify for penalty protection. Contemporaneous means the documentation must be prepared at the time of the transaction or the tax return filing (by the return due date, including extensions). Documentation prepared after an audit begins does not qualify as contemporaneous and does not provide penalty protection. The documentation must be provided to the IRS within 30 days of a request.

Understand the contemporaneous requirement. The documentation must be prepared by the return due date (including extensions) for the tax year. Documentation prepared after an IRS audit begins is not contemporaneous and does not provide penalty protection. The documentation must be provided to the IRS within 30 days of a written request. The taxpayer must maintain the documentation for the retention period (generally the statute of limitations period). Prepare the documentation as part of the tax return process, not as an afterthought. The contemporaneous requirement is strict; late documentation does not qualify.

### Prepare The Required Documentation Elements

The Section 6662 documentation must include specific elements: a description of the related-party transactions, the method selected and the rationale, the comparability analysis and the comparables used, the arm's length result, and the supporting data. The documentation must also include a description of the business, the organizational structure, and any cost-sharing arrangements or other relevant agreements. The elements must be complete and must support the arm's length pricing.

Prepare the required documentation elements. Include: a description of the related-party transactions (the products, services, intangibles, or loans), the method selected (CUP, Resale Price, Cost Plus, CPM, Profit Split) and the rationale, the comparability analysis (the FAR analysis, the comparables, the adjustments), the arm's length result (the price or profit range), and the supporting data (financial data, market data, valuation reports). Include the business description, the organizational structure, and any cost-sharing arrangements. The documentation must be complete and must support the arm's length pricing. Incomplete documentation does not provide penalty protection.

### Apply The OECD Three-Tier Approach

The OECD three-tier approach, adopted in the U.S. and many other jurisdictions, requires three layers of documentation: the master file, the local file, and the country-by-country (CbC) report. The master file provides a high-level overview of the multinational group's global operations and transfer pricing policies. The local file provides detailed information on the related-party transactions in each jurisdiction. The CbC report provides financial and tax data for each jurisdiction where the group operates. The three-tier approach applies to large multinational groups (generally those with annual revenue above 850 million dollars for the CbC report).

Apply the OECD three-tier approach. The master file provides the global overview (the group's organizational structure, business description, intangibles, intercompany financial activities, and financial and tax positions). The local file provides the detailed transaction-level information (the related-party transactions, the method selection, the comparability analysis, and the arm's length result for each jurisdiction). The CbC report provides the jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction financial and tax data (revenue, profit, taxes paid, employees, assets for each country). The CbC report is required for large groups (revenue above 850 million dollars); the master file and local file are required for all groups with related-party transactions. Prepare each layer in accordance with the regulations.

### Meet The Penalty Protection Standard

To qualify for penalty protection under Section 6662, the documentation must meet a specific standard: the taxpayer must maintain contemporaneous documentation, the documentation must support the arm's length pricing, and the taxpayer must provide the documentation to the IRS within 30 days of a request. If the documentation meets the standard, the taxpayer avoids the transfer pricing penalty (20% for a transactional penalty, 40% for a net adjustment penalty) even if the IRS makes an adjustment. If the documentation does not meet the standard, the penalty applies to the adjustment.

Meet the penalty protection standard. Ensure the documentation is contemporaneous (prepared by the return due date), complete (covering all required elements), and supports the arm's length pricing (the method, the comparables, the result). Provide the documentation to the IRS within 30 days of a request. If the standard is met, the taxpayer avoids the penalty even if the IRS makes an adjustment. If the standard is not met (late documentation, incomplete documentation, or documentation that does not support the pricing), the penalty applies. The penalty can be 20% (transactional) or 40% (net adjustment) of the adjustment amount, which can be substantial. Treat the documentation as a critical defense.

### Handle The Country-By-Country Report

The CbC report is required for large multinational groups (annual consolidated revenue above 850 million dollars in the prior year). The report provides financial and tax data for each jurisdiction where the group operates, including revenue, profit, income taxes paid, employees, and tangible assets. The report is filed with the group's parent's tax authority and is exchanged with other jurisdictions under tax treaties or intergovernmental agreements. The CbC report is used by tax authorities to assess transfer pricing risk.

Handle the CbC report. If the group's annual consolidated revenue exceeds 850 million dollars, the CbC report is required. The report is filed on Form 8974 (for U.S. parents) or through the local filing procedure (for foreign parents). The report provides jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction data. The report is exchanged with other jurisdictions, which use it to identify transfer pricing risk and target audits. Ensure the report is accurate and consistent with the master file and local file. The CbC report is a transparency tool; the data can trigger audits in multiple jurisdictions. Prepare the report carefully and review the data for consistency.

### Coordinate With The Tax Return And The Audit Defense

The transfer pricing documentation must be coordinated with the tax return and the audit defense. The documentation supports the pricing reported on the tax return. In an audit, the documentation is the primary defense against an adjustment. The documentation must be consistent with the tax return positions and must be updated annually. The audit defense requires presenting the documentation, defending the method and the comparables, and potentially negotiating a settlement or invoking the competent authority procedure.

Coordinate with the tax return and the audit defense. Ensure the documentation supports the pricing reported on the tax return. Update the documentation annually to reflect changes in the transactions, the comparables, or the methodology. In an audit, present the documentation, defend the method and the comparables, and respond to the IRS's challenges. If the IRS proposes an adjustment, evaluate the strength of the defense, consider a settlement, and consider the competent authority procedure for double taxation relief. The documentation is the foundation of the audit defense. Maintain it carefully and update it annually.

## Common Traps

### Preparing Documentation After An Audit Begins

The documentation must be contemporaneous (prepared by the return due date). Documentation prepared after an audit is not contemporaneous and does not provide penalty protection.

### Omitting Required Documentation Elements

The documentation must include the method selection, the comparability analysis, the arm's length result, and the supporting data. Incomplete documentation does not provide penalty protection.

### Failing The 30-Day Response Deadline

The documentation must be provided to the IRS within 30 days of a request. Missing the deadline forfeits penalty protection.

### Overlooking The Three-Tier Approach

The master file, local file, and CbC report have specific content requirements. Failing to prepare one layer or omitting required content results in non-compliance.

### Not Filing The CbC Report For Large Groups

The CbC report is required for groups with revenue above 850 million dollars. Failure to file triggers penalties and increases audit risk.

### Inconsistency Between The Documentation And The Tax Return

The documentation must support the pricing reported on the tax return. Inconsistency undermines the defense and can trigger an adjustment.

### Underestimating The Penalty Exposure

The transfer pricing penalty is 20% to 40% of the adjustment. Without contemporaneous documentation, the penalty applies. Treat the documentation as a critical defense.
