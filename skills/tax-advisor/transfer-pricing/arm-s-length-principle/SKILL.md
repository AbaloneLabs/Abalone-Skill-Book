# Arm's Length Principle

The arm's length principle requires that transactions between related parties (a U.S. parent and its foreign subsidiary, or two foreign affiliates) be priced as if they were between independent parties. Under Section 482 and the OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines, the IRS can reallocate income and deductions among related parties if the pricing does not reflect arm's length. Transfer pricing is one of the highest-risk areas in international tax, and an agent who treats related-party transactions as freely priced will expose the taxpayer to significant IRS adjustments and penalties.

Agents commonly miss that the arm's length principle applies to all related-party transactions (goods, services, intangibles, loans), that the method must be selected based on comparability, that the burden of proof is on the taxpayer, and that penalties apply for mispricing. The harm is a taxpayer whose transfer pricing is challenged and who faces adjustments, penalties, and double taxation.

This skill covers the arm's length principle under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; transfer pricing is complex and fact-intensive, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Arm's Length Standard

The arm's length standard requires that the price charged in a related-party transaction equal the price that would be charged between independent parties in a comparable transaction. If the related-party price deviates from the arm's length price, the IRS can reallocate income and deductions to reflect the arm's length result. The standard applies to all types of related-party transactions: sales of goods, provision of services, licensing of intangibles, and loans.

Apply the arm's length standard. For each related-party transaction, determine the price that would be charged between independent parties in a comparable transaction. The standard applies to goods (the transfer price of inventory sold between affiliates), services (the fee for services provided between affiliates), intangibles (the royalty for IP licensed between affiliates), and loans (the interest rate on intercompany loans). If the related-party price deviates from the arm's length price, the IRS can adjust. The burden of proof is on the taxpayer to demonstrate that the pricing is arm's length. Document the pricing methodology and the comparability analysis.

### Select The Appropriate Transfer Pricing Method

The regulations under Section 482 provide several transfer pricing methods, and the taxpayer must select the method that provides the most reliable arm's length result based on the facts and circumstances. The specified methods for transactions involving tangible property include the Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP) method, the Resale Price method, the Cost Plus method, the Comparable Profits method (CPM), and the Profit Split method. For intangibles, the Comparable Uncontrolled Transaction method and other methods apply. The method selection depends on comparability and reliability.

Select the appropriate transfer pricing method. Evaluate the available methods and select the one that provides the most reliable arm's length result. The CUP method compares the price in the controlled transaction to the price in a comparable uncontrolled transaction; it is the most direct but requires a comparable uncontrolled transaction. The Resale Price method starts with the resale price to independent parties and subtracts an arm's length markup. The Cost Plus method starts with the cost of production and adds an arm's length markup. The CPM compares the controlled party's profitability to that of comparable uncontrolled parties. The Profit Split method splits the combined profit based on the relative contributions. Document the method selection and the rationale.

### Perform The Comparability Analysis

The comparability analysis is the core of transfer pricing. It compares the controlled transaction to uncontrolled transactions (or uncontrolled parties) to determine the arm's length price or profit. Comparability depends on the functions performed, the assets used, the risks assumed, the contractual terms, the market conditions, and the nature of the property or services. A transaction is comparable only if these factors are sufficiently similar; adjustments may be needed for differences.

Perform the comparability analysis. Identify the controlled transaction and the functions performed, assets used, and risks assumed (the FAR analysis). Identify comparable uncontrolled transactions or comparable uncontrolled parties. Assess the comparability based on the FAR analysis, the contractual terms, the market conditions, and the nature of the property or services. If there are differences, make adjustments to improve comparability. The reliability of the method depends on the quality of the comparables and the adjustments. A transaction with no comparables requires a less direct method (such as CPM or Profit Split). Document the comparability analysis, including the comparables selected, the adjustments made, and the reliability assessment.

### Document The Transfer Pricing Under Section 6662

Transfer pricing is subject to the Section 6662 penalty regime. A taxpayer with related-party transactions must maintain contemporaneous transfer pricing documentation to support the arm's length pricing and to avoid penalties. The documentation must include the method selection, the comparability analysis, the arm's length result, and the rationale. Without contemporaneous documentation, a transfer pricing adjustment can trigger a 20% to 40% penalty on the adjustment.

Maintain contemporaneous transfer pricing documentation. The documentation must be prepared contemporaneously (at the time of the transaction or the tax return filing) and must include: a description of the related-party transactions, the method selected and the rationale, the comparability analysis and the comparables, the arm's length result, and the supporting data. The documentation must be provided to the IRS within 30 days of a request. Without contemporaneous documentation, a transfer pricing adjustment triggers a penalty (20% for a transactional penalty, 40% for a net adjustment penalty). The documentation is critical for penalty protection. Prepare and maintain the documentation for each tax year.

### Handle Intangibles And Cost-Sharing Arrangements

Intangibles (patents, trademarks, trade names, know-how) are a high-risk area in transfer pricing. The transfer or license of intangibles between related parties must be at arm's length, which requires valuing the intangible and determining an arm's length royalty. Cost-sharing arrangements (CSAs), where related parties share the costs and risks of developing intangibles, must be structured with arm's length contributions and platform contributions. The valuation of intangibles and CSAs is complex and often disputed.

Handle intangibles and cost-sharing arrangements. For the transfer or license of intangibles, value the intangible (using discounted cash flow, market comparables, or other valuation methods) and determine an arm's length royalty. For CSAs, ensure the cost-sharing contributions and the platform contributions (the value of pre-existing intangibles) are arm's length. The IRS scrutinizes intangibles transfers and CSAs, especially where the intangible is moved to a low-tax jurisdiction. Document the valuation and the arm's length analysis. Intangibles are a primary target for IRS transfer pricing audits; the documentation must be robust.

### Coordinate With The Foreign Tax Authority And Double Taxation

Transfer pricing adjustments by the IRS can lead to double taxation: the U.S. increases the U.S. tax (by reallocating income to the U.S.), and the foreign country does not correspondingly reduce its tax (unless a competent authority agreement or a mutual agreement procedure resolves the double taxation). The taxpayer can request relief from double taxation through the competent authority procedure under the applicable tax treaty. The coordination with the foreign tax authority is critical for resolving transfer pricing disputes.

Coordinate with the foreign tax authority. If the IRS makes a transfer pricing adjustment, the taxpayer may face double taxation (U.S. tax increased, foreign tax not reduced). The taxpayer can request competent authority relief under the applicable tax treaty, asking the U.S. and the foreign tax authorities to negotiate a resolution that eliminates the double taxation. The competent authority procedure can take years but is often the only way to resolve double taxation. Advise the taxpayer to consider the competent authority procedure when a transfer pricing adjustment is made. Track the adjustment and the competent authority request.

## Common Traps

### Treating Related-Party Transactions As Freely Priced

Related-party transactions must be at arm's length. The IRS can reallocate income if the pricing deviates. Do not assume related parties can price freely.

### Selecting The Wrong Transfer Pricing Method

The method must be selected based on comparability and reliability. The CUP method requires a comparable uncontrolled transaction. Selecting the wrong method produces unreliable results.

### Failing The Comparability Analysis

The comparability analysis is the core of transfer pricing. Using non-comparable transactions or failing to make adjustments undermines the arm's length result.

### Not Maintaining Contemporaneous Documentation

Transfer pricing documentation must be contemporaneous. Without it, a transfer pricing adjustment triggers a 20% to 40% penalty. Prepare and maintain the documentation.

### Overlooking Intangibles And Cost-Sharing Arrangements

Intangibles and CSAs are high-risk areas. The valuation and the arm's length analysis must be robust. The IRS scrutinizes intangibles transfers to low-tax jurisdictions.

### Ignoring The Risk Of Double Taxation

A U.S. transfer pricing adjustment can lead to double taxation. The competent authority procedure under the tax treaty may provide relief. Advise the taxpayer accordingly.

### Underestimating The Burden Of Proof

The burden of proof is on the taxpayer to demonstrate arm's length pricing. The taxpayer must maintain documentation and be prepared to defend the methodology.
