# Household Payroll Reporting

Household payroll reporting is where the nanny tax decision becomes a compliance deliverable. Many families correctly conclude they have a household employee and correctly compute the tax, but then fail at the reporting stage because household reporting is split across several systems that do not talk to each other. Schedule H goes on the personal return, the W-2 goes to the employee and to the Social Security Administration, state unemployment returns go to the state, and new-hire reporting goes to a separate state registry. Missing any one of these produces a partial compliance position that an auditor treats as noncompliance.

The harm comes from fragmentation. Unlike business payroll, which is usually run through a single payroll provider that handles every filing, household payroll is often managed manually by the family, and each filing has its own deadline, form, and agency. The agent who advises only on the federal Schedule H leaves the family exposed on state unemployment, new-hire reporting, and W-2 transmittal. The second source of harm is reconciliation. The wages on Schedule H must match the W-2, the W-2 must match the state filings, and the deposits must match the returns. When these diverge, the family faces notices, penalties, and rework even when the underlying tax was correct.

This skill applies before advising on Schedule H preparation, W-2 issuance for household employees, state quarterly unemployment filings, new-hire reporting, transmittal of W-2s to SSA and states, reconciliation of household payroll across deposits and returns, and documentation of worker classification for nannies, caregivers, and other domestic workers. It is U.S. federal income and employment tax oriented and does not replace review of specific facts by a qualified tax professional.

## Core Rules

### Prepare Schedule H With All Three Tax Components

Schedule H reports household employment taxes and must capture every component that applies, Social Security and Medicare taxes, federal unemployment tax, and any withheld federal income tax. The agent must confirm that each component is computed, that the wages reconcile to the W-2 issued to the employee, and that the employee and employer shares of Social Security and Medicare are correctly allocated.

A common error is reporting only the employer share or only the employee share, or double-counting wages that were already reported through a business payroll. Schedule H wages should reflect cash wages only, with non-cash benefits handled separately, and the FUTA computation must apply the correct wage base and credit for state unemployment tax paid. The agent should walk through each line of Schedule H, confirm the wage base for FUTA, confirm the Social Security and Medicare rates, and confirm that any income tax withholding elected by the employee is included. Schedule H attaches to the family's Form 1040 and is due with that return, including extensions, which is a later deadline than business payroll filings.

### Issue Form W-2 And File All Required Copies

The household employee must receive Form W-2 by the end of January, and the employer must file Copy A with the Social Security Administration by the end of January along with Form W-3, the transmittal. State copies must be filed with the applicable state agency by the state deadline, which can differ from the federal deadline.

The agent must treat the W-2 as a multi-recipient deliverable, not a single document. Copy B and C go to the employee, Copy A goes to SSA with the W-3, and state copies go to each required state. The W-2 must show wages in box 1, Social Security wages in box 3, Medicare wages in box 5, and any withheld taxes in the corresponding boxes, and must reconcile exactly to Schedule H. A household employer who prepares Schedule H but never issues a W-2 has not complied, because the employee needs the W-2 to file their own return and SSA uses it to credit the employee's earnings record. Confirm that the W-2 uses the household employer EIN, not the family's personal Social Security number.

### Complete State New Hire Reporting

Most states require employers, including household employers, to report newly hired employees to a state new-hire registry within a short window, often 20 days of hire. This reporting supports child support enforcement and is mandatory even for a single household employee. Families almost universally miss this step because it is separate from payroll and has no federal analog.

The agent should confirm whether the state requires new-hire reporting, identify the registry, and confirm the filing was made within the deadline. Some states accept online reporting and some require specific forms. Re-hiring a former employee or recalling a seasonal worker can also trigger new-hire reporting in some states, so the trigger is not only the first hire. Treat new-hire reporting as a standalone checklist item, because it is the most commonly omitted household payroll filing and it carries its own penalties.

### File State Unemployment And Withholding Returns

State unemployment insurance generally applies to household employees, often at lower thresholds than federal FUTA, and requires quarterly wage reporting and contribution payments to the state unemployment agency. State income tax withholding may also apply, requiring quarterly or annual withholding returns depending on the state.

The agent must identify the state unemployment agency, confirm the quarterly filing cadence, and confirm the wage base and rate. State unemployment returns report wages paid in the quarter and compute the contribution due, and they must reconcile to the W-2 at year-end. State withholding returns, if applicable, report income tax withheld from the employee and must reconcile to the W-2 box 17 or its state equivalent. Missing a quarterly filing can cause the family to lose the FUTA credit, which increases the effective federal unemployment tax rate, so state unemployment compliance directly affects the federal return. Do not treat state filings as optional or secondary.

### Reconcile Across Deposits, Returns, And The W-2

Every dollar of household wages must reconcile across four touchpoints, the deposits or payments made, the quarterly state returns, Schedule H, and the W-2. If any two disagree, the family has a reporting error even if the underlying tax was paid. The agent should perform a year-end reconciliation that compares gross wages per the payroll records to each filing.

Build a reconciliation worksheet that lists gross cash wages, Social Security and Medicare wages, FUTA wages, state unemployment wages, and withheld income tax, and confirm each figure appears identically on the W-2 and on the corresponding return. Differences typically arise from non-cash benefits counted inconsistently, from wages paid after a cutoff date but before year-end, or from manual arithmetic errors. A reconciliation performed before the W-2 is issued catches these problems while they are still fixable. After issuance, corrections require a W-2c and amended returns, which multiply the cost.

### Document Worker Classification And Wage Records

Because household payroll is often informal, the family's records are what sustain the reporting on audit. The agent should recommend maintaining a written employment agreement or offer letter, a wage log or pay stubs, the worker's Form W-4 and any state withholding certificate, the I-9 employment eligibility verification, and the classification rationale if there is any contractor-like element.

The wage log should record each pay date, gross wages, withheld taxes, and net pay, and should reconcile to the bank records. The W-4 supports the income tax withholding election, and the I-9 is a federal requirement for any employee regardless of household status. A family that pays a nanny for years with no written records cannot easily defend its reporting if the worker later disputes the wages or files for unemployment. Treat documentation as part of the reporting deliverable, not a separate administrative task.

### Coordinate Timing Across Federal And State Deadlines

Household payroll deadlines do not align neatly. New-hire reporting is due within days of hire. State unemployment returns are due quarterly. W-2s are due to the employee by the end of January, to SSA by the end of January, and to states by varying deadlines. Schedule H is due with the personal return, including extensions, which is months later than the W-2 deadlines.

The agent should build a deadline calendar for each household employer that captures every filing with its specific due date. Do not assume that filing Schedule H on time satisfies the W-2 or state deadlines, because they are earlier and independent. Conversely, do not assume that issuing the W-2 completes the obligation, because Schedule H and state annual reconciliations remain. A calendar that lists each filing, its recipient, and its due date is the simplest way to prevent the most common household payroll failure, which is missing an early deadline while focusing on the later annual return.

## Common Traps

### Reporting On Schedule H Without Issuing A W-2

Schedule H reports the tax, but the employee needs a W-2 and SSA needs Copy A with a W-3. Preparing Schedule H alone is incomplete compliance.

### Missing State New Hire Reporting

New-hire reporting is mandatory in most states, has a short deadline, and has no federal analog. It is the most commonly omitted household payroll filing.

### Letting State Unemployment Filings Lapse

State unemployment returns are quarterly and losing them forfeits the FUTA credit, which raises the effective federal unemployment tax rate. State compliance directly affects the federal return.

### Filing The W-2 With The Wrong Identifier

The household W-2 must use the household employer EIN, not the family's personal Social Security number or a business EIN. Using the wrong identifier creates mismatches and privacy exposure.

### Assuming Schedule H Timing Covers Everything

Schedule H is due with the personal return, but W-2s, state unemployment, and new-hire reporting have earlier and independent deadlines. Filing Schedule H on time does not cure missed earlier filings.

### Skipping The Year-End Reconciliation

Wages must reconcile across deposits, state returns, Schedule H, and the W-2. Performing reconciliation only after the W-2 is issued forces expensive W-2c and amended returns.

### Treating Documentation As Optional

Without a wage log, W-4, I-9, and classification rationale, the family cannot defend its reporting if the worker disputes wages or files for unemployment.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does Schedule H capture Social Security and Medicare, FUTA, and any elected income tax withholding, with wages reconciling to the W-2?
- [ ] Has Form W-2 been issued to the employee by the end of January, with Copy A and Form W-3 filed with SSA by the federal deadline?
- [ ] Have state copies of the W-2 been filed with each applicable state agency by the state deadline?
- [ ] Has state new-hire reporting been completed with the correct registry within the deadline, including for rehires where applicable?
- [ ] Are state unemployment quarterly returns filed on time with contributions paid, preserving the FUTA credit?
- [ ] Has a year-end reconciliation confirmed that gross wages, Social Security and Medicare wages, FUTA wages, state unemployment wages, and withheld tax match across the payroll records, state returns, Schedule H, and the W-2?
- [ ] Does the W-2 use the household employer EIN rather than the family's personal Social Security number or a business EIN?
- [ ] Are wage logs, the W-4, state withholding certificates, the I-9, and the classification rationale maintained for each household employee?
- [ ] Has a deadline calendar been built that captures new-hire reporting, quarterly state returns, W-2 issuance, SSA and state filing, and Schedule H separately?
- [ ] Has the agent reminded the user that this is general U.S. federal tax guidance, not tax advice, and that late filings, W-2 corrections, back-wage situations, and multi-state cases must be escalated to a qualified tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney?
