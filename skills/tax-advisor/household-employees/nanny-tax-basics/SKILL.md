# Nanny Tax Basics

Hiring someone to work in a home, whether a nanny, housekeeper, senior caregiver, gardener, or home health aide, can create household employer tax obligations that are completely separate from business payroll. Many families assume that because the worker is paid personally, from a personal account, for personal services, no employment taxes apply. That assumption is wrong and is one of the most common and most expensive errors in personal tax. The household employer can owe Social Security and Medicare taxes, federal unemployment tax, and state unemployment and withholding, all of which have their own thresholds, forms, and deadlines.

The harm comes from the gap between intuition and law. Families pay nannies in cash or via personal apps, treat them as independent contractors, or rely on the worker's preference to avoid formal payroll, and then face back taxes, penalties, and interest when the relationship ends or the worker files for unemployment. The agent's job is to force the threshold and classification analysis before any payment structure is finalized, because once cash has changed hands informally, unwinding the arrangement is costly. The second source of harm is underapplying the rules, for example assuming all domestic workers are employees or assuming a low-hours sitter always triggers tax, when the thresholds and exceptions actually drive the answer.

This skill applies before advising on nanny payroll setup, caregiver employment taxes, Schedule H preparation, household employer identification numbers, worker classification for domestic help, and any review of whether a person providing services in a home is a household employee or an independent contractor. It is U.S. federal income and employment tax oriented and does not replace review of specific facts by a qualified tax professional.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Cash Wage Threshold Before Concluding Social Security And Medicare Apply

Social Security and Medicare taxes apply to household employees only when cash wages paid in a calendar year reach a specific threshold, which is adjusted periodically and is in the low thousands of dollars. Below the threshold, no Social Security or Medicare tax is owed on those wages. The agent must identify the threshold for the relevant year and test the total cash wages against it before advising that nanny taxes apply.

The threshold is an annual cash-wages test, so non-cash items such as meals, lodging, or transportation generally do not count toward it, and the test is applied per employer per year, not per job. If a family pays two nannies, each nanny's wages are tested separately against the threshold for that employer. If a nanny works for two families, each family applies its own threshold. Do not annualize or project, and do not assume the threshold never changes, because it is indexed and rises over time. The first dollar above the threshold makes the wages above the threshold subject to Social Security and Medicare, and the employer should plan to begin withholding at that point.

### Confirm Worker Classification Before Setting Up Payroll

Not everyone who provides services in a home is a household employee. A worker is a household employee when the family has the right to control what work is done and how it is done. A nanny whose hours, duties, and methods the family directs is an employee. A plumber, electrician, or independent housecleaner who offers services to the public, brings their own tools, and controls how the work is performed is an independent contractor.

The control test is the same common-law framework used in business contexts, applied to the home. Factors include who sets the schedule, who provides tools and supplies, whether the worker has other clients, whether the worker can send a substitute, whether the work is part of the family's regular routine, and how payment is structured. The worker's preference for contractor status does not control, and paying via a personal payment app does not convert an employee into a contractor. When the facts are mixed, the agent should lean toward employee status and escalate, because misclassifying an employee as a contractor shifts all the tax and penalty exposure onto the family.

### Identify Which Taxes Apply And At What Threshold

Household employment can trigger several distinct taxes, each with its own threshold. Social Security and Medicare apply above the cash-wage threshold. Federal unemployment tax applies when total cash wages paid in a quarter or a year reaches a separate threshold and the family paid wages in each quarter of the current or prior year. State unemployment insurance generally applies at lower thresholds and often from the first dollar in some states. State income tax withholding may apply depending on the state.

The agent must build a tax-by-tax matrix for the specific facts. Confirm whether Social Security and Medicare apply, whether FUTA applies, whether state unemployment applies, and whether state income tax withholding is required or optional. Do not assume that crossing the Social Security threshold automatically triggers FUTA or state unemployment, because each has its own test. Equally, do not assume that being below the federal thresholds means no state obligations, because state unemployment and withholding frequently start earlier.

### Obtain An Employer Identification Number For The Household

A household employer needs an Employer Identification Number, not their personal Social Security number, to report household employment taxes. The EIN is obtained from the IRS, typically online, and is used on Schedule H and related forms. Using the family's personal Social Security number for payroll reporting is an error and creates privacy exposure.

The agent should confirm that the EIN has been obtained before any wage reporting is prepared and that it is used consistently on Schedule H, state unemployment filings, and W-2s issued to the household employee. The EIN is tied to the household as an employer and is distinct from any EIN the family may use for a business. If the family already has a business EIN, they generally need a separate household employer EIN unless the worker is employed by the business and assigned to the home.

### Withhold And Deposit Correctly

For Social Security and Medicare, the household employer withholds the employee share from the worker's wages and pays the employer share, and may choose to pay the employee share themselves rather than withholding it. Federal income tax withholding is optional at the employee's request, but if elected it must be done correctly. State income tax withholding rules vary and may be mandatory.

The practical question is whether to withhold during the year or pay the household employment taxes as a lump sum at year-end. Many household employers pay the full Social Security, Medicare, and FUTA liability on Schedule H with their personal tax return rather than making quarterly deposits, which is permitted when the taxes are paid through the estimated tax or year-end process. The agent must confirm the permitted payment path and avoid advising deposits that are not required while also ensuring the year-end liability is actually funded. Estimated tax payments or increased wage withholding at the family member's regular job can cover the household tax to avoid an underpayment penalty.

### Prepare Schedule H And Issue Form W-2

Household employment taxes are reported on Schedule H attached to the family's personal federal income tax return, and the household employee must receive a Form W-2 by the end of January showing wages and withheld taxes. The W-2 is filed with the Social Security Administration, and copies go to the employee and to state agencies as required.

The agent should confirm that Schedule H captures Social Security, Medicare, and FUTA, that the W-2 wages reconcile to Schedule H, and that state copies are filed where required. A common error is preparing Schedule H without issuing a W-2, or issuing a W-2 without filing the state copy. New hire reporting is also required in most states, meaning the household employer must report the new employee to the state new-hire registry, a step families frequently miss. The deliverable is the complete set, Schedule H, W-2 to the employee, W-2 filing with SSA, state filings, and new-hire reporting.

### Handle Special Worker Categories And Exclusions

Certain household workers are excluded from Social Security and Medicare even when wages exceed the threshold. Workers under age 18 are generally exempt unless household work is their principal occupation, and workers under 21 are exempt regardless of hours. A worker who is the employer's parent, spouse, or child is generally exempt from Social Security and Medicare and FUTA, though different rules apply to caregivers for a disabled or aged family member.

The agent must identify the relationship and age of the worker before applying the standard threshold analysis. Paying a parent to provide childcare does not trigger nanny taxes in the usual way, but paying a parent to care for a disabled or aged family member can. The exemptions are narrow and fact-specific, and the agent should not stretch them. When a worker qualifies for an exemption from Social Security and Medicare, confirm whether the exemption also covers FUTA and state unemployment, because the rules are not identical across taxes.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Cash Payment Avoids Employment Taxes

Paying a nanny in cash or through a personal payment app does not avoid Social Security, Medicare, FUTA, or state unemployment obligations. Cash wages count toward every threshold.

### Treating Every Domestic Worker As An Independent Contractor

A nanny, housekeeper, or caregiver whose work the family controls is an employee, not a contractor. The worker's preference or the use of a 1099 does not control classification.

### Applying One Threshold To All Taxes

Social Security and Medicare, FUTA, and state unemployment each have separate thresholds. Crossing one does not automatically trigger the others, and being below federal thresholds does not eliminate state obligations.

### Skipping The Employer Identification Number

A household employer needs an EIN for Schedule H and W-2 filing. Using the family's personal Social Security number for payroll reporting is an error and a privacy risk.

### Forgetting New Hire Reporting

Most states require household employers to report new employees to a state new-hire registry, a step families routinely miss even when they correctly file Schedule H and W-2s.

### Misapplying The Age And Relationship Exemptions

Workers under 18, under 21, and certain family members have narrow exemptions that do not apply uniformly across Social Security, Medicare, FUTA, and state unemployment. Each tax must be tested separately.

### Paying The Tax Only At Year-End Without Funding It

Household taxes are often paid on Schedule H at year-end, but if the liability is large and unfunded it can trigger underpayment penalties. Estimated payments or increased withholding should cover the expected amount.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have total cash wages been tested against the current-year Social Security and Medicare threshold before concluding nanny taxes apply?
- [ ] Has the worker been classified under the control test, with schedule, tools, other clients, substitution, and integration weighed rather than relying on payment method or worker preference?
- [ ] Has a tax-by-tax matrix been built for Social Security and Medicare, FUTA, state unemployment, and state income tax withholding, each with its own threshold?
- [ ] Has a household employer EIN been obtained and used for Schedule H, W-2s, and state filings rather than the family's personal Social Security number?
- [ ] Has the correct payment path been chosen, year-end Schedule H, estimated payments, or increased withholding, with the liability actually funded to avoid penalties?
- [ ] Is Schedule H prepared with reconciling W-2 wages, W-2 issued to the employee by the end of January, W-2 filed with SSA, and state copies filed as required?
- [ ] Has new hire reporting been completed with the state new-hire registry?
- [ ] Have age and relationship exemptions been tested separately for Social Security and Medicare, FUTA, and state unemployment rather than assumed to apply across the board?
- [ ] Has the agent avoided treating cash payment, a 1099, or worker preference as decisive of employee status?
