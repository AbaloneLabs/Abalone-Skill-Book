# Household Employee Thresholds

Household employment taxes are threshold-driven, and each tax has its own threshold measured differently. The single most common error in this area is treating all household employment taxes as a single on-or-off switch, when in fact Social Security and Medicare, federal unemployment tax, and state unemployment each turn on at different wage or quarter thresholds and have different exemptions. An agent who applies one threshold to all taxes will either overtax a family that owes nothing or, more dangerously, will conclude no tax is owed when state unemployment or FUTA actually applies.

The harm comes from conflating thresholds. A family that pays a nanny $2,500 in a year may be above the Social Security and Medicare threshold but below the FUTA threshold, or above both, depending on the year and the payment pattern. A senior caregiver who is a non-relative may trigger state unemployment from the first dollar while a relative caregiver may be exempt from Social Security and Medicare entirely. The agent's job is to test each tax against its own threshold and its own exemption set, and to re-test whenever wages, hours, or the worker's status change. Thresholds are also indexed, so a figure that was correct last year may be wrong this year.

This skill applies before advising on whether nanny taxes, FUTA, or state unemployment apply to a domestic worker, before setting up or declining household payroll, before applying age or relationship exemptions, and whenever wages, hours, or worker status change mid-year. It is U.S. federal income and employment tax oriented and does not replace review of specific facts by a qualified tax professional.

## Core Rules

### Test Social Security And Medicare Against The Annual Cash Wage Threshold

Social Security and Medicare taxes apply to a household employee only when cash wages paid in a calendar year reach a specific threshold, which is indexed for inflation and adjusted periodically. The threshold is a low-thousands figure that rises over time, so the agent must confirm the figure for the specific tax year rather than relying on memory or a prior year's number. Wages at or above the threshold are subject to Social Security and Medicare, and the employer withholds the employee share and pays the employer share.

The threshold is measured in cash wages per employer per calendar year. Non-cash items such as meals, lodging, bus fare, or tuition generally do not count toward the threshold. If a family employs two household workers, each worker's cash wages are tested separately against the threshold for that employer, so one worker could trigger the tax while the other does not. If a worker has multiple employers, each employer applies its own threshold independently. Do not aggregate across employers or across workers, and do not project partial-year wages forward. The test is actual cash wages paid in the calendar year, and once the threshold is crossed the wages above it become taxable.

### Apply The FUTA Threshold By Quarter And Annual Wages

Federal unemployment tax applies to household employees under a different test than Social Security and Medicare. FUTA applies when the employer paid cash wages of $1,000 or more in any quarter of the current or prior year, and the FUTA wage base is the first $7,000 of wages per employee per year. The agent must test both the quarterly trigger and the annual wage base.

The $1,000 quarterly test is the activation trigger. If in any single calendar quarter the family paid a household employee $1,000 or more, FUTA applies for that year and the next, regardless of whether later quarters fall below the threshold. A family that pays a housekeeper $1,200 in a summer quarter but nothing the rest of the year still triggers FUTA for that year. The wage base then caps the FUTA tax at the first $7,000 per employee, and the effective rate is reduced by a credit for state unemployment tax paid, which is why state unemployment compliance matters to the federal computation. Do not assume that being below the Social Security threshold means FUTA does not apply, because the FUTA quarterly test can be met even when annual wages are modest.

### Determine State Unemployment Thresholds Separately

State unemployment insurance for household employees has its own thresholds, wage bases, and rules, which differ from both Social Security and Medicare and from FUTA. Some states require coverage from the first dollar of wages, some apply a quarterly or annual threshold similar to but lower than FUTA, and some have special household-specific provisions. The wage base also varies widely by state.

The agent must identify the state where the work is performed, confirm that state's household unemployment threshold, and confirm the wage base and rate. Do not assume that a worker below the federal FUTA threshold is also below the state threshold, because state thresholds are frequently lower and can start at zero. Equally, do not assume that crossing the Social Security threshold automatically enrolls the employer in state unemployment, because the state may use a different test. State unemployment compliance is also what preserves the FUTA credit, so an employer who skips state filings can see their effective FUTA rate increase. Build the state threshold into the analysis as an independent test, not a derivative of the federal ones.

### Apply Age Exemptions With Their Conditions

Workers under age 18 are generally exempt from Social Security and Medicare for household work unless household employment is their principal occupation, meaning it is their main job. A high school student who babysits occasionally is exempt, while an 18-year-old whose primary job is nannapping may not be. Workers under age 21 are exempt from FUTA regardless of their occupation or hours, which is a broader exemption than the under-18 Social Security and Medicare rule.

The agent must apply each age exemption to the correct tax. The under-18 exemption applies to Social Security and Medicare and turns on principal occupation, while the under-21 exemption applies to FUTA and is unconditional. A 19-year-old nanny may be exempt from FUTA but subject to Social Security and Medicare if wages exceed the threshold, because the two exemptions do not align. Confirm the worker's age as of the tax year and test each exemption against each tax separately. Do not assume that one age exemption covers all household employment taxes, because the ages and conditions differ.

### Apply Relationship Exemptions Carefully

A worker who is the employer's parent, spouse, or child is generally exempt from Social Security and Medicare and FUTA for household work. The exemption is narrow and turns on the specific family relationship, and it does not extend to all relatives. A sibling, grandparent, or in-law providing household services is not automatically exempt under the parent-spouse-child rule.

There is an important exception for caregivers. A parent who cares for a child is exempt, but a parent or other relative who cares for an aged, disabled, or infirm family member may be subject to Social Security and Medicare, because the caregiver exception narrows the relationship exemption. The agent must identify both the relationship and the nature of the care. Paying a spouse to clean the home is exempt, but paying a parent to provide in-home care for a disabled adult child may not be. State unemployment rules for relatives are separate and often stricter, so a relative exempt federally may still trigger state unemployment. Test the relationship exemption for each tax and confirm the caregiver exception before concluding no tax applies.

### Re-Evaluate Thresholds When Wages Or Status Change Mid-Year

Household employment thresholds are measured over the calendar year, so a worker who starts below a threshold can cross it as wages accumulate. The agent must re-test the thresholds whenever additional wages are paid, whenever a worker's hours increase, whenever a second worker is hired, or whenever a worker's age or relationship status is reclassified.

A common failure is setting up payroll based on a projected annual wage that turns out low, then never revisiting the analysis when actual wages exceed the projection. A family that expects to pay a nanny $2,000 but actually pays $3,000 crosses the Social Security threshold mid-year and owes tax on the wages above the threshold from that point. Build a monitoring step into the advice, such as reviewing cumulative wages each quarter, so that crossing a threshold triggers the correct withholding and reporting rather than a year-end surprise. Thresholds also change annually with indexing, so a worker who was below the threshold last year may be above it this year at the same wage.

### Distinguish Cash Wages From Non-Cash Benefits In Every Threshold Test

The Social Security and Medicare threshold and the FUTA threshold are measured in cash wages, and non-cash benefits such as meals, lodging, transportation, or tuition provided to the household employee generally do not count toward those thresholds. This distinction matters when a family provides room and board to a live-in nanny or caregiver, because the cash wage portion may be below the threshold even though the total compensation package is larger.

The agent must separate cash wages from non-cash value before testing any threshold, and must then determine the taxability of the non-cash benefits separately. Lodging provided for the convenience of the employer on the employer's premises may be excludable, but the convenience test is strict and the value of non-cash items that do not qualify is taxable even if it does not count toward the threshold. Do not assume that a live-in arrangement with low cash wages avoids all household employment tax, because the non-cash value may be taxable wages even when the cash portion is below the threshold. State thresholds may also count non-cash value differently, so confirm the state's definition of wages.

## Common Traps

### Applying One Threshold To All Household Taxes

Social Security and Medicare, FUTA, and state unemployment each have separate thresholds. Crossing one does not trigger the others, and being below federal thresholds does not eliminate state obligations.

### Using A Prior-Year Threshold For The Current Year

The Social Security and Medicare cash wage threshold is indexed and rises over time. A figure that was correct last year may understate the current-year trigger.

### Missing The FUTA Quarterly Trigger

FUTA activates when cash wages reach $1,000 in any quarter of the current or prior year. A single high quarter triggers FUTA for the year even if other quarters are zero.

### Conflating The Under-18 And Under-21 Exemptions

The under-18 exemption applies to Social Security and Medicare and turns on principal occupation, while the under-21 exemption applies to FUTA and is unconditional. The two do not align and must be tested separately.

### Overextending The Relationship Exemption

The parent-spouse-child exemption is narrow and does not cover siblings, grandparents, or in-laws. The caregiver exception can also remove the exemption for relatives caring for aged or disabled family members.

### Ignoring Non-Cash Value In Live-In Arrangements

Cash wage thresholds do not count non-cash benefits, but non-cash value that fails the convenience-of-employer test is taxable wages even when the cash portion is below the threshold.

### Failing To Re-Test When Wages Accumulate

A worker who starts below a threshold can cross it mid-year. Without quarterly monitoring, the family discovers the obligation at year-end with back taxes and penalties.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have cash wages been tested against the current-year Social Security and Medicare threshold, confirmed for the specific tax year rather than a prior year?
- [ ] Has the FUTA $1,000 quarterly trigger been tested for every quarter of the current and prior year, and has the $7,000 wage base been applied?
- [ ] Has the state unemployment threshold, wage base, and rate been confirmed for the state where the work is performed, independently of the federal thresholds?
- [ ] Has each age exemption, under-18 for Social Security and Medicare and under-21 for FUTA, been tested separately against the correct tax with the principal-occupation condition applied?
- [ ] Has the relationship exemption been confined to parent, spouse, and child, with the caregiver exception checked for relatives caring for aged or disabled family members?
- [ ] Have cash wages been separated from non-cash benefits before testing each threshold, and has the taxability of any non-cash value been determined separately?
- [ ] Has a monitoring step been added so that crossing a threshold mid-year triggers correct withholding and reporting rather than a year-end surprise?
- [ ] Has each worker's wages been tested per employer per year, without aggregating across workers or across employers?
- [ ] Has the agent avoided treating a single threshold crossing or exemption as decisive for all household employment taxes?
- [ ] Has the agent reminded the user that this is general U.S. federal tax guidance, not tax advice, and that threshold computations, exemption disputes, and multi-state cases must be escalated to a qualified tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney?
