# Self-Employment Tax

Self-employment (SE) tax is the mechanism by which a self-employed individual pays Social Security and Medicare taxes equivalent to what an employer and employee pay combined. The combined rate is 15.3% — 12.4% for Social Security (capped at a wage base that is indexed annually, approximately $168,600 for 2024) and 2.9% for Medicare (no cap). The judgment problem is that SE tax is separate from income tax and applies to a different base, so an owner can owe substantial SE tax even with a low income-tax liability, and the choice of entity (sole proprietor, partnership, LLC, S corporation) determines how much owner income is exposed to it.

Agents frequently conflate SE tax with income tax, treat it as a flat surcharge on profit, or assume that forming an S corporation automatically eliminates it. The harm is underestimating total tax for a high-earning sole proprietor (who pays 15.3% on the first ~$168,600 of net earnings plus 2.9% above that, on top of income tax), or over-promising the S corporation strategy without understanding the reasonable compensation requirement. A second silent failure is missing that partners pay SE tax on their distributive share of partnership income and on guaranteed payments, while S corporation shareholders pay it only on W-2 wages — a structural difference that drives entity selection.

This skill covers the structure of SE tax, the Social Security wage base, the deduction for one-half of SE tax, and the S corporation reasonable compensation strategy. It is not tax advice; SE tax rules have detailed definitions of net earnings, exceptions for limited partners, real estate professionals, and church employees, and outcomes depend on facts that must be verified. Consult a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) before relying on any conclusion. Figures cited are 2024 baselines and change annually.

## Core Rules

### Separate SE Tax From Income Tax In Every Analysis

SE tax funds Social Security and Medicare; income tax funds general government. They are computed on related but distinct bases and reported on different forms. Net earnings from self-employment (the SE tax base) is roughly Schedule C profit multiplied by 0.9235 (to remove the employer-equivalent portion), while income tax is computed on taxable income after all deductions. A sole proprietor with $100,000 of Schedule C profit owes SE tax of approximately $14,130 (15.3% of $92,350) plus income tax on the profit (reduced by the one-half SE tax deduction and the QBI deduction).

The trap is treating the 15.3% as part of the income tax rate or forgetting it entirely when quoting a marginal rate. An agent analyzing a self-employed taxpayer's effective rate must layer both: income tax at the marginal bracket plus SE tax on the SE base. For a high-earning sole proprietor, the SE tax can rival or exceed the income tax, and any planning that ignores it understates the true cost. Always present SE tax and income tax as separate line items that combine into the total federal burden.

### Apply The Social Security Wage Base And The Medicare Split

The 12.4% Social Security component applies only up to the annual wage base (approximately $168,600 for 2024, indexed annually), while the 2.9% Medicare component applies to all net earnings with no cap. A self-employed individual who also has W-2 wages uses up the wage base with wages first, so SE income above the remaining base is subject only to the 2.9% Medicare portion. For example, a taxpayer with $168,600 of W-2 wages and $50,000 of SE profit pays no Social Security tax on the SE income (base already used) but pays 2.9% Medicare on it.

The wage-base interaction is a frequent source of error. An agent must determine how much of the Social Security wage base remains unused by W-2 wages before applying the 12.4% to SE income. The base is shared across all employment and self-employment of the individual, not computed separately. Conversely, a sole proprietor whose only earnings are from self-employment pays the full 15.3% on the first ~$168,600 and 2.9% above it. State the wage base, the amount used by wages, and the residual before computing the Social Security portion.

### Compute Net Earnings From Self-Employment Correctly

Net earnings from self-employment is the base for SE tax, and it is not simply Schedule C profit. It is net SE profit multiplied by 92.35% (0.9235), where net SE profit is the combined profit from all Schedule C activities (and certain other self-employment income). The 0.9235 factor removes the employer-equivalent portion of the tax, mirroring the deduction an employee gets for the employer share. If net SE profit is under $400, no SE tax return is required.

The trap is applying 15.3% directly to Schedule C profit rather than to the 0.9235-adjusted net earnings, which slightly overstates the tax. An agent should show the conversion explicitly: Schedule C profit, times 0.9235, equals net earnings; SE tax is 15.3% of net earnings up to the wage base and 2.9% above. Also confirm that the income is actually self-employment income — certain amounts (rental income, interest, dividends, capital gains) are not SE income even if received by a business owner, and a partner's distributive share of partnership income is SE income (subject to the limited partner exception) while a partner's return of capital is not.

### Deduct One-Half Of SE Tax As An Adjustment To Income

One-half of the SE tax is deductible as an above-the-line adjustment to income (on Schedule 1), reflecting the employer-equivalent portion. This deduction reduces adjusted gross income and thus income tax, but it does not reduce the SE tax itself. For a sole proprietor with $14,130 of SE tax, the deduction is approximately $7,065, which reduces taxable income and can improve QBI and other AGI-based computations.

The trap is forgetting the deduction when computing effective tax rates, or double-counting it by treating it as a business expense on Schedule C (it belongs on Schedule 1, not Schedule C). The one-half deduction is already baked into the 0.9235 factor used to compute net earnings, so the mechanics are internally consistent, but an agent presenting a total-tax picture must show the deduction reducing income tax separately from the SE tax increasing the burden. Do not present SE tax as a pure cost without the offsetting deduction.

### Understand The S Corporation Strategy And Its Reasonable Compensation Constraint

The principal SE-tax advantage of an S corporation is that only the W-2 wages paid to a shareholder-employee are subject to Social Security and Medicare taxes (paid as payroll tax, not SE tax); the remaining pass-through income distributed to the shareholder is not subject to SE tax. For a profitable service business, this can substantially reduce total tax compared to a sole proprietorship or partnership, where all net earnings are subject to SE tax. However, a shareholder who provides services must be paid reasonable compensation before distributions, and the IRS actively challenges zero- or low-wage S corporations.

Reasonable compensation is a facts-and-circumstances determination based on the shareholder's role, hours, services performed, what comparable businesses pay, and the relationship between wages and distributions. An agent should never bless a zero-wage arrangement for a working shareholder, nor assume any specific salary is reasonable without benchmarking. The reclassification risk is severe: the IRS recharacterizes distributions as wages, creating back payroll taxes, penalties, and interest. Flag any S corporation where the shareholder works in the business but takes little or no W-2 wages as a high-risk position requiring professional review.

### Determine Which Partner And LLC Income Is Subject To SE Tax

General partners pay SE tax on their distributive share of partnership ordinary income and on guaranteed payments for services. Limited partners generally pay SE tax only on guaranteed payments for services, not on their distributive share — but the limited partner exception is narrow and depends on state-law limited partner status plus lack of participation in control. A member of a multi-member LLC taxed as a partnership is generally treated like a general partner for SE tax purposes unless specific facts support limited partner status.

The trap is assuming all LLC income is SE-taxable or that none of it is. An agent must classify each owner's role (general vs. limited, active vs. passive) and determine whether their distributive share is SE income. Rental income is generally not SE income unless the LLC provides significant services to tenants, and a real estate professional's rental may still not be SE income if it is passive investment. Do not apply a blanket rule; examine the income source and the owner's role.

### Flag The Additional Medicare Tax At Higher Incomes

Above certain thresholds, an additional 0.9% Medicare tax applies to earned income (including self-employment earnings): $200,000 single, $250,000 married filing jointly, $125,000 married filing separately. This is separate from the 2.9% base Medicare and is not matched by an employer portion (the employer pays only the 1.45% base). For self-employed individuals, the additional 0.9% is computed on net SE earnings above the threshold, considering W-2 wages that use up the threshold first.

The trap is forgetting the additional 0.9% when quoting the top SE/Medicare rate for high earners, making it 3.8% (2.9% + 0.9%) rather than 2.9% above the wage base. An agent should layer the additional Medicare tax explicitly for high-earning self-employed clients and note that it is reported on Form 8959. The interaction with the NIIT (another 3.8% on investment income) means a high-earning owner can face a 3.8% surtax on earned income and a separate 3.8% on investment income.

## Common Traps

### Conflating SE Tax With Income Tax

SE tax funds Social Security and Medicare and is computed on net earnings; income tax is computed on taxable income. The trap is quoting a single rate or forgetting SE tax entirely, understating the total federal burden for self-employed clients.

### Applying 15.3% To Schedule C Profit Directly

Net earnings for SE tax is Schedule C profit times 0.9235, not the raw profit. The trap is applying 15.3% to the raw profit, slightly overstating the tax and missing the employer-equivalent adjustment.

### Ignoring The Social Security Wage Base When There Are Also W-2 Wages

The 12.4% Social Security component is capped at the annual wage base, shared across W-2 wages and SE earnings. The trap is applying the full 15.3% to all SE income when W-2 wages have already used part or all of the base, overstating the Social Security portion.

### Assuming An S Corporation Eliminates SE Tax Without Reasonable Compensation

The S corporation strategy reduces SE tax only on income paid as distributions, but a working shareholder must receive reasonable W-2 wages first. The trap is recommending a zero-wage S corporation, which the IRS recharacterizes with back payroll taxes and penalties.

### Treating All LLC Or Partnership Income As SE-Taxable

General partners pay SE tax on their distributive share, but limited partners generally do not, and rental income is often not SE income. The trap is applying a blanket SE tax to all LLC income regardless of owner role and income source.

### Forgetting The Additional 0.9% Medicare Tax At High Incomes

Above $200,000 single / $250,000 MFJ, an additional 0.9% applies to earned income including SE earnings. The trap is quoting 2.9% as the top Medicare rate for high earners when it is 3.8% including the additional tax.

### Missing The One-Half SE Tax Deduction In Effective Rate Analysis

One-half of SE tax is deductible above the line, reducing income tax. The trap is presenting SE tax as a pure cost without the offsetting deduction, overstating the effective rate.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The Social Security wage base (2024 approx. $168,600) has been applied, with any W-2 wages using up the base accounted for so the 12.4% applies only to the residual.
- [ ] Net earnings from self-employment has been computed as Schedule C profit times 0.9235, not as raw profit, and the 15.3% / 2.9% split has been applied correctly above the wage base.
- [ ] One-half of the SE tax has been deducted as an above-the-line adjustment on Schedule 1 (not as a Schedule C business expense).
- [ ] For S corporation shareholders, reasonable W-2 compensation has been required before distributions, and any zero- or low-wage arrangement has been flagged as a high-risk IRS challenge position.
- [ ] Partner and LLC member income has been classified by role (general vs. limited, active vs. passive) to determine SE tax on distributive shares and guaranteed payments.
- [ ] Rental and investment-type income has been excluded from the SE tax base unless significant services are provided.
- [ ] The additional 0.9% Medicare tax has been layered for earned income above $200,000 single / $250,000 MFJ (reported on Form 8959).
- [ ] The interaction between SE tax, the one-half deduction, and the QBI deduction has been reflected in any total-tax or effective-rate presentation.
