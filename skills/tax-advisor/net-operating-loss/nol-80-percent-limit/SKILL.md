# NOL 80 Percent Limit

The 80% of taxable income limitation on net operating loss (NOL) usage, enacted by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) for NOLs arising in tax years beginning after 2017, is the single most consequential change to NOL mechanics. It means a corporation or individual can never fully offset taxable income with a post-2017 NOL; at least 20% of taxable income remains subject to tax each year until the NOL is exhausted. The judgment problem is that agents treat the NOL as fully absorbing taxable income (as pre-2018 NOLs did at 100%) and understate the current-year tax by the 20% residual, which can be a large dollar amount for profitable years.

Agents frequently miss that the 80% base is computed before the NOL deduction and before the qualified business income (QBI) deduction, that the limitation creates a permanent partial disallowance in high-income years (not merely a deferral), and that the limit interacts with the alternative minimum tax and tax credits in ways that further constrain the effective benefit. The deeper blind spot is failing to plan around the limit: because the 80% cap means some taxable income always remains, accelerating or deferring income across years can change how much NOL is absorbed and how much tax is paid. A corporation that understands the limit can sequence income to maximize NOL usage; one that ignores it pays more tax than necessary.

This skill covers the 80% limitation computation, the base definition, permanent disallowance dynamics, interaction with AMT and credits, and planning around the limit. It is not tax advice; the limitation rules have detailed regulations, and a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Compute The 80% Limit On Taxable Income Before The NOL And QBI Deductions

The 80% limitation is applied to taxable income computed without the NOL deduction itself and without the qualified business income (QBI) deduction under Section 199A. The base is taxable income after most other deductions, including the dividends-received deduction, but before the NOL and QBI. This pre-NOL, pre-QBI base is the figure that the 80% cap is measured against. A corporation with $1,000,000 of taxable income (before NOL and QBI) and a $2,000,000 NOL carryforward can use $800,000 of the NOL (80% of $1,000,000), leaving $200,000 of taxable income and carrying forward $1,200,000 of NOL.

The base definition is critical because using the wrong base changes the allowed NOL. If the base were computed after the NOL deduction, the computation would be circular (the NOL reduces the base that limits the NOL). The regulations specify the before-NOL base to avoid circularity. Always state the base explicitly as taxable income before the NOL deduction and before QBI, and apply 80% to that figure. For individuals, the base is also before the QBI deduction and the NOL, with additional modifications for capital gains and other items per the NOL computation rules.

### Recognize The 80% Limit Creates Permanent Partial Disallowance In High-Income Years

Because the NOL can offset only 80% of taxable income, 20% of taxable income remains taxable each year the NOL is used. In a year with high taxable income, the 20% residual is a large dollar amount of tax that cannot be avoided with the NOL. This is not a deferral (the residual is not carried forward as a deduction); it is tax actually paid in that year. The NOL carryforward is reduced by the 80% used, and the remaining NOL carries forward, but the 20% residual tax is permanent for that year.

The permanent nature of the residual is the key insight. A corporation with $5,000,000 of taxable income and a large NOL pays tax on $1,000,000 (the 20% residual) regardless of the NOL size. Over multiple high-income years, the cumulative residual tax can be substantial. Model the NOL usage trajectory across years to see the total residual tax, rather than treating the NOL as eliminating tax. The 80% limit means a post-2017 NOL is inherently less valuable per dollar than a pre-2018 NOL (which had no limit), and this difference should be reflected in any valuation or planning analysis.

### Distinguish Post-2017 80% Usage From Pre-2018 100% Usage

Pre-2018 NOLs (arising in tax years beginning before January 1, 2018) are not subject to the 80% limitation; they can offset 100% of taxable income. Post-2017 NOLs are subject to the 80% limit. When a taxpayer holds both vintages, the pre-2018 NOLs are used first (they are more valuable and have finite life), fully absorbing taxable income up to their balance, and then the post-2017 NOL is applied to the remaining taxable income subject to the 80% limit on that remainder.

The sequencing interaction is subtle. If a corporation has $1,000,000 of taxable income, a $400,000 pre-2018 NOL, and a $2,000,000 post-2017 NOL, the pre-2018 NOL fully absorbs $400,000 (leaving $600,000), and then the post-2017 NOL can offset 80% of the remaining $600,000, or $480,000, leaving $120,000 taxable. The 80% limit applies to the taxable income after the pre-2018 NOL, not to the original taxable income. Confirm the vintage of each NOL and apply the correct limitation to each, sequencing pre-2018 before post-2017. A common error is applying the 80% limit to the total taxable income before the pre-2018 NOL, which understates the post-2017 NOL usage.

### Plan Income Timing To Maximize NOL Absorption

Because the 80% limit means some taxable income always remains, the timing of income recognition affects how much NOL is absorbed and how much residual tax is paid. In a year with lower taxable income, the 80% limit allows a smaller absolute NOL deduction (80% of a smaller base), but the residual 20% is also smaller. In a year with higher taxable income, more NOL is absorbed in absolute dollars, but the residual tax is larger. The optimal strategy depends on the NOL size, the income trajectory, and the marginal rate.

Planning levers include accelerating income into a year with a large NOL (to increase the base and absorb more NOL at the 80% cap), or deferring income out of a year with a small or expiring NOL. For example, a corporation with a large post-2017 NOL and a pending asset sale might accelerate the sale into the current year to generate taxable income that the NOL can absorb at 80%, rather than deferring the sale and letting the NOL linger. Conversely, a corporation with an expiring pre-2018 NOL should ensure sufficient taxable income exists to absorb it before expiration. Each planning move has tradeoffs (accelerating income may increase current tax on the 20% residual; deferring may delay cash) and must be modeled on the specific facts.

### Account For The Interaction With AMT And Tax Credits

The 80% NOL limitation interacts with the alternative minimum tax (for individuals, where applicable) and with tax credits in ways that can further constrain the effective benefit. For corporations subject to the corporate alternative minimum tax (CAMT), the NOL deduction interacts with the CAMT computation on adjusted financial statement income; the NOL may reduce regular tax but not fully reduce the CAMT base, leaving a minimum tax liability even with a large NOL. For individuals, the NOL does not reduce alternative minimum taxable income in the same way it reduces regular taxable income, which can create AMT exposure.

Tax credits (general business credits, research and development credits, etc.) are limited by the tax liability, and the NOL reduces taxable income and thus the tax liability against which credits can be applied. A corporation with a large NOL that reduces tax liability near zero may find that credits cannot be used currently (they carry forward), even though the NOL did not fully eliminate taxable income (due to the 80% limit). The interaction means the effective benefit of the NOL plus credits is constrained by both the 80% limit and the credit limitation. Model the NOL and credits together to see the combined effect on current tax.

### Model The NOL Usage Trajectory Over Multiple Years

Because the 80% limit extends NOL usage over multiple years, the analysis should model the trajectory: how much NOL is absorbed each year, how much residual tax is paid, and when the NOL is exhausted. A corporation with a $10 million post-2017 NOL and $2 million of annual taxable income absorbs $1.6 million per year (80% of $2 million), pays tax on $400,000 per year, and exhausts the NOL in roughly 7 years (with the final year using the remaining balance subject to the 80% limit on that year's income). If income grows, the absorption accelerates and the NOL is used faster, but the residual tax grows proportionally.

The trajectory model informs planning: a corporation expecting income growth may want to ensure the NOL is absorbed before it would otherwise expire (for finite pre-2018 vintages) or before rate changes reduce its value. A corporation with declining income may find the NOL lingers, extending the period of partial tax. Model multiple scenarios (base case, high-income, low-income) to understand the range of outcomes and the total residual tax over the NOL's life.

### Value The NOL Reflecting The 80% Constraint

For purposes of financial reporting, transaction pricing (in an acquisition where NOLs transfer subject to Section 382), or internal planning, the value of a post-2017 NOL must reflect the 80% limitation. A dollar of post-2017 NOL is worth less than a dollar of pre-2018 NOL (which has no limit) because it can never fully offset taxable income and because it is consumed over a longer period (time value of money). The discount depends on the projected income trajectory, the time to absorption, and the marginal tax rate.

A corporation valuing its NOL for an acquisition or restructuring must apply the 80% constraint in the valuation model, not assume 100% absorption. Similarly, a buyer acquiring a corporation with NOLs must model the 80% limit and any Section 382 limitation on post-ownership-change NOL usage. Overvaluing an NOL by ignoring the 80% limit overstates the transaction benefit and can lead to overpayment. Apply a present-value discount reflecting the usage trajectory and the permanent residual tax cost.

## Common Traps

### Applying 100% NOL Usage To A Post-2017 NOL

Post-2017 NOLs are limited to 80% of taxable income. Applying the pre-2018 100% usage rule overstates the deduction and understates the current tax by the 20% residual.

### Computing The 80% Base After The NOL Deduction

The base must be taxable income before the NOL deduction (and before QBI). Computing the base after the NOL creates a circular understatement of the allowed deduction.

### Treating The 20% Residual As A Deferral

The 20% residual is permanent tax paid in that year, not a deferred deduction. Modeling it as deferrable understates the total tax cost over the NOL's life.

### Applying The 80% Limit To Total Income Before The Pre-2018 NOL

When both vintages exist, the pre-2018 NOL is used first at 100%, and the 80% limit applies to the remaining income after the pre-2018 NOL. Applying 80% to the original income understates post-2017 usage.

### Ignoring The NOL And Credit Interaction

The NOL reduces tax liability, which limits the credits that can be used currently. Modeling the NOL without the credit interaction overstates the combined benefit.

### Overvaluing An NOL By Ignoring The 80% Limit

A post-2017 NOL is worth less than face value because of the 80% limit and the extended usage period. Valuing it at 100% of face overstates the benefit in transactions or planning.

### Forgetting CAMT Can Override The NOL Benefit For Large Corporations

For applicable corporations subject to CAMT, the NOL may reduce regular tax but not fully reduce the 15% book-income minimum. The NOL benefit can be eroded by CAMT.
