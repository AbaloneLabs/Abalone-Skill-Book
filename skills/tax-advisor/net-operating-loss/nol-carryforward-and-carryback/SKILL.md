# NOL Carryforward And Carryback

Net operating loss (NOL) carryforward and carryback rules changed dramatically under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) of 2017, and a single taxpayer can now hold NOLs from multiple eras that follow entirely different rules. The judgment problem is that agents apply one set of rules (usually the current post-TCJA indefinite carryforward) to all NOLs, including pre-2018 vintages that still follow the old 2-year carryback and 20-year carryforward with no 80% usage limit. Mixing vintages produces incorrect usage ordering, wrong expiration dates, and overstated or understated deductions.

Agents frequently miss that the CARES Act temporarily reopened a 5-year carryback for NOLs arising in 2018, 2019, and 2020 (a window that has now closed for new elections), that pre-2018 and post-2017 NOLs must be tracked and used in a specific order, and that state NOL rules diverge widely from federal (different carryback periods, different carryforward lives, different expiration). The deeper blind spot is treating an NOL as a single number rather than a portfolio of vintages, each with its own rules, usage limits, and expiration. A corporation with a $10 million NOL carryforward may actually hold three separate vintages with different usability, and aggregating them into one figure hides the expiration risk and the usage ordering.

This skill covers post-TCJA indefinite carryforward with the 80% limit, pre-2018 2-year carryback and 20-year carryforward, the closed CARES Act window, vintage tracking, and state NOL differences. It is not tax advice; NOL rules have detailed regulations and elections, and a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply Post-TCJA Indefinite Carryforward With The 80% Limitation

For NOLs arising in tax years beginning after December 31, 2017, the TCJA rules apply: the NOL carries forward indefinitely (no expiration) but can offset only 80% of taxable income in each carryforward year. There is no carryback for post-2017 NOLs under the general rule (with limited exceptions for farming businesses and certain insurance companies, which retain a 2-year carryback). The indefinite carryforward means the NOL never expires, but the 80% limit means the corporation can never fully zero out taxable income with the NOL; at least 20% of taxable income remains taxable each year.

The combination of indefinite life and the 80% limit creates a slow-burn usage pattern. A corporation with a $10 million post-2017 NOL and $2 million of annual taxable income uses $1.6 million per year (80% of $2 million), carrying the remaining $400,000 of taxable income as taxable and reducing the NOL by $1.6 million annually. The NOL is fully used in approximately 7 to 8 years, depending on income growth. Model the usage trajectory rather than assuming immediate absorption, and recognize that the 80% limit creates a permanent partial tax cost each year until the NOL is exhausted.

### Track Pre-2018 NOLs Under The Old 2-Year Carryback And 20-Year Carryforward

NOLs arising in tax years beginning before January 1, 2018 follow the pre-TCJA rules: a 2-year carryback (to the earliest of the two prior years first, then forward) and a 20-year carryforward, after which any unused NOL expires. Critically, pre-2018 NOLs are not subject to the 80% usage limitation; they can offset 100% of taxable income. This makes pre-2018 NOLs more valuable per dollar than post-2017 NOLs, because they fully absorb taxable income and do not leave a 20% residual.

A corporation holding both pre-2018 and post-2017 NOLs must use the pre-2018 NOLs first (they are used before post-2017 NOLs under the ordering rules), because the pre-2018 NOLs have a finite 20-year life and will expire, while post-2017 NOLs carry forward indefinitely. Failing to use an expiring pre-2018 NOL before a post-2017 NOL wastes the finite resource. Track the vintage year of each NOL component, its applicable rules (carryback, carryforward life, usage limit), and its expiration year. A pre-2018 NOL from 2017 carries forward 20 years and expires after 2037; a pre-2018 NOL from 2015 expires after 2035.

### Recognize The CARES Act 5-Year Carryback Window Has Closed

The CARES Act (2020) temporarily allowed NOLs arising in 2018, 2019, and 2020 to be carried back 5 years (instead of the TCJA's no-carryback rule), and it allowed the 80% limitation to be suspended for NOLs carried back to pre-2018 years. This created a valuable opportunity for corporations with losses in those years to carry back and claim refunds from profitable prior years. However, the election window for the 5-year carryback has closed; the deadline to file or waive the carryback was generally within the extended statute of limitations, which has passed for most taxpayers.

Agents should not advise a new 5-year carryback election for 2018-2020 NOLs unless a specific statute extension applies (rare). The CARES Act provision is largely historical for current planning. However, corporations that made the election and carried back should have claimed refunds and adjusted their NOL carryforward balances accordingly. Verify whether a prior CARES Act carryback was executed and reflected in the current NOL carryforward schedule; a carryback that was overlooked is a missed refund.

### Track Separate NOL Vintages With Their Rules And Expiration

An NOL carryforward is not a single number; it is a portfolio of vintages, each arising in a specific tax year and subject to the rules in effect for that year. A corporation may hold a 2016 NOL (pre-TCJA, 20-year life, expires 2036, no 80% limit), a 2019 NOL (post-TCJA, indefinite life, 80% limit, possibly carried back under CARES Act), and a 2023 NOL (post-TCJA, indefinite life, 80% limit). Each vintage must be tracked separately for usage ordering, limitation percentage, and expiration.

The NOL carryforward schedule should list each vintage year, the original NOL amount, the remaining balance, the applicable usage limit (100% for pre-2018, 80% for post-2017), the carryforward life (20 years for pre-2018, indefinite for post-2017), and the expiration year for finite vintages. Usage in each year is applied to the vintages in the correct order (pre-2018 first, then post-2017 by year). A schedule that aggregates all NOLs into one balance hides the expiration risk of finite vintages and the usage-limit differences. Reconcile the schedule annually.

### Apply The Correct NOL Usage Ordering

When a corporation has multiple NOL vintages, the usage order matters. The general rule is that NOLs are used in the order of the tax years in which they arose, earliest first. Pre-2018 NOLs (which expire) are used before post-2017 NOLs (which do not), to preserve the indefinite-life NOLs and avoid wasting finite ones. Within the pre-2018 group, the earliest vintage is used first; within the post-2017 group, the earliest vintage is used first.

This ordering is not optional; it follows the regulations and ensures that expiring NOLs are consumed before non-expiring ones. A corporation that applies a post-2017 NOL before a pre-2018 NOL that is near expiration may let the finite NOL expire unused, a permanent loss of deduction. Confirm the usage order each year and document which vintage was applied to the current year's taxable income. Where a corporation made a CARES Act carryback election, the carryback adjusted the vintage balances and the remaining carryforward must reflect the post-carryback amounts.

### Reconcile State NOL Differences From Federal

State NOL rules diverge significantly from federal, and assuming state conformity produces incorrect state tax. States differ in: carryback periods (some allow 2 or 3 years, some allow none), carryforward lives (some 5, 10, 15, or 20 years, some indefinite), usage limitations (some follow the 80% federal rule, some have no limit, some have different percentages), the definition of the NOL base (some start from federal taxable income, some have state-specific modifications), and whether the state decoupled from TCJA or CARES Act changes.

A corporation with a $5 million federal NOL carryforward may have a very different state NOL in each state where it operates, based on apportionment and state-specific rules. Some states may have no NOL carryforward at all (or a short life), meaning the state taxable income is higher than federal would suggest. Do not apply the federal NOL to state returns without confirming each state's NOL rules. Maintain separate state NOL carryforward schedules, with each state's carryback, carryforward life, usage limit, and apportionment-adjusted balance.

### Document NOL Elections And Waivers

Several NOL rules involve elections that must be made timely and documented. A corporation can elect to waive the carryback (for pre-2018 NOLs or where a carryback is allowed) and carry forward only, which may be advantageous if the prior years have low tax rates or expiring attributes. The CARES Act 5-year carryback involved an election and a waiver option. Elections to waive must be made by the deadline prescribed in the regulations, generally with the return for the loss year or an extended period.

Verify whether elections were made for each NOL year and whether they are reflected in the carryforward schedule. An election to waive a carryback that was intended but not timely filed can change the NOL's availability. Document the election status for each vintage, and where an election was not made, confirm the default treatment (carryback applied) was correctly computed and any refund claimed. NOL elections are time-sensitive and often irrevocable, so professional review is essential before relying on an election's effect.

## Common Traps

### Applying Post-TCJA Rules To Pre-2018 NOLs

Pre-2018 NOLs have a 2-year carryback, 20-year carryforward, and no 80% limit. Applying the indefinite carryforward and 80% limit to a pre-2018 NOL understates its value and misstates its expiration.

### Aggregating NOL Vintages Into A Single Number

NOLs from different years follow different rules. Aggregating them hides expiration risk, usage-limit differences, and the correct usage ordering. Track each vintage separately.

### Using Post-2017 NOLs Before Expiring Pre-2018 NOLs

Pre-2018 NOLs expire after 20 years; post-2017 NOLs carry forward indefinitely. Using the indefinite NOL first lets the finite one expire unused, a permanent loss.

### Advising A New CARES Act 5-Year Carryback After The Window Closed

The CARES Act 5-year carryback election window has closed for most taxpayers. Advising a new election without a specific statute extension is incorrect, though prior elections should be verified.

### Assuming State NOL Conformity To Federal

States differ widely in carryback, carryforward life, usage limits, and NOL base definition. Applying federal NOL rules to state returns without checking each state's rules produces incorrect state tax.

### Forgetting The 80% Limit Leaves A Permanent Partial Tax Cost

Post-2017 NOLs offset only 80% of taxable income, leaving 20% taxable each year. Modeling the NOL as fully absorbing taxable income overstates the deduction and understates tax.

### Overlooking NOL Elections And Waivers

Carryback waivers and other NOL elections are time-sensitive and often irrevocable. Failing to verify whether an election was made can misstate the NOL's availability and the carryforward balance.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have post-2017 NOLs been given indefinite carryforward with the 80% of taxable income usage limitation, and has the general no-carryback rule been applied (except farming and insurance exceptions)?
- [ ] Have pre-2018 NOLs been tracked under the 2-year carryback and 20-year carryforward rules with no 80% limitation, and have their expiration years been identified?
- [ ] Has the NOL carryforward been broken into separate vintages by year of origin, each with its applicable rules (carryback, carryforward life, usage limit) and expiration?
- [ ] Has the usage ordering been applied correctly, with pre-2018 (finite) NOLs used before post-2017 (indefinite) NOLs to avoid wasting expiring attributes?
- [ ] Has the CARES Act 5-year carryback window been confirmed as closed for new elections, with any prior 2018-2020 carryback elections verified and reflected in the carryforward schedule?
- [ ] Has the 80% limitation been modeled so that the NOL does not fully absorb taxable income, leaving a 20% residual taxable each year until the NOL is exhausted?
- [ ] Have state NOL rules been checked separately for each state, with carryback, carryforward life, usage limit, and apportionment-adjusted balances maintained on separate schedules?
- [ ] Have NOL elections and waivers (carryback waivers, CARES Act elections) been verified as timely filed and reflected in the vintage balances?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that NOL rules have detailed regulations and elections, this is not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) for the specific NOL portfolio?
