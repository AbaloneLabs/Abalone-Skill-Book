# Eligibility And HDHP Coupling

Health Savings Account (HSA) eligibility is not based on income, employment, or age; it is based entirely on health coverage, and the gate is strict and month-by-month. The judgment problem is that an agent can recommend a perfectly sized contribution that is nonetheless illegal because the taxpayer's coverage disqualifies them for one or more months, converting valid-looking contributions into excess contributions subject to a 6 percent excise tax. The coupling between the HSA and a qualifying High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) is the single most overlooked rule in health account planning. An agent who confirms the taxpayer has "a high-deductible plan" without testing it against the indexed statutory thresholds, or who clears the taxpayer's own coverage but ignores a spouse's general-purpose FSA, is building the recommendation on a false foundation.

The blind spots here are numerous and expensive. Agents routinely assume any plan labeled "high deductible" qualifies, but the IRS defines an HDHP by specific minimum deductible and maximum out-of-pocket limits that are indexed annually, and a plan that misses either threshold by a dollar disqualifies the taxpayer. They forget that disqualifying coverage is broader than the taxpayer's own plan, extending to a general-purpose FSA, an HRA that pays before the deductible, Medicare (including premium-free Part A), TRICARE, and a spouse's general-purpose FSA that can cover the taxpayer's expenses. They forget the last-month rule, which lets a taxpayer contribute a full year's amount based on December 1 eligibility but locks them into HDHP coverage through the next December 31, and that breaking that testing period converts the excess to income plus a 10 percent additional tax. They also forget that family versus self-only coverage, determined on the first day of each month, sets the contribution limit, and that mid-year coverage changes require proration. The harm is excess contributions, excise taxes, and surprise disqualification.

This skill covers HSA eligibility and the HDHP coupling requirement under U.S. federal tax law, focused on HDHP qualification thresholds, disqualifying coverage, the last-month rule and testing period, and the family-versus-self-only limit determination. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; HSA rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change frequently, and dollar amounts are indexed annually. A qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Test The Health Plan Against The Indexed HDHP Thresholds

An HDHP is not whatever an employer labels "high deductible"; it is a plan that satisfies specific statutory thresholds for the minimum annual deductible and the maximum annual out-of-pocket limit, both indexed annually. For 2024, an HDHP must have a minimum annual deductible of at least $1,600 for self-only coverage or $3,200 for family coverage, and a maximum annual out-of-pocket limit of $8,050 for self-only or $16,100 for family. For 2025, these thresholds rise to a $1,660 minimum deductible (self-only) and $3,320 (family), with out-of-pocket maximums of $8,300 (self-only) and $16,600 (family). A plan that fails either the minimum deductible or the maximum out-of-pocket test disqualifies HSA contributions entirely, even if the taxpayer believes it is high-deductible.

Never accept a plan name or marketing label as proof of HDHP status. Obtain the plan's Summary of Benefits and Coverage or the plan document, and test both thresholds for the specific coverage tier (self-only or family). A common failure is a plan with a deductible above the minimum but an out-of-pocket maximum that exceeds the cap, often because of embedded copays or a non-HSA-compliant design. Some plans are explicitly branded "HSA-eligible" or "HDHP" by the insurer, which is a strong signal, but the agent should still confirm the current-year thresholds because plans can change design annually. State the figures as illustrative for the cited year and require verification against the current year's indexed amounts before relying on them.

### Identify All Disqualifying Coverage, Including A Spouse's FSA

HSA eligibility requires not only an HDHP but also the absence of any disqualifying first-dollar coverage, and this is where most eligibility determinations fail. Disqualifying coverage includes a general-purpose health FSA, a health reimbursement arrangement (HRA) that pays medical costs before the deductible is met, Medicare (including premium-free Part A once enrolled), TRICARE, being claimed as a dependent on another person's return, and any other plan that pays medical expenses before the HDHP deductible is satisfied. The rule is strict and applies even if the disqualifying coverage has a zero balance or is never used.

The most insidious disqualifier is a spouse's general-purpose FSA. Because a spouse's FSA can cover the taxpayer's medical expenses, it counts as disqualifying coverage for the taxpayer even though the taxpayer did not elect it. Check both spouses' coverage before clearing HSA eligibility. The safe alternatives are a limited-purpose FSA (dental and vision only) or a post-deductible FSA, which do not provide the disqualifying first-dollar medical coverage and can pair with an HSA. Confirm the FSA type in the plan document, because a plan labeled "FSA" may be general-purpose by default. Also note that a dependent child's coverage under a family HDHP does not by itself disqualify the parent, but a separate general-purpose FSA covering the child can.

### Apply Eligibility Month By Month On The First Day Of The Month

HSA eligibility is determined for each month separately, based on coverage held on the first day of that month. A taxpayer is eligible for a given month if, on the first day, they are covered by an HDHP and have no disqualifying coverage. This means a taxpayer who switches from a PPO to an HDHP on July 15 becomes eligible for August, not July, because they did not hold the HDHP on July 1. Conversely, a taxpayer who enrolls in Medicare effective July 1 loses eligibility for July and every month after, even if they were HDHP-covered for the first six months.

The month-by-month rule makes mid-year coverage changes a proration problem. A taxpayer who holds self-only HDHP coverage for January through June and family HDHP coverage for July through December does not get to use the higher family limit for the full year; the contribution is prorated by the number of months in each coverage tier, unless the last-month rule applies. Compute eligibility for each month, identify the coverage tier for each eligible month, and sum the prorated limits before recommending any annual contribution amount. Never annualize a partial-year eligibility without applying the proration math.

### Apply The Last-Month Rule And Its Testing Period Carefully

The last-month rule is a special provision that lets a taxpayer contribute the full annual limit for the year if they are HSA-eligible on the first day of the last month of the tax year, December 1 for calendar-year taxpayers, regardless of when eligibility began during the year. This is valuable for a taxpayer who becomes HDHP-eligible late in the year, because it lets them fund the full year's limit rather than a prorated fraction. For 2024, a taxpayer who becomes eligible on December 1 could contribute the full $4,150 (self-only) or $8,300 (family) rather than one-twelfth of that amount.

The catch is the testing period. A taxpayer who uses the last-month rule must remain HSA-eligible (HDHP coverage, no disqualifying coverage) through the last day of the 12th month following the end of the tax year, which is December 31 of the following year. If the testing period is broken, for example by enrolling in Medicare or dropping the HDHP mid-year, the contributions that exceeded the prorated amount are included in income and subject to a 10 percent additional tax (not the 20 percent penalty on non-medical withdrawals, but a specific testing-period penalty). Flag the testing period whenever a late-year contribution is made, and warn the taxpayer that the commitment extends into the next calendar year. The last-month rule is a powerful tool but a multi-year obligation.

### Determine The Contribution Limit From The Coverage Tier, Not Headcount

The contribution limit is determined by the HDHP coverage tier, self-only or family, held on the first day of each month, not by the number of family members covered. For 2024, self-only HDHP coverage yields a $4,150 limit and family HDHP coverage yields an $8,300 limit, plus a $1,000 catch-up for age 55 or older, rising to $4,300 self-only and $8,550 family for 2025. A family HDHP is one that covers the taxpayer and at least one other person (spouse or dependent); covering two parents and three children under one family plan still produces a single $8,300 family limit, not five separate limits.

The family limit is a shared pool among all HSA-eligible family members, not a per-person allowance. Two spouses covered under one family HDHP split the $8,300 (2024) or $8,550 (2025) between their separate HSAs in any proportion, and each spouse who is 55 or older can add the $1,000 catch-up to their own share. This is because an HSA is individually owned, so the catch-up attaches to the person, not the family pool. Compute the family limit as one pool, allocate it among eligible family members, and add the catch-up per qualifying person. Never double the family limit by treating it as per-person.

### Coordinate Medicare Enrollment As The Hard Stop On Contributions

Medicare enrollment is the hard stop on HSA contributions, and the timing interacts badly with Social Security claiming. Enrolling in any part of Medicare, including premium-free Part A, ends HSA eligibility from the first month of coverage. Because Social Security benefits claimed at or after age 65 typically require concurrent Medicare Part A enrollment, a taxpayer who claims Social Security at 65 cannot continue HSA contributions. Worse, Medicare Part A can be retroactive for up to six months prior, which retroactively disqualifies contributions made in those months and converts them into excess contributions subject to the 6 percent excise tax.

Anyone approaching age 65 must time Social Security, Medicare, and HSA contributions together. A taxpayer who delays Medicare to keep working under an employer HDHP (and whose employer plan is primary under the working-aged rules) can continue contributing, including the $1,000 catch-up, but must stop contributions six months before the month they enroll in Medicare to avoid the retroactive excess. Note that Medicare ends contributions but does not end spending; the existing HSA balance remains a tax-free spending vehicle for Medicare premiums and all qualified expenses for life. Never tell a 65-year-old to keep maxing the HSA without first asking about Medicare and Social Security status.

### Correct Excess Contributions Before The Return Due Date

When eligibility is misjudged and excess contributions result, the taxpayer faces a 6 percent excise tax on the excess for each year it remains in the account, and the problem compounds if not corrected. The correction is to withdraw the excess contribution plus any net income attributable to it before the tax return due date (including extensions) for the year the excess was made. A timely corrected withdrawal is not included in income and avoids the excise tax, though the earnings portion withdrawn is taxable as other income.

Prevention is far better than correction. Confirm eligibility month by month, test the HDHP thresholds, clear all disqualifying coverage including a spouse's FSA, and apply the proration or last-month rule before recommending any contribution amount. If a mid-year event changes eligibility, recompute the allowable contribution immediately and adjust remaining payroll contributions to avoid building up excess. For excess that is discovered after the return due date, the only remedy is to withdraw the excess in a later year (counting it against that year's limit) and pay the 6 percent excise tax each intervening year. Flag the correction mechanics whenever eligibility is uncertain or changes during the year.

## Common Traps

### Trusting The Plan Label Instead Of The Statutory Thresholds

The symptom is clearing HSA eligibility because the employer calls the plan a "high-deductible health plan." The trap is that HDHP status is defined by indexed minimum deductible and maximum out-of-pocket limits, and a plan that misses either threshold by a dollar disqualifies contributions. The direction is to obtain the Summary of Benefits and Coverage and test both thresholds against the current-year figures before clearing eligibility.

### Clearing The Taxpayer's Own Coverage But Ignoring The Spouse's FSA

The symptom is confirming the taxpayer has an HDHP and no personal FSA, then recommending contributions. The trap is that a spouse's general-purpose FSA can cover the taxpayer's expenses and therefore disqualifies the taxpayer's HSA contributions even with a zero balance. The direction is to check both spouses' coverage, including any general-purpose FSA, before clearing eligibility.

### Annualizing A Partial-Year Eligibility Without Proration

The symptom is telling a taxpayer who became HDHP-eligible mid-year that they can contribute the full annual limit. The trap is that eligibility is month-by-month, so a mid-year start yields a prorated limit unless the last-month rule applies, and over-contributing triggers the 6 percent excise tax. The direction is to compute the limit by prorating across eligible months or applying the last-month rule explicitly.

### Using The Last-Month Rule Without Flagging The Testing Period

The symptom is contributing a full year's amount based on December 1 eligibility without mentioning the obligation. The trap is that the testing period requires HDHP coverage through the next December 31, and breaking it converts the excess to income plus a 10 percent additional tax. The direction is to flag the testing period as a multi-year commitment whenever the last-month rule is used.

### Treating The Family Limit As A Per-Person Allowance

The symptom is telling two spouses they can each contribute the full family limit. The trap is that the family HDHP limit is one shared pool split between eligible spouses, so doubling it over-contributes and triggers the 6 percent excise tax. The direction is to compute the family limit as a single pool and allocate it between separate HSAs, adding the catch-up per qualifying person.

### Forgetting Retroactive Medicare Part A Ends Prior Contributions

The symptom is telling a 65-year-old to keep contributing until they formally enroll in Medicare. The trap is that retroactive Part A can disqualify up to six prior months, converting contributions made in those months into excess subject to the 6 percent excise tax. The direction is to stop contributions six months before the planned Medicare enrollment month.

### Assuming Medicare Enrollment Makes The HSA Useless

The symptom is telling a Medicare enrollee their HSA is now dead money. The trap is that Medicare ends contributions but the existing balance remains a tax-free spending vehicle for Medicare premiums and all qualified expenses for life. The direction is to reframe the post-Medicare account as a spending vehicle, not a dead account.
