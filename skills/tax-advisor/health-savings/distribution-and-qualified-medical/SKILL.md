# Distribution And Qualified Medical Expense

Health Savings Account (HSA) distributions are where the triple tax advantage either pays off or collapses, because the custodian does not verify eligibility at withdrawal and the taxpayer self-certifies and bears the full audit risk. The judgment problem is that a distribution that looks like a routine reimbursement can be reclassified years later as taxable income plus a 20 percent additional tax if the expense was not qualified, was incurred before the account was established, was already reimbursed, or was already deducted on Schedule A. An agent who treats withdrawals as automatic and self-evident, without testing each expense against the IRC section 213(d) standard and the substantiation rules, is creating penalty exposure that can surface long after the money is spent.

The blind spots here are numerous and expensive. Agents routinely forget that the HSA custodian never checks whether a withdrawal is qualified, so the taxpayer's self-certification is the only gate, and the IRS can demand substantiation years later with no statute of limitations on the underlying qualification question. They forget that there is no time limit on reimbursing a past qualified expense, which is the foundation of the long-term strategy, but only if the expense was incurred after the HSA was established and the receipt survives. They forget that non-qualified distributions before age 65 are taxable as income plus a 20 percent additional tax, and that after 65 the penalty is waived but the income tax remains, so non-medical withdrawals are never fully tax-free. They also forget that an expense already deducted as an itemized medical deduction on Schedule A cannot be reimbursed tax-free from the HSA, because that double-counts the tax benefit. The harm is reclassified withdrawals, the 20 percent penalty, and lost tax savings from double-dipping.

This skill covers HSA distributions and the qualified medical expense determination under U.S. federal tax law, focused on the IRC section 213(d) standard, the no-time-limit reimbursement rule, non-qualified distributions and the 20 percent penalty, the recordkeeping strategy, and coordination with itemized medical deductions. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; HSA rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change frequently, and dollar amounts are indexed annually. A qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Every Distribution On The IRC Section 213(d) Standard

The single governing standard for a qualified medical expense is IRC section 213(d), which the IRS explains in detail in Publication 502. An expense qualifies if it is primarily for the diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease, or for the purpose of affecting any structure or function of the body. This covers doctor and hospital services, prescription drugs, dental and vision care, diagnostic devices, and medically necessary equipment. It excludes anything primarily for general health, cosmetic purposes, or personal comfort, even if the item is health-adjacent. Because the HSA custodian does not verify qualification at withdrawal, the taxpayer applies this standard themselves and bears the audit risk.

Apply the standard to the specific expense, not to a category label. A prescribed CPAP machine qualifies because it treats a diagnosed condition, but a general-purpose air purifier bought for comfort does not. Insulin and prescribed reading glasses qualify; vitamins taken for general wellness and cosmetics do not. The 2020 repeal of the prescription requirement means over-the-counter medicines and drugs, plus menstrual care products, now qualify without a prescription, but general wellness and personal care products still do not. When an expense is ambiguous, require documentation of medical necessity, such as a physician's letter, and default to conservative treatment when in doubt. Never treat a withdrawal as qualified solely because a retailer labels the item HSA-eligible, because retailer labeling is marketing, not a legal determination.

### Exploit The No-Time-Limit Rule With Permanent Receipt Retention

A defining feature of the HSA, and the foundation of the long-term strategy, is that there is no time limit on reimbursing a qualified medical expense, provided the expense was incurred after the HSA was established and the receipt is preserved. A taxpayer who pays a $2,000 surgery out of pocket in 2025 can reimburse themselves from the HSA in 2045, tax-free, as long as the receipt survives and the expense was never otherwise reimbursed or deducted. This converts every unreimbursed qualified expense into a stored tax-free withdrawal option that can be exercised whenever the taxpayer wants cash from the account, while the invested balance continues to compound.

The rule has two hard conditions that agents routinely miss. First, the expense must have been incurred after the HSA was established, meaning after the account was opened, not after the taxpayer became eligible; an HSA opened in November cannot reimburse a surgery performed in January of the same year even if the taxpayer was eligible all year. Second, the receipt must be retained indefinitely, because the IRS can challenge any withdrawal lacking substantiation regardless of how much time has passed. Acceptable documentation includes itemized receipts identifying the service or item, the date, and the patient, Explanation of Benefits statements showing the patient-paid portion, pharmacy receipts identifying the medication, and prescriptions or letters of medical necessity. A credit card receipt showing only a merchant name and total is insufficient. Recommend a permanent digital archive of receipts matched to a log of unreimbursed expenses before relying on the no-time-limit rule.

### Treat Non-Qualified Distributions By Age And Penalty Correctly

Not every HSA withdrawal is qualified, and the consequences depend sharply on the taxpayer's age. A non-qualified distribution, meaning a withdrawal used for a non-qualified expense, is taxable as ordinary income in all cases. Before age 65, it is also subject to a 20 percent additional tax on top of the income tax, which makes early non-medical withdrawals punitive. After age 65, the 20 percent additional tax is waived, so a non-qualified distribution is taxed as income only, identical to a withdrawal from a traditional IRA. This is why the post-65 HSA is sometimes called a stealth retirement account.

The trap is overstating the post-65 benefit. Non-medical withdrawals after 65 are penalty-free but not tax-free; they remain taxable as income, unlike qualified medical withdrawals which are fully tax-free at any age. Only qualified medical withdrawals deliver the tax-free outcome, before or after 65. Frame the post-65 account as tax-free for medical spending and tax-deferred for non-medical spending, never as universally tax-free. Also note that the 20 percent penalty does not apply to distributions after death, disability, or for certain exceptions, but for the ordinary taxpayer the age-65 line is the key threshold. Match every withdrawal to a qualified expense, or reframe it explicitly as a taxable (and pre-65 penalized) event.

### Treat Insurance Premiums As Generally Excluded With Narrow Exceptions

The most common distribution error is reimbursing health insurance premiums from the HSA, because taxpayers reasonably assume that premiums, being medical costs, must be reimbursable. In general, premiums for health insurance, including employer-sponsored plan premiums, ACA marketplace premiums, and COBRA premiums, are not qualified medical expenses for HSA reimbursement. A taxpayer who reimburses their monthly premium from the HSA has made a non-qualified distribution that is taxable plus the 20 percent additional tax if they are under 65.

The narrow exceptions must be stated precisely. Qualified long-term care insurance premiums are qualified up to age-based limits. Premiums for health care continuation coverage required by law, such as COBRA, and premiums paid while receiving unemployment compensation, can qualify. And for individuals age 65 and older, Medicare premiums, including Part B, Part D, and Medicare Advantage premiums, plus Medigap, are qualified expenses for HSA reimbursement. Note that enrolling in Medicare ends HSA contribution eligibility, but it does not end the ability to spend existing HSA funds on Medicare premiums tax-free. Always test a premium against these exceptions before approving or denying it, and never approve a general health plan premium reimbursement.

### Enforce The Incurred-After-Establishment Timing Rule

A qualified expense must have been incurred after the HSA was established, meaning after the account was opened, not after the taxpayer became eligible or after the first contribution. This rule is strict and is a frequent source of denied reimbursements, especially for taxpayers who open their HSA late in the year after incurring early-year medical costs. An HSA opened in November cannot reimburse a surgery performed in January of the same year, even if the taxpayer was HDHP-eligible all year, because the expense predates the account's establishment.

The timing rule interacts with the no-time-limit rule in a way that creates both opportunity and risk. There is no deadline to reimburse, so a taxpayer can pay out of pocket now and reimburse from the HSA years later, but only for expenses incurred after the account was established. Track the HSA establishment date as a hard cutoff, and reject any expense that predates it. For taxpayers who delay opening the HSA, warn them that early-year expenses incurred before the account existed are permanently unreimbursable, and recommend opening the account as early as possible in the eligibility period to capture the widest window of reimbursable expenses.

### Avoid Double-Dipping With Itemized Medical Deductions

A qualified expense cannot be reimbursed tax-free from the HSA if it was already deducted as an itemized medical expense on Schedule A, because that would double-count the tax benefit. This rule catches taxpayers who reimburse an expense from the HSA that they had already claimed on Schedule A in a prior year. The cross-check is essential whenever the taxpayer has itemized deductions in any year, because medical expenses above the 7.5 percent adjusted gross income floor create the double-dip problem.

Apply the cross-check carefully. Only the portion of the expense actually deducted creates the double-dip; an expense that was included in the Schedule A medical total but did not exceed the 7.5 percent AGI floor was not deducted and can still be reimbursed from the HSA. Confirm whether the taxpayer itemized in the relevant year, recompute the actual deducted amount against the AGI floor, and default to not reimbursing any expense that was anywhere near a prior-year Schedule A medical deduction. Also confirm the expense was the taxpayer's out-of-pocket cost after insurance and was not reimbursed by an FSA or HRA, because an already-reimbursed expense cannot be reimbursed again. When in doubt, treat the expense as non-reimbursable.

### Require Substantiation For Every Tax-Free Withdrawal

Every tax-free HSA withdrawal must be backed by documentation sufficient to prove the expense was qualified, was incurred by the account holder or an eligible family member after the HSA was established, and was not otherwise reimbursed or deducted. Because the custodian does not verify eligibility at withdrawal, the taxpayer self-certifies and bears the full audit risk, and the IRS can request substantiation years later. This makes disciplined recordkeeping essential rather than optional.

Maintain a dedicated file of receipts matched to each HSA distribution, retain EOBs that show the patient-paid portion, and keep proof that the expense was not reimbursed by insurance or another account. For the long-term reimbursement strategy, where the taxpayer pays out of pocket now and reimburses from the HSA years later, the receipts must be preserved indefinitely. Recommend a permanent digital archive with a log tracking each unreimbursed expense, the date incurred, the amount, the patient, and the receipt location. Never promise a tax-free withdrawal to a taxpayer who will not maintain the records, because an unsubstantiated withdrawal is reclassified as non-qualified and taxed plus the 20 percent penalty (or taxed as income after 65).

## Common Traps

### Reimbursing Health Insurance Premiums From The HSA

The symptom is using HSA funds to pay the monthly health plan premium. The trap is that general health insurance premiums are not qualified HSA expenses, so the withdrawal is non-qualified, taxable as income, and subject to the 20 percent additional tax if the taxpayer is under 65. The direction is to reserve HSA premium reimbursement for the narrow exceptions, such as Medicare premiums at 65 plus, long-term care premiums within age limits, and COBRA during unemployment.

### Treating All Pharmacy Aisle Items As Qualified

The symptom is reimbursing sunscreen, vitamins, cosmetics, or protein powder because they came from a pharmacy. The trap is that only over-the-counter medicines and drugs qualify without a prescription post-2020, while general wellness and personal care products never qualified. The direction is to separate qualifying OTC medicines from non-qualifying personal care items on every pharmacy receipt and test each against the 213(d) standard.

### Spending HSA Funds Without Keeping Receipts

The symptom is making HSA withdrawals and assuming the custodian's lack of verification means no proof is needed. The trap is that the custodian does not check, but the IRS can demand substantiation years later, and a withdrawal without a matching receipt is reclassified as non-qualified and taxed plus the 20 percent penalty. The direction is to retain itemized receipts and EOBs for every distribution in a permanent archive.

### Reimbursing An Expense Incurred Before The HSA Was Established

The symptom is reimbursing an early-year surgery from an HSA opened later that year. The trap is that the incurred-after-establishment rule disallows any expense predating the account's opening, even if the taxpayer was eligible all year. The direction is to track the HSA establishment date as a hard cutoff and reject expenses incurred before it.

### Double-Dipping With A Prior Schedule A Deduction

The symptom is reimbursing an expense from the HSA that was already deducted as an itemized medical expense. The trap is that this double-counts the tax benefit and the expense is no longer qualified for HSA reimbursement. The direction is to cross-check against prior-year Schedule A medical deductions, applying the 7.5 percent AGI floor, before reimbursing.

### Treating Non-Medical Post-65 Withdrawals As Tax-Free

The symptom is describing the HSA after age 65 as a tax-free account for any purpose. The trap is that only qualified medical withdrawals are tax-free after 65, while non-medical withdrawals are penalty-free but still taxable as income, like a traditional IRA. The direction is to frame the post-65 account as tax-free for medical spending and tax-deferred for non-medical spending, never as universally tax-free.

### Approving Expenses By Retailer Label Alone

The symptom is approving a reimbursement because the retailer's website marks the item HSA-eligible. The trap is that retailer labeling is a marketing convenience, not a legal determination, and some items are mislabeled or only conditionally eligible. The direction is to test every item against the IRC section 213(d) and Publication 502 standard regardless of the retailer's tag.
