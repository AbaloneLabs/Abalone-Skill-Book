# Eligible Expense And Documentation

Determining whether a specific medical cost qualifies for tax-free reimbursement from a Health Savings Account (HSA) or Flexible Spending Arrangement (FSA) is a documentation and scope judgment problem, not a matter of intuition. The governing standard is IRC section 213(d), operationalized by IRS Publication 502, and the scope is narrower than most taxpayers assume. The judgment problem is that the line between a qualified medical expense and a personal, disallowed cost is often blurry, and getting it wrong converts a tax-free withdrawal into taxable income plus a 20% penalty (for HSAs before age 65) or a disallowed claim (for FSAs). An agent who approves reimbursements by category label, such as "supplements" or "gym membership," without testing the expense against the Publication 502 standard and the substantiation rules, is creating audit exposure and penalty risk.

The blind spots here are numerous and expensive. Agents routinely forget that insurance premiums are generally not qualified expenses for HSA or FSA reimbursement, with narrow exceptions, and that this trips up taxpayers who try to reimburse their monthly health plan premium. They forget that the pre-2020 rule requiring a prescription for over-the-counter medicines was repealed, so OTC drugs like pain relievers and allergy medicines now qualify without a prescription, but they conflate this with all OTC items and approve things like cosmetic products or general wellness goods that do not qualify. They forget that Medicare premiums become a qualified expense for individuals age 65 and older, that expenses must be incurred after the HSA was established, and that every tax-free withdrawal must be backed by contemporaneous documentation that survives scrutiny. The harm is disallowed withdrawals reclassified as taxable income, the 20% additional tax on non-qualified HSA distributions, and forfeited FSA claims.

This skill covers the eligible-expense determination and the documentation required to support tax-free HSA and FSA withdrawals under U.S. federal tax law. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice. Health account rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, the Publication 502 scope and the OTC expansion are tied to current law, and dollar amounts and age thresholds must be verified against the current tax year. A qualified tax professional, CPA, or benefits specialist must be consulted before relying on any reimbursement determination.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Every Determination On The Publication 502 Standard

The single governing standard for a qualified medical expense is IRC section 213(d), which the IRS explains in detail in Publication 502. An expense qualifies if it is primarily for the diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease, or for the purpose of affecting any structure or function of the body. This covers a wide band including doctor and hospital services, prescription drugs, dental and vision care, diagnostic devices, and medically necessary equipment. It excludes anything primarily for general health, cosmetic purposes, or personal comfort, even if the item is health-adjacent.

Apply the standard to the specific expense, not to a category label. For example, a prescribed CPAP machine qualifies because it treats a diagnosed condition, but a general-purpose air purifier bought for comfort does not. A medically necessary weight-loss program prescribed by a doctor to treat a diagnosed disease qualifies, but a commercial diet program joined for general wellness does not. Crutches, insulin, and reading glasses prescribed by an optometrist qualify; vitamins taken for general health, toothpaste, and cosmetics do not. When an expense is ambiguous, require documentation of medical necessity, such as a physician's letter of medical necessity, and default to conservative treatment when in doubt. Never approve an expense solely because a retailer labels it FSA-eligible.

### Apply The Pre-2020 Over-The-Counter Expansion Correctly

Before 2020, over-the-counter medicines required a prescription to qualify as a medical expense, which effectively blocked reimbursement for common items like pain relievers, cold medicines, and allergy drugs. The CARES Act of 2020 repealed that prescription requirement permanently, so OTC medicines now qualify for HSA and FSA reimbursement without a prescription. This includes items like acetaminophen, ibuprofen, antacids, and allergy medications purchased off the shelf. Menstrual care products were also added as qualified expenses at the same time, including tampons, pads, cups, and similar products.

The trap is over-generalizing the expansion. The repeal applies to medicines and drugs, not to every health-adjacent product on a pharmacy shelf. Cosmetics, sunscreen (unless prescribed), general toiletries, vitamins and supplements taken for general wellness, and sports nutrition products still do not qualify even though they sit in the same aisle. Distinguish OTC medicines, which now qualify without a prescription, from general wellness and personal care products, which never qualified. When a taxpayer submits a pharmacy receipt, separate the qualifying OTC medicines from the non-qualifying personal care items on the same receipt before approving reimbursement.

### Treat Insurance Premiums As Generally Excluded With Narrow Exceptions

The most common reimbursement error is treating health insurance premiums as qualified HSA or FSA expenses. In general, premiums for health insurance, including employer-sponsored plan premiums, ACA marketplace premiums, and COBRA premiums, are not qualified medical expenses for HSA or FSA reimbursement. A taxpayer who reimburses their monthly premium from the HSA has made a non-qualified distribution that is taxable plus the 20% additional tax if they are under 65. This rule surprises taxpayers who reasonably assume that because premiums are medical costs, they must be reimbursable.

The narrow exceptions matter and should be stated precisely. Qualified long-term care insurance premiums are deductible as medical expenses up to age-based limits, and can be reimbursed from an HSA within those limits. Premiums for health care continuation coverage required by law, such as COBRA, and premiums paid while receiving unemployment compensation, can qualify. And for individuals age 65 and older, Medicare premiums, including Part B, Part D, and Medicare Advantage premiums, are qualified expenses for HSA reimbursement. Note that enrolling in Medicare ends HSA contribution eligibility, but it does not end the ability to spend existing HSA funds on Medicare premiums tax-free. Always test a premium against these exceptions before approving or denying it.

### Permit Medicare Premiums For Age 65 And Older

For individuals who have reached age 65, Medicare premiums become a qualified medical expense for HSA reimbursement, and this is a valuable but frequently overlooked use of accumulated HSA balances. This includes Medicare Part B premiums, Part D prescription drug coverage premiums, and Medicare Advantage premiums, all of which can be paid tax-free from the HSA. It also includes Medicare Supplement (Medigap) premiums. This creates a natural use for HSA funds in retirement, when the taxpayer is no longer contributing but is drawing on the balance for current medical costs.

The interaction with contribution eligibility must be stated clearly to avoid contradiction. Enrolling in Medicare Part A ends HSA contribution eligibility from the first month of coverage, but it does not prevent spending existing HSA funds on qualified expenses including Medicare premiums. A taxpayer age 65 or older who is still working under an employer HDHP and has not enrolled in Medicare can continue contributing, and once they enroll they stop contributing but can begin reimbursing Medicare premiums. Never tell a Medicare enrollee that their HSA is useless; it remains a tax-free spending vehicle for Medicare premiums and all other qualified expenses for life.

### Enforce The Incurred-After-Establishment Timing Rule

A qualified expense must have been incurred after the HSA was established, meaning after the account was opened, not after the taxpayer became eligible. An HSA opened in November cannot reimburse a surgery performed in January of the same year, even if the taxpayer was HDHP-eligible all year, because the expense predates the account's establishment. This rule is strict and is a frequent source of denied reimbursements, especially for taxpayers who open their HSA late in the year after incurring early-year medical costs.

The FSA timing rule differs and is more forgiving for liquidity but stricter for the plan year. An FSA can reimburse any qualified expense incurred during the plan year, plus any grace period, regardless of when the funds were deposited, because the full annual election is available on day one. But an FSA cannot reimburse expenses incurred before the plan year began or after it ended (plus grace period). Track the HSA establishment date and the FSA plan year separately when evaluating reimbursements, and never assume the two accounts share the same timing logic. For the HSA, also note that there is no deadline to reimburse, so a taxpayer can pay out of pocket now and reimburse themselves from the HSA years later, provided the expense was incurred after the HSA was established and the receipt is preserved.

### Require Substantiation For Every Tax-Free Withdrawal

Every tax-free HSA or FSA withdrawal must be backed by documentation sufficient to prove the expense was qualified, was incurred by the account holder or eligible family member, and was not otherwise reimbursed. Acceptable documentation includes itemized receipts, Explanation of Benefits statements from an insurer, pharmacy receipts identifying the medication, and prescriptions or letters of medical necessity where applicable. A credit card receipt showing only a total and a merchant name is generally insufficient because it does not identify the item or service. A canceled check alone is insufficient for the same reason.

For the HSA specifically, the custodian does not verify eligibility at the time of withdrawal, which means the taxpayer self-certifies and bears the full audit risk. This makes disciplined recordkeeping essential, because the IRS can request substantiation years later. Maintain a dedicated file of receipts matched to each HSA distribution, retain EOBs that show the patient-paid portion, and keep proof that the expense was not reimbursed by insurance or another account. For the long-term reimbursement strategy, where the taxpayer pays out of pocket now and reimburses from the HSA years later, the receipts must be preserved indefinitely, because the IRS can challenge any withdrawal lacking substantiation regardless of how much time has passed.

### Avoid Double-Dipping And Already-Reimbursed Expenses

A qualified expense cannot be reimbursed twice or reimbursed from the HSA or FSA if it was already paid by insurance or another account. If insurance paid the full cost, nothing remains to reimburse. If the taxpayer deducted the expense on Schedule A as an itemized medical deduction in a prior year, it cannot also be reimbursed tax-free from the HSA, because that would double-count the tax benefit. This rule catches taxpayers who reimburse an expense from the HSA that they had already claimed as an itemized deduction.

Cross-check every reimbursement against prior tax treatment and other coverage. Confirm the expense was the taxpayer's out-of-pocket cost after insurance, confirm it was not previously reimbursed by an FSA or HRA, and confirm it was not deducted on Schedule A. For expenses that straddle the 7.5% adjusted gross income floor on Schedule A, remember that only the portion actually deducted creates the double-dip problem; an expense that was included in the medical total but did not exceed the AGI floor was not deducted and can still be reimbursed from the HSA. When in doubt, default to not reimbursing an expense that was anywhere near a prior-year Schedule A medical deduction.

## Common Traps

### Reimbursing Health Insurance Premiums From The HSA

The symptom is using HSA funds to pay the monthly health plan premium. The trap is that general health insurance premiums are not qualified HSA expenses, so the withdrawal is non-qualified, taxable as income, and subject to the 20% additional tax if the taxpayer is under 65. Reserve HSA premium reimbursement for the narrow exceptions, such as Medicare premiums at 65 plus, long-term care premiums within age limits, and COBRA during unemployment.

### Treating All Pharmacy Aisle Items As Qualified

The symptom is reimbursing sunscreen, vitamins, cosmetics, or protein powder because they came from a pharmacy. The trap is that only OTC medicines and drugs qualify without a prescription post-2020, while general wellness and personal care products never qualified. Separate qualifying OTC medicines from non-qualifying personal care items on every pharmacy receipt.

### Approving Expenses By Retailer Label Alone

The symptom is approving a reimbursement because the retailer's website marks the item FSA or HSA eligible. The trap is that retailer labeling is a marketing convenience, not a legal determination, and some items are mislabeled or only conditionally eligible. Test every item against the Publication 502 standard regardless of the retailer's tag.

### Reimbursing An Expense Incurred Before The HSA Was Established

The symptom is reimbursing an early-year surgery from an HSA opened later that year. The trap is that the incurred-after-establishment rule disallows any expense predating the account's opening, even if the taxpayer was eligible all year. Confirm the HSA establishment date and reject expenses incurred before it.

### Spending HSA Funds Without Keeping Receipts

The symptom is making HSA withdrawals and assuming the custodian's lack of verification means no proof is needed. The trap is that the custodian does not check, but the IRS can demand substantiation years later, and a withdrawal without a matching receipt is reclassified as non-qualified and taxed plus the 20% penalty. Retain itemized receipts and EOBs for every distribution.

### Double-Dipping With A Prior Schedule A Deduction

The symptom is reimbursing an expense from the HSA that was already deducted as an itemized medical expense. The trap is that this double-counts the tax benefit and the expense is no longer qualified for HSA reimbursement. Cross-check against prior-year Schedule A medical deductions before reimbursing.

### Assuming Non-Medical HSA Withdrawals Are Penalty-Free

The symptom is treating the HSA as a general savings account after age 65 with no tax cost. The trap is that non-medical withdrawals after 65 are penalty-free but still taxable as income, unlike qualified medical withdrawals which remain fully tax-free. Only medical withdrawals deliver the tax-free outcome at any age.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has every reimbursed expense been tested against the IRC section 213(d) and Publication 502 standard of diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease, rather than approved by category label?
- [ ] Has the pre-2020 OTC expansion been applied correctly, qualifying OTC medicines and drugs without a prescription while still excluding general wellness, cosmetic, and personal care products?
- [ ] Have health insurance premiums been treated as generally excluded, with reimbursement limited to narrow exceptions such as long-term care premiums within age limits, COBRA during unemployment, and Medicare premiums for age 65 plus?
- [ ] Have Medicare premiums (Part B, Part D, Medicare Advantage, Medigap) been confirmed as qualified HSA expenses for individuals 65 and older, without implying that Medicare enrollment allows continued contributions?
- [ ] Has the incurred-after-establishment rule been enforced for the HSA, rejecting any expense predating the account opening, and has the FSA plan-year timing been applied separately?
- [ ] Does every tax-free withdrawal have matching substantiation, such as itemized receipts, EOBs, pharmacy receipts, and letters of medical necessity, sufficient to identify the item and prove it was not otherwise reimbursed?
- [ ] Has each expense been cross-checked to confirm it was the taxpayer's out-of-pocket cost after insurance, was not reimbursed by another account, and was not previously deducted on Schedule A?
- [ ] Have non-medical HSA withdrawal consequences been stated correctly, namely income tax plus 20% additional tax before age 65 and income tax (penalty-free) after age 65?
- [ ] For the long-term reimbursement strategy, have receipts been preserved indefinitely to support future tax-free withdrawals?
- [ ] Has the taxpayer been advised that this is general educational guidance and not personalized tax advice, and that a qualified tax professional, CPA, or benefits specialist must be consulted before relying on any reimbursement determination?
