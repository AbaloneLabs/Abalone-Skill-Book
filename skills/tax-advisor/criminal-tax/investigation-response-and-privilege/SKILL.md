# Investigation Response And Privilege

A criminal tax investigation is categorically different from a civil examination, and the moment a matter develops criminal potential, the entire response strategy must change—because the protections, the procedures, and the consequences are entirely distinct. The judgment problem is that criminal tax investigations often begin under the guise of a civil examination, and the transition from civil to criminal can happen without a clear announcement, leaving a taxpayer (and a non-attorney representative) building the government's criminal case through civil-style cooperation. Privilege is the primary shield in a criminal matter, and the privilege landscape shifts dramatically when criminal potential appears: the limited tax-practitioner privilege under section 7525 does not apply in criminal matters, leaving only the attorney-client privilege and the Fifth Amendment as protections.

Agents tend to continue the civil examination strategy—cooperating, producing documents, allowing interviews—after criminal potential has emerged, without recognizing the signals or shifting the representation. They may not recognize that a Special Agent's involvement signals criminal investigation, that the section 7525 privilege evaporates in criminal matters, that Fifth Amendment protection must be invoked deliberately, that work-product protection depends on the anticipation of litigation, or that a Kovel arrangement (engaging an accountant through counsel) is necessary to extend privilege to non-attorney work. The harm is a discoverable record of admissions and documents that builds the criminal case, a waiver of privilege through careless disclosure, or a failure to invoke the Fifth Amendment that leaves the taxpayer's statements available to the prosecution.

This skill covers the response to a criminal tax investigation, the privilege protections available, the recognition of criminal signals during a civil examination, and the management of the taxpayer's rights in U.S. federal criminal-tax practice. It is educational guidance, not legal advice; criminal investigation response and privilege decisions are high-stakes and depend on the specific facts, the procedural posture, and the taxpayer's exposure, and qualified tax litigation counsel must be consulted immediately for any matter with criminal potential. Criminal procedures and privilege rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Criminal Signals During A Civil Examination

Criminal tax investigations frequently develop from civil examinations, and the transition is often signaled rather than announced. The key signals include the involvement of a Special Agent from the IRS Criminal Investigation (CI) division, questions focused on intent or willfulness rather than on the tax computation, requests for documents that suggest a reconstruction of concealed income (such as bank-deposit analyses or net-worth reconstructions), inquiries about foreign accounts or structuring, a shift from examining the return to investigating the taxpayer's conduct, the use of a grand jury subpoena, or a parallel investigation by CI alongside the civil exam. The appearance of any of these signals indicates the matter has developed criminal potential.

Watch for the criminal signals continuously during any examination, and escalate the response immediately when they appear. The failure to recognize the signals—and the resulting continuation of civil-style cooperation—is the most common and most damaging error in criminal-tax practice. A Special Agent's contact, whether a surprise visit or a formal interview request, is a clear escalation that requires an immediate shift to counsel and to privilege-protected channels. The signals must be taken seriously, because the government does not announce the criminal transition, and the taxpayer's representative must detect it.

### Shift Representation To Counsel Immediately When Criminal Potential Appears

When criminal potential appears, the representation must shift to qualified tax litigation counsel, because only counsel can provide the protections a criminal matter requires. The section 7525 tax-practitioner privilege does not apply in criminal matters, so communications between the taxpayer and a non-attorney representative (CPA or enrolled agent) are not privileged and can be discovered and used by the prosecution. The attorney-client privilege protects communications with counsel for legal advice, and the work-product doctrine protects materials prepared in anticipation of litigation. A non-attorney representative who continues to develop facts after criminal potential appears can create a discoverable record and can build the government's case.

Refer the matter to counsel immediately upon detecting criminal potential, and cease fact development through non-privileged channels. The transition must be prompt, because every communication and document generated through a non-attorney representative after criminal potential appears is potentially discoverable. The taxpayer should be instructed not to discuss the matter with the non-attorney representative except through or at the direction of counsel, to preserve the ability to extend privilege through a Kovel arrangement. The representation shift is not optional; it is the prerequisite to protecting the taxpayer in a criminal matter.

### Invoke The Fifth Amendment Deliberately And Specifically

The Fifth Amendment protects against compelled self-incrimination, and in a criminal tax investigation it is the taxpayer's primary protection against being forced to provide testimony or documents that could be incriminating. The privilege must be invoked deliberately and specifically, because a blanket or generic refusal may not be honored, and the privilege must be claimed in response to specific questions or requests. The privilege protects testimony and the production of documents in certain circumstances (the act-of-production doctrine), but it does not protect existing records that were created voluntarily, and it does not prevent the government from using independent evidence.

Coordinate the Fifth Amendment invocation with counsel, ensuring it is invoked specifically and appropriately in response to questions or requests that could be incriminating. Do not assume silence alone protects the taxpayer; the privilege must be affirmatively claimed. The act-of-production doctrine can protect against the compelled production of documents where the production itself would be testimonial (admitting the documents exist, that they are authentic, or that they are responsive), but the doctrine has limits and does not protect pre-existing records. The Fifth Amendment is a powerful shield, but it must be wielded precisely and with counsel.

### Use The Kovel Arrangement To Extend Privilege To Non-Attorney Work

Because tax investigations often require accounting and financial analysis that attorneys may not perform themselves, the Kovel arrangement—engaging an accountant or other non-attorney professional through counsel for the purpose of providing legal advice—extends the attorney-client privilege to the non-attorney's work. Under a Kovel arrangement, the accountant is engaged by the attorney (not directly by the taxpayer) to assist in the legal representation, and communications among the taxpayer, counsel, and the accountant are privileged because the accountant is functioning as an agent of counsel. Without a Kovel arrangement, the accountant's work and communications are not privileged and can be discovered.

Establish a Kovel arrangement through counsel whenever non-attorney financial expertise is needed in a criminal matter, ensuring the engagement is structured correctly (the accountant is engaged by counsel, the purpose is to assist legal representation, and the communications flow through counsel). A poorly structured Kovel arrangement may not extend privilege, so the arrangement must be set up with care. The alternative—engaging an accountant directly—leaves the work discoverable, which in a criminal matter can be devastating. The Kovel arrangement is the mechanism for combining financial expertise with privilege.

### Protect The Attorney-Client Privilege Against Waiver

The attorney-client privilege protects confidential communications between the taxpayer and counsel for the purpose of legal advice, but it can be waived inadvertently. Waiver occurs when the communication is disclosed to a third party outside the privilege (such as forwarding counsel's advice to a business partner or a bank), when the communication is not for legal advice (such as business advice mixed with legal advice), or when the taxpayer puts the communication at issue in the case (such as relying on advice of counsel as a defense). Once waived, the privilege cannot be reclaimed, and the communication becomes discoverable.

Guard the privilege carefully throughout the investigation. Ensure communications with counsel are confidential and not shared with third parties, keep legal advice separate from business communications, and avoid putting privileged communications at issue unless intended. The privilege is fragile, and a single careless disclosure can waive it, exposing strategy and analysis to the prosecution. Privilege protection is an active discipline, not a passive assumption, and counsel should guide the taxpayer on how to preserve it.

### Manage Document Production To Avoid Creating Evidence

In a criminal investigation, document production is a two-edged sword: the taxpayer may have obligations to produce certain documents (through summons or subpoena), but every document produced is potential evidence, and the production itself can be testimonial. The strategy is to produce what is legally required while protecting what is privileged, and to avoid creating new evidence through the production process. Reconstructing records, creating summaries, or making statements during production can all create new evidence that did not previously exist.

Manage document production through counsel, producing only what is legally required (and challenging overbroad or privileged demands), and avoiding the creation of new evidence. Do not reconstruct records or create analyses that could become evidence unless directed by counsel and protected by privilege. The act-of-production doctrine should be considered where the production itself would be testimonial. Document production in a criminal matter is a strategic exercise, not a routine compliance task, and it must be handled with privilege and evidence-management discipline.

### Respond To Special Agent Contact Appropriately

A Special Agent's contact—whether a surprise visit to the taxpayer's home or business, a phone call, or a formal interview request—is the most direct signal of criminal investigation, and the response must be careful and immediate. The taxpayer should not answer questions or provide documents without counsel present, should not lie or mislead (which can constitute separate crimes such as section 1001 false statements or section 7212 obstruction), and should politely decline to answer and refer the agent to counsel. The Special Agent may read the taxpayer their rights (the constitutional rights warning), which further signals the criminal nature of the inquiry.

Instruct the taxpayer in advance (if criminal potential is anticipated) on how to respond to a Special Agent's contact: be polite, do not answer substantive questions, do not lie or obstruct, take the agent's card, and contact counsel immediately. The taxpayer must understand that lying to a Special Agent is a separate crime, and that even seemingly harmless statements can become evidence. The response to Special Agent contact should be scripted with counsel and executed consistently, because the first contact often sets the tone for the entire investigation.

## Common Traps

### Continuing Civil-Style Cooperation After Criminal Potential Appears

The symptom is a non-attorney representative continuing to produce documents and allow interviews after a Special Agent appears. The trap is that the section 7525 privilege does not apply criminally, so the cooperation generates a discoverable record that builds the government's case, and the representative may inadvertently create admissions. The direction is to recognize the criminal signals and to shift immediately to counsel and privilege-protected channels.

### Assuming The Section 7525 Privilege Protects Criminal Communications

The symptom is a CPA or enrolled agent continuing to develop facts believing the communications are privileged. The trap is that section 7525 explicitly does not apply in criminal matters, so the communications are discoverable and can be used by the prosecution. The direction is to recognize that only the attorney-client privilege protects criminal matters and to route sensitive communications through counsel.

### Failing To Invoke The Fifth Amendment Specifically

The symptom is remaining silent or issuing a blanket refusal in response to questions. The trap is that the Fifth Amendment must be invoked specifically in response to particular questions, and a blanket refusal may not be honored, leaving the taxpayer's silence unprotected or construed adversely. The direction is to invoke the privilege deliberately and specifically with counsel for each potentially incriminating question or request.

### Neglecting The Kovel Arrangement For Non-Attorney Expertise

The symptom is engaging an accountant directly to analyze the financial facts in a criminal matter. The trap is that without a Kovel arrangement, the accountant's work and communications are not privileged and can be discovered, providing the government with analysis and admissions. The direction is to engage the accountant through counsel under a properly structured Kovel arrangement to extend privilege.

### Waiving Privilege Through Careless Disclosure

The symptom is forwarding counsel's advice to a business partner, bank, or family member. The trap is that disclosure to a third party outside the privilege waives it, making the communication discoverable and exposing strategy to the prosecution. The direction is to keep privileged communications confidential, separate legal advice from business communications, and avoid disclosure to third parties.

### Lying Or Misleading A Special Agent

The symptom is the taxpayer trying to talk their way out of a Special Agent's questions. The trap is that false statements to a federal agent are a separate crime under section 1001, and misleading conduct can constitute obstruction under section 7212, creating new criminal exposure beyond the underlying tax matter. The direction is to decline to answer, refer the agent to counsel, and never lie or mislead.

### Creating New Evidence Through Document Production

The symptom is reconstructing records or preparing summaries during production. The trap is that newly created documents can become evidence that did not previously exist, and the production process itself can be testimonial, so careless production builds the government's case. The direction is to manage production through counsel, produce only what is legally required, and avoid creating new evidence.
