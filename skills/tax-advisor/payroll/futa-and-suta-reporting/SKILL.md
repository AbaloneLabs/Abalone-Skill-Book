# FUTA And SUTA Reporting

Unemployment tax is an employer-paid obligation that exists at two levels: the federal unemployment tax under FUTA and the state unemployment insurance tax under SUTA. Unlike income tax and FICA, nothing is withheld from the employee; the employer bears both taxes entirely. The two systems are linked through a credit mechanism, so FUTA is not simply a standalone tax but interacts with whether the employer has paid its state tax on time. A business that is current on SUTA can still owe a higher effective FUTA rate if its state has lost full credit, and a business that ignores SUTA registration entirely can find both taxes, plus penalties, accruing silently.

Agents tend to misunderstand the credit structure and the wage base. They may treat FUTA as a flat tax with no state interaction, assume the SUTA rate is the same in every state, or forget that unemployment tax applies to a separate, lower wage base than Social Security. They may not realize that new employers start at a standard state rate and only earn an experience rating over time, or that hiring in a new state triggers a separate registration and account. The harm is quiet accumulation of liability and penalties, often discovered only at year-end Form 940 preparation.

This skill covers U.S. federal FUTA reporting on Form 940 and the interaction with state SUTA obligations, wage bases, credit reductions, and multi-state registration. It does not cover FICA, income tax withholding, or deposit scheduling for those taxes. Rates, wage bases, and credit-reduction lists change annually and vary by state; confirm current figures from the IRS and the relevant state agencies. Unemployment tax is high-risk; this is not tax advice, and state-specific and penalty matters should be referred to a qualified tax professional.

## Core Rules

### Understand That Unemployment Tax Is Employer-Only

FUTA and SUTA are not withheld from employee wages. The employer pays both taxes in full. This is the single most important distinction from FICA, and it changes the cash-flow planning: every dollar of unemployment tax is an employer cost on top of wages and the FICA match. The agent should never represent unemployment tax as a deduction from employee pay.

Because the tax is employer-only, it does not appear on the employee's W-2 as withheld tax, though the wage base used for FUTA and SUTA is derived from the same wage records. The employer must track unemployment wages separately from Social Security and Medicare wages, because the unemployment wage base is far lower and the definition of includible wages can differ in narrow respects. Confirm the employer-only nature in every payroll cost projection so the true labor cost is not understated.

### Apply The FUTA Wage Base And Gross Rate With The Credit Structure

FUTA applies a gross tax rate to the first $7,000 of wages paid to each employee in a year. The $7,000 wage base is fixed in statute and does not adjust for inflation, unlike the Social Security wage base. However, the gross FUTA rate is reduced by a credit for timely payment of state unemployment tax, up to a maximum credit. When the full credit applies, the effective FUTA rate is a small fraction of the gross rate.

The agent must distinguish the gross rate from the effective rate. The full credit is available only when the employer pays state unemployment tax on time and the state itself is not a credit-reduction state. If the employer pays SUTA late, the credit is reduced and the effective FUTA rate rises. If the state has not repaid federal unemployment loans and lands on the credit-reduction list, the credit is further reduced for all employers in that state, raising their FUTA cost regardless of their own timeliness. Never quote a single FUTA rate without stating whether it is gross or net of credit and whether a credit reduction applies.

### Treat SUTA As State-Specific And Experience-Rated

State unemployment insurance is administered by each state under its own law, wage base, rate schedule, and experience-rating formula. The SUTA wage base varies widely and is often higher than the $7,000 FUTA base. New employers generally pay a standard new-employer rate until they build an experience history, after which the rate adjusts based on claims activity, payroll size, and the state's rating method.

The agent should never assume a single SUTA rate or wage base across states. When a business hires in a new state, it must register for a state unemployment account before paying wages there, and the rate and wage base for that state govern. Experience ratings can be contested or protested when a former employee's claim is improper, and the employer should track charge statements. A high-turnover employer can see its SUTA rate climb sharply, materially increasing labor cost, so the rate is a managed figure, not a constant.

### Check The Annual Credit Reduction Status

Each year the IRS publishes a list of states that have not repaid federal unemployment loans and therefore face a FUTA credit reduction. Employers in a credit-reduction state lose part of the normal credit, increasing their effective FUTA rate, and the reduction can compound across multiple years until the state repays the loans. Some states offer a way to mitigate part of the reduction through additional state contributions.

The agent should check the current year's credit-reduction list before finalizing Form 940. A business operating in a credit-reduction state must compute the additional FUTA tax owed and may need to make a deposit earlier than the annual Form 940 deadline if the liability exceeds the threshold. Ignoring the credit reduction understates FUTA and creates a late-deposit or underpayment exposure. Because the list changes annually, prior-year assumptions are not reliable.

### Register In Every State Where Employees Work

Unemployment tax jurisdiction generally follows where the employee performs services, not where the employer is headquartered. Hiring a remote employee in another state can create a SUTA registration and filing obligation in that state, plus potential state income tax withholding and registration issues that are outside this skill's scope. The agent should map each employee's work location to the required state unemployment account.

Do not assume the headquarters state covers all employees. Multi-state employers must register separately in each state where they have covered employment, file the state returns, and pay the state tax to preserve the FUTA credit. When an employee relocates mid-year, the wage base and rate treatment must be coordinated between states under that state's reciprocity or transfer rules. Treat each new work location as a trigger for a registration review, not an afterthought.

### Apply Successor And Transfer Rules Where Relevant

When a business is acquired, merged, or reorganized, unemployment tax liability and experience rating may transfer to the successor under state law. Some states allow the successor to take over the predecessor's experience rating, which can be advantageous or harmful depending on the predecessor's claims history. FUTA also has successor rules that can affect whether wages already paid count toward the wage base in the same year.

The agent should not treat a transaction as a clean break for unemployment purposes. Review the asset or stock purchase structure, the state's transfer rules, and whether a joint or combined account election is available or mandatory. Failing to transfer or register correctly can leave the successor liable for the predecessor's unpaid tax in some structures, or forfeit a favorable experience rating. Flag any acquisition, reorganization, or change in legal entity for a state-by-state unemployment review before the first payroll under the new entity.

### File Form 940 And Reconcile To State Reporting

Form 940 is the annual federal unemployment tax return, due at the end of January for the prior calendar year. If the FUTA liability exceeds a threshold during the year, the employer must make at least one quarterly deposit rather than paying the full amount with the return. The form reconciles total FUTA wages, the credit for state tax paid, any credit reduction, and the final tax due or overpayment.

The agent should reconcile Form 940 to the state unemployment wage reports and to the payroll register. Confirm that wages above the $7,000 base per employee are excluded, that the state tax credit matches what was actually paid on time, and that any credit-reduction adjustment is included. A mismatch between Form 940 and state filings is a common notice trigger. If SUTA was paid late, the reduced credit must be reflected, and the agent should not assume the full credit applies simply because the state tax was eventually paid.

## Common Traps

### Treating FUTA As A Flat Standalone Tax

FUTA's effective rate depends on the state tax credit and credit-reduction status. Quoting the gross rate as the actual cost overstates the tax in normal cases and understates it in credit-reduction states or when SUTA is paid late.

### Withholding Unemployment Tax From Employees

FUTA and SUTA are employer-only taxes. Deducting them from employee pay is an error that requires correction and can create wage and hour exposure, not just a tax fix.

### Assuming The SUTA Wage Base And Rate Are Uniform

SUTA wage bases vary by state and often exceed $7,000, and rates are experience-rated and differ by employer and state. Using one state's figures for another under- or over-computes the liability.

### Ignoring The Annual Credit Reduction List

A credit-reduction state raises FUTA for every employer there regardless of their own timeliness. Reusing last year's assumption misses a year-over-year change that can require an interim deposit.

### Forgetting State Registration For Remote Employees

Unemployment jurisdiction follows where services are performed. Hiring across state lines without registering for a SUTA account in that state leaves state tax unpaid and erodes the FUTA credit.

### Assuming A Business Acquisition Resets Unemployment History

State transfer and successor rules can carry over both liability and experience rating. Treating a transaction as a clean start can forfeit a good rate or inherit an unexpected liability.

### Paying SUTA Late And Assuming The Full FUTA Credit Still Applies

The FUTA credit requires timely state tax payment. Late SUTA reduces the credit and raises effective FUTA, even if the state tax is eventually paid in full.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is unemployment tax treated as employer-only, with no withholding from employee wages, and reflected as an employer labor cost in projections?
- [ ] Is the FUTA $7,000-per-employee wage base applied, with the gross rate distinguished from the effective rate net of the state tax credit?
- [ ] Is the SUTA rate and wage base confirmed for each state where employees work, recognizing new-employer rates and experience-rating adjustments?
- [ ] Has the current year's FUTA credit-reduction list been checked, with any additional tax and required interim deposit computed?
- [ ] Is a state unemployment account registered in every state where an employee performs services, including remote and relocated employees?
- [ ] For any acquisition, merger, or entity change, have state successor and transfer rules been reviewed for liability and experience-rating carryover?
- [ ] Does Form 940 reconcile to state wage reports and the payroll register, with wages over the wage base excluded and the credit matched to timely-paid SUTA?
- [ ] If SUTA was paid late, is the reduced FUTA credit reflected rather than the full credit assumed?
- [ ] Have current FUTA and SUTA rates, wage bases, and the credit-reduction list been verified from the IRS and the relevant state agencies for the applicable year?
- [ ] Has the agent escalated multi-state registration, credit-reduction, transaction, and penalty matters to a qualified tax professional, recognizing this is not tax advice?
