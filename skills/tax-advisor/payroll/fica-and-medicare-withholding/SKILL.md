# FICA And Medicare Withholding

FICA, the Federal Insurance Contributions Act, is the mechanism by which Social Security and Medicare taxes are collected from wages. It is a two-part tax with a shared structure: the employee pays a portion through withholding, and the employer pays a matching portion. Social Security tax applies only up to an annual wage base that adjusts each year, while Medicare tax has no wage cap. On top of the base Medicare rate, high earners trigger an Additional Medicare Tax that is the employee's responsibility alone, with no employer match. Getting these calculations wrong creates under-withholding, incorrect wage statements, deposit shortfalls, and employee tax-return problems.

Agents frequently mishandle FICA in a few predictable ways. They apply the Social Security wage base as if it never changes, forget that Additional Medicare Tax begins at a different threshold than the base Medicare rate, or treat all supplemental pay the same. They may not realize that employer-provided benefits, tips, noncash compensation, and certain reimbursements are wages subject to FICA, or that the employer matching obligation is separate from the amount withheld. The harm is both financial and administrative: employees receive incorrect W-2s, employers under-deposit, and corrections ripple into amended returns.

This skill covers U.S. federal Social Security and Medicare withholding and employer matching, the wage base, Additional Medicare Tax, and the wages subject to these taxes. It does not cover income tax withholding mechanics, unemployment taxes, or deposit scheduling, which are separate. Rates and the Social Security wage base change annually; confirm the current figures from official IRS sources. Payroll tax is high-risk; this is not tax advice, and specific calculations should be verified by a qualified tax professional or payroll specialist.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Two-Part FICA Structure Correctly

FICA has two components with different rate mechanics. Social Security tax is a flat percentage split evenly between employee and employer, applied to wages up to an annual wage base that is adjusted for inflation and announced each year. Medicare tax is also a flat percentage split evenly between employee and employer, but it applies to all wages with no annual cap. The combined employee-and-employer rate is the figure most people recognize as FICA.

The agent must use the current year's wage base for Social Security, because applying a prior-year limit produces systematic under- or over-withholding for high earners. For each employee, track year-to-date Social Security-taxed wages and stop withholding the Social Security portion once the wage base is reached, while continuing Medicare withholding on all remaining wages. The employer match stops for Social Security at the same point. Never blend the two components into a single uncapped rate, because that breaks the wage-base tracking.

### Handle Additional Medicare Tax Separately From The Base Rate

Once an employee's wages exceed the Additional Medicare Tax threshold, an extra employee-only percentage must be withheld in addition to the regular Medicare rate. The thresholds depend on filing status and are fixed in the statute, not indexed for inflation. Critically, the Additional Medicare Tax has no employer match; only the employee owes it, and only the employee portion is withheld.

The agent should treat Additional Medicare Tax as a distinct withholding event. It begins only after wages cross the applicable threshold, so the calculation is marginal, not flat across all wages. Employees may also request additional withholding on Form W-4 to cover Additional Medicare Tax on self-employment or investment income, and the employer should honor that election. A frequent error is to ignore the threshold because the employer believes it is the employee's problem; the employer still has a withholding obligation once wages pass the threshold, and failing to withhold creates its own exposure even though the underlying tax is the employee's.

### Identify Which Payments Are Wages Subject To FICA

FICA wages are broader than cash salary. They include regular pay, supplemental wages such as bonuses and commissions, tips above the reporting threshold, certain fringe benefits, noncash compensation, employer contributions to some benefit plans, and the personal use of employer assets. Some payments are specifically excluded, such as qualified reimbursements under an accountable plan, certain qualified benefits, and specific statutory categories.

The agent should not assume FICA applies only to base salary. Review the full compensation package: bonuses, severance, accrued vacation payouts, group-term life insurance over the exemption amount, third-party sick pay, dependent care benefits above the exclusion, and moving expense reimbursements in years when the exclusion is suspended. Each category has its own rule, and treating an includible item as excludible under-withholds both employee and employer tax. When a benefit is borderline, flag it for plan-document and current-law review rather than defaulting to no tax.

### Compute The Employer Match As An Independent Obligation

The employer portion of Social Security and Medicare is a separate tax owed by the employer, not merely a mirror of what was withheld from employees. Even if the employee portion is correctly withheld, the employer must pay its own matching share and deposit it alongside the withheld amounts. The match is not optional, not reducible by the employee's situation, and not affected by the employee's filing status.

The agent should confirm that the employer match is included in every deposit and reflected on Form 941. A subtle error occurs when software withholds the employee share but the employer share is omitted from the deposit liability, or when a benefit coded as noncash is excluded from the employer match even though it is includible. Because the employer match is employer-only tax, it does not appear on the employee's W-2 as withheld tax, but the underlying wage amount must still be reflected correctly for both employee and employer tax computation.

### Treat Supplemental Wages Under The Correct Method

Supplemental wages, such as bonuses, commissions, severance, and back pay, can be withheld at a flat supplemental rate if paid separately from regular wages, or aggregated with regular wages if paid in the same check. The choice affects federal income tax withholding, but the FICA treatment still applies to the full supplemental amount regardless of method.

The agent should confirm that supplemental payments are included in Social Security and Medicare wages, that the wage-base tracking accounts for the timing of the payment, and that Additional Medicare Tax withholding is triggered if the supplemental payment pushes year-to-date wages over the threshold. A large year-end bonus can cross the Additional Medicare Tax threshold mid-payment, requiring the extra withholding on the portion above the threshold. Do not apply the flat supplemental income tax rate as if it replaced FICA; the two are independent.

### Account For Self-Employment Tax As The Analog To FICA

Self-employed individuals do not have FICA withheld, but they pay self-employment tax, which combines the employer and employee Social Security and Medicare portions into a single tax on net earnings. The self-employment tax rate is structured so that, after an adjustment for the employer-equivalent portion, the net burden approximates the combined employer-plus-employee FICA. The same Social Security wage base applies, and the same Additional Medicare Tax threshold concept applies through the income tax system.

The agent should not treat a contractor's lack of FICA withholding as a tax saving. The contractor generally owes self-employment tax instead, and the classification decision determines who bears it. When advising on owner compensation, note that an S corporation shareholder-employee's reasonable salary is subject to FICA while distributions are not, which is a common planning point but also a frequent audit target. Reasonable compensation must be documented and defensible, not set arbitrarily low to avoid FICA.

### Reconcile Withholding To W-2 And Form 941

FICA withholding must reconcile across three touchpoints: the payroll register, the quarterly Form 941, and the annual W-2. Social Security wages and Medicare wages are reported in separate boxes on the W-2, and the tax amounts shown must match the rate applied to those wage bases. Any discrepancy generates a notice and can trigger a wage verification request.

The agent should run a reconciliation that ties year-to-date Social Security wages to the wage base per employee, confirms Medicare wages include all includible items, and verifies that Additional Medicare Tax withholding is reported in the correct W-2 box. Corrections require Form W-2c and a corresponding Form 941-X, and the deposit implications of any correction must be evaluated. Do not treat a W-2 as final until the wage-base and rate math has been verified per employee.

## Common Traps

### Using A Stale Social Security Wage Base

The wage base increases most years. Applying last year's limit over-withholds Social Security tax from high earners and over-deposits, while applying an old lower limit under-withholds. The current year's figure must be confirmed from official sources before the first payroll.

### Ignoring Additional Medicare Tax Because It Is The Employee's Tax

Additional Medicare Tax is owed by the employee, but the employer has an independent withholding obligation once wages cross the threshold. Dismissing it as the employee's responsibility does not remove the employer's exposure for failing to withhold.

### Excluding Noncash And Fringe Compensation From FICA Wages

Group-term life insurance over the exclusion, personal use of a company vehicle, and certain dependent care or education benefits are wages for FICA. Defaulting to no tax on anything that is not a paycheck under-withholds both employee and employer portions.

### Blending Employee And Employer Portions Into One Number

The employee withholding and the employer match are distinct obligations even though the rates look symmetric. Omitting the employer match from deposits or returns, while the employee share is correct, leaves the employer owing its own tax plus penalties.

### Assuming Supplemental Pay Follows The Same Rules As Regular Pay

Supplemental wages have a flat income tax withholding option, but FICA still applies to the full amount, and a large supplemental payment can cross the Additional Medicare Tax threshold mid-payment. Treating a bonus as a simple add-on misses the marginal threshold calculation.

### Treating A Contractor's Lack Of FICA As Pure Savings

A properly classified contractor owes self-employment tax instead of FICA, so the tax is not eliminated, only shifted. Misclassifying an employee to avoid FICA does not remove the tax; it relocates the liability and adds penalties.

### Failing To Reconcile W-2 Boxes To Form 941

Social Security wages, Medicare wages, and the respective tax amounts must tie across the payroll register, Form 941, and the W-2. A mismatch generates notices and can reclassify discrepancies as unpaid tax subject to penalties.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the current year's Social Security wage base being applied, with year-to-date tracking that stops Social Security withholding at the cap while continuing Medicare on all wages?
- [ ] Is Additional Medicare Tax withheld at the correct marginal threshold based on filing status, with the employee-only nature and no employer match respected?
- [ ] Have all includible wages been identified, including supplemental pay, tips, group-term life insurance over the exclusion, third-party sick pay, and noncash fringe benefits?
- [ ] Is the employer matching share of Social Security and Medicare computed and deposited as a separate obligation from the employee withholding?
- [ ] Are supplemental wages included in FICA wages with wage-base and Additional Medicare Tax threshold effects checked, independent of the income tax withholding method chosen?
- [ ] For self-employed and owner-employee situations, is self-employment tax or reasonable-compensation FICA evaluated rather than treated as avoided tax?
- [ ] Do Social Security wages, Medicare wages, Additional Medicare Tax withholding, and tax amounts reconcile across the payroll register, Form 941, and W-2 boxes?
- [ ] Are corrections handled through Form W-2c and Form 941-X with deposit implications assessed before the corrected forms are filed?
- [ ] Has the agent confirmed current rates, the Social Security wage base, and Additional Medicare Tax thresholds from official IRS sources for the applicable year?
- [ ] Has the agent escalated FICA calculation disputes, classification-driven FICA exposure, and correction matters to a qualified tax professional or payroll specialist, recognizing this is not tax advice?
