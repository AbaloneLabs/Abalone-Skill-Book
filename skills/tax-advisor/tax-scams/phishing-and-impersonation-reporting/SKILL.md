# Phishing and Impersonation Reporting

IRS impersonation scams, phishing emails, and robocalls are pervasive frauds in which a scammer poses as the IRS (or another tax authority) to steal the taxpayer's money, their personal information, or their identity. The scams take many forms: a phone call threatening arrest for unpaid taxes, an email asking the taxpayer to verify their account, a text message with a link to a fake IRS website, or a letter that looks official but demands payment in a non-standard way (such as gift cards or cryptocurrency). The judgment problem is that the scams are sophisticated and constantly evolving, and a taxpayer who falls for the scam can lose thousands of dollars, have their identity stolen, or have their tax account compromised. An agent who treats a scam communication as genuine (and advises the taxpayer to pay or to provide information), or who ignores the reporting, allows the scam to continue and exposes the taxpayer to harm. The reporting is also important for the broader enforcement, because the IRS and the law enforcement agencies track the scams and pursue the scammers.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the real IRS does not initiate contact by email, text message, or social media to request personal or financial information, and it does not threaten arrest or demand immediate payment by gift card, cryptocurrency, or wire transfer. Second, the phishing emails should be forwarded to phishing@irs.gov (not just deleted), because the IRS tracks the phishing campaigns and issues alerts. Third, the phone scams and the other impersonation scams should be reported to the Treasury Inspector General for Tax Administration (TIGTA) and the FTC, because these agencies investigate and pursue the scammers. Fourth, the taxpayer who provided information or money to the scammer should take immediate protective action (the credit freeze, the account monitoring, the IP PIN), because the scammer may use the information for identity theft. Fifth, the state tax authorities have their own impersonation scams, and the taxpayer should be aware of the state variants. Sixth, the scams target specific populations (such as the elderly, the immigrants, and the non-English speakers), and the agent should be sensitive to the targeting.

This skill covers the recognition and the reporting of phishing and IRS impersonation scams under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Hallmarks Of IRS Impersonation Scams

The real IRS has established contact procedures, and a communication that deviates from these procedures is likely a scam. The real IRS generally initiates contact by mail (a letter), not by phone, email, text, or social media. The real IRS does not demand immediate payment (it provides the taxpayer an opportunity to question or to appeal), it does not require a specific payment method (such as gift cards, cryptocurrency, or a wire transfer), it does not threaten arrest or revocation of a license, and it does not request personal or financial information by email or text. The scams deviate from these procedures: the scammer calls and threatens arrest, the scammer emails and asks for verification, the scammer texts a link, or the scammer demands a gift card or a cryptocurrency payment.

Recognize the hallmarks of the scams. Evaluate the communication against the IRS's procedures. If the communication is a phone call that threatens arrest or demands immediate payment, it is a scam. If the communication is an email or a text that requests personal or financial information or that contains a link, it is a scam. If the communication demands a gift card, a cryptocurrency, or a wire transfer, it is a scam. If the communication is a letter that looks official but demands a non-standard payment, it may be a scam (verify the letter through the IRS's official channels). The agent should educate the taxpayer on the hallmarks, because the recognition is the first line of defense. The scams are sophisticated (they may use caller ID spoofing, official-looking logos, and the taxpayer's personal information to appear genuine), so the taxpayer should verify any suspicious communication through the IRS's official channels (the IRS.gov website or the IRS phone number).

### Forward Phishing Emails To Phishing At IRS

The phishing emails that impersonate the IRS should be forwarded to phishing@irs.gov, not just deleted. The IRS tracks the phishing campaigns, issues alerts, and works with the law enforcement agencies to shut down the phishing sites. The forwarding should include the full email (with the headers, so the IRS can trace the source), and the taxpayer should not click any links or open any attachments in the email. The phishing emails may also target the state tax agencies, the tax software companies, and the financial institutions, and the taxpayer should report to the appropriate entity.

Forward the phishing emails to phishing@irs.gov. When the taxpayer receives a phishing email that impersonates the IRS, forward the full email (with the headers) to phishing@irs.gov, and then delete the email. Do not click any links or open any attachments, because they may install malware. If the phishing email targets a state tax agency, a tax software company, or a financial institution, report to the appropriate entity (through the entity's phishing reporting address). The forwarding is important, because the IRS tracks the campaigns and issues alerts, and the law enforcement agencies use the information to pursue the scammers. The agent should advise the taxpayer to forward the phishing emails, because the reporting contributes to the broader enforcement and the alerts.

### Report Phone And Other Impersonation Scams To TIGTA And The FTC

The phone scams and the other impersonation scams (such as the letters and the in-person visits) should be reported to the Treasury Inspector General for Tax Administration (TIGTA) and the Federal Trade Commission (FTC). TIGTA investigates the IRS impersonation scams, and it maintains a reporting system (through the TIGTA website or the phone line). The FTC tracks the scams through IdentityTheft.gov and the FTC Complaint Assistant, and it coordinates with the law enforcement agencies. The reporting is important for the enforcement, and it supports the taxpayer's case (if the taxpayer lost money or provided information).

Report the phone and the other impersonation scams to TIGTA and the FTC. For a phone scam (or a letter or an in-person visit), report to TIGTA through the TIGTA website (tigta.gov) or the phone line (1-800-366-4484). Provide the details of the scam (the phone number, the caller's name, the demand, the date and time). Report to the FTC through IdentityTheft.gov (if the taxpayer provided personal information) or the FTC Complaint Assistant (for the scam complaint). The reporting triggers the investigation and the tracking, and it supports the taxpayer's case. The agent should document the scam (the phone number, the email, the letter, the demand), because the documentation is the basis for the reports. The reporting is the primary mechanism to combat the scams, so it should be done promptly.

### Take Immediate Protective Action If Information Or Money Was Provided

If the taxpayer provided personal information (such as their SSN, their bank account, or their credit card) or money to the scammer, the taxpayer should take immediate protective action. For the personal information, the taxpayer should place a fraud alert or a credit freeze with the three major credit bureaus, monitor their financial accounts, and enroll in the IRS's IP PIN system (to prevent tax identity theft). For the money, the taxpayer should contact their bank or their credit card company (to dispute the charge or to stop the payment), and they should report the loss to TIGTA and the FTC. The immediate action can mitigate the harm, because the scammer may act quickly to use the information or to move the money.

Take the immediate protective action. If the taxpayer provided personal information, place a fraud alert or a credit freeze with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion, and monitor the financial accounts for suspicious activity. Enroll in the IRS's IP PIN system (to prevent tax identity theft), and file Form 14039 (if the SSN was compromised). If the taxpayer provided money, contact the bank or the credit card company immediately (to dispute the charge or to stop the payment), and report the loss to TIGTA and the FTC. The immediate action is critical, because the scammer may act within hours, and the protective measures can prevent the broader identity theft. The agent should advise the taxpayer of the urgency and should help the taxpayer implement the measures, because the taxpayer may be stressed or confused after the scam.

### Verify Genuine IRS Contact Through Official Channels

If the taxpayer is unsure whether a communication is genuine, they should verify it through the IRS's official channels. The genuine IRS letters can be verified by logging into the taxpayer's IRS online account (to see if the letter corresponds to a real account action) or by calling the IRS at the official number (1-800-829-1040 for individuals). The taxpayer should not call the number on the suspicious letter or the suspicious email, because it may connect to the scammer. The verification through the official channels confirms the communication's authenticity and prevents the taxpayer from falling for the scam.

Verify the genuine IRS contact through the official channels. If the taxpayer receives a letter or a notice that they are unsure about, advise them to log into their IRS online account (to see if the letter corresponds to a real account action) or to call the IRS at the official number (1-800-829-1040 for individuals, or the number on the IRS.gov website). The taxpayer should not call the number on the suspicious letter or email, and they should not click any links or provide any information until the communication is verified. The verification is the safe way to confirm the communication, and it prevents the taxpayer from acting on the scam. The agent should emphasize the verification, because the scams are sophisticated and may appear genuine, and the only reliable way to confirm is through the official channels.

### Address The State And The Specialized Scams

The IRS impersonation scams have state variants, where the scammer poses as the state tax agency (such as the state revenue department), and the taxpayer should be aware of the state scams. The scams also target specific populations, such as the elderly (who may be more trusting of official-sounding calls), the immigrants (who may fear the tax authority), and the non-English speakers (who may not understand the communication). The scams may also target the tax professionals (to steal the client data and to file fraudulent returns), and the businesses (to steal the employee data). The agent should be aware of the state and the specialized scams, and they should educate the taxpayer accordingly.

Address the state and the specialized scams. For the state scams, advise the taxpayer to verify the communication through the state tax agency's official channels (the state's website or phone number), and to report the state impersonation scams to the state agency. For the specialized scams (targeting the elderly, the immigrants, the non-English speakers, the tax professionals, and the businesses), educate the taxpayer on the targeting and the protective measures. The tax professionals should secure their client data (through the strong passwords, the multi-factor authentication, and the encryption), because the scams target the professionals to steal the data. The businesses should secure their employee data (such as the Forms W-2), because the scams target the businesses to file fraudulent returns. The agent should be sensitive to the targeting, because the scams exploit the vulnerability, and the education should be tailored to the taxpayer's situation.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Threatening Phone Call As Genuine

The symptom is a taxpayer who receives a phone call threatening arrest for unpaid taxes and believes it is the IRS. The trap is that the real IRS does not threaten arrest or demand immediate payment, so the call is a scam. The direction is to recognize the hallmarks and to verify through the official channels.

### Clicking A Link In A Phishing Email

The symptom is a taxpayer who clicks a link in an email that appears to be from the IRS. The trap is that the link may install malware or lead to a fake website that steals the information, so the click compromises the taxpayer. The direction is to forward the email to phishing@irs.gov and to delete it without clicking.

### Demanding Payment By Gift Card Or Cryptocurrency

The symptom is a scammer who demands payment by gift card or cryptocurrency. The trap is that the real IRS does not require these payment methods, so the demand is a hallmark of a scam. The direction is to recognize the non-standard payment demand and to refuse to pay.

### Ignoring The Reporting

The symptom is a taxpayer who receives a phishing email or a scam call but does not report it. The trap is that the reporting (to phishing@irs.gov, TIGTA, and the FTC) contributes to the enforcement and the alerts, so the failure to report allows the scam to continue. The direction is to report the scams to the appropriate entities.

### Not Taking Protective Action After Providing Information

The symptom is a taxpayer who provided their SSN to a scammer but does not place a credit freeze or enroll in the IP PIN. The trap is that the scammer may use the SSN for identity theft, so the failure to protect exposes the taxpayer. The direction is to take the immediate protective action (the credit freeze, the account monitoring, the IP PIN, the Form 14039).

### Calling The Number On A Suspicious Letter

The symptom is a taxpayer who calls the number on a suspicious letter to verify. The trap is that the number may connect to the scammer, so the verification is compromised. The direction is to verify through the IRS's official channels (the IRS online account or the official IRS number).
