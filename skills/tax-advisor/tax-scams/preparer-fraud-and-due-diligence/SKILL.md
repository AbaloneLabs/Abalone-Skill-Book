# Preparer Fraud and Due Diligence

Return preparer fraud and preparer misconduct are significant risks in tax practice, because a fraudulent or negligent preparer can expose the taxpayer to assessments, penalties, and examinations, while the taxpayer's own due diligence obligations (for certain credits and filing statuses) can create independent liability. The judgment problem is that the taxpayer is generally liable for the information on their return, even if a preparer committed the fraud or the error, so the taxpayer must evaluate the preparer's conduct and their own due diligence. An agent who conflates preparer negligence with fraud, or who overlooks the taxpayer's due diligence duties, misjudges the case and the remedies. The preparer fraud also interacts with the IRS's return preparer program (the Preparer Tax Identification Number, the due diligence requirements, and the disciplinary actions), the taxpayer's reasonable cause defense, and the potential for a refund of the preparer's fees.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the taxpayer is generally liable for the return, even if the preparer committed the fraud, so the taxpayer cannot simply blame the preparer. Second, the preparer fraud may involve fabricated deductions or credits (to inflate the refund and the preparer's fee), and the taxpayer may be a victim (if they were unaware) or a participant (if they were aware). Third, the taxpayer's due diligence obligations (under Section 6695(g)) require the taxpayer and the preparer to document the eligibility for certain credits (the Earned Income Tax Credit, the Child Tax Credit, the American Opportunity Tax Credit, and the Head of Household filing status), and a failure can result in penalties. Fourth, the Form 14157 (Return Preparer Complaint) and the Form 14157-A (Return Preparer Fraud or Misconduct) are the mechanisms to report the preparer to the IRS, and they may support a penalty abatement or a fee recovery. Fifth, the reasonable cause defense may excuse the taxpayer's penalties if the taxpayer relied on the preparer in good faith, but the reliance must be reasonable (the preparer must be competent, and the taxpayer must provide the accurate information). Sixth, the preparer fraud may trigger an examination of multiple years, not just the year of the fraud.

This skill covers preparer fraud and the taxpayer's due diligence obligations under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Taxpayer's Liability For The Return

The taxpayer is generally liable for the information on their return, even if a preparer prepared or filed the return. The taxpayer signs the return (or authorizes the e-file), attesting to the accuracy, and the IRS holds the taxpayer responsible for the tax, the penalties, and the interest. The preparer has a separate liability (for the preparer penalties under Section 6694 and the due diligence penalties under Section 6695(g)), but the preparer's liability does not relieve the taxpayer's liability. The taxpayer may pursue the preparer for the fees and the damages (through a civil action), but the taxpayer remains liable to the IRS.

Understand the taxpayer's liability. The taxpayer is responsible for the return, regardless of the preparer's conduct, so the taxpayer cannot simply blame the preparer. The taxpayer should review the return before signing (or authorizing the e-file), and the taxpayer should ask the preparer about any items they do not understand. If the preparer fabricated deductions or credits, the taxpayer is still liable for the additional tax, but the taxpayer may have remedies (the reasonable cause defense for the penalties, the Form 14157 for the preparer complaint, and the civil action for the fees and the damages). The agent should advise the taxpayer of their liability and the remedies, and the taxpayer should not assume that the preparer's error excuses the liability. The taxpayer's liability is the starting point, and the remedies are pursued separately.

### Identify Preparer Fraud And Misconduct

Preparer fraud involves the preparer's intentional fabrication of deductions, credits, or other items to inflate the refund (and the preparer's fee, which is often based on the refund amount). Preparer misconduct includes the negligent preparation of the return (such as the failure to ask the right questions or to document the eligibility), the unauthorized filing of the return, the diversion of the refund (to the preparer's account), and the failure to sign the return or to provide a copy. The fraud and the misconduct may be identified through the IRS's examination (which finds the fabricated items), the taxpayer's review of the return (which finds the errors), or the IRS's preparer investigations (which target the fraudulent preparers).

Identify the preparer fraud and the misconduct. Review the return for the fabricated items (such as deductions or credits that the taxpayer did not claim or that are inconsistent with the taxpayer's situation), the unauthorized filings (such as a return the taxpayer did not authorize), the diverted refunds (such as a refund that went to the preparer's account), and the missing preparer signature or copy. If the fraud or the misconduct is identified, gather the evidence (the return, the preparer's engagement letter, the correspondence, the refund records), and evaluate the taxpayer's role (victim or participant). The preparer fraud may involve multiple taxpayers, so the IRS's preparer investigations may be relevant. The agent should document the fraud or the misconduct, because the documentation supports the Form 14157, the reasonable cause defense, and the civil action.

### Apply The Due Diligence Requirements

The due diligence requirements under Section 6695(g) apply to the preparer (and, indirectly, to the taxpayer) for certain credits and filing statuses: the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), the Child Tax Credit (CTC), the Additional Child Tax Credit (ACTC), the American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC), and the Head of Household (HOH) filing status. The preparer must document the eligibility (by completing the Form 8867, the Paid Preparer's Due Diligence Checklist, and by retaining the supporting records), and the failure to meet the due diligence can result in a penalty (USD 665 per failure, subject to adjustment). The taxpayer also has a role, because the taxpayer must provide the accurate information and must not claim the credit or the filing status if they are not eligible.

Apply the due diligence requirements. For the EITC, the CTC, the ACTC, the AOTC, and the HOH filing status, confirm that the preparer completed the Form 8867 and retained the supporting records, and that the taxpayer provided the accurate information. If the due diligence was not met, the preparer may be subject to the penalty, and the taxpayer may be subject to the additional tax (if the credit or the filing status was incorrect) and the penalties (such as the accuracy-related penalty, unless the reasonable cause defense applies). The taxpayer should document their eligibility (with the records of the relationship, the residency, the income, and the education expenses), because the documentation supports the credit or the filing status and the reasonable cause defense. The due diligence requirements are a shared obligation, so the agent should evaluate both the preparer's and the taxpayer's roles.

### File Form 14157 And 14157-A To Report The Preparer

The Form 14157 (Return Preparer Complaint) and the Form 14157-A (Return Preparer Fraud or Misconduct) are the mechanisms to report the preparer to the IRS. The Form 14157 is the general complaint, and the Form 14157-A is the detailed fraud or misconduct report, which requires the description of the fraud, the tax years, the amounts, and the supporting evidence. The forms trigger the IRS's Return Preparer Office investigation, which may result in the disciplinary action (such as the suspension or the disbarment of the preparer). The forms also support the taxpayer's reasonable cause defense and the civil action for the fees and the damages.

File the Form 14157 and the 14157-A. Prepare the Form 14157 with the preparer's information (name, PTIN, firm), the taxpayer's information, and the description of the complaint. Prepare the Form 14157-A with the detailed fraud or misconduct description, the tax years, the amounts, and the supporting evidence (the return, the engagement letter, the correspondence, the refund records). File the forms with the IRS's Return Preparer Office. The forms trigger the investigation, which may result in the disciplinary action, and they support the taxpayer's reasonable cause defense and the civil action. The agent should document the fraud or the misconduct thoroughly, because the documentation is the basis for the forms and the remedies. The forms are the primary mechanism to report the preparer, so they should be filed promptly.

### Evaluate The Reasonable Cause Defense For Penalties

The reasonable cause defense may excuse the taxpayer's penalties (such as the accuracy-related penalty under Section 6662) if the taxpayer relied on the preparer in good faith. The reliance must be reasonable: the preparer must be competent (a licensed professional or a registered preparer), the taxpayer must provide the accurate information, and the taxpayer must review the return for obvious errors. A taxpayer who relies on a fraudulent preparer (who fabricated items the taxpayer did not claim) may have a reasonable cause defense, because the taxpayer did not know of the fabrication. A taxpayer who was aware of the fabrication (a participant) does not have the defense. The reasonable cause defense is fact-intensive, and the taxpayer bears the burden of proof.

Evaluate the reasonable cause defense. Determine whether the taxpayer relied on the preparer in good faith, based on the preparer's competence, the taxpayer's provision of the accurate information, and the taxpayer's review of the return. If the taxpayer was a victim (unaware of the fabrication), the reasonable cause defense may apply, and the taxpayer should document the reliance (the engagement letter, the information provided, the review of the return). If the taxpayer was a participant (aware of the fabrication), the defense does not apply. The reasonable cause defense excuses the penalties, not the tax, so the taxpayer still owes the additional tax. The agent should prepare the defense with the supporting documentation, because the IRS evaluates the defense based on the facts. The reasonable cause defense is the primary remedy for the penalties, so it should be pursued where the taxpayer was a victim.

### Address The Examination Of Multiple Years

The preparer fraud may trigger an examination of multiple years, not just the year of the fraud, because the IRS may investigate the preparer's other returns and the taxpayer's other years. The examination of multiple years increases the taxpayer's exposure (the additional tax, the penalties, and the interest for each year), and the taxpayer should be prepared to address each year. The examination may also reveal additional errors (in the other years) that were not part of the original fraud, so the taxpayer should review all the years prepared by the fraudulent preparer.

Address the examination of multiple years. If the IRS examines the taxpayer, cooperate with the examination and provide the records for each year. Review all the years prepared by the fraudulent preparer, and identify any additional errors (beyond the original fraud), because the IRS may find them. If the additional errors are identified, consider filing amended returns (to correct the errors and to minimize the interest and the penalties) before the IRS assesses. The examination of multiple years is a significant exposure, so the taxpayer should be prepared and should consider engaging a qualified tax professional to manage the examination. The agent should coordinate the response across the years, because the errors may be consistent (the preparer's pattern), and the correction should be comprehensive.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The Preparer's Liability Excuses The Taxpayer

The symptom is a taxpayer who believes the preparer's fraud excuses their liability. The trap is that the taxpayer is liable for the return, regardless of the preparer's conduct, so the taxpayer owes the additional tax. The direction is to advise the taxpayer of their liability and to pursue the remedies (the reasonable cause defense, the Form 14157, the civil action) separately.

### Conflating Preparer Negligence With Fraud

The symptom is an agent who treats a preparer's error as fraud. The trap is that fraud requires intent, while negligence is a failure to exercise reasonable care, so the characterization affects the remedies and the penalties. The direction is to evaluate the preparer's intent and to distinguish fraud from negligence.

### Overlooking The Due Diligence Requirements

The symptom is a taxpayer who claims the EITC without documenting the eligibility. The trap is that the due diligence requirements apply to the EITC, the CTC, the ACTC, the AOTC, and the HOH filing status, so the failure can result in penalties and the disallowance of the credit. The direction is to document the eligibility and to complete the Form 8867.

### Not Filing The Form 14157

The symptom is a taxpayer who was a victim of preparer fraud but does not report the preparer. The trap is that the Form 14157 triggers the IRS's investigation and supports the reasonable cause defense, so the failure to file weakens the taxpayer's case. The direction is to file the Form 14157 and the 14157-A with the supporting evidence.

### Claiming Reasonable Cause For A Participating Taxpayer

The symptom is a taxpayer who was aware of the fabricated items and claims the reasonable cause defense. The trap is that the reasonable cause defense requires good-faith reliance, so a participating taxpayer does not qualify. The direction is to evaluate the taxpayer's role and to reserve the defense for victims.

### Failing To Review The Other Years

The symptom is a taxpayer who corrects the year of the fraud but does not review the other years prepared by the same preparer. The trap is that the fraud may extend to multiple years, so the other years may have errors. The direction is to review all the years and to correct the errors proactively.
