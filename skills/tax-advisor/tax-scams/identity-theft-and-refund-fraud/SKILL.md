# Identity Theft and Refund Fraud

Tax-related identity theft occurs when someone uses a taxpayer's Social Security number (SSN) or Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) to file a fraudulent return and claim a refund. The taxpayer typically discovers the theft when the IRS rejects their e-filed return (because a return was already filed with their SSN), when they receive a notice about a return they did not file, or when they receive a refund or a transcript they did not request. The judgment problem is that the response must be prompt and correct, because a delay extends the refund freeze (which can last months) and allows the fraud to compound (such as the thief claiming additional years or credits). An agent who mishandles the documentation, who misses the notice deadline, or who fails to enroll the taxpayer in the IP PIN system exposes the taxpayer to repeated theft and prolonged financial harm. The refund fraud also interacts with the IRS's fraud filters (which may freeze a legitimate return), the victim assistance program, and the state tax agencies (which may have their own identity theft).

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the Form 14039 (Identity Theft Affidavit) must be filed to alert the IRS, and it should be filed as soon as the theft is discovered, not after the IRS's investigation begins. Second, the IRS issues a CP01 or 5071C notice to verify the return, and the taxpayer must respond within the deadline (often 14 days), or the IRS may treat the return as fraudulent and deny the refund. Third, the Identity Protection PIN (IP PIN) is a six-digit number that the taxpayer must use to file future returns, and enrollment (for victims) prevents future theft, but the taxpayer must remember the PIN each year. Fourth, the IRS's victim assistance program (the Identity Theft Victim Assistance unit) can resolve the case, but it takes time (often 120 to 180 days), and the taxpayer should be advised of the timeline. Fifth, the state tax agencies may have their own identity theft procedures, and the taxpayer should report to the states as well. Sixth, the taxpayer should also report to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) and the credit bureaus, because the tax identity theft may be part of a broader identity theft.

This skill covers tax-related identity theft and refund fraud under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### File Form 14039 Promptly Upon Discovery

The Form 14039 (Identity Theft Affidavit) is the primary document to alert the IRS to the identity theft. It should be filed as soon as the theft is discovered, whether the taxpayer discovers it through a rejected e-file, a notice, or a suspicious refund or transcript. The Form 14039 can be filed electronically (through the IRS's online tool, for certain cases) or by paper (attached to the paper return or mailed separately). The form requires the taxpayer's identifying information, the description of the identity theft, and the tax years affected. The prompt filing triggers the IRS's identity theft investigation and the victim assistance program.

File Form 14039 promptly. As soon as the taxpayer discovers the identity theft (through a rejected e-file, a notice, or a suspicious refund), prepare and file Form 14039. Include the taxpayer's identifying information (name, SSN, address), the description of the theft (how it was discovered), and the tax years affected. File the form electronically (if available) or by paper. The prompt filing triggers the IRS's investigation, which can resolve the case and release the refund. A delayed filing extends the refund freeze and allows the fraud to compound, so the agent should file the form immediately upon discovery. The Form 14039 is the foundation of the identity theft response, and it should be complete and accurate.

### Respond To IRS Verification Notices Within The Deadline

The IRS issues verification notices (such as the 5071C, the CP01B, or the LTR 5747C) to verify a return that the fraud filters flagged or that was filed by the identity thief. The notices ask the taxpayer to verify their identity and their return, often through the IRS's online Identity Verification Service or by phone. The notices have a deadline (often 14 to 30 days), and a failure to respond within the deadline may result in the IRS treating the return as fraudulent and denying the refund. The taxpayer should respond promptly, with the required documentation (such as a prior-year return, a photo ID, and a Social Security card).

Respond to the verification notices within the deadline. When the taxpayer receives a verification notice, identify the deadline (often 14 to 30 days) and the response method (the online service or the phone). Gather the required documentation (a prior-year return, a photo ID, a Social Security card, and the notice itself), and respond within the deadline. The verification confirms the taxpayer's identity and their return, and it allows the IRS to process the return and release the refund. A missed deadline may result in the denial of the refund and a lengthy appeal, so the agent should monitor the deadline and respond promptly. The verification notices are a common point of failure, because the taxpayer may ignore the notice or miss the deadline, so the agent should track the notices and the deadlines.

### Enroll The Taxpayer In The IP PIN System

The Identity Protection PIN (IP PIN) is a six-digit number that the taxpayer must use to file their federal tax return, and it prevents an identity thief from filing a fraudulent return with the taxpayer's SSN (because the thief would not have the PIN). The IP PIN is available to all taxpayers (through the online Get an IP PIN tool), and it is mandatory for victims of identity theft (the IRS issues the PIN to victims automatically). The IP PIN is valid for one calendar year, and a new PIN is issued each year, so the taxpayer must retrieve the new PIN each January. The IP PIN is a powerful protection, but it requires the taxpayer to remember the PIN and to use it on the return (or the return is rejected).

Enroll the taxpayer in the IP PIN system. For a victim of identity theft, the IRS issues the IP PIN automatically (after the Form 14039 is processed), and the taxpayer receives the PIN by mail (or through the online tool). For a non-victim, the taxpayer can opt in through the online Get an IP PIN tool (which requires identity verification). Advise the taxpayer to retrieve the new PIN each January and to use it on the return. The IP PIN prevents future theft, so it is a critical protection for a victim. The taxpayer should store the PIN securely (because a lost PIN requires a retrieval process through the IRS), and the agent should confirm the PIN is used on each year's return. The IP PIN is the best preventive measure, so the agent should enroll the taxpayer and emphasize the annual retrieval.

### Navigate The IRS Victim Assistance Program

The IRS's Identity Theft Victim Assistance (IDTVA) program resolves identity theft cases and releases the refunds. The program is triggered by the Form 14039, and it assigns the case to a specialist who investigates the theft, corrects the account, and releases the refund. The process takes time (often 120 to 180 days, and sometimes longer for complex cases), and the taxpayer should be advised of the timeline. The taxpayer can check the status through the IRS's online tool or by phone, and they can contact the Taxpayer Advocate Service if the case is delayed or if they are experiencing a hardship.

Navigate the victim assistance program. After filing Form 14039, monitor the case through the IRS's online tool or by phone. The IDTVA program takes 120 to 180 days (and sometimes longer), so the taxpayer should be advised of the timeline and the potential for a longer resolution. If the case is delayed beyond the timeline, or if the taxpayer is experiencing a financial hardship (such as an inability to pay bills due to the frozen refund), contact the Taxpayer Advocate Service, which can expedite the case. The agent should document the case number, the specialist's contact information, and the status updates, because the case may require follow-up over several months. The victim assistance program is the resolution mechanism, but it is slow, so the agent should manage the taxpayer's expectations and advocate for a timely resolution.

### Report To State Agencies And The FTC

The tax identity theft may affect the state tax returns as well, because the thief may file a fraudulent state return (or the state may have its own fraud filters). The taxpayer should report the identity theft to the state tax agency (or agencies) where they file, following the state's procedures (which may include a state identity theft form or a phone report). The taxpayer should also report to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) through IdentityTheft.gov, which creates a recovery plan and notifies the relevant parties. The credit bureaus should be notified (to place a fraud alert or a credit freeze), because the tax identity theft may be part of a broader identity theft.

Report to the state agencies and the FTC. For each state where the taxpayer files, report the identity theft to the state tax agency, following the state's procedures. File a report with the FTC through IdentityTheft.gov, which creates a recovery plan and provides a sworn affidavit that the taxpayer can use with the IRS, the state agencies, and the creditors. Place a fraud alert or a credit freeze with the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion), because the theft may extend beyond the tax return. The broader reporting is important, because the tax identity theft is often part of a larger identity theft, and the taxpayer should protect their credit and their financial accounts. The agent should coordinate the federal, the state, and the FTC reporting, because the theft may have multiple dimensions.

### Prevent Future Theft Through Protective Measures

The taxpayer should take protective measures to prevent future identity theft, including the IP PIN enrollment, the secure storage of personal information, the use of strong passwords and multi-factor authentication for financial accounts, and the caution with phishing emails and phone calls (which are common vectors for identity theft). The taxpayer should also monitor their IRS account (through the online tool) and their credit reports (through AnnualCreditReport.com) for suspicious activity. The preventive measures reduce the risk of repeat theft, which is a significant concern for victims.

Implement the protective measures. Enroll the taxpayer in the IP PIN system (as above). Advise the taxpayer to store their personal information securely (including their SSN card, their tax documents, and their IP PIN) and to use strong passwords and multi-factor authentication for their financial and tax software accounts. Caution the taxpayer about phishing emails and phone calls (which impersonate the IRS or the tax software), because these are common vectors for identity theft. Advise the taxpayer to monitor their IRS account (through the online tool) and their credit reports (through AnnualCreditReport.com) for suspicious activity. The preventive measures are the long-term protection, and the agent should educate the taxpayer on the measures, because a victim is at a higher risk of repeat theft. The IP PIN is the most important measure, but the broader security practices are also essential.

## Common Traps

### Delaying The Form 14039 Filing

The symptom is a taxpayer who discovers the identity theft but waits to file Form 14039. The trap is that the delay extends the refund freeze and allows the fraud to compound, so the resolution takes longer. The direction is to file Form 14039 promptly upon discovery.

### Missing A Verification Notice Deadline

The symptom is a taxpayer who receives a 5071C notice and does not respond within 14 days. The trap is that the IRS may treat the return as fraudulent and deny the refund, so the taxpayer must appeal. The direction is to respond to the verification notices within the deadline.

### Failing To Enroll In The IP PIN System

The symptom is a victim who does not enroll in the IP PIN system. The trap is that the victim is exposed to repeat theft, because the thief can file another fraudulent return. The direction is to enroll the victim in the IP PIN system and to retrieve the PIN each January.

### Overlooking The State Tax Agencies

The symptom is a taxpayer who reports the federal identity theft but not the state. The trap is that the thief may have filed a fraudulent state return, so the state may deny the legitimate return. The direction is to report to the state tax agency for each state where the taxpayer files.

### Not Reporting To The FTC And Credit Bureaus

The symptom is a taxpayer who reports the tax identity theft but not the broader identity theft. The trap is that the theft may extend beyond the tax return, so the taxpayer's credit and financial accounts are at risk. The direction is to report to the FTC and to place a fraud alert or a credit freeze.

### Forgetting The Annual IP PIN Retrieval

The symptom is a victim who enrolled in the IP PIN system but forgot to retrieve the new PIN the next January. The trap is that the return is rejected without the PIN, so the filing is delayed. The direction is to retrieve the new IP PIN each January and to use it on the return.
