# AGI Limit And Carryforward Mechanics

Charitable contribution deductions are not limited by the amount given but by a percentage of the donor's adjusted gross income (AGI), and the percentage itself depends on two variables that agents routinely conflate: the type of donee organization and the type of property contributed. The judgment problem is that a single taxpayer can face three different ceilings in the same year — 60% for cash to a public charity, 30% for appreciated long-term capital gain property to a public charity, and 20% for capital gain property to a private foundation — and excess contributions above the applicable ceiling do not vanish but carry forward for up to five years, subject to the same limits in each carryforward year. Getting the percentage wrong, misclassifying the charity, or botching the carryforward sequencing changes both the current-year deduction and the timing of every future deduction.

The harm from casual AGI-limit reasoning is a deduction that is either overstated (triggering disallowance, accuracy-related penalties, and the loss of carryforward capacity that was never properly tracked) or understated (leaving usable deduction on the table and letting carryforwards expire unused). Agents frequently apply a flat 50% limit that no longer reflects current law, fail to distinguish a public charity from a private foundation or donor-advised fund sponsor, forget that the carryforward is consumed in a specific order against each year's fresh ceiling, and never reconcile the running carryforward balance. The result is that a large gift made in a high-income year can silently expire five years later because nobody tracked the remaining balance against each intervening year's AGI.

This skill covers the computation of the charitable AGI percentage limits and the five-year carryforward mechanics under U.S. federal income tax law. It does not cover substantiation requirements, appreciated-asset donation strategy, or the qualified-organization determination, each of which warrants separate analysis. Nothing here is tax advice; the limits involve fact-specific determinations (charity classification, property characterization, AGI computation) that must be verified, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Dollar amounts and percentage limits reflect current law, and certain TCJA-era provisions are scheduled to change, so confirm the rules applicable to the specific tax year.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Donee Type Before Selecting The Percentage

The AGI percentage limit turns first on whether the recipient is a public charity or a private foundation. Cash contributions to public charities — which include churches, schools, hospitals, and the sponsoring organizations of donor-advised funds — are deductible up to 60% of AGI. Cash contributions to most private foundations (non-operating) are limited to 30% of AGI. A donor-advised fund is itself a public charity for limit purposes because the sponsoring organization is a public charity, so cash to a DAF gets the 60% limit, not the 30% private-foundation limit.

Before applying any percentage, confirm the donee's classification using its IRS letter or the Tax Exempt Organization Search, because the label a donor uses casually ("my foundation," "the charity") does not control. A community foundation is generally a public charity; a family private foundation is not. The difference is large: a $120,000 cash gift by a taxpayer with $100,000 AGI is fully deductible in one year at the 60% limit (which permits $60,000), with $60,000 carrying forward, whereas the same gift to a private foundation permits only $30,000 currently with $90,000 carrying forward. Do not assume 60%; verify the donee type first.

### Match The Property Type To The Correct Ceiling

The second variable is the type of property contributed. Cash to a public charity is 60% of AGI, but appreciated long-term capital gain property (stock held more than one year, real estate) contributed to a public charity is limited to 30% of AGI when the donor deducts fair market value. Ordinary-income property and short-term capital gain property are generally limited to the lesser of basis or fair market value and are treated under the 50%/30% framework depending on the donee. Capital gain property contributed to a private foundation is generally limited to 20% of AGI.

The property-type limit is the most commonly misapplied rule. A donor giving $100,000 of appreciated stock (held long-term) to a public charity with $100,000 AGI can deduct only $30,000 in the current year, not $60,000 — the 30% capital-gain-property limit overrides the 60% cash limit even though the donee is a public charity. Confirm the holding period (long-term versus short-term changes everything), whether the property is capital gain property, and whether the donor is electing to reduce the deduction by the long-term capital gain (the basis-only election, relevant when the 50% limit is preferable). Do not apply the 60% cash limit to a stock or real-estate gift.

### Compute The Limit On AGI, Not Taxable Income

All charitable percentage limits are computed against AGI, not taxable income or gross income. This matters because above-the-line deductions (retirement contributions, student loan interest, the deductible part of self-employment tax, HSA contributions) reduce AGI and therefore shrink the charitable ceiling. A taxpayer with $100,000 of wage income who contributes $19,000 to a 401(k) has AGI of roughly $81,000, so the 60% cash ceiling is $48,600, not $60,000.

When planning a large gift, compute the AGI ceiling after all above-the-line adjustments, and recognize that the ceiling can move year to year as income fluctuates. A bonus, a Roth conversion, or a business sale increases AGI and raises the ceiling, which is why bunching charitable gifts into a high-income year is a common and valid strategy. Conversely, a retirement year with low AGI may be a poor year to realize a large charitable deduction because the ceiling is low. Always state the ceiling as a percentage of the specific year's AGI, not a fixed dollar amount.

### Carry Forward Excess Contributions For Up To Five Years

Contributions exceeding the AGI ceiling are not lost; they carry forward for up to five successive tax years. The carryforward is subject to the same percentage limits in each carryforward year, applied against that year's AGI. Any amount still unused after the fifth carryforward year expires permanently and yields no deduction.

The five-year window is a hard deadline, and expiration is silent and unforgiving. A donor with $200,000 AGI who gives $200,000 of appreciated stock to a public charity deducts $60,000 (30%) in year one and carries forward $140,000. In year two, if AGI is again $200,000 and no new gift is made, $60,000 of carryforward is used, leaving $80,000, and so on. Track the running balance and the year-by-year expiration schedule, because once a carryforward layer ages past five years it cannot be recovered. Do not treat the carryforward as indefinite or self-managing; it must be affirmatively claimed on each return.

### Sequence Current-Year Contributions Ahead Of Carryforwards

Within each tax year, current-year contributions are deducted first, and carryforwards from prior years are deducted only to the extent the current-year contributions have not exhausted the AGI ceiling. This ordering rule means a fresh large gift in a carryforward year can starve the older carryforward, pushing it closer to expiration. Each carryforward year retains its own character (the percentage limit and donee type it carried when originally contributed).

When a donor has a carryforward and is considering a new gift, model the sequencing. If year-one carryforward of $60,000 (30% property limit) exists and the donor makes a new $50,000 cash gift in year two with a $100,000 AGI ceiling of $60,000 cash, the current-year cash gift consumes $50,000 of the $60,000 ceiling, leaving only $10,000 for any carryforward that shares or is subordinate to the remaining ceiling — though carryforwards retain their own category limit and are applied in order of time (oldest first). The interaction is intricate; compute it explicitly rather than assuming the carryforward is automatically absorbed. Prioritize using carryforwards in years when new giving is low or AGI is high.

### Apply The Special 100% Cash Limit Only Where It Applies

For tax years 2020 and 2021 only, the CARES Act temporarily raised the cash-to-public-charity limit to 100% of AGI. That temporary provision has expired; the limit reverted to 60% for 2022 and later. A separate enhanced limit of up to 60% (formerly 30%) applies to certain cash contributions to private foundations and fraternal societies, but the baseline rules above govern the common cases.

Do not apply the 100% limit to current or future years, and do not assume a special limit without confirming the exact statute and year. Agents who learned the CARES Act rules during 2020-2021 sometimes carry the 100% figure forward erroneously. Confirm the limit applicable to the specific contribution year, and flag that temporary enhancements are not permanent.

### Reconcile Carryforwards Across Returns And Filing Status

Carryforwards must be tracked consistently across successive returns, and a change in filing status (marriage, divorce, death of a spouse) affects how remaining carryforwards are used. On a joint return, each spouse's carryforwards are applied against that spouse's share of joint AGI. At death, a decedent's unused charitable carryforward generally expires; it does not pass to the surviving spouse or the estate.

Maintain a running schedule of each carryforward layer: original year, donee type, property type, percentage limit, original amount, amount used each year, and remaining balance. This schedule is essential for accurate return preparation and for audit defense, because the IRS may question a carryforward claim that lacks a documented trail back to the contribution year. Do not let carryforwards exist only in someone's memory; they expire on a fixed clock.

## Common Traps

### Applying A Flat 50% Limit That No Longer Reflects Law

The symptom is a donor or preparer who defaults to "charitable is 50% of AGI." The trap is that the 50% cash limit was the pre-2018 rule for many cases and has been superseded by 60% for cash to public charities, while appreciated property remains 30%. Applying 50% understates the cash deduction and misstates the property deduction. Confirm the current-year percentage for the specific donee and property type rather than relying on a remembered round number.

### Treating A Donor-Advised Fund As A Private Foundation

The symptom is assuming a DAF contribution is limited to 30% because it sounds like a "fund" or "foundation." The trap is that a DAF is sponsored by a public charity, so cash contributions qualify for the 60% limit and appreciated property for the 30% limit, the same as direct gifts to a public charity. Misclassifying the DAF as a private foundation understates the deduction and inflates the carryforward unnecessarily.

### Giving Appreciated Stock But Applying The 60% Cash Limit

The symptom is deducting a large stock gift up to 60% of AGI. The trap is that long-term appreciated capital gain property to a public charity is capped at 30% of AGI, so applying 60% overstates the current-year deduction and creates an understated carryforward. Verify the holding period and property characterization before selecting the percentage.

### Letting A Carryforward Expire Because Nobody Tracked It

The symptom is a large gift made years ago with no follow-through, and a deduction silently vanishing after year five. The trap is that the five-year carryforward is not automatic; it must be claimed each year, and once a layer ages out it is permanently lost. The direction is to maintain a year-by-year carryforward schedule and affirmatively use it, especially in low-giving or high-AGI years.

### Starving Old Carryforwards With New Gifts In The Same Year

The symptom is a donor who keeps giving generously while an older carryforward dwindles toward expiration. The trap is the sequencing rule that current-year contributions are deducted first, so a fresh gift can consume the ceiling and leave no room for the carryforward. The direction is to model the interaction before making a new gift when a material carryforward exists, and to consider pausing new giving to let the carryforward absorb.

### Forgetting The Limit Is On AGI, Not Taxable Income

The symptom is computing the ceiling on taxable income or after the standard deduction. The trap is that the percentage limits apply to AGI, which is before itemized deductions, so using the wrong base misstates the ceiling. Compute the ceiling on AGI as reported, after above-the-line adjustments but before itemized or standard deductions.

### Assuming Carryforwards Survive Death Or Filing-Status Change

The symptom is expecting a decedent's charitable carryforward to help the surviving spouse or estate. The trap is that a decedent's unused carryforward generally expires at death and does not transfer, and filing-status changes complicate joint-return allocation. The direction is to use material carryforwards before anticipated death or status changes and not to assume transferability.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the donee been classified as a public charity or private foundation (using its IRS determination letter or the Tax Exempt Organization Search) before selecting the percentage limit?
- [ ] Has the property type been confirmed — cash (60% to public charity), long-term appreciated capital gain property (30% to public charity, 20% to private foundation) — with the holding period verified?
- [ ] Is the percentage limit computed against AGI (after above-the-line adjustments, before itemized or standard deductions) for the specific contribution year?
- [ ] Has any excess contribution been carried forward for up to five years, with a running schedule tracking original year, donee type, property type, percentage limit, amount used, and remaining balance?
- [ ] Has the sequencing rule been applied — current-year contributions deducted first, carryforwards in chronological order — so that new gifts do not inadvertently starve older carryforwards toward expiration?
- [ ] Has the temporary 100% cash limit been confined to 2020-2021 only, with the 60% limit confirmed for 2022 and later years?
- [ ] Has the effect of filing-status change or death on carryforwards been considered, recognizing that a decedent's unused carryforward generally expires?
- [ ] Has the carryforward schedule been documented on each successive return to support both accurate deduction and audit defense?
- [ ] Are uncertain classification, property-characterization, and carryforward-reconciliation questions escalated to a qualified tax professional rather than answered from general assumptions, since this guidance is educational and not personalized tax advice?
