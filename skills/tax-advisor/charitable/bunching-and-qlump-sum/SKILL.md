# Bunching And Qualified Charitable Distribution

Two of the most valuable charitable planning moves share a single judgment problem: both only produce a benefit when they cross a specific threshold, and both are easy to recommend in a form that yields nothing. Bunching concentrates two or more years of intended giving into one tax year so that total itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction, letting the donor itemize that year and take the standard deduction in the surrounding years. The Qualified Charitable Distribution lets an IRA owner who is age 70 and a half or older direct up to an indexed limit (roughly $100,000, rising with inflation) straight from the IRA to a qualified charity, where the distribution counts toward the required minimum distribution and is excluded from gross income rather than being taken into income and deducted. The judgment problem is that bunching only helps when the bunched total actually clears the standard deduction, and the QCD only beats a cash donation when the donor would otherwise take the standard deduction — because a QCD is an exclusion, not an itemized deduction, it benefits donors who cannot use a charitable deduction at all.

The harm from casual bunching and QCD reasoning is recommending the right strategy in the wrong form. Agents recommend bunching without running the itemized-versus-standard math, so the donor irrevocably locks funds into a donor-advised fund for no benefit. They recommend a QCD to a donor who already itemizes, where it is at best neutral and sometimes worse than a cash deduction. They miss that the QCD must flow directly from the IRA custodian to the charity — a check written to the charity and deposited by the donor first is not a QCD. They forget the age trigger is 70 and a half, not 73 or the RMD age. They overlook that QCDs cannot fund donor-advised funds, private foundations, or split-interest gifts except for the one-time election. And they double-count by treating the QCD as both an income exclusion and a charitable deduction.

This skill covers the bunching strategy and the Qualified Charitable Distribution from IRAs under U.S. federal income tax law, and how each interacts with the standard deduction. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; charitable deduction rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and dollar amounts are indexed. A qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Run The Itemized-Versus-Standard Math Before Bunching

The charitable deduction only reduces tax when the donor itemizes, and itemizing only beats the standard deduction when total itemized deductions (charitable plus state and local tax up to the SALT cap, mortgage interest, medical over the floor) exceed the standard deduction for the filing status. In a year where itemized deductions fall short, charitable gifts produce no federal tax benefit at all. Bunching is the primary tool to clear that hurdle, by concentrating two or more years of intended giving into one tax year so the bunched total pushes the donor over the standard deduction.

Model the comparison for the bunching year and each surrounding year. A married couple with $15,000 of annual giving who take the standard deduction get no benefit in normal years; bunching three years ($45,000) into one year, often through a donor-advised fund, can push them over the standard deduction so the full $45,000 is deductible that year, while they take the standard deduction in the other two years. The net benefit is the extra deduction captured in the bunching year, not the gross bunched amount. Bunching only helps when the bunched total plus other itemized deductions actually exceeds the standard deduction — confirm the arithmetic before locking funds irrevocably, and remember the AGI percentage ceiling still applies to the bunched amount. ### Use A Donor-Advised Fund To Make Bunching Feasible

Bunching is mechanically hard without a holding vehicle, because the donor would have to choose all the ultimate operating charities in the funding year. A donor-advised fund solves this: the donor makes an irrevocable, immediately deductible contribution in the bunching year and then recommends grants to operating charities in later years. The DAF contribution is deductible when funded, which is what makes the bunching year's itemized total exceed the standard deduction.

Before recommending a DAF for bunching, confirm three things. First, that the bunched amount clears the standard deduction (the math above). Second, that the donor understands the contribution is irrevocable and grants must go to IRS-qualified public charities — not individuals, not political organizations, not private benefit — and that the donor retains advisory privilege only, not control. Third, that funding the DAF with appreciated long-term stock rather than cash layers the capital-gains-avoidance benefit on top of the bunching benefit. Do not present a DAF as a personal savings account; its only legitimate use is to time a genuinely charitable gift.

### Recognize The QCD As An Income Exclusion, Not A Deduction

A Qualified Charitable Distribution is a direct transfer from an IRA to a qualified charity by an owner who has reached age 70 and a half. The distributed amount (up to the indexed limit) counts toward the year's required minimum distribution and is excluded from gross income. This is fundamentally different from taking the distribution into income and then deducting it as a charitable gift: the QCD is an above-the-line exclusion, so it lowers adjusted gross income, which can lower the taxable portion of Social Security, preserve the SALT-cap headroom, and reduce the threshold for medical and miscellaneous floors.

The QCD's distinctive value is for donors who take the standard deduction and therefore get no benefit from a charitable deduction at all. A standard-deduction donor who gives $10,000 to charity gets no federal tax benefit from the gift, but the same $10,000 sent as a QCD from the IRA is excluded from income, lowering AGI and taxable income directly. For an itemizing donor, the QCD is roughly neutral against a cash deduction (and sometimes worse, because the exclusion does not add to itemized deductions), so do not reflexively steer itemizers into QCDs. Model both paths against the donor's actual deduction posture.

### Confirm The Age Trigger, The Direct-Transfer Mechanic, And The Limit

Three conditions must be met exactly or the distribution is not a QCD. The IRA owner must be age 70 and a half or older at the time of the distribution — not 73, not the RMD age, not "around 70." The distribution must go directly from the IRA custodian to the qualified charity (a trustee-to-trustee transfer, or a check payable to the charity that the custodian issues from the IRA); a distribution paid to the owner who then writes a check to the charity is not a QCD, even if the money ends up at the charity. And the total QCDs for the year cannot exceed the indexed limit (roughly $100,000 per owner, adjusted for inflation in future years).

Verify each condition before treating a distribution as a QCD. Confirm the owner's date of birth against the 70-and-a-half threshold, not a rounded age. Confirm the transfer path with the custodian — a check made payable to the charity but delivered through the owner can still qualify if it is an IRA-issued check, but a check payable to the owner does not. Track the cumulative QCD total for the year against the indexed limit, and confirm the charity is a qualified public charity, because QCDs cannot go to donor-advised funds, supporting organizations, or private foundations. Dollar figures are illustrative and indexed.

### Time The QCD Against The Required Minimum Distribution

Because a QCD counts toward the required minimum distribution, the timing of the QCD relative to other RMD distributions matters. If the owner takes the full RMD as taxable income first and then does a QCD, the QCD no longer reduces the RMD (the RMD is already satisfied), so the income-reduction benefit is lost — the QCD still works as a charitable transfer but it does not reduce taxable RMD income. The highest-value sequence is to do the QCD early in the year, before or in lieu of other RMD distributions, so it satisfies part or all of the RMD with pre-tax dollars that never enter income.

Plan the sequence before year-end. For an owner whose RMD is $20,000 and who intends $10,000 of charitable giving, directing $10,000 as a QCD first satisfies half the RMD with excluded income, leaving $10,000 of taxable RMD to take. If the owner instead takes the full $20,000 RMD as income and then donates $10,000, the donor has $20,000 of taxable income and, if taking the standard deduction, no offsetting deduction — strictly worse. Coordinate QCD timing with the RMD schedule, and note that QCDs can be made before the RMD age (the 70-and-a-half threshold is earlier than the current RMD age), which is a planning advantage for owners in that window.

### Respect The QCD Recipient Restrictions And The Split-Interest Election

A QCD can go only to a qualified charity — generally a public charity eligible to receive deductible contributions — and cannot fund a donor-advised fund, a supporting organization, or a private foundation. This restriction is what makes the QCD unavailable for the bunching-via-DAF strategy; a donor who wants to bunch must use after-tax cash or appreciated assets into a DAF, not IRA dollars via QCD. A separate one-time election allows a QCD of up to the indexed limit to fund a split-interest gift (a charitable remainder trust or charitable gift annuity), but only once per lifetime and subject to specific rules.

Before directing a QCD, confirm the recipient is an eligible public charity and not a disallowed vehicle. Do not attempt to fund a DAF or family foundation with a QCD; it will be recharacterized as a taxable distribution. If a split-interest vehicle is intended, confirm the one-time election has not already been used and that the structure meets the statutory requirements. The recipient restriction is a hard line that converts an otherwise valid transfer into ordinary taxable income if crossed.

## Common Traps

### Bunching Without Clearing The Standard Deduction

The symptom is funding a donor-advised fund to bunch giving without running the itemized-versus-standard math. The trap is that if the bunched total plus other itemized deductions does not exceed the standard deduction, bunching yields no incremental benefit and irrevocably locks up the funds. The direction is to model the comparison for the bunching year and surrounding years before funding.

### Recommending A QCD To A Donor Who Already Itemizes

The symptom is steering an itemizing donor into a QCD as if it were always better than cash. The trap is that for an itemizer the QCD is roughly neutral against a cash deduction and sometimes worse, because the exclusion does not add to itemized deductions; the QCD's real value is for standard-deduction donors who get no benefit from a charitable deduction. The direction is to model both paths against the donor's actual deduction posture before choosing.

### Missing The 70-And-A-Half Age Trigger

The symptom is treating the RMD age (73) or a rounded age as the QCD eligibility threshold. The trap is that the QCD age trigger is 70 and a half, which is earlier than the current RMD age, so a donor who waits until RMD age loses years of available QCD capacity. The direction is to confirm the owner's date of birth against the 70-and-a-half threshold.

### Letting The Distribution Pass Through The Owner's Hands

The symptom is an IRA distribution paid to the owner who then writes a check to the charity. The trap is that a distribution payable to the owner is not a QCD even if the money ends up at the charity; it is taxable income to the owner with a separate (possibly unusable) charitable deduction. The direction is to use a direct trustee-to-trustee transfer or an IRA-issued check payable to the charity.

### Taking The Full RMD Before The QCD

The symptom is an owner who withdraws the full required minimum distribution as taxable income and then does a QCD. The trap is that the RMD is already satisfied, so the QCD no longer reduces taxable RMD income; the income-reduction benefit is lost. The direction is to do the QCD early in the year, before or in lieu of other RMD distributions.

### Trying To Fund A DAF Or Private Foundation With A QCD

The symptom is directing IRA dollars to a donor-advised fund or family foundation expecting QCD treatment. The trap is that QCDs can go only to qualified public charities, not DAFs, supporting organizations, or private foundations, so the transfer is recharacterized as a taxable distribution. The direction is to confirm the recipient is an eligible public charity before directing the transfer.

### Double-Counting The QCD As Both Exclusion And Deduction

The symptom is treating the QCD amount as excluded from income and also claiming it as a charitable deduction. The trap is that the same dollars cannot be both excluded and deducted; the QCD is an exclusion, not an itemized deduction. The direction is to exclude the QCD from income and not claim a separate charitable deduction for it.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Before recommending bunching, has the itemized-versus-standard deduction math been run for the bunching year and each surrounding year, confirming the bunched total plus other itemized deductions actually exceeds the standard deduction?
- [ ] If a donor-advised fund is used for bunching, has the donor acknowledged the contribution is irrevocable, grants are restricted to qualified public charities, and the AGI percentage ceiling still applies to the bunched amount?
- [ ] Has the Qualified Charitable Distribution been evaluated against the donor's actual deduction posture, recognizing that the QCD's distinctive value is for standard-deduction donors who get no benefit from a charitable deduction?
- [ ] Has the IRA owner's age been confirmed against the 70-and-a-half threshold (not the RMD age), and has the indexed 100,000 dollar limit been applied to the cumulative QCD total for the year?
- [ ] Has the transfer been structured as a direct trustee-to-trustee transfer or an IRA-issued check payable to the charity, rather than a distribution payable to the owner?
- [ ] Has the QCD been timed before or in lieu of other required minimum distributions so it reduces taxable RMD income, rather than after the full RMD has been taken?
- [ ] Has the recipient been confirmed as an eligible public charity, with no attempt to fund a donor-advised fund, supporting organization, or private foundation via QCD (except for the one-time split-interest election if applicable)?
- [ ] Has the QCD been excluded from income without also claiming a separate charitable deduction for the same dollars?
- [ ] Has the one-time QCD-to-split-interest election been checked for prior use before any charitable remainder trust or gift annuity funding?
- [ ] Are uncertain threshold, timing, and QCD-eligibility questions escalated to a qualified tax professional rather than answered from general assumptions, since this guidance is educational and not personalized tax advice?
