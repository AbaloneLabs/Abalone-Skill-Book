# Appreciated Asset And DAF Giving

Donating appreciated long-term assets instead of selling them and donating the cash is one of the few tax moves that produces two independent benefits at once: the donor avoids the capital gains tax that a sale would trigger, and the donor deducts the asset's fair market value rather than the lower cost basis. The judgment problem is that this double benefit is not automatic — it depends on a precise stack of conditions that an agent must verify before recommending the strategy. The asset must be long-term capital gain property held more than one year, the donee must be a qualified public charity or donor-advised fund sponsor, the deduction is capped at 30% of AGI for capital gain property, and any bargain-sale, partial-interest, related-party, or quid pro quo wrinkle can collapse the benefit into a basis-only deduction or a taxable event. Layered on top is the donor-advised fund, a vehicle that turns a one-time appreciated-asset gift into a multi-year granting plan.

The harm from casual appreciated-asset reasoning is that agents present the strategy as universally beneficial and skip the conditions that decide whether it works. They recommend donating stock without confirming the holding period, so short-term stock yields only a basis deduction and zero capital-gains avoidance. They recommend donating to a private foundation and expect the 30% fair-market-value limit, when capital gain property to a private foundation is generally capped at 20%. They donate depreciated assets, which is strictly worse than selling to harvest the loss and donating cash. They sell the asset first "to keep it simple" and destroy the capital-gains avoidance, the strategy's core benefit. And they treat a donor-advised fund as a personal savings account, ignoring that the contribution is irrevocable, that grants must go to qualified charities, and that bunching only helps when the bunched amount clears the standard deduction.

This skill covers the judgment needed to evaluate donating appreciated long-term assets and to use a donor-advised fund for timing and bunching under U.S. federal income tax law. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; charitable deduction rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and dollar amounts are indexed. A qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Property Is Long-Term Capital Gain Property

The fair-market-value deduction and the capital-gains avoidance apply only to long-term capital gain property — assets held more than one year that would produce long-term capital gain on sale. Publicly traded stock, mutual fund shares, ETFs, real estate, and certain cryptocurrency held over one year qualify. Short-term holdings (one year or less), inventory, and ordinary-income property do not qualify; they are deductible only at the lesser of basis or fair market value, and donating short-term property does not avoid the short-term gain that a sale would have triggered.

Verify the holding period against brokerage or acquisition records, not the donor's memory, before recommending the donation. A donor who bought shares nine months ago and donates them gets only a basis deduction and no capital-gains benefit — materially worse than waiting three weeks to cross the one-year mark. Confirm the asset is capital gain property (not a collectible with special rules, not inventory) and that it has actually appreciated. The entire double benefit rides on this single threshold, so do not recommend donating an asset whose holding period or character is unverified.

### Donate Appreciated Assets, Never Depreciated Ones

The strategy is sharply asymmetric: it is excellent for assets that have risen and counterproductive for assets that have fallen. For an appreciated asset, donating it directly avoids the capital gains tax on the embedded gain and deducts the full fair market value. For a depreciated asset, donating it forfeits the unrealized loss — the charity takes the donor's basis, and the donor cannot claim the loss — so the donor is strictly better off selling to realize the deductible capital loss and then donating the cash proceeds.

When evaluating a portfolio for charitable giving, separate appreciated from depreciated lots and handle each correctly. Donate the highest-gain, longest-held shares first, because they carry the largest embedded gain to avoid. For depreciated holdings, sell first to realize the deductible loss, then donate cash. A common error is to donate a mixed basket of lots without this analysis, inadvertently giving away loss assets and surrendering their tax value. Confirm each lot's unrealized gain or loss before designating it for donation.

### Quantify The Double Benefit Against The Sell-Then-Donate-Cash Alternative

When long-term appreciated capital gain property is donated to a qualified public charity or donor-advised fund sponsor, the donor deducts the fair market value at the contribution date and does not recognize the long-term capital gain that a sale would have triggered. For publicly traded stock, fair market value is the average of the high and low trading prices on the contribution date. The benefit relative to selling then donating cash is exactly the capital gains tax that would have been paid.

Quantify the comparison before recommending the strategy. A donor with $50,000 of stock purchased for $10,000 faces a $40,000 long-term gain if sold; at a 15% rate plus possible 3.8% net investment income tax, the sale costs roughly $7,500 in tax, leaving $42,500 to donate. Donating the shares directly yields a $50,000 deduction and zero capital gains tax. The spread is the tax saved. Dollar figures are illustrative. For high-basis-impact assets like real estate or concentrated stock, the savings can be large, but confirm the fair market value is supportable (market price for traded assets, qualified appraisal for non-traded) and that the donee is a public charity, not a private foundation where the 20% limit and basis-only rules may apply.

### Apply The 30% AGI Limit And Carry Forward The Excess

Appreciated capital gain property contributed to a public charity or donor-advised fund sponsor is deductible up to 30% of AGI, not 60%, with the excess carrying forward for up to five years. A donor who makes a very large appreciated-asset gift in a single year often cannot deduct it all currently, which is precisely where a donor-advised fund's bunching function interacts with the carryforward.

Compute the 30% ceiling against the year's AGI and project the carryforward schedule before finalizing a large gift. A donor with $200,000 AGI giving $200,000 of appreciated stock deducts $60,000 currently (30%) and carries $140,000 forward, used over subsequent years subject to the same 30% limit. Confirm the donor will have enough future AGI to absorb the carryforward before it expires, and consider timing the gift into a high-income year (a business sale, a bonus, a Roth conversion) to widen the ceiling. Do not assume a large appreciated-asset gift is fully deductible in the contribution year; the 30% limit is binding and the carryforward must be modeled.

### Use A Donor-Advised Fund As A Timing And Bunching Vehicle

A donor-advised fund is an account held at a sponsoring public charity (a community foundation or a commercial DAF sponsor) that lets a donor make an irrevocable, immediately deductible contribution in one year and then recommend grants to operating charities in later years. Because the contribution to the DAF is deductible in the year funded, a donor can bunch several years of intended giving into one tax year to exceed the standard deduction, itemize that year, and take the standard deduction in the surrounding years. The DAF makes bunching feasible because the donor need not choose the ultimate operating charities in the funding year.

Model the comparison before funding. A married couple with $15,000 of annual giving who take the standard deduction get no charitable benefit in normal years; bunching three years ($45,000), often by transferring appreciated stock into a DAF, lets them itemize the funding year and deduct the full $45,000, while taking the standard deduction in the other two years. Bunching only helps when the bunched total plus other itemized deductions exceeds the standard deduction for the year. Confirm the donor understands the contribution is irrevocable, that grants must go to IRS-qualified public charities (not individuals, not political organizations, not private benefit), and that the donor retains advisory privilege only, not control. Do not present a DAF as a way to retain personal benefit from the funds.

### Transfer The Asset Directly, Never Sell Then Donate

For the capital-gains avoidance to work, the donor must transfer the appreciated asset directly to the charity or DAF — reassigning shares, transferring the deed, or moving the asset in kind. If the donor sells the asset and donates the cash proceeds, the sale is a taxable event and the capital gains tax is owed, eliminating the primary benefit of the strategy. This is the single most important mechanical step and the easiest to botch under time pressure.

Confirm the transfer mechanism before acting. For brokerage stock, initiate a direct in-kind transfer of shares to the charity's or DAF's brokerage account, not a sale followed by a cash wire. For real estate, a deed transfer is required, and the charity must accept the property (many have acceptance policies and may decline encumbered or illiquid property). Document the contribution date, because fair market value is measured on that date and the holding-period and limit computations depend on it. A donor who sells first "to keep it simple" has inadvertently destroyed the strategy's core benefit.

### Screen For Bargain Sales, Partial Interests, And Related Parties

Several structures erode or eliminate the appreciated-asset benefit. A bargain sale — selling appreciated property to a charity for less than fair market value — is partly a sale and partly a gift; the donor recognizes gain on the sale portion proportional to the consideration received, so only part of the transaction gets charitable treatment. A partial-interest contribution (less than the donor's entire interest) generally is not deductible unless it fits a qualified exception such as a remainder interest in a personal residence. A contribution where the donor receives a benefit (naming rights, event tickets, goods) must reduce the deduction by the value of the benefit received, and gifts to family-controlled private foundations face the 20% limit and excess-business-holdings scrutiny.

Before treating a transfer as a clean appreciated-asset donation, confirm there is no consideration, no retained interest, and no quid pro quo. A donor who "donates" real estate but keeps a life estate has made a partial-interest gift with different deduction rules. A donor who receives gala tickets worth $200 for a $1,000 gift may deduct only $800. Flag any structure that is not a clean, outright, no-benefit transfer of an entire interest to an unrelated public charity, because those wrinkles quietly convert a strong strategy into a weak one.

## Common Traps

### Donating Short-Term Or Ordinary-Income Property Expecting Fair Market Value

The symptom is a donor giving recently purchased or inventory-like property and expecting to deduct fair market value. The trap is that short-term and ordinary-income property is deductible only at the lesser of basis or fair market value, and no capital gain is avoided. The direction is to verify the holding period and property character first; if short-term, either wait until long-term or donate a different long-term lot.

### Donating Depreciated Assets Instead Of Selling And Donating Cash

The symptom is donating an asset that has lost value. The trap is that the donor forfeits the unrealized capital loss, which could have been harvested by selling, while the charity takes the donor's basis anyway. The direction is to sell depreciated assets first to realize the deductible loss, then donate the cash proceeds.

### Selling The Asset Before Donating And Losing The Capital Gains Avoidance

The symptom is a donor who liquidates shares "to donate the proceeds" and is surprised by the capital gains tax. The trap is that a sale is a taxable event; only an in-kind transfer avoids the gain. The direction is to transfer the asset directly and never sell first unless the asset is depreciated.

### Expecting The 30% Limit At A Private Foundation

The symptom is assuming a gift of appreciated stock to a family foundation gets the 30% fair-market-value limit. The trap is that capital gain property to a private foundation is generally limited to 20% of AGI, and certain property may be deductible only at basis. The direction is to confirm the donee classification and use a public charity or DAF for the full 30% fair-market-value treatment when possible.

### Treating A Donor-Advised Fund As A Personal Or Tax-Free Savings Account

The symptom is a donor who funds a DAF but expects to direct grants to themselves, family, or non-charitable purposes, or to reclaim the funds. The trap is that the contribution is irrevocable, grants must go to qualified public charities, and the donor retains only advisory privilege. The direction is to explain the irrevocability and qualified-grant restriction before funding, and to confirm the donor's intent is genuinely charitable.

### Bunching Into A DAF Without Clearing The Standard Deduction

The symptom is funding a DAF to bunch giving without checking whether the bunched amount exceeds the standard deduction. The trap is that if the bunched total plus other itemized deductions does not exceed the standard deduction, bunching yields no incremental benefit and just locks up funds irrevocably. The direction is to model itemized-versus-standard for the bunching year and surrounding years before funding.

### Overlooking Quid Pro Quo Benefits That Reduce The Deduction

The symptom is a donor claiming the full gift amount when they received goods, services, naming rights, or event admission. The trap is that the deductible amount is reduced by the fair market value of any benefit received, and the charity must disclose that value. The direction is to subtract the benefit value and deduct only the net charitable portion.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the property been confirmed as long-term capital gain property (held more than one year, capital gain character), with the holding period verified against brokerage or acquisition records?
- [ ] Have appreciated and depreciated lots been separated, with only appreciated lots designated for direct donation and depreciated lots sold first to harvest losses?
- [ ] Has the double benefit — fair-market-value deduction plus capital-gains avoidance — been quantified against the sell-then-donate-cash alternative, using the correct fair market value (average of high and low for traded stock, qualified appraisal for non-traded assets)?
- [ ] Has the 30% AGI limit on appreciated capital gain property to public charities (20% to private foundations) been applied, with excess carried forward up to five years against future AGI?
- [ ] If a donor-advised fund is used for timing or bunching, has the bunched amount been modeled against the standard deduction to confirm a real benefit, and has the donor acknowledged the contribution is irrevocable with grants restricted to qualified charities?
- [ ] Has the asset been transferred directly (in-kind share transfer or deed), not sold first, to preserve the capital-gains avoidance?
- [ ] Has the transfer been screened for bargain-sale, partial-interest, related-party, or quid pro quo features that would reduce the deduction or trigger gain, and confirmed as a clean outright gift?
- [ ] Has the donee been confirmed as a qualified public charity or DAF sponsor (not a private foundation where lower limits apply), and has substantiation for the appreciated gift been planned (Form 8283, qualified appraisal over $5,000)?
- [ ] Are uncertain holding-period, valuation, and DAF-suitability questions escalated to a qualified tax professional rather than answered from general assumptions, since this guidance is educational and not personalized tax advice?
