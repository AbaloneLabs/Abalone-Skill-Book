# AGI Limit And Carryforward Strategy

The charitable AGI percentage limits are usually treated as a compliance calculation — figure out the ceiling, apply it, carry the rest forward. The judgment problem is that this framing throws away the limits' real value, which is as a forward-looking planning constraint. The 60% ceiling on cash to public charities, the 30% ceiling on appreciated capital gain property, and the 20% ceiling on capital gain property to private foundations are not just caps to obey; they are levers a planner can pull by choosing the donee type, the property type, the gift year, and the size of each gift. Layered on top is the five-year carryforward, which is not a passive parking lot for excess deductions but a depleting, expiring asset that must be sequenced against each future year's fresh ceiling or it dies silently in year five.

The harm from treating the limits as a back-office computation is twofold. First, agents let the deduction happen to them instead of shaping it — a donor gives appreciated stock to a private foundation, hits the 20% ceiling, and carries forward a mountain that never gets absorbed, when routing the same gift to a public charity or donor-advised fund sponsor would have doubled the current-year deduction to 30%. Second, agents fail to model the multi-year sequencing: a fresh gift in a carryforward year is deducted first and starves the older layer toward expiration, and nobody notices until the carryforward vanishes after year five with no recovery. The planner's job is to choose the ceiling deliberately, time the gift into a high-AGI year, and consume carryforwards before they age out.

This skill covers the strategic use of AGI percentage limits and the five-year carryforward as a planning tool under U.S. federal income tax law. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; charitable deduction rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and dollar amounts are indexed. A qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Ceiling As A Planning Lever, Not Just A Cap

The percentage ceiling is determined by two choices the donor largely controls: the donee type and the property type. Cash to a public charity (including a donor-advised fund sponsor) is deductible up to 60% of AGI; appreciated long-term capital gain property to a public charity is 30%; capital gain property to most private foundations is 20%. Because these differ by a factor of three, the same dollar of giving can produce very different current-year deductions depending on how it is routed.

Use this consciously. A donor with $300,000 AGI who wants to give $120,000 can deduct the full amount currently if the gift is cash to a public charity (60% = $180,000 ceiling), but only $60,000 currently if the gift is appreciated stock to a private foundation (20% = $60,000 ceiling), carrying $60,000 forward. Before defaulting to any one structure, compare the current-year deduction across donee and property combinations and pick the one that maximizes the deduction in the year where the donor's marginal rate is highest. ### Choose The Donee Type To Maximize The Current-Year Deduction

The donee classification is the single biggest swing in the limit. Public charities — churches, schools, hospitals, and the sponsoring organizations of donor-advised funds — get the favorable 60% cash limit and 30% appreciated-property limit. Most private (non-operating) foundations get 30% for cash and 20% for capital gain property. A donor-advised fund is a public charity for limit purposes because its sponsor is one, so funding a DAF secures the 60% cash limit without requiring the donor to name ultimate grantees in the funding year.

When a donor's intent is genuinely flexible, prefer the donee type that lifts the ceiling. If the donor insists on a family private foundation, flag that the 20% capital-gain-property ceiling will throttle large stock or real-estate gifts and force large carryforwards. Verify the classification with the IRS determination letter or the Tax Exempt Organization Search rather than the donor's casual label, because "my foundation" is often a DAF or a community-foundation fund that is actually a public charity. Do not assume the restrictive private-foundation limit applies without confirming it.

### Match The Property Type To The Year Where The Deduction Is Needed

Property type sets the second ceiling. Cash gets 60% to a public charity; long-term appreciated capital gain property gets 30% (deducted at fair market value); ordinary-income and short-term property is generally limited to the lesser of basis or fair market value. The choice of what to give is therefore a choice of ceiling and a choice of how much deduction lands in the current year.

When the goal is to maximize the current-year deduction against a high-income year, cash or cash-equivalent giving clears the 60% ceiling fastest. When the goal is to also shed embedded capital gain, appreciated property is better economically but is throttled to 30%, so a larger share carries forward. Model both paths against the donor's projected AGI for the contribution year and the following five years. If AGI is high this year and expected to fall (a pending retirement, a one-time business sale), front-load cash to use the wide 60% ceiling while it exists, and reserve appreciated-property gifts for years where the 30% ceiling is not binding.

### Sequence Multi-Year Giving To Exhaust Carryforwards Before Year Five

The five-year carryforward is a depleting asset on a hard clock. Each carryforward layer is subject to the same percentage limit in each carryforward year, applied against that year's AGI, and current-year contributions are deducted first within each year. After the fifth carryforward year, any remaining balance expires permanently and yields nothing. The trap is that a generous donor who keeps making fresh gifts can unintentionally starve an older carryforward until it dies.

Build a year-by-year carryforward schedule and plan around it. For each layer, record the original year, donee type, property type, percentage limit, original amount, amount used, and remaining balance. When a material carryforward exists, model the interaction before authorizing a new gift: in low-giving or high-AGI years, let the carryforward absorb; in years where a fresh gift is essential, confirm the older layer still has runway. A donor with a $140,000 appreciated-stock carryforward (30% limit) and $200,000 AGI can absorb $60,000 per year with no new giving, clearing it in roughly three years — but a new $50,000 cash gift in year two consumes ceiling first and pushes the older layer toward its fifth-year cliff.

### Time Large Gifts Into High-AGI Years To Raise The Ceiling

Because every limit is a percentage of AGI, raising AGI raises the ceiling. A bonus, a Roth conversion, a business sale, an exercised stock option, or an unusually profitable year widens the charitable ceiling in that same year. This is why bunching a multi-year charitable plan into a high-income year is often the highest-value move available — the deduction lands in a high marginal-rate year and the ceiling is wide enough to absorb it.

Project AGI before finalizing gift timing. If a donor expects a large liquidity event next year, deferring a planned gift into that year can convert a carryforward situation into a fully deductible current-year gift. Conversely, a retirement year with low AGI is usually a poor year to realize a large charitable deduction, because the low ceiling forces a carryforward that may never absorb against reduced retirement income. Coordinate gift timing with the donor's income trajectory rather than the donor's emotional impulse to give in December.

### Bunch Gifts To Clear The Standard-Deduction Hurdle

The charitable deduction only reduces tax when the donor itemizes, and itemizing only beats the standard deduction when total itemized deductions (charitable plus state and local tax up to the SALT cap, mortgage interest, medical over the floor) exceed the standard deduction for the filing status. In years where itemized deductions fall short, charitable gifts produce no tax benefit at all. Bunching — concentrating two or more years of intended giving into one tax year — is the primary tool to clear that hurdle.

Model the itemized-versus-standard comparison for the bunching year and the surrounding years. A married couple with $15,000 of annual giving who take the standard deduction get no benefit in normal years; bunching three years ($45,000) into one year, often through a donor-advised fund, can push them over the standard deduction so the full $45,000 is deductible that year, while they take the standard deduction in the other two years. Bunching only helps when the bunched total plus other itemized deductions actually exceeds the standard deduction — confirm the math before locking funds into a DAF, and remember the AGI ceiling still applies to the bunched amount.

### Reconcile The Carryforward Across Returns, Filing Status, And Death

Carryforwards must be tracked consistently across successive returns, and life events disrupt them. On a joint return, each spouse's carryforwards apply against that spouse's share of joint AGI. A change in filing status (marriage, divorce) changes how remaining carryforwards are allocated. At death, a decedent's unused charitable carryforward generally expires; it does not pass to the surviving spouse or the estate.

Maintain the running schedule on each return and plan around disruptions. If a donor with a large carryforward anticipates a filing-status change, declining health, or a low-AGI retirement, prioritize consuming the carryforward while it can still be used. Do not assume the carryforward survives death or transfers automatically. Document the trail back to the contribution year on every return, because the IRS may challenge a carryforward claim that lacks a documented history, and an undocumented layer is effectively lost.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To A Flat 50% Limit From Memory

The symptom is a planner who applies "charitable is 50% of AGI" from a remembered round number. The trap is that the 50% cash limit was the pre-2018 rule for many cases and has been superseded by 60% for cash to public charities, while appreciated property remains 30% and private-foundation capital gain property 20%. The direction is to confirm the current-year percentage for the specific donee and property type rather than relying on a remembered figure.

### Routing Appreciated Stock To A Private Foundation Out Of Habit

The symptom is a donor who always gives to the family foundation and is surprised by a thin current-year deduction. The trap is that capital gain property to a private foundation is generally capped at 20% of AGI, half the 30% available at a public charity or DAF sponsor, so the same gift produces a larger carryforward that may never absorb. The direction is to compare donee types when the gift is appreciated property and prefer a public charity or DAF when the donor's intent allows.

### Letting A Carryforward Expire Because Nobody Modeled It

The symptom is a large gift made years ago with no follow-through, and a deduction silently vanishing after year five. The trap is that the five-year carryforward is not automatic — it must be claimed each year, current-year gifts are deducted first, and once a layer ages out it is permanently lost. The direction is to maintain a year-by-year carryforward schedule and affirmatively consume layers, especially in low-giving or high-AGI years.

### Starving Old Carryforwards With Fresh Gifts In The Same Year

The symptom is a generous donor who keeps giving while an older carryforward dwindles toward expiration. The trap is the sequencing rule that current-year contributions are deducted first, so a fresh gift consumes the ceiling and leaves no room for the older layer. The direction is to model the interaction before authorizing a new gift when a material carryforward exists, and to consider pausing new giving to let the carryforward absorb.

### Bunching Without Checking Whether The Standard Deduction Is Cleared

The symptom is funding a donor-advised fund to bunch giving without running the itemized-versus-standard math. The trap is that if the bunched total plus other itemized deductions does not exceed the standard deduction, bunching yields no incremental benefit and irrevocably locks up the funds. The direction is to model the comparison for the bunching year and surrounding years before funding.

### Forgetting The Ceiling Is On AGI, Not Taxable Income

The symptom is computing the ceiling on taxable income or after the standard deduction. The trap is that the percentage limits apply to AGI, which is before itemized or standard deductions, so using the wrong base misstates the ceiling and the carryforward. The direction is to compute the ceiling on AGI as reported — after above-the-line adjustments but before itemized or standard deductions.

### Assuming A Carryforward Survives Death Or A Filing-Status Change

The symptom is expecting a decedent's charitable carryforward to help the surviving spouse or estate. The trap is that a decedent's unused carryforward generally expires at death and does not transfer, and filing-status changes complicate joint-return allocation. The direction is to consume material carryforwards before anticipated death or status changes and not to assume transferability.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the donee been classified (public charity versus private foundation, confirmed by IRS letter) before selecting the percentage, recognizing that a donor-advised fund sponsor is a public charity for limit purposes?
- [ ] Has the property type been matched to the correct ceiling — cash at 60% to a public charity, appreciated long-term capital gain property at 30%, capital gain property to a private foundation at 20% — to maximize the current-year deduction?
- [ ] Has the ceiling been computed against AGI (after above-the-line adjustments, before itemized or standard deductions) for the specific contribution year, with projected AGI used for forward planning?
- [ ] Has a multi-year carryforward schedule been built — original year, donee type, property type, percentage limit, amount used, remaining balance — and modeled so each layer is consumed before its fifth-year expiration?
- [ ] Has the sequencing rule been applied so that new gifts do not inadvertently starve older carryforwards, with new giving paused or timed when a material carryforward is at risk?
- [ ] Has gift timing been coordinated with the donor's income trajectory, front-loading gifts into high-AGI years and avoiding low-AGI years where the ceiling is binding?
- [ ] Has bunching been modeled against the standard deduction for the bunching year and surrounding years, confirming the bunched total actually clears the hurdle before funds are locked into a DAF?
- [ ] Has the effect of filing-status change or death on carryforwards been considered, with material layers consumed before any anticipated disruption?
- [ ] Has the carryforward schedule been documented on each successive return to support both accurate deduction and audit defense?
- [ ] Are uncertain classification, sequencing, and multi-year optimization questions escalated to a qualified tax professional rather than answered from general assumptions, since this guidance is educational and not personalized tax advice?
