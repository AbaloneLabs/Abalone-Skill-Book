# Income Type Identification

Income type determines the tax rate, the form on which the income is reported, the deductions available against it, and whether it is subject to payroll taxes or the Net Investment Income Tax. The judgment problem is that income streams that look similar to a layperson can be taxed very differently: a short-term capital gain is taxed at ordinary rates up to 37%, while a long-term gain on the same dollar amount is taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20%; a qualified dividend is taxed at capital-gains rates while an ordinary dividend is taxed at full ordinary rates; self-employment income carries a 15.3% self-employment tax that wages do not, because the employer half is already paid.

Agents frequently classify income by its source label rather than by its legal character. They treat all investment income as "capital gains," missing that interest and non-qualified dividends are ordinary; they treat all stock sale proceeds as gains, missing that the holding period governs the rate; and they treat rental income as ordinary income without recognizing that a real estate professional may have different passive-loss treatment. The harm is an underestimated tax liability, a misreported return, and in the case of self-employment income, a missed self-employment tax that triggers penalties and interest when the IRS reconstructs the income.

This skill covers the classification of individual income into ordinary income, capital gains (short-term and long-term), qualified and ordinary dividends, interest, self-employment income, rental income, and royalty income, and how each classification drives the rate and the form, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; classification depends on facts (holding periods, payer type, material participation) that must be verified, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Rate thresholds are 2024 figures and are indexed annually.

## Core Rules

### Separate Ordinary Income From Capital Gains By Character

Ordinary income (wages, interest, non-qualified dividends, short-term capital gains, business income) is taxed at the taxpayer's marginal rates, which for 2024 range from 10% to 37%. Capital gains are taxed at preferential rates, but only if they are long-term (held more than one year). Long-term capital gains and qualified dividends are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on taxable income (2024 breakpoints approximately $47,025 single / $94,050 MFJ for the 0% rate, and approximately $518,900 single / $583,750 MFJ for the 20% rate).

The character of a gain is fixed by the holding period, not by the type of asset. A stock held for 11 months produces a short-term gain taxed at ordinary rates; the same stock held for 13 months produces a long-term gain taxed at preferential rates. Confirm the holding period (purchase date to sale date, using the trade date, not settlement) before assigning a rate. Do not treat all stock sales as long-term, and do not treat all investment income as capital gains, because interest and non-qualified dividends are ordinary regardless of how long they were held.

### Distinguish Short-Term From Long-Term By The One-Year Holding Period

A capital gain is long-term if the asset was held for more than one year and short-term if held one year or less. The holding period begins the day after acquisition and ends on the day of disposition. Short-term gains are netted against short-term losses, long-term gains against long-term losses, and then short-term net is combined with long-term net to produce the final result; short-term net gain is taxed at ordinary rates, long-term net gain at preferential rates.

The one-year boundary is strict. An asset held exactly one year is short-term; it must be held for one year and one day to be long-term. Agents often round the holding period and misclassify a borderline sale. Confirm the exact acquisition and sale dates, account for any holding-period tacks (such as from a like-kind exchange or an inherited asset, which is automatically long-term), and net the gains and losses within each category before applying the rate.

### Separate Qualified From Ordinary Dividends

Qualified dividends are taxed at the same 0%/15%/20% rates as long-term capital gains, while ordinary dividends are taxed at full ordinary rates. Qualified status requires that the dividend be paid by a U.S. corporation or qualified foreign corporation and that the taxpayer hold the stock for more than 60 days during the 121-day window beginning 60 days before the ex-dividend date. Dividends from REITs, money market funds, and MLPs are generally ordinary.

Do not assume all dividends are qualified. Review Form 1099-DIV: Box 1a is total ordinary dividends, Box 1b is the qualified portion. The qualified portion is a subset of Box 1a, not a separate amount. Capital gain distributions (Box 2a) are taxed as long-term gains regardless of holding period, and return-of-capital distributions (Box 3) reduce basis and are not taxed currently. Categorize each component correctly, because treating an ordinary dividend as qualified understates tax, and treating a return of capital as a dividend overstates income and fails to reduce basis.

### Classify Interest Income As Ordinary, With Limited Exceptions

Interest is generally ordinary income, reported on Form 1040 line 2b and Schedule B if over $1,500. Taxable interest includes bank account interest, bond interest (corporate, Treasury, and most municipal bond interest on private-activity bonds), certificate of deposit interest, and loan interest the taxpayer received. Certain interest is excluded or has special treatment: interest on U.S. Treasury obligations is taxable federally but exempt from state tax; interest on most municipal bonds is exempt from federal tax (and often state tax if in-state); interest on U.S. savings bonds may be deferred or excluded for education under IRC Section 135.

The common error is treating all interest the same for state purposes. Treasury interest is federally taxable but state-exempt, which matters in high-tax states, and municipal bond interest, while federally tax-exempt, is added back for the alternative minimum tax if it is from private-activity bonds, and is used in the Social Security taxation and the Net Investment Income Tax MAGI computations. Confirm the source of each interest item and apply the federal, state, and AMT treatment separately.

### Treat Self-Employment Income As Ordinary Plus Payroll Tax

Net self-employment income (Schedule C profit, partnership guaranteed payments, and similar) is ordinary income, but it also carries self-employment tax of 15.3% on the first $168,600 (2024) of net earnings (12.4% Social Security plus 2.9% Medicare), plus an additional 0.9% Medicare on earnings above $200,000 single / $250,000 MFJ. The taxpayer can deduct half of the self-employment tax as an adjustment to income.

The self-employment tax is the trap that converts a modest Schedule C profit into a much larger liability. A taxpayer with $50,000 of net self-employment income owes income tax on $50,000 (less the half-SE-tax deduction) plus roughly $7,065 in self-employment tax. Agents who classify self-employment income as "just ordinary income" and ignore the SE tax understate the liability. Compute the SE tax on Schedule SE, apply the deduction for half of it, and confirm the Social Security wage base is not double-counted if the taxpayer also has W-2 wages from an employer.

### Report Rental Income On Schedule E With Passive-Loss Limits

Rental income is generally ordinary income reported on Schedule E, but rental losses are subject to the passive activity loss rules, which generally limit the deduction of passive losses to passive income unless the taxpayer is a real estate professional or qualifies for the $25,000 special allowance for active participation (which phases out between $100,000 and $150,000 of MAGI). Rental income is not subject to self-employment tax unless the taxpayer provides substantial services (hotel-like services), in which case it becomes Schedule C income subject to SE tax.

Distinguish rental income from rental of property where services are significant. A long-term residential rental is Schedule E with no SE tax; a short-term rental with substantial services (daily maid service, concierge) may be Schedule C with SE tax. Confirm the level of services before choosing the form. Also confirm whether the taxpayer materially participates or is a real estate professional, because that determines whether losses are currently deductible. Do not assume rental losses are fully deductible.

### Classify Royalty Income By Its Source

Royalty income from intellectual property the taxpayer created (a book, a patent) is generally ordinary income reported on Schedule E, and if the taxpayer is still actively creating, it may be subject to self-employment tax. Royalty income from property the taxpayer inherited or holds as a passive investment (mineral rights, oil and gas) is generally Schedule E income not subject to SE tax. Royalties from a working interest in oil and gas may be Schedule C if held directly.

The source of the royalty drives both the form and the SE tax. A novelist's royalties are Schedule E and often subject to SE tax; an heir's oil and gas royalties are Schedule E and not subject to SE tax. Confirm how the royalty was acquired and whether the taxpayer is active in the property before classifying, because the SE tax consequence is material and easy to miss.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Investment Income As Capital Gains

Interest and non-qualified dividends are ordinary income, taxed at full marginal rates. Lumping all investment income into "capital gains" understates tax.

### Misclassifying A Borderline Holding Period

An asset held exactly one year is short-term. Rounding the holding period up converts an ordinary-rate gain into a preferential-rate gain and understates tax.

### Assuming All Dividends Are Qualified

REIT, money market fund, and MLP dividends are generally ordinary. The qualified portion is a subset of total dividends, reported in Box 1b of Form 1099-DIV.

### Ignoring Self-Employment Tax On Schedule C Income

Self-employment income carries a 15.3% SE tax on top of income tax. Classifying it as ordinary income without the SE tax understates the liability.

### Treating All Rental Income As Schedule E Without Services

Short-term rentals with substantial services may be Schedule C income subject to SE tax. Assuming all rentals are passive Schedule E misses the SE tax.

### Overlooking State Treatment Of Interest

Treasury interest is federally taxable but state-exempt; municipal interest is federally exempt but may affect AMT and MAGI-based computations. Treating all interest the same for state and federal produces errors.

### Forgetting That Inherited Assets Are Automatically Long-Term

Property acquired from a decedent is treated as held long-term regardless of the actual holding period. Selling it shortly after inheritance still produces a long-term gain.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has each income item been classified as ordinary or capital gain, with capital gains separated into short-term (one year or less) and long-term (more than one year) based on the exact holding period?
- [ ] Have dividends been separated into qualified (Box 1b, preferential rates) and ordinary (Box 1a), with capital gain distributions (Box 2a) and return of capital (Box 3) categorized separately?
- [ ] Has interest income been classified by source, with Treasury interest flagged as state-exempt and municipal bond interest flagged for AMT and MAGI interactions?
- [ ] Has self-employment income been identified, with self-employment tax computed on Schedule SE, the Social Security wage base checked against any W-2 wages, and the half-SE-tax deduction claimed?
- [ ] Has rental income been classified as Schedule E (passive, no SE tax) versus Schedule C (substantial services, SE tax), and have passive activity loss limits been applied?
- [ ] Has royalty income been classified by source (created property with possible SE tax versus inherited or passive property without SE tax)?
- [ ] Have the long-term capital gains and qualified dividends rate breakpoints (2024 0%/15%/20%) been applied based on taxable income?
- [ ] Is each income item reported on the correct form (Schedule 1, Schedule B, Schedule C, Schedule D, Schedule E) based on its classification?
- [ ] Have inherited assets been confirmed as automatically long-term, regardless of the heir's actual holding period?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal income-classification information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
