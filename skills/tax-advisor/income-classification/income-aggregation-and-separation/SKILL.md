# Income Aggregation And Separation

A taxpayer with multiple income streams faces a structural decision: which income must be combined on one schedule, which must be kept separate, and which errors arise from mixing them. The judgment problem is that the tax law requires both aggregation and separation in different contexts, and the wrong choice misstates tax, loses deductions, or triggers penalties. Schedule C activities must be separated by trade or business unless aggregation is elected under specific rules; capital gains and losses must be netted within categories before combining; wash sales can disallow losses across accounts the taxpayer does not directly control; and the Section 199A qualified business income deduction rewards aggregation of certain businesses but forbids it for others.

Agents frequently lump all self-employment income into a single Schedule C, missing that separate trades or businesses have different QBI, loss, and audit profiles. They net capital gains and losses across short-term and long-term without the required two-step netting, and they overlook wash sales that cross account boundaries (a loss in a brokerage account and a repurchase in an IRA). The harm is a return that looks complete but contains aggregation errors that collapse deductions on examination, or separation errors that forfeit beneficial netting.

This skill covers when income must be grouped or separated, Schedule C versus Schedule E versus W-2 allocation, wash sale and netting rules, and allocation across activities, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; aggregation and separation decisions depend on facts (separate trades, material participation, account ownership) that must be verified, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Separate Trades Or Businesses For Schedule C Unless Properly Aggregated

Each trade or business is generally reported on its own Schedule C, because separate schedules preserve the distinct income, expense, and QBI profile of each activity. A consultant who also runs an unrelated retail shop should file two Schedule Cs, because mixing them distorts the net profit of each and can affect the QBI deduction, the hobby-loss exposure, and the audit risk profile. The IRS expects separate schedules for separate trades or businesses with different NAICS codes or materially different activities.

The exception is that closely related activities may be combined on a single Schedule C if they constitute a single trade or business, but this is a facts-and-circumstances test. Combining is acceptable when the activities share customers, location, accounting, and purpose; combining distinct businesses to average out a loss is not. Confirm whether the activities are genuinely one business or should be separated, and document the rationale. For QBI, the Section 199A aggregation election is separate and has its own commonality requirements, so do not confuse Schedule C combination with QBI aggregation.

### Allocate Income To The Correct Schedule By Character

Income must be reported on the schedule that matches its character: wages on Form 1040 line 1 (from W-2), self-employment income on Schedule C, partnership and S corporation income on Schedule E (from K-1), rental income on Schedule E, and investment income on Schedule B and Schedule D. Misallocating income to the wrong schedule changes the deductions available against it, the payroll tax treatment, and the audit profile.

A common error is reporting income that should be Schedule C on Schedule E, or vice versa. Consulting income is Schedule C (subject to SE tax) even if the taxpayer thinks of it as "like a salary"; rental income is Schedule E (not subject to SE tax) even if the taxpayer manages the property actively. Royalty income from created works may be Schedule E but subject to SE tax, while royalty income from mineral rights is Schedule E without SE tax. Confirm the character of each income stream and assign it to the schedule that matches, because the wrong schedule produces the wrong tax and the wrong deductions.

### Apply Capital Gain And Loss Netting In The Required Two Steps

Capital gains and losses are netted in a specific sequence: first, net all short-term transactions (short-term gains minus short-term losses), then net all long-term transactions (long-term gains minus long-term losses), then combine the short-term net with the long-term net. Short-term net gain is taxed at ordinary rates; long-term net gain is taxed at preferential rates. If the combined result is a net capital loss, up to $3,000 ($1,500 MFS) is deductible against ordinary income per year, and the rest carries forward.

Skipping the two-step netting is a frequent error. Agents sometimes net all transactions together and apply a single rate, which misstates the tax when there are both short-term and long-term components. Confirm that short-term and long-term are netted separately first, then combined, and that the $3,000 ordinary loss limit and carryforward are applied correctly. Also confirm that any capital loss carryover from prior years is netted into the current-year computation at the correct stage (short-term carryover into short-term, long-term carryover into long-term).

### Apply Wash Sale Rules Across All Accounts The Taxpayer Controls

A wash sale occurs when the taxpayer sells a security at a loss and buys a substantially identical security within 30 days before or after the sale. The loss is disallowed and added to the basis of the replacement shares. Critically, the wash sale rule applies across all accounts the taxpayer controls, including a spouse's account in some circumstances, and most importantly, an IRA account, where the disallowed loss is permanently lost (it does not increase IRA basis).

The IRA wash sale is the silent killer. A taxpayer who sells a stock at a loss in a brokerage account and repurchases it in their IRA within 30 days triggers a wash sale, and because the IRA is a separate taxpayer with no basis tracking for this purpose, the loss is permanently disallowed. Confirm all accounts the taxpayer (and spouse) control, check for repurchases of substantially identical securities within the 61-day window, and flag any IRA involvement as a permanent loss. Do not rely on the broker's 1099-B to catch cross-account wash sales, because brokers only track within their own accounts.

### Allocate Income And Deductions Across Activities For Passive Loss And QBI

When a taxpayer has multiple activities, income and deductions must be allocated to each activity to apply the passive activity loss rules and the QBI deduction correctly. Passive losses from one rental activity can generally offset passive income from another, but not nonpassive income; QBI is computed per trade or business before combining. Misallocating shared expenses (a home office used for two businesses, a vehicle used for business and personal) distorts both the per-activity profit and the downstream deductions.

Allocate shared expenses on a reasonable, consistent basis (square footage for home office, mileage for vehicles, time for shared software) and document the method. For QBI, compute QBI per business, then combine, because the wage and property limits apply per business unless aggregation is elected. For passive activities, group activities that make up a larger rental enterprise only if the grouping rules are met. Do not allocate expenses to whichever activity produces the best tax result without a defensible method, because the allocation must withstand examination.

### Do Not Mix Business And Personal Expenses On Schedule C

Schedule C is for ordinary and necessary business expenses; personal expenses are not deductible. Mixing personal expenses into Schedule C (personal phone, personal travel, clothing that is not a required uniform) overstates deductions and is a primary audit trigger. Each expense must have a business purpose, and the personal portion of mixed-use items must be allocated out.

The trap is the "mostly business" assumption. A phone used 70% for business and 30% personally should have only 70% deducted; a trip that combined business and personal days should deduct only the business portion. Maintain contemporaneous records (mileage logs, receipts, calendars) that substantiate the business purpose and the allocation. Agents who accept the taxpayer's total without an allocation are building a return that fails on examination, and the disallowed expenses often pull related deductions into question.

### Reconcile K-1 Income With The Owner's Other Activities

Pass-through income from partnerships and S corporations arrives on Schedule K-1, and each K-1 must be reconciled with the owner's other activities for passive loss, QBI, basis, and at-risk purposes. A K-1 loss is deductible only to the extent of the owner's basis and at-risk amount, and passive losses are limited to passive income unless the owner materially participates. Ordinary business income from a K-1 feeds QBI; separately stated items (capital gains, charitable contributions, Section 179) are reported in their own categories.

The trap is treating the K-1 as a single number. Each separately stated item must be carried to the correct line of the return, and the loss limitations (basis, at-risk, passive) must be tested before deducting a K-1 loss. Confirm the owner's basis (contributions, loans, income, distributions) and at-risk amount, because a loss that exceeds basis is suspended, not deducted. Do not net a K-1 loss against the owner's W-2 income without first testing the limitations.

## Common Traps

### Lumping Distinct Businesses Into One Schedule C

Separate trades or businesses should have separate Schedule Cs. Combining them distorts QBI, loss profiles, and audit risk, and cannot be undone easily later.

### Reporting Income On The Wrong Schedule

Schedule C income is subject to SE tax; Schedule E rental income is not. Putting SE-taxable income on Schedule E understates tax, and the reverse overstates it.

### Netting Capital Gains And Losses In One Step

Short-term and long-term must be netted separately before combining. A single-step net misstates the rate when both categories are present.

### Missing Cross-Account And IRA Wash Sales

Wash sales apply across all accounts the taxpayer controls. An IRA repurchase permanently disallows the loss, and brokers do not catch cross-account washes.

### Allocating Shared Expenses Without A Defensible Method

Home office, vehicle, and mixed-use expenses need a documented allocation. Allocating to maximize the deduction without a consistent method fails on audit.

### Deducting Personal Expenses On Schedule C

Personal portions of mixed-use items are not deductible. Including them overstates deductions and is a primary audit trigger.

### Treating A K-1 Loss As Automatically Deductible

K-1 losses are limited by basis, at-risk, and passive rules. Deducting a loss without testing these limitations overstates the deduction.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have separate trades or businesses been reported on separate Schedule Cs, with any combination justified by shared customers, location, accounting, and purpose?
- [ ] Has each income stream been allocated to the schedule matching its character (W-2 wages, Schedule C self-employment, Schedule E rental and pass-through, Schedule B interest and dividends, Schedule D capital gains)?
- [ ] Have capital gains and losses been netted in the required two steps (short-term net, long-term net, then combined), with the $3,000 ordinary loss limit and carryforward applied?
- [ ] Have wash sales been checked across all accounts the taxpayer and spouse control, with any IRA repurchase within 30 days flagged as a permanent loss?
- [ ] Have shared expenses been allocated on a reasonable, consistent, documented basis (square footage, mileage, time) rather than to maximize the deduction?
- [ ] Has personal-use portion of mixed expenses been excluded from Schedule C, with contemporaneous records maintained?
- [ ] For QBI, has income and the wage/property limit been computed per trade or business before combining, with aggregation evaluated separately?
- [ ] For K-1 losses, have the basis, at-risk, and passive activity loss limitations been tested before deducting?
- [ ] Have separately stated K-1 items (capital gains, charitable contributions, Section 179) been carried to the correct return lines rather than netted into ordinary income?
