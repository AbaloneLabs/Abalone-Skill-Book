# Active Versus Passive Income Classification

The character of income as active, passive, or portfolio determines whether losses can offset it, whether the net investment income tax applies, whether the qualified business income deduction is available, and how self-employment tax attaches. The judgment problem is that agents tend to classify income by its source label rather than by the participation and activity tests that the code requires. Rental income is often assumed passive, but the real estate professional rules and the short-term rental exception can make it active. Business income is often assumed active, but a limited partner or an investor who does not materially participate may have passive income that cannot offset wages or other active income. The classification is not a label; it is a conclusion that depends on hours, participation, and the specific activity structure.

The common failures are allowing passive losses to offset active or portfolio income in violation of Section 469, treating rental real estate as passive when the taxpayer qualifies as a real estate professional, failing to group activities in a way that satisfies material participation, and misapplying the net investment income tax by assuming all investment income is passive or that all business income is active. The harm is a return that claims disallowed losses, misses allowed losses, or misapplies surtaxes, each of which cascades into examination exposure or foregone benefit.

This skill covers the classification of income as active, passive, or portfolio, the passive activity loss rules, material participation, the rental real estate exceptions, and the interaction with the net investment income tax and the qualified business income deduction. It is not tax advice; classification depends on documented participation hours and activity structure, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Classify Income Into Active, Passive, And Portfolio Categories

The three functional categories drive different rules. Active income includes wages, salaries, self-employment earnings, and income from a trade or business in which the taxpayer materially participates. Passive income includes income from a trade or business in which the taxpayer does not materially participate, and from rental activities, subject to exceptions. Portfolio income includes interest, dividends, annuities, and royalties that are not derived in the ordinary course of a trade or business. The category determines which losses can offset the income and which surtaxes apply.

Begin classification by placing each income stream into one of the three categories based on the activity and participation tests, not on the source label. A business can produce active income for an owner who materially participates and passive income for an owner who does not. The same entity can generate different character income for different owners.

### Apply The Section 469 Passive Activity Loss Limitation

Section 469 provides that losses from passive activities can offset only income from passive activities, not active or portfolio income. A passive loss that exceeds passive income in a year is suspended and carried forward to offset future passive income or until the entire interest in the activity is disposed of in a fully taxable transaction. This limitation is the core constraint, and it is frequently violated when an agent nets all losses against all income without separating passive from active.

Identify each activity, classify it as active or passive, and apply the limitation at the activity level. Suspended passive losses must be tracked by activity and released only when the rules permit. A taxpayer with a large passive loss cannot use it to reduce wages or portfolio income, and an agent who allows the offset produces a return that will be adjusted on examination.

### Determine Material Participation Using The Seven Tests

Material participation is the gateway to active classification for a trade or business. The regulations provide seven tests, any one of which establishes material participation: participating for more than 500 hours during the year; the taxpayer's participation constituting substantially all participation in the activity; participating for more than 100 hours and no one else participating more; significant participation in multiple significant-participation activities totaling more than 500 hours; material participation in the activity for any five of the prior ten years; material participation in a personal service activity for any three prior years; and regular, continuous, and substantial participation based on all facts and circumstances.

The most common test is the 500-hour test, but the others matter for taxpayers whose participation is irregular or spread across activities. Document the hours and the basis for the test relied upon, because material participation is a factual determination that the IRS can challenge. Contemporaneous time records or logs are the strongest evidence; after-the-fact reconstruction is weaker.

### Handle Rental Activities And Their Exceptions

Rental real estate is per se passive under Section 469, regardless of material participation, subject to two main exceptions. The real estate professional exception applies to a taxpayer who spends more than half of their personal service time and more than 750 hours in real property trades or businesses, and who materially participates in the specific rental activity; qualifying converts the rental from passive to active, allowing losses to offset other income. The short-term rental exception applies to a rental averaging seven days or less of customer use, where the taxpayer materially participates, treating it as a business rather than a passive rental.

Confirm whether the taxpayer qualifies for either exception before treating rental losses as deductible against active income. The real estate professional test requires detailed hour documentation across all real estate activities, and the short-term rental exception depends on average stay length and material participation. Defaulting to passive treatment without checking the exceptions forecloses legitimate loss deductions, while claiming an exception without meeting the tests exposes the return to disallowance.

### Consider Grouping Elections To Satisfy Participation Tests

A taxpayer can elect to group multiple activities into a single activity for purposes of the material participation and passive loss rules, if the activities constitute an appropriate economic unit. Grouping can help a taxpayer meet the material participation test by combining hours across related activities, which is especially relevant for real estate professionals with multiple rental properties. Once activities are grouped, they are treated as a single activity for all subsequent years unless there is a material change.

Evaluate whether grouping is beneficial before the return is filed, because the election is generally binding. Grouping can convert multiple marginally-passive activities into one active activity, but it can also combine a profitable activity with a loss activity in ways that change the loss character. Document the grouping rationale and the economic-unit factors.

### Apply The Net Investment Income Tax Based On Income Character

The 3.8 percent net investment income tax applies to the lesser of net investment income or the excess of modified adjusted gross income over the thresholds. Net investment income includes portfolio income and passive business income, but excludes active business income from a material-participation trade or business and wages. Therefore, the active-versus-passive classification directly determines whether business income is subject to the NIIT.

A taxpayer whose business is active avoids NIIT on that income, while a taxpayer whose business is passive pays NIIT. Confirm the material participation status before concluding whether business income is NIIT-exempt, because the same business can be active for one owner and passive for another. Also note that gains from the sale of a passive business interest are generally investment income, while gains from the sale of an active business may be excluded.

### Check The Qualified Business Income Deduction Interaction

The Section 199A qualified business income deduction is available for income from a qualified trade or business, which generally requires the business not be a specified service trade or business above income thresholds, and which is subject to W-2 wage and limitations. Passive income from a business can still qualify for QBI if it is from a qualified trade or business, but the interaction with the passive loss rules and the NIIT must be tracked, because the same income is characterized differently for different provisions.

## Common Traps

### Allowing Passive Losses To Offset Active Or Portfolio Income

Section 469 limits passive losses to passive income. Netting all losses against all income without separating categories violates the rule and produces an understated tax.

### Treating All Rental Income As Passive

Rental is passive by default, but the real estate professional and short-term rental exceptions can make it active. Failing to check the exceptions forecloses legitimate deductions.

### Claiming Material Participation Without Documented Hours

Material participation is factual and hour-based. Asserting it without contemporaneous records invites disallowance on examination.

### Ignoring Grouping Elections Until Too Late

Grouping can help meet participation tests, but the election is generally binding and must be made deliberately. Missing the opportunity or grouping poorly locks in a suboptimal structure.

### Misapplying The NIIT Based On Wrong Income Character

Active business income is NIIT-exempt; passive business income is not. Classifying a business wrong leads to either overpaying or underpaying the surtax.

### Forgetting Suspended Passive Losses Carry Forward

Passive losses that cannot be used currently are suspended and carried forward, released on future passive income or disposition. Losing track of suspended losses forfeits future deductions.
