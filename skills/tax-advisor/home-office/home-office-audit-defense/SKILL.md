# Home Office Audit Defense

The home office deduction has historically been an IRS audit target, and defending it requires contemporaneous documentation that proves the space meets the exclusive use, regular use, and principal place of business tests — not merely the taxpayer's assertion. The judgment problem is that substantiation is built (or destroyed) during the year, not reconstructed after an audit notice, and the records that hold up under examination are specific: dated photographs, measurements, a business-use log, and a clear narrative of how the space functions in the business. An agent who helps a taxpayer claim the deduction without setting up the documentation is setting up a disallowance.

Agents frequently treat the home office as a low-risk, routine deduction and fail to build the evidence file until an audit arrives, at which point reconstruction is weak and the IRS discounts it. The harm is a disallowed deduction with back tax, penalties, and interest, plus the loss of the deduction in future years if the taxpayer becomes discouraged. A second silent failure is conflating personal and business space in the records — claiming a percentage of the home that includes personal areas, or failing to photograph the office in a way that shows its exclusive business character. A third is relying on a generic description ("home office for consulting") rather than evidence of where clients are met, where administrative work occurs, and how the space is essential to the business.

This skill covers documentation requirements, Form 8829 completion, the audit risk profile, substantiating exclusivity and principal place of business, and the common reasons the IRS disallows the deduction. It is not tax advice; audit defense depends on the specific facts and the examination posture, and outcomes depend on facts that must be verified. Consult a qualified tax professional (CPA or tax attorney) before relying on any conclusion or responding to an audit notice.

## Core Rules

### Build The Documentation File Contemporaneously, Not After An Audit Notice

The strongest audit defense is documentation created during the year the deduction is claimed, not reconstructed afterward. The file should include: dated photographs of the office space showing its exclusive business use (desk, equipment, no personal items, no bed or TV); a floor plan or measurement of the office and the total home with the business-use percentage computed; a business-use log or calendar showing the hours and activities conducted in the space; and a narrative explaining how the space functions as the principal place of business. Contemporaneous records are given far more weight by examiners than reconstructed estimates.

The trap is waiting for an audit notice to assemble documentation, at which point the examiner knows the records are reconstructed and discounts them. An agent should help the taxpayer create the file at the time the deduction is claimed — take the photographs, record the measurements, start the log, and write the narrative while the facts are current. Store the file with the tax return for the year. A photograph taken the week of an audit is obvious; a photograph dated during the tax year is credible. Make contemporaneous documentation a condition of claiming the deduction.

### Complete Form 8829 Accurately And Reconcile To Schedule C

Form 8829 (Expenses for Business Use of Your Home) is the form used under the actual expense method to compute the deduction and carry it to Schedule C. The form requires the square footage of the office and the home, the business-use percentage, the direct and indirect expenses, the depreciation computation, and the net-income limitation. Errors on Form 8829 are a common audit finding: an incorrect percentage, mortgage interest or property taxes not allocated between Schedule C and Schedule A, or depreciation computed on the wrong basis or recovery period.

An agent should complete Form 8829 carefully and reconcile it to Schedule C and Schedule A. The business-use percentage must match the measured square footage. The indirect expenses must be the full home amounts times the percentage. Depreciation must use the correct basis (the lower of adjusted basis or fair market value at the time the home was first used for business) and the correct recovery period (39 years for the business portion of a personal residence). The deduction cannot exceed net business income, with any excess carried forward. A Form 8829 that does not tie to the underlying records is a red flag.

### Document Exclusive Use With Evidence, Not Assertion

Exclusive use is the test most often challenged in audit, and it must be proven with evidence, not the taxpayer's statement. The evidence includes photographs showing the space is used only for business (no personal items, no dual-use furniture like a bed or dining table), a description of what is in the space and how it is arranged, and confirmation that no one uses the space personally. If the space is a room, photograph the whole room; if it is a portion of a room, the boundaries must be clearly demarcated and the photographs must show the demarcation.

The trap is a photograph that inadvertently shows personal use — a guest bed in the corner, a child's toys, a television — which destroys the exclusive use claim. An agent should review the photographs critically before they go into the file and ensure they show a space used exclusively for business. If the space is shared (a corner of a living room), the deduction should not be claimed because exclusivity cannot be established. Do not let the taxpayer claim exclusive use based on intent; require photographic and descriptive evidence that the space is business-only.

### Substantiate Principal Place Of Business With A Functional Narrative

Principal place of business must be proven with a functional narrative that explains where the substantive business activities occur and why the home office is essential. For a consultant who performs services at client sites, the narrative should establish that all administrative and management activities (billing, scheduling, recordkeeping, report preparation) occur in the home office and that there is no other fixed location for those activities. For a business where the home office is the only location, the narrative is simpler but still should describe the activities conducted there.

The trap is a generic narrative ("I run my consulting business from home") that does not address the two prongs of the principal place test (relative importance of activities and time spent) or the administrative/management exception. An agent should write a specific narrative: list the administrative activities performed in the office, confirm no other fixed location exists, and note the time spent there versus at client sites. If the taxpayer rents another office or conducts administrative work elsewhere, the home office may not qualify, and the narrative should reflect that honestly. A functional narrative that matches the actual business operations is far more defensible than a boilerplate description.

### Separate Personal And Business Space In All Records

The business-use percentage must reflect only the space used exclusively and regularly for business, not the total area the taxpayer happens to use during the workday. A common error is including personal areas (a break room, a personal bathroom, a hallway) in the business percentage, or counting a home gym or workshop that is not part of the business. The office square footage should be the dedicated business space, and the home total should be the total living area measured consistently.

The trap is an inflated business-use percentage that includes personal space, which the examiner can detect by comparing the claimed percentage to the photographs and measurements. An agent should measure only the exclusive business space for the numerator and apply a consistent definition of total home area for the denominator. If the taxpayer uses multiple rooms exclusively for business, each can be included, but each must meet the exclusive use test. Do not let convenience or a desire for a larger deduction inflate the percentage beyond what the space actually supports.

### Understand The Audit Risk Profile And Common Disallowance Reasons

The home office deduction is a known audit target, particularly for sole proprietors with high deductions relative to income, for taxpayers claiming a large percentage of the home, and for those whose business description does not clearly support a home office. Common disallowance reasons are: failure to meet exclusive use (shared space), failure to meet principal place of business (substantive work done elsewhere), lack of contemporaneous records, an inflated business-use percentage, and claiming the deduction as an employee (disallowed 2018-2025). The IRS also challenges deductions that create or increase a loss.

An agent should assess the audit risk before claiming the deduction: Is the space genuinely exclusive? Is the principal place narrative supportable? Are the records contemporaneous? Is the percentage reasonable? Is the taxpayer self-employed (not an employee)? If any element is weak, the deduction is a poor risk and should be reduced or not claimed. A modest, well-documented home office is defensible; a large, poorly documented one invites examination. Frame the decision in terms of risk, not just the dollar amount.

### Maintain The Multi-Year Record For Depreciation And Carryforward

The actual method creates records that span multiple years: the depreciation schedule (basis, recovery period, accumulated depreciation) and the carryforward of unused expenses. These must be maintained for as long as the deduction is claimed and through the sale of the home (because of recapture). An agent should ensure the taxpayer retains the Form 8829, the depreciation computation, and the carryforward schedule for each year, because a gap in the records makes the current-year deduction and the recapture computation indefensible.

The trap is discarding prior-year records after a few years, leaving the taxpayer unable to prove the accumulated depreciation or carryforward in a later audit or at sale. An agent should set up a permanent file for the home office that is updated each year and retained through the sale of the home plus the statute of limitations. The simplified method does not create depreciation or carryforward records, which is an administrative advantage, but if the taxpayer ever used the actual method, those records must be kept.

## Common Traps

### Reconstructing Documentation After An Audit Notice

Contemporaneous records are credible; reconstructed ones are discounted. The trap is waiting for an audit to build the file, when the examiner knows the records are after-the-fact.

### A Photograph That Shows Personal Use

A photo revealing a guest bed, toys, or a TV destroys the exclusive use claim. The trap is submitting a photo without reviewing it for personal-use elements.

### An Inflated Business-Use Percentage

Including personal space in the business percentage is detectable from the measurements and photos. The trap is inflating the percentage for a larger deduction beyond what the space supports.

### A Generic Principal-Place Narrative

A boilerplate description does not address the two prongs of the test or the administrative exception. The trap is relying on "I work from home" rather than a functional narrative of activities and locations.

### Errors On Form 8829

Incorrect percentage, unallocated mortgage interest or property taxes, or wrong depreciation basis are common findings. The trap is completing the form without reconciling to the underlying records.

### Claiming The Deduction As An Employee

Employees cannot claim the home office deduction from 2018 through 2025. The trap is claiming it for a W-2 worker based on pre-2018 rules.

### Discarding Multi-Year Depreciation And Carryforward Records

The actual method creates records that span years and survive until the home is sold. The trap is discarding prior-year records, making the current deduction and recapture indefensible.

## Self-Check

- [ ] A contemporaneous documentation file has been created during the tax year, including dated photographs of the office showing exclusive business use, measurements of the office and total home, a business-use log or calendar, and a functional narrative of principal place of business.
- [ ] Form 8829 has been completed accurately, with the business-use percentage matching measured square footage, indirect expenses computed correctly, depreciation on the correct basis and recovery period, and the net-income limit and carryforward applied.
- [ ] Photographs of the office have been reviewed to confirm no personal-use elements (bed, TV, toys) appear that would undermine the exclusive use claim.
- [ ] The principal place of business narrative addresses the relative-importance and time-spent prongs or the administrative/management exception, and confirms no other fixed location for those activities.
- [ ] The business-use percentage reflects only the exclusive business space in the numerator and a consistent total home area in the denominator, with no personal areas inflated into the percentage.
- [ ] The audit risk profile has been assessed (exclusivity, principal place support, record contemporaneity, percentage reasonableness, self-employed status), and weak elements have been corrected or the deduction reduced.
- [ ] The taxpayer's status has been confirmed as self-employed (not a W-2 employee), since employees cannot claim the deduction from 2018 through 2025.
- [ ] Multi-year records (depreciation schedule, carryforward of unused expenses) have been retained in a permanent file through the sale of the home and the statute of limitations.
- [ ] The documentation supports the specific business conducted (where clients are met, where administrative work occurs, how the space is essential) rather than relying on a generic description.
