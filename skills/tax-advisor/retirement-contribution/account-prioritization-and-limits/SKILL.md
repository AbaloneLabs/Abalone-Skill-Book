# Account Prioritization And Limits

Deciding which retirement account to fund first is not a single-correct-answer problem, and that is precisely why agents get it wrong. When a taxpayer has limited dollars to save, the ordering matters because each account type offers a structurally different benefit: the employer match is an immediate and guaranteed return, the HSA offers a rare triple tax advantage, the traditional pre-tax account shifts income out of a current bracket, and the Roth account buys tax-free growth in a future bracket. An agent who sequences these casually, or who applies a generic "max the 401(k) then the IRA" template, can leave free money unclaimed, forfeit a deduction that was available for only a narrow income window, or push savings into the wrong tax treatment for the taxpayer's bracket trajectory.

The blind spots are predictable and costly. Agents frequently overlook that the employer match has the highest risk-adjusted return of any contribution and should be captured before any discretionary saving, or they recommend a traditional deductible IRA without checking the active-participant deductibility phase-out, which silently disallows the deduction for a worker covered by a workplace plan. They assume a Roth IRA is always available regardless of income and miss the phase-out that phases the direct contribution to zero, and they treat the HSA as a spend-now medical account rather than the most tax-favored savings vehicle available. They also double-count the 401(k) deferral limit when a taxpayer changes employers mid-year, or they ignore that Roth versus traditional is fundamentally a current-versus-future-marginal-rate comparison rather than a matter of preference. The harm is permanent: a missed match is never recovered, a wrong tax treatment compounds for decades, and a disallowed deduction is a wasted contribution.

This skill covers the prioritization logic for funding 401(k), IRA (traditional and Roth), and HSA accounts, the contribution limits and phase-outs that govern each, and the sequencing tradeoffs under U.S. federal tax law. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; retirement tax planning is high-risk and a qualified tax professional must be consulted before acting. Rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, SECURE 2.0 altered several mechanics, and dollar amounts are indexed annually. Figures shown are 2024 and 2025 amounts and must be re-verified for the applicable tax year.

## Core Rules

### Capture The Employer Match Before Any Discretionary Saving

The employer match is the single highest-return contribution available because it is an immediate, guaranteed, often 100 percent return on the employee's deferred salary. A typical plan matches 50 percent of contributions up to 6 percent of salary, or 100 percent up to 3 to 5 percent. Whatever the formula, the unmatched portion of the deferral is leaving free money on the table. Prioritization logic must anchor here: capture the full match before funding any IRA, HSA, or taxable account, because no other contribution offers a guaranteed doubling in the year it is made.

Concretely, a worker earning 80,000 dollars with a 100 percent match up to 4 percent of salary must defer 3,200 dollars to capture the full 3,200 dollar match. If that worker has only 5,000 dollars to save, 3,200 should go to the 401(k) match before anything goes to an IRA or HSA. The only exception is a taxpayer whose employer offers no match, in which case the 401(k) deferral competes directly with the IRA and HSA on tax-advantage and cost grounds. Always identify the match formula and the minimum deferral to capture it fully, and treat that amount as the non-negotiable first tier.

### Evaluate The HSA As A Retirement Vehicle, Not Just A Health Account

An HSA, available only to those enrolled in a qualifying high-deductible health plan, offers triple tax advantage: contributions are pre-tax (or deductible), growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. For 2024 the limit is 4,150 dollars self-only or 8,300 dollars family (plus a 1,000 dollar catch-up at age 55+); for 2025 it rises to 4,300 dollars self-only or 8,550 dollars family. Because there is no use-it-or-lose-it rule and balances carry forward indefinitely, an HSA can function as a stealth retirement account, and after age 65 non-medical withdrawals are penalty-free (though taxable), behaving much like a traditional IRA.

The prioritization question is where the HSA sits relative to the IRA. Many advisors place the fully matched 401(k) first, then the HSA, then the IRA, because the HSA's triple advantage can exceed even a Roth IRA's double advantage for those who pay medical expenses. The decision depends on whether the taxpayer will incur qualified medical expenses (most will, especially in retirement) and whether they can afford to pay current medical costs out of pocket while letting the HSA grow. Confirm HDHP eligibility and the absence of disqualifying coverage (such as a general-purpose FSA or Medicare) before recommending any HSA contribution, because contributing while ineligible creates excess contributions and a 6 percent excise tax.

### Decide Roth Versus Traditional On Current Versus Future Marginal Rate

The Roth-versus-traditional choice is fundamentally a comparison of the marginal rate saved today against the marginal rate expected in retirement. A traditional pre-tax contribution reduces taxable income at today's marginal rate, while a Roth contribution pays tax today and withdraws tax-free later. If the taxpayer's current marginal rate is higher than the expected retirement marginal rate, traditional is better; if lower, Roth is better. When the rates are close, Roth's flexibility (no RMDs on Roth IRAs, tax-free inheritance characteristics) can tip the decision, but the rate comparison is the spine of the analysis.

Concretely, a worker in the 24 percent bracket who expects to retire in the 12 percent bracket should favor traditional deferrals, saving 24 cents per dollar now and paying 12 cents later. A young worker in the 12 percent bracket expecting to retire in the 22 percent or 24 percent bracket should favor Roth. The error is treating this as a static preference rather than a per-year, per-bracket decision: a taxpayer's bracket changes with income, and the optimal split can change annually. Also account for the fact that traditional withdrawals fill the lower brackets first, so even a retiree with substantial assets may face a low effective rate on early withdrawals, which favors some traditional saving.

### Apply The Traditional IRA Deductibility Phase-Out For Active Participants

A traditional IRA contribution is fully deductible only if the taxpayer (and spouse, if filing jointly) is not an active participant in an employer plan, or if income falls within the deductibility phase-out range for active participants. For 2024, an active-participant single filer loses the deduction between 77,000 and 87,000 dollars of modified AGI, and a joint filer (both covered) between 123,000 and 143,000 dollars. For 2025 these ranges rise to 80,000 to 90,000 dollars (single) and 126,000 to 146,000 dollars (joint). Above the range, the contribution is non-deductible and should generally be directed to Roth instead (subject to Roth eligibility) or treated as a non-deductible basis contribution only with full awareness.

The common failure is recommending a deductible IRA for a worker who is an active participant in a 401(k) without checking the phase-out, producing a non-deductible contribution the taxpayer believed was deductible. There is also a separate phase-out for a spouse who is not an active participant but whose partner is covered, which is more generous. Always determine active-participant status (a worker is active if eligible, even minimally, under an employer plan for any part of the year) and compute modified AGI against the correct range before promising a deduction. When the deduction is disallowed, redirect to Roth if eligible or reconsider whether the IRA is the right vehicle at all.

### Apply The Roth IRA Income Phase-Out And Consider Alternatives

Direct Roth IRA contributions phase out at higher incomes. For 2024, single filers phase out between 146,000 and 161,000 dollars of modified AGI, and joint filers between 230,000 and 240,000 dollars. For 2025 these ranges rise to 150,000 to 165,000 dollars (single) and 236,000 to 246,000 dollars (joint). Above the upper bound, no direct Roth IRA contribution is allowed, and a contribution made in error is an excess contribution subject to a 6 percent excise tax each year it remains. Below the lower bound, the full amount is allowed.

When a taxpayer's income exceeds the Roth phase-out, the prioritization must account for the alternatives. The Roth 401(k) (designated Roth deferral) has no income limit, so a high earner can still get Roth treatment through the workplace plan. The backdoor Roth strategy (a non-deductible traditional IRA contribution followed by a conversion) is widely used but carries the pro-rata rule, which taxes the conversion based on the ratio of pre-tax to total IRA balances, and can produce an unexpected tax bill if the taxpayer holds other pre-tax IRA assets. Do not present the backdoor as a clean workaround without modeling the pro-rata aggregation across all traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRA balances, and flag that legislative attention to this strategy makes its long-term availability uncertain.

### Coordinate Limits Across Accounts And Watch Aggregation Rules

The contribution limits for each account type are separate and do not share a single cap, but there are important aggregation rules. The 401(k) employee deferral limit (23,000 dollars for 2024, 23,500 dollars for 2025, plus catch-up) is a per-person limit aggregated across all 401(k)-type plans an individual participates in during the year, not per employer. A worker who maxes a 401(k) at one employer and then changes jobs cannot start fresh at the new employer; the total deferral across both cannot exceed the annual limit. The IRA limit (7,000 dollars for both 2024 and 2025, plus catch-up) is a combined limit across all traditional and Roth IRAs, not per account.

The HSA limit is separate and per-eligible-individual. The employer 401(k) match does not count against the employee deferral limit but does count toward the overall annual addition limit (approximately 69,000 dollars for 2024, approximately 70,000 dollars for 2025, including employer and employee amounts). When a taxpayer has access to multiple account types, confirm which limits interact and which are independent, and track year-to-date deferrals across employers to avoid an over-contribution. An over-contribution to a 401(k) must be corrected to avoid double taxation, and an IRA over-contribution incurs the 6 percent excise tax until withdrawn.

## Common Traps

### Funding An IRA Before Capturing The Employer Match

The symptom is a taxpayer who directs limited savings to an IRA while deferring less than the match-maximizing amount in the 401(k). The trap is leaving a guaranteed, often 100 percent return unclaimed in order to fund a contribution with an uncertain or lower return. The direction is to capture the full match first, then evaluate the IRA and HSA for any remaining dollars.

### Recommending A Deductible IRA Without Checking Active-Participant Status

The symptom is promising a deduction that the IRS later disallows. The trap is assuming any worker can deduct a traditional IRA contribution, when active participants in an employer plan face a phase-out that can eliminate the deduction entirely at moderate incomes. The direction is to determine active-participant status and compute modified AGI against the correct phase-out range before treating the contribution as deductible.

### Assuming A Roth IRA Is Always Available Regardless Of Income

The symptom is a high earner making a direct Roth IRA contribution that becomes an excess contribution. The trap is ignoring the income phase-out that phases the contribution to zero above the upper bound, triggering a 6 percent excise tax annually. The direction is to check modified AGI against the phase-out, and for ineligible taxpayers to route Roth saving through the Roth 401(k) or model the backdoor strategy with the pro-rata rule.

### Treating The HSA As A Spend-Now Account Rather Than An Investment

The symptom is a taxpayer who fully funds an HSA but spends it on current medical bills, foregoing decades of tax-free growth. The trap is underestimating the HSA as a retirement vehicle and prioritizing a taxable or even a Roth account above it. The direction is to consider paying current medical costs from cash flow and letting the HSA grow invested, especially when the taxpayer expects future medical expenses.

### Double-Counting The 401(k) Deferral Limit Across Employers

The symptom is an over-contribution discovered at year-end when a worker changed jobs. The trap is treating the deferral limit as per-employer rather than per-person aggregated across all 401(k)-type plans. The direction is to track year-to-date deferrals across all plans and request a return of excess before the deadline to avoid double taxation.

### Ignoring The Pro-Rata Rule In A Backdoor Roth Strategy

The symptom is an unexpectedly large tax bill on a conversion that was intended to be tax-free. The trap is executing a non-deductible IRA contribution and conversion without accounting for the aggregation of all pre-tax IRA balances (traditional, SEP, SIMPLE), which taxes the conversion proportionally. The direction is to model the pro-rata calculation across all IRA balances before recommending the backdoor, and to consider rolling pre-tax IRA assets into a 401(k) first if the plan allows.

### Prioritizing Tax Deferral When The Current Bracket Is Unusually Low

The symptom is a taxpayer in a temporarily low bracket (a sabbatical, a loss year, early retirement) who nonetheless maxes traditional pre-tax contributions. The trap is forgoing Roth saving at the exact moment when the current rate is lowest and future rates are likely higher. The direction is to tilt toward Roth when the current marginal rate is below the expected long-run rate, and to tilt toward traditional only when the current rate is high relative to the expected retirement rate.
