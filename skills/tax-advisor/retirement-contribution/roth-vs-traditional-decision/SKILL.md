# Roth Versus Traditional Decision

The Roth-versus-traditional contribution decision is not a lifestyle preference between two equally good accounts; it is a per-dollar, per-year bet on the relationship between today's marginal tax rate and the marginal rate those same dollars will face when withdrawn. A traditional pre-tax contribution deducts at today's marginal rate and is taxed later at the future marginal rate, while a Roth contribution pays tax now and withdraws tax-free. Because the two paths are mathematically equivalent only when the two rates are identical, the entire economic advantage comes from getting the rate comparison right, and the rate comparison is the part agents most often botch by comparing the wrong rates, ignoring bracket fill-up, or treating a one-time answer as permanent.

The blind spots are expensive and compounding. Agents routinely compare today's top marginal rate to a guessed single future rate, when the correct comparison is marginal now against the effective-plus-next-dollar rate later, because traditional withdrawals fill the lower brackets first and only the last dollars out hit the headline bracket. They forget that Roth IRAs have no required minimum distributions while traditional accounts do, that Roth assets pass to heirs tax-free and on a compressed inherited timeline, and that state tax at contribution and at withdrawal can swamp a small federal rate gap. They also treat the decision as binary and static, missing that a taxpayer's optimal tilt flips year to year as income, bracket, and residency change, and that splitting a single year's contributions between the two vehicles is often the correct answer under uncertainty. The harm is decades of paying tax at a rate that was avoidable, or locking in tax now that was unnecessary.

This skill covers the per-dollar and per-year Roth-versus-traditional contribution decision under U.S. federal income tax law, including the marginal-versus-effective rate nuance, RMD and inheritance effects, state tax, the case for splitting, and breakeven reasoning. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; retirement tax rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change frequently under SECURE 2.0, and dollar amounts are indexed annually. A qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific situation.

## Core Rules

### Compare Marginal Now Against The Realistic Future Marginal Rate

The spine of the decision is a rate comparison: the marginal rate saved this year by a traditional deduction versus the marginal rate the converted-or-withdrawn dollars would face in retirement. If today's marginal rate is clearly higher than the realistic future marginal rate, traditional wins; if today's is clearly lower, Roth wins. The decision must be made per contribution dollar and re-evaluated each year, because a worker's bracket shifts with income, filing status, and career stage. A resident physician in the 12 percent bracket expecting peak earnings in the 32 percent bracket should tilt Roth now, while a peak-earning partner in the 37 percent bracket expecting a lean retirement should tilt traditional.

The error is comparing today's rate to a vague average. Model the realistic future marginal rate by projecting taxable income in retirement, including pensions, Social Security, RMDs, and other income, then identify the bracket the next dollar of traditional withdrawal would land in. Stress-test the assumption, because overconfidence in a low future rate is the most common mistake and, unlike a conversion, a contribution decision repeated annually lets you correct course when the picture changes.

### Use The Effective-Rate Versus Marginal-Rate Nuance For Traditional

Traditional withdrawals do not come out at a single rate; they fill the brackets from the bottom up, so the first dollars out are taxed at 0 percent (within the standard deduction), then 10 percent, then 12 percent, and only the last dollars reach the headline bracket. This means a retiree with a moderate traditional balance may face a low effective rate on early withdrawals even if their last dollar sits in the 22 percent bracket. The correct comparison for a traditional contribution is the marginal rate saved now against the marginal rate on the last dollar withdrawn later, not the effective rate on the whole balance.

This nuance often favors traditional more than agents expect. A worker in the 24 percent bracket whose retirement withdrawals will mostly fill the 10 and 12 percent brackets should favor traditional, because the deduction is worth 24 cents now and the withdrawals cost roughly 10 to 12 cents on the early dollars. Conversely, a worker whose projected RMDs and other income will push every marginal dollar into the 24 percent or higher bracket in retirement should favor Roth, because deferring the deduction gains nothing and loses flexibility. Always distinguish effective from marginal, and never quote a single average rate as if it were the decision variable.

### Weigh RMDs And Tax-Free Inheritance

Traditional IRAs and traditional 401(k)s are subject to required minimum distributions beginning at age 73 (or age 75 for those born in 1960 or later under SECURE 2.0), which force taxable withdrawals whether the retiree needs the cash or not and can push income into higher brackets, trigger IRMAA Medicare surcharges, and increase the taxability of Social Security. Roth IRAs have no RMDs during the original owner's lifetime, so the balance can grow undisturbed and be timed for the owner's needs. Roth 401(k)s do have RMDs, but rolling a Roth 401(k) to a Roth IRA removes that obligation, a sequencing point worth noting.

Roth assets also pass to heirs income-tax-free, whereas inherited traditional balances are taxable to the beneficiary and, under the SECURE Act's 10-year rule, generally must be fully distributed within 10 years of the original owner's death, often creating a tax spike for the heir. For a taxpayer whose estate planning matters, the Roth treatment of inherited assets can be worth more than a marginal rate comparison suggests. Factor RMD exposure and inheritance taxation into the tilt, especially for savers who will not need the funds and expect to leave a legacy.

### Incorporate State Income Tax At Contribution And Withdrawal

State tax can dominate a decision where the federal rate gap is small. A traditional contribution deducted in a high-tax state like California or New York saves both federal and state tax at the combined marginal rate, while a withdrawal in a no-tax state like Florida or Texas pays only federal tax, making traditional more attractive for someone who will relocate to a low- or no-tax state in retirement. The reverse is also true: a Roth contribution made while living in a no-tax state, followed by withdrawals in a high-tax state, forfeits the state arbitrage and can make Roth worse.

Model state tax at both ends of the transaction, not just one. The combined federal-plus-state marginal rate now versus the combined federal-plus-state marginal rate later is the true comparison, and a planned relocation can swing a close call decisively. Note that some states tax Roth conversions or distributions differently, and state rules change independently of federal law, so verify the rules of both the current and the expected future state of residence rather than assuming uniformity.

### Split A Year's Contributions When The Rate Picture Is Uncertain

The decision does not have to be all-or-nothing within a single year. When the current-versus-future rate comparison is genuinely ambiguous, splitting a year's contributions between Roth and traditional is a rational hedge against rate uncertainty, legislative risk, and the unknowable future of tax law. A worker near a bracket boundary, or one whose retirement income trajectory is unclear, can fund part traditional to lock in the sure deduction and part Roth to secure tax-free upside, capturing diversification across tax treatments.

Diversification across tax treatments also creates optionality in retirement, because having both pre-tax and Roth buckets lets the retiree control taxable income year by year, manage IRMAA and ACA cliffs, and smooth bracket exposure. The split is not a cop-out; it is the correct response to irreducible uncertainty, and it should be sized deliberately rather than by default. Revisit the split annually as income and law evolve, because the optimal mix this year may differ from last year's.

### Run A Breakeven Analysis And Identify The Indifference Point

The mathematical breakeven occurs when the current marginal rate equals the future marginal rate, at which point Roth and traditional produce identical outcomes assuming constant rates and no external effects. Below the breakeven current rate, Roth wins; above it, traditional wins. Stating the breakeven explicitly forces the agent to commit to a rate assumption rather than hand-waving, and it reveals how sensitive the decision is to small changes in the assumed future rate.

Build the breakeven with the realistic future marginal rate, then sensitivity-test it by raising and lowering the assumed future rate by a few points to see whether the conclusion flips. If the decision is robust across a plausible range, commit to a tilt; if it flips within the range, that is precisely the signal to split or to favor the more flexible Roth. The breakeven is a diagnostic, not a forecast, and it should be revisited whenever income, filing status, or law changes materially.

### Respect Eligibility Constraints Before Choosing A Vehicle

The Roth-versus-traditional choice is constrained by eligibility before it is optimized by rates. Direct Roth IRA contributions phase out at higher modified AGI, traditional IRA deductibility phases out for active participants in an employer plan, and Roth 401(k) deferrals have no income limit while traditional 401(k) deferrals are always allowed. A high earner who cannot deduct a traditional IRA contribution and cannot directly contribute to a Roth IRA faces a constrained choice, and the answer may be a non-deductible contribution paired with a conversion, a Roth 401(k) deferral, or simply maxing the workplace plan.

Confirm eligibility for the chosen treatment before recommending it, because a contribution made in the wrong vehicle becomes an excess contribution or a wasted non-deductible basis. The rate comparison only matters among options that are actually available to the taxpayer, so map the eligible vehicles first, then apply the rate logic within that set. Do not let an elegant rate analysis override a hard eligibility constraint.

## Common Traps

### Comparing Today's Marginal Rate To An Average Future Rate

The symptom is a worker who chooses traditional because their average retirement tax rate "will be low." The trap is comparing the marginal rate saved now to an effective rate later, which understates the cost of traditional withdrawals on the last dollars out and biases the decision toward traditional. The direction is to compare marginal now to marginal later, recognizing that early traditional withdrawals fill low brackets first.

### Treating The Decision As A Permanent Lifestyle Choice

The symptom is a taxpayer who picked Roth at age 25 and never revisits it, even after their income quintupled. The trap is assuming a one-time election answers every future year, when the optimal tilt flips as income, bracket, and residency change. The direction is to re-evaluate the Roth-versus-traditional tilt annually for each year's contributions.

### Ignoring RMDs When Projecting Retirement Tax Rates

The symptom is a saver who assumes a low retirement tax rate and over-funds traditional, then faces large forced RMDs that spike their bracket and trigger IRMAA. The trap is projecting retirement income without modeling required minimum distributions from traditional accounts. The direction is to include RMDs beginning at age 73 or 75 in the future-rate projection and to value Roth's lack of lifetime RMDs.

### Forgetting State Tax At One End Of The Transaction

The symptom is a worker in a no-tax state who defaults to Roth, then retires to a high-tax state and pays tax on traditional withdrawals they could have deducted at zero state benefit. The trap is modeling state tax at only one end, missing the arbitrage of deducting in a high-tax state and withdrawing in a no-tax state. The direction is to include combined federal-plus-state rates at both contribution and withdrawal.

### Defaulting To All-Or-Nothing When The Rate Picture Is Uncertain

The symptom is an agent who forces a single Roth-or-traditional answer on a taxpayer near a bracket boundary. The trap is refusing to split when uncertainty is genuine, forgoing the diversification and retirement-income optionality that mixed buckets provide. The direction is to split the year's contributions between Roth and traditional when the breakeven is ambiguous or rate assumptions are fragile.

### Overlooking The Inheritance Tax Character Of Roth Assets

The symptom is an estate-minded saver who ignores that inherited traditional balances are taxable to heirs and often forced out within 10 years. The trap is valuing only the owner's lifetime rate and missing the heir-side tax cost of traditional assets. The direction is to factor the tax-free inheritance character of Roth assets into the tilt for taxpayers who expect to leave a legacy.

### Choosing A Vehicle The Taxpayer Is Not Eligible For

The symptom is a high earner directed to a direct Roth IRA contribution that becomes an excess, or a deductible traditional IRA that the active-participant phase-out disallows. The trap is running a rate analysis on an unavailable option. The direction is to confirm Roth and traditional eligibility and phase-outs first, then optimize within the eligible set.
