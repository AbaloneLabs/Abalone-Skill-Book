# Contribution Deadlines And Catch-Up

Retirement contribution deadlines and catch-up rules look mechanical, but they are a frequent source of wasted opportunity and excess-contribution penalties because each account type has a different deadline, a different catch-up structure, and a different age trigger, and the SECURE 2.0 Act added new layers that interact in non-obvious ways. The judgment problem is that a taxpayer who assumes one uniform deadline, typically the April filing deadline, will miss the year-end deadline that governs salary-deferral accounts, while a taxpayer who assumes all catch-ups begin at 50 will miss the larger super catch-up available at ages 60 through 63. An agent who treats these rules as interchangeable forfeits deferrals that cannot be recovered, because a missed salary-deferral deadline is gone for good.

The blind spots are predictable. Agents confuse the 401(k) deferral deadline, which is the last payroll of the plan year, with the IRA deadline, which is the tax filing deadline excluding extensions, and they assume the extension extends the IRA contribution window when it does not. They apply the age 50 catch-up amount uniformly across account types when the dollar amounts differ (the 401(k) catch-up is far larger than the IRA catch-up), and they miss the SECURE 2.0 super catch-up for ages 60 through 63 that applies only to defined contribution plans and not to IRAs. They mishandle the new requirement that high-earning participants make their catch-up contributions on a Roth basis, and they forget that catch-up is generally an election that must be actively set in the plan rather than an automatic grant. The harm is permanent: a salary deferral not made by the final paycheck cannot be retroactively contributed, and an excess contribution incurs a 6 percent excise tax each year it remains.

This skill covers the contribution deadlines and catch-up contribution rules for 401(k), 403(b), IRA, SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, solo 401(k), and HSA accounts under U.S. federal tax law, including the SECURE 2.0 super catch-up and the Roth mandatory catch-up income requirement. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; retirement tax planning is high-risk and a qualified tax professional must be consulted before acting. Rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, SECURE 2.0 introduced several mechanics with delayed and adjusted effective dates, and contribution limits and catch-up amounts are indexed annually. Figures shown are 2024 and 2025 amounts and must be re-verified for the applicable tax year, and several SECURE 2.0 provisions have had their effective dates postponed by IRS guidance.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish The Year-End Salary Deferral Deadline From The Filing Deadline

Salary-deferral contributions to a 401(k), 403(b), or 457(b) are made through payroll withholding, so the deadline is the last payroll of the plan year, effectively December 31 for a calendar-year plan. There is no extension to the filing deadline; once the final paycheck of the year is processed, no further employee deferral can be made for that year. This is fundamentally different from IRA contributions, which can be made up to the tax filing deadline. A taxpayer who waits until April to "max the 401(k)" discovers the deferral window closed months earlier.

Plan deferrals early in the year and adjust the per-paycheck rate to hit the target by year-end, especially for catch-up-eligible employees whose higher cap requires a higher deferral rate. A common failure is an employee who front-loads deferrals and hits the cap in October, forfeiting match in November and December if the plan uses a per-paycheck match formula, or conversely an employee who deferrs too slowly and cannot catch up after the final paycheck. Confirm the plan's true-up and match-frequency rules, and verify the last payroll date, because a deferral election made after the final payroll has no effect for that year. Note that the employer profit-sharing or matching contribution has a later deadline (the extended return due date for the plan), so only the employee deferral is locked at year-end.

### Apply The IRA Deadline As The Filing Deadline Excluding Extensions

Traditional and Roth IRA contributions for a tax year can be made up to the tax filing deadline, typically April 15, but the filing deadline does not include extensions, so filing an extension does not extend the IRA contribution window. A taxpayer on extension who contributes in, say, August and attributes it to the prior year has made an excess contribution for the current year. The deadline is a hard calendar date that is the same whether or not the return is extended.

Confirm the exact filing deadline for the year, which can shift for weekends and holidays, and communicate that an extension does not buy more time. A contribution made between January 1 and the filing deadline must be explicitly designated for the prior year or it defaults to the current year, so the taxpayer must instruct the custodian correctly. SEP-IRA and SIMPLE IRA have their own deadlines tied to the employer's return, and a solo 401(k) splits its deadlines between the employee deferral (year-end) and the employer profit-sharing (extended return due date), so do not treat all retirement vehicles as sharing the IRA deadline.

### Apply The Correct Age 50 Catch-Up Amount Per Account Type

Catch-up contributions for participants age 50 or older differ by account type in both eligibility and amount. For 2024, the 401(k) and 403(b) catch-up is 7,500 dollars on top of the 23,000 dollar deferral limit, and for 2025 the base limit rises to 23,500 dollars while the catch-up remains 7,500 dollars. The IRA catch-up is only 1,000 dollars (not indexed) on top of the 7,000 dollar limit for both 2024 and 2025. The HSA catch-up is 1,000 dollars but begins at age 55, not 50, and is a separate vehicle entirely. A taxpayer who applies the 7,500 dollar 401(k) catch-up to an IRA over-contributes and triggers the 6 percent excise tax.

Determine the age trigger carefully: a participant who turns 50 by the end of the plan year is eligible for the catch-up for the entire year, so someone turning 50 on December 31 can make the full catch-up, while someone turning 50 on January 1 of the next year cannot. Confirm the specific account type's catch-up amount and age trigger before quoting a contribution ceiling, and remember that the catch-up is generally an active election in an employer plan, not automatic. For 2025, SECURE 2.0 also introduced a higher limit for ages 60 to 63 (see the next rule), so the standard age 50 catch-up is no longer the ceiling for that age band in defined contribution plans.

### Apply The SECURE 2.0 Super Catch-Up For Ages 60 Through 63

Under SECURE 2.0, participants in a 401(k), 403(b), or governmental 457(b) who are age 60, 61, 62, or 63 at any point in the plan year are eligible for an enhanced catch-up contribution that is the greater of 10,000 dollars or 50 percent more than the regular catch-up, indexed for inflation starting in 2025. For 2025, this super catch-up raises the total deferral ceiling for eligible participants to roughly 34,750 dollars (the 23,500 dollar base plus the enhanced catch-up), compared to 31,000 dollars for a standard age 50 catch-up. This super catch-up applies only to defined contribution plans and does not apply to IRAs, which retain the flat 1,000 dollar catch-up.

The age window is narrow and specific: the enhanced amount applies only during the years the participant is 60 through 63, reverting to the standard catch-up at 64. A participant who turns 60 during the year is eligible for that entire year, and one who turns 64 during the year is not eligible for that year. Confirm the participant's age band for the specific plan year and verify the plan has been amended to allow the super catch-up, because plan adoption of this optional feature varies. This is a powerful but easily missed opportunity for late-career savers, and an agent who quotes only the standard catch-up understates the deferral ceiling for this age band.

### Handle The Roth Mandatory Catch-Up Income Requirement

SECURE 2.0 added a rule that participants whose wages exceeded a threshold (145,000 dollars in prior-year wages, indexed) in the preceding calendar year must make their catch-up contributions on a Roth (after-tax) basis rather than pre-tax. This rule was originally effective for 2024 but the IRS issued a two-year administrative transition delaying it to 2026, so for 2024 and 2025 catch-up contributions can still be made pre-tax regardless of income, but the Roth requirement takes effect for 2026 and later. A participant above the income threshold who makes a pre-tax catch-up after the rule is effective has an excess contribution that must be corrected.

Determine whether the taxpayer's prior-year wages exceed the indexed threshold and flag that, once effective, their catch-up must be designated Roth, which changes the tax treatment of that portion of the deferral. Also note a separate SECURE 2.0 provision that makes catch-up contributions mandatory for plans to offer (plans must allow catch-up for 401(k)-eligible participants 50 and older), though participants still elect whether to use it. Because the effective dates have shifted via IRS guidance, always verify the current status of these provisions for the applicable year rather than relying on the original statutory dates, and confirm the plan's Roth catch-up availability.

### Track The HSA Age 55 Catch-Up And The Spousal Rule Separately

The HSA catch-up is 1,000 dollars and begins at age 55, not 50, distinct from the retirement-account age 50 trigger. For 2024 the HSA limit is 4,150 dollars self-only or 8,300 dollars family plus the 1,000 dollar catch-up, and for 2025 it rises to 4,300 dollars self-only or 8,550 dollars family. An HSA-eligible individual age 55 or older can make the catch-up, but each spouse needs their own HSA to make their own catch-up, because the family limit is a shared pool and the catch-up is per individual, not per family.

A special rule allows an HSA-eligible individual age 55 or older to make a one-time transfer from an IRA to an HSA, subject to conditions, but this is distinct from the annual catch-up. Confirm HDHP eligibility and the absence of disqualifying coverage before recommending any HSA contribution including the catch-up, and remember that enrolling in Medicare ends HSA eligibility immediately. Do not conflate the HSA age 55 catch-up with the retirement-account age 50 catch-up, because applying the wrong age trigger or amount creates excess contributions subject to the 6 percent excise tax.

### Confirm Catch-Up Is An Election And Verify Plan Adoption

In employer plans, catch-up contributions are generally an active election the participant must set, not an automatic grant, and SECURE 2.0 made it mandatory for plans to offer catch-up to eligible participants but participants still choose whether to defer it. A participant who assumes the catch-up happens automatically may under-defer. Additionally, several SECURE 2.0 features (the super catch-up, Roth catch-up) require plan amendments, and plan adoption is uneven, so a feature available in the law may not yet be available in the taxpayer's specific plan.

Verify the specific plan's features before promising a contribution ceiling, because the statutory maximum is only achievable if the plan permits it. Confirm the participant has actively elected the catch-up rate, that the plan allows the super catch-up if the participant is 60 to 63, and that the plan supports Roth catch-up if the income requirement applies. Communicate that a missed deferral due to a late or absent election cannot be recovered after year-end, so the election should be set early and reviewed mid-year against the deferral pace.

## Common Traps

### Treating The 401(k) Deadline As The Filing Deadline

The symptom is a taxpayer who plans to "max the 401(k)" in April and finds the deferral window closed at year-end. The trap is assuming salary-deferral accounts share the IRA's filing deadline when their deadline is the last payroll of the plan year with no extension. The direction is to set the deferral rate early in the year and verify the last payroll date.

### Assuming An Extension Extends The IRA Deadline

The symptom is a taxpayer on extension who contributes in the summer and attributes it to the prior year, creating an excess. The trap is believing the filing extension extends the IRA contribution window, when the deadline is the filing deadline excluding extensions. The direction is to treat the IRA deadline as a hard date unaffected by the extension.

### Missing The SECURE 2.0 Super Catch-Up At Ages 60 To 63

The symptom is a 62-year-old who defers only the standard age 50 catch-up, leaving the larger super catch-up unused. The trap is quoting the standard catch-up ceiling for an age band that qualifies for the enhanced amount in defined contribution plans. The direction is to check the participant's age against the 60 to 63 window and confirm the plan allows the super catch-up.

### Mishandling The Roth Mandatory Catch-Up Income Requirement

The symptom is a high-earning participant whose pre-tax catch-up becomes an excess once the SECURE 2.0 rule is effective. The trap is ignoring the income threshold that forces catch-up to be Roth, or assuming the original 2024 effective date when IRS guidance delayed it to 2026. The direction is to check prior-year wages against the indexed threshold and verify the rule's current effective date.

### Applying The Wrong Catch-Up Amount Across Account Types

The symptom is an over-contribution from applying the 7,500 dollar 401(k) catch-up to a 1,000 dollar IRA catch-up. The trap is assuming catch-up amounts are uniform when they differ sharply by account type and age trigger. The direction is to confirm the specific account's catch-up amount and the age at which it begins (50 for retirement accounts, 55 for HSA).

### Assuming Catch-Up Is Automatic In An Employer Plan

The symptom is a participant who expects the catch-up to happen on its own and under-defers for the year. The trap is treating catch-up as automatic when it is generally an active election the participant must set. The direction is to confirm the catch-up election is active in the plan and review deferral pace mid-year.

### Forgetting The Solo 401(k) Dual Deadlines

The symptom is a self-employed owner who misses the year-end employee-deferral deadline while assuming the extended-return employer deadline covers everything. The trap is treating the solo 401(k) as having a single deadline when the employee deferral is due by year-end and only the employer profit-sharing gets the extended return date. The direction is to track the two deadlines separately and make any employee deferral before the final payroll.

### Ignoring State And Plan-Specific Deadline Variations

The symptom is a contribution made by a deadline that turns out to be wrong because of a state holiday, a non-calendar plan year, or a plan-specific cutoff. The trap is assuming a single universal deadline when plan years, state holidays, and custodial cutoffs vary. The direction is to confirm the specific plan year and the custodian's contribution cutoff for the prior-year designation.

- [ ] Has the IRA contribution deadline been confirmed as the tax filing deadline excluding extensions, with the understanding that an extension does not extend the IRA window and that a contribution between January 1 and the deadline must be designated for the prior year?
