# Installment Sale

An installment sale is a sale of property where the seller receives at least one payment after the tax year of sale. The installment method allows the seller to spread the gain over the years in which payments are received, rather than recognizing the entire gain in the year of sale. This can provide significant cash-flow and tax-deferral benefits, especially for sellers of real estate or businesses who finance the buyer. However, the installment method has specific requirements, exclusions, and computational rules that must be followed precisely. An agent who applies installment reporting without checking the exclusions or computing the gross profit ratio correctly will misstate the timing and amount of gain recognition.

Agents commonly miss that certain sales are prohibited from using the installment method (inventory, dealer property, depreciation recapture), that the gross profit ratio determines how much of each payment is gain, that interest must be imputed on deferred payments, and that installment obligations can have complex disposition consequences. The harm is a seller who misreports the gain (understating or overstating tax in a given year) or who fails to use a beneficial method.

This skill covers installment sale rules under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the rules are technical and interact with interest imputation and recapture, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Eligibility For The Installment Method

The installment method is available for sales of property where at least one payment is received after the close of the tax year of sale. Most capital assets and property used in a trade or business qualify. However, several categories of sales are prohibited from using the installment method: sales of inventory or property held primarily for sale to customers (dealer property), sales by dealers of real property or stock, sales that require depreciation recapture under Section 1245 or Section 1250 (the recapture portion is recognized in the year of sale, not deferred), and sales of publicly traded securities.

Confirm the property type and the seller's status before applying the installment method. A sale of a rental house by an investor generally qualifies. A sale of a house by a developer (dealer) does not. A sale of equipment with Section 1245 depreciation recapture requires the recapture to be recognized in the year of sale, while the remaining gain can use the installment method. Inventory sales never qualify. Do not assume installment reporting is available; check the exclusions for the specific property and seller.

### Compute The Gross Profit Ratio

The installment method spreads gain based on the gross profit ratio, which is the gross profit (sale price minus adjusted basis minus selling expenses) divided by the total contract price (the total payments the seller will receive, excluding stated interest). Each payment received is multiplied by the gross profit ratio to determine the portion that is gain. The remainder of each payment (the ratio complement) is the recovery of basis and is not taxed.

Compute the gross profit, the contract price, and the ratio precisely. The gross profit is the total gain if the entire sale were recognized in one year. The contract price is the total amount the seller will receive under the installment contract, excluding stated interest (which is taxed separately as interest income). The gross profit ratio is applied to each payment (principal portion, excluding interest) to determine the gain recognized in that year. Track the payments received each year and compute the gain using the ratio. The ratio does not change over the life of the installment obligation unless there is a modification.

### Recognize Recapture In The Year Of Sale

Depreciation recapture under Section 1245 or Section 1250 cannot be deferred using the installment method. The recapture portion of the gain must be recognized in full in the year of sale, regardless of when payments are received. Only the gain in excess of the recapture amount can be spread using the installment method. For real property with Section 1250 depreciation, the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (up to 25% rate) is recognized in the year of sale to the extent of payments received, with special ordering rules.

Compute the recapture amount and recognize it in the year of sale (for Section 1245 property) or apply the special rules for Section 1250 property. The recapture reduces the amount of gain eligible for installment spreading. Confirm the recapture computation and the year of recognition before applying the installment method to the remaining gain. Do not defer recapture using the installment method; the rules require current recognition of the recapture portion.

### Impute Interest On Deferred Payments

If an installment sale contract provides for deferred payments without adequate stated interest, the tax law imputes interest at the applicable federal rate (AFR). The imputed interest is treated as original issue discount or as additional interest income, and it reduces the principal portion of the payments (which affects the gain computation). The imputation ensures that the seller recognizes interest income at market rates rather than disguising interest as additional principal (which would be capital gain at a lower rate).

Confirm whether the contract has adequate stated interest (at least the AFR for the term). If the stated interest is below the AFR, impute interest and recharacterize part of each payment as interest income rather than principal. The imputed interest is ordinary income, and the reduced principal changes the gross profit ratio application. Use the AFR for the month of the sale (or the prior or subsequent month under certain rules) and the compounding convention specified. Do not ignore the interest imputation; it materially affects the character and timing of income.

### Handle The Disposition Of Installment Obligations

If the seller disposes of the installment obligation before it is fully paid (for example, by selling the note, gifting it, or canceling it), the disposition is a taxable event. The seller recognizes gain or loss based on the difference between the value received (or the obligation's fair market value) and the seller's basis in the obligation (the unpaid principal minus the deferred gross profit). Cancellation or forgiveness of the obligation by the seller may result in a gift or a deductible loss depending on the circumstances.

Track the basis in each installment obligation (the unpaid principal minus the remaining deferred gain). When the obligation is disposed of, compute the gain or loss based on the value received versus the basis. A seller who sells a note at a discount recognizes a loss; a seller who gifts a note may make a taxable transfer. Do not treat the disposition of an installment obligation as a non-event; it triggers recognition of the remaining deferred gain.

### Apply The Installment Method Election Rules

The installment method is the default for qualifying sales; the seller does not need to elect it. However, a seller can elect out of the installment method and recognize the entire gain in the year of sale. The election out is made on the tax return for the year of sale by reporting the entire gain and indicating the election. Electing out may be beneficial if the seller expects tax rates to rise, wants to use capital losses, or wants to complete the transaction for tax purposes.

Consider whether electing out is beneficial for the specific sale. The decision depends on the seller's expected future tax rates, the size of the gain, the interest rate environment, and the seller's cash needs. Electing out accelerates the gain but eliminates the ongoing installment reporting burden and the risk of buyer default on the note. Do not default to the installment method without considering whether electing out is better for the seller's situation.

### Report Installment Sales On Form 6252

Installment sales are reported on Form 6252 (Installment Sale Income) for each year a payment is received. The form computes the gross profit ratio, the payments received in the current year, and the gain recognized. The gain is then transferred to Form 4797 (for business property) or Schedule D (for capital assets) depending on the property type. The interest portion of payments is reported as interest income on Schedule B.

Ensure Form 6252 is filed for each year of the installment obligation and that the gain is correctly transferred to the appropriate form. Track the cumulative payments received and the remaining deferred gain over the life of the obligation. The form must be filed even in years when no payment is received, to maintain the record. Reconcile the installment reporting with the contract terms and the payments actually received.

## Common Traps

### Applying Installment Reporting To Inventory Or Dealer Property

Inventory and dealer property sales cannot use the installment method. The entire gain is recognized in the year of sale. Confirm the property type before applying the method.

### Deferring Depreciation Recapture

Recapture under Section 1245 or 1250 must be recognized in the year of sale. Only the non-recapture gain can be spread. Deferring recapture understates the year-of-sale tax.

### Failing To Impute Interest On Below-Market Contracts

If the stated interest is below the AFR, interest must be imputed. Ignoring the imputation mischaracterizes interest as principal and understates ordinary income.

### Using The Wrong Gross Profit Ratio

The ratio is gross profit divided by contract price (excluding interest). Errors in either component misstate the gain recognized on each payment. Compute carefully.

### Forgetting To Report The Disposition Of An Installment Note

Selling, gifting, or canceling an installment obligation triggers gain or loss recognition based on the obligation's basis. Treating the disposition as a non-event misses a taxable event.

### Defaulting To Installment Method Without Considering Election Out

Electing out may be beneficial if rates are expected to rise or if the seller wants to complete the transaction. Defaulting to installment without analysis may forgo a better outcome.

### Not Filing Form 6252 Every Year

Form 6252 must be filed for each year of the installment obligation, even with no payment. Skipping years breaks the record and risks errors in later reporting.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the property and seller been confirmed as eligible for the installment method (not inventory, not dealer property, not prohibited securities)?
- [ ] Has the gross profit ratio been computed as gross profit divided by contract price (excluding stated interest), and applied to each payment received?
- [ ] Has depreciation recapture under Section 1245 or 1250 been recognized in the year of sale, with only the non-recapture gain spread via installment?
- [ ] Has adequate stated interest been confirmed, or has interest been imputed at the AFR, recharacterizing part of each payment as interest income?
- [ ] Has the basis in each installment obligation been tracked (unpaid principal minus deferred gain) for disposition reporting?
- [ ] Has the election out of the installment method been considered and analyzed for benefit before defaulting to installment reporting?
- [ ] Is Form 6252 filed for each year of the obligation, with the gain transferred to Form 4797 or Schedule D and interest reported on Schedule B?
- [ ] Is the installment sale analysis documented with the eligibility, gross profit ratio, recapture, interest imputation, payment tracking, and reporting forms?
