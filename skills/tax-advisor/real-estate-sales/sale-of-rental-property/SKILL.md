# Sale Of Rental Property

Selling a rental property triggers a cascade of tax consequences that go far beyond a simple gain computation. The sale requires calculating the adjusted basis (original cost plus improvements minus depreciation claimed or claimable), separating the gain into ordinary recapture and capital gain portions, releasing any suspended passive losses, and determining whether the character is Section 1231 gain (potentially long-term capital gain) or ordinary income. An agent who computes the gain as sale price minus original purchase price, ignoring depreciation recapture and suspended losses, will dramatically misstate the tax and may advise a sale that is far more expensive than projected.

Agents commonly miss that depreciation must be recaptured as ordinary income (up to the unrecaptured Section 1250 amount), that suspended passive losses are released on full disposition, that Section 1231 netting can recharacterize gains and losses, and that the land portion of the sale is not subject to recapture. The harm is a seller who underestimates the tax by tens of thousands of dollars and faces an unexpected bill, or who fails to use suspended losses that could offset the gain.

This skill covers the tax consequences of selling rental property under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the rules involve recapture, passive activity, and Section 1231 netting, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Compute The Adjusted Basis Correctly

The adjusted basis of rental property is the original cost (excluding land, which is not depreciable) plus capitalized improvements and certain closing costs, minus depreciation allowed or allowable over the holding period. The phrase allowed or allowable is critical: the basis is reduced by the depreciation the taxpayer was entitled to claim, whether or not they actually claimed it. A landlord who failed to claim depreciation still has their basis reduced by the amount they should have claimed, and they face recapture on that amount despite never receiving the deduction.

Reconstruct the adjusted basis from the original closing statement, records of improvements, and the depreciation schedule. Confirm the original cost allocation between land and building (only the building is depreciable). Add the cost of improvements that were capitalized (new roof, addition, major renovation). Subtract the total depreciation claimed or claimable over the holding period. The result is the adjusted basis, which is the starting point for the gain computation. Do not use the original purchase price as the basis without adjusting for depreciation; this overstates basis and understates gain.

### Separate The Gain Into Recapture And Capital Portions

The gain on sale of rental real estate is divided into portions with different tax characters. First, the gain attributable to depreciation is recaptured as unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, taxed at a maximum federal rate of 25% (not the preferential 0/15/20% long-term capital gains rate). The recapture amount is the lesser of the total depreciation claimed or claimable, or the total gain on the property. Second, any remaining gain (the gain in excess of the recapture amount) is Section 1231 gain, which is generally long-term capital gain taxed at the 0/15/20% rate (subject to the Section 1231 netting rules and recapture of prior ordinary losses).

Compute the recapture amount and the remaining capital gain separately. The recapture amount is the depreciation taken (capped at the total gain), taxed at up to 25%. The remaining gain is Section 1231 gain, taxed at up to 20% (plus the 3.8% net investment income tax may apply to both portions). Present both portions and their respective rates so the seller understands the total tax. Do not treat the entire gain as a single long-term capital gain at 15% or 20%; the recapture portion is taxed higher.

### Release Suspended Passive Losses On Full Disposition

If the rental property generated suspended passive losses (because the owner did not have enough passive income to absorb them, and the active participation or real estate professional exceptions did not apply), those losses are released and deductible in full in the year of a complete disposition of the activity. The disposition must be a sale of the entire interest to an unrelated party in a transaction that fully terminates the passive activity. The released losses offset any income (including the gain on the sale and other nonpassive income) in that year.

Identify and total the suspended passive losses for the property from prior years. Confirm the disposition qualifies for release (complete sale to an unrelated party, not a partial sale or related-party transfer). The released losses reduce the taxable income in the year of sale, which can substantially offset the gain. Track the suspended losses carefully over the holding period, because they are a valuable deduction that reduces the net tax on the sale. A seller who forgets the suspended losses overpays tax on the sale.

### Apply Section 1231 Netting Rules

Section 1231 gains and losses (from the sale of business property held more than one year, including rental real estate) are netted together at year-end. If the net result is a gain, it is treated as long-term capital gain (favorable). If the net result is a loss, it is treated as ordinary loss (also favorable, because it offsets ordinary income without the capital loss limitations). However, the lookback rule requires recapturing as ordinary income any Section 1231 gains that are offset by Section 1231 losses recognized in the prior five years.

Compute the Section 1231 netting for all business property sales in the year, including the rental property and any other business assets. If the net is a gain, confirm whether the lookback rule applies (recapturing prior ordinary losses as ordinary income). If the net is a loss, the loss is ordinary and fully deductible. The netting can change the character of the rental sale gain depending on other business property transactions in the year. Do not analyze the rental sale in isolation; consider all Section 1231 transactions for the year.

### Allocate The Sale Between Land And Building

The sale price of a rental property must be allocated between the land and the building, because only the building is subject to depreciation recapture. The land portion of the gain is pure capital gain (Section 1231 gain if held for business use), not subject to the 25% recapture rate. The building portion of the gain includes the recapture of depreciation. The allocation should be based on the relative fair market values of the land and building at the time of sale.

Obtain or compute the fair market value allocation between land and building. The property tax assessment may provide a starting allocation, but the fair market value allocation at sale may differ. If the sale contract specifies an allocation, that allocation is generally used if it reflects fair market value. Apply the recapture rules to the building portion and the capital gain rules to the land portion. Do not apply recapture to the land gain or treat the building gain as pure capital gain without recapture.

### Consider Installment Sale And Like-Kind Exchange Alternatives

A sale that generates a large gain may be structured as an installment sale (receiving payments over multiple years, spreading the gain) or as a like-kind exchange under Section 1031 (deferring the gain by reinvesting in replacement property). Each alternative has specific requirements and tradeoffs. An installment sale spreads the gain and the tax over the payment period, which can help with cash flow but extends the tax obligation. A Section 1031 exchange defers the gain entirely if the requirements are met, but requires timely identification and acquisition of replacement property.

Evaluate whether an installment sale or a Section 1031 exchange is feasible and beneficial before finalizing a sale. The decision depends on the seller's goals (cash needs, desire to exit real estate, ability to identify replacement property), the tax cost of an outright sale, and the complexity of the alternative structures. Do not assume a straight sale is the only option; the alternatives can substantially reduce or defer the tax. See the installment sale and 1031 exchange skills for the detailed requirements.

### Report The Sale On The Correct Forms

The sale of rental property is reported on Form 4797 (Sales of Business Property) for the building portion (including recapture and Section 1231 gain), and the land portion may also be reported on Form 4797 or on Form 8949 and Schedule D depending on the classification. The depreciation recapture is computed on Form 4797 Part III (for Section 1250 property). The released suspended passive losses are reported on Form 8582. The Section 1231 netting is computed on Form 4797 Part I.

Ensure each component of the sale is reported on the correct form. The recapture, the capital gain, the land allocation, the suspended loss release, and the Section 1231 netting all have specific reporting locations. Misreporting (for example, reporting the entire gain on Schedule D without Form 4797 recapture) understates the recapture tax. Reconcile the reporting with the closing statement and the depreciation schedule.

## Common Traps

### Using Original Purchase Price As Basis Without Depreciation Adjustment

Depreciation reduces basis whether or not it was claimed (allowed or allowable). Using the original price overstates basis and understates gain, especially for property held many years.

### Treating The Entire Gain As Long-Term Capital Gain

The depreciation recapture portion is taxed at up to 25%, not the preferential rate. Only the excess gain is Section 1231 long-term capital gain. Lumping them understates tax.

### Forgetting Suspended Passive Losses On Disposition

Suspended passive losses are released and deductible on a complete disposition. Forgetting them overpays tax on the sale. Track and release them.

### Applying Recapture To The Land Portion

Land is not depreciable and not subject to recapture. Only the building gain includes recapture. Allocate the sale between land and building and apply recapture correctly.

### Ignoring Section 1231 Netting With Other Business Sales

Section 1231 gains and losses are netted at year-end. Other business property sales can change the character of the rental sale gain or loss. Analyze all Section 1231 transactions together.

### Reporting The Sale On Schedule D Without Form 4797

Rental property is business property reported on Form 4797, with recapture computed on Part III. Reporting only on Schedule D misses the recapture computation and understates tax.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the adjusted basis been computed as original cost (excluding land) plus improvements minus depreciation allowed or allowable, with the land-building allocation confirmed?
- [ ] Has the gain been separated into the unrecaptured Section 1250 portion (up to 25% rate) and the remaining Section 1231 capital gain portion (up to 20% rate)?
- [ ] Have suspended passive losses been identified and released on the complete disposition, offsetting the gain and other income in the year of sale?
- [ ] Has the Section 1231 netting been computed for all business property sales in the year, including the lookback rule for prior ordinary losses?
- [ ] Has the sale price been allocated between land (capital gain, no recapture) and building (recapture plus capital gain) based on fair market value?
- [ ] Have installment sale and Section 1031 exchange alternatives been evaluated for feasibility and benefit before finalizing a straight sale?
- [ ] Is the sale reported on Form 4797 (building, recapture, Section 1231 netting) and Form 8582 (suspended loss release), with each component on the correct form?
- [ ] Is the sale analysis documented with the basis computation, gain separation, recapture calculation, suspended loss release, land allocation, and reporting forms?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal rental property sale information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
