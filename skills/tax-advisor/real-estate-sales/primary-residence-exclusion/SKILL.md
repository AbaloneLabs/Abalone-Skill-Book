# Primary Residence Exclusion

The Section 121 exclusion allows a taxpayer to exclude up to 250,000 dollars of gain (500,000 dollars for married filing jointly) on the sale of a principal residence, provided the ownership and use tests are met. This is one of the most valuable exclusions in the tax code, potentially shielding a large gain from any federal tax. But the exclusion has strict requirements: the taxpayer must have owned and used the home as a principal residence for at least two of the five years preceding the sale, and the exclusion can generally be used only once every two years. An agent who applies the exclusion without verifying the tests, or who misses the special rules for partial exclusions, married couples, and non-qualifying use, will misstate the exclusion amount and the taxable gain.

Agents commonly miss the ownership and use test details, the two-year waiting period between exclusions, the partial exclusion for unforeseen circumstances, the rules for married couples where only one spouse meets the tests, and the non-qualifying use rule that limits exclusion for periods of rental or business use. The harm is a seller who claims an exclusion they are not entitled to (creating examination exposure) or who fails to claim a partial exclusion they qualify for (overpaying tax).

This skill covers the Section 121 primary residence exclusion under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the rules have many conditions and special cases, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Verify The Ownership And Use Tests

To qualify for the Section 121 exclusion, the taxpayer must have owned and used the home as a principal residence for at least two of the five years immediately preceding the sale. The two years of ownership and use do not need to be consecutive; they are aggregated over the five-year period. The ownership test and the use test are separate requirements, and both must be met. The use test requires actual residence as the principal home, not merely ownership or intent.

Document the ownership period (from the purchase date to the sale date) and the use period (the months the taxpayer actually lived in the home as their principal residence) over the five years preceding the sale. Confirm that both the ownership and use tests are met with at least 24 months each. Short absences (vacations, temporary trips) count as use. A taxpayer who owned the home for three years and lived in it for two years qualifies. A taxpayer who owned for five years but lived in it for only one year (renting it out for four years) fails the use test. The tests are strict; partial years are prorated by days.

### Apply The Correct Exclusion Amount

The exclusion is 250,000 dollars of gain for a single filer and 500,000 dollars for a married couple filing jointly. For the 500,000 dollar amount, both spouses must meet the ownership test (either spouse can own), both must meet the use test (both must have lived in the home for two years), and neither spouse must have used the exclusion on another home in the past two years. If only one spouse meets the tests, the exclusion is limited to 250,000 dollars (the qualifying spouse's amount).

Determine the filing status and whether both spouses meet the tests for the 500,000 dollar amount. If married filing jointly and both meet ownership and use, the full 500,000 dollars applies. If one spouse fails a test, the exclusion is 250,000 dollars. If single, the exclusion is 250,000 dollars. The gain in excess of the exclusion is taxable as long-term capital gain (assuming the ownership and use periods exceed one year). Confirm the exclusion amount before computing the taxable gain.

### Apply The Two-Year Waiting Period

The exclusion can be used only once every two years. A taxpayer who sold a principal residence and claimed the exclusion within the past two years cannot claim it again on a subsequent sale, even if the ownership and use tests are met for the new home. The two-year period is measured from the date of the prior sale to the date of the current sale.

Confirm whether the taxpayer (or either spouse, for married filing jointly) claimed the exclusion on a prior home sale within the past two years. If so, the exclusion is not available for the current sale, and the full gain is taxable. The two-year waiting period is a separate requirement from the ownership and use tests; all three must be met. A taxpayer who meets ownership and use but sold a prior home with the exclusion 18 months ago cannot claim the exclusion again.

### Handle Partial Exclusions For Unforeseen Circumstances

If the taxpayer does not meet the two-year ownership and use tests due to unforeseen circumstances, a partial exclusion may be available. The partial exclusion is prorated based on the fraction of the two-year period that was met. Unforeseen circumstances include change in employment, health issues, or other unforeseen events specified by the IRS (such as divorce, multiple births, natural disasters, or death of a spouse). The prorated exclusion is the full amount multiplied by the qualified use period divided by two years.

Determine whether the taxpayer qualifies for a partial exclusion due to a premature sale. Document the unforeseen circumstance (job relocation, health change, divorce, etc.) and the date. Compute the prorated exclusion based on the time the home was used as a principal residence. For example, a taxpayer who lived in the home for one year (half of the two-year requirement) due to a job relocation can exclude up to 125,000 dollars (half of 250,000 dollars). Do not assume the full exclusion is lost if the tests are not met; the partial exclusion may provide meaningful relief.

### Apply The Non-Qualifying Use Rule

The exclusion does not apply to gain attributable to periods of non-qualifying use, which are periods after 2008 during which the property was not used as the taxpayer's principal residence. The most common non-qualifying use is rental use. If a taxpayer lived in a home for two years, rented it for three years, and then sold it, the gain attributable to the rental period is non-qualifying and cannot be excluded, even though the ownership and use tests are met for the residence period.

Compute the non-qualifying use fraction (the non-qualifying use period divided by the total ownership period) and allocate the gain between qualifying and non-qualifying portions. The exclusion applies only to the qualifying portion. The non-qualifying portion is taxable gain (subject to depreciation recapture for the rental period). This rule prevents taxpayers from converting rental property to personal use briefly and excluding the entire gain. Track the periods of qualifying and non-qualifying use over the ownership period, especially for properties that were rented before being sold.

### Consider The Depreciation Recapture After Rental Use

If the home was used as a rental (or for business) at any point, the depreciation claimed or allowable during the rental period must be recaptured as ordinary income (unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, up to 25%) on the sale, to the extent of gain. This recapture applies regardless of the Section 121 exclusion. The exclusion can shield the remaining gain, but not the depreciation recapture. A taxpayer who rented the home for years and then moved back in cannot exclude the recapture through Section 121.

Compute the depreciation recapture separately from the excludable gain. The recapture is recognized in full (to the extent of gain) at the 25% maximum rate, and the Section 121 exclusion applies to the remaining gain (after the non-qualifying use allocation). This interaction is complex: the non-qualifying use rule allocates gain between qualifying and non-qualifying periods, the depreciation recapture is recognized on the rental depreciation, and the exclusion applies to the qualifying non-recapture gain. Model each component separately.

### Report The Sale Correctly

If the entire gain is excluded (gain is below the exclusion amount and all tests are met), the sale does not need to be reported on the tax return. If the gain exceeds the exclusion, or if any portion is taxable (non-qualifying use, depreciation recapture), the sale is reported on Form 8949 and Schedule D, with the excluded portion not shown as income. The depreciation recapture is reported on Form 4797. Form 1099-S (Proceeds from Real Estate Transactions) may be issued by the closing agent, and the taxpayer should reconcile it with their own records.

Confirm whether the sale must be reported. If the gain is fully excluded, no reporting is required (but the taxpayer should retain records). If any gain is taxable, report it on the correct forms. The 1099-S reports the gross proceeds, which the IRS receives; ensure the return reconciles with the 1099-S if one was issued. Misreporting (failing to report a taxable sale, or reporting an excluded sale unnecessarily) creates errors.

## Common Traps

### Claiming The Exclusion Without Meeting The Use Test

The use test requires actual residence for two years. Owning the home for five years but living in it for one year fails the use test. Confirm both ownership and use.

### Using The Exclusion Within The Two-Year Waiting Period

The exclusion can be used only once every two years. A prior sale within two years disqualifies the current sale, even with ownership and use met.

### Forgetting The Non-Qualifying Use Allocation For Rental Periods

Gain attributable to post-2008 rental or non-residence use cannot be excluded. The gain must be allocated between qualifying and non-qualifying periods.

### Overlooking Depreciation Recapture After Rental Use

Depreciation during a rental period is recaptured at up to 25%, regardless of the Section 121 exclusion. The exclusion does not shield recapture.

### Missing The Partial Exclusion For Unforeseen Circumstances

A premature sale due to job change, health, or other unforeseen events may qualify for a prorated exclusion. Missing this overstates the taxable gain.

### Claiming 500000 For Married Filing Jointly When One Spouse Fails A Test

Both spouses must meet ownership and use for the 500,000 dollar amount. If one fails, the exclusion is 250,000 dollars. Confirm both spouses' tests.

### Failing To Report A Sale With Taxable Gain

If any gain is taxable (exceeds exclusion, non-qualifying use, or recapture), the sale must be reported. Assuming no reporting is needed because it is a home sale understates tax.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have the ownership and use tests been verified, with at least 24 months of ownership and 24 months of principal residence use in the five years preceding the sale?
- [ ] Has the correct exclusion amount been determined (250,000 dollars single, 500,000 dollars married filing jointly with both spouses meeting the tests)?
- [ ] Has the two-year waiting period been checked, confirming no prior exclusion was claimed on another home within the past two years?
- [ ] If the tests are not met, has the partial exclusion for unforeseen circumstances (job change, health, divorce, etc.) been analyzed and the prorated amount computed?
- [ ] Has the non-qualifying use rule been applied, allocating gain between qualifying residence use and non-qualifying rental or business use (for post-2008 periods)?
- [ ] Has depreciation recapture from any rental or business use period been computed and recognized at up to 25%, separate from the excludable gain?
- [ ] Is the sale reported correctly, with fully excluded sales not reported and partially taxable sales reported on Form 8949, Schedule D, and Form 4797 as applicable?
- [ ] Is the exclusion analysis documented with the ownership and use periods, the exclusion amount, any partial exclusion, non-qualifying use allocation, and recapture computation?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal primary residence exclusion information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
