# Lot Identification Methods

When a taxpayer sells part of a position acquired through multiple purchases, the tax law requires identifying which specific shares were sold in order to determine the gain, loss, and holding period. The choice of identification method can dramatically change the tax outcome of a partial sale, because different lots may have very different basis values and holding periods. Specific share identification allows the taxpayer to select the most tax-favorable lots, while the default first-in-first-out method may produce a very different result. An agent who ignores lot identification, or who lets a broker default to FIFO, is leaving tax planning opportunities on the table or creating unintended consequences.

Agents commonly miss that specific share identification must be made at the time of the sale and documented adequately, that the default method when no identification is made is FIFO, that brokers may impose their own default methods, and that the method choice interacts with loss harvesting and holding period strategy. The harm is a taxpayer who inadvertently sells low-basis long-term shares (maximizing gain at preferential rates) when they could have sold high-basis short-term shares (minimizing gain or generating a deductible loss), or vice versa depending on the goal.

This skill covers lot identification methods for U.S. federal capital gains purposes. It is not tax advice; broker procedures and documentation requirements vary, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Available Identification Methods

The two primary methods for identifying shares sold are specific share identification and first-in-first-out (FIFO). Under specific share identification, the taxpayer (or broker on the taxpayer's instruction) identifies the particular lots to be sold at the time of the sale. Under FIFO, the oldest lots are deemed sold first. Average cost is available only for mutual fund shares and certain regulated investment company positions, not for individual stocks or most other securities.

Specific share identification is generally the most flexible and tax-favorable method because it allows the taxpayer to choose lots based on their basis and holding period. FIFO is the default that applies when no identification is made, and it tends to sell the oldest (often lowest-basis, long-term) shares first. The choice between methods is not a one-time election; it is made (or defaulted) on each sale, though once a sale settles under a method it cannot generally be reversed. Confirm which method applies to each sale before it executes.

### Require Identification At The Time Of Sale

Specific share identification is valid only if the specific shares are identified at the time of the sale and received by the broker or taxpayer within a reasonable time. The identification must be made before or at the time of the transaction, not after the fact when preparing the tax return. A taxpayer who decides at tax time which lots "should have been" sold has not made a valid identification and is stuck with the default method.

The documentation requirement is that the taxpayer must specify to the broker which lots to sell (by lot identifier, acquisition date, or similar), and the broker must confirm the identification in writing or in the trade records. Some brokers allow online lot selection at the time of sale; others require a separate instruction. Do not assume a post-sale reallocation of lots is valid. If the taxpayer wants specific share identification, ensure the broker's system is used to select lots at the time of the trade.

### Recognize FIFO As The Default

If the taxpayer does not make a valid specific share identification, the default method is first-in-first-out. The oldest lots are deemed sold first, regardless of their basis or the taxpayer's preference. FIFO often produces the largest gain because the oldest lots tend to have the lowest basis (especially in a rising market), and those gains are often long-term, taxed at preferential rates.

The default to FIFO can be a costly surprise for a taxpayer who assumed they were selling specific lots. Brokers may apply FIFO as their default, or they may default to another method such as average cost for mutual funds. Check the broker's default method and confirm it matches the taxpayer's intent. If the taxpayer wants a different outcome, they must actively select lots at the time of sale.

### Use Specific Identification For Tax Optimization

Specific share identification allows the taxpayer to choose lots that achieve a desired tax outcome. To minimize gain, sell the highest-basis lots. To generate a loss for harvesting, sell lots with a basis above the current market price. To ensure long-term treatment, sell lots held more than one year. To manage the short-term/long-term mix, select lots based on both basis and holding period.

The optimization depends on the taxpayer's goal in the specific transaction. A taxpayer harvesting losses may want to sell high-basis short-term lots to generate a short-term loss (which offsets ordinary income first when netted). A taxpayer realizing gains in a low-income year may want to sell low-basis long-term lots to use the 0% rate. The method is a tool; the strategy must be defined before selecting lots. Present the lot options with their basis, holding period, and resulting gain or loss so the taxpayer can make an informed choice.

### Handle Average Cost For Mutual Funds

For mutual fund shares and certain dividend reinvestment plan shares, taxpayers may elect the average cost method. Under average cost, the basis of shares sold is the average cost per share of all shares in the account for that fund. Once average cost is elected for a fund, it applies to all future sales of that fund's shares unless the taxpayer revokes the election and switches to another permissible method.

Average cost simplifies recordkeeping but reduces flexibility compared to specific share identification, because individual lots are averaged and cannot be selectively sold for tax optimization. The election is made by reporting the average cost basis on the tax return for the first sale to which it applies. Brokers may default to average cost for mutual funds, so confirm whether the taxpayer has inadvertently elected it. Switching from average cost to specific identification requires care and may have holding period complications.

### Coordinate Lot Selection With Wash Sale Awareness

Lot selection for loss harvesting must be coordinated with the wash sale rules. Selling a lot at a loss and repurchasing substantially identical securities within the 30-day window before or after triggers a wash sale, disallowing the loss and adjusting the basis of the replacement shares. Specific share identification does not avoid wash sale treatment; it only determines which lot's loss is disallowed.

When harvesting losses using specific identification, plan the repurchase of replacement securities to avoid the wash sale window, either by waiting more than 30 days or by purchasing a different (not substantially identical) security. Do not assume that selecting a specific lot for sale insulates the loss from wash sale rules if replacement is purchased within the window. The wash sale analysis is separate from the lot identification analysis and both must be satisfied.

### Document The Method And Lots For Each Sale

For each sale, document the identification method used and the specific lots sold (if specific identification) or the FIFO application. This documentation supports the gain or loss reported on the tax return and is essential if the position is later examined. The documentation should include the sale date, the lots identified (by acquisition date and basis), the broker confirmation, and the resulting gain or loss computation.

For positions with many lots, reinvestments, and corporate actions, the documentation can be complex. Maintain the lot-level basis schedule and update it with each sale. Brokers report the method and basis for covered securities on the 1099-B, but the taxpayer should verify this against their own records, especially for noncovered securities or sales where the broker's default method was used.

## Common Traps

### Letting The Broker Default To FIFO Without Consideration

FIFO is the default when no identification is made, and it often sells the lowest-basis oldest shares first, maximizing gain. A taxpayer who does not actively select lots may pay far more tax than necessary.

### Attempting Specific Identification After The Sale

Specific share identification must be made at the time of the sale. Deciding at tax time which lots were sold is not valid identification and will not override the default method.

### Assuming Average Cost Applies To All Securities

Average cost is available only for mutual fund and certain DRIP shares, not for individual stocks, ETFs, or most other securities. Applying it to non-eligible securities is an error.

### Ignoring The Interaction With Wash Sales

Selecting a lot for loss harvesting does not avoid wash sale treatment if replacement securities are purchased within the window. The wash sale rules apply regardless of the identification method.

### Overlooking Holding Period When Selecting Lots

Selecting a high-basis lot that is short-term may generate a short-term loss (good for offsetting ordinary income) or may convert what could have been a long-term gain into a short-term gain (bad). Basis and holding period must be considered together.

### Failing To Document The Identification

Without documentation of the method and lots at the time of sale, the taxpayer cannot support a specific identification claim if examined. The default method will be imposed.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the identification method (specific share identification, FIFO, or average cost for eligible mutual funds) been determined for each sale, rather than defaulting without consideration?
- [ ] For specific share identification, was the lot selection made at the time of the sale and confirmed by the broker in writing or trade records?
- [ ] Has the broker's default method been checked and confirmed to match the taxpayer's intent, with active lot selection where the default is undesirable?
- [ ] For loss harvesting, have lots been selected based on both basis and holding period to achieve the desired tax outcome (short-term loss, long-term gain, etc.)?
- [ ] Has the wash sale window been considered when selecting lots for loss harvesting, with replacement purchases planned to avoid disallowance?
- [ ] For mutual funds, has the average cost election status been confirmed, and has the taxpayer understood the reduced flexibility versus specific identification?
- [ ] Is each sale documented with the method, the lots sold (or FIFO application), the broker confirmation, and the resulting gain or loss computation?
- [ ] Has the lot-level basis schedule been maintained and updated with each sale, especially for positions with reinvestments and corporate actions?
