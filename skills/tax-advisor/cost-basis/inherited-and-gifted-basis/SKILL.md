# Inherited And Gifted Basis

Property received through inheritance or as a gift does not take the recipient's purchase price as its basis. Instead, special rules determine the basis, and these rules are fundamentally different for inherited property versus gifted property. Inherited property generally receives a stepped-up (or stepped-down) basis equal to the fair market value at the date of death, which can eliminate all accumulated gain. Gifted property generally carries over the donor's basis, which preserves all the accumulated gain and shifts it to the recipient. Confusing these two regimes, or applying them mechanically without checking the special rules, produces dramatically wrong gain and loss computations.

Agents commonly miss the date-of-death versus alternate valuation date choice for inherited property, the special rule for property that declined in value between purchase and death, the dual basis rule for gifts (different basis for gain versus loss), the holding period carryover for gifts, and the interaction with the gift tax annual exclusion and lifetime exemption. The harm is a recipient who sells inherited property using the decedent's original basis (overstating gain and overpaying tax) or who sells gifted property using the fair market value at the date of gift (understating gain and underpaying tax).

This skill covers basis rules for inherited and gifted property under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; estate and gift tax rules are complex and fact-specific, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply Stepped-Up Basis To Inherited Property

Property acquired from a decedent generally takes a basis equal to the fair market value at the date of the decedent's death. This stepped-up basis replaces the decedent's original cost basis, and it applies to most assets passing through an estate, including individually owned assets, assets held in a revocable trust, and certain jointly held property. The effect is that all the gain that accumulated during the decedent's lifetime is eliminated for income tax purposes; the recipient's basis is the date-of-death value, regardless of what the decedent paid.

A stepped-down basis applies if the fair market value at death is lower than the decedent's basis, in which case the basis is reduced to the date-of-death value. The step-up or step-down is determined as of the date of death unless the estate elects the alternate valuation date. Confirm which valuation date applies, because it directly determines the basis. Obtain a qualified appraisal or reliable valuation for non-traded assets, because the date-of-death value must be substantiated. For publicly traded securities, the date-of-death value is typically the average of the high and low trading prices on the valuation date.

### Use The Alternate Valuation Date Only If Elected

An estate may elect to use the alternate valuation date, which is six months after the date of death, for valuing estate assets. If elected, the basis of inherited property is the fair market value at the alternate date rather than the date of death. The election applies to all assets in the estate; it cannot be applied selectively to some assets and not others. The election is available only if it reduces both the gross estate value and the estate tax liability.

The alternate valuation date election is primarily an estate tax planning tool, but it has income tax consequences because it changes the basis of inherited assets. If an asset is sold or distributed before the alternate date, its value is determined as of the date of sale or distribution, not the alternate date. Confirm whether the estate made the alternate valuation date election before using date-of-death values as basis. The election is made on the estate tax return and is binding.

### Apply Carryover Basis To Gifted Property

Property received as a gift generally takes the donor's adjusted basis as its basis in the hands of the recipient. This carryover basis means all the gain that accumulated while the donor held the asset is preserved and shifted to the recipient. If the donor bought stock for 10,000 dollars and gifted it when it was worth 50,000 dollars, the recipient's basis is 10,000 dollars, and a sale for 50,000 dollars produces a 40,000 dollar gain.

The carryover basis rule is the default for gifts, and it is the opposite of the stepped-up basis for inherited property. The recipient essentially steps into the donor's tax position. Confirm the donor's adjusted basis from the donor's records, because the recipient inherits not just the basis but also the responsibility to substantiate it. If the donor's basis records are incomplete, the recipient may be unable to prove basis and could face a zero-basis presumption on examination.

### Apply The Dual Basis Rule For Gifts

When gifted property has a fair market value at the time of the gift that is lower than the donor's basis, a special dual basis rule applies. For determining gain, the recipient's basis is the donor's basis (carryover basis). For determining loss, the recipient's basis is the lower of the donor's basis or the fair market value at the date of gift. This means the recipient may have a different basis depending on whether the sale results in a gain or a loss.

The dual basis rule prevents the recipient from claiming a loss that the donor could not have claimed. If the donor bought stock for 10,000 dollars, it declined to 7,000 dollars, and the donor gifted it, the recipient's basis for gain purposes is 10,000 dollars but for loss purposes is 7,000 dollars. A sale for 8,000 dollars produces neither gain nor loss, because the sale price is between the two basis amounts. Always compute both basis amounts when the gift value was below the donor's basis, and determine the result based on the sale price relative to both.

### Carry Over The Holding Period For Gifts

The holding period of gifted property carries over from the donor. The recipient's holding period includes the time the donor held the asset, so a gift of stock the donor held for five years is long-term in the recipient's hands even if sold immediately. This contrasts with inherited property, which is automatically long-term regardless of the decedent's holding period.

The holding period carryover interacts with the basis rules. For carryover basis gifts, both the basis and the holding period come from the donor. Confirm the donor's acquisition date to establish the holding period, and document it from the donor's records. A recipient who cannot establish the donor's holding period may be unable to claim long-term treatment.

### Account For Gift Tax Paid On Appreciation

If gift tax was paid on the transfer of appreciated property, a portion of the gift tax may be added to the recipient's basis. The adjustment applies to the gift tax attributable to the net appreciation in the property's value (the excess of the gift's fair market value over the donor's basis). This rule applies to gifts made under the gift tax rules in effect before 2010 and to certain later gifts; the specifics depend on the year of the gift and the applicable gift tax regime.

This adjustment is often overlooked because gift tax is rarely paid on annual-exclusion or lifetime-exemption gifts. However, for large taxable gifts of appreciated property, the basis adjustment can be meaningful. Confirm whether gift tax was paid and, if so, compute the basis adjustment under the applicable rules. Do not assume no adjustment is available just because the rule is obscure.

### Distinguish Community Property And Tenancy Rules

In community property states, the death of one spouse can step up the basis of both the deceased spouse's half and the surviving spouse's half of community property, because all community property receives a stepped-up basis. This is a significant benefit compared to common law states, where only the deceased's interest in jointly held property is stepped up. The treatment depends on state property law classification.

For jointly held property in common law states, only the deceased's fractional interest receives a step-up; the surviving joint tenant's interest retains its original basis. For tenancy by the entirety and joint tenancy with rights of survivorship, the step-up applies to the portion attributable to the deceased. Confirm the state property law classification and the form of ownership before computing basis, because the difference between full step-up (community property) and partial step-up (common law joint tenancy) can be substantial.

## Common Traps

### Using The Decedent's Original Basis For Inherited Property

Inherited property generally gets a stepped-up basis to date-of-death value. Using the decedent's purchase price overstates gain and overpays tax. This is a costly and common error.

### Using Fair Market Value At Gift Date As Basis For Gifts

Gifted property generally carries over the donor's basis. Using the date-of-gift value as basis understates gain and underpays tax when the property has appreciated.

### Forgetting The Dual Basis Rule For Loss Gifts

When the gift value is below the donor's basis, the basis for loss is the lower fair market value. A sale between the two values produces no gain or loss. Ignoring this rule can misstate the result.

### Overlooking The Alternate Valuation Date Election

If the estate elected the alternate valuation date, basis is the value six months after death, not the date of death. Confirm whether the election was made before using date-of-death values.

### Assuming Only The Deceased's Share Steps Up

In community property states, both halves of community property may step up. In common law states, only the deceased's interest steps up. The form of ownership and state law determine the result.

### Ignoring Holding Period Carryover For Gifts

Gifted property carries over the donor's holding period. A recipient who cannot establish the donor's holding period may lose long-term treatment.

## Self-Check

- [ ] For inherited property, has the basis been set to the fair market value at the date of death (or the alternate valuation date if elected), with a qualified appraisal for non-traded assets?
- [ ] Has the alternate valuation date election been checked, and if elected, has the basis been set to the value six months after death (or the date of sale or distribution if earlier)?
- [ ] For gifted property, has the donor's adjusted basis been established from the donor's records and used as the recipient's carryover basis?
- [ ] For gifts of property that declined in value, has the dual basis rule been applied, computing both the gain basis (donor's basis) and the loss basis (lower fair market value at gift)?
- [ ] Has the donor's holding period been carried over for gifted property, and has the donor's acquisition date been documented?
- [ ] For large taxable gifts of appreciated property, has any gift tax basis adjustment been considered under the applicable rules?
- [ ] For property held in community property states or joint tenancy, has the state property law classification and form of ownership been confirmed to determine the extent of step-up?
- [ ] Is the basis conclusion documented with the valuation date, the source of the value, the donor's basis (for gifts), and any special rules applied?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal basis information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
