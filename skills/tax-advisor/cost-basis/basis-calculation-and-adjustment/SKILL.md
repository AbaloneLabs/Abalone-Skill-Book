# Basis Calculation And Adjustment

Cost basis is the amount the tax law uses to measure gain or loss when an asset is sold, and it is far more than the original purchase price. Basis must be adjusted upward for purchase commissions and certain acquisition costs, increased for reinvested distributions, reduced for return-of-capital payments and depreciation, and recalculated entirely after wash sales, corporate actions, gifts, and inheritances. An agent who treats basis as a static number copied from a brokerage statement will produce incorrect gain and loss figures, which cascade into incorrect tax estimates, incorrect loss harvesting, and potential examination exposure.

Agents commonly miss that broker-reported basis may be missing or wrong for noncovered securities, that wash sales and corporate actions require basis adjustments that brokers may not fully reflect, that return-of-capital and depreciation reduce basis, and that the tax basis and the accounting or economic basis can diverge. The harm is a taxpayer who sells based on an overstated basis, understating gain and underpaying tax, or who uses an understated basis, overpaying tax and missing legitimate loss harvesting.

This skill covers the calculation and adjustment of cost basis for U.S. federal capital gains purposes. It is not tax advice; basis rules are detailed and fact-specific, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Start With Purchase Price Plus Acquisition Costs

The initial basis of a purchased capital asset is the cost, which includes the purchase price plus any commissions, fees, and directly attributable acquisition costs. For securities, this means the broker commission and any transaction fees are added to the purchase price to form the basis, not deducted separately as an expense. For real estate, basis includes the purchase price, closing costs such as legal fees and recording fees, and certain other acquisition costs, but excludes amounts that are financing-related or personal.

Establish the initial basis from the trade confirmation or closing statement, not from memory or from a rounded figure on a statement. For securities purchased in multiple transactions, each lot has its own basis. Confirm whether the reported basis includes commissions or whether commissions were billed separately and must be added back. A basis that omits a commission understates gain by the commission amount on every sale.

### Increase Basis For Reinvested Distributions

Reinvested dividends and capital gains distributions are treated as additional purchases that increase basis. Each reinvestment creates a new lot with its own basis (the amount reinvested) and its own holding period (from the reinvestment date). A taxpayer who has reinvested distributions over many years has accumulated many small lots, and the total basis of the position is the sum of the original purchase plus all reinvested amounts.

The trap is selling the entire position and using only the original purchase price as basis, which overstates gain by the total of all reinvested distributions. This is a common and costly error, because reinvested distributions were already taxed as dividend or gain income in the year received, and they must be added to basis to avoid double taxation. Reconstruct the reinvestment history from broker records or from prior-year 1099-DIV and 1099-DIV statements when computing basis for a long-held position with reinvestment.

### Reduce Basis For Return Of Capital And Depreciation

Certain distributions and deductions reduce basis rather than being taxed currently. Return-of-capital distributions, common with master limited partnerships, real estate investment trusts, and certain mutual funds, are not taxed as income when received but instead reduce the basis of the investment. Once basis is reduced to zero, further return-of-capital distributions become capital gains. Depreciation deductions on real property reduce the basis by the amount of depreciation allowed or allowable, whether or not the taxpayer actually claimed it.

Track basis reductions over the holding period. A taxpayer who has received return-of-capital distributions for years may have a basis far below the original purchase price, and selling based on the original price overstates basis and understates gain. Similarly, real property basis must be reduced by depreciation claimed or claimable, which affects the gain and the recapture computation on sale. Maintain a running basis schedule for any investment subject to basis reductions.

### Adjust Basis For Wash Sales

When a loss is disallowed under the wash sale rules, the disallowed loss is added to the basis of the replacement shares. This basis adjustment preserves the loss economically by embedding it in the replacement position, to be recognized when the replacement is sold. The replacement shares' basis equals the purchase price plus the disallowed loss.

Brokers are required to adjust basis for wash sales within the same account for covered securities, but wash sales across accounts, across spouses, or involving noncovered securities may not be fully reflected in broker-reported basis. Review the 1099-B for wash sale adjustments and reconcile against the taxpayer's actual transactions. A wash sale that occurred in one account but whose replacement was purchased in another account will not appear on either broker's 1099-B and must be computed manually.

### Recalculate Basis After Corporate Actions

Corporate actions such as stock splits, reverse splits, mergers, spinoffs, and reorganizations require basis reallocation among the securities received. For a stock split, the original basis is divided among the total post-split shares. For a merger or reorganization, the basis of the old shares carries over to the new shares in a nontaxable transaction, but must be allocated if multiple securities or cash were received (boot). For a spinoff, basis must be allocated between the parent stock and the spun-off stock based on their relative fair market values.

Brokers typically adjust basis for covered securities after corporate actions and report the adjusted basis on the 1099-B, but the adjustment may be wrong or incomplete, especially for older or complex transactions. Obtain the Form 8937 (Organizational Action) issued by the company for the corporate action, which provides the basis allocation methodology. For spinoffs, the allocation ratios are usually published in the spinoff documentation or tax guidance. Do not accept an unadjusted basis after a corporate action.

### Separate Covered And Noncovered Securities

Broker reporting of basis depends on whether the security is covered or noncovered. Covered securities (generally equities acquired after 2011, mutual funds and dividend reinvestment plans after 2012, and other specified securities after later dates) have basis tracked and reported by the broker on Form 1099-B. Noncovered securities (acquired before the effective dates) may have basis reported as unknown or based on the taxpayer's own records.

For noncovered securities, the broker may report proceeds only, with basis left blank or marked as noncovered. The taxpayer is responsible for computing and reporting the correct basis. Do not treat a blank or zero basis as the actual basis; reconstruct it from historical records. This is a common source of overstated gain, because a taxpayer who accepts a zero basis pays tax on the full proceeds rather than on the actual gain.

### Reconcile Broker-Reported Basis With Taxpayer Records

Broker-reported basis is a starting point, not a final answer. It may be incomplete (noncovered securities), incorrect (corporate action errors, missing wash sale adjustments across accounts), or based on a default method (FIFO) rather than the taxpayer's chosen identification method. Before relying on broker-reported basis for a tax return or planning decision, reconcile it against the taxpayer's own records and the supporting documentation.

Discrepancies should be investigated and resolved, and the correct basis reported on the return even if it differs from the 1099-B. The IRS receives a copy of the 1099-B, so material differences should be explainable and documented. Maintain the basis reconciliation as part of the tax file.

## Common Traps

### Using Purchase Price Only And Ignoring Commissions

Omitting commissions and acquisition fees from basis understates basis and overstates gain. The commission is part of the cost and must be included.

### Forgetting Reinvested Distributions Increase Basis

Reinvested dividends and capital gains distributions are taxed when received and added to basis. Using only the original purchase price as basis double-taxes the reinvested amounts.

### Ignoring Return-Of-Capital Basis Reductions

Return-of-capital distributions reduce basis. Failing to track these reductions overstates basis and understates gain, especially for MLPs and certain funds held for years.

### Accepting Broker Basis For Noncovered Securities

Noncovered securities may have no basis reported. Accepting a blank or zero basis overstates gain dramatically. Reconstruct basis from historical records.

### Missing Wash Sale Basis Adjustments Across Accounts

Wash sales across accounts or spouses are not reflected on any single broker's 1099-B. The basis adjustment must be computed manually and reported correctly.

### Failing To Allocate Basis After Corporate Actions

Mergers, spinoffs, and reorganizations require basis reallocation. Using the pre-event basis or an unadjusted figure produces incorrect gain or loss on the eventual sale.

### Confusing Tax Basis With Book Or Economic Basis

The tax basis, adjusted for the rules above, is what determines gain or loss for tax purposes. It may differ from the carrying value on financial statements or the taxpayer's personal records.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the initial basis include the purchase price plus commissions, fees, and directly attributable acquisition costs from the trade confirmation or closing statement?
- [ ] Have all reinvested dividends and capital gains distributions been added to basis as separate lots with their own holding periods?
- [ ] Have return-of-capital distributions and depreciation been tracked as basis reductions over the holding period?
- [ ] Have wash sale disallowed losses been added to the basis of replacement shares, including wash sales across accounts or spouses not shown on any single 1099-B?
- [ ] Have corporate actions (splits, mergers, spinoffs, reorganizations) been analyzed and basis reallocated using Form 8937 or the published allocation methodology?
- [ ] Have noncovered securities been identified and their basis reconstructed from historical records rather than accepted as blank or zero?
- [ ] Has broker-reported basis been reconciled against taxpayer records and supporting documentation, with discrepancies investigated and documented?
- [ ] Is the final basis conclusion supported by a lot-level or transaction-level schedule that can be verified?
- [ ] Has the agent noted that this is general U.S. federal basis information, not tax advice, and recommended consulting a qualified tax professional for the specific situation?
