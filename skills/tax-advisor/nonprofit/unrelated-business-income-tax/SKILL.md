# Unrelated Business Income Tax

A tax-exempt organization that engages in a trade or business that is regularly carried on and not substantially related to its exempt purpose must pay unrelated business income tax (UBIT) under Section 511 on the net income from that activity. The purpose of UBIT is to prevent exempt organizations from using their tax-exempt status to gain an unfair competitive advantage over taxable businesses. The judgment problem is that the three-part test (trade or business, regularly carried on, not substantially related) is applied to the facts of each activity, the exceptions are narrow and specific, and the net income computation has special rules for nonrecognition of losses from exempt activities and for the allocation of expenses. An agent who treats all revenue as exempt, or who treats all unrelated revenue as taxable without analyzing the exceptions and the expense allocation, will either expose the organization to UBIT and penalties or overpay tax by losing legitimate related deductions.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the "substantially related" test is measured by whether the activity contributes importantly to the accomplishment of the exempt purpose, not by whether the activity is generally connected to the organization's field, so a museum gift shop that sells educational items related to its exhibits may be related while one that sells general merchandise is not. Second, the exceptions for activities conducted substantially by volunteers, for the convenience of members, and for the sale of donated goods are narrow and each has its own conditions, so a thrift shop selling donated goods qualifies but a thrift shop selling purchased inventory does not. Third, losses from exempt activities cannot offset unrelated business income, so an organization with a profitable unrelated business and a loss-making exempt activity cannot net them, and the reverse (a loss in an unrelated business) is generally deductible only against unrelated business income, not against exempt income. Fourth, the UBIT estimated-tax safe harbor and the USD 1,000 gross-income threshold determine filing and payment obligations, and a failure to file Form 990-T or pay estimated tax triggers penalties.

This skill covers unrelated business income tax under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Three-Part UBI Test To Each Activity

Unrelated business income is income from a trade or business that is regularly carried on and is not substantially related to the organization's exempt purpose. Each element must be present for the income to be UBI. A trade or business includes any activity carried on for the production of income from the sale of goods or the performance of services, and it is interpreted broadly. Regularly carried on means the activity is conducted with a frequency and continuity similar to comparable commercial activities, so a one-time event or an intermittent activity is not regular. Not substantially related means the activity does not contribute importantly to the accomplishment of the exempt purpose, measured by the relationship between the business activity and the exempt purpose, not by the use of the profits.

Apply the three-part test to each revenue-generating activity separately. An activity that fails any element is not UBI: a one-time fundraiser is not regular, a service provided for the convenience of members may not be a trade or business in the competitive sense, and an activity that contributes importantly to the exempt purpose is substantially related. Document the analysis for each activity, because the IRS examines the facts, and an activity that looks commercial may be related if it furthers the exempt purpose. The most common disputes are over the substantial-relatedness of ancillary activities such as gift shops, advertising in publications, and member services.

### Measure Substantial Relatedness By Contribution To Exempt Purpose

The substantial-relatedness test asks whether the business activity contributes importantly to the accomplishment of the organization's exempt purpose. The relationship is measured by the intrinsic connection between the activity and the exempt purpose, not by the profitability of the activity or the use of the profits for exempt purposes. A hospital's pharmacy that serves patients is related to the hospital's exempt health-care purpose; a hospital's gift shop selling general merchandise is generally not related. A university's bookstore selling textbooks is related to the educational purpose; a university's online apparel store selling branded merchandise to the general public is generally not related, although the analysis depends on the connection to the educational mission.

Evaluate the intrinsic connection, not the use of proceeds. A common error is treating an activity as related because the profits fund the exempt purpose, but the use of profits is irrelevant to the substantial-relatedness test; the test is whether the activity itself furthers the exempt purpose. Advertising income in an exempt organization's publication is a frequent source of UBI, because selling advertising is a trade or business that is generally not related to the exempt purpose, even if the publication is educational. Sponsorship payments that provide only acknowledgment are not UBI under the Section 513(i) safe harbor, but payments that provide advertising or substantial benefits back to the sponsor are UBI. Distinguish sponsorship from advertising carefully, because the line determines whether the income is taxable.

### Apply The Statutory Exceptions Correctly

Section 512 provides several exceptions to UBI that agents frequently misapply. The volunteer-labor exception excludes an activity conducted substantially by volunteers (where 85 percent or more of the labor is performed by volunteers). The convenience exception excludes an activity conducted primarily for the convenience of the organization's members, students, patients, officers, or employees. The donated-goods exception excludes income from the sale of merchandise substantially all of which was received as gifts (such as a thrift shop run by a charity selling donated clothing). There are also specific exclusions for dividends, interest, annuities, royalties, and rents from real property (with modifications for debt-financed property and unrelated debt-financed income).

Confirm the conditions for each exception before excluding income. The volunteer-labor exception requires that 85 percent of the work be done by volunteers, so an activity with paid staff does not qualify. The convenience exception requires that the activity serve a population the organization serves in its exempt function (such as a hospital cafeteria for staff and patients), not the general public. The donated-goods exception requires that substantially all merchandise be donated, so a thrift shop mixing donated and purchased goods does not qualify for the donated portion unless it is separately tracked. Royalties (such as licensing the organization's name) are excluded, but the IRS scrutinizes arrangements that provide services or substantial benefits to the licensee, which can convert the royalty into taxable service income.

### Compute Net UBI With The Special Loss And Allocation Rules

UBIT is imposed on the gross unrelated business income less the deductions directly connected with the unrelated business, computed separately for each unrelated trade or business. Losses from one unrelated business can generally offset income from another unrelated business, but losses from an exempt activity cannot offset UBI, and an overall loss in unrelated activities cannot offset exempt income. The directly-connected deductions include expenses that have a proximate and primary relationship to the unrelated business, and shared costs (such as overhead and executive salaries) must be allocated on a reasonable basis between the unrelated business and the exempt activities.

Apply the dual-characterization and allocation rules carefully. An expense that benefits both an unrelated business and an exempt activity must be allocated based on a reasonable method (such as revenue, square footage, or time), and only the allocated portion is deductible against UBI. A loss from an unrelated business is deductible against UBI from other unrelated businesses, but if the total unrelated activities show a loss, the loss cannot reduce exempt income and is generally not carried forward (except in limited circumstances). The separate computation for each unrelated business and the allocation of shared costs are where most UBI computation errors occur, so document the allocation method and the per-activity net income.

### Apply The Debt-Financed Property And Control Rules

Income from rents and royalties is generally excluded from UBI, but the exclusion does not apply to unrelated debt-financed income under Section 514. If an exempt organization acquires property with debt, a portion of the income from the property (rents, dividends, or gains) is treated as unrelated debt-financed income, taxable in proportion to the debt-to-basis ratio. The debt-financed rules apply to real and personal property, and they can turn an otherwise excluded rental into taxable income. There are exceptions for properties used directly in the exempt purpose (such as a school using a debt-financed building for classrooms) and for certain small loans.

Compute the debt-financed percentage for any debt-financed property producing rents, royalties, or investment income. The debt-financed percentage is the average acquisition indebtedness divided by the average adjusted basis, and that percentage of the gross income (less the directly connected expenses) is UBI. Also apply the controlled-entity rules under Section 512(b)(13), which treat certain payments from a controlled entity (such as a taxable subsidiary) to the exempt organization as UBI if the payment reduces the payor's taxable income. The debt-financed and control rules are technical and frequently missed, so any exempt organization with debt-financed property or a controlled subsidiary must review its UBI position carefully.

### Satisfy Filing And Estimated-Tax Obligations

An exempt organization with gross unrelated business income of USD 1,000 or more must file Form 990-T and pay UBIT on the net income. The organization must also make estimated tax payments if it expects to owe USD 500 or more in UBIT for the year, using the estimated-tax safe harbor (the lesser of the prior year's tax or 90 percent of the current year's tax). The filing deadline is generally the 15th day of the fifth month after the organization's year-end (April 15 for a calendar-year organization), and a failure to file or pay triggers penalties and interest.

Confirm the filing and estimated-tax obligations for each year. An organization with multiple unrelated activities must aggregate them on a single Form 990-T, with separate schedules for each activity. Track the estimated-tax payments against the safe harbor, because UBIT penalties accumulate and can exceed the tax for an organization that ignores the obligation. The Form 990-T is a public document for some organizations (it was made public for 501(c)(3) organizations under recent law), so the accuracy and the disclosure of activities matter for reputation as well as tax.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Revenue As Exempt Because The Organization Is Exempt

The symptom is an exempt organization that does not file Form 990-T because it assumes exemption covers all income. The trap is that unrelated business income is taxable, so the failure to file and pay triggers UBIT plus penalties and interest. The direction is to apply the three-part UBI test to each revenue-generating activity and to file Form 990-T where gross UBI is USD 1,000 or more.

### Treating An Activity As Related Because The Profits Fund The Exempt Purpose

The symptom is an organization that treats a commercial activity as related because the profits support the charity. The trap is that the use of profits is irrelevant to the substantial-relatedness test, which measures the intrinsic connection between the activity and the exempt purpose, so the commercial activity is UBI. The direction is to evaluate the intrinsic connection and to treat unrelated activities as UBI regardless of how the profits are used.

### Misapplying The Volunteer, Convenience, Or Donated-Goods Exception

The symptom is an organization that excludes an activity under an exception without meeting the conditions. The trap is that each exception has specific conditions (85 percent volunteer labor, convenience of a served population, substantially all donated goods), and missing a condition makes the exclusion invalid. The direction is to confirm the conditions for each exception before excluding the income.

### Offsetting UBI With Losses From Exempt Activities

The symptom is an organization that nets a profitable unrelated business against a loss-making exempt activity. The trap is that losses from exempt activities cannot offset UBI, so the offset is invalid and the full UBI is taxable. The direction is to compute UBI separately and to offset only with losses from other unrelated businesses.

### Forgetting Unrelated Debt-Financed Income

The symptom is an exempt organization that excludes rental income from a debt-financed property. The trap is that the debt-financed rules under Section 514 make a proportion of the rental income taxable as unrelated debt-financed income, so the full exclusion overstates the benefit. The direction is to compute the debt-financed percentage and to report the taxable portion.

### Missing The Form 990-T Filing Or Estimated-Tax Payment

The symptom is an exempt organization that owes UBIT but has not filed Form 990-T or made estimated payments. The trap is that the USD 1,000 gross-income filing threshold and the USD 500 estimated-tax threshold are easy to miss, and the failure triggers penalties that compound. The direction is to monitor gross UBI against the thresholds and to file and pay on time.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the three-part UBI test (trade or business, regularly carried on, not substantially related) been applied to each revenue-generating activity?
- [ ] Has substantial relatedness been measured by the intrinsic contribution to the exempt purpose rather than by the use of profits?
- [ ] Has each statutory exception (volunteer labor, convenience, donated goods, and the investment-income exclusions) been confirmed against its specific conditions before excluding income?
- [ ] Has sponsorship income been distinguished from advertising income under the Section 513(i) safe harbor?
- [ ] Has net UBI been computed separately for each unrelated business, with shared costs allocated on a reasonable basis and exempt-activity losses excluded from the offset?
- [ ] Has unrelated debt-financed income been computed for any debt-financed property producing rents, royalties, or investment income, with the debt-financed percentage applied?
- [ ] Has the controlled-entity rule under Section 512(b)(13) been considered for payments from controlled taxable subsidiaries?
- [ ] Has Form 990-T been filed where gross UBI is USD 1,000 or more, with estimated-tax payments made where the expected UBIT is USD 500 or more?
