# Private Inurement and Excess Benefit

Tax exemption for a 501(c)(3) organization rests on the promise that the organization's assets and income will be devoted to the public benefit, not to the private benefit of insiders. The private-inurement doctrine, rooted in the operational test, prohibits an exempt organization's net earnings from inuring to the benefit of any private shareholder or individual, and repeated or substantial inurement can revoke exemption. The excess-benefit transaction rules under Section 4958 impose intermediate sanctions, an excise tax of 25 percent on the excess benefit paid to a disqualified person and 10 percent on the organization manager who knowingly approved it, with an additional 200 percent tax if the excess is not corrected. The judgment problem is that compensation, contracts, leases, and sales between an exempt organization and its insiders are common and often legitimate, but the line between reasonable compensation and an excess benefit turns on comparability data and a documented process, and the consequences of crossing the line are severe. An agent who evaluates insider transactions only by whether they seem fair, without applying the rebuttable-presumption process and the comparability standards, will expose the insiders and managers to excise taxes and the organization to revocation.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the excise tax under Section 4958 applies to excess-benefit transactions, defined as transactions in which an economic benefit is provided directly or indirectly to a disqualified person if the value exceeds the consideration received, and the standard is whether the arrangement is reasonable, not merely whether it is arm's-length in form. Second, the rebuttable presumption of reasonableness is a process, not a conclusion: it requires approval by an authorized, independent body, reliance on appropriate comparability data, and adequate contemporaneous documentation, and missing any element loses the presumption. Third, the disqualified-person category is broad, including substantial contributors, organization managers (officers, directors, trustees), certain family members, and 35-percent-controlled entities, and the look-back for status extends five years. Fourth, revenue-sharing and percentage-compensation arrangements are particularly suspect, because they can produce compensation that bears no relationship to the value of services, and the safe harbors are narrow.

This skill covers private inurement and excess-benefit transactions under U.S. federal intermediate sanctions rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Identify Disqualified Persons Before Evaluating Transactions

A disqualified person for Section 4958 purposes is anyone who was, at any point during the five-year period ending on the date of the transaction, in a position to exercise substantial influence over the organization's affairs. This includes organization managers (officers, directors, trustees, and persons with similar policy-making roles), substantial contributors (those who have given cumulatively USD 5,000 or more if that exceeds 2 percent of total contributions), certain family members of disqualified persons (spouses, ancestors, children, grandchildren, and spouses of children and grandchildren), and entities (corporations, partnerships, trusts, estates) in which disqualified persons hold more than 35 percent of the ownership. The five-year look-back means a former director or officer remains a disqualified person for five years after leaving the position.

Identify all disqualified persons before evaluating any insider transaction. The category is broader than the board of directors; it includes founders, substantial donors, key employees, and their related entities. A transaction with a person who does not appear to be an insider may still be a disqualified-person transaction if the person exercised substantial influence or is a family member or controlled entity of someone who did. Map the disqualified-person relationships at the outset, because the excise tax applies only to transactions with disqualified persons, and missing a relationship leads to an unanalyzed excess-benefit transaction.

### Apply The Rebuttable Presumption Of Reasonableness Process

The rebuttable presumption of reasonableness is a safe-harbor process that, if followed, shifts the burden to the IRS to prove a transaction is an excess benefit. To obtain the presumption for a compensation arrangement or property transfer, the transaction must be approved in advance by an authorized body of the organization (typically the board or a committee) that is composed entirely of individuals without a conflict of interest, that relied on appropriate comparability data before making its decision, and that adequately documented the basis for its decision concurrently with the decision. The comparability data must include information about compensation paid by similarly situated organizations, the value of services, or the fair market value of property, and the documentation must be in the form of an approved resolution or minutes that show the data relied upon and the terms approved.

Apply the full process for every insider transaction, not just the largest ones. The independent body must have no conflict, which means the disqualified person cannot participate in or be present during the deliberations or vote. The comparability data must be appropriate, which for compensation means data on similar positions at comparable organizations (size, field, geography), and for property means independent appraisals or comparable sales. The documentation must be contemporaneous, recorded at the time of approval, not reconstructed later. Missing any element loses the presumption and shifts the burden of proof to the organization, making it far harder to defend the transaction on examination.

### Measure Reasonable Compensation Against Comparability Data

For compensation arrangements, an excess benefit occurs when the aggregate economic benefit exceeds the value of the services rendered. Reasonable compensation is measured by what a similarly situated organization would pay for the same services, considering the organization's size, budget, geographic location, and the scope of the role. Comparability data may include compensation surveys, published salary studies, data from comparable organizations, and written offers from competing employers. The initial-contract exception provides that a fixed-payment arrangement in an initial employment contract is not subject to the excess-benefit rules, but this exception is narrow and does not apply to renewals or contingent arrangements.

Gather and document the comparability data before approving compensation. A single data point is generally insufficient; the IRS expects multiple sources reflecting comparable organizations. Be cautious with revenue-sharing and percentage-compensation arrangements, because compensation that is a percentage of revenue can produce amounts that bear no relationship to the value of services, and the IRS scrutinizes these arrangements closely. If a percentage arrangement is used, cap it or benchmark the resulting amount against comparable fixed compensation, and document why the percentage is reasonable. A founder or long-tenured executive whose compensation has grown with the organization's revenue without comparability support is a prime candidate for an excess-benefit finding.

### Evaluate Non-Compensation Transactions For Fair Market Value

Excess-benefit rules apply to any transaction in which a disqualified person receives an economic benefit, not just compensation. Sales, exchanges, leases, loans, and service contracts between the organization and a disqualified person must provide the organization with consideration at least equal to the value of the benefit provided. A sale of organization property to an insider below fair market value, a lease of insider property to the organization above market rent, or a loan to an insider on below-market terms are all potential excess-benefit transactions. The standard is fair market value, established by independent appraisal or comparable transactions.

Obtain independent valuation for any non-cash insider transaction. For real property, a qualified appraisal is the strongest evidence. For service contracts, compare to market rates for similar services. For loans, the organization should generally avoid lending to insiders, and any loan must bear at least the applicable federal rate and be adequately secured. Document the fair-market-value determination contemporaneously, because a transaction challenged years later is far harder to defend without contemporaneous valuation. Also be alert to revenue-sharing and reversionary interests, where the insider receives a stream of payments that may exceed the value provided.

### Recognize The Correction And Excise-Tax Structure

If a transaction is determined to be an excess benefit, the disqualified person must correct it by undoing the excess, typically by returning the excess amount to the organization or by forgoing future benefits. The initial excise tax is 25 percent of the excess benefit, imposed on the disqualified person. If the excess is not corrected within the applicable period, an additional excise tax of 200 percent of the excess benefit is imposed on the disqualified person. A separate 10 percent excise tax is imposed on each organization manager who knowingly approved the excess-benefit transaction, up to a maximum of USD 10,000 per transaction. Knowing approval requires actual knowledge of sufficient facts to make a reasonable person aware, and a manager who participated in the rebuttable-presumption process in good faith has a strong defense.

Understand the correction and tax structure so the consequences are clear. The 25 percent tax is on the disqualified person, the 10 percent is on the manager, and the 200 percent is the penalty for failing to correct. Correction requires restoring the organization to its financial position had the excess not occurred, which may mean repaying cash, returning property, or forgoing future compensation. An organization that discovers a potential excess benefit should correct it promptly, because correction before IRS contact avoids the 200 percent tax and may mitigate the 25 percent. The manager tax incentivizes directors to follow the process, so the rebuttable-presumption process is the primary protection.

### Distinguish Inurement Revocation From Intermediate Sanctions

Private inurement and excess benefits are related but distinct concepts. Private inurement is an operational-test violation that can revoke exemption, and it applies to any net earnings inuring to a private individual, whether or not the individual is a disqualified person. Historically, the only sanction for inurement was revocation, which was severe and infrequent, so Congress enacted the intermediate sanctions under Section 4958 to impose a measured tax on excess-benefit transactions with disqualified persons without revoking exemption. In practice, the IRS uses intermediate sanctions for most insider transactions, but revocation remains available for repeated, substantial, or egregious inurement that goes beyond a compensable excess benefit.

Recognize when a transaction risks revocation rather than just excise taxes. A single excess-benefit transaction that is corrected generally results in excise taxes, not revocation. But a pattern of insider enrichment, an organization operated primarily for the benefit of its founders, or a transaction so egregious that it indicates the organization is not operated for exempt purposes can trigger revocation. The distinction matters for advising the organization, because revocation is existential while excise taxes are remediable. An organization with a pattern of insider transactions should restructure its governance and compensation to demonstrate that it is operated for public benefit, not just correct individual transactions.

## Common Traps

### Skipping The Rebuttable-Presumption Process For Smaller Transactions

The symptom is a board that approves insider compensation without independent comparability data or contemporaneous documentation. The trap is that the presumption is lost and the burden shifts to the organization to prove reasonableness, which is far harder on examination. The direction is to apply the full process to every insider transaction, regardless of size.

### Using Revenue-Sharing Compensation Without A Cap Or Benchmark

The symptom is an executive whose compensation is a percentage of revenue and has grown without comparability support. The trap is that percentage compensation can produce amounts unrelated to the value of services, and the IRS treats uncapped revenue sharing as a likely excess benefit. The direction is to cap or benchmark percentage arrangements against comparable fixed compensation and to document the reasonableness.

### Overlooking Family And Entity Relationships In The Disqualified-Person Analysis

The symptom is a transaction with a vendor who is a family member or controlled entity of a director. The trap is that the family member or 35-percent-controlled entity is a disqualified person, so the transaction is subject to the excess-benefit rules even though the vendor is not a director. The direction is to map all family and entity relationships before evaluating insider transactions.

### Selling Organization Property To An Insider Without An Independent Appraisal

The symptom is a board that approves a sale of organization property to a founder at a price set by the board. The trap is that without an independent appraisal, the price may be below fair market value and the transaction is an excess benefit, triggering excise taxes and correction. The direction is to obtain an independent appraisal for any non-cash insider transaction and to document the fair-market-value determination.

### Treating A Loan To An Insider As Acceptable Because It Bears Some Interest

The symptom is an organization that lends to a founder at a below-market rate. The trap is that a below-market loan is an excess benefit measured by the difference between the market rate and the actual rate, and loans to insiders are heavily scrutinized. The direction is to avoid insider loans or to set the rate at the applicable federal rate with adequate security.

### Assuming Intermediate Sanctions Replace Revocation Risk

The symptom is an organization that treats excess-benefit excise taxes as the only risk. The trap is that repeated, substantial, or egregious inurement can revoke exemption, so a pattern of insider enrichment is existential, not just remediable. The direction is to recognize when the pattern risks revocation and to restructure governance and compensation to demonstrate public-benefit operation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have all disqualified persons been identified, including organization managers, substantial contributors, family members, and 35-percent-controlled entities, with the five-year look-back applied?
- [ ] Has the rebuttable-presumption-of-reasonableness process been applied to each insider transaction, with independent approval, appropriate comparability data, and contemporaneous documentation?
- [ ] Has compensation been measured against multiple comparability sources reflecting similar organizations, with revenue-sharing arrangements capped or benchmarked?
- [ ] Has each non-compensation insider transaction (sale, lease, loan, service contract) been evaluated for fair market value using independent appraisal or comparable data?
- [ ] Have loans to insiders been avoided or structured at the applicable federal rate with adequate security?
- [ ] Has the correction and excise-tax structure (25 percent on the disqualified person, 10 percent on the manager, 200 percent for failure to correct) been understood so that prompt correction is pursued on discovery?
- [ ] Has the distinction between remediable excess-benefit excise taxes and existential inurement revocation been recognized, with governance restructured where a pattern of insider enrichment exists?
- [ ] Has the organization been advised that the rebuttable presumption is a process, not a conclusion, and that losing it shifts the burden to the organization?
