# Residency and Domicile Test

State residency for income tax purposes is determined by two distinct tests — domicile and statutory residency — and a taxpayer can be a resident of more than one state (a dual resident) if they are domiciled in one state and a statutory resident of another. The judgment problem is that domicile is a subjective, fact-intensive determination based on the taxpayer's intent to remain indefinitely, while statutory residency is an objective, day-count test, and a misapplication of either test can lead to double taxation (where two states tax the same income) or an unexpected residency audit. An agent who conflates domicile with statutory residency, or who overlooks the day-count or the domicile factors, misclassifies the taxpayer and exposes them to back taxes, penalties, and interest in the state they left (or the state they moved to). The residency determination also drives the sourcing of income, the availability of credits, and the filing obligations (a resident files a resident return, a nonresident files a nonresident return, and a part-year resident files both).

Agents commonly miss several things. First, domicile is not abandoned by simply moving; the taxpayer must demonstrate a clear intent to change domicile, and the former state may challenge the change. Second, the statutory residency 183-day test counts any part of a day as a full day (in most states), and the taxpayer must maintain a day count to defend their position. Third, a domicile change requires the abandonment of the former domicile and the establishment of a new domicile, and the factors (the home, the family, the business, the time, and the items near and dear) are weighed together. Fourth, a part-year resident is a resident for the portion of the year they were domiciled or a statutory resident, and a nonresident for the rest, with the income sourced accordingly. Fifth, the "convenience of the employer" rule (in states like New York and Connecticut) can source employment income to the resident state even if the employee works remotely in another state. Sixth, the residency audit triggers (a change of address, the sale of a home, the retention of a home in the former state) are well-known to the state tax authorities, and the taxpayer should be prepared to defend the residency.

This skill covers the residency and domicile tests for state income tax purposes. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent (each state has its own rules) and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Domicile Test Based On Intent And Factors

Domicile is the place that the taxpayer treats as their permanent home, where they intend to remain indefinitely. Every person has exactly one domicile at a time, and a new domicile is established only by abandoning the former domicile and establishing a new one. The domicile is determined by the taxpayer's subjective intent, but the intent is inferred from the objective factors. The primary factors (which carry more weight) are the home (where the taxpayer maintains their primary residence), the active business or employment (where the taxpayer's primary business or employment is located), and the family (where the taxpayer's immediate family lives). The secondary factors (which carry less weight) include the time spent in each state, the items near and dear (such as the heirlooms, the pets, and the personal effects), the location of the bank accounts and the safe deposit box, the voter registration, the driver's license, the vehicle registration, and the club and religious affiliations.

Apply the domicile test based on the factors. Evaluate the primary factors (the home, the business, the family) and the secondary factors (the time, the items near and dear, the registrations, the affiliations). The home is the most important factor: a taxpayer who sells their home in the former state and buys a home in the new state has a strong domicile change, while a taxpayer who retains a home in the former state and rents in the new state has a weaker change. The factors are weighed together, and no single factor is dispositive, but the cumulative pattern should demonstrate the intent to remain indefinitely in the new state. The agent should document the factors (the home purchase, the business relocation, the family move, the registrations, the affiliations), because the documentation is the defense in a residency audit. The domicile change is a major event, and the taxpayer should execute it comprehensively (not just on paper) to defend against the former state's challenge.

### Apply The Statutory Residency 183-Day Test

A statutory resident is a person who is not domiciled in the state but who maintains a permanent place of abode in the state and spends more than 183 days of the taxable year in the state. The statutory residency is an objective test, and it is separate from the domicile. A taxpayer can be a statutory resident of a state where they are not domiciled (such as a person domiciled in Florida who maintains an apartment in New York and spends more than 183 days in New York). The 183-day test counts any part of a day as a full day (in most states), so a brief presence (a meeting, a dinner) counts as a day. The "permanent place of abode" is a residence that is suitable for year-round living (not a hotel or a transient accommodation), and it can be owned, rented, or leased.

Apply the statutory residency test. Determine whether the taxpayer maintains a permanent place of abode in the state (a residence suitable for year-round living), and count the days spent in the state. If the count exceeds 183 days, the taxpayer is a statutory resident. The day count should be maintained contemporaneously (a calendar or a log of the days), because a reconstructed count is less persuasive. The "permanent place of abode" is broadly defined, so a rented apartment, a condo, or a house counts, but a hotel or a transient accommodation does not. The taxpayer should be aware that any part of a day counts, so frequent visits (for business or personal reasons) can add up. The statutory residency is a common trap for a taxpayer who maintains a residence in a high-tax state (such as New York or California) while claiming domicile in a no-tax state (such as Florida or Texas), so the day count and the abode should be monitored.

### Evaluate Part-Year Residency And The Transition

A part-year resident is a taxpayer who changed their domicile during the year (or who became a statutory resident during the year), and they are a resident for the portion of the year they were domiciled or a statutory resident, and a nonresident for the rest. The part-year resident files a part-year resident return (which taxes the income from all sources during the resident period) and a nonresident return (which taxes the income from the in-state sources during the nonresident period). The transition date is the date of the domicile change (or the date the statutory residency began), and the income is sourced based on the transition date.

Evaluate the part-year residency. Determine the transition date (the date of the domicile change or the start of the statutory residency), and source the income based on the transition date. The income earned before the transition date is sourced to the former resident state (all sources for a resident), and the income earned after the transition date is sourced to the new state (all sources for a resident) and to the former state (in-state sources for a nonresident). The part-year resident return and the nonresident return must be coordinated, because the same income may be taxed by both states (with a credit for the taxes paid to the other state). The transition is a complex filing, and the agent should compute the income, the deductions, and the credits for each period carefully, because the sourcing and the credits depend on the transition date.

### Address The Risk Of Dual Residency And Double Taxation

A dual resident is a taxpayer who is domiciled in one state and a statutory resident of another, and they may be subject to double taxation (where both states tax the same income). The resident state (the domicile) taxes the worldwide income, and the statutory resident state also taxes the worldwide income, so the same income is taxed twice. The double taxation is mitigated by the credit for taxes paid to other states (which the domicile state may allow), but the credit may not fully eliminate the double taxation (because the credit is limited to the tax that the domicile state would impose on the income). The dual residency is a common situation for a taxpayer who moves between states without fully abandoning the former domicile.

Address the risk of the dual residency. Determine whether the taxpayer is a dual resident (domiciled in one state and a statutory resident of another), and compute the tax for each state. The domicile state taxes the worldwide income, and the statutory resident state also taxes the worldwide income. Apply the credit for the taxes paid to the other state (in the domicile state), to mitigate the double taxation. The credit may not fully eliminate the double taxation, because the credit is limited, so the taxpayer may still owe tax to both states. The dual residency should be avoided if possible (by abandoning the former domicile or by reducing the days in the statutory resident state), because the double taxation is costly. The agent should advise the taxpayer of the risk and the mitigation, and the taxpayer should consider restructuring their residency (such as selling the home in the former state or reducing the days in the statutory resident state).

### Apply The Convenience Of The Employer Rule

The "convenience of the employer" rule (in states like New York, Connecticut, Arkansas, Delaware, Nebraska, and Pennsylvania) sources the employment income to the resident state (the state where the employer is located) even if the employee works remotely in another state, unless the remote work is for the necessity of the employer (not the convenience of the employee). The rule applies to a resident of the state who works remotely for an in-state employer, and it can result in the income being taxed by the resident state (where the employer is located) and by the state where the employee works remotely (if it is a different state). The rule is a significant trap for a remote employee who moved out of the state but continues to work for the same employer.

Apply the convenience of the employer rule. Determine whether the state (where the employer is located) applies the rule, and whether the remote work is for the necessity of the employer (not the convenience of the employee). If the rule applies and the remote work is for the employee's convenience, the employment income is sourced to the resident state (where the employer is located), even if the employee works in another state. The employee may also owe tax to the state where they work remotely (if it taxes the income), resulting in double taxation (mitigated by the credit). The rule is a major trap for a remote employee, so the agent should evaluate the rule and advise the employee of the sourcing. The necessity exception is narrow (it requires that the employer requires the remote work, not just that the employee prefers it), so the employee should document the employer's requirement if they claim the exception.

### Document The Residency For An Audit

The residency determination is frequently audited by the state tax authorities, especially when a taxpayer moves from a high-tax state to a no-tax state. The documentation should include the domicile factors (the home purchase or sale, the business relocation, the family move, the registrations, the affiliations) and the day count (for the statutory residency). The documentation should be contemporaneous, because reconstructed records are less persuasive. The state may request the credit card statements, the cell phone records, the E-ZPass records, and the travel records to verify the day count, so the taxpayer should be prepared.

Document the residency for an audit. Maintain the contemporaneous documentation of the domicile change (the home purchase, the business relocation, the family move, the new registrations, the new affiliations) and the day count (a calendar or a log of the days in each state). Preserve the records that the state may request (the credit card statements, the cell phone records, the E-ZPass records, the travel records), because these records verify the day count. The documentation is the defense in a residency audit, and the burden of proof is on the taxpayer (in most states), so the documentation should be thorough. The agent should help the taxpayer assemble the documentation each year, especially in the year of the move, because the residency audit is most likely in the year of the move.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Domicile With Statutory Residency

The symptom is an agent who treats the domicile and the statutory residency as the same test. The trap is that they are separate tests, and a taxpayer can be a statutory resident of a state where they are not domiciled, so the conflation misclassifies the taxpayer. The direction is to apply each test separately and to evaluate the dual residency risk.

### Counting Days Without A Contemporaneous Log

The symptom is a taxpayer who estimates the day count without a contemporaneous log. The trap is that the state audits the day count with the credit card and the cell phone records, so the estimate is challenged. The direction is to maintain a contemporaneous day count and to preserve the supporting records.

### Claiming A Domicile Change Without Abandoning The Former Home

The symptom is a taxpayer who buys a home in a new state but retains the home in the former state. The trap is that the retention of the former home is strong evidence of the retained domicile, so the former state challenges the change. The direction is to abandon the former domicile (sell or rent the home) and to document the abandonment.

### Overlooking The Convenience Of The Employer Rule

The symptom is a remote employee who moved out of New York but continues to work for the same employer, and the agent does not consider the rule. The trap is that the rule sources the income to New York, so the employee owes New York tax. The direction is to apply the rule and to evaluate the necessity exception.

### Filing As A Nonresident While A Statutory Resident

The symptom is a taxpayer who files as a nonresident but who spent more than 183 days in the state with a permanent abode. The trap is that the taxpayer is a statutory resident, so the nonresident filing is incorrect. The direction is to apply the statutory residency test and to file as a resident if the test is met.

### Missing The Part-Year Filing Coordination

The symptom is a part-year resident who files only the resident return or the nonresident return, not both. The trap is that the part-year resident must file both returns, with the income sourced to each period, so the single filing is incomplete. The direction is to file the part-year resident return and the nonresident return, with the income sourced to each period.
