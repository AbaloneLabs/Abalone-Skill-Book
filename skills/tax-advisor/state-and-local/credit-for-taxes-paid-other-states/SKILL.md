# Credit for Taxes Paid Other States

The credit for taxes paid to other states is the primary mechanism to mitigate the double taxation that arises when a resident earns income in another state and both the resident state and the nonresident state tax the same income. The judgment problem is that the credit rules are deceptively complex: not all taxes qualify (only net income taxes, not franchise taxes or excise taxes), the credit is limited to the tax that the resident state would impose on the income (not the full amount paid to the other state), the sequencing matters when three or more states are involved (the credit is computed in a specific order), and the reciprocal agreements (between certain states) can exempt the income from the nonresident state's withholding entirely. An agent who claims a credit for a non-qualifying tax, or who exceeds the limitation, triggers a state adjustment and a potential penalty. The credit also interacts with the pass-through entity tax (PTET) credits, the alternative minimum tax, and the local taxes (which may or may not allow the credit).

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the credit is limited to the lesser of the tax paid to the other state or the tax that the resident state would impose on the same income, so a high-tax nonresident state does not always produce a full credit. Second, the credit is for the net income tax (the state income tax), not for the franchise taxes, the excise taxes, or the local taxes (unless the resident state allows the local tax credit). Third, the sequencing matters when the resident earns income in three or more states, because the credit is computed in a specific order (often by the highest-tax state first), and the order affects the total credit. Fourth, the reciprocal agreements (between states like New Jersey and Pennsylvania, or Maryland and Virginia) can exempt the resident's wage income from the nonresident state's withholding, so the credit is not needed (because the other state does not tax the income). Fifth, the PTET credits (for the pass-through entity taxes paid at the entity level) are separate from the traditional credit, and the rules vary by state. Sixth, the credit is generally not available for the income sourced to a foreign country (the foreign tax credit is a federal mechanism, and the states treat it differently).

This skill covers the credit for taxes paid to other states under U.S. state tax rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent (each state has its own rules) and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Confirm That The Tax Qualifies For The Credit

The credit for taxes paid to other states is generally available for the net income taxes (the state income taxes) imposed by the other state on the income that is also taxed by the resident state. The credit is not available for the franchise taxes, the excise taxes, the gross receipts taxes, the license taxes, or the local taxes (unless the resident state specifically allows the local tax credit). The credit is also not available for the penalties or the interest paid to the other state, or for the taxes paid to a foreign country (the states treat the foreign taxes differently, and some states allow a credit while others do not). The qualifying tax is the net income tax, and the taxpayer must confirm that the tax paid to the other state is a net income tax.

Confirm that the tax qualifies. Review the tax paid to the other state, and determine whether it is a net income tax (the state income tax on the sourced income). If the tax is a franchise tax, an excise tax, a gross receipts tax, or a license tax, the credit is not available (unless the resident state specifically allows it). If the tax includes the penalties or the interest, the credit is available only for the tax portion. If the tax is paid to a foreign country, confirm the resident state's rule (some states allow a credit, others do not). The qualifying tax is the net income tax, and the taxpayer should document the tax paid (the nonresident return and the payment records), because the documentation is the basis for the credit. The agent should not assume that all taxes paid to the other state qualify, because the non-qualifying taxes (the franchise and the excise taxes) are a common trap.

### Apply The Limitation Based On The Resident State's Tax

The credit is limited to the lesser of the tax paid to the other state or the tax that the resident state would impose on the same income. The limitation prevents the credit from exceeding the resident state's tax on the income, so a high-tax nonresident state does not always produce a full credit. For example, if the taxpayer paid USD 5,000 to the nonresident state on USD 50,000 of income, and the resident state's tax on USD 50,000 is USD 4,000, the credit is USD 4,000 (the lesser amount). The limitation is computed on a per-income, per-state basis, so the credit for each item of income and each state is computed separately.

Apply the limitation. Compute the tax paid to the other state on the sourced income, and compute the tax that the resident state would impose on the same income (the resident state's tax rate times the income). The credit is the lesser of the two amounts. The limitation is computed on a per-income, per-state basis, so the agent should compute the credit for each item of income and each state. The limitation means that a high-tax nonresident state (such as California or New York) may produce a credit that is less than the full amount paid, because the resident state's tax rate may be lower. The limitation is a common trap, because the taxpayer may expect a full credit, but the credit is capped at the resident state's tax on the income. The agent should compute the limitation carefully, because the excess (the amount paid above the limitation) is not creditable and not refundable.

### Sequence The Credits For Three Or More States

When the resident earns income in three or more states, the credit is computed in a specific order, which affects the total credit. Some states require the credit to be computed by the highest-tax state first (to maximize the credit), while others require a pro-rata computation. The sequencing matters because the limitation (the resident state's tax on the income) is applied to each state's income, and the order can affect the total credit. The agent should confirm the resident state's sequencing rule and compute the credits in the correct order.

Sequence the credits for the three or more states. Determine the resident state's sequencing rule (the highest-tax first, the pro-rata, or another method), and compute the credits in the correct order. For the highest-tax first, compute the credit for the state with the highest tax (up to the limitation), then the next highest, and so on. For the pro-rata, compute the credit proportionally across the states. The sequencing affects the total credit, because the limitation is applied to each state's income, and the order can change the result. The agent should document the sequencing, because the state may audit the order. The three-or-more-state situation is complex, and the agent should use a worksheet or a software tool to compute the credits correctly, because a manual computation is error-prone.

### Evaluate The Reciprocal Agreements

Some states have reciprocal agreements that exempt the resident's wage income from the nonresident state's withholding (and taxation), so the credit is not needed (because the other state does not tax the income). The reciprocal agreements are typically between neighboring states (such as New Jersey and Pennsylvania, Maryland and Virginia, Ohio and Indiana, and Illinois and Iowa), and they apply to the wage income (the compensation), not to the business income or the investment income. Under a reciprocal agreement, the resident of one state who works in the other state is taxed only by the resident state, and the nonresident state does not withhold (provided the employee files the exemption certificate).

Evaluate the reciprocal agreements. Determine whether the resident state and the nonresident state have a reciprocal agreement, and whether the income is the wage income (the compensation). If the agreement applies, the employee should file the exemption certificate with the employer (to stop the nonresident withholding), and the employee is taxed only by the resident state (no credit is needed, because the nonresident state does not tax the income). If the nonresident state withheld despite the agreement, the employee should file a nonresident return (to claim a refund of the withheld tax). The reciprocal agreements are a valuable relief, because they eliminate the double taxation and the need for the credit, but they apply only to the wage income and only between the specific states. The agent should confirm the agreement and the income type, and the employee should file the exemption certificate to avoid the unnecessary withholding.

### Address The PTET Credits And The Entity-Level Taxes

The pass-through entity tax (PTET) credits are separate from the traditional credit for taxes paid to other states. When the pass-through entity elects the PTET, the entity pays the state tax at the entity level, and the owners receive a credit (or a deduction) on their resident state return. The PTET credit rules vary by state: some states allow a full credit for the PTET paid to the other state, others allow a partial credit, and some do not allow a credit (treating the PTET as a business expense). The PTET credits must be coordinated with the traditional credits, because the same income may be subject to both.

Address the PTET credits and the entity-level taxes. Determine whether the pass-through entity elected the PTET in the other state, and whether the owner's resident state allows a credit for the PTET. If the resident state allows the credit, compute the PTET credit (based on the owner's share of the entity's PTET), and coordinate it with the traditional credit (for any other income sourced to the other state). The PTET credit rules are evolving, because the PTET regimes are recent, and the states are still developing their credit rules. The agent should confirm the resident state's current PTET credit rule, and the owner should document the PTET paid (the entity's return and the owner's K-1), because the documentation is the basis for the credit. The PTET credits are a specialized area, and the agent should coordinate the entity-level and the owner-level filings carefully.

### Coordinate With The Federal SALT Deduction And The AMT

The credit for taxes paid to other states interacts with the federal SALT deduction (the deduction for the state and local taxes, capped at USD 10,000 for individuals through 2025) and the alternative minimum tax (AMT). The credit is a state mechanism (it reduces the resident state's tax), and it does not affect the federal SALT deduction directly, but the total state and local taxes paid (to all states) are aggregated for the SALT deduction. The AMT (for individuals) adds back the state and local taxes, so the credit does not directly affect the AMT, but the total state taxes are a preference item. The coordination is at the federal level, and the agent should be aware of the interaction.

The credit reduces the resident state's tax, but the total state and local taxes paid (to all states, including the nonresident states) are aggregated for the federal SALT deduction (capped at USD 10,000 for individuals through 2025). The AMT (for individuals) adds back the state and local taxes, so the total state taxes are a preference item. The agent should compute the total state and local taxes paid (across all states), and apply the SALT cap for the federal deduction. The credit does not directly affect the federal SALT deduction or the AMT, but the total state taxes are relevant for both, so the agent should coordinate the state credits with the federal computation. The SALT cap (and the PTET, which is deductible without the cap) is a major consideration, and the agent should evaluate the PTET election as a workaround for the cap.

## Common Traps

### Claiming A Credit For A Non-Qualifying Tax

The symptom is a taxpayer who claims a credit for a franchise tax or an excise tax paid to the other state. The trap is that the credit is for the net income tax only, so the non-qualifying tax is disallowed. The direction is to confirm that the tax is a net income tax and to claim the credit only for the qualifying tax.

### Exceeding The Limitation

The symptom is a taxpayer who claims a credit for the full USD 5,000 paid to a high-tax state, when the resident state's tax on the income is USD 3,000. The trap is that the credit is limited to the lesser amount (USD 3,000), so the excess is disallowed. The direction is to compute the limitation (the lesser of the tax paid or the resident state's tax on the income) and to claim the credit accordingly.

### Sequencing The Credits Incorrectly

The symptom is a taxpayer with income in three states who computes the credits in the wrong order. The trap is that the sequencing affects the total credit, so the wrong order understates the credit. The direction is to confirm the resident state's sequencing rule and to compute the credits in the correct order.

### Overlooking A Reciprocal Agreement

The symptom is a New Jersey resident who works in Pennsylvania and pays the Pennsylvania tax, then claims the credit on the New Jersey return. The trap is that the reciprocal agreement exempts the wage income from the Pennsylvania tax, so the employee should file the exemption certificate instead of claiming the credit. The direction is to evaluate the reciprocal agreement and to file the exemption certificate.

### Double-Counting The PTET Credit

The symptom is an owner who claims both the PTET credit and the traditional credit for the same income. The trap is that the PTET and the traditional credit are for the same income, so the double-counting overstates the credit. The direction is to coordinate the PTET credit and the traditional credit, and to claim each for the appropriate income.

### Ignoring The SALT Cap Interaction

The symptom is a taxpayer who claims the credit on the state return but does not consider the federal SALT cap. The trap is that the total state and local taxes are capped at USD 10,000 for the federal deduction, so the state credit does not increase the federal deduction. The direction is to compute the total state and local taxes and to apply the SALT cap for the federal deduction.
