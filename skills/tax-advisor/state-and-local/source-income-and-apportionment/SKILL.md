# Source Income and Apportionment

The sourcing of income to a state determines what income a nonresident (or a part-year resident) is taxed on by that state, and it is one of the most error-prone areas of state taxation. The judgment problem is that the sourcing rules differ by the type of income (compensation, business income, investment income, pass-through income) and by the state, and a mis-sourcing can lead to double taxation (where two states tax the same income) or an under-reporting (where a state's income is omitted). For compensation, most states use a day-count fraction (the days worked in the state divided by the total days worked), but some states source the compensation to the employer's location. For business income, states use an apportionment formula (typically a three-factor formula of property, payroll, and sales, or a single-factor sales formula), and the throwback rule (and the throwout rule in some states) can source the sales of tangible property to the origin state if they are not taxed by the destination state. For pass-through income, the sourcing is done at the entity level (under the pass-through entity tax regimes) or at the owner level, depending on the state. An agent who applies the wrong sourcing rule, or who overlooks the throwback rule or the pass-through entity tax, produces an incorrect nonresident filing.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the compensation day-count fraction counts the working days (not the calendar days), and the non-working days (weekends, holidays, sick days) are generally allocated to the working state, but the rules vary by state. Second, the business income apportionment formula varies by state (some use a single-factor sales formula, others use a three-factor formula), and the sales factor may be weighted differently (such as double-weighted sales). Third, the throwback rule sources the sales of tangible property to the origin state if the destination state does not tax them, which can significantly increase the apportioned income. Fourth, the pass-through entity tax (PTET) regimes (adopted by many states after the SALT deduction cap) allow the pass-through entity to pay the state tax at the entity level, and the owners receive a credit, but the PTET is an election and the rules vary by state. Fifth, the investment income (interest, dividends, capital gains) is generally sourced to the taxpayer's domicile, but some states source it differently for a part-year resident. Sixth, the nonresident withholding and the estimated tax requirements may apply to the sourced income, and the taxpayer should be aware of the compliance.

This skill covers the sourcing of income and the apportionment for state income tax purposes. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent (each state has its own rules) and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Source Compensation Using The Day-Count Fraction

For a nonresident employee, most states source the compensation to the state based on the days worked in the state. The day-count fraction is the days worked in the state divided by the total days worked, and the compensation is multiplied by the fraction to determine the income sourced to the state. The working days are the days the employee actually performed services (not the weekends, the holidays, or the sick days, which are generally allocated to the working state, but the rules vary). The non-working days (such as the weekends and the holidays that are not tied to a specific work location) are generally allocated to the state where the employee was working before the non-working day, but the treatment varies by state. Some states (such as Pennsylvania) source the compensation to the employer's location, regardless of where the employee works.

Source the compensation using the day-count fraction. Determine the total working days (the days the employee performed services) and the working days in the state, and compute the fraction. Multiply the compensation by the fraction to determine the income sourced to the state. The non-working days (the weekends, the holidays, the sick days) are generally allocated to the working state, but the treatment varies, so the agent should confirm the state's rule. For a state that sources the compensation to the employer's location (such as Pennsylvania), the day count is not used, and the compensation is sourced to the employer's location. The day-count fraction should be documented (a calendar or a log of the working days), because the state may audit the fraction. The agent should compute the fraction carefully, because the sourcing determines the nonresident tax.

### Apply The Apportionment Formula For Business Income

For a nonresident business (a corporation or a pass-through entity with income from the state), the business income is sourced to the state through an apportionment formula. The traditional three-factor formula averages the property factor (the in-state property divided by the total property), the payroll factor (the in-state payroll divided by the total payroll), and the sales factor (the in-state sales divided by the total sales). Many states have shifted to a single-factor sales formula (using only the sales factor), and some states double-weight the sales factor. The sales factor for tangible property is generally sourced to the destination state (where the property is delivered), but the throwback rule (and the throwout rule) can source the sales to the origin state if the destination state does not tax them.

Apply the apportionment formula. Determine the state's formula (the three-factor, the single-factor sales, or the double-weighted sales), and compute the factors. The property factor is the average in-state property divided by the average total property, the payroll factor is the in-state payroll divided by the total payroll, and the sales factor is the in-state sales divided by the total sales. For the sales factor of tangible property, source the sales to the destination state (where the property is delivered), and apply the throwback rule (if the destination state does not tax the sales, source them to the origin state). The throwback rule can significantly increase the apportioned income, because the sales that are not taxed by the destination state are "thrown back" to the origin state. The agent should compute the apportionment carefully, because the formula and the throwback rule determine the nonresident business tax. The apportionment is complex, and the agent should confirm the state's formula and the throwback rule.

### Evaluate The Throwback And Throwout Rules

The throwback rule sources the sales of tangible property to the origin state if the destination state does not tax them (such as sales to a state where the business has no nexus, or sales to a foreign country). The throwout rule (in states like Maryland) excludes the sales from the denominator of the sales factor if the destination state does not tax them, which increases the sales factor and the apportioned income. Both rules prevent a business from reducing its apportioned income by selling to states where it has no nexus, and they can significantly increase the tax. The rules apply to the sales of tangible property, and the services and the intangibles are sourced differently (often to the destination or the market).

Evaluate the throwback and the throwout rules. For the sales of tangible property, determine whether the destination state taxes the sales (whether the business has nexus in the destination state). If the destination state does not tax the sales, apply the throwback rule (source the sales to the origin state) or the throwout rule (exclude the sales from the denominator), depending on the origin state's rule. The throwback and the throwout rules can significantly increase the apportioned income, so the agent should evaluate them carefully. For the services and the intangibles, confirm the state's sourcing rule (often the market-based sourcing, which sources the services to the state where the customer receives the benefit). The throwback and the throwout rules are common traps, because a business may assume the sales are not taxed by the origin state, while the rules source them back.

### Address The Pass-Through Entity Tax And The Owner-Level Sourcing

The pass-through entity tax (PTET) regimes (adopted by many states after the federal SALT deduction cap of USD 10,000) allow the pass-through entity to pay the state tax at the entity level (which is deductible for the federal tax, without the SALT cap), and the owners receive a credit. The PTET is an election, and the rules vary by state. For the sourcing, the PTET is generally computed at the entity level (using the apportionment formula), and the owners' credit is based on their share of the entity's income sourced to the state. For the non-PTET states, the pass-through income is sourced at the owner level (the owner's share of the entity's income is sourced based on the owner's residency and the entity's apportionment), and the owner files the nonresident return.

Address the PTET and the owner-level sourcing. Determine whether the state has a PTET regime and whether the entity elected it. If the PTET is elected, the entity pays the tax at the entity level (using the apportionment formula), and the owners receive a credit. The PTET is deductible for the federal tax (without the SALT cap), so it is a valuable election for the owners. If the PTET is not elected (or not available), the pass-through income is sourced at the owner level, and the owner files the nonresident return (based on their share of the entity's income sourced to the state). The agent should coordinate the PTET and the owner-level filings, because the double taxation or the gaps can arise if the coordination is not done. The PTET is a recent development, and the rules are evolving, so the agent should confirm the state's current PTET rules.

### Source The Investment Income For Part-Year Residents

The investment income (interest, dividends, capital gains) is generally sourced to the taxpayer's domicile, so a full-year resident's investment income is sourced to the resident state, and a full-year nonresident's investment income is not sourced to the nonresident state. For a part-year resident, the investment income is sourced based on the residency period: the income earned (or accrued) during the resident period is sourced to the resident state, and the income earned during the nonresident period is not sourced. Some states source the capital gains based on the transaction date, while others source them based on the residency period, and the rules vary.

Source the investment income for the part-year residents. Determine the residency periods (the resident period and the nonresident period), and source the investment income based on the state's rule. For the interest and the dividends, source the income based on the accrual or the payment date (depending on the state). For the capital gains, source the income based on the transaction date or the residency period (depending on the state). The investment income is generally sourced to the domicile for a full-year resident, and it is not sourced to the nonresident state for a full-year nonresident. The part-year resident sourcing is complex, because the same income may be sourced differently based on the timing, so the agent should confirm the state's rule and compute the sourcing carefully.

### Coordinate The Nonresident Filings And The Credits

A nonresident (or a part-year resident) who has income sourced to multiple states may face double taxation, which is mitigated by the credit for taxes paid to other states. The credit is generally allowed by the resident state (the domicile) for the tax paid to the nonresident state on the sourced income, but the credit is limited to the tax that the resident state would impose on the income. The coordination requires filing the nonresident returns (for each state with sourced income) and the resident return (with the credit), and the order of the filings (and the credit computations) must be correct.

Coordinate the nonresident filings and the credits. File the nonresident returns for each state with the sourced income, and compute the tax for each state. File the resident return (for the domicile) with the credit for the taxes paid to the other states, limited to the tax that the resident state would impose on the income. The credit may not fully eliminate the double taxation (because the credit is limited), so the taxpayer may still owe tax to both states. The coordination is essential, because a failure to file a nonresident return (or to claim the credit) results in the double taxation or the under-reporting. The agent should compute the sourced income, the nonresident taxes, and the resident credit carefully, and the taxpayer should be advised of the total tax exposure across the states.

## Common Traps

### Sourcing Compensation Without A Day-Count Log

The symptom is a nonresident employee who estimates the day count without a log. The trap is that the state audits the day count, so the estimate is challenged and the sourcing is adjusted. The direction is to maintain a contemporaneous day-count log and to document the working days.

### Using The Wrong Apportionment Formula

The symptom is a business that uses a three-factor formula in a single-factor sales state. The trap is that the formula determines the apportioned income, so the wrong formula produces an incorrect tax. The direction is to confirm the state's apportionment formula and to compute the factors accordingly.

### Overlooking The Throwback Rule

The symptom is a business that sells to states where it has no nexus and does not apply the throwback rule. The trap is that the throwback rule sources the sales to the origin state, so the omission understates the apportioned income. The direction is to evaluate the throwback rule and to source the sales accordingly.

### Missing The PTET Election

The symptom is a pass-through entity that does not elect the PTET and pays the state tax at the owner level (subject to the SALT cap). The trap is that the PTET is deductible without the SALT cap, so the non-election loses the federal benefit. The direction is to evaluate the PTET election and to coordinate the entity-level and the owner-level filings.

### Sourcing Investment Income To The Nonresident State

The symptom is a full-year nonresident who sources their investment income to the nonresident state. The trap is that the investment income is sourced to the domicile, so the nonresident sourcing is incorrect. The direction is to source the investment income to the domicile for a full-year nonresident.

### Failing To Claim The Credit For Taxes Paid To Other States

The symptom is a resident who pays tax to a nonresident state but does not claim the credit on the resident return. The trap is that the credit mitigates the double taxation, so the failure to claim it results in the double taxation. The direction is to file the nonresident returns and to claim the credit on the resident return.
