# Section 1245 Recapture

Section 1245 governs the depreciation recapture rules for personal property and certain other assets, including machinery, equipment, vehicles, furniture, fixtures, and intangible assets. Unlike Section 1250 real property (where recapture is capped at 25%), Section 1245 recapture is full and ordinary: the entire gain attributable to depreciation is recaptured as ordinary income, regardless of the holding period. This means a sale of equipment can produce ordinary income rather than capital gain, even when the asset was held for years. An agent who treats the sale of business equipment as a capital gain, ignoring Section 1245 recapture, will understate the tax and mischaracterize the income.

Agents commonly miss that Section 1245 applies to personal property (not real property), that the recapture is full and ordinary (not capped), that the recapture is limited to the gain (so a loss is not recaptured), and that the character of any excess gain depends on the facts. The harm is a seller who expects capital gain treatment and faces ordinary income rates on the full depreciation.

This skill covers Section 1245 depreciation recapture under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; recapture rules depend on the asset and depreciation history, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Identify Section 1245 Property

Section 1245 property includes depreciable personal property, certain other tangible personal property, and intangible assets subject to amortization. Common examples include machinery, equipment, vehicles, furniture, fixtures, computers, software, and certain intangible assets. Buildings and structural components are Section 1250 property, not Section 1245 property. However, certain building components that are inherently structural may be reclassified depending on the facts.

Confirm the asset is Section 1245 property (depreciable personal property or amortizable intangible, not real property). For a sale of business assets, classify each asset as Section 1245 (personal property), Section 1250 (real property), or non-depreciable (land, inventory). The classification determines which recapture rules apply. A sale of equipment and a sale of a building follow different recapture rules, and mixing them misstates the tax. Obtain the asset list and the depreciation schedule to classify each asset.

### Compute Full Ordinary Recapture To The Extent Of Gain

Section 1245 recapture requires that the gain on the sale be recognized as ordinary income to the extent of the depreciation claimed or allowable. Unlike Section 1250 (where straight-line depreciation is recaptured at up to 25%), Section 1245 recaptures the full depreciation as ordinary income, regardless of the depreciation method. The recapture is the lesser of the total depreciation or the total gain. Any gain in excess of the recapture amount is Section 1231 gain (potentially long-term capital gain if held more than one year).

Compute the total depreciation claimed or allowable on the asset. Compare to the total gain. The recapture (ordinary income) is the lesser amount. The remaining gain, if any, is Section 1231 gain. For example, equipment purchased for 100,000 dollars, depreciated by 60,000 dollars (adjusted basis 40,000 dollars), and sold for 90,000 dollars produces a 50,000 dollar gain, all of which is recaptured as ordinary income (because the depreciation of 60,000 dollars exceeds the gain of 50,000 dollars). If the same equipment sold for 150,000 dollars, the gain of 110,000 dollars consists of 60,000 dollars ordinary recapture (the depreciation) and 50,000 dollars Section 1231 capital gain. The full depreciation is recaptured, not capped.

### Apply The Recapture Limit At The Gain

The Section 1245 recapture is limited to the gain on the asset. If the asset is sold at a loss, there is no recapture; the loss is a Section 1231 loss (ordinary if the net Section 1231 result is a loss). If the asset is sold for a small gain that is less than the depreciation, only the gain is recaptured as ordinary income. The remaining depreciation does not create additional ordinary income beyond the gain. This protects the seller from recapturing more than the economic gain.

Compute the gain on the asset (sale price minus adjusted basis). The recapture is the lesser of the gain or the depreciation. If the sale produces a 5,000 dollar gain and the depreciation was 40,000 dollars, the recapture is 5,000 dollars (the gain) as ordinary income, and there is no Section 1231 capital gain. If the sale produces a loss, there is no recapture; the loss is Section 1231 (subject to netting). Apply the limit correctly to avoid overstating the ordinary recapture.

### Handle Section 1231 Gain In Excess Of Recapture

If the sale price exceeds the original cost (so the gain exceeds the total depreciation), the excess gain is Section 1231 gain. Section 1231 gain on property held more than one year is treated as long-term capital gain (favorable) if the net Section 1231 result for the year is a gain, or as ordinary loss if the net is a loss. The Section 1231 gain is netted with other Section 1231 gains and losses for the year.

Compute the Section 1231 gain as the total gain minus the Section 1245 recapture amount. Include this gain in the Section 1231 netting for the year (with other business property sales). The netting determines whether the Section 1231 gain is capital or ordinary. Do not treat the excess gain as capital without the Section 1231 netting; the character depends on the net result and the lookback rule. Analyze all Section 1231 transactions together.

### Apply Like-Kind Exchange And Other Nonrecognition Rules

Section 1245 recapture does not apply if the sale qualifies for nonrecognition treatment, such as a like-kind exchange under Section 1031 (for real property, though personal property like-kind exchanges are limited after the TCJA), an involuntary conversion, or a transfer to a controlled corporation. In these cases, the gain is deferred or nonrecognized, and the recapture is also deferred. However, the recapture potential carries over to the replacement property, to be recognized on a future taxable disposition.

Confirm whether the transaction qualifies for nonrecognition before applying recapture. If a like-kind exchange or other nonrecognition provision applies, the recapture is deferred along with the gain, and the replacement property takes a carryover basis with the recapture potential attached. The recapture is recognized when the replacement property is sold in a taxable transaction. Do not apply recapture to a nonrecognition transaction; track the deferred recapture for the future sale.

### Report Section 1245 Recapture On Form 4797

Section 1245 recapture is reported on Form 4797 Part III for each asset. The form computes the ordinary income recapture (the depreciation to the extent of gain) and the Section 1231 gain (the excess). The ordinary recapture flows to Form 4797 Part II as ordinary income. The Section 1231 gain flows to Form 4797 Part I for netting. The reporting must separately show the ordinary and capital portions.

Ensure each Section 1245 asset sale is reported on Form 4797 Part III with the recapture computed. The ordinary recapture is taxed at the taxpayer's ordinary income rate (up to 37%), which is significantly higher than the capital gains rate. The Section 1231 excess gain is taxed at the long-term capital gains rate (subject to netting). Do not report the entire gain on Schedule D as a capital gain; the recapture portion is ordinary income on Form 4797. Reconcile the reporting with the depreciation schedule and the asset list.

## Common Traps

### Treating Equipment Sale Gain As Capital Gain

Section 1245 recaptures the full depreciation as ordinary income. Only the excess gain is Section 1231 capital gain. Treating the full gain as capital understates the ordinary income and the tax.

### Confusing Section 1245 With Section 1250

Section 1245 applies to personal property (equipment, vehicles) with full ordinary recapture. Section 1250 applies to real property with recapture capped at 25%. Applying the wrong section misstates the recapture.

### Recapturing More Than The Gain

The recapture is limited to the gain. If the gain is less than the depreciation, only the gain is recaptured. Applying recapture to the full depreciation when the gain is smaller overstates ordinary income.

### Ignoring The Section 1231 Netting For The Excess Gain

The excess gain (above recapture) is Section 1231 gain, subject to netting with other Section 1231 transactions. Treating it as automatic capital gain without netting misstates the character.

### Applying Recapture To A Nonrecognition Transaction

Like-kind exchanges, involuntary conversions, and certain transfers defer the gain and the recapture. Applying recapture to a nonrecognition transaction overstates current ordinary income.

### Reporting The Gain On Schedule D Instead Of Form 4797

Section 1245 recapture is reported on Form 4797 Part III as ordinary income. Reporting on Schedule D as capital gain misses the ordinary recapture and understates tax.

### Forgetting Depreciation Allowed Or Allowable

The recapture is based on depreciation allowed or allowable, whether or not claimed. A taxpayer who failed to claim depreciation still faces recapture on the amount they should have claimed.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the asset been confirmed as Section 1245 property (depreciable personal property or amortizable intangible, not real property)?
- [ ] Has the ordinary recapture been computed as the lesser of total depreciation (allowed or allowable) or total gain, recognizing the full depreciation as ordinary income to the extent of gain?
- [ ] Has the recapture limit at the gain been applied, ensuring no recapture when the asset is sold at a loss and only gain-amount recapture when the gain is below the depreciation?
- [ ] Has the Section 1231 excess gain (gain above the recapture amount) been computed and included in the Section 1231 netting for the year?
- [ ] Has the transaction been checked for nonrecognition (like-kind exchange, involuntary conversion) before applying recapture, with deferred recapture tracked for the replacement property?
- [ ] Is the recapture reported on Form 4797 Part III with the ordinary portion in Part II and the Section 1231 gain in Part I?
- [ ] Is the recapture analysis documented with the asset classification, depreciation schedule, gain computation, recapture amount, Section 1231 netting, and reporting forms?
- [ ] Has the interaction with bonus depreciation and Section 179 expensing been considered, as these increase the depreciation subject to recapture?
