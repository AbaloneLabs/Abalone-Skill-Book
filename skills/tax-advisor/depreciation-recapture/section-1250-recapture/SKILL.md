# Section 1250 Recapture

Section 1250 governs the depreciation recapture rules for real property (buildings and improvements, not land). When depreciable real property is sold, the gain attributable to depreciation is subject to special recapture rules. For most real property held by individual taxpayers, the recapture takes the form of unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, taxed at a maximum federal rate of 25% rather than the preferential long-term capital gains rate. An agent who treats the sale of depreciable real estate as a pure capital gain, ignoring the recapture, will understate the tax and mislead the seller about the net proceeds.

Agents commonly miss that the recapture applies to straight-line depreciation (which is the norm for real property), that the rate is 25% rather than ordinary, that additional depreciation (from accelerated methods) is fully recaptured as ordinary income, and that the recapture is capped at the total gain. The harm is a seller whose tax estimate omits the 25% recapture layer and who faces a higher-than-expected tax bill.

This skill covers Section 1250 depreciation recapture under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; recapture rules interact with other provisions and depend on the property and depreciation history, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Identify Section 1250 Property

Section 1250 property is depreciable real property, including buildings and structural components. Residential rental property (27.5-year recovery) and commercial real property (39-year recovery) are both Section 1250 property. Land is never Section 1250 property because it is not depreciable. The building and the land must be treated separately for recapture purposes, with recapture applying only to the building portion of the gain.

Confirm the property is Section 1250 property (a depreciable building or structural component, not land). For a sale of improved real estate, allocate the sale price between land and building based on fair market value. The recapture rules apply to the building portion. The land portion of the gain is pure Section 1231 capital gain, not subject to recapture. Do not apply recapture to the land gain or treat the entire property as one recapture unit.

### Compute Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain

For real property depreciated under the straight-line method (which is required for real property placed in service after 1986), the recapture takes the form of unrecaptured Section 1250 gain. This is the gain attributable to straight-line depreciation, taxed at a maximum federal rate of 25% (not the preferential 0/15/20% long-term rate). The unrecaptured gain is the lesser of the total depreciation claimed or allowable, or the total gain on the property. If the gain is less than the depreciation, only the gain is recaptured; if the gain exceeds the depreciation, the excess is Section 1231 capital gain.

Compute the total depreciation claimed or allowable over the holding period. This is the potential recapture amount. Compare it to the total gain on the building. The unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is the lesser of the two. This amount is taxed at up to 25%. The remaining gain (total gain minus the recapture amount) is Section 1231 long-term capital gain, taxed at up to 20% (plus the 3.8% NIIT may apply to both portions). Present both portions and their respective rates. Do not treat the entire building gain as one capital gain at 15% or 20%; the recapture layer is taxed higher.

### Distinguish Additional Depreciation (Ordinary Recapture)

If the real property was depreciated under an accelerated method (more rapid than straight-line), the excess depreciation over straight-line is called additional depreciation and is fully recaptured as ordinary income (not capped at 25%). For real property placed in service after 1986, the law requires straight-line depreciation, so additional depreciation is generally not an issue for modern property. However, for property placed in service before 1987 that used accelerated methods (ACRS), or for property with a mix of methods, additional depreciation may exist and is recaptured as ordinary income.

Confirm the depreciation method used over the holding period. For post-1986 straight-line property, there is no additional depreciation, and all recapture is unrecaptured Section 1250 gain at up to 25%. For pre-1987 ACRS property or property with accelerated depreciation, compute the additional depreciation (the excess over straight-line) and recapture it as ordinary income. The ordinary recapture is recognized first, then the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain at 25%, then the remaining Section 1231 gain at capital gains rates. The method history must be traced to apply the correct recapture.

### Apply The Recapture Cap At Total Gain

The unrecaptured Section 1250 gain cannot exceed the total gain on the property. If the property is sold at a loss, there is no recapture (a loss is not recaptured). If the property is sold for a small gain that is less than the total depreciation, only the gain is recaptured, and the remaining depreciation does not create additional ordinary income. The recapture is capped at the gain, which protects the seller from recapturing more than the economic gain realized.

Compute the total gain on the building (sale price minus adjusted basis, where adjusted basis is cost minus depreciation). Compare to the total depreciation. The recapture is the lesser amount. If the sale produces a 10,000 dollar gain and the depreciation was 50,000 dollars, the recapture is 10,000 dollars (the gain), taxed at up to 25%, and there is no remaining capital gain. If the sale produces a 100,000 dollar gain and the depreciation was 50,000 dollars, the recapture is 50,000 dollars at up to 25%, and the remaining 50,000 dollars is Section 1231 capital gain at up to 20%. Apply the cap correctly.

### Separate Recapture From The Land Gain

The recapture applies only to the building portion of the gain, not the land. Land is not depreciable and not subject to recapture. The gain on the land is pure Section 1231 capital gain (for property held in a trade or business) or Section 1221 capital gain (for investment property), taxed at the long-term capital gains rate. The allocation of the sale price between land and building determines how much gain is subject to recapture and how much is pure capital gain.

Allocate the sale price between land and building based on their relative fair market values at the time of sale. The property tax assessment may provide a starting allocation, but the fair market value allocation may differ. Apply the recapture rules to the building portion (the depreciation recapture plus the excess capital gain) and the pure capital gain rules to the land portion. Do not apply the 25% recapture rate to the land gain. The allocation affects the total tax and must be reasonable and documented.

### Report Section 1250 Recapture On Form 4797

Section 1250 recapture is reported on Form 4797 (Sales of Business Property). The unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is computed on Form 4797 Part III for real property. The gain is then subject to the Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain Worksheet (in the Form 1040 instructions) to apply the 25% maximum rate. The remaining Section 1231 gain flows through the Section 1231 netting on Form 4797 Part I and then to Schedule D as long-term capital gain.

Ensure the recapture is reported on Form 4797 Part III and that the 25% rate is applied through the worksheet. Do not report the recapture gain on Schedule D directly as a standard capital gain; the 25% rate requires the Form 4797 computation. The reporting must distinguish the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (25% rate) from the Section 1231 capital gain (20% rate) and the land gain (capital gain rate). Reconcile the reporting with the depreciation schedule and the closing statement.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Entire Real Estate Gain As Long-Term Capital Gain

The depreciation recapture portion is taxed at up to 25%, not the preferential rate. Only the excess gain is long-term capital gain. Lumping them understates tax.

### Applying Recapture To The Land Portion

Land is not depreciable and not subject to recapture. Only the building gain includes recapture. Allocate the sale between land and building and apply recapture to the building only.

### Ignoring Additional Depreciation From Pre-1987 ACRS

Pre-1987 property depreciated under ACRS may have additional depreciation (excess over straight-line) that is recaptured as ordinary income, not capped at 25%. Trace the depreciation method history.

### Failing To Apply The Recapture Cap At Total Gain

Recapture cannot exceed the total gain. If the gain is less than the depreciation, only the gain is recaptured. Applying recapture to the full depreciation when the gain is smaller overstates tax.

### Reporting Recapture On Schedule D Instead Of Form 4797

Section 1250 recapture is reported on Form 4797 Part III with the 25% rate applied through the worksheet. Reporting on Schedule D as a standard capital gain misses the rate computation.

### Forgetting That Depreciation Allowed Or Allowable Is Recaptured

The recapture is based on depreciation allowed or allowable, whether or not claimed. A taxpayer who failed to claim depreciation still faces recapture on the amount they should have claimed.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the property been confirmed as Section 1250 property (depreciable real building or structural component, not land), with the sale allocated between land and building?
- [ ] Has the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain been computed as the lesser of total depreciation (allowed or allowable) or total gain on the building, taxed at up to 25%?
- [ ] Has additional depreciation (from pre-1987 ACRS or accelerated methods) been identified and recaptured as ordinary income, separate from the 25% unrecaptured gain?
- [ ] Has the recapture cap been applied, ensuring recapture does not exceed the total gain on the building?
- [ ] Has the land gain been separated and treated as pure capital gain (not subject to recapture)?
- [ ] Is the recapture reported on Form 4797 Part III with the 25% rate applied through the Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain Worksheet?
- [ ] Is the remaining Section 1231 gain reported through the Form 4797 Part I netting and then to Schedule D?
- [ ] Is the recapture analysis documented with the depreciation schedule, the land-building allocation, the gain computation, and the reporting forms?
