# Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain

Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is the specific portion of gain on the sale of depreciable real property that is attributable to straight-line depreciation and taxed at a maximum federal rate of 25%. It is not ordinary income (which would be up to 37%), but it does not receive the full preferential long-term capital gains rate (0/15/20%). It sits in its own rate bracket, and computing it correctly requires separating the total gain into the unrecaptured portion and the remaining capital gain portion. An agent who lumps the entire real estate gain into one rate category will misstate the tax, because the unrecaptured portion is taxed 5 to 20 percentage points higher than the remaining capital gain.

Agents commonly miss that the unrecaptured gain is the lesser of depreciation or gain, that the rate is a maximum of 25% (not the taxpayer's full ordinary rate), that the rate interacts with the taxpayer's ordinary bracket (the effective rate can be below 25% for lower-income taxpayers), and that the NIIT stacks on top. The harm is a seller whose tax estimate is wrong in either direction, leading to poor planning or unexpected tax.

This skill covers unrecaptured Section 1250 gain under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the computation depends on the depreciation history and the taxpayer's income, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Define Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain

Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is the portion of gain on the sale of depreciable real property (Section 1250 property) that is attributable to depreciation that would be recaptured as ordinary income under the full Section 1250 rules but is instead taxed at a special maximum rate of 25%. For real property placed in service after 1986 and depreciated straight-line (the required method), all the depreciation is unrecaptured Section 1250 gain when the property is sold at a gain. There is no ordinary recapture for straight-line depreciation; the special 25% rate applies instead.

Confirm the property is Section 1250 property depreciated under straight-line (the norm for post-1986 real property). Compute the total depreciation claimed or allowable over the holding period. This is the potential unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, subject to the cap at the total gain. The remaining gain (if the sale price exceeds the original cost) is Section 1231 long-term capital gain taxed at the 0/15/20% rate. Do not confuse unrecaptured Section 1250 gain with ordinary Section 1245 recapture (which applies to personal property and is fully ordinary).

### Apply The Lesser Of Depreciation Or Gain Rule

The unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is the lesser of the total depreciation claimed or allowable, or the total gain on the property. If the gain is less than the depreciation, only the gain is unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, and there is no remaining capital gain. If the gain exceeds the depreciation, the unrecaptured portion equals the depreciation, and the excess is Section 1231 capital gain. This rule ensures the recapture does not exceed the economic gain.

Compute the total gain on the building (sale price minus adjusted basis, where adjusted basis is cost minus depreciation). Compare to the total depreciation. The unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is the lesser amount. For example, a building with 80,000 dollars of depreciation sold for a 60,000 dollar gain has 60,000 dollars of unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (the gain, because it is less than the depreciation), and no remaining capital gain. The same building sold for a 120,000 dollar gain has 80,000 dollars of unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (the depreciation) and 40,000 dollars of Section 1231 capital gain. Apply the lesser-of rule precisely.

### Apply The 25% Maximum Rate With Ordinary Bracket Interaction

The unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is taxed at a maximum federal rate of 25%, but the actual rate depends on the taxpayer's ordinary income bracket. For taxpayers in the 10% or 15% ordinary brackets, the unrecaptured gain may be taxed at those lower rates (up to 15%, not 25%, because the 25% is a cap). For taxpayers in the 25%, 28%, 33%, 35%, or 37% ordinary brackets, the unrecaptured gain is taxed at 25% (the cap). The 25% is a maximum, not a flat rate.

Determine the taxpayer's ordinary marginal bracket before the real estate sale. If the taxpayer is in a bracket below 25%, the unrecaptured gain is taxed at the ordinary rate (up to 15%), which is favorable. If the taxpayer is in a bracket at or above 25%, the unrecaptured gain is taxed at the 25% cap. Do not assume the rate is always 25%; for lower-income taxpayers, it can be lower. Model the sale with the taxpayer's income to determine the actual rate on the unrecaptured portion.

### Stack The Net Investment Income Tax

The 3.8% net investment income tax (NIIT) applies to the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain if the taxpayer's modified AGI exceeds the thresholds (200,000 dollars single, 250,000 dollars married filing jointly). The NIIT stacks on top of the 25% rate, making the effective federal rate up to 28.8% on the unrecaptured portion for higher-income taxpayers. The NIIT also applies to the remaining Section 1231 capital gain and to the land gain.

Include the NIIT in the federal cost estimate for taxpayers above the thresholds. The combined rate on the unrecaptured portion (25% plus 3.8% NIIT) is 28.8%, which is higher than the combined rate on the remaining capital gain (20% plus 3.8% equals 23.8%). The rate differential between the unrecaptured portion and the capital gain portion is therefore 5 percentage points (before NIIT) or 5 percentage points (after NIIT, 28.8% versus 23.8%). Present both rates so the seller understands the layering.

### Separate The Land Gain From The Building Gain

The unrecaptured Section 1250 gain applies only to the building portion of the sale. The land portion of the gain is not subject to recapture (land is not depreciable) and is taxed as Section 1231 long-term capital gain at the 0/15/20% rate (plus NIIT). The allocation of the sale price between land and building determines how much gain is unrecaptured Section 1250 gain and how much is pure capital gain.

Allocate the sale price between land and building based on fair market value. Apply the unrecaptured Section 1250 rules to the building gain (the depreciation recapture plus any excess capital gain). Apply the pure capital gain rules to the land gain. Do not apply the 25% rate to the land gain. The allocation affects the total tax and must be reasonable and documented. For a property where the land has appreciated significantly, a large portion of the gain may be land gain at the lower capital gains rate, reducing the overall tax.

### Report Through Form 4797 And The Rate Worksheet

Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is reported on Form 4797 Part III for the real property sale. The gain is then transferred to the Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain Worksheet (in the Form 1040 instructions) to apply the 25% maximum rate. The worksheet computes the tax on the unrecaptured portion separately from the tax on other capital gains. The remaining Section 1231 gain and the land gain flow through the Schedule D capital gain computation at the 0/15/20% rate.

Ensure the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is reported on Form 4797 Part III and that the 25% rate is applied through the worksheet. Do not report the entire real estate gain on Schedule D as a standard capital gain; the unrecaptured portion requires the Form 4797 computation and the separate rate worksheet. The reporting must distinguish the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (25% rate), the Section 1231 capital gain (20% rate), and the land gain (capital gain rate). Reconcile the reporting with the depreciation schedule and the land-building allocation.

## Common Traps

### Taxing The Entire Real Estate Gain At The Capital Gains Rate

The unrecaptured Section 1250 portion is taxed at up to 25%, not the 0/15/20% rate. Only the excess gain and the land gain get the preferential rate. Lumping them understates tax.

### Assuming The Rate Is Always 25%

The 25% is a maximum. For taxpayers in ordinary brackets below 25%, the unrecaptured gain is taxed at the lower ordinary rate. Model the actual rate with the taxpayer's income.

### Applying The 25% Rate To The Land Gain

Land is not depreciable and not subject to recapture. The land gain is pure capital gain at the 0/15/20% rate. Apply the 25% rate only to the building depreciation recapture.

### Forgetting The NIIT Stacks On Top

The 3.8% NIIT applies to the unrecaptured Section 1250 gain for higher-income taxpayers. The combined rate is up to 28.8%. Omitting the NIIT understates the federal cost.

### Reporting On Schedule D Without Form 4797 And The Worksheet

The unrecaptured Section 1250 gain requires Form 4797 Part III and the Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain Worksheet. Reporting on Schedule D as a standard capital gain misses the 25% rate computation.

### Overlooking The Lesser Of Depreciation Or Gain

The unrecaptured gain is capped at the total gain. If the gain is less than the depreciation, only the gain is recaptured. Applying recapture to the full depreciation overstates tax.
