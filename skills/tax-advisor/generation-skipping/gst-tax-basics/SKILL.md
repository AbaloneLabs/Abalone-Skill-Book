# GST Tax Basics

The generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax is a separate federal transfer tax layered on top of the gift and estate tax system, designed to prevent taxpayers from avoiding one level of transfer tax by transferring wealth to a generation two or more levels below the donor, typically grandchildren. A transfer to a grandchild that skips the child's generation can otherwise escape the estate tax that would apply when the child dies, so the GST tax imposes a flat tax roughly equal to the tax that would have been collected at the skipped generation. Agents frequently treat GST as a minor footnote to estate planning when it is actually a distinct tax with its own exemption, its own allocation mechanics, its own rate, and its own return reporting.

The harm from misjudging GST is severe because the tax is a flat percentage applied to the full transfer value, with no graduated brackets and no further exemption once the GST exemption is exhausted. An agent may tell a client that a gift to a grandchild "just uses the annual exclusion" or "comes out of the lifetime exemption" without recognizing that the transfer is also a generation-skipping transfer subject to the separate GST tax, which can be 40 percent of the amount transferred. Conversely, an agent may overstate exposure by failing to recognize that the GST exemption can be allocated to shelter the transfer, or that a trust can be structured as GST-exempt so that all future distributions to skip persons are protected.

This skill covers the judgment needed to identify generation-skipping transfers, distinguish the three transfer types, apply the GST exemption, and recognize when GST reporting is required. It does not cover the detailed mechanics of GST exemption allocation strategy, the automatic versus manual allocation election, or advanced dynasty trust design, which warrant separate analysis. Because GST amounts, rates, and allocation rules are statutory and change with legislation, nothing here is tax advice, and a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney must be consulted before relying on any conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Identify Whether A Transfer Reaches A Skip Person

The GST tax applies only to transfers to or for the benefit of a skip person. A skip person is generally a recipient who is two or more generations below the donor, such as a grandchild or great-grandchild, or a person assigned to a generation more than one level below the donor under the generation-assignment rules. A trust can also be a skip person if all of its possible beneficiaries are skip persons, or under the more complex rules that look at the trust's beneficiary structure.

Before concluding a transfer has GST consequences, identify the recipient's generation relative to the donor, whether the transfer is made directly or through a trust, and whether any non-skip persons can benefit from the trust. A transfer to a child is generally not a GST transfer, while a transfer to a grandchild or to a trust for grandchildren typically is. Do not assume a transfer is GST-free simply because it qualifies for the gift tax annual exclusion; a gift to a grandchild above the GST-exempt threshold can still be a taxable direct skip.

### Treat The GST Exemption As Separate But Equal In Amount

Each individual receives a GST exemption equal in dollar amount to the basic exclusion amount used for gift and estate tax. Unlike the unified gift and estate exemption, however, the GST exemption is a separate pool that must be allocated to specific transfers or trusts to shelter them. Unused GST exemption cannot be carried over to a surviving spouse through portability in the same way DSUE operates for the estate exemption.

To shelter a generation-skipping transfer, the donor or the executor must allocate GST exemption to it, either automatically under the statutory rules or through an affirmative election on a return. Request and review prior Form 709 and Form 706 returns to determine how much GST exemption has already been allocated, because clients routinely forget prior allocations to trusts. Do not describe the GST exemption as automatically applied or as the same pool as the estate exemption; it is a distinct amount that must be affirmatively allocated to be effective.

### Apply The Flat GST Rate With No Graduated Brackets

The GST tax is imposed at a flat rate equal to the highest federal estate tax rate, which is 40 percent under current law, applied to the taxable amount of the transfer. There are no graduated brackets, so once the GST exemption is exhausted, every additional dollar transferred to a skip person is taxed at the full flat rate. This makes the marginal cost of an unsheltered generation-skipping transfer very high relative to ordinary transfers.

When modeling GST exposure, compute the flat tax on the full taxable amount after any allocated exemption, and recognize that the tax is generally payable by the transferee or the trust rather than the donor in a direct skip context. For indirect skips through trusts, the tax may be triggered later when a distribution is made or a termination occurs. Do not estimate GST cost using a graduated rate table or by analogy to income tax brackets; the flat rate is the governing framework.

### Distinguish The Three Transfer Types

The GST tax applies to three distinct transfer types, each with different timing and reporting. A direct skip is an outright transfer to a skip person, such as a gift directly to a grandchild, taxed immediately. A taxable distribution is a distribution from a trust to a skip-person beneficiary, taxed when the distribution occurs. A taxable termination occurs when an interest in a trust terminates and the remaining assets pass to or for the benefit of skip persons, such as when a child's interest ends and the trust continues for grandchildren.

For each potential GST transfer, classify it as a direct skip, taxable distribution, or taxable termination, because the timing of the tax and the responsible party differ. A direct skip is reported on Form 709 if made during life or Form 706 at death, while taxable distributions and terminations are reported on Form 706-GS for the trust. Do not lump all GST transfers together; misclassifying a taxable termination as a direct skip, or vice versa, changes when tax is due and who pays it.

### Recognize The Automatic And Manual Allocation Rules

GST exemption can be allocated automatically or through an affirmative election. For direct skips during life, the donor is generally deemed to have allocated enough GST exemption to cover the transfer to the extent exemption remains, reported on Form 709. For transfers in trust, the automatic allocation rules may shelter certain trusts, but complex trusts, including those with non-skip beneficiaries, may require a late allocation election or an affirmative allocation to achieve full GST-exempt status.

Confirm whether the automatic allocation rules apply to the specific trust structure, and consider whether an affirmative allocation election on Form 709 or Form 706 is needed to fully shelter the trust. For trusts created before the automatic allocation rules applied, a timely or late allocation election may be the only way to make the trust GST-exempt. Do not assume a trust is automatically GST-exempt because the donor had sufficient exemption; the allocation must be properly made and documented on the relevant return.

### Coordinate GST With Gift And Estate Tax

A single transfer can implicate all three transfer taxes simultaneously: gift tax on the value transferred, estate tax through exemption consumption, and GST tax because the recipient is a skip person. The GST exemption is allocated in addition to the gift or estate exemption used, so a fully sheltered generation-skipping transfer consumes both pools. Failing to coordinate the three can leave a transfer sheltered for gift tax but exposed to GST tax, or vice versa.

Evaluate each transfer for combined gift, estate, and GST consequences, and confirm that the correct exemptions are allocated on the correct returns. A gift to a grandchild trust may use gift tax annual exclusion and lifetime exemption while still requiring a separate GST exemption allocation to avoid GST tax on future trust distributions. Present the combined picture rather than treating GST as an isolated afterthought.

### Confirm Reporting And Responsible Party

GST tax reporting depends on the transfer type and whether it occurs during life or at death. Lifetime direct skips are reported on Form 709, while at-death direct skips are reported on Form 706. Taxable distributions and taxable terminations from a trust are reported on Form 706-GS, generally filed by the trustee, and the tax is typically paid from trust assets.

Identify who is responsible for filing and paying the GST tax before the transfer is completed, because the responsible party differs by transfer type and the trustee may bear liability for trust-level GST tax. Confirm the correct form and filing deadline, and ensure the trust agreement addresses who bears the economic cost of any GST tax. Do not leave GST reporting unspecified, since an unreported GST transfer can generate tax, interest, and penalties that compound over time.

## Common Traps

### Assuming A Grandchild Gift Is Automatically Sheltered

A gift to a grandchild may qualify for the gift tax annual exclusion but still be a taxable direct skip for GST purposes if it exceeds the GST-exempt amount. Treating the annual exclusion as fully covering GST exposure can leave an unsheltered GST tax liability.

### Treating GST Exemption As The Same Pool As Estate Exemption

The GST exemption is equal in dollar amount to the estate and gift exemption but is a separate pool that must be allocated to specific transfers or trusts. Assuming one allocation covers both taxes can leave a transfer exposed to GST tax.

### Ignoring The Flat Rate On Unsheltered Transfers

Once GST exemption is exhausted, the tax is a flat 40 percent on the full transfer value with no graduated brackets. Estimating GST cost using graduated rates understates the true liability and can mislead the client about affordability.

### Misclassifying The Transfer Type

Direct skips, taxable distributions, and taxable terminations have different timing, forms, and responsible parties. Misclassifying a taxable termination as a direct skip changes when tax is due and who pays it, creating reporting errors and potential penalties.

### Assuming A Trust Is Automatically GST-Exempt

The automatic allocation rules shelter some trusts but not all, and pre-rule trusts may require an affirmative or late allocation election. Assuming a trust is exempt because the donor had sufficient exemption ignores whether the allocation was properly made and documented.

### Overlooking Form 706-GS For Trust-Level GST Tax

Taxable distributions and taxable terminations are reported on Form 706-GS by the trustee, not Form 709. Filing the wrong form or omitting the trust-level return can leave GST tax unreported and accruing interest.

### Forgetting That GST Tax Is Often Paid By The Trust Or Transferee

For indirect skips, the GST tax is generally paid by the trust or the transferee, not the donor. Failing to address who bears the cost in the trust agreement can create disputes and unexpected reductions in trust assets.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the recipient been confirmed as a skip person two or more generations below the donor, or has the trust's beneficiary structure been checked under the generation-assignment rules?
- [ ] Has the GST exemption been treated as a separate pool equal in amount to the estate exemption, with prior Form 709 and Form 706 allocations reviewed to determine remaining exemption?
- [ ] Has the flat 40 percent GST rate been applied to the taxable amount, recognizing there are no graduated brackets once exemption is exhausted?
- [ ] Has the transfer been classified as a direct skip, taxable distribution, or taxable termination, with the correct timing and responsible party identified?
- [ ] Has the automatic allocation rule been evaluated for the specific trust, and has an affirmative or late allocation election been considered if automatic allocation is insufficient?
- [ ] Has the transfer been evaluated for combined gift, estate, and GST consequences rather than GST as an isolated afterthought?
- [ ] Has the correct reporting form been confirmed, Form 709 or Form 706 for direct skips and Form 706-GS for trust-level transfers, with the responsible party and filing deadline documented?
- [ ] Has the trust agreement been checked for who bears the economic cost of any GST tax, since the trust or transferee often pays for indirect skips?
