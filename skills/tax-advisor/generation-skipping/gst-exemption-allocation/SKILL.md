# GST Exemption Allocation

The generation-skipping transfer (GST) exemption must be affirmatively allocated to specific transfers or trusts to shelter them from GST tax; merely having exemption available does not automatically protect every transfer. The allocation decision determines whether a trust becomes fully GST-exempt, partially exempt, or entirely nonexempt, and this status is measured by the inclusion ratio that governs the GST tax rate on all future distributions and terminations from the trust. Agents frequently treat GST exemption allocation as a mechanical checkbox when it is actually a strategic decision with irrevocable consequences, because an allocation to the wrong trust, at the wrong time, or in the wrong amount can waste exemption or fail to achieve the intended exempt status.

The harm from poor allocation is twofold and asymmetric. An under-allocation leaves a trust partially or fully subject to the flat 40 percent GST tax on every future distribution to skip persons, eroding dynasty wealth over generations. An over-allocation wastes finite GST exemption on a trust that did not need full allocation to achieve exempt status, leaving no exemption to shelter other transfers. The interaction between the automatic allocation rules, the inclusion ratio computation, the timing of late allocations, and the value used for allocation, especially for trusts holding hard-to-value or discounted assets, creates multiple points where a casual decision produces a permanent, costly result.

This skill covers the judgment needed to allocate GST exemption effectively, choose between automatic and affirmative allocation, compute and interpret the inclusion ratio, and evaluate late allocation elections. It does not cover the fundamentals of identifying skip persons or the three GST transfer types, nor advanced dynasty trust design, which warrant separate analysis. Because allocation rules, valuation discounts, and election deadlines are statutory and procedural, nothing here is tax advice, and a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney must be consulted before relying on any conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Understand That Allocation Is What Makes A Trust GST-Exempt

A trust's GST status is not inherent; it is created by the allocation of GST exemption to the trust. When exemption equal to the trust's value is allocated, the inclusion ratio becomes zero and the trust is fully GST-exempt, meaning no GST tax applies to future distributions or terminations. When no exemption is allocated, the inclusion ratio is one and the trust is fully GST-nonexempt, so the flat GST rate applies to all taxable events.

Confirm for every trust with skip-person beneficiaries whether GST exemption has been allocated and in what amount, by reviewing the relevant Form 709 or Form 706. Do not describe a trust as a dynasty trust or GST-exempt trust unless the allocation has actually been made and documented; the label in the trust agreement does not confer exempt status. A trust intended for grandchildren that lacks a proper allocation remains fully exposed to GST tax regardless of the drafter's intent.

### Compute And Interpret The Inclusion Ratio

The inclusion ratio measures how much of a trust is subject to GST tax, ranging from zero, fully exempt, to one, fully nonexempt, with fractional values for partially exempt trusts. It is computed as one minus the applicable fraction, where the applicable fraction equals the amount of GST exemption allocated to the trust divided by the net value of the property transferred to the trust, reduced by certain gift and estate tax attributable to the transfer. A partial allocation produces a fractional inclusion ratio, so that portion of each future distribution is taxed and the remainder is exempt.

When evaluating a trust, compute the inclusion ratio from the allocation and the trust value, and recognize that a fractional ratio means each distribution is partly taxed at the flat GST rate and partly exempt. Confirm the net value used in the denominator, because valuation discounts, liabilities, and prior gifts affect the fraction. Do not assume that allocating some exemption makes a trust mostly exempt; a partial allocation only shelters the corresponding fraction of future transfers, and the remainder remains fully taxed.

### Evaluate Automatic Allocation Before Relying On It

The automatic allocation rules deem GST exemption allocated to certain direct skips and to certain transfers to trusts, generally covering direct skips and GST trusts created after the rules took effect. For trusts that qualify, exemption is automatically allocated up to the transfer value without an affirmative election, which simplifies compliance but only for the trusts and transfers the rules reach.

Confirm whether the specific trust qualifies for automatic allocation, because trusts with non-skip beneficiaries, complex distribution standards, or pre-rule creation dates often do not. For trusts that do not qualify, an affirmative allocation on Form 709 or Form 706 is required, and the automatic rules provide no fallback protection. Do not rely on automatic allocation as a universal safety net; verify the trust structure against the statutory tests before assuming exemption was allocated.

### Consider Affirmative And Late Allocation Elections

When automatic allocation does not apply or is insufficient, a taxpayer can make an affirmative allocation of GST exemption on a timely filed Form 709 or Form 706, or a late allocation under the extended deadline rules. A late allocation allows exemption to be allocated to a trust after the transfer, generally by the later of the return due date or a fixed period, but the value used for a late allocation may be the trust's current value rather than the date-of-transfer value, which can change the inclusion ratio.

Evaluate whether a late allocation is advantageous or necessary for trusts that did not receive a timely allocation, and confirm the deadline and the valuation date applicable to the late election. For appreciating trusts, a late allocation at current higher value consumes more exemption for the same exempt status, while for depreciated trusts it may be more efficient. Do not let a trust drift indefinitely without an allocation decision, because the late allocation window is finite and missing it can leave the trust permanently nonexempt.

### Use The Correct Value For Allocation

The amount of GST exemption needed to make a trust fully exempt equals the net value of the property transferred to the trust, adjusted for valuation discounts and certain tax. For hard-to-value assets such as closely held business interests, real estate, or discounted entity interests, the value used for allocation directly determines how much exemption is consumed and whether the trust achieves a zero inclusion ratio.

Obtain a qualified appraisal for hard-to-value trust assets before allocating exemption, and confirm whether valuation discounts are defensible, because an overstated value wastes exemption while an understated value risks an inclusion ratio above zero on audit. Document the value used and reconcile it with the value reported on the gift or estate tax return, since inconsistencies create audit risk. Do not allocate exemption based on a rough estimate of trust value when the assets require formal valuation.

### Avoid Wasting Exemption On Over-Allocation

GST exemption is finite and cannot be recovered once allocated. Allocating more exemption than the net trust value requires does not improve exempt status beyond a zero inclusion ratio; it simply wastes the excess. This is especially harmful when exemption could have been used to shelter another trust or transfer.

Compute the exact net value needed to achieve a zero inclusion ratio before allocating, and avoid rounding up or allocating a round number out of convenience. For trusts that will receive additional contributions, plan the allocation across contributions rather than over-allocating to the first transfer. Do not treat exemption as plentiful enough to allocate carelessly, particularly before the scheduled sunset of the elevated exemption amounts.

### Coordinate Allocation With The Estate Tax Return At Death

At death, the executor can allocate the decedent's remaining GST exemption on the estate tax return, Form 706, to shelter transfers and trusts passing through the estate. This is often the last opportunity to allocate the decedent's personal GST exemption, and the allocation interacts with the estate's overall funding and marital or charitable deduction planning.

Ensure the executor evaluates GST allocation on Form 706 as part of the estate administration, because an unallocated decedent exemption is lost and cannot benefit skip-person beneficiaries. Coordinate the allocation with the estate's liquidity, the funding of credit shelter or marital trusts, and any disclaimer planning. Do not assume the executor will automatically allocate GST exemption optimally; it requires affirmative analysis and election on the return.

## Common Traps

### Assuming A Trust Is GST-Exempt Because It Is Labeled A Dynasty Trust

The trust agreement's label does not confer exempt status. A trust is exempt only if GST exemption is allocated to it on a return, and many labeled dynasty trusts remain fully nonexempt due to a missing allocation.

### Over-Allocating Exemption Beyond The Zero Inclusion Ratio

Allocating more exemption than the net trust value wastes the excess without improving exempt status. Since exemption is finite and unrecoverable, over-allocation permanently reduces shelter available for other transfers.

### Relying On Automatic Allocation For A Trust That Does Not Qualify

The automatic rules reach certain GST trusts and direct skips but not trusts with non-skip beneficiaries or pre-rule trusts. Assuming automatic coverage without verifying the trust structure can leave the trust fully nonexempt.

### Computing The Inclusion Ratio With The Wrong Trust Value

The inclusion ratio depends on the net value of transferred property, adjusted for discounts and tax. Using a rough estimate or ignoring valuation discounts produces an inaccurate ratio that can leave partial GST exposure.

### Missing The Late Allocation Deadline

The late allocation window is finite, generally tied to return due dates plus a fixed period. Letting a trust drift without an allocation decision can close the late window and leave the trust permanently nonexempt.

### Forgetting The Executor's Final Allocation Opportunity On Form 706

At death, the executor can allocate remaining GST exemption on Form 706. Failing to evaluate this allocation loses the decedent's personal exemption permanently and can expose skip-person beneficiaries to GST tax.

### Allocating Based On An Unsupportable Valuation Discount

For closely held business or entity interests, valuation discounts reduce the exemption needed. Claiming an aggressive, indefensible discount risks an inclusion ratio above zero on audit, plus tax and penalties on the uncovered portion.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has GST exemption allocation to each trust with skip-person beneficiaries been confirmed by reviewing Form 709 or Form 706, rather than assuming exempt status from the trust label?
- [ ] Has the inclusion ratio been computed from the allocated exemption and the net trust value, with fractional ratios recognized as partially taxing each future distribution?
- [ ] Has the trust been tested against the automatic allocation rules, with an affirmative or late allocation election made if automatic allocation does not apply?
- [ ] Has a late allocation been evaluated for its deadline and valuation date, recognizing that appreciating trusts may consume more exemption at current value?
- [ ] Has the net value used for allocation been supported by a qualified appraisal for hard-to-value assets, with valuation discounts assessed for defensibility?
- [ ] Has over-allocation beyond the zero inclusion ratio been avoided by computing the exact exemption needed rather than allocating a round number?
- [ ] Has the executor's GST allocation on Form 706 been evaluated as the final opportunity to use the decedent's personal exemption, coordinated with estate funding and deduction planning?
- [ ] Has the allocation been coordinated with gift and estate exemption usage so that a transfer is not sheltered for one tax while exposed to GST tax?
- [ ] Are uncertain allocation, inclusion ratio, valuation, and election questions escalated to a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney rather than answered from general assumptions, since this guidance is not tax advice?
