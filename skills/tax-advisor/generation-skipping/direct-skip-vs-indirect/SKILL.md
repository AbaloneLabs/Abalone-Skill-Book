# Direct Skip Versus Indirect Transfers

Generation-skipping transfers fall into three categories that determine everything downstream of the transfer: when the GST tax is triggered, who is responsible for paying it, and which return reports it. A direct skip is taxed immediately at the time of transfer, while the two indirect types, taxable distributions and taxable terminations, are taxed later when an event occurs within a trust. Agents frequently collapse all three into a single concept of GST tax, but the classification drives the timing, the payer, and the form, and a misclassification produces incorrect reporting, missed deadlines, and potential liability allocated to the wrong party.

The harm from misclassification is concrete and often irreversible. An agent may treat a trust distribution to a grandchild as a direct skip reportable on the donor's Form 709, when it is actually a taxable distribution reportable on Form 706-GS by the trustee, due 75 days after the close of the trust's tax year. Conversely, an agent may treat a termination of a child's trust interest as a mere administrative event when it is a taxable termination that triggers GST tax on the entire trust corpus passing to skip persons. Because the tax can attach to the full trust value at a flat 40 percent rate, getting the category wrong can mean a large, unexpected liability with no mechanism to correct it after the deadline.

This skill covers the judgment needed to classify a generation-skipping transfer correctly, identify the triggering event for indirect transfers, determine the responsible party, and select the reporting form. It does not cover the fundamentals of the GST exemption or inclusion ratio computation, which warrant separate analysis. Because classification rules, timing, and form requirements are statutory and procedural, nothing here is tax advice, and a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney must be consulted before relying on any conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Define A Direct Skip And Its Immediate Tax Trigger

A direct skip is a transfer, either during life or at death, of property to a skip person that bypasses all intervening generations, such as an outright gift from a grandparent to a grandchild or a bequest directly to a grandchild. The defining feature is that the transfer is subject to both estate or gift tax and GST tax simultaneously, and the GST tax is triggered immediately at the time of the transfer. The tax is generally paid by the transferee or, for a direct skip in trust, by the trust.

When evaluating an outright transfer to a skip person, classify it as a direct skip and confirm the tax is computed and reported at the time of transfer. A lifetime direct skip is reported on Form 709 alongside the gift tax return, while a direct skip at death is reported on Form 706. Do not defer the GST analysis for a direct skip to a later date; the tax attaches immediately and the reporting deadline is the same as the gift or estate tax return.

### Define A Taxable Distribution And Its Distribution Trigger

A taxable distribution is a distribution from a trust to a skip-person beneficiary that is not a direct skip, occurring when a trust holds property for both skip and non-skip persons and makes a distribution to a skip person. The GST tax is triggered not when the trust is funded but when the distribution is actually made to the skip-person beneficiary. The tax is paid by the skip-person recipient and is computed on the amount of the distribution.

When a trust makes a distribution to a grandchild beneficiary, classify it as a taxable distribution and confirm the GST tax is triggered at distribution, reportable on Form 706-GS by the trustee. The reporting deadline is tied to the trust's tax year, generally due by the last day of the fourth month after the close of the trust's tax year, not the donor's calendar year. Do not report a taxable distribution on the donor's Form 709; it is a trust-level event with its own return and its own payer.

### Define A Taxable Termination And Its Termination Trigger

A taxable termination occurs when an interest in trust terminates, either by death, lapse of time, or release, and the remaining property passes to or for the benefit of skip persons, with no non-skip person having an interest in the property after the termination. The classic example is a trust that pays income to a child for life, and upon the child's death the principal passes to grandchildren. The GST tax is triggered at the moment of termination and is imposed on the trust, computed on the entire value of the property subject to the termination.

When a trust interest ends and the assets shift to skip persons, classify the event as a taxable termination and confirm the GST tax attaches to the full corpus passing to skip persons, not merely to a distribution amount. The termination is reported on Form 706-GS, and the tax is paid from trust assets. Do not treat a termination as a passive administrative milestone; it is a taxable event that can trigger a flat 40 percent tax on the entire trust value if the trust is not GST-exempt.

### Identify The Triggering Event For Each Category

The three categories differ fundamentally in what event triggers the tax. A direct skip is triggered by the transfer itself, a taxable distribution by the act of distribution, and a taxable termination by the ending of a non-skip interest. Identifying the triggering event is the first step, because it determines the tax timing and the deadline for reporting and payment.

For each potential GST event, isolate the precise moment that triggers the tax: the transfer date for a direct skip, the distribution date for a taxable distribution, or the termination date for a taxable termination. Confirm that the event has actually occurred before reporting, because premature or deferred reporting creates errors. Do not report GST tax based on the trust's creation or funding alone unless a direct skip occurred; indirect transfers wait for their triggering event.

### Determine Who Pays The Tax For Each Category

The responsible party for GST tax differs by category and must be identified before the transfer or event. For a direct skip during life, the tax is generally paid by the transferee, though the donor often effectively bears it. For a taxable distribution, the tax is paid by the skip-person recipient of the distribution. For a taxable termination, the tax is paid by the trust itself, reducing the assets available for the skip-person beneficiaries.

Identify the payer for each category and confirm the trust agreement or governing instrument addresses who bears the economic cost, because the statutory payer may differ from the party the settlor intended to burden. For taxable terminations, ensure the trust has sufficient liquid assets to pay a tax that can equal 40 percent of the entire corpus. Do not assume the donor or the estate always pays; indirect transfers shift the burden to the recipient or the trust.

### Select The Correct Reporting Form And Deadline

Each category has a distinct reporting form and deadline. A lifetime direct skip is reported on Form 709, due April 15 of the year after the gift with the gift tax return. A direct skip at death is reported on Form 706, due nine months after death with the estate tax return. Taxable distributions and taxable terminations are reported on Form 706-GS, due by the last day of the fourth month after the close of the trust's tax year.

Confirm the correct form and deadline for each classified transfer, and calendar the deadline based on the triggering event rather than the trust's creation. Filing the wrong form, such as Form 709 for a taxable distribution, or missing the trust-year deadline for Form 706-GS, creates penalties and interest. Do not assume all GST events share the donor's calendar-year deadline; trust-level events follow the trust's own tax year.

### Confirm Whether The Trust Is GST-Exempt Before Reporting

Before reporting any indirect GST event, confirm whether the trust is fully or partially GST-exempt, because exempt trusts generate no GST tax on distributions or terminations and partially exempt trusts tax only the nonexempt fraction. The inclusion ratio, determined by prior GST exemption allocation, governs whether a taxable distribution or termination actually produces tax.

Review the trust's GST exemption allocation history and compute the inclusion ratio before concluding a distribution or termination is taxable. A fully exempt trust, inclusion ratio zero, has no GST reporting obligation for indirect events, while a partially exempt trust reports only the taxable fraction. Do not file Form 706-GS or compute tax without first confirming the trust's exempt status, because reporting tax on an exempt trust wastes trust assets on a non-existent liability.

## Common Traps

### Reporting A Taxable Distribution On The Donor's Form 709

A taxable distribution is a trust-level event reported on Form 706-GS by the trustee, not on the donor's Form 709. Filing the wrong form leaves the GST tax unreported and accruing interest.

### Treating A Termination As An Administrative Non-Event

A taxable termination can trigger GST tax on the entire trust corpus passing to skip persons. Treating the end of a child's trust interest as a mere formality can produce a large, unexpected flat-rate liability.

### Deferring GST Analysis For A Direct Skip

A direct skip triggers GST tax immediately at transfer, with the same deadline as the gift or estate return. Deferring the analysis past the filing deadline creates late reporting and penalties.

### Assuming The Donor Or Estate Always Pays

For indirect transfers, the tax is paid by the recipient, for taxable distributions, or by the trust, for taxable terminations. Assuming the donor bears the cost can leave the trust or beneficiary with an unexpected liability and insufficient liquid assets.

### Missing The Trust-Year Deadline For Form 706-GS

Taxable distributions and terminations follow the trust's tax year, generally due by the last day of the fourth month after year-end. Applying the donor's calendar-year deadline causes late filing.

### Computing Tax On An Exempt Or Partially Exempt Trust Without Checking The Inclusion Ratio

A fully exempt trust has no GST tax on indirect events, and a partially exempt trust taxes only the nonexempt fraction. Reporting tax without confirming the inclusion ratio can waste trust assets on a non-existent liability.

### Confusing A Distribution To A Skip Person With A Direct Skip

A distribution from an existing trust to a skip-person beneficiary is a taxable distribution, not a direct skip, because the original funding transfer was the relevant event. Mislabeling it as a direct skip changes the form, payer, and timing.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the transfer been classified as a direct skip, taxable distribution, or taxable termination based on whether it is an outright transfer, a trust distribution, or an interest termination?
- [ ] For a direct skip, has the immediate tax trigger at transfer been recognized, with reporting on Form 709 for lifetime skips or Form 706 for at-death skips?
- [ ] For a taxable distribution, has the distribution-date trigger been identified, with reporting on Form 706-GS by the trustee on the trust's tax-year deadline?
- [ ] For a taxable termination, has the termination trigger been recognized as taxing the entire corpus passing to skip persons, reported on Form 706-GS and paid from trust assets?
- [ ] Has the precise triggering event been isolated before reporting, confirming the transfer, distribution, or termination has actually occurred?
- [ ] Has the responsible party been identified for each category, with the trust agreement checked for who bears the economic cost of the tax?
- [ ] Has the correct reporting form and deadline been confirmed, distinguishing Form 709, Form 706, and Form 706-GS and their respective due dates?
- [ ] Has the trust's GST exemption allocation history and inclusion ratio been reviewed before reporting, to confirm whether the trust is fully exempt, partially exempt, or fully nonexempt?
- [ ] Are uncertain classification, timing, payer, and reporting questions escalated to a qualified estate tax professional, CPA, or tax attorney rather than answered from general assumptions, since this guidance is not tax advice?
