# Original Issue Discount

Original issue discount (OID) is the excess of a bond's stated redemption price at maturity over its issue price. It represents interest that is baked into the bond's structure rather than paid periodically as coupon interest. Zero-coupon bonds, which pay no periodic interest, are the clearest example: they are issued at a deep discount and redeemed at face value, with the entire difference being OID. The tax rule requires the OID to be included in income as interest annually as it accrues, even though the taxpayer receives no cash payment until maturity or sale. This creates a mismatch between cash flow and taxable income that agents and taxpayers frequently mishandle.

Agents commonly miss that OID must be included annually regardless of cash receipt, that the constant yield method governs the accrual, that the rules apply to notes and other debt instruments not just bonds, and that acquisition premium and market discount interact with OID in different ways. The harm is a taxpayer who fails to report annual OID (understating income), or who reports the entire discount at sale or maturity (misstating the timing and character).

This skill covers original issue discount rules under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; OID rules are technical and the computations depend on the specific instrument, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Identify Whether The Instrument Has OID

Original issue discount exists when a debt instrument is issued for less than its stated redemption price at maturity. The issue price is the first price at which a substantial amount of the instrument is sold. The stated redemption price is the amount payable at maturity other than qualified stated interest (periodic coupon interest). For a zero-coupon bond issued at 600 dollars and redeemable at 1,000 dollars, the OID is 400 dollars. For a bond with a below-market coupon issued at a slight discount, the OID is smaller but still present.

Confirm whether the instrument was issued at a discount by comparing the issue price to the stated redemption price. Obtain the offering documents or the issuer's OID schedule. The rules apply to bonds, notes, debentures, and other debt instruments, including certain installment sale contracts and seller-financed mortgages. Do not assume OID applies only to publicly traded bonds; private notes and loans can also have OID if issued below the stated redemption price. The de minimis rule disregards OID below 0.25% of the stated redemption price times the number of years to maturity.

### Include OID Annually As It Accrues

OID must be included in gross income as interest annually as it accrues, regardless of the taxpayer's accounting method and regardless of whether any cash interest is received. This is the core rule and the one most often missed. A holder of a zero-coupon bond must report increasing OID income each year, even though no payment is received until maturity. The inclusion increases the holder's basis in the bond by the same amount, so the OID is not taxed again on sale or redemption.

The annual inclusion is reported as interest income on Schedule B. Brokers report OID on Form 1099-OID for covered instruments, and the taxpayer should reconcile the reported amount with the issuer's OID schedule. For instruments without broker reporting (private notes, certain bonds), the taxpayer must compute the OID inclusion using the constant yield method and report it without a 1099. Failing to report annual OID understates income and can create examination exposure, especially because the IRS receives the 1099-OID for reported instruments.

### Apply The Constant Yield Method

OID accrues under the constant yield method, which allocates the discount over the life of the instrument using a fixed yield to maturity. The accrual is not straight-line; it produces increasing dollar amounts of OID each year (because the accrued OID is added to the basis, and the fixed yield is applied to a growing base). The issuer typically provides an OID schedule showing the annual accrual amounts, and the taxpayer should use the issuer's schedule if available.

For instruments without a provided schedule, compute the OID using the constant yield formula: determine the yield to maturity at issuance, apply it to the adjusted issue price each period, and subtract any qualified stated interest paid. The computation is technical and may require financial calculator or spreadsheet tools. Do not approximate OID as straight-line unless the de minimis rule or a specific exception applies. The constant yield method ensures the interest accrual reflects the instrument's economics.

### Handle Acquisition Premium And Market Discount On OID Bonds

When an OID bond is purchased in the secondary market, the purchase price may create acquisition premium or market discount, each of which adjusts the OID inclusion. Acquisition premium exists when the purchase price exceeds the issue price plus accrued OID (but is still below the stated redemption price); it reduces the daily OID includible in income. Market discount exists when the purchase price is below the issue price plus accrued OID; it follows the market discount rules (ordinary income on sale to the extent of accrued market discount) rather than the OID rules for the discount portion.

Determine whether the secondary market purchase created acquisition premium or market discount, because the treatment differs. For acquisition premium, the OID inclusion is reduced by the premium amortization. For market discount, the discount is not currently included as OID but is recognized as ordinary income on sale or redemption (subject to the current inclusion election). Obtain the purchase price, the issue price, and the accrued OID at the purchase date to classify the position correctly. Misclassifying an acquisition premium bond as a market discount bond, or vice versa, applies the wrong rules.

### Distinguish OID From Qualified Stated Interest

A debt instrument may have both qualified stated interest (periodic coupon payments) and OID. Qualified stated interest is included in income when received (or accrued, for accrual-basis taxpayers) under the normal interest rules. OID is included under the OID rules as it accrues. The two are separate components, and both must be reported. For a bond with a small coupon issued at a discount, the coupon is qualified stated interest and the discount is OID.

Confirm whether the instrument has both components, and report each correctly. The stated redemption price excludes qualified stated interest, so the OID computation is based on the principal amount, not the total payments. Do not lump coupon interest and OID together; they follow different inclusion rules and are reported on different parts of the return.

### Apply Special Rules To Specific Instrument Types

OID rules apply to various instruments with specific modifications. Treasury bonds and notes issued at a discount follow the OID rules with broker reporting on 1099-OID. Corporate bonds follow the OID rules, with the issuer providing the schedule. Inflation-protected Treasury securities (TIPS) have OID-like treatment for the inflation adjustments to principal. Certificates of deposit with terms over one year may have OID. Seller-financed mortgages and installment sale notes may have OID if the stated interest rate is below the applicable federal rate.

Identify the instrument type and confirm whether special rules or reporting applies. For TIPS, the inflation adjustment to principal is included in income annually as OID-like interest, increasing basis. For below-market loans (including gift loans and employer loans), the forgone interest may be treated as OID and may have additional imputed transfer consequences. Do not apply a single OID treatment to all instruments; verify the specific rules for the instrument type.

### Reconcile Broker Reporting And Adjust For Partial-Year Holdings

For covered instruments, brokers report OID on Form 1099-OID. The reported amount should reflect the OID for the period the taxpayer held the instrument during the year. If the bond was purchased or sold mid-year, the 1099-OID should reflect only the accrual during the holding period, but the broker's accrual may need adjustment if the acquisition premium or other basis adjustments apply.

Reconcile the 1099-OID with the taxpayer's records and the issuer's OID schedule. If the taxpayer held the bond for only part of the year, confirm the reported OID reflects the correct period. If the bond was purchased at acquisition premium, reduce the OID inclusion accordingly. Report the correct OID amount even if it differs from the 1099-OID, and document the reconciliation. The IRS receives the 1099-OID, so material differences should be explainable.

## Common Traps

### Failing To Report Annual OID On Zero-Coupon Bonds

Zero-coupon bonds generate OID income each year with no cash payment. Taxpayers often overlook the annual inclusion and report nothing until maturity. This understates income annually.

### Treating The Entire Discount As Income At Sale Or Maturity

OID accrues annually. Reporting the full discount at maturity misstates the timing and may mischaracterize interest as capital gain. Include OID annually as interest.

### Confusing OID With Market Discount

OID arises at issuance; market discount arises from secondary market purchase below par. The inclusion rules differ. Classify the instrument before applying rules.

### Using Straight-Line Instead Of Constant Yield

OID accrues under the constant yield method, producing increasing annual amounts. Straight-line approximation misstates the annual inclusion and basis.

### Overlooking Acquisition Premium Reductions

OID bonds purchased in the secondary market at acquisition premium have reduced OID inclusion. Failing to apply the premium reduction overstates interest income.

### Forgetting OID On Private Notes And Below-Market Loans

OID rules apply to private debt instruments, including seller-financed notes and below-market loans, not just publicly traded bonds. These may have no broker reporting and require manual computation.

### Ignoring TIPS Inflation Adjustments

The inflation adjustment to TIPS principal is included annually as interest, increasing basis. Treating it as non-taxable or deferring it to maturity understates annual income.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the instrument been confirmed as having OID (issue price below stated redemption price), with the de minimis threshold checked?
- [ ] Is the annual OID accrual included in income as interest each year, regardless of cash receipt, using the constant yield method or the issuer's schedule?
- [ ] Has the OID inclusion been reconciled with the Form 1099-OID for covered instruments, and computed manually for instruments without broker reporting?
- [ ] For secondary market purchases, has the position been classified as acquisition premium (reduces OID), market discount (separate rules), or neither?
- [ ] Have qualified stated interest (coupons) and OID been separated and reported under their respective rules?
- [ ] For specific instrument types (Treasuries, TIPS, CDs, seller-financed notes, below-market loans), have the applicable special rules been confirmed?
- [ ] For partial-year holdings, has the OID been adjusted to reflect only the accrual during the taxpayer's holding period?
- [ ] Is the OID analysis documented with the issue price, redemption price, yield, annual accrual schedule, basis adjustments, and reconciliation with broker reporting?
