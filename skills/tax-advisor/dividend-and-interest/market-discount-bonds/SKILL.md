# Market Discount Bonds

When a bond is purchased in the secondary market for less than its stated redemption price (face value), the difference is market discount. Market discount arises because interest rates have risen, the issuer's credit has deteriorated, or other market factors have pushed the price below par. The tax treatment of market discount is distinct from original issue discount and from the general rule for bonds purchased at par. A taxpayer who sells or redeems a market discount bond must treat part of the gain as ordinary interest income rather than capital gain, and an election may require current accrual of the discount as interest income even before sale. Agents who treat market discount bonds like any other bond, or who confuse market discount with capital gain, misstate both the character and timing of the income.

Agents commonly miss that market discount converts to ordinary income on sale or redemption, that the accrual period and de minimis threshold matter, that a current inclusion election changes the timing, and that the rules interact with bond premium amortization. The harm is a taxpayer who reports a capital gain on a market discount bond sale when part is ordinary income, or who fails to make a beneficial current inclusion election.

This skill covers market discount bond rules under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; bond tax rules are technical and fact-specific, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Identify Market Discount And Its Source

Market discount exists when a bond is purchased in the secondary market for less than its stated redemption price at maturity. The market discount is the excess of the stated redemption price over the purchase price. It arises from market factors after issuance, typically rising interest rates or credit deterioration, which distinguish it from original issue discount (which exists at issuance because the coupon rate was below the market rate). The distinction matters because market discount and original issue discount follow different rules.

Confirm the bond was acquired in the secondary market (not at issuance) and that the purchase price was below the stated redemption price. If the bond was acquired at issuance at a discount, it is original issue discount, not market discount, and different rules apply. Obtain the purchase price, the stated redemption price, and the acquisition date to compute the market discount amount. For bonds purchased near par, check whether the discount is below the de minimis threshold.

### Apply The De Minimis Exception

A small amount of market discount is disregarded under the de minimis rule. If the market discount is less than one-quarter of one percent (0.25%) of the stated redemption price multiplied by the number of full years to maturity from the acquisition date, the discount is considered zero and the bond is treated as having no market discount. This avoids tracking tiny amounts of discount on bonds purchased near par.

Compute the de minimis threshold and compare it to the actual market discount. If the discount is below the threshold, no market discount rules apply, and any gain on sale is capital gain. If the discount exceeds the threshold, the full discount (not just the excess) is subject to the market discount rules. The de minimis rule is all-or-nothing; a discount just above the threshold is fully subject to the rules, not partially.

### Treat Gain As Ordinary Income To The Extent Of Accrued Market Discount

When a market discount bond is sold or redeemed, any gain is treated as ordinary interest income to the extent of the market discount that accrued during the holding period. Only the gain in excess of the accrued market discount is capital gain. This ordinary income treatment is the core rule of market discount and is the main difference from a bond purchased at par (where the entire gain would be capital gain).

The accrued market discount is computed under either the ratable accrual method (straight-line over the period from acquisition to maturity) or the constant yield method, with ratable accrual being the default. Compute the accrued market discount as of the sale date and compare it to the gain. The lesser of the gain or the accrued market discount is ordinary income; any excess gain is capital gain (long-term or short-term based on holding period). A loss on the sale is capital loss; the market discount does not convert a loss to ordinary. Do not treat the entire gain as capital gain when market discount has accrued.

### Consider The Current Inclusion Election

By default, market discount is not included in income until the bond is sold or redeemed. However, the taxpayer may elect to include market discount in income currently as it accrues, rather than deferring it to sale. The current inclusion election is made on the tax return for the first year it applies and is revocable only with IRS consent. If elected, the accrued market discount is reported as interest income each year, increasing basis by the same amount to avoid double taxation on sale.

The current inclusion election can be beneficial for taxpayers in low-income years who want to recognize interest income at a low rate, or for taxpayers who want to avoid the ordinary income character on sale (because the discount has already been included as interest). The cost is the administrative burden of annual accrual and the acceleration of income. Analyze whether the election is beneficial based on the taxpayer's current and expected future tax rates and the size of the market discount. The election applies to all market discount bonds acquired during or after the year of election; it cannot be made bond-by-bond.

### Account For Bond Premium Amortization

If a bond is purchased at a premium (above the stated redemption price), the taxpayer may elect to amortize the premium, which reduces the interest income each year and reduces basis. When a bond has both market discount and premium features (which is not typical for the same bond, but can arise in complex situations or across a portfolio), the rules interact. More commonly, a taxpayer with a portfolio of bonds may have some at a discount and some at a premium, and the treatment must be tracked separately for each.

For bonds purchased at a premium, confirm whether the taxpayer has elected premium amortization. If so, the premium reduces interest income and basis annually. Do not confuse premium amortization with market discount accrual; they are opposite adjustments. Track each bond's basis adjustments separately to compute gain or loss correctly on sale.

### Separate Market Discount From Acquisition Premium

Acquisition premium arises when a bond with original issue discount is purchased in the secondary market at a price that exceeds the issue price plus accrued original issue discount but is still below the stated redemption price. The acquisition premium reduces the daily OID includible in income. This is a different concept from market discount and from bond premium, and it applies only to OID bonds purchased in the secondary market.

Confirm whether the bond is an OID bond with acquisition premium, a market discount bond, or a premium bond, because the rules differ. The categorization depends on the issue price, the purchase price, and the stated redemption price. Obtain the bond's original issue details to classify it correctly. Misclassifying an acquisition premium OID bond as a market discount bond applies the wrong accrual rules.

### Report Market Discount On The Correct Forms

Accrued market discount recognized on sale or redemption is reported as interest income on Schedule B, and the capital gain or loss portion is reported on Form 8949 and Schedule D. If the current inclusion election is in effect, the annually accrued market discount is reported as interest income on Schedule B, and basis is adjusted accordingly. Brokers report market discount on Form 1099-OID or 1099-INT, and the amounts should be reconciled with the taxpayer's own computation.

Ensure the ordinary interest income portion and the capital gain portion are reported on the correct forms. Reporting the entire gain as capital gain on Form 8949 without separating the ordinary market discount portion understates ordinary income and overstates capital gain. Reconcile the broker reporting with the taxpayer's records and report the correct character for each portion.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Gain On A Discount Bond As Capital Gain

Gain on a market discount bond is ordinary interest income to the extent of accrued market discount. Only the excess is capital gain. Treating the full gain as capital misstates the character.

### Confusing Market Discount With Original Issue Discount

Market discount arises from secondary market purchase below par; OID arises at issuance. The accrual and inclusion rules differ. Classify the bond correctly before applying rules.

### Overlooking The De Minimis Exception

Small market discounts below 0.25% of redemption price times years to maturity are disregarded. Failing to check the threshold subjects tiny discounts to unnecessary tracking.

### Forgetting The Current Inclusion Election Option

The election to include market discount currently can be beneficial in low-income years or to avoid ordinary character on sale. Not considering it forgoes a planning opportunity.

### Misreporting The Ordinary Versus Capital Split

The ordinary interest portion goes on Schedule B; the capital gain portion goes on Form 8949. Reporting both on one form misstates income character.

### Ignoring Bond Premium Amortization Interactions

Premium bonds may have amortization elections that reduce interest and basis. These are separate from market discount and must be tracked per bond.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the bond been confirmed as a market discount bond (secondary market purchase below stated redemption price) rather than an OID bond or premium bond?
- [ ] Has the de minimis threshold (0.25% of redemption price times years to maturity) been checked, and is the bond either fully subject to or fully exempt from market discount rules?
- [ ] Has the accrued market discount been computed as of the sale date using the ratable or constant yield method, and has the gain been split into ordinary interest income and capital gain?
- [ ] Has the current inclusion election been considered, analyzing the benefit of current accrual versus deferral based on current and expected tax rates?
- [ ] For bonds with acquisition premium or original issue discount, has the correct classification and accrual method been applied separately from market discount?
- [ ] Has bond premium amortization been tracked separately for premium bonds, with interest and basis adjusted accordingly?
- [ ] Is the ordinary interest income portion reported on Schedule B and the capital gain portion on Form 8949, reconciled with the broker's 1099-OID or 1099-INT?
- [ ] Is the market discount analysis documented with the purchase price, redemption price, accrual computation, character split, and any election made?
