# Personal Holding Company Classification

The personal holding company (PHC) rules under Sections 541 through 547 impose a 20 percent penalty tax on the undistributed personal holding company income of a corporation that is, in substance, a vehicle for holding passive investments for a small number of individuals. The judgment problem is that the classification turns on two mechanical tests that look simple but are riddled with definitional traps: an ownership test and an income test. A corporation can trip the income test in a single profitable year for its investments even if its operating business is small, and once classified it owes the penalty tax on undistributed PHC income unless it distributes the income. Agents tend to focus on the 60 percent income threshold and miss how "adjusted ordinary gross income" and the related-deduction offsets actually move the ratio.

The harm is that PHC status is often invisible until an audit. A family-owned corporation that holds a building, receives royalty income from a license, or earns substantial interest on cash balances can cross the line without anyone noticing, because the operating business still files a normal Form 1120. The penalty tax is separate from the regular corporate tax and from the accumulated earnings tax, so a corporation can face multiple penalty layers at once. Agents frequently misclassify income (treating rents that include services as PHC rent, or forgetting that royalties are PHC income while gains on asset sales generally are not), miscount owners under the attribution rules, or assume that paying some dividends cures the problem when the distribution must equal the undistributed PHC income.

This skill covers federal US corporate tax classification of personal holding companies. It does not cover the accumulated earnings tax except to distinguish it, and it does not address S corporations, which are statutorily exempt. All percentages and the 20 percent rate reflect the current federal framework but must be verified against current law. This is issue-spotting and planning analysis, not tax advice; a qualified tax professional must be consulted for any specific classification determination.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Two-Prong Test In The Correct Order

A corporation is a PHC only if both tests are met. First, the ownership test under Section 542(a)(2): at any time during the last half of the taxable year, more than 50 percent in value of its outstanding stock must be owned, directly or indirectly, by five or fewer individuals. Second, the income test under Section 542(a)(1): at least 60 percent of the corporation's adjusted ordinary gross income for the year must be personal holding company income as defined in Section 543. Both must be satisfied; failing either means the corporation is not a PHC for that year.

The order of analysis matters because the ownership test is often the easier one to confirm or rule out. If the corporation is publicly held with dispersed ownership, the ownership test likely fails and the income analysis is unnecessary. If the corporation is family-owned or closely held, the ownership test usually passes and the entire analysis collapses into the income test. Agents should confirm ownership first, then focus analytical effort on the income computation.

### Master The Ownership Test And The Attribution Rules

The ownership test counts individuals only. "Five or fewer individuals" includes individuals, trusts, and certain estates, but partnerships and corporations are looked through to their underlying owners under the attribution rules of Section 544. Stock owned by a partnership is treated as owned proportionally by its partners. Stock owned by a corporation is attributed to its shareholders under specified rules. This look-through can concentrate ownership into fewer hands than the corporate cap table suggests.

The test is "more than 50 percent in value" of outstanding stock, measured at any time during the last half of the taxable year. Note value, not vote, though the two often coincide. A single moment of concentrated ownership in the second half of the year can satisfy the test. Agents must verify ownership across the relevant period, not just at year-end, and must apply attribution to family members under Section 318-style constructive ownership rules where incorporated by reference.

### Compute Adjusted Ordinary Gross Income Correctly

The income test denominator is "adjusted ordinary gross income" (AOGI), not gross income and not taxable income. The computation under Section 543(b) starts with gross income, subtracts certain items to arrive at "ordinary gross income," and then makes further adjustments. Ordinary gross income excludes capital gains and Section 1231 gains. AOGI then subtracts certain deductions directly allocable to specific classes of gross income, such as interest expense allocable to investment income, and it removes rents and royalties net of related deductions in certain computations.

The trap is treating the denominator as a simple figure. The adjustments can materially change the ratio. If a corporation has significant interest expense allocable to its interest income, the net figure reduces both the numerator and denominator in ways that can move the 60 percent threshold. The agent should build the AOGI computation step by step from the statutory formula rather than approximating from the income statement.

### Classify Personal Holding Company Income Precisely

PHC income under Section 543(a) includes dividends, interest, royalties (minus related deductions), rents (minus related deductions), annuities, and certain other items such as personal service contracts where the work is done by someone other than an employee, and certain mineral, oil, and gas royalties. Each category has definitional edges. Rents are excluded from PHC income if the corporation renders significant services to the occupant; rent from personal property can be PHC income depending on the use. Interest includes most interest but excludes certain types such as interest on installment obligations arising from inventory sales in some cases.

The agent must not lump income categories together. A corporation that earns rental income from a building it manages with its own employees may argue the rents are not PHC income because of the services component. Royalty income from licensing intellectual property is classic PHC income. The classification of each income stream drives the numerator, and small misclassifications across several streams can flip the 60 percent test.

### Understand What Is Excluded From PHC Income

Several items are expressly excluded from PHC income even though they look passive. Capital gains and Section 1231 gains are generally excluded from ordinary gross income entirely, so they do not help or hurt the ratio directly. Income from the active conduct of a trade or business that involves substantial services or manufacturing is generally not PHC income. Certain dividends received from controlled corporations and certain interest on state and local obligations may be excluded. The exclusions matter because a corporation can reduce its PHC income ratio by increasing its active business income or by structuring income into excluded categories legitimately.

The legitimate path is to grow genuine active business income or to restructure so that income is earned through active services rather than passive holding. The illegitimate path is to relabel passive income or to create sham services. The agent should distinguish real active business activity from cosmetic arrangements designed to dilute the PHC ratio.

### Apply The PHC Tax To Undistributed PHC Income

If a corporation is a PHC, the 20 percent tax under Section 541 applies to its "undistributed personal holding company income" for the year. This is computed on Schedule PH (Form 1120). The base is PHC income reduced by the dividends paid deduction, by the amount of certain deductions properly allocable to PHC income, by the net operating loss deduction, and by the income tax imposed on the corporation. Distributions of property can reduce undistributed PHC income, but the distribution must be a dividend under Section 316, and the timing and characterization matter.

The key planning lever is the dividends paid deduction. A PHC can eliminate the penalty tax by distributing its PHC income as dividends before the end of the year or within the time allowed for filing the return, including by using the deficiency dividend procedure under Section 547. But the distribution triggers shareholder-level tax, so the decision is a trade-off between the 20 percent penalty tax at the corporate level and the dividend tax at the shareholder level. The agent must model both sides.

### Distinguish PHC Status From Accumulated Earnings Tax And From Regular Tax

A PHC can simultaneously owe regular corporate tax under Section 11, the PHC tax under Section 541, and the accumulated earnings tax under Section 531. The PHC tax targets passive income concentration; the AET targets unreasonable accumulation of operating earnings. The credits differ: the AET credit is $250,000 generally but only $150,000 for a corporation that is also a PHC. An agent analyzing a closely held corporation should check both regimes, because solving one does not solve the other, and the interaction can be punitive.

## Common Traps

### Treating The 60 Percent Threshold As A Simple Gross Income Ratio

The denominator is adjusted ordinary gross income, not gross income. Capital gains are excluded, and allocable deductions adjust the figure. Approximating from the income statement produces wrong ratios.

### Misclassifying Rents That Include Significant Services

Rents are PHC income only net of deductions, and rents are excluded entirely if the corporation renders significant services to occupants. Misjudging the services component can wrongly include or exclude a major income stream.

### Forgetting That Royalties Are Classic PHC Income

Royalty income from licensing intellectual property, mineral rights, or franchise arrangements is personal holding company income. Agents sometimes treat royalties as business income because they appear in operations.

### Miscounting Owners By Ignoring Attribution Through Partnerships And Corporations

Stock held by a partnership or another corporation is attributed to underlying individuals. A cap table showing many entities can collapse to five or fewer individuals once attribution is applied.

### Assuming One Year Of Dividends Permanently Cures PHC Status

PHC status is determined annually. A dividend eliminates the undistributed PHC income for that year but does not prevent PHC status in a later year if the tests are again met. The classification must be re-tested each year.

### Confusing The PHC Tax With The Accumulated Earnings Tax

These are separate regimes. A corporation can be a PHC and still face the AET, and the AET credit is reduced to $150,000 for a PHC. Analyzing one does not dispose of the other.

### Overlooking That A Single Profitable Investment Year Can Trigger Status

A corporation with a modest operating business can cross the 60 percent line in a year when investment income spikes (for example, a large dividend or a high-interest environment). PHC status is not reserved for pure investment vehicles.

## Self-Check

- Has the ownership test been applied for the last half of the taxable year, with more than 50 percent in value held by five or fewer individuals after applying partnership, corporate, and family attribution rules?
- Has the income test been computed using adjusted ordinary gross income as the denominator, with capital gains excluded and allocable deductions applied, rather than gross income?
- Is each income stream classified correctly as PHC or non-PHC income, with attention to rents-with-services, royalties, annuities, and personal service contract income?
- Have capital gains and Section 1231 gains been excluded from the ordinary gross income computation rather than incorrectly included or excluded?
- If the corporation is a PHC, has undistributed PHC income been computed on Schedule PH with the dividends paid deduction, allocable deductions, NOL, and corporate income tax properly applied?
- Have the trade-offs between paying the 20 percent PHC penalty tax and distributing PHC income as taxable dividends been modeled at both corporate and shareholder levels?
- Has the agent separately checked for accumulated earnings tax exposure, recognizing the reduced $150,000 AET credit for a PHC and the possibility of multiple penalty layers?
- Is PHC status understood as an annual determination that must be re-tested each year rather than a permanent classification?
- Are all percentages, the 20 percent rate, and definitional thresholds verified against current law for the year at issue?
- Has the agent escalated to a qualified tax professional or CPA for any specific PHC classification or distribution decision, recognizing this analysis is not tax advice?
