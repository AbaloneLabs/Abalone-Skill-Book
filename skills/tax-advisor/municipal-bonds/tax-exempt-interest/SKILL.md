# Tax-Exempt Interest From Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds pay interest that is generally exempt from US federal income tax, which is their defining advantage. A muni yielding 3.5% tax-free can be worth more to a high-bracket taxpayer than a taxable bond yielding 5%, because the after-tax comparison favors the muni once the tax shield is priced in. But "tax-exempt" is not a blanket statement, and treating all municipal interest as universally tax-free leads to errors: some muni interest is subject to state tax, some is subject to the alternative minimum tax, and some is fully taxable. The value of the exemption depends entirely on the investor's tax bracket and jurisdiction.

The judgment problem is that agents often quote the tax-exempt yield as if it is automatically superior, without computing the taxable-equivalent yield against the investor's actual marginal rate, without checking whether the bond's interest is exempt at the state level, and without recognizing that placing munis inside a tax-advantaged account wastes the exemption entirely. They may also ignore that tax-exempt interest, while not itself taxed, can indirectly raise tax elsewhere by increasing the taxable portion of Social Security benefits or triggering the Medicare IRMAA surcharge. The harm is holding an asset whose tax benefit does not apply, or whose hidden interactions raise tax more than the exemption saves.

This skill applies to evaluating municipal bond interest, computing taxable-equivalent yield, assessing state and AMT treatment, deciding whether munis suit a given investor, and placing munis in the right account. It covers US federal treatment as a baseline framework. Tax rules and state conformity vary and change. This is not tax advice; consult a qualified tax professional or CPA before acting on municipal bond decisions.

## Core Rules

### Compute The Taxable-Equivalent Yield Against The Actual Bracket

The headline tax-exempt yield is meaningless without comparison to the investor's marginal tax bracket. The taxable-equivalent yield (TEY) is the yield a taxable bond would need to match the muni's after-tax return, calculated as the muni yield divided by (1 minus the marginal tax rate). A 3.5% muni for an investor in a 37% federal bracket has a TEY of about 5.6% (3.5 / 0.63), meaning a taxable bond would need to yield over 5.6% to match it after federal tax. The higher the bracket, the more valuable the exemption.

Always compute the TEY using the investor's actual combined marginal rate, including federal, state, and local taxes where the interest is also exempt. Do not assume munis are better just because they are tax-exempt; for an investor in a 12% bracket, a 3.5% muni has a TEY of only about 4.0%, and a taxable bond or even a high-yield savings account may beat it. The exemption only pays off when the bracket is high enough that the after-tax gap favors the muni.

### Check State Tax Treatment, Not Just Federal

Federal exemption is the headline, but most states also tax interest from bonds issued by other states, while exempting interest from in-state bonds. An investor in a high-tax state holding an out-of-state muni fund may find the interest subject to state income tax, eroding the benefit. Conversely, an in-state muni may be exempt from both federal and state tax, a "double tax-free" advantage that materially raises the effective TEY when the state rate is included.

Identify the bond's issuing state and the investor's residency state, then determine whether the interest is exempt at the state level. For muni bond funds rather than individual bonds, review the fund's state composition; a national muni fund will generate interest from many states, most of which is taxable to a resident of any single state, while a single-state fund targets in-state exemption. Factor the state rate into the TEY calculation. A double-tax-exempt in-state bond can be the better deal even at a lower headline yield.

### Identify Whether The Bond Is A Private Activity (AMT) Bond

Most municipal bonds are general obligation or essential-purpose revenue bonds whose interest is exempt from the regular federal tax and also from the alternative minimum tax. However, private activity bonds (PABs), which finance projects like airports, stadiums, hospitals, and housing that benefit non-governmental entities, pay interest that is exempt from regular tax but is a tax preference item for the AMT. For a taxpayer subject to the AMT, that interest becomes taxable under the AMT calculation, eliminating the federal exemption exactly when it matters.

Check whether a bond or fund holds private activity bonds before assuming full exemption. Fund fact sheets often disclose the percentage of AMT-subject interest. For taxpayers near or above the AMT exemption threshold, AMT bond interest can trigger or increase AMT liability, making a nominally tax-exempt bond effectively taxable. This is a silent trap because the interest looks exempt on the 1099 but reappears on the AMT form.

### Never Place Munis In A Tax-Advantaged Account

The entire value of a municipal bond is its tax exemption. Placing a muni inside a tax-deferred account (traditional IRA, 401(k)) converts the tax-exempt interest into tax-deferred growth that will be taxed as ordinary income on withdrawal, destroying the exemption. Placing it in a Roth account wastes the Roth shield on an asset that did not need shielding. Munis belong exclusively in taxable accounts where the exemption can operate.

Treat this as a hard rule of asset location. If a household holds munis, they must sit in the taxable bucket. The only exception is a muni trading at a deep discount where the market discount rules create ordinary income on the discount portion, but even then the coupon interest's exemption is only realized in a taxable account. Review any household holding munis in a retirement account as a likely error to be corrected.

### Recognize Indirect Tax Effects Of Tax-Exempt Interest

Tax-exempt interest is not taxed directly, but it is still income for several indirect purposes. It is included in the calculation that determines how much of Social Security benefits are taxable, so a retiree receiving Social Security may find that muni interest increases the taxable portion of their benefits. It is also counted in modified AGI for purposes of the Medicare Part B and D IRMAA surcharges, and for certain credit and deduction phaseouts. The interest is added back on the relevant worksheets even though it is not in taxable income.

Model the indirect effects before assuming munis are pure benefit. A retiree whose muni interest pushes them over an IRMAA threshold may pay a surcharge that exceeds the federal tax saved by the exemption. Similarly, the Social Security interaction can mean that a portion of the muni interest effectively gets taxed through the benefits calculation. These interactions are most consequential for retirees near the relevant thresholds.

### Verify The Bond Is Truly A Tax-Exempt Municipal Issue

Not every bond that sounds governmental is tax-exempt. Some obligations are federally taxable despite a governmental issuer, such as certain Build America Bonds (which pay taxable interest to the investor with a credit to the issuer), some tribal economic development bonds, and bonds that lose exemption due to arbitrage or other compliance failures. Conversely, some non-municipal instruments, like certain US Treasury obligations, are exempt from state but not federal tax, which is a different and smaller benefit.

Confirm the instrument's actual tax status from the official statement, the 1099-INT box, or the fund's tax information, rather than the name. The 1099-INT reports tax-exempt interest in a specific box, but the AMT portion is reported separately and the state-exempt portion requires the state composition. Do not infer exemption from the issuer's name or the fund's label; verify from the tax documents.

### Compare On An After-Tax, Risk-Adjusted Basis

Tax-exempt does not mean risk-free or higher-return. Municipal bonds carry credit risk (especially revenue bonds tied to a specific project), call risk (many munis are callable, limiting upside if rates fall), and liquidity risk. A muni's higher TEY may simply be compensation for lower credit quality or less liquidity, not a free tax arbitrage. Compare munis to taxable alternatives of comparable credit quality and duration, not to a generic Treasury, to judge whether the after-tax yield is genuinely attractive.

Evaluate the credit and call features alongside the yield. A high-TEY muni from a stressed issuer may not be a better deal than a lower-TEY high-grade taxable bond once default risk is considered. Insured munis or general obligation bonds from strong issuers are lower risk than non-rated revenue bonds. The tax exemption is one factor in the total return and risk picture, not the whole picture.

## Common Traps

### Quoting The Tax-Exempt Yield Without The Taxable-Equivalent Calculation

A 3.5% muni yield means nothing without the bracket. The trap is presenting it as automatically superior. Always compute the TEY against the actual marginal rate.

### Assuming All Muni Interest Is State Tax-Exempt

Most states tax out-of-state muni interest. The trap is treating federal exemption as universal exemption. Check the issuing state and residency.

### Holding Private Activity Bonds Without Checking AMT Exposure

PAB interest is an AMT preference item. The trap is assuming exemption holds under AMT. Review the fund's AMT-subject percentage.

### Putting Munis Inside A Tax-Advantaged Account

This destroys the exemption. The trap is location-blind bond buying. Munis belong only in taxable accounts.

### Ignoring Indirect Effects On Social Security And IRMAA

Tax-exempt interest still counts toward benefit taxation and IRMAA thresholds. The trap is treating it as invisible income. Model the worksheet interactions.

### Inferring Exemption From The Bond Or Fund Name

Names can mislead; some governmental bonds are taxable. The trap is trusting the label. Verify status from the 1099-INT and official statement.

### Comparing Munis Only To Treasuries

Munis carry more credit and call risk than Treasuries. The trap is treating the TEY spread as pure arbitrage. Compare to same-quality taxable bonds.
