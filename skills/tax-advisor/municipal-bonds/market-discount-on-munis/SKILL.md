# Market Discount On Municipal Bonds

A municipal bond bought at a discount promises two streams of return: the tax-exempt coupon interest, and the accretion of the discount as the bond moves toward par at maturity or is sold above the purchase price. The coupon interest retains its tax exemption, but the discount portion is governed by a separate and less favorable set of rules. Whether that discount is taxed, how it is taxed, and when it is recognized depends on whether the discount arose at original issuance (original issue discount, OID) or was created later by market price movement after issuance (market discount). Conflating the two is a common and costly error, because OID on a tax-exempt bond generally remains exempt, while market discount is taxable as ordinary income.

The judgment problem is that agents see "municipal bond at a discount" and assume the entire return is tax-exempt, ignoring that market discount creates ordinary taxable income on the discount portion. They fail to apply the de minimis exception that treats tiny discounts as capital gain, they mischaracterize accrued market discount as exempt interest, and they overlook the election to include market discount currently versus at disposition. The harm is a tax surprise: an investor who buys a muni at 90 expecting all tax-free income discovers at sale or maturity that the 10-point gain is ordinary income taxed at their full marginal rate, and that they may owe interest on underpayment because the income was not reported as it accrued.

This skill applies to evaluating discounted municipal bonds, distinguishing market discount from original issue discount, determining the tax treatment of the discount, applying the de minimis rule, and reporting gain on disposition. It covers US federal treatment as a baseline framework. Market discount rules are technical and interact with bond premium and OID rules. This is not tax advice; consult a qualified tax professional or CPA before acting on discounted muni positions.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Market Discount From Original Issue Discount

The tax treatment of a muni discount hinges on its origin. Original issue discount (OID) arises when a bond is issued at a price below its stated redemption price at maturity; for a tax-exempt muni, the OID accretion generally remains tax-exempt because it is treated as part of the bond's tax-exempt yield. Market discount arises when a bond that was issued at or near par later trades below par in the secondary market due to interest rate changes or credit deterioration; the market discount is a separate amount that is taxable, because it represents market-driven gain, not the bond's original exempt yield.

Establish the bond's original issue price and issue date first. If the bond was issued at a discount, the OID portion follows exempt treatment; if the discount exists because the price fell after issuance, it is market discount and is taxable. For a bond bought in the secondary market, the purchase price relative to the adjusted issue price (roughly the issue price plus prior OID accretion) determines whether market discount exists. Do not assume a discount is OID just because the bond is a muni; most deep discounts on seasoned munis are market discount and are taxable.

### Apply The De Minimis Exception Correctly

Market discount is not taxed as ordinary income if it is small enough to qualify for the de minimis exception. The rule treats market discount as zero (and any gain up to that amount as capital gain) if the discount is less than one-quarter of one percent (0.25%) of the bond's stated redemption price at maturity, multiplied by the number of full years remaining to maturity at acquisition. For a $10,000 par bond with 10 years to maturity, the de minimis threshold is $25 per year times 10, or $250; a discount at or below $250 is ignored for ordinary income purposes.

Calculate the de minimis threshold for each discounted muni before concluding the discount is taxable. A discount just above the threshold flips entirely to ordinary income treatment on the full discount, not just the excess, so the threshold is a cliff rather than a gradual phase-in. This makes precise calculation important: a bond bought at a 0.24% annualized discount is capital gain, while one at 0.26% is fully ordinary income on the discount. Do not approximate; compute the exact threshold against the actual purchase price and remaining maturity.

### Recognize That Market Discount Is Ordinary Income At Disposition

When market discount is non-de minimis, the portion of any gain on sale or redemption equal to the accrued market discount is treated as ordinary income, not capital gain, regardless of how long the bond was held. The ordinary income is capped at the total market discount; any gain beyond the discount can be capital gain. This means a bond held for years, which an investor might assume produces long-term capital gain, generates ordinary income on the discount portion at the investor's full marginal rate, often 32-37%, rather than the preferential 15-20% capital gains rate.

Model the disposition tax before buying a market-discount muni. A 5-point market discount on a $10,000 bond is $500 taxed as ordinary income at sale or maturity; at a 35% bracket that is $175 of tax that would not exist on an OID or par muni. The after-tax total return of a market-discount muni can be lower than a comparable par bond once the ordinary income on the discount is netted, even if the headline yield looks higher. Compare discounted and par munis on an after-tax basis.

### Decide Between Current Inclusion And Deferral Election

By default, market discount is not taxed until disposition; it accrues silently and is recognized as ordinary income when the bond is sold, called, or matures. A taxpayer can instead elect to include market discount in income currently as it accrues, paying tax each year on the accrued amount. The current-inclusion election is irrevocable for that bond and applies to all market discount bonds the taxpayer holds then or acquires later. Current inclusion can simplify reporting and align tax with economic income, but it forgoes deferral and requires annual computation.

Evaluate which election fits the investor. Current inclusion avoids a lump of ordinary income at maturity and matches tax to accrual, useful for investors who want predictable annual reporting or who expect to be in a similar or lower bracket later. The default deferral is preferable when deferral has value and the investor can plan for the eventual ordinary income hit. The election is binding, so model both paths before choosing; for most buy-and-hold muni investors, deferral is the default and the election is rarely made, but it must be a conscious decision, not an oversight.

### Account For Accrual Methods And The Constant Yield Framework

Market discount accrues under a constant-yield method, not linearly, based on the bond's yield to maturity at acquisition and the adjusted basis. The accrued market discount each year is the excess of the bond's adjusted issue price (computed at the acquisition yield) over the basis, grown at the constant yield minus any coupon payments. This produces an accelerating accrual pattern for deep-discount bonds. The exact accrual matters for the current-inclusion election and for determining how much ordinary income is recognized on a partial sale or call.

Compute or obtain the accrual schedule rather than estimating linearly. Brokerage 1099-OID and 1099-B reporting often provides accrued market discount information, but the taxpayer remains responsible for accuracy, especially for bonds bought between coupon dates or with odd first-period accruals. For partial sales, the accrued market discount must be allocated to the portion sold. Rely on the constant-yield computation; linear approximation can materially misstate the ordinary income portion in early or late years.

### Handle Bond Premium And Market Discount Interaction

Many seasoned munis trade at a premium when rates fall, and the bond premium rules allow a taxpayer to amortize premium against tax-exempt interest (reducing basis) or elect not to amortize. When a bond is bought at a premium and later has market discount characteristics, or when premium and discount rules interact across a portfolio, the basis adjustments must be tracked carefully. Premium amortization on a tax-exempt bond reduces basis but cannot create a deduction; it only reduces the otherwise-exempt interest. Misapplying premium rules alongside market discount can distort the ordinary income calculation at disposition.

Track basis adjustments from premium amortization separately from market discount accruals. If a bond was bought at a premium and amortized, its adjusted basis may be below the purchase price, which affects whether a later price decline creates market discount relative to the adjusted issue price. Maintain the basis history for each muni position so that at disposition the ordinary income (market discount) and capital gain (the rest) are computed on correctly adjusted figures. Do not let premium and discount adjustments cancel informally; apply each rule in sequence.

### Coordinate With Call Features And Early Redemption

Many munis are callable before maturity, and a call can force disposition earlier than the investor planned. When a market-discount muni is called at the call price, the accrued market discount up to the call date is ordinary income, and any additional gain or loss depends on the call price versus adjusted basis. Because calls typically happen when rates fall (the issuer refinances), the investor may be repaid at the worst time and still owe ordinary income tax on the discount, with reinvestment at lower prevailing yields. The call feature converts a tax-deferred accrual into an immediate taxable event.

Assess call risk alongside the market discount analysis. A callable market-discount muni carries both tax risk (forced ordinary income recognition) and reinvestment risk (proceeds reinvested at lower yields). Price the call option into the after-tax yield comparison; a non-callable or low-call-risk muni may be superior even at a lower headline yield. Identify the call schedule and call price, and model the outcome if the bond is called at the earliest date, before relying on the stated maturity for the discount accrual.

## Common Traps

### Assuming All Muni Discount Income Is Tax-Exempt

Only OID on a tax-exempt muni is exempt; market discount is ordinary income. The trap is conflating the two. Determine the discount's origin from issue price and date.

### Skipping The De Minimis Calculation

The 0.25%-of-par-times-years threshold is a cliff. The trap is approximating. Compute the exact threshold against the actual purchase price and remaining maturity.

### Treating Market Discount Gain As Capital Gain

Non-de minimis market discount is ordinary income at disposition, even if held long-term. The trap is applying long-term capital gains rates. Use ordinary rates on the discount portion.

### Forgetting The Current-Inclusion Election Is Irrevocable

Electing to include market discount currently binds all present and future market discount bonds. The trap is electing without modeling both paths. Treat the election as a permanent portfolio-level decision.

### Estimating Accrual Linearly Instead Of Constant-Yield

Market discount accrues on a constant-yield, accelerating schedule. The trap is straight-line approximation. Use the constant-yield computation from the 1099 or a schedule.

### Ignoring Call Risk That Forces Early Ordinary Income

A called market-discount muni triggers ordinary income recognition early plus reinvestment at lower yields. The trap is planning to maturity. Model the earliest call outcome.

### Letting Bond Premium And Discount Rules Cancel Informally

Premium amortization and market discount are separate basis adjustments applied in sequence. The trap is netting them by hand. Track each adjustment to adjusted basis.
