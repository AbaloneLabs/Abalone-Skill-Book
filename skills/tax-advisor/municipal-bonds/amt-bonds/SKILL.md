# AMT Bonds And Private Activity Bonds

Most municipal bonds pay interest that is exempt from both the regular federal income tax and the alternative minimum tax (AMT). A distinct subset, called private activity bonds (PABs), pays interest that is exempt from the regular tax but is classified as a tax preference item for the AMT. For the majority of taxpayers who are not subject to the AMT, this distinction is invisible and the interest remains effectively tax-free. But for a taxpayer who is in or near the AMT, that interest becomes taxable under the AMT calculation, silently eliminating the very benefit the bond was bought for. The same bond can be tax-exempt for one investor and taxable for another.

The judgment problem is that agents treat "tax-exempt municipal bond" as a uniform category and ignore the AMT bifurcation. They recommend AMT bonds to high-income investors without checking whether the investor is subject to the AMT, or they fail to disclose the AMT-subject portion of a bond fund's distributions. The harm is concentrated and severe for affected taxpayers: a portfolio of private activity bonds can push a taxpayer into the AMT or increase an existing AMT liability, converting a 3.5% tax-exempt yield into a 3.5% yield taxed at the 26% or 28% AMT rate, which may be worse than a plain taxable bond once the preference is priced in.

This skill applies to evaluating private activity bonds, identifying AMT-subject interest, assessing whether an investor should hold AMT bonds, and reviewing a muni portfolio for AMT exposure. It covers US federal AMT treatment as a baseline framework. AMT rules, exemption amounts, and thresholds change over time. This is not tax advice; AMT analysis is fact-specific, and a qualified tax professional or CPA must be consulted.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Private Activity Bonds From Public Purpose Bonds

The AMT distinction turns on the bond's purpose. General obligation bonds, issued by a state or municipality backed by its taxing power, and essential-purpose revenue bonds funding core governmental functions (water, sewer, roads, public schools), pay interest that is exempt from both regular tax and the AMT. Private activity bonds, defined by specific tests in the tax code, finance projects that benefit non-governmental entities, such as airports, stadiums, hospitals, housing, industrial development, and student loans. PAB interest is a tax preference item for the AMT.

Identify the bond type before assuming full exemption. The official statement and the 1099-INT disclose whether interest is AMT-subject. For bond funds, the annual tax letter reports the percentage of distributions that are AMT preference items. Do not treat all munis as AMT-free; a fund labeled "high-yield municipal" often holds a large PAB component precisely because higher yields come from these private-purpose issues.

### Determine Whether The Investor Is Actually Subject To The AMT

The AMT only matters for taxpayers whose alternative minimum taxable income, after the AMT exemption, exceeds the regular tax under the AMT's two-tier rate structure (26% and 28%). Many taxpayers are not subject to the AMT at all because their income and preferences fall below the exemption, which phases out at higher incomes. For these taxpayers, PAB interest remains effectively tax-free and the AMT distinction is harmless. The decision to hold AMT bonds depends entirely on whether the investor is in, near, or far from the AMT.

Assess the investor's AMT status using recent tax returns (Form 6251) or a projection. If the investor is consistently below the AMT threshold with margin, AMT bonds can be held without consequence. If the investor is in or near the AMT, PAB interest is effectively taxed at 26-28% under the AMT, and the bond's advantage evaporates. The recommendation must be conditional on the investor's actual AMT position, not generic.

### Quantify The Effective Yield Under AMT

When PAB interest is taxed under the AMT, the bond's effective yield drops. A 4% PAB for an investor in the 28% AMT bracket yields about 2.9% after AMT (4% times 0.72), which may be below the after-tax yield of a comparable non-AMT muni or even a taxable bond of similar risk. The higher headline yield of PABs exists partly to compensate for the AMT risk, but that compensation only helps investors who are not subject to the AMT; for those who are, the higher yield is consumed by the AMT tax.

Compute the after-AMT yield and compare it to alternatives. For an AMT-exposed investor, a non-AMT muni at a lower headline yield often produces a higher after-tax return because its interest is genuinely exempt. For a non-AMT investor, the PAB's higher yield is pure advantage. The analysis must be specific to the investor's bracket and AMT status, because the same bond flips from attractive to unattractive depending on who holds it.

### Review Bond Funds For Their AMT Percentage

Individual investors often hold municipal bonds through funds rather than individual issues. A fund's distributions are a blend of its holdings, so a national or high-yield muni fund may distribute a significant AMT-subject percentage, sometimes 20% or more. This is disclosed in the fund's year-end tax information letter, which breaks out the AMT preference portion separately from the federally tax-exempt portion. Ignoring this breakdown treats the entire distribution as AMT-free, which is wrong for funds with PAB holdings.

Obtain the fund's AMT percentage before assuming full exemption. For AMT-exposed investors, a high AMT percentage in a fund can create the same liability as holding individual PABs, but spread across many small positions and thus easy to overlook. Prefer AMT-free muni funds (often labeled as such) for investors who are AMT-sensitive, and reserve higher-yielding PAB-heavy funds for investors safely below the AMT threshold.

### Consider The Interaction With Other AMT Preference Items

PAB interest is one of several AMT preference items; others include certain depletion and intangible drilling cost deductions, and the AMT calculation also disallows some regular-tax deductions and applies different depreciation. An investor's total AMT exposure is the sum of all preferences and adjustments, so adding PAB interest can be the item that tips a taxpayer from just under to just over the AMT threshold. The marginal impact of a dollar of PAB interest can therefore be larger than the headline AMT rate suggests, because it can activate AMT on other income too.

Evaluate PAB holdings in the context of the investor's full AMT picture, not in isolation. An investor near the threshold should treat PAB interest as potentially taxing other income at the margin, making the effective cost higher than 28%. For investors far below the threshold, the marginal effect is zero. This is why the same bond can be fine for one investor and costly for another with nearly identical income but a different preference profile.

### Time And Size AMT Bond Holdings Relative To The Exemption

The AMT exemption amount and its phaseout create zones where PAB interest is more or less damaging. Below the exemption, PAB interest is absorbed tax-free. In the phaseout range, the effective AMT rate on preference items rises above the nominal 26-28% because each dollar of preference reduces the exemption, a hidden rate increase. Above the phaseout, the nominal rate applies cleanly. Knowing where the investor sits relative to these zones informs whether and how much PAB exposure is tolerable.

Project the investor's AMT position over the holding period, not just the current year, because income and law changes can move them across thresholds. Size PAB holdings conservatively if the investor is near a threshold, since a modest amount of PAB interest can be harmless while a large amount triggers liability. For investors who want muni exposure but must limit AMT risk, non-AMT munis or AMT-free funds are the safer structural choice.

### Disclose The AMT Status Explicitly In Any Recommendation

Because the AMT effect is conditional and invisible on the face of the 1099, any recommendation involving private activity bonds or PAB-heavy funds must explicitly state the AMT status and the condition under which the benefit holds. A recommendation that says only "tax-exempt municipal bond" without flagging the AMT preference is incomplete and can mislead an AMT-exposed investor into holding a bond whose benefit does not apply to them. The disclosure is the protection against silent harm.

State, for each muni holding or fund: whether it contains PABs, the AMT-subject percentage of distributions, and the condition (investor not subject to AMT) under which the exemption is realized. Recommend Form 6251 review or professional AMT projection for any investor whose income could place them in AMT range. Make the conditional nature of the benefit explicit rather than burying it.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Municipal Bonds As AMT-Free

Only public-purpose bonds are AMT-free; PABs are preference items. The trap is ignoring the bond's purpose. Identify PABs from the official statement and 1099.

### Recommending AMT Bonds Without Checking The Investor's AMT Status

The same bond is tax-free for some and taxable for others. The trap is generic recommendation. Assess AMT status via Form 6251 or projection.

### Ignoring The AMT Percentage In Bond Funds

High-yield and national muni funds often hold PABs. The trap is treating fund distributions as fully exempt. Check the year-end AMT breakdown.

### Forgetting The Phaseout Raises The Effective AMT Rate

In the exemption phaseout, preference items are taxed above the nominal 28%. The trap is using the headline rate. Locate the investor relative to the phaseout zone.

### Overlooking That PAB Interest Can Tip A Taxpayer Into AMT

Adding preference items can activate AMT on other income. The trap is evaluating PABs in isolation. Consider the full preference and adjustment picture.

### Quoting The Headline Yield Without The After-AMT Comparison

A 4% PAB is 2.9% after 28% AMT. The trap is presenting the gross yield. Compute the after-AMT yield and compare to non-AMT alternatives.

### Assuming AMT Status Is Stable Across Years

Income and law changes move taxpayers across thresholds. The trap is a single-year snapshot. Project AMT position over the holding period.
