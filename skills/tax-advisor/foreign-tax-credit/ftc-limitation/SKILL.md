# FTC Limitation

The foreign tax credit limitation under Section 904 caps the foreign tax credit at the U.S. tax attributable to foreign source income. Without the limitation, a taxpayer could use foreign taxes on foreign income to offset U.S. tax on U.S. income, effectively sheltering U.S. income with foreign credits. The limitation is computed separately for each category of income and requires allocating and apportioning expenses between U.S. and foreign source income. An agent who applies the FTC without the limitation, or who fails to allocate expenses, will overstate the credit and expose the taxpayer to an IRS adjustment.

Agents commonly miss that the limitation is per-category (not overall), that expenses must be allocated and apportioned to reduce foreign source income, that the limitation can be zero (if foreign source taxable income is zero or negative), and that overall domestic losses and net operating losses interact with the limitation. The harm is a taxpayer who claims a credit that exceeds the allowed limitation.

This skill covers the foreign tax credit limitation under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the limitation rules are complex, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Per-Category Limitation

The FTC limitation is computed separately for each statutory category of income. The categories include general category income (active business income, foreign branch income) and passive category income (dividends, interest, royalties). The per-category limitation ensures that foreign taxes on one category cannot offset U.S. tax on another category. Each category has its own foreign source income, its own foreign taxes, and its own limitation.

Apply the per-category limitation. For each category, compute the limitation as the U.S. tax before the FTC multiplied by (foreign source taxable income in the category / worldwide taxable income). The credit in each category is the lesser of the foreign taxes paid in that category or the limitation for that category. Foreign taxes on passive income cannot offset U.S. tax on general category income. Track the income, taxes, and limitation by category. The per-category rule prevents cross-substitution of credits and is the core of the limitation computation.

### Compute The Limitation Formula

The FTC limitation formula is: the limitation equals the U.S. tax before the FTC multiplied by (foreign source taxable income in the category / worldwide taxable income). The U.S. tax before the FTC is the total U.S. tax liability computed without the FTC. The foreign source taxable income is the foreign source gross income in the category minus the expenses allocated and apportioned to that income. The worldwide taxable income is the total taxable income (U.S. and foreign).

Compute the limitation using the formula. Determine the U.S. tax before the FTC (the total tax liability without the credit). Determine the foreign source taxable income in the category (foreign source gross income minus allocated expenses). Determine the worldwide taxable income. Apply the formula: limitation = U.S. tax before FTC multiplied by (foreign source taxable income / worldwide taxable income). The credit is the lesser of the foreign taxes or the limitation. If the foreign source taxable income is zero or negative, the limitation is zero, and no credit is allowed (the foreign taxes are carried over). Track the computation carefully.

### Allocate And Apportion Expenses To Foreign Source Income

The foreign source taxable income in each category is the foreign source gross income minus the expenses allocated and apportioned to that income. Expenses must be allocated and apportioned between U.S. and foreign source income based on the relationship of the expense to the income. Direct expenses (expenses directly related to a class of income) are allocated to that class. Indirect expenses (general expenses not directly related to a specific class) are apportioned based on a reasonable method (such as gross income or assets).

Allocate and apportion the expenses. Direct expenses (expenses directly related to foreign source income, such as foreign business expenses) are allocated to the foreign source income. Indirect expenses (general interest, administrative expenses) are apportioned between U.S. and foreign source income based on the tax regulations. Interest expense is apportioned based on the assets method or the modified gross income method. The allocation and apportionment reduce the foreign source taxable income, which reduces the limitation. A taxpayer who does not allocate expenses overstates the foreign source taxable income and the limitation. Follow the IRS allocation and apportionment regulations.

### Handle A Zero Or Negative Limitation

If the foreign source taxable income in a category is zero or negative (because expenses exceed the foreign source gross income), the limitation for that category is zero. No FTC is allowed for that category in that year. The foreign taxes paid in that category are carried back one year and forward ten years (as discussed in the carryback/carryforward skill). A zero limitation is common for taxpayers with high expenses or losses in a category.

Handle the zero or negative limitation. If the foreign source taxable income is zero or negative, the limitation is zero, and no credit is allowed. The foreign taxes are carried over. This can happen when a taxpayer has a net operating loss, high interest expense apportioned to foreign income, or overall foreign losses. Track the carryover for future years. A taxpayer who claims a credit despite a zero limitation over-claims and faces an adjustment. Confirm the limitation is positive before claiming the credit.

### Handle Overall Domestic Loss

An overall domestic loss (ODL) occurs when the U.S. source taxable income is negative (a U.S. loss) and the foreign source taxable income is positive. The ODL reduces the foreign source taxable income in future years, which reduces the future FTC limitation. The ODL is recaptured over 10 years, reducing the foreign source income by a portion each year. This prevents the taxpayer from benefiting from the U.S. loss (which reduced worldwide income) while also claiming a full FTC on the foreign income.

Handle the overall domestic loss. If the U.S. source taxable income is negative and the foreign source taxable income is positive, an ODL arises. The ODL is recaptured over 10 years (2% per year for the first 5 years and the remainder in year 10, or under a similar schedule), reducing the foreign source taxable income in future years. This reduces the future FTC limitation. Track the ODL and the recapture. The ODL recapture ensures the U.S. loss is not double-counted (reducing worldwide income while preserving the FTC). A taxpayer who ignores the ODL recapture overstates the future limitation.

### Handle Overall Foreign Loss

An overall foreign loss (OFL) occurs when the foreign source taxable income is negative (a foreign loss) and the U.S. source taxable income is positive. The OFL is recaptured in future years when the taxpayer has foreign source gains. The OFL recapture recharacterizes a portion of future foreign source income as U.S. source, which increases the U.S. tax and reduces the foreign source income for the FTC limitation. This prevents the taxpayer from benefiting from the foreign loss while preserving the U.S. tax base.

Handle the overall foreign loss. If the foreign source taxable income is negative and the U.S. source taxable income is positive, an OFL arises. The OFL is recaptured in future years when the taxpayer has foreign source gains. The recapture recharacterizes future foreign source income as U.S. source (up to the OFL amount), which reduces the foreign source income for the FTC limitation and increases the U.S. source income. Track the OFL and the recapture. The OFL recapture ensures the foreign loss is not double-counted (reducing worldwide income while preserving the FTC base). A taxpayer who ignores the OFL recapture misstates the future limitation.

## Common Traps

### Applying The FTC Without The Per-Category Limitation

The limitation is per-category. Foreign taxes on one category cannot offset U.S. tax on another. Compute the limitation separately for each category.

### Failing To Allocate And Apportion Expenses

Expenses must be allocated and apportioned between U.S. and foreign source income. Failing to allocate overstates the foreign source taxable income and the limitation.

### Claiming A Credit With A Zero Limitation

If the foreign source taxable income is zero or negative, the limitation is zero. No credit is allowed. The foreign taxes are carried over.

### Ignoring Overall Domestic Loss Recapture

An overall domestic loss reduces the future foreign source taxable income and the FTC limitation. Track the ODL recapture over 10 years.

### Ignoring Overall Foreign Loss Recapture

An overall foreign loss is recaptured in future years, recharacterizing foreign source income as U.S. source. Track the OFL recapture.

### Using Worldwide Taxable Income Incorrectly In The Formula

The worldwide taxable income is the total taxable income (U.S. and foreign). Using only U.S. income or only foreign income misstates the fraction and the limitation.

### Overlooking The Interaction With Net Operating Losses

A net operating loss can reduce the worldwide taxable income and the U.S. tax before the FTC, affecting the limitation. Model the NOL interaction.
