# FTC Carryback and Carryforward

When a taxpayer's foreign taxes paid exceed the foreign tax credit limitation for a category, the excess foreign taxes cannot be credited in the current year. Instead, they are carried back one year and carried forward ten years. The carryover is tracked by category and can be used in a future year when the limitation exceeds the current-year foreign taxes. An agent who ignores the carryover, or who applies it to the wrong category or year, will cause the taxpayer to lose valuable credits.

Agents commonly miss that the carryover is per-category (excess in one category cannot be used in another), that the carryback is one year and the carryforward is ten years, that the carried-over credits are used after current-year credits, and that expired credits are lost permanently. The harm is a taxpayer who loses credits due to expiration or misapplication.

This skill covers the foreign tax credit carryback and carryforward under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the carryover rules have specific timing and category requirements, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Understand The One-Year Carryback And Ten-Year Carryforward

Excess foreign taxes (foreign taxes paid that exceed the FTC limitation for a category) are carried back one year and carried forward ten years. The carryback is applied first: the excess is carried back to the prior year, and if the prior year has unused limitation (the limitation exceeded the foreign taxes in the prior year), the excess can be credited in the prior year (requiring an amended return). If the carryback cannot fully absorb the excess, the remainder is carried forward ten years.

Apply the carryback and carryforward. Excess foreign taxes are first carried back one year. If the prior year has unused limitation (the FTC limitation exceeded the foreign taxes used in the prior year), the excess can be credited in the prior year, requiring an amended return for that year. If the carryback cannot absorb the excess (or the prior year has no unused limitation), the remainder is carried forward ten years. The carryforward can be used in any of the ten future years when the limitation exceeds the current-year foreign taxes. Track the excess, the carryback, and the carryforward by year.

### Track The Carryover By Category

The carryover is tracked by statutory category (general category, passive category). Excess foreign taxes in one category cannot be carried over to or used in another category. The general category excess is carried back and forward within the general category. The passive category excess is carried back and forward within the passive category. The per-category tracking prevents cross-substitution of credits.

Track the carryover by category. Excess foreign taxes in the general category are carried back and forward within the general category. Excess in the passive category is carried back and forward within the passive category. The carryover cannot move between categories. A taxpayer with excess in the passive category and unused limitation in the general category cannot use the passive excess in the general category. Track the carryover separately for each category on Form 1116 (for individuals) or Form 1118 (for corporations). The per-category tracking is critical for applying the carryover correctly.

### Apply The Carryover After Current-Year Credits

In a future year, the FTC is computed using current-year foreign taxes first, then the carried-over foreign taxes from prior years. The current-year foreign taxes are applied to the limitation first. If the limitation exceeds the current-year foreign taxes, the unused limitation can absorb the carried-over foreign taxes (on a first-in, first-out basis, using the oldest carryover first). The carried-over credits are used after the current-year credits.

Apply the carryover after the current-year credits. In a future year, compute the FTC limitation for the category. Apply the current-year foreign taxes to the limitation first. If the limitation exceeds the current-year foreign taxes, the unused limitation can absorb the carried-over foreign taxes. Use the oldest carryover first (first-in, first-out). For example, if the current-year limitation is 20,000 dollars, the current-year foreign taxes are 15,000 dollars, and the carryover from two years ago is 8,000 dollars, the taxpayer credits 15,000 dollars of current-year taxes and 5,000 dollars of the carryover (the oldest), leaving 3,000 dollars of carryover to be used in future years or to expire. Track the order of the carryover.

### Handle The Expiration Of Carryforwards

Carryforward foreign taxes expire after ten years. Credits that are not used within the ten-year carryforward period are lost permanently; they cannot be carried forward further or recovered. The expiration is tracked by year: the excess from year 1 expires at the end of year 11. The first-in, first-out rule ensures the oldest credits are used first, minimizing expiration.

Track the expiration of carryforwards. Excess foreign taxes from a given year must be used within ten years (carried forward). Credits not used within the ten-year period expire and are lost. The first-in, first-out rule (using the oldest carryover first) minimizes expiration by using the credits closest to expiration before newer credits. Track the age of each carryover batch. A taxpayer with large carryforwards should plan to use them before expiration, potentially by accelerating foreign source income or deferring U.S. deductions to increase the limitation. The expiration is permanent; expired credits cannot be recovered.

### Handle The Carryback Amended Return

The carryback to the prior year requires an amended return (Form 1040-X for individuals) if the prior year has unused limitation. The taxpayer files the amended return to claim the additional FTC for the prior year, using the excess foreign taxes from the current year. The amended return must be filed within the applicable statute of limitations (generally three years from the original filing date or two years from the payment date, whichever is later).

Handle the carryback amended return. If the prior year has unused limitation, the excess foreign taxes can be carried back and claimed on an amended return for the prior year. File Form 1040-X (for individuals) or the equivalent for corporations, claiming the additional FTC. The amended return must be filed within the statute of limitations. If the statute has expired, the carryback cannot be used, and the excess is carried forward only. Track the statute of limitations for the prior year. The carryback is optional; the taxpayer can elect to forgo the carryback and carry forward only (the election is made on the current-year return).

### Manage The Election To Forgo The Carryback

The taxpayer can elect to forgo the carryback and carry the excess forward only. This election is made on the tax return for the year the excess arises. Forgoing the carryback may be beneficial if the prior year has no unused limitation (the carryback would be wasted) or if the taxpayer prefers to simplify the filing (no amended return). Once made, the election is binding for that year.

Manage the election to forgo the carryback. If the prior year has no unused limitation, the carryback would be wasted, and the taxpayer may elect to forgo it and carry forward only. The election is made on the current-year return. If the prior year has unused limitation, the carryback may be valuable, and the taxpayer should not forgo it. Model the benefit of the carryback before deciding. The election is binding for that year and cannot be changed. Document the election and the rationale.

## Common Traps

### Mixing Carryover Categories

The carryover is per-category. Excess in the general category cannot be used in the passive category, and vice versa. Track the carryover separately by category.

### Using Current-Year Credits Before Carryover Incorrectly

Current-year foreign taxes are applied to the limitation first, then the carryover. Applying the carryover first can waste current-year credits that would expire if not used (though current-year credits do not expire until carried over themselves).

### Letting Carryforwards Expire

Carryforwards expire after ten years. Credits not used within the period are lost permanently. Track the age of the carryover and plan to use it before expiration.

### Forgetting The Carryback Amended Return

The carryback requires an amended return if the prior year has unused limitation. Failing to file the amended return wastes the carryback benefit.

### Ignoring The First-In, First-Out Rule

The oldest carryover is used first. Using newer carryover before older carryover can cause the older credits to expire unnecessarily.

### Overlooking The Election To Forgo The Carryback

The taxpayer can elect to forgo the carryback. If the prior year has no unused limitation, forgoing the carryback simplifies the filing. Model the benefit before deciding.

### Failing To Track The Carryover On Form 1116 Or 1118

The carryover is tracked on Form 1116 (individuals) or Form 1118 (corporations). Failing to track the carryover on the form can cause the credits to be lost or misapplied.
