# FTC Calculation

The foreign tax credit (FTC) under Section 901 allows a U.S. taxpayer to credit foreign income taxes paid against their U.S. federal income tax liability, mitigating double taxation of foreign source income. The credit is dollar-for-dollar, making it more valuable than a deduction. However, the FTC is subject to a limitation that prevents crediting foreign taxes on foreign income against U.S. tax on U.S. income. The calculation requires separating income into categories, sourcing the income, and applying the limitation. An agent who applies the FTC as a simple credit without the limitation and categorization will overstate the benefit.

Agents commonly miss that the FTC is subject to a limitation (it cannot exceed the U.S. tax on the foreign income), that income must be separated into statutory categories, that not all foreign taxes are creditable, and that the choice between the FTC and the deduction affects the result. The harm is a taxpayer who over-claims the credit and faces an IRS adjustment.

This skill covers the foreign tax credit calculation under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the FTC rules are complex and depend on the income and the foreign taxes, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Foreign Taxes Are Creditable

Not all foreign taxes are creditable. To be creditable, the foreign tax must be an income tax (or a tax in lieu of an income tax) that is compulsory and that meets the U.S. definition of an income tax. The tax must be based on net income (not gross receipts, not a value-added tax, not a property tax). A tax in lieu of an income tax (such as a withholding tax on dividends or royalties) may be creditable if it is a substitute for an income tax.

Confirm the foreign taxes are creditable. The tax must be an income tax based on net income, or a tax in lieu of an income tax. Value-added taxes (VAT), sales taxes, property taxes, and payroll taxes are generally not creditable (they may be deductible as business expenses, but not creditable). Withholding taxes on dividends, interest, and royalties are often creditable (they are in lieu of an income tax). Confirm the tax meets the U.S. definition of an income tax. Obtain the foreign tax documentation (tax receipts, withholding statements). A tax that is not creditable may still be deductible, but not as an FTC.

### Separate Income Into Statutory Categories

The FTC is computed separately for each statutory category (or "basket") of income. The categories include general category income (most foreign business income, foreign branch income, and certain other income) and passive category income (dividends, interest, royalties, annuities, and certain other passive income). The separation ensures that foreign taxes on one category cannot offset U.S. tax on another category. Each category has its own limitation.

Separate the income into the statutory categories. The most common categories are general category (active business income, foreign branch income) and passive category (dividends, interest, royalties). The foreign taxes and the foreign income must be matched within each category. The FTC limitation is computed separately for each category. Foreign taxes on passive income cannot offset U.S. tax on general category income, and vice versa. Track the income and the taxes by category. The categorization is critical for the limitation computation.

### Source The Income To Determine Foreign Source Income

The FTC applies only to foreign source income (income sourced outside the U.S.). U.S. source income is not eligible for the FTC. The sourcing rules depend on the income type. For services, the source is where the services are performed. For inventory sales, the source depends on the title passage or the production method. For dividends, the source is the residence of the paying corporation. For interest, the source is the residence of the borrower.

Source the income correctly. For services, the source is where the services are performed (services performed abroad are foreign source). For inventory sales, the source depends on the title passage rule (for purchases) or the production method (for manufactured goods). For dividends, the source is the residence of the paying corporation (dividends from a foreign corporation are foreign source). For interest, the source is the residence of the borrower. The sourcing determines what income is foreign source and eligible for the FTC. Incorrect sourcing overstates or understates the foreign source income and the FTC limitation.

### Compute The FTC Limitation

The FTC cannot exceed the U.S. tax on the foreign source income. The limitation is computed separately for each category. The formula is: the FTC limitation equals the U.S. tax before the FTC multiplied by the foreign source taxable income in the category divided by the worldwide taxable income. If the foreign taxes paid exceed the limitation, the excess is carried back one year and forward ten years (as discussed in the carryback/carryforward skill).

Compute the FTC limitation for each category. The limitation is the U.S. tax before the FTC multiplied by (foreign source taxable income in the category / worldwide taxable income). The credit is the lesser of the foreign taxes paid in the category or the limitation. If the foreign taxes exceed the limitation, the excess is carried back and forward. For example, if the U.S. tax before the FTC is 100,000 dollars, the foreign source taxable income in the general category is 40,000 dollars, and the worldwide taxable income is 200,000 dollars, the general category limitation is 100,000 multiplied by 40,000/200,000 = 20,000 dollars. The credit is the lesser of the foreign taxes paid in the general category or 20,000 dollars. Track the limitation and the carryover.

### Choose Between The Credit And The Deduction

The taxpayer can choose to take the foreign taxes as a credit or as an itemized deduction. The credit is generally more valuable because it reduces U.S. tax dollar-for-dollar, while the deduction reduces taxable income (and the benefit depends on the marginal rate). However, in some cases (for example, when the taxpayer has a net operating loss or very low U.S. tax), the deduction may be more valuable. The choice is made annually.

Analyze the credit versus the deduction. The credit is generally more valuable (dollar-for-dollar reduction in U.S. tax). The deduction reduces taxable income, and the benefit is the deduction multiplied by the marginal rate. For a taxpayer in the 24% bracket, a 10,000 dollar foreign tax deduction saves 2,400 dollars, while a 10,000 dollar credit saves 10,000 dollars. The credit is almost always better. However, if the taxpayer has a net operating loss (no U.S. tax to offset) or the FTC limitation is zero, the deduction may be more valuable. Model both options. The choice is made annually and can be changed each year.

### Report The FTC On Form 1116

The FTC is reported on Form 1116 (Foreign Tax Credit) for individuals, or Form 1118 for corporations. The form separates the income by category, computes the limitation, and determines the credit. The form is attached to Form 1040 (for individuals). Taxpayers with only passive income and foreign taxes below 600 dollars (300 dollars for married filing separately) may claim the FTC directly on Form 1040 without Form 1116 (the de minimis exception).

Ensure Form 1116 is filed (or the de minimis exception is used). The form computes the FTC by category, applies the limitation, and determines the credit. The de minimis exception (600 dollars for single, 300 dollars for married filing separately) allows claiming the FTC without Form 1116, but only for passive category income. For general category income or larger amounts, Form 1116 is required. Track the FTC and the carryover on the form. The FTC carryover (excess foreign taxes) is tracked on Form 1116 for future years.

## Common Traps

### Crediting Non-Creditable Taxes

Not all foreign taxes are creditable. VAT, sales taxes, property taxes, and payroll taxes are generally not creditable. Confirm the tax is an income tax or a tax in lieu of an income tax.

### Mixing Income Categories

The FTC is computed separately for each category (general, passive). Foreign taxes on one category cannot offset U.S. tax on another. Track the income and taxes by category.

### Incorrectly Sourcing The Income

The FTC applies only to foreign source income. Incorrect sourcing (for example, sourcing U.S. services as foreign) overstates the foreign source income and the limitation.

### Ignoring The FTC Limitation

The FTC cannot exceed the U.S. tax on the foreign source income. Applying the credit without the limitation overstates the benefit. Compute the limitation for each category.

### Overlooking The De Minimis Exception

Taxpayers with only passive income and foreign taxes below 600 dollars (300 dollars for married filing separately) can claim the FTC without Form 1116. Do not require Form 1116 if the exception applies.

### Failing To Choose Between Credit And Deduction

The credit is generally more valuable, but the deduction may be better in some cases. Model both options and choose annually.

### Forgetting The Carryback And Carryforward

Excess foreign taxes (above the limitation) are carried back one year and forward ten years. Track the carryover for future years.
