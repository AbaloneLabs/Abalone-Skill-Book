# Solo 401(k)

A solo 401(k) (also called a one-participant 401(k) or an individual 401(k)) is a qualified plan designed for a business owner with no employees (or only a spouse who works in the business), and it allows the largest contribution for a self-employed individual with moderate earnings because it combines an employee salary deferral with an employer profit-sharing contribution. The judgment problem is that the plan is only available to a genuinely employee-less business, that the plan must be adopted by December 31 of the contribution year (even though the contribution can be funded later), and that the combined limit interacts with the self-employed adjustment in a way that differs from a SEP-IRA. An agent who recommends a solo 401(k) without confirming the no-employees rule, or who misses the December 31 adoption deadline, sets up a plan that cannot be used or that triggers full plan-administration requirements.

Agents frequently overlook that even one common-law employee (other than a spouse) disqualifies the simplified solo 401(k) and converts it into a full 401(k) plan with ERISA-style testing and filing requirements, or they assume the plan can be adopted after year-end like a SEP-IRA. They miscompute the employer profit-sharing portion by applying 25 percent to raw self-employment earnings without the self-employed adjustment, and they miss that high-balance plans trigger the Form 5500-EZ filing requirement. The harm is a plan that must be terminated or expanded into a full plan, a missed deadline, and an overpromised contribution.

This skill covers the solo 401(k) contribution structure, the no-employees eligibility rule, the December 31 adoption deadline, the Form 5500-EZ filing threshold, and the Roth and loan features, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the plan depends on the business structure, employee status, and compensation, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Dollar amounts are 2024 figures and are indexed annually.

## Core Rules

### Combine The Employee Deferral And The Employer Profit-Sharing Contribution

A solo 401(k) allows two contribution components: an employee salary deferral of up to 23,000 dollars for 2024 (22,500 dollars for 2023), plus a catch-up of 7,500 dollars for participants 50 or older; and an employer profit-sharing contribution of up to 25 percent of compensation (approximately 20 percent of net self-employment earnings for an owner, after the SE tax deduction). The combined total is capped at the annual defined contribution limit of approximately 69,000 dollars for 2024 (approximately 76,500 dollars for those 50 and older including the catch-up). Because the owner is both the employee and the employer, the owner can use both components, which is why the solo 401(k) allows a larger contribution than a SEP-IRA at lower income levels.

Compute both components. For a self-employed owner under age 50 with 100,000 dollars of net self-employment earnings (after the SE tax deduction), the employee deferral is up to 23,000 dollars (but cannot exceed 100 percent of compensation), and the employer profit-sharing is approximately 20,000 dollars (20 percent of the adjusted earnings), for a combined 43,000 dollars, well within the 69,000 dollar cap. A SEP-IRA for the same owner would allow only the 20,000 dollar employer portion. The solo 401(k) advantage is the employee deferral on top of the employer contribution, which is most powerful at moderate earnings. Apply the self-employed adjustment to the employer portion (20 percent, not 25 percent of raw earnings), and confirm the combined total does not exceed the annual cap.

### Confirm The No-Employees Eligibility Rule Strictly

A solo 401(k) is available only to a business owner with no common-law employees other than a spouse who works in the business. A common-law employee is determined by the facts and circumstances of the relationship (behavioral, financial, and relationship type control), not by the label of contractor. If the business has even one common-law employee who meets the plan's eligibility requirements, the plan is no longer a solo 401(k) and becomes a full 401(k) plan subject to ERISA, nondiscrimination testing, top-heavy rules, and Form 5500 filing. Part-time and seasonal workers may count under the SECURE Act long-term part-time rules.

Confirm there are no common-law employees before adopting or maintaining a solo 401(k). Scrutinize any 1099 contractors for whether they are truly independent or are common-law employees under the three-prong test. A single reclassified contractor as an employee can disqualify the plan and trigger the full plan requirements retroactively. The spouse-employee exception allows a spouse who works in the business to participate, but a spouse does not count as a disqualifying employee. Short-term employees under 1,000 hours historically did not count, but the SECURE Act long-term part-time rules (generally three consecutive years of 500 or more hours for 401(k) eligibility, reduced to two years for 2024 and later) can bring part-timers into the plan, so monitor hours. If any common-law employee becomes eligible, the plan must be amended or terminated.

### Adopt The Plan By December 31 Of The Contribution Year

A solo 401(k) plan document must be adopted by December 31 of the year for which contributions are made, even though the contributions themselves can be funded by the extended return due date. This differs from a SEP-IRA, which can be both adopted and funded by the extended return due date. The December 31 adoption deadline is firm for the employee deferral component (the deferral election must be in place by year-end for the salary to be deferred), and the plan must exist by December 31 for any contribution (employer or employee) to count for that year.

Flag the December 31 deadline early. A self-employed taxpayer who decides in January to contribute for the prior year cannot adopt a solo 401(k) retroactively; the plan must have been adopted by December 31 of that prior year. The employee deferral must be elected before the compensation is paid, so for a self-employed owner without payroll, the deferral is typically elected by year-end and funded by the return due date. The employer profit-sharing can be funded by the extended return due date as long as the plan was adopted by December 31. A taxpayer who misses the December 31 deadline must use a SEP-IRA (which has the later adoption deadline) for that year, so the deadline is a hard constraint that should be communicated before year-end.

### File Form 5500-EZ When The Balance Exceeds The Threshold

A solo 401(k) with plan assets of 250,000 dollars or more at the end of the plan year must file Form 5500-EZ (Annual Return of One-Participant Retirement Plan) by the end of the seventh month after the plan year-end (July 31 for a calendar-year plan, with an extension available). Plans below the 250,000 dollar threshold are exempt from filing. The filing requirement recurs each year the balance is above the threshold, and failure to file triggers penalties that can be significant (though the IRS offers a Delinquent Filer Voluntary Compliance Program to reduce them).

Monitor the plan balance against the 250,000 dollar threshold and file Form 5500-EZ when it is exceeded. A common error is assuming a solo 401(k) never requires a filing because it is a one-participant plan; the filing is required once the balance crosses the threshold, and the balance grows quickly with maximum contributions. The filing is relatively simple (no audit required for a one-participant plan), but it must be timely. Use the DFVCP if a prior year's filing was missed, because the penalties for non-filing are otherwise steep. Track the filing obligation as part of the plan administration.

### Evaluate The Roth And Loan Features Against The Tradeoffs

A solo 401(k) can include a Roth (designated Roth) contribution feature, allowing the employee deferral to be made on an after-tax basis with tax-free qualified distributions, and can allow participant loans (typically up to 50 percent of the vested balance or 50,000 dollars, whichever is less, repaid within five years). These features are not available in a SEP-IRA. The Roth feature allows diversification of tax treatment, and the loan feature provides liquidity access, but both add administrative complexity and the loan must be repaid on schedule or it becomes a deemed distribution.

Weigh the Roth and loan features against the added complexity. The Roth deferral is attractive for an owner who expects higher future tax rates or who wants tax-free retirement income, but it forgoes the current deduction for that portion. The loan feature is a liquidity backstop but a defaulted loan becomes taxable income. Confirm the plan document includes the desired features, because a basic plan document may not. Note that the Roth employer profit-sharing is generally not permitted (employer contributions are pre-tax), so only the employee deferral can be Roth. For a taxpayer who values the Roth option or the loan access, the solo 401(k) may dominate a SEP-IRA even where the contribution limits are similar.

### Apply The Compensation And Earnings Base For Each Component

The employee deferral is limited to 100 percent of the owner's compensation (earned income for a self-employed owner), and the employer profit-sharing is limited to 25 percent of compensation (approximately 20 percent of net self-employment earnings). The combined limit is the annual defined contribution cap (approximately 69,000 dollars for 2024). For an owner with low earnings, the deferral may be capped by the 100 percent of compensation rule (an owner with 15,000 dollars of earnings can defer at most 15,000 dollars, not the full 23,000 dollar limit). For an owner with very high earnings, the annual cap binds before the percentage does.

Compute each component against the compensation base. For an owner with 300,000 dollars of net self-employment earnings, the employee deferral is 23,000 dollars (under the 23,000 dollar limit and under 100 percent of compensation), and the employer profit-sharing is approximately 60,000 dollars (20 percent of adjusted earnings), but the combined total is capped at 69,000 dollars, so the employer portion is reduced to 46,000 dollars. The self-employed adjustment applies to the employer portion only, not the employee deferral. Confirm the compensation base (net earnings after the SE tax deduction for the employer portion), and apply the annual cap to the combined total.

## Common Traps

### Having A Common-Law Employee Disqualifies The Solo Plan

Even one common-law employee (other than a spouse) who meets eligibility converts the plan into a full 401(k) with ERISA, testing, and filing. A reclassified contractor can trigger this retroactively.

### Missing The December 31 Plan Adoption Deadline

The plan document must be adopted by December 31 of the contribution year, even though funding can wait until the extended return due date. A January decision cannot create a retroactive plan for the prior year.

### Applying 25 Percent To Raw Self-Employment Earnings For The Employer Portion

The employer profit-sharing is approximately 20 percent of net self-employment earnings (after the SE tax deduction), not 25 percent of raw earnings. Using 25 percent overstates the contribution.

### Forgetting Form 5500-EZ Above 250,000 Dollars

A solo 401(k) with 250,000 dollars or more in assets must file Form 5500-EZ annually. Assuming a one-participant plan never files triggers non-filing penalties.

### Capping The Employee Deferral Above 100 Percent Of Compensation

The deferral cannot exceed 100 percent of compensation. For a low-earning owner, the deferral is capped by earnings, not by the 23,000 dollar statutory limit.

### Assuming The Plan Can Be Adopted After Year-End Like A SEP-IRA

A SEP-IRA can be adopted and funded by the extended return due date. A solo 401(k) must be adopted by December 31. Treating them the same misses the deadline.

### Overlooking The SECURE Act Long-Term Part-Time Rules

Part-time employees with 500 or more hours over two or three years (depending on the year) may become eligible under SECURE, potentially disqualifying the solo plan. Ignoring hours tracking misses this risk.
