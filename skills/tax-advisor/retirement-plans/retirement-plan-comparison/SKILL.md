# Retirement Plan Comparison

Choosing among the small-business and self-employed retirement plans (SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, solo 401(k), and defined benefit) is a multi-factor decision in which the dominant plan depends on the taxpayer's earnings level, the W-2 versus self-employed income mix, the employee count, the administrative tolerance, and the deadlines. The judgment problem is that no single plan dominates across all situations: the solo 401(k) maximizes contributions at moderate earnings but requires no employees and a December 31 adoption; the SEP-IRA maximizes at high earnings and allows late adoption but forces uniform employee contributions; the SIMPLE IRA is low-cost and allows employee deferrals but has a low cap and an October 1 deadline; the defined benefit plan maximizes contributions for older high earners but carries funding rigidity and actuarial cost. An agent who recommends a plan by the headline limit, without modeling the employee-coverage cost and the deadline and admin constraints, picks the wrong plan.

Agents frequently recommend a SEP-IRA by default for any self-employed taxpayer without checking whether a solo 401(k) would allow a larger contribution at the taxpayer's earnings, or they recommend a solo 401(k) without confirming there are no common-law employees. They overlook the defined benefit option for an older high-earning owner who could contribute far more than the defined contribution cap, and they ignore the Roth and loan differences that can matter for the taxpayer's strategy. The harm is a suboptimal plan that underfunds retirement, a plan that must be terminated or expanded due to employee issues, or a missed deadline that forfeits the plan for the year.

This skill covers the decision framework for comparing SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, solo 401(k), and defined benefit plans, the factors that determine which dominates, and the Roth, loan, RMD, and early-withdrawal differences, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the plan choice depends on the taxpayer's specific facts, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Dollar amounts are 2024 figures and are indexed annually.

## Core Rules

### Build The Decision Framework On The Key Factors

The plan choice turns on several factors: the contribution goal (how much the owner wants to defer), the earnings level and the W-2 versus self-employed income mix, the employee count and the cost of covering them, the administrative tolerance and cost, the deadlines (December 31 for solo 401(k), October 1 for SIMPLE IRA, extended return due date for SEP-IRA), and the desired features (Roth, loan, RMD flexibility). No plan dominates on all factors, so the framework requires modeling the owner's contribution under each plan and weighing the employee-coverage cost and the admin burden.

Lay out the factors for the specific taxpayer before recommending a plan. For a self-employed owner with no employees and 100,000 dollars of net earnings who wants to maximize the contribution, the solo 401(k) allows roughly 43,000 dollars (employee deferral plus employer profit-sharing) versus roughly 20,000 dollars for a SEP-IRA, so the solo 401(k) dominates. For the same owner with 500,000 dollars of net earnings, both plans hit the 69,000 dollar cap, so the admin and feature differences decide. For an owner with several employees, the SEP-IRA's uniform percentage or the SIMPLE IRA's match/nonelective determines the total cost. Model the owner's contribution and the employee cost under each viable plan, and choose based on the combined result.

### Recognize When The Solo 401(k) Dominates

The solo 401(k) dominates for a self-employed owner (or an owner and spouse) with no common-law employees and moderate earnings, because the employee deferral (23,000 dollars for 2024, plus the 7,500 dollar catch-up for 50+) stacks on top of the employer profit-sharing (approximately 20 percent of net self-employment earnings), up to the combined cap of approximately 69,000 dollars. At earnings below roughly 250,000 to 300,000 dollars of net self-employment income, the solo 401(k) allows a larger contribution than a SEP-IRA (which provides only the employer portion). At higher earnings, both hit the cap and the difference narrows.

Recommend the solo 401(k) where the owner has no employees and moderate earnings and wants the maximum contribution. Confirm the no-employees rule strictly (any common-law employee disqualifies the simplified plan) and the December 31 adoption deadline. The solo 401(k) also offers the Roth deferral and the loan feature, which a SEP-IRA lacks. The tradeoff is the slightly higher setup cost and the Form 5500-EZ filing once the balance exceeds 250,000 dollars. For a no-employee owner at moderate earnings, the solo 401(k) is usually the best choice; for a no-employee owner at very high earnings, the solo 401(k) and SEP-IRA converge at the cap, and the features and admin decide.

### Recognize When The SEP-IRA Dominates

The SEP-IRA dominates for a self-employed owner with no employees and very high earnings (where both plans hit the cap and the SEP-IRA's lower admin and later adoption deadline matter), or for an owner who decides late (after December 31) and needs to adopt and fund by the extended return due date. The SEP-IRA also dominates where the simplicity of a single employer contribution is valued and the Roth and loan features are not needed. The SEP-IRA's key advantage is the late adoption deadline (the extended return due date), which makes it the fallback for post-year-end planning.

Recommend the SEP-IRA where the owner has high earnings (cap-bound), values simplicity, decides late, or has employees but wants a uniform percentage contribution that scales with profits. The tradeoff is the employee-coverage cost: a 20 percent contribution for the owner requires 20 percent for every eligible employee, which can be expensive for a business with many employees. The SEP-IRA has no employee deferral (except the obsolete SARSEP) and no Roth or loan feature. For a no-employee owner at high earnings who decides by December 31, the solo 401(k) and SEP-IRA are both viable; the SEP-IRA wins on simplicity and the late deadline, the solo 401(k) wins on features.

### Recognize When The SIMPLE IRA Dominates

The SIMPLE IRA dominates for a small employer with employees who wants to offer a plan with low administrative cost and no nondiscrimination testing, where the employer cost is a fixed match (3 percent) or nonelective (2 percent) rather than a profit-scaling percentage. The SIMPLE IRA allows employee salary deferrals (16,000 dollars for 2024 plus the 3,500 dollar catch-up), funded by the employees, with a modest mandatory employer contribution. It is attractive for a business that wants to offer a benefit plan without the complexity of a full 401(k).

Recommend the SIMPLE IRA where the employer wants employee deferrals with low admin and a predictable employer cost, and where the October 1 deadline can be met. The tradeoff is the lower contribution cap (the deferral plus the match is generally below the SEP-IRA or solo 401(k) cap for high earners) and the strict October 1 establishment deadline. The SIMPLE IRA cannot coexist with a SEP-IRA for the same employer in the same year. For a business with employees where the owner's own contribution goal is modest, the SIMPLE IRA is often the right choice; for an owner who wants to maximize personal contributions, the SEP-IRA or solo 401(k) (if no employees) usually allows more.

### Consider The Defined Benefit Plan For Older High Earners

A defined benefit (DB) plan, including a cash balance plan, can allow contributions far exceeding the defined contribution cap (approximately 69,000 dollars for 2024) for an older high-earning owner, because the contribution is actuarially determined to fund a target retirement benefit. A 60-year-old owner with high earnings might contribute 200,000 dollars or more annually to a cash balance plan, versus the 69,000 dollar DC cap. The DB plan is often paired with a 401(k) (a combined DC-plus-DB arrangement) to maximize the total. The tradeoff is the funding rigidity (the annual contribution is required, not discretionary), the actuarial cost, and the Form 5500 with schedule and often an audit requirement.

Evaluate a DB or cash balance plan for an older owner (typically 50+) with consistently high earnings who wants to maximize contributions and can commit to the required annual funding. The DB plan dominates on contribution size but requires professional administration and a multi-year funding commitment; a downturn in income can make the required contribution burdensome. The DB plan is unsuitable for an owner with volatile income or a short remaining horizon. For the right taxpayer, the DB plan plus a 401(k) can defer a very large amount, but the complexity and rigidity mean it is not a default choice. Confirm the actuarial funding requirement and the exit strategy before recommending.

### Compare The Roth Loan RMD And Early-Withdrawal Differences

The plans differ on features that affect the taxpayer's strategy. Roth (designated Roth) contributions are available in a solo 401(k) (and in a full 401(k)), allowing after-tax deferrals with tax-free qualified distributions; they are not available in a SEP-IRA or a SIMPLE IRA (though a taxpayer can convert to a Roth IRA). Participant loans are available in a 401(k) (solo or full), typically up to 50 percent of the balance or 50,000 dollars; they are not available in a SEP-IRA or SIMPLE IRA (an IRA cannot have a loan). RMDs apply to all the plans after age 73 (under current law), though the SECURE 2.0 changes allow some Roth 401(k) RMD flexibility. Early withdrawals (before age 59.5) are generally subject to a 10 percent penalty (25 percent for a SIMPLE IRA within the first two years), with limited exceptions.

Weigh the feature differences against the taxpayer's goals. For a taxpayer who wants Roth tax-free growth, the solo 401(k) with a Roth feature (or a conversion strategy) is the route. For a taxpayer who wants liquidity access, the 401(k) loan feature is unique. For a taxpayer near retirement, the RMD rules and the early-withdrawal penalty matter. The SEP-IRA and SIMPLE IRA are simpler but lack these features. Confirm the feature needs before choosing, because a plan that lacks a needed feature (Roth, loan) may force a later conversion or plan change. Note that the Roth employer contribution is generally not permitted in any of these plans, so only the employee deferral can be Roth.

## Common Traps

### Recommending A SEP-IRA By Default Without Modeling The Solo 401(k)

At moderate earnings, the solo 401(k) allows a larger contribution than the SEP-IRA because of the employee deferral. Defaulting to the SEP-IRA underfunds the owner's retirement.

### Recommending A Solo 401(k) Without Confirming No Employees

Any common-law employee (other than a spouse) disqualifies the simplified solo plan and triggers full plan requirements. Recommending it without checking the employee status sets up a plan that must be expanded or terminated.

### Missing The Plan-Specific Deadlines

The solo 401(k) must be adopted by December 31; the SIMPLE IRA by October 1; the SEP-IRA by the extended return due date. Treating the deadlines as interchangeable forfeits the plan for the year.

### Overlooking The Defined Benefit Plan For Older High Earners

A DB or cash balance plan can allow contributions far above the DC cap for an older high earner. Defaulting to a DC plan underfunds the owner's deferral opportunity.

### Ignoring The Employee-Coverage Cost In The Comparison

The SEP-IRA's uniform percentage and the SIMPLE IRA's match/nonelective apply to all eligible employees. Modeling only the owner's contribution understates the total cost and can flip the optimal choice.

### Assuming The Plans Can Be Stacked

A SEP-IRA and a SIMPLE IRA generally cannot coexist for the same employer. The overall annual addition cap (approximately 69,000 dollars for 2024) limits the total across DC plans. Assuming they stack overstates the contribution.

### Forgetting The Roth And Loan Feature Differences

Roth deferrals and loans are available in a 401(k) but not in a SEP-IRA or SIMPLE IRA. Choosing a plan without the needed feature forces a later conversion or plan change.
