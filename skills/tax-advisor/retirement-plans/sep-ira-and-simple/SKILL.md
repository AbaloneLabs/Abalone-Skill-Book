# SEP-IRA and SIMPLE

SEP-IRA and SIMPLE IRA are the two lowest-administration retirement plans available to small businesses and self-employed taxpayers, but they operate by very different contribution mechanics, eligibility mandates, and deadlines, and choosing between them (or choosing one over a solo 401(k)) is a consequential decision. The judgment problem is that the SEP-IRA contribution is employer-only and is a uniform percentage of each eligible employee's compensation (which forces contributions for employees if the owner contributes), while the SIMPLE IRA centers on employee salary deferrals plus a fixed employer match or nonelective contribution. An agent who picks a plan by the headline limit, without modeling the employee-coverage cost and the deadline constraints, misstates the achievable contribution and the administrative burden.

Agents frequently apply the SEP-IRA 25 percent of compensation limit to a self-employed owner without adjusting for the self-employment tax deduction (the effective limit is roughly 20 percent of self-employed net earnings), or they assume the owner can defer salary into a SEP-IRA (a SEP has no employee deferral except for the obsolete SARSEP). They forget that a SIMPLE IRA requires the employer match or nonelective contribution for all eligible employees, and they miss the strict October 1 deadline to establish a SIMPLE IRA for the current year. The harm is an overpromised contribution that cannot be made, a missed deadline that forfeits the plan for the year, or a nondeductible excess contribution with excise taxes.

This skill covers SEP-IRA and SIMPLE IRA contribution limits, the self-employed adjustment, employer contribution mechanics, eligibility, and deadlines, under U.S. federal tax law. It is not tax advice; the plan choice and contribution depend on the business structure, employee count, and compensation, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for actual situations. Dollar amounts are 2024 figures and are indexed annually.

## Core Rules

### Apply The SEP-IRA Employer Contribution Limit With The Self-Employed Adjustment

A SEP-IRA allows employer contributions of up to 25 percent of each eligible employee's compensation, or for a self-employed individual, up to 20 percent of net self-employment earnings (after deducting half of self-employment tax), capped at an annual maximum of approximately 69,000 dollars for 2024 (66,000 dollars for 2023). The 25 percent figure applies to common-law employees on W-2 compensation; for the self-employed owner, the effective percentage is lower (approximately 20 percent) because the contribution itself reduces the base on which the percentage is computed, requiring the special self-employed adjustment. A SEP-IRA has no employee salary deferral component (except for a SARSEP established before 1997, which is obsolete for new plans), so the entire contribution is the employer's.

Compute the SEP-IRA contribution for a self-employed owner using the self-employed adjustment: the deductible contribution is approximately 20 percent of net profit minus half of self-employment tax, not 25 percent of the raw net profit. For an owner with 100,000 dollars of net self-employment earnings (after the SE tax deduction), the maximum SEP-IRA contribution is approximately 20,000 dollars, not 25,000 dollars. For employees, the contribution is a uniform percentage of compensation up to the per-employee cap (the 69,000 dollar limit and the compensation cap of approximately 345,000 dollars for 2024). The employer must contribute the same percentage for all eligible employees, which is the key cost driver for a business with employees.

### Apply The SIMPLE IRA Deferral And Employer Contribution Mechanics

A SIMPLE IRA allows eligible employees to defer salary up to 16,000 dollars for 2024 (15,500 dollars for 2023), plus a catch-up of 3,500 dollars for participants 50 or older (the SECURE 2.0 enhancements may increase catch-ups in some cases). The employer must make either a matching contribution of up to 3 percent of each eligible employee's compensation (matching the employee's deferral dollar for dollar up to 3 percent of pay), or a 2 percent nonelective contribution of each eligible employee's compensation regardless of whether the employee defers. The 3 percent match can be reduced to as low as 1 percent in two of five years under certain rules, but the 2 percent nonelective is fixed and applies to all eligible employees including those who do not defer.

Compute the SIMPLE IRA components: the employee deferral (up to 16,000 dollars plus catch-up) and the employer contribution (3 percent match or 2 percent nonelective). For an owner-employee who defers 16,000 dollars and earns 100,000 dollars, the 3 percent match adds 3,000 dollars, for a total of 19,000 dollars. The 2 percent nonelective would add 2,000 dollars (2 percent of compensation up to a cap) for all eligible employees regardless of deferral. The employer contribution is mandatory for all eligible employees, so a SIMPLE IRA with employees has a fixed cost that does not scale with profits the way a SEP-IRA percentage does. Confirm which employer contribution formula applies and apply it to all eligible employees.

### Apply The Eligibility Rules For Coverage

SEP-IRA eligibility: an employer can set eligibility requirements no more restrictive than age 21 and three years of service in the preceding five years (the employer can use less restrictive rules). Employees who meet the eligibility requirements must be covered and receive the same percentage contribution. The employer can exclude employees covered by a union agreement with retirement benefits and nonresident aliens without U.S. source compensation. SIMPLE IRA eligibility: an employer must allow any employee who received at least 5,000 dollars in compensation in any two prior years and is reasonably expected to receive at least 5,000 dollars in the current year to participate (the employer can use less restrictive rules).

Confirm which employees must be covered under each plan before recommending it. A SEP-IRA with a 20 percent contribution for the owner requires a 20 percent contribution for every eligible employee, which can be costly for a business with many lower-paid employees. A SIMPLE IRA requires the match or nonelective for all eligible employees, but the employee deferral is funded by the employee, so the employer cost is the 2 percent or 3 percent. The eligibility rules are mandatory minimums; the employer can be more inclusive but not more restrictive. Count the eligible employees and model the total employer cost before electing the plan.

### Respect The Deadlines For Adoption And Funding

A SEP-IRA can be adopted and funded by the due date (including extensions) of the employer's tax return for the year, which gives a self-employed taxpayer until the extended due date (typically October 15 for a Schedule C filer) to both adopt the plan and make the contribution for the prior year. This flexibility makes the SEP-IRA attractive for year-end and post-year-end planning. A SIMPLE IRA must be established by October 1 of the year for which contributions are made (for a calendar-year plan), and employee notices must be provided before the plan year; a SIMPLE IRA established after October 1 generally cannot be effective for that calendar year (with limited exceptions for new businesses).

Confirm the deadlines before recommending a plan. A SEP-IRA can be set up and funded as late as the extended return due date, so it is the fallback for a taxpayer who decides late in the year or after year-end. A SIMPLE IRA must be established by October 1, so a taxpayer deciding in November cannot use a SIMPLE IRA for the current year (and must wait until the next year or use a SEP-IRA). The employee salary deferrals for a SIMPLE IRA must be deposited within the payroll deposit rules, and the employer contribution is due by the return due date. Missing the October 1 SIMPLE deadline forfeits the plan for the year, so flag this constraint early.

### Recognize The Rollover And Creditor Protection Differences

SEP-IRA and SIMPLE IRA accounts are IRAs for legal purposes, so they follow the IRA rules for rollovers, distributions, and creditor protection. A SEP-IRA can be rolled over to another IRA or to a qualified plan, and a SIMPLE IRA can be rolled over after a two-year period from the first contribution (the two-year rule), with a 25 percent penalty on early distributions within the two years (in addition to the standard 10 percent early distribution penalty for pre-age-59.5 distributions). Both are protected from creditors in bankruptcy under the IRA bankruptcy exemption, and both are subject to required minimum distributions after age 73 (under current law).

Apply the two-year rule for SIMPLE IRA rollovers and early distributions: a distribution within two years of the first contribution is subject to a 25 percent penalty (not 10 percent), and a rollover within the two years is restricted. Confirm the two-year period has elapsed before recommending a rollover. Both plans are subject to RMDs after age 73, so they are not indefinite accumulation vehicles. The creditor protection is meaningful but follows state IRA exemption law outside bankruptcy. Note that a SIMPLE IRA cannot accept SEP-IRA or other plan rollovers during the two-year period, and the contribution limits are separate from the IRA contribution limits (a SEP-IRA contribution does not reduce the traditional IRA contribution room).

### Coordinate With Other Plans And Avoid Double Counting

A taxpayer can maintain multiple plans, but the contribution limits interact. A SEP-IRA and a SIMPLE IRA generally cannot both be used by the same employer for the same year (a SIMPLE IRA is exclusive in that sense). A SEP-IRA can coexist with an individual's traditional or Roth IRA without reducing the IRA limit. A solo 401(k) generally displaces a SEP-IRA for the same business because the 401(k) has its own contribution limits that aggregate the employer and employee portions. The total employer contribution across plans cannot exceed the overall defined contribution limit (approximately 69,000 dollars for 2024) per employer per individual.

Confirm the plan interactions before recommending multiple plans. A taxpayer with a SEP-IRA cannot also use a SIMPLE IRA for the same business in the same year. A taxpayer who wants both employee deferral and employer profit-sharing should consider a solo 401(k) rather than layering a SEP-IRA. The overall per-person annual addition limit (approximately 69,000 dollars for 2024) caps the total across all defined contribution plans of one employer, so contributions cannot be stacked beyond that. See the retirement plan comparison skill for the cross-plan decision framework.

## Common Traps

### Applying 25 Percent To A Self-Employed Owner Without The Adjustment

The SEP-IRA limit is 25 percent of employee compensation but approximately 20 percent of net self-employment earnings for an owner, because the contribution reduces the base. Using 25 percent of raw net profit overstates the deductible contribution.

### Assuming A SEP-IRA Allows Employee Deferral

A SEP-IRA is employer-only (except for the obsolete SARSEP). The owner cannot defer salary into a SEP. Assuming deferral is available overstates the achievable contribution.

### Forgetting The SIMPLE IRA October 1 Establishment Deadline

A SIMPLE IRA must be established by October 1 for the calendar year. Establishing it later forfeits the plan for that year. Missing the deadline cannot be cured.

### Underestimating The Employee Coverage Cost

A SEP-IRA requires the same percentage for all eligible employees; a SIMPLE IRA requires the match or nonelective for all eligible employees. Modeling only the owner's contribution understates the total employer cost.

### Using The 3 Percent Match And 2 Percent Nonelective Interchangeably

The 3 percent match requires the employee to defer to receive it; the 2 percent nonelective goes to all eligible employees regardless of deferral. They produce different costs and must be chosen deliberately.

### Violating The SIMPLE IRA Two-Year Rollover Rule

A SIMPLE IRA distribution or rollover within two years of the first contribution triggers a 25 percent penalty and rollover restrictions. Treating a SIMPLE IRA like a regular IRA in the first two years causes the penalty.

### Stacking A SEP-IRA And A SIMPLE IRA For The Same Business

A SEP-IRA and a SIMPLE IRA generally cannot both be used by the same employer in the same year. Assuming they can stack overstates the contribution.
