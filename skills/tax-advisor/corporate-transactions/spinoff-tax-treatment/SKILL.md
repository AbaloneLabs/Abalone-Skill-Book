# Spinoff Tax Treatment

Section 355 permits a corporation to distribute the stock of a controlled subsidiary to its shareholders without recognizing gain or loss, provided a demanding set of requirements is met. This is the tax-free spinoff, and it is one of the most heavily conditioned exceptions to gain recognition in the Code. The judgment problem is that the requirements are numerous, fact-intensive, and anti-abuse-laden, and a failure on any one converts the distribution into a taxable dividend or sale. Agents tend to focus on the control test and the active trade or business test and miss the device-to-distribute-earnings test, the continuity requirements, and the Section 355(e) anti-abuse rule that imposes tax if the spinoff is part of a plan pursuant to which a 50 percent or greater acquisition of the distributing or controlled corporation occurs.

The harm is that a failed Section 355 distribution can generate massive tax: the distribution is recharacterized as a taxable dividend to the extent of earnings and profits, then as a sale or exchange of stock, with gain measured on the appreciated value of the distributed subsidiary. Because spinoffs typically involve large, appreciated businesses, the tax can be enormous and there is often no cash distributed to pay it. Agents frequently misjudge the active trade or business history, accept thin documentation of non-device intent, or fail to coordinate the spinoff with surrounding acquisitions that trip Section 355(e).

This skill covers federal US tax-free spinoff analysis under Section 355. It addresses the qualification requirements and the anti-abuse rules. It complements the reorganization types analysis (a spinoff can also be a Type D reorganization) but focuses on the Section 355 distribution regime. All statutory references reflect the current federal framework but must be verified against current law. This is structural transactional tax analysis, not tax advice; a qualified tax professional, tax attorney, or CPA must be consulted for any specific transaction.

## Core Rules

### Treat Section 355 As An Exception With A Heavy Burden Of Proof

Section 355 nonrecognition is an exception to the rule that a distribution of appreciated property triggers gain recognition under Section 311 and that shareholders recognize gain on distributions in excess of basis. The exception is available only when all requirements are met, and the taxpayer bears the burden of demonstrating qualification. An agent should begin from the presumption that a distribution of subsidiary stock is taxable and identify each requirement that supports nonrecognition.

The framing is essential because Section 355 is a favorite vehicle for tax planning, and the IRS scrutinizes spinoffs closely through rulings, examinations, and anti-abuse regulations. The agent should not accept a spinoff opinion at face value without tracing each requirement to the facts. The consequences of failure are disproportionate to the planning benefit, so conservative verification is warranted.

### Satisfy The Control Requirement At Distribution

Section 355(a)(1)(A) and Section 368(c) require that the distributing corporation own control of the controlled corporation immediately before the distribution. Control means ownership of stock possessing at least 80 percent of the total voting power and at least 80 percent of all other classes of stock. The distributing corporation must hold this control before the spinoff, and it distributes that control to its shareholders. The 80 percent test is measured by vote and by total shares of each class, not value.

The agent must verify the distributing corporation's ownership of the controlled corporation against both dimensions of the control test, including all classes of stock. A controlled corporation with multiple classes of equity, preferred stock, or nonvoting common must be tested class by class. If the distributing corporation does not hold 80 percent control, the spinoff cannot qualify, and the analysis stops. Pre-distribution recapitalizations to achieve control must themselves be scrutinized for tax consequences.

### Establish The Active Trade Or Business Requirement

Section 355(b) requires that the distributing corporation and the controlled corporation each be engaged, immediately after the distribution, in the active conduct of a trade or business that has been actively conducted throughout the five-year period ending on the date of distribution. The business must not have been acquired within the five-year period in a taxable transaction. This is the active trade or business (ATB) requirement, and it is the substantive gatekeeper that ensures Section 355 is used to separate real operating businesses, not to monetize passive assets.

The agent must identify the specific active trade or business of each corporation, confirm it has been actively conducted for the full five years, and confirm it was not acquired in a taxable purchase during that period. A business that was dormant, a shell, or acquired recently will not qualify. The five-year history must be documented with operating evidence, not merely asserted. Each corporation must have its own qualifying ATB; a shared or nominal business will not satisfy the requirement for both.

### Apply The Not-A-Device Test

Section 355(a)(1)(B) and the device regulations under Treas Reg 1.355-2 require that the distribution not be used principally as a device for the distribution of the earnings and profits of the distributing corporation or the controlled corporation. Device is a facts-and-circumstances test that looks at whether the spinoff is really a way to bail out accumulated earnings without dividend tax. Factors include the history of earnings, the use of the businesses after the distribution, the nature and amount of boot, and the relative maturity of the businesses.

The agent must assess device risk across the enumerated factors. A corporation with large accumulated earnings and profits, distributed into a business with limited operating needs, presents device risk. Mitigating factors include a genuine business purpose for the separation, continuing active business conduct by both corporations, and the absence of disproportionate cash or boot. Device is not cured by a single factor; it is a holistic judgment that must be documented, often with a device analysis supporting non-device intent.

### Establish A Corporate Business Purpose

Section 355(a)(1)(D) and the business purpose regulations require that the distribution be carried out for one or more corporate business purposes. A business purpose must be real and substantial to the business of the distributing or controlled corporation, not merely a shareholder benefit or tax purpose. Acceptable purposes include separating incompatible businesses, facilitating a legitimate acquisition, resolving operational conflicts, or improving management focus. Tax savings alone is not a sufficient business purpose.

The agent must identify and document the corporate business purpose with operational evidence. A purpose stated only in legal opinion, without underlying business facts, is weak. The purpose should be articulated before the distribution and supported by board resolutions, business plans, and operational rationale. The IRS has narrowed acceptable business purposes over time, so the agent should test the purpose against current authority.

### Satisfy Continuity Of Interest And Continuity Of Business

Section 355 requires continuity of interest, meaning the historic shareholders of the distributing corporation must retain an equity interest in the controlled corporation after the distribution, and continuity of business enterprise, meaning the historic business must continue. These continuity requirements parallel those in reorganizations and serve the same anti-abuse function: preventing Section 355 from being used as a disguised sale. The continuity must be substantial and continuing.

The agent should confirm that the shareholders receiving controlled corporation stock retain a meaningful interest and that the controlled corporation's historic business continues after the spinoff. Prearranged sales of the distributed stock by shareholders can threaten continuity, and the step transaction doctrine may apply. The continuity analysis should consider post-distribution trading, lock-ups, and any arrangements that effectively require disposition.

### Apply The Section 355(e) Anti-Acquisition Rule

Section 355(e) imposes tax on a distribution that is part of a plan pursuant to which one or more persons acquire directly or indirectly stock representing a 50 percent or greater interest in the distributing corporation or the controlled corporation. The acquisition can occur before or after the distribution, within a window around it, and the rule looks to whether the spinoff and the acquisition are pursuant to a plan. If triggered, the otherwise tax-free distribution becomes taxable to the distributing corporation as if it had sold the controlled corporation stock at fair market value.

The agent must scrutinize any acquisition activity around the spinoff. A spinoff followed by an acquisition of the spun-off entity, or of the distributing corporation, within the relevant period can trigger Section 355(e) if the facts show a plan. The plan determination is facts-and-circumstances and looks at discussions, agreements, and the timing of the acquisition. The 50 percent threshold and the plan concept make this rule a trap for spinoffs that are preludes to deals, and the agent should coordinate the spinoff timing with any acquisition planning.

### Distinguish Distributing And Controlled Corporations And Watch Tracking Stock

The distributing corporation is the corporation making the distribution; the controlled corporation is the subsidiary whose stock is distributed. Each must independently satisfy the requirements. A related structure is tracking stock, which purports to track the performance of a division without a separate corporation; tracking stock is not a Section 355 distribution and does not receive Section 355 treatment. The agent must confirm that the distribution is of stock of a genuine separate corporation, not tracking stock or a similar synthetic interest.

The distinction matters because the requirements apply separately to each corporation. If the controlled corporation lacks a qualifying active trade or business, the entire distribution fails, even if the distributing corporation qualifies. The agent should analyze each corporation against every requirement, not assume that one corporation's qualification covers both.

## Common Traps

### Focusing On Control And Missing The Active Trade Or Business History

Control is necessary but not sufficient. Each corporation needs a five-year actively conducted business not acquired in a taxable transaction within the period.

### Accepting Thin Documentation Of Non-Device Intent

Device is a holistic facts-and-circumstances test. A single asserted factor does not cure device risk; the analysis must address all enumerated factors.

### Overlooking The Section 355(e) 50 Percent Acquisition Rule

A spinoff that is part of a plan leading to a 50 percent acquisition of either corporation triggers tax on the distribution as if the stock were sold at fair market value.

### Stating A Business Purpose Without Operational Substance

Tax savings is not a business purpose. The purpose must be real and substantial to the business, documented with board action and operational rationale.

### Misjudging Continuity With Prearranged Shareholder Dispositions

Prearranged sales of distributed stock can destroy continuity under the step transaction doctrine, recharacterizing the spinoff.

### Treating Tracking Stock As A Section 355 Distribution

Tracking stock is not stock of a separate corporation and does not qualify for Section 355 treatment. The distribution must be of genuine corporate stock.

### Assuming One Corporation's Qualification Covers Both

Each corporation must independently satisfy control, active trade or business, and the other requirements. A failure by either defeats the spinoff.

## Self-Check

- Has the distribution been analyzed from the presumption of taxability, with each Section 355 requirement traced to supporting facts?
- Does the distributing corporation own control of the controlled corporation immediately before the distribution, measured by 80 percent of voting power and 80 percent of each class of stock?
- Is each corporation engaged in an active trade or business that has been actively conducted for the full five-year period and not acquired in a taxable transaction during that period?
- Has the device-to-distribute-earnings test been assessed across all enumerated factors, with non-device intent documented holistically?
- Is there a real and substantial corporate business purpose, documented with board resolutions and operational rationale, not merely tax savings?
- Are continuity of interest and continuity of business enterprise satisfied, with no prearranged dispositions that would trigger the step transaction doctrine?
- Has the Section 355(e) anti-acquisition rule been evaluated for any acquisition of 50 percent or more of either corporation within the relevant window, with plan factors analyzed?
- Have the distributing and controlled corporations each been analyzed separately against every requirement, recognizing that a failure by either defeats the spinoff?
- Has the distributed interest been confirmed as genuine stock of a separate corporation rather than tracking stock or a synthetic interest?
- Has the agent escalated to a qualified tax professional, tax attorney, or CPA for any specific Section 355 determination, recognizing this analysis is not tax advice?
