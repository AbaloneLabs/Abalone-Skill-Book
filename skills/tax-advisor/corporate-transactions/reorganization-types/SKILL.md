# Reorganization Types

Section 368(a) defines a set of corporate reorganization transactions that qualify for tax-free treatment, meaning gain or loss is not recognized at the time of the exchange (subject to boot and other limitations). The judgment problem is that "tax-free reorganization" is a technical term of art, not a description of any friendly corporate combination. Each of the seven statutory types, A through G, has its own structural requirements, and all reorganizations must satisfy the overarching continuity requirements: continuity of interest and continuity of the business enterprise. Agents tend to fixate on the formal type label and miss that the continuity doctrines, the plan of reorganization, and the step-transaction doctrine can collapse a facially qualifying structure into a taxable sale.

The harm is severe. A transaction that fails reorganization status is a taxable sale, triggering gain on appreciated assets, often with no cash to pay the tax, and potentially imposing double tax at both the entity and shareholder levels. Agents frequently misclassify types (calling a stock-for-assets deal a Type A merger, or a divisive spinoff a Type C), under-document the plan of reorganization, or assume that continuity of interest is satisfied by a small equity stake when the doctrine requires a substantial and continuing interest. The IRS and courts look through form to substance, and aggressive prearranged dispositions of stock can destroy continuity retroactively.

This skill covers federal US corporate reorganization analysis under Section 368. It addresses type selection and the continuity requirements. It does not cover the detailed mechanics of Section 355 spinoffs, which are a separate skill, nor the consolidated return or liquidation rules. All statutory references reflect the current federal framework but must be verified against current law. This is structural transactional tax analysis, not tax advice; a qualified tax professional, tax attorney, or CPA must be consulted for any specific transaction.

## Core Rules

### Treat Tax-Free Reorganization As A Privilege That Must Be Earned, Not A Default

Nonrecognition under Section 368 is an exception to the general rule that a sale or exchange of appreciated property triggers gain recognition. The exception applies only when the transaction fits one of the statutory types and satisfies the continuity requirements. The burden is on the taxpayer to establish qualification. An agent should begin every reorganization analysis from the presumption of taxability and identify the specific basis for nonrecognition, rather than assuming a corporate combination is automatically tax-free.

This framing matters because the consequences of failure are asymmetric. A taxable transaction that was assumed to be tax-free can leave the parties with a large tax liability and no liquidity, while a tax-free transaction that was conservatively treated as taxable merely defers a benefit. The agent should verify each requirement affirmatively and document the basis for the opinion, recognizing that the reorganization label is only as good as the underlying facts.

### Distinguish The Seven Statutory Types By Structure

The types differ by the form of the transaction. Type A is a statutory merger or consolidation under applicable state or foreign law, the most flexible type because it permits the use of cash and other consideration and is not limited to stock. Type B is a stock-for-stock acquisition in which the acquiring corporation obtains control of the target solely in exchange for voting stock of the acquiring corporation (or its parent). Type C is a stock-for-assets acquisition in which the acquiring corporation obtains substantially all of the target's properties in exchange for voting stock and limited other consideration. Type D is an acquisitive or divisive reorganization involving a transfer of assets followed by distribution of stock, governed by Section 355-type requirements. Type E is a recapitalization. Type F is a mere change in identity, form, or place of organization. Type G is a transfer of assets by a corporation in a Title 11 bankruptcy case.

The agent must match the transaction structure to the correct type, because each type imposes different constraints on consideration, asset scope, and follow-on distributions. A Type B requires solely voting stock; a Type C requires substantially all properties and limits non-stock consideration; a Type A permits cash and mixed consideration but requires a statutory merger. Selecting the wrong type leads to failing that type's requirements and losing nonrecognition.

### Apply Continuity Of Interest As A Universal Requirement

Continuity of interest requires that the target's shareholders retain a substantial and continuing proprietary interest in the acquiring corporation or its successor. This is a judicial doctrine applied to all reorganization types. A historical benchmark is that the target shareholders must receive acquiring corporation stock representing at least 40 percent of the total consideration, though the regulations provide a more nuanced framework and the precise threshold can depend on the facts. The continuity must be real and continuing, not a momentary transfer followed by a disposition.

The trap is prearranged sales of the acquiring stock by target shareholders. If the target shareholders are bound or effectively required to sell the stock they receive, continuity is destroyed and the transaction becomes taxable. The agent must examine any prearranged disposition arrangements, lock-up agreements, and the substance of the consideration. A small or ephemeral equity stake will not satisfy continuity, and the step-transaction doctrine can combine separate steps into a single taxable sale.

### Apply Continuity Of The Business Enterprise

Continuity of the business enterprise requires that the acquiring corporation either continue the target's historic business or use a significant portion of the target's historic business assets in a business. The doctrine prevents a reorganization from being used as a vehicle for an asset sale followed by liquidation. If the acquiring corporation simply sells off the target's assets and exits the business, continuity of enterprise fails.

The agent should confirm that the acquiring corporation has a genuine plan to continue the business or use the assets in an ongoing business. A transaction structured as a reorganization but followed immediately by an asset disposal is vulnerable. The historic business and historic assets must be identified, and the acquiring corporation's post-transaction use must satisfy the continuity standard.

### Require And Draft A Proper Plan Of Reorganization

A reorganization must be carried out pursuant to a plan of reorganization. The plan identifies the transactions that constitute the reorganization and bounds the steps that are part of the integrated transaction. The plan matters for several reasons: it defines what is included in the reorganization for purposes of the continuity and other tests, it affects the tax treatment of affected parties, and it is referenced on the returns and ruling requests. A poorly drafted or missing plan can cause steps to be treated separately, breaking the reorganization.

The agent should ensure the plan is comprehensive, identifies all constituent steps, and is adopted before the transactions occur. Post-hoc plans are weak. The plan should also address the treatment of dissenting shareholders, the assumption of liabilities, and any distributions of boot, because these affect recognition.

### Evaluate Boot And Other Limitation Rules

Even in a tax-free reorganization, the receipt of boot (cash or other property in addition to stock) can trigger gain recognition. In a Type A or Type C, the target or its shareholders may recognize gain to the extent of boot received. The agent must compute the boot, determine which party recognizes gain, and apply the limitation that recognized gain cannot exceed realized gain. Assumption of liabilities is generally not treated as boot, but the liability assumption rules have anti-abuse exceptions, particularly where liabilities are assumed for non-business reasons or in excess of basis.

The agent should not assume that a reorganization is entirely tax-free when cash or other property changes hands. Each dollar of boot can trigger a dollar of gain (up to realized gain), and the interaction with basis, holding period, and character must be traced. The liability rules also require scrutiny, because assumed liabilities that exceed basis or that lack a business purpose can create unexpected recognition.

### Use The Step Transaction Doctrine Cautiously

The step transaction doctrine treats multiple formally separate steps as a single integrated transaction when they are parts of a prearranged plan to achieve a particular result. In reorganizations, the doctrine can combine steps to either support or defeat nonrecognition. A binding commitment for target shareholders to sell their acquiring stock, combined with the reorganization, can be collapsed into a taxable sale. Conversely, related steps can be combined to satisfy a reorganization type's requirements.

The agent should analyze the transaction as the IRS and courts would, asking whether the steps are interdependent and prearranged. The doctrine cuts both ways, so the agent should not rely on formal separation of steps to achieve a tax result if the substance is an integrated plan. Where the intended result depends on step treatment, the facts supporting integration or separation should be documented contemporaneously.

### Consider Rulings And Reporting For Complex Or Novel Structures

For novel, large, or aggressive reorganization structures, taxpayers often seek an advance ruling from the IRS to confirm qualification. Even without a ruling, the reorganization must be reported correctly on the parties' returns, including the disclosure of the plan of reorganization and the treatment of each step. The agent should flag structures where qualification is uncertain and recommend ruling consideration or at minimum robust disclosure and opinion support.

Uncertain reorganization positions carry significant risk because failure recharacterizes the entire transaction as taxable. The agent should not bury uncertainty; the facts and the basis for the position should be documented, and where the stakes are high, a qualified tax opinion should be obtained.

## Common Traps

### Labeling A Transaction With The Wrong Reorganization Type

Each type has distinct requirements. A Type B requires solely voting stock; a Type C requires substantially all assets. Mismatching the structure to the type fails qualification.

### Assuming Continuity Of Interest Is Satisfied By A Small Or Ephemeral Equity Stake

Continuity requires a substantial and continuing proprietary interest. Prearranged dispositions of acquiring stock can destroy continuity retroactively.

### Overlooking Continuity Of The Business Enterprise

If the acquiring corporation exits the target's business or sells the assets, continuity of enterprise fails, even if the form is a reorganization.

### Failing To Draft Or Adopt A Plan Of Reorganization

Without a proper plan, steps may be treated separately, breaking the reorganization. Post-hoc plans are weak and may be disregarded.

### Ignoring Boot And Gain Recognition Within A Reorganization

Cash or other property received as boot triggers gain recognition up to realized gain. A reorganization with boot is not entirely tax-free.

### Relying On Formal Separation Of Steps When Substance Is Integrated

The step transaction doctrine can collapse formally separate steps into a taxable sale if they are prearranged parts of a single plan.

### Treating Any Friendly Corporate Combination As Automatically Tax-Free

Nonrecognition is an exception that must be earned by fitting a type and satisfying continuity. The default is taxability.

## Self-Check

- Has the transaction been analyzed from the presumption of taxability, with the specific statutory type and basis for nonrecognition identified and documented?
- Does the transaction structure match the requirements of the claimed type, including consideration form for Type B, asset scope for Type C, and statutory merger for Type A?
- Is continuity of interest satisfied by a substantial and continuing proprietary interest, with no prearranged dispositions that would destroy continuity under the step transaction doctrine?
- Is continuity of the business enterprise satisfied by continuation of the target's historic business or significant use of its historic assets?
- Has a comprehensive plan of reorganization been drafted and adopted before the transactions, identifying all constituent steps?
- Has boot been computed and gain recognition determined for any cash or other property received, with the limitation to realized gain applied?
- Have assumed liabilities been reviewed for anti-abuse exceptions, including liabilities exceeding basis or lacking business purpose?
- Has the step transaction doctrine been applied as the IRS and courts would, testing whether formally separate steps are prearranged parts of a single plan?
- For novel or aggressive structures, has an advance ruling or qualified tax opinion been considered given the high cost of failed requalification?
- Has the agent escalated to a qualified tax professional, tax attorney, or CPA for any specific reorganization determination, recognizing this analysis is not tax advice?
