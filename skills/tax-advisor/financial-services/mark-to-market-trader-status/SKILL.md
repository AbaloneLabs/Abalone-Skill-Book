# Mark-to-Market Trader Status

The mark-to-market election under Section 475(f) is one of the most powerful but most frequently misapplied tools for an active securities trader. A trader in securities (or commodities) who makes the election reports their trading positions at fair market value at year-end, recognizing unrealized gains and losses as if the positions were sold, and the resulting gains and losses are ordinary rather than capital. The benefit is that a mark-to-market trader can deduct net trading losses in full against ordinary income, with no capital loss limitation, and can carry back a net operating loss. The judgment problem is that the election is available only to a taxpayer whose trading activity rises to the level of a trade or business (a trader, not an investor), the election must be made by a strict deadline, and the conversion of capital losses to ordinary losses is a one-way ratchet that cannot be undone. An agent who recommends the election without confirming trader status and the deadline, or who fails to explain that the election converts all trading gains and losses to ordinary, will produce a disallowed election or an unwelcome surprise.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, trader status requires that the activity be substantial, regular, frequent, and continuous, and the IRS and the courts look to the number of trades, the frequency, the dollar amounts, the holding periods, and the time devoted, with no single bright-line threshold, so an investor with moderate activity will not qualify. Second, the Section 475(f) election for an existing trader must be made by the due date of the prior-year return (with extensions), and a new trader must make the election by two and a half months after the year begins, and a missed deadline generally cannot be cured. Third, the election applies to all securities held as trading positions, so a trader who also holds investment positions must identify which are which, because the election does not apply to investment positions and the failure to identify can taint the investment portfolio. Fourth, the year-one election requires a catch-up adjustment that includes the unrealized gain on prior positions in ordinary income, which can produce a large year-one tax bill.

This skill covers the Section 475(f) mark-to-market election and trader status under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Trader Status Before Recommending The Election

The mark-to-market election under Section 475(f) is available only to a trader in securities or commodities, defined as a person whose trading activity rises to the level of a trade or business. The IRS and the courts apply a facts-and-circumstances test focusing on whether the taxpayer seeks to profit from the short-term price swings of securities (rather than from long-term appreciation or dividends), whether the activity is substantial, and whether the activity is carried on with continuity and regularity. The key indicators are the number and frequency of trades (often several trades per day, several days per week), the dollar volume, the short holding periods (often intraday or a few days), and the substantial time devoted (often several hours per day). There is no single bright-line threshold, but the cumulative pattern must demonstrate a business-like trading operation.

Document the trading activity to support trader status before making the election. Obtain the brokerage statements and the trade logs, and compute the number of trades, the frequency, the dollar amounts, the average holding period, and the time the taxpayer devoted. A taxpayer with a few hundred trades per year, longer holding periods, and limited daily time is more likely an investor and will not qualify. A taxpayer with thousands of short-term trades executed frequently throughout the year with substantial daily time is a stronger candidate. Trader status is the gating condition, because an election by a non-trader is void, and the IRS audits mark-to-market elections by first challenging trader status. If the activity does not clearly rise to the trade-or-business level, do not make the election.

### Make The Election By The Strict Deadline

The timing of the Section 475(f) election depends on whether the taxpayer is an existing trader or a new trader. For an existing trader (one who was a trader in the prior year), the election must be made by the unextended due date of the prior-year return (April 15 for an individual, or the extended due date if an extension was filed, but the election statement and the Form 3115 must be filed by the original due date for the accounting-method-change approach). For a new trader (one who begins trading in the current year), the election must be made by two and a half months after the beginning of the year (March 15 for a calendar-year taxpayer). The election is made by filing a statement of election and, for an existing trader, by filing Form 3115 (Application for Change in Accounting Method) with the return.

Calendar the deadline before recommending the election. A missed deadline generally cannot be cured, and the election is not available for the year if the deadline passes. Confirm whether the taxpayer is an existing or new trader, and file the election statement and the Form 3115 (if required) by the applicable date. The deadline is strict, and the IRS does not grant extensions for the election itself, so the timing must be managed proactively. A taxpayer who wants the election for the current year should be identified well before the deadline, so the trader-status analysis and the election filing can be completed on time.

### Apply The Election To Trading Securities And Identify Investment Positions

The Section 475(f) election applies to all securities that are held as part of the taxpayer's trading business, and it does not apply to securities held for investment. A trader who holds both trading and investment positions must identify which positions are trading and which are investment at the time of acquisition (or at the time of the election for existing positions), because the identification determines whether the mark-to-market treatment applies. Securities held in a retirement account or other tax-deferred vehicle are not affected, and the election does not apply to commodities unless a separate election is made.

Identify and segregate the trading and investment positions. The identification must be made by the end of the day on which the position is acquired (or by the date of the election for existing positions), and it should be documented in the brokerage records. A common error is failing to identify investment positions, which can result in all positions being treated as trading positions subject to mark-to-market, converting investment gains and losses to ordinary. Conversely, a trader who holds positions for investment must keep them separate to preserve capital treatment. The identification requirement is strict, and the IRS examines whether the positions were properly identified.

### Compute The Year-One Catch-Up Adjustment

In the first year of the Section 475(f) election, the taxpayer must include in ordinary income a catch-up adjustment equal to the difference between the fair market value and the cost (or prior adjusted basis) of the trading positions held at the beginning of the election year, as if those positions were sold at the beginning of the year. This catch-up recognizes the unrealized gain (or loss) on prior positions as ordinary income (or loss) in the election year, converting what would have been capital gain to ordinary. For a trader with substantial unrealized gains on prior positions, the catch-up can produce a large year-one tax bill.

Model the catch-up adjustment before making the election. Compute the unrealized gain on the trading positions held at the beginning of the election year, and confirm the taxpayer can absorb the resulting ordinary income. If the catch-up produces a large tax liability that the taxpayer cannot fund, the election may be counterproductive despite the long-term benefit. For a trader with unrealized losses, the catch-up produces an ordinary loss that may generate a net operating loss carryback, which is a benefit. The catch-up is a one-time consequence of the election, but it must be understood and planned for before electing.

### Treat All Trading Gains And Losses As Ordinary

Once the Section 475(f) election is in effect, all gains and losses on trading securities are ordinary, not capital. This is the core benefit for a trader with net losses, because ordinary losses are not subject to the capital loss limitation (USD 3,000 annual deduction against ordinary income for individuals) and can be carried back as a net operating loss. However, the conversion is a two-way ratchet: net trading gains are also ordinary, so a profitable trader loses the benefit of the long-term capital gains rate and the lower ordinary rates on trading gains. The election is most valuable for a trader who expects net losses or high volatility, and least valuable for a consistently profitable trader.

Model the ordinary treatment against the capital treatment before and after the election. A trader with consistent net gains pays more tax under mark-to-market (ordinary rates on all gains) than under the default (capital rates on long-term gains, although most trading gains are short-term anyway). A trader with net losses benefits from the full ordinary deduction and the NOL carryback. The conversion is permanent for the years the election is in effect, and revoking the election requires IRS consent (another accounting-method change), so the decision should be based on a realistic projection of the trading results. Also confirm that the trading expenses (such as margin interest, data feeds, and home office) are deductible as business expenses under Section 162, which is a separate benefit of trader status.

### File The Required Forms And Maintain The Records

A mark-to-market trader files Form 4797 (for the trading gains and losses, treated as ordinary) and Form 3115 (in the year of election, for the accounting-method change), and the brokerage 1099-B must be reconciled to the mark-to-market computation. The trader must maintain the year-end position statements showing the fair market value of each position, the cost basis, and the unrealized gain or loss, and the records must support the mark-to-market computation. The identification of trading versus investment positions must be documented.

Maintain the records and file the forms each year. The year-end position statements are the basis for the mark-to-market computation, and the brokerage 1099-B (which reports actual sales) must be reconciled to the Form 4797 ordinary income. A common error is double-counting (reporting the sales on Schedule D and the mark-to-market on Form 4797), so the reconciliation is essential. The records must be retained to support the computation, because the IRS audits mark-to-market returns for both the trader-status qualification and the computation accuracy.

## Common Traps

### Making The Election Without Confirming Trader Status

The symptom is a taxpayer with moderate trading activity who makes the mark-to-market election. The trap is that the activity may not rise to a trade or business, so the election is void and the mark-to-market treatment is disallowed. The direction is to document the trading activity (number, frequency, dollar amounts, holding periods, time) and to confirm trader status before electing.

### Missing The Election Deadline

The symptom is a trader who decides to elect mark-to-market after the deadline. The trap is that the deadline (prior-year return due date for existing traders, March 15 for new traders) is strict and generally cannot be cured, so the election is unavailable for the year. The direction is to calendar the deadline and to file the election statement and Form 3115 on time.

### Failing To Identify Investment Positions

The symptom is a trader who holds both trading and investment positions without identification. The trap is that the failure to identify can taint the investment portfolio, converting investment gains and losses to ordinary. The direction is to identify trading and investment positions at acquisition and to document the identification in the brokerage records.

### Overlooking The Year-One Catch-Up Tax

The symptom is a trader who elects mark-to-market and is surprised by a large year-one tax bill. The trap is that the catch-up adjustment includes the unrealized gain on prior positions as ordinary income, which can be substantial. The direction is to model the catch-up before electing and to confirm the taxpayer can absorb the income.

### Treating A Profitable Trader's Gains As Capital

The symptom is a mark-to-market trader who reports net trading gains as capital. The trap is that the election converts all trading gains to ordinary, so the capital treatment is incorrect and understates the tax. The direction is to report all trading gains and losses as ordinary on Form 4797.

### Double-Counting Sales On Schedule D And Form 4797

The symptom is a mark-to-market trader who reports sales on both Schedule D and Form 4797. The trap is that the actual sales are reflected in the mark-to-market computation, so reporting them on Schedule D double-counts the income. The direction is to reconcile the brokerage 1099-B to the Form 4797 and to avoid double-counting.
