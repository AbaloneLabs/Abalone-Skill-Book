# Derivative and Hedge Accounting

Derivative instruments (options, futures, forwards, swaps, and similar contracts) present some of the most complex tax classification questions in financial taxation because their character, timing, and rate depend on the type of instrument, the taxpayer's status, and whether the position is part of a hedge or a straddle. A regulated futures contract traded on a U.S. exchange is subject to the Section 1256 60/40 treatment (60 percent long-term, 40 percent short-term capital gain or loss) and is marked to market at year-end. A non-Section 1256 option or swap is generally capital, but its timing depends on whether it is marked to market (for a trader who elected Section 475(f)) or settled. A hedging transaction under Regulation Section 1.1221-2 is ordinary in character, and the gain or loss is matched to the hedged item, which changes both the character and the timing. A straddle under Section 1092 defers the loss on one leg to the extent of the unrealized gain on the offsetting leg. The judgment problem is that the same instrument can be capital or ordinary, long-term or short-term, current or deferred, depending on classification, and a mischaracterization produces a misstated return with significant tax and penalty exposure.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the Section 1256 60/40 treatment is automatic for qualifying contracts, and it applies even to a single-day hold, so a one-day gain on a regulated futures contract is 60 percent long-term. Second, a hedging transaction is ordinary, not capital, and the identification and the recordkeeping requirements must be met, or the hedge is treated as capital. Third, the straddle rules under Section 1092 apply to offsetting positions (including a long stock and a short call, or a long put and a long call) and defer the loss on the loss leg to the extent of the unrealized gain on the gain leg, so a taxpayer who closes the loss leg cannot deduct the loss while the gain leg is open. Fourth, the wash sale rule under Section 1091 applies to options and contracts as well as to stock, and it can be triggered by an option that is substantially identical to the stock. These rules interact, so a derivative position may be subject to the 60/40 treatment, the hedging rules, the straddle rules, and the wash sale rules simultaneously.

This skill covers the U.S. federal tax treatment of derivatives, hedging transactions, and straddles. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Section 1256 60/40 Treatment To Qualifying Contracts

Section 1256 governs the tax treatment of regulated futures contracts, foreign currency contracts, nonequity options, and dealer equity options. The key benefit is the 60/40 treatment: 60 percent of the gain or loss is treated as long-term capital and 40 percent as short-term capital, regardless of the actual holding period, so even a one-day gain on a regulated futures contract is 60 percent long-term. The contracts are marked to market at the end of the tax year, so open positions are treated as sold at fair market value and the gain or loss is recognized, with the basis adjusted to the fair market value. The mark-to-market and the 60/40 treatment apply automatically to qualifying contracts, and the gains and losses are reported on Form 6781.

Confirm whether a contract qualifies for Section 1256. A regulated futures contract is a contract traded on or subject to the rules of a qualified board or exchange. A nonequity option is an option whose underlying is not a single stock (such as a broad-based index option or a currency option), but narrow-based equity indexes may not qualify. Dealer equity options are options held by a dealer. Report the 60/40 split and the mark-to-market on Form 6781, and carry the results to Schedule D. A trader who elected Section 475(f) mark-to-market may elect out of Section 1256 for the 60/40 treatment, but this is a narrow election. The 60/40 treatment is automatic and beneficial, so confirm the contract type and apply the treatment.

### Identify And Document Hedging Transactions Under Regulation Section 1.1221-2

A hedging transaction is a transaction that the taxpayer enters into in the normal course of business primarily to manage the risk of price changes, interest rate changes, or currency fluctuations. The gain or loss on a hedging transaction is ordinary, not capital, and the character and the timing are matched to the hedged item, so the hedge's gain or loss is recognized when the hedged item's gain or loss is recognized. The hedging transaction must be identified in the taxpayer's records on the day it is entered into (the identification requirement), and the hedged item must be identified in the records (the recordkeeping requirement), or the hedge is treated as capital.

Identify and document the hedging transactions. The identification must be made on the day the hedge is entered into, and the recordkeeping must link the hedge to the hedged item. A business that hedges inventory price risk with a futures contract must identify the futures contract as a hedge and link it to the inventory, so the gain or loss on the futures is ordinary and is recognized when the inventory is sold. A business that hedges interest rate risk with a swap must identify the swap as a hedge and link it to the debt. The identification and recordkeeping requirements are strict, and a failure to identify or to document results in capital treatment, which can be a significant detriment if the hedge produces a large gain. The hedging rules apply to businesses (not to individual investors), and the hedge must be in the normal course of the business's operations.

### Apply The Straddle Rules Under Section 1092 To Offsetting Positions

A straddle is a set of offsetting positions in actively traded personal property, where the positions substantially reduce the risk of loss. The straddle rules under Section 1092 defer the loss on one leg to the extent of the unrealized gain on the offsetting leg, so a taxpayer who closes the loss leg cannot deduct the loss while the gain leg is open and has unrealized gain. The deferred loss is allowed when the gain leg is closed or when the unrealized gain is eliminated. The straddle rules apply to offsetting positions in stock, options, futures, and other actively traded personal property, and they are designed to prevent a taxpayer from recognizing a loss on one leg while holding an offsetting gain.

Identify the straddles and apply the loss deferral. A common straddle is a long stock and a short call (a covered call), or a long put and a long call on the same underlying. The straddle rules defer the loss on the loss leg to the extent of the unrealized gain on the gain leg, so the taxpayer must compute the unrealized gain on the open leg and defer the loss accordingly. The wash sale rule under Section 1091 also applies to straddles, so a loss on a leg that is replaced by a substantially identical position within the wash sale period is disallowed and added to the basis of the replacement position. The straddle rules and the wash sale rules interact, and a taxpayer with offsetting positions must apply both. The straddle rules do not apply to hedging transactions (which are ordinary) or to Section 1256 contracts (which are marked to market).

### Distinguish Capital Derivatives From Ordinary Derivatives

The character of a derivative depends on whether it is a capital asset in the hands of the taxpayer. A derivative held for investment is generally a capital asset, so the gain or loss is capital. A derivative held by a dealer (holding inventory for sale to customers) is ordinary. A derivative that is part of a hedging transaction is ordinary under Regulation Section 1.1221-2. A derivative held by a trader who elected Section 475(f) is ordinary, because the election converts the trading gains and losses to ordinary. A notional principal contract (such as a swap) that is not a hedging transaction is generally capital for a non-dealer, but the periodic payments may be ordinary.

Classify the derivative based on the taxpayer's status and the purpose. An investor's options and futures are capital (with the Section 1256 60/40 treatment for qualifying contracts). A dealer's derivatives are ordinary. A hedger's derivatives are ordinary (with the identification and recordkeeping). A trader's derivatives are capital unless the Section 475(f) election is made, in which case they are ordinary. The character drives the rate and the loss limitation, so a mischaracterization (such as treating a hedge as capital) produces a misstated return. Confirm the status and the purpose before classifying the derivative, and apply the hedging rules, the straddle rules, and the Section 1256 treatment as applicable.

### Evaluate The Wash Sale Rule For Options And Contracts

The wash sale rule under Section 1091 disallows a loss on a sale of stock or securities if the taxpayer acquires substantially identical stock or securities within the 61-day period (30 days before to 30 days after the sale). The rule applies to options and contracts as well as to stock, so a loss on a call option can be disallowed if the taxpayer buys a substantially identical call option within the wash sale period, and a loss on stock can be disallowed if the taxpayer buys a call option on the same stock within the wash sale period (because the option is considered substantially identical to the stock). The disallowed loss is added to the basis of the replacement position.

Apply the wash sale rule to the derivative positions. A loss on a derivative is disallowed if a substantially identical position is acquired within the wash sale period, and the disallowed loss is added to the basis of the replacement. The wash sale rule applies to options on the same stock, and it can be triggered by an option that is considered substantially identical to the stock (such as an in-the-money call option). The wash sale rule interacts with the straddle rules, so a taxpayer with offsetting positions must apply both. The wash sale rule does not apply to Section 1256 contracts (which are marked to market), and it does not apply to a trader who elected Section 475(f) (because the gains and losses are ordinary). The wash sale rule is a common trap for active traders, so the trade logs should be checked for wash sales before the return is prepared.

### Address The Mark-To-Market Election And The Trader Interaction

A trader who elected Section 475(f) mark-to-market reports the trading gains and losses as ordinary, and the trading positions are marked to market at year-end. The election affects the derivative treatment because the 60/40 treatment under Section 1256 is overridden for the trader's trading positions (the trader may elect to keep the 60/40 treatment for Section 1256 contracts, but this is a narrow election). The mark-to-market election also overrides the straddle rules and the wash sale rules for the trading positions, because the positions are marked to market and the gains and losses are ordinary.

Confirm the mark-to-market election and its interaction with the derivative rules. A trader with the election reports the trading derivatives as ordinary, with no capital loss limitation, no straddle loss deferral, and no wash sale rule for the trading positions. A trader without the election reports the trading derivatives as capital, with the Section 1256 60/40 treatment for qualifying contracts, the straddle rules, and the wash sale rule. The election is a major decision that affects the character, the timing, and the applicable rules, so it should be evaluated separately. The mark-to-market election is covered in the mark-to-market trader status skill, and the two skills should be used together for a trader with derivatives.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Hedging Transaction As Capital

The symptom is a business that hedges inventory or debt price risk and reports the hedge gain or loss as capital. The trap is that a hedging transaction is ordinary under Regulation Section 1.1221-2, and the gain or loss is matched to the hedged item, so the capital treatment misstates the character and the timing. The direction is to identify and document the hedging transaction and to report the gain or loss as ordinary.

### Overlooking The Section 1256 60/40 Treatment

The symptom is a taxpayer who reports a gain on a regulated futures contract as short-term capital based on the one-day holding period. The trap is that Section 1256 applies the 60/40 treatment automatically, so the gain is 60 percent long-term. The direction is to confirm the contract type and to report the 60/40 split on Form 6781.

### Ignoring The Straddle Loss Deferral

The symptom is a taxpayer who closes the loss leg of a straddle and deducts the loss while the gain leg is open. The trap is that Section 1092 defers the loss to the extent of the unrealized gain on the gain leg, so the loss is not deductible. The direction is to compute the unrealized gain on the open leg and to defer the loss accordingly.

### Triggering A Wash Sale Through An Option

The trigger is a taxpayer who sells stock at a loss and buys a call option on the same stock within the wash sale period. The trap is that the option is considered substantially identical to the stock, so the loss is disallowed and added to the basis of the option. The direction is to check the trade logs for wash sales involving options and to defer the loss and adjust the basis.

### Failing To Identify A Hedge In The Records

The symptom is a business that enters a hedging transaction but does not identify it in the records on the day it is entered into. The trap is that the identification requirement is strict, and the failure to identify results in capital treatment, which can be a significant detriment. The direction is to identify the hedge on the day it is entered into and to maintain the recordkeeping linking the hedge to the hedged item.

### Assuming A Swap Is Always Capital

The symptom is a taxpayer who reports the gain on a swap as capital without analyzing the character. The trap is that a notional principal contract's periodic payments may be ordinary, and a swap that is part of a hedging transaction is ordinary. The direction is to classify the swap based on the taxpayer's status and the purpose, and to report the character accordingly.
