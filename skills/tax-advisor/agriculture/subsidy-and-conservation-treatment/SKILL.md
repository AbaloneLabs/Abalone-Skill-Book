# Subsidy and Conservation Treatment

Agricultural operations receive money from many sources that look alike but have very different tax characters. Commodity support payments, conservation reserve program (CRP) rent, federal cost-sharing grants, state conservation subsidies, and payments for conservation easements each sit in a different part of the tax code, and the wrong classification changes self-employment tax, the qualified business income deduction, the basis of the land, and the deductibility of conservation expenses. The judgment problem is that the payment instruments and 1099s rarely state the statutory character, so the agent must determine it from the program purpose, the recipient's role, and the specific statute. An agent who reports all government and conservation money as Schedule F farm income, or who excludes all of it, will produce a return that is wrong on self-employment tax, wrong on basis, and exposed on examination.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the same CRP payment can be self-employment income for a materially participating farmer and rental income for a passive landowner, and the IRS has litigated this distinction, so the recipient's participation level is decisive. Second, cost-sharing payments under Section 126 are excludable but only to a limit and only if the program is a qualifying federal or state program, and the exclusion reduces the basis of the improved property, so a farmer who excludes without reducing basis understates gain on a later sale. Third, conservation expenses under Section 175 are deductible currently only for land in production, while soil and water conservation expenditures under Section 182 have their own rules, and the cost of a permanent easement is generally capitalized and not deductible, so conflating these provisions produces either a disallowed deduction or a missed current deduction. Fourth, conservation easement donations under Section 170(h) are heavily scrutinized and require a qualified appraisal, a qualified organization, and conservation purposes, and syndicated easement transactions are on the IRS abusive-list.

This skill covers the tax treatment of agricultural subsidies, conservation program payments, cost-sharing exclusions, and conservation expenses and easements under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are fact-intensive, jurisdiction-dependent, and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Determine The Statutory Character Of Each Government Payment

The first step with any government or program payment is to determine its statutory character. Commodity support payments (such as Agriculture Risk Coverage and Price Loss Coverage) are payments for the production or sale of agricultural commodities and are Schedule F farm income subject to self-employment tax for a farmer in business. Conservation Reserve Program (CRP) payments are annual rental payments for taking environmentally sensitive land out of production, and their character depends on the recipient's participation. Federal disaster and emergency relief payments are generally income but may be deferrable or excludable depending on the statute. State agricultural program payments follow the federal character unless a specific state rule applies.

Obtain the program documentation and the 1099-G or 1099-MISC for each payment and determine the character from the program purpose, not from the form title. A payment tied to producing or selling commodities is farm income; a payment for retiring land is rent; a payment that reimburses capital costs may be a cost-sharing exclusion. Do not rely on the 1099 box alone, because payors sometimes use the wrong box, and the tax character follows the substance of the payment. Document the determination so it is defensible on examination.

### Apply The CRP Participation Test For Self-Employment Tax

CRP payments are the most common source of misclassification. For a farmer who is materially participating in the conservation effort (such as performing the required maintenance, weed control, and practice implementation), the IRS and the Tax Court have treated CRP payments as income from a farming trade or business subject to self-employment tax, reportable on Schedule F. For a passive landowner who merely receives rent and does not materially participate, CRP payments are rental income reportable on Schedule E and generally not subject to self-employment tax. The distinction turns on the recipient's actual participation, not on the land's use.

Determine the recipient's participation level before choosing the schedule. A retired farmer or an absentee landowner who simply receives the annual CRP rent and does not perform the conservation practices is generally a passive recipient reporting on Schedule E. An active farmer who manages the enrolled acreage as part of the farming operation and performs the required practices reports on Schedule F and pays SE tax. The wrong choice produces an SE tax error in one direction or the other, and because CRP enrollment often lasts ten or fifteen years, the error compounds. Confirm the participation facts for each year, because participation can change.

### Apply The Section 126 Cost-Sharing Exclusion And Its Basis Reduction

Section 126 allows a farmer to exclude from gross income all or part of a cost-sharing payment received under a qualifying federal or state conservation program, such as the Environmental Quality Incentives Program (EQIP) or certain state programs. The exclusion is limited to the greater of the excludable portion determined by a formula (the present value of the forgone income from the conservation practice) or 50 percent of the payment, with the balance includible in income. The exclusion is only available for qualifying programs, and the Secretary of Agriculture publishes the list of qualifying programs.

Apply the basis-reduction rule whenever the exclusion is claimed. The excluded amount reduces the basis of the property improved by the conservation practice, so that the benefit is not received tax-free and then recovered again through depreciation or reduced gain on sale. A farmer who excludes a cost-sharing payment and then depreciates the full cost of the improvement, or who fails to reduce basis, double-counts the benefit and understates gain on a later sale. Track the excluded amount and the basis reduction in the property records, and confirm the program qualifies under Section 126 before claiming the exclusion.

### Deduct Conservation Expenses Under The Correct Section

Conservation expenditures are governed by two separate sections that are often confused. Section 175 allows a current deduction for soil and water conservation expenses that are consistent with a plan approved by the Natural Resources Conservation Service or a comparable state agency, but only for land used in farming, and the deduction does not apply to the cost of acquiring or improving depreciable assets or to land clearing that creates a permanent improvement with a useful life beyond the deduction year. Section 182 allows a current deduction for fertilizers and soil conditionants (lime, gypsum) applied to farmland, with the deduction limited to 25 percent of the gross income from farming during the year and the excess carried forward.

Distinguish current-deductible conservation expenses from capitalized land improvements. Ordinary erosion-control structures such as terraces, waterways, and diversions that do not extend beyond the deduction year may be deductible under Section 175 if they are part of an approved plan, but the cost of land clearing, draining, or leveling that creates a permanent improvement is generally capitalized and added to land basis, recoverable only on sale. The cost of a permanent conservation easement is generally capitalized (or, if donated, may be a charitable deduction under Section 170(h)). Do not deduct a capitalized land improvement as a current conservation expense, because the deduction will be disallowed and the basis lost.

### Handle Conservation Easement Donations Under Section 170(h)

A donation of a qualified conservation contribution under Section 170(h) allows a deduction if the easement is granted to a qualified organization, serves at least one of the listed conservation purposes (public recreation, natural habitat, open space, historic), and is perpetual. The deduction is generally limited to 50 percent of the donor's contribution base (100 percent for qualified farmers and ranchers), with a 15-year carryforward. The substantiation requirements are strict: a qualified appraisal for easements valued over USD 5,000, a contemporaneous written acknowledgment, and a deed with the required conservation purpose and perpetual protection.

Treat conservation easement donations as high-scrutiny transactions. The IRS actively audits easement donations, and the most common disqualifiers are an appraisal that does not meet the qualified-appraisal standard, a deed that allows too much reserved development (defeating the conservation purpose), and a lack of perpetuity. Syndicated conservation easements, where investors buy into a partnership that donates an easement valued far above the investment, are on the IRS listed-transaction and abusive-promotion lists and carry severe penalties. Confirm the qualified organization, the qualified appraisal, the conservation purpose, and the perpetuity provisions before claiming any easement deduction, and decline to support a syndicated easement that exhibits the abuse pattern.

### Separate Subsidy And Conservation Income From Land-Sale Gain

Government payments and conservation expenses interact with the basis of the land, and a farmer who receives cost-sharing exclusions, deducts conservation expenses, or grants easements has a basis picture that changes over time. On the sale of the farm, the gain depends on the adjusted basis, which reflects prior cost-sharing exclusions (basis reduced), capitalized conservation improvements (basis increased), and easement grants (basis allocated to the easement or reduced). An agent who ignores the multi-year basis history will misreport the gain on a farm sale.

Reconstruct the land basis before reporting any farm or ranch sale. Confirm whether cost-sharing exclusions reduced basis, whether capitalized improvements increased basis, and whether an easement grant reduced or allocated basis. The same applies to involuntary conversion of farmland and to like-kind exchanges of real property, where the basis carries over and the prior conservation history must be reflected. The basis reconstruction is essential because conservation and subsidy events often occurred years earlier and the records are incomplete.

## Common Traps

### Reporting All CRP Payments As Schedule F Farm Income

The symptom is a farmer or landowner whose CRP payments are all on Schedule F with SE tax. The trap is that a passive landowner's CRP rent is Schedule E rental income not subject to SE tax, so the blanket Schedule F treatment overstates SE tax for passive recipients. The direction is to determine material participation for each recipient and year and to split the reporting accordingly.

### Excluding Cost-Sharing Without Reducing Basis

The symptom is a farmer who excludes an EQIP payment under Section 126 and then depreciates the full practice cost. The trap is that the exclusion requires a basis reduction, so failing to reduce it double-counts the benefit and understates gain on a later sale. The direction is to reduce the basis of the improved property by the excluded amount and to track the reduction in the property records.

### Deducting A Permanent Land Improvement As A Conservation Expense

The symptom is a farmer who deducts land clearing, draining, or leveling as a Section 175 soil-and-water expense. The trap is that permanent improvements with a useful life beyond the deduction year are not deductible under Section 175 and must be capitalized, so the deduction is disallowed and the basis is lost. The direction is to distinguish current conservation practices under an approved plan from permanent capitalized improvements.

### Claiming An Easement Deduction Without A Qualified Appraisal

The symptom is a landowner who claims a large easement deduction with an appraisal prepared by a non-qualified appraiser or lacking the required detail. The trap is that the substantiation rules for easements over USD 5,000 require a qualified appraisal and contemporaneous acknowledgment, and a failure disqualifies the entire deduction. The direction is to confirm the appraiser's qualifications, the appraisal method, and the deed's conservation purpose and perpetuity before claiming the deduction.

### Supporting A Syndicated Conservation Easement

The symptom is an investor pitched a partnership interest with an easement deduction far exceeding the cash invested. The trap is that syndicated conservation easements are on the IRS abusive-promotion list, carry accuracy-related and promoter penalties, and are routinely disallowed in full. The direction is to recognize the pattern (inflated appraisal, promoted deduction-to-investment ratio, partnership formed to donate) and to decline to support or recommend the transaction.

### Lumping Subsidy And Conservation Payments Into One Schedule F Line

The symptom is a farmer who reports commodity, CRP, and cost-sharing payments on a single Schedule F line. The trap is that each payment has a different character affecting SE tax, the Section 126 exclusion, and QBI, so the lumping produces errors across multiple computations. The direction is to classify each payment by its statutory character and report it on the correct line or schedule.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the statutory character of each government and program payment been determined from the program purpose and documentation, not from the 1099 box alone?
- [ ] Has the CRP participation test been applied for each recipient and year, with materially participating farmers on Schedule F and passive landowners on Schedule E?
- [ ] Has the Section 126 cost-sharing exclusion been confirmed to apply only to qualifying programs, with the exclusion amount limited and the property basis reduced by the excluded amount?
- [ ] Has each conservation expense been classified under Section 175 (approved-plan soil and water), Section 182 (fertilizer and soil conditionants), or as a capitalized permanent land improvement?
- [ ] Has any conservation easement donation been confirmed to meet the Section 170(h) requirements, including qualified organization, qualified appraisal, conservation purpose, and perpetuity?
- [ ] Has any syndicated conservation easement been recognized by its abuse pattern and declined rather than supported?
- [ ] Has the land basis been reconstructed before reporting any farm or ranch sale, reflecting cost-sharing exclusions, capitalized improvements, and easement grants?
- [ ] Has each subsidy and conservation payment been reported on the correct line or schedule to avoid SE tax, QBI, and basis errors?
