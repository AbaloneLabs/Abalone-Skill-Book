# Net Operating Loss Carryback

A net operating loss (NOL) arises when a taxpayer's allowable deductions exceed their gross income for the tax year, and the NOL can be carried back to offset income in prior years (producing a refund) or carried forward to offset income in future years (reducing future tax). The judgment problem is that the NOL rules have changed dramatically over time, and the carryback period, the carryforward period, and the election rules depend on the year the NOL arose. Under current law (post-TCJA), most NOLs cannot be carried back and are carried forward indefinitely, but they are limited to 80 percent of taxable income in the carryforward year. However, prior law (and specific statutory windows, such as the CARES Act for 2018-2020 NOLs) allowed two-year or three-year carrybacks, and certain NOLs (such as farming losses and insurance losses) have special carryback rules. An agent who applies the wrong year's rules, or who misses a carryback election deadline, forfeits a refund that the taxpayer was entitled to.

Agents commonly miss several things. First, the NOL computation is not simply deductions minus income: it requires adjustments (adding back nonbusiness deductions, removing personal exemptions, and accounting for the Section 199A deduction) to isolate the business loss. Second, the carryback election and the waiver have strict deadlines, and a missed deadline cannot be extended. Third, the Form 1045 tentative carryback application is faster than a Form 1040-X amended return, but it has a 12-month deadline. Fourth, the 80-percent limitation in the carryforward year (post-TCJA) means an NOL cannot fully eliminate taxable income, so the taxpayer may still owe tax in a carryforward year. Fifth, an NOL from a pass-through entity (a partnership or an S corporation) is computed at the entity level under the post-2022 rules (the BBA regime), but the NOL is used at the owner level, creating coordination issues.

This skill covers the computation, carryback, and carryforward of net operating losses under U.S. federal rules. It is educational guidance, not personalized tax advice; the rules are jurisdiction-dependent and change over time, and a qualified tax professional must be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Compute The NOL With The Required Adjustments

The NOL is not simply the excess of deductions over income on the tax return. It requires adjustments to isolate the business loss. The starting point is the taxable income shown on the return (which may be negative). Then, add back the nonbusiness deductions (such as the standard deduction or itemized deductions that are not attributable to a trade or business), and add back the personal exemptions (for years before 2018). Remove any nonbusiness income (such as interest, dividends, or capital gains that are not attributable to a trade or business) if it exceeds nonbusiness deductions. For years after 2017, account for the Section 199A qualified business income deduction. The result is the NOL for the year.

Compute the NOL with the adjustments. Start with the taxable income (loss) from the return, and use Form 1045 Schedule A (or the equivalent worksheet) to make the adjustments. Add back the nonbusiness deductions (the standard deduction or the itemized deductions, to the extent they exceed nonbusiness income), and add back the personal exemptions (for pre-2018 years). For post-2017 years, add back the Section 199A deduction. Remove any excess nonbusiness income over nonbusiness deductions. The result is the NOL, which is the amount available for carryback or carryforward. The adjustments are essential, because a failure to add back nonbusiness deductions overstates the NOL, and a failure to account for the Section 199A deduction understates it.

### Determine The Carryback And Carryforward Periods By Year

The carryback and carryforward periods depend on the year the NOL arose. Under the pre-2018 law, NOLs could be carried back two years and carried forward 20 years. Under the TCJA (for NOLs arising after 2017), most NOLs cannot be carried back and are carried forward indefinitely, subject to the 80-percent limitation. The CARES Act temporarily restored the two-year carryback for NOLs arising in 2018, 2019, and 2020, and allowed a five-year carryback for farming losses and insurance losses. For NOLs arising after 2020, the TCJA rules apply (no carryback, indefinite carryforward, 80-percent limit). Certain NOLs (such as farming losses and insurance company losses) have special carryback rules, often allowing a two-year carryback.

Determine the carryback and carryforward periods by the year the NOL arose. For a pre-2018 NOL, the two-year carryback and 20-year carryforward apply. For a 2018-2020 NOL, the CARES Act two-year carryback may apply (with an election to waive). For a post-2020 NOL, no carryback and indefinite carryforward apply (with the 80-percent limit). For a farming loss or an insurance loss, the special carryback rules may apply. Confirm the year the NOL arose and the applicable law, because the carryback right and the carryforward limit depend on the year. A failure to apply the correct year's rules forfeits the carryback or misapplies the carryforward limit.

### Evaluate The Carryback Election And Waiver Deadlines

The carryback election and the waiver have strict deadlines. Under the CARES Act, a taxpayer with a 2018-2020 NOL could elect to waive the two-year carryback and carry the NOL forward, and the election had to be made by a specified deadline (often the extended due date of the return for the year the NOL arose, or a later deadline set by the IRS). Under the TCJA, a taxpayer with a post-2020 NOL generally cannot carry back, but an election to carry back a specified number of years may be available for certain NOLs (such as farming losses). The waiver of a carryback must be made by the due date (including extensions) of the return for the year the NOL arose, and a missed waiver cannot be revoked.

Evaluate the carryback election and the waiver. For a 2018-2020 NOL, determine whether the taxpayer wants the two-year carryback (the default) or the waiver (carry forward only), and make the election or waiver by the deadline. For a post-2020 NOL, determine whether a carryback is available (for farming losses or other special NOLs) and make the election by the deadline. The waiver of a carryback is irrevocable, so it should be evaluated carefully. A missed deadline forfeits the election or the waiver, so the deadlines must be monitored. The carryback election and the waiver are major decisions that affect the timing of the tax benefit, so they should be evaluated with the taxpayer's cash-flow needs and the prior years' tax rates.

### File Form 1045 For A Tentative Carryback Within 12 Months

A tentative carryback application on Form 1045 is a fast-track mechanism to claim an NOL carryback refund. The Form 1045 must be filed within 12 months of the end of the NOL year (the year the loss arose), and the IRS is required to act on it within 90 days. The Form 1045 is faster than a Form 1040-X amended return, which can take several months. If the 12-month deadline for Form 1045 is missed, the taxpayer can still claim the carryback refund on a Form 1040-X, but the processing is slower.

File Form 1045 for a tentative carryback within 12 months. If the NOL year has ended and a carryback is available, prepare Form 1045 (with Schedule A for the NOL computation and the carryback schedules) and file it within 12 months of the end of the NOL year. The IRS will process the Form 1045 within 90 days, producing a faster refund. If the 12-month deadline is missed, file a Form 1040-X for each carryback year, but expect a longer processing time. The Form 1045 is the preferred mechanism for an NOL carryback, because of the speed, so it should be filed within the 12-month window whenever possible.

### Apply The 80-Percent Limitation In The Carryforward Year

Under the TCJA, an NOL carried forward to a tax year ending after 2020 is limited to 80 percent of the taxable income (computed without the NOL deduction) for the carryforward year. This means the NOL cannot fully eliminate taxable income, and the taxpayer may still owe tax in a carryforward year. The 80-percent limit applies to the taxable income before the NOL deduction, and it does not apply to NOLs arising before 2018 (which are subject to the prior law's full offset and the 20-year carryforward).

Apply the 80-percent limitation in the carryforward year. For a post-2020 NOL carried forward, compute the taxable income for the carryforward year without the NOL deduction, and limit the NOL deduction to 80 percent of that amount. The remaining NOL is carried forward to the next year. The 80-percent limit means the taxpayer may owe tax even with a large NOL carryforward, so the cash-flow planning should account for the residual tax. For a pre-2018 NOL, the full offset applies (no 80-percent limit), and the 20-year carryforward applies. The 80-percent limit is a significant change that reduces the value of the NOL carryforward, so it should be applied carefully.

### Coordinate NOLs From Pass-Through Entities

An NOL from a pass-through entity (a partnership or an S corporation) is computed and used at the owner level, not the entity level, for individual owners. The entity reports the income or loss on Schedule K-1, and the owner includes the loss in the owner's NOL computation. However, the loss is subject to the basis limitations, the at-risk limitations, and the passive activity loss limitations before it can be used by the owner. Under the post-2022 BBA regime, partnerships have centralized partnership audit rules, but the NOL is still used at the owner level for individuals. The coordination is complex, because the entity's loss may not be fully usable by the owner due to the limitations.

Coordinate the NOLs from pass-through entities. Obtain the Schedule K-1 from the entity, and apply the basis, at-risk, and passive activity loss limitations to determine the amount of the loss that is usable by the owner. Include the usable loss in the owner's NOL computation. For a partnership under the BBA regime, the centralized audit rules may affect the entity's adjustments, but the owner's NOL is computed based on the K-1 as issued (or as adjusted under the BBA). The coordination is essential, because an owner who claims an NOL based on the full K-1 loss without the limitations may overstate the NOL and face an IRS adjustment.

## Common Traps

### Computing The NOL Without The Required Adjustments

The symptom is a taxpayer who computes the NOL as the negative taxable income from the return. The trap is that the NOL requires adjustments (adding back nonbusiness deductions, personal exemptions, and the Section 199A deduction), so the unadjusted figure overstates or understates the NOL. The direction is to use Form 1045 Schedule A to compute the NOL with the adjustments.

### Applying The Wrong Year's Carryback Rules

The symptom is a taxpayer who carries back a 2022 NOL two years under the CARES Act. The trap is that the CARES Act two-year carryback applies only to 2018-2020 NOLs, so the 2022 NOL cannot be carried back. The direction is to confirm the year the NOL arose and the applicable carryback rules.

### Missing The Form 1045 12-Month Deadline

The symptom is a taxpayer who files a Form 1045 for an NOL carryback 14 months after the end of the NOL year. The trap is that the Form 1045 deadline is 12 months, so the tentative carryback is not available, and the taxpayer must use a slower Form 1040-X. The direction is to file Form 1045 within 12 months of the end of the NOL year.

### Ignoring The 80-Percent Carryforward Limit

The symptom is a taxpayer who uses an NOL carryforward to fully eliminate taxable income in 2023. The trap is that the TCJA limits the NOL carryforward to 80 percent of taxable income, so the taxpayer still owes tax on the remaining 20 percent. The direction is to apply the 80-percent limit to the taxable income before the NOL deduction.

### Claiming A Pass-Through Loss Without The Limitations

The symptom is an S corporation shareholder who claims an NOL based on the full K-1 loss. The trap is that the loss is subject to the basis, at-risk, and passive activity loss limitations, so the usable loss may be less than the K-1 loss. The direction is to apply the limitations and to include only the usable loss in the NOL computation.

### Waiving A Carryback Without Evaluating The Cash-Flow Impact

The symptom is a taxpayer who waives the two-year carryback for a 2019 NOL to simplify the filing. The trap is that the carryback would have produced a refund of prior-year tax, and the waiver forfeits the refund. The direction is to evaluate the carryback refund against the carryforward benefit and the cash-flow needs before waiving.
