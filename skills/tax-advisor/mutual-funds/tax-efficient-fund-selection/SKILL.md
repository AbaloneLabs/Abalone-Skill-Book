# Tax-Efficient Fund Selection

Tax-efficient fund selection is the practice of choosing which funds to hold in a taxable account versus a tax-advantaged account, and within the taxable account, choosing funds that generate the least tax drag for a given investment goal. It is not the same as choosing the best-performing fund. Two funds with identical pre-tax returns can produce very different after-tax returns depending on how much ordinary income, qualified income, and realized capital gains they distribute. An agent who selects funds on pre-tax performance alone is giving the taxpayer a portfolio that may bleed several percentage points of return to tax over a decade.

Agents commonly miss that broad market index funds and ETFs are typically far more tax-efficient than actively managed mutual funds, that bond funds and REIT funds are tax-inefficient and belong in tax-advantaged accounts, that turnover rate is a leading indicator of capital gains distributions, and that the same asset class can be delivered through a more or less tax-efficient vehicle. The harm is a taxpayer who holds a high-turnover active fund in a taxable account, pays tax every year on distributions, and ends up with materially less wealth than a comparable investor who held a tax-efficient equivalent.

This skill covers the principles and tradeoffs of selecting tax-efficient funds and locating asset classes across account types. It is not investment or tax advice; fund characteristics change, after-tax outcomes depend on the individual taxpayer's rates and circumstances, and a qualified tax and financial professional must be consulted for actual situations.

## Core Rules

### Rank Broad Index Funds And ETFs As More Tax-Efficient Than Active Funds

The single most reliable predictor of a fund's tax efficiency is its turnover and structure. Broad market index funds, whether mutual fund or ETF, hold a relatively fixed basket of securities and trade primarily when the index reconstitutes, so they realize few internal capital gains and distribute little. Actively managed mutual funds trade frequently to pursue alpha, realize gains on each sale, and must distribute those net gains to shareholders annually. Over time, active funds have historically distributed meaningfully more in capital gains than index funds.

When building a taxable account, default to broad index funds or ETFs for core equity exposure. This does not mean active funds are always wrong, but in a taxable account their tax drag must be justified by sufficient after-tax outperformance, which is difficult to achieve persistently. Quantify the historical distribution yield (distributions as a percentage of NAV) of any candidate fund and compare it across peers before selecting.

### Use Turnover Ratio As A Leading Indicator Of Capital Gains Distributions

A fund's turnover ratio, reported in its prospectus and annual report, measures how much of the portfolio is replaced each year. A turnover of 100% means the entire portfolio is sold and replaced annually; a turnover of 5% means very little changes. High turnover is strongly correlated with higher realized capital gains and larger capital gains distributions, because frequent trading in an appreciating portfolio constantly locks in gains.

When screening funds for a taxable account, prefer turnover below roughly 20-30% for equity funds. A fund with 5% turnover is unlikely to distribute large capital gains; a fund with 80% or 100% turnover is likely to distribute gains regularly. Note that turnover is a backward-looking metric and a low-turnover fund can still distribute a large gain in a year with redemptions or a manager change, so use it as one signal among several, not as a guarantee.

### Locate Tax-Inefficient Asset Classes In Tax-Advantaged Accounts First

Asset location is the companion to fund selection: holding each asset class in the account type where it does the least tax harm. Tax-inefficient asset classes, those that generate high ordinary income or non-qualified distributions, should be prioritized for tax-advantaged accounts (IRA, 401(k)). These include taxable bond funds, high-yield bond funds, REIT funds, actively managed stock funds with high turnover, and funds holding non-qualified dividend payers.

Tax-efficient asset classes, those that generate mostly qualified dividends and few capital gains distributions, are better suited to taxable accounts. These include broad U.S. and international stock index funds and ETFs. When account space is limited, fill tax-advantaged accounts with the most tax-inefficient holdings first, then place the remainder, typically the broad equity index exposure, in the taxable account. This sequencing minimizes the total tax drag across the household portfolio.

### Evaluate The Vehicle, Not Just The Asset Class

The same asset class can be delivered through a more or less tax-efficient vehicle. A total stock market exposure can be held as an actively managed mutual fund (tax-inefficient), an index mutual fund (moderately efficient), or an ETF (often most efficient due to in-kind redemption). For a taxable account, the vehicle choice can matter as much as the asset class choice. A taxpayer who wants active management may still find the ETF wrapper more tax-efficient than the mutual fund wrapper for the same strategy.

When selecting, compare the after-tax historical returns reported in the fund's prospectus, which show the fund's return after taxes on distributions and gains for the highest federal bracket. A large gap between pre-tax and after-tax return signals tax inefficiency. Prefer the vehicle with the smallest gap for taxable accounts. Note that ETF tax advantages are most pronounced for equities and can be smaller or absent for certain fixed-income or actively managed ETFs.

### Treat Bond Funds And REIT Funds As Tax-Inefficient

Taxable bond funds generate interest income taxed at ordinary rates up to 37%, with no preferential rate, plus possible net investment income tax. REIT funds are required to distribute most of their taxable income, and those distributions are largely taxed as ordinary dividends (non-qualified), making them highly tax-inefficient in a taxable account. Both asset classes erode after-tax return severely when held outside a tax-advantaged account.

When a taxable account must hold bonds (for example, when tax-advantaged space is full), prefer tax-exempt municipal bond funds, whose interest is federally tax-exempt, over taxable bond funds. For REIT exposure, prefer tax-advantaged account placement, or consider a REIT ETF structure that may defer some gains, but recognize the ordinary-income distribution drag remains. Do not assume a bond fund and a stock fund with the same pre-tax yield produce the same after-tax outcome; the bond fund is almost always more heavily taxed.

### Factor In The Investor's Tax Bracket And Time Horizon

Tax efficiency matters more for taxpayers in higher brackets and with longer horizons. A taxpayer in the 37% bracket plus 3.8% net investment income tax loses over 40% of ordinary distributions to federal tax, so fund selection and location have a large wealth impact. A taxpayer in the 0% or 12% bracket faces much less drag, and the complexity of asset location may not be worth it. Similarly, a long horizon compounds the annual drag, while a short horizon limits the cumulative effect.

Scale the effort to the taxpayer's bracket and horizon. For a high-bracket, long-horizon investor, rigorous asset location and vehicle selection can add a meaningful fraction of annual return after tax. For a low-bracket or short-horizon investor, the same effort may add little. Do not impose complex location rules where the tax savings are trivial relative to the complexity and the risk of overweighting or underweighting asset classes to fit account constraints.

### Watch For Municipal Bond Tax-Equivalent Yield Conversions

For taxpayers in high brackets, tax-exempt municipal bond funds can offer a higher after-tax yield than taxable bond funds, even though the nominal yield is lower. The tax-equivalent yield is the taxable yield that would produce the same after-tax income: it equals the municipal yield divided by (1 minus the marginal tax rate). A 3.5% municipal yield for a taxpayer in a 40% combined bracket is equivalent to a 5.83% taxable yield.

When comparing bond funds for a taxable account, convert municipal yields to tax-equivalent terms before comparing to taxable alternatives. Note that municipal interest is federally exempt but may be subject to state tax unless the bonds are in-state, and that a portion of municipal fund distributions can be subject to the alternative minimum tax if the bonds are private-activity bonds. Confirm the fund's AMT exposure and state treatment, not just the federal exemption.

## Common Traps

### Selecting Funds On Pre-Term Return Alone

Two funds with the same pre-tax return can differ by a full percentage point or more after tax due to distribution character and frequency. The trap is ignoring the after-tax return figures in the prospectus. Always compare after-tax historical returns for taxable-account candidates.

### Holding High-Turnover Active Funds In A Taxable Account

Active funds distribute more capital gains, creating annual tax drag that compounds. The trap is assuming the manager's pre-tax alpha will overcome the tax cost. In a taxable account, the after-tax bar for active management is much higher than the pre-tax bar.

### Putting Bonds In The Taxable Account And Equities In The IRA

Bonds generate ordinary income taxed at high rates; equities generate qualified dividends and deferred gains taxed at lower rates. The trap is locating them backwards, which maximizes tax drag. Prioritize tax-inefficient assets for tax-advantaged space.

### Assuming All ETFs Are Equally Tax-Efficient

Most equity ETFs are tax-efficient, but actively managed ETFs, fixed-income ETFs, and ETFs in less liquid markets can and do distribute capital gains. The trap is assuming the ETF wrapper guarantees zero distributions. Check the specific fund's distribution history.

### Ignoring Turnover Ratio When Screening Funds

A fund with 100% turnover will likely distribute gains even if it is labeled an index fund (some "enhanced" or smart-beta funds have high turnover). The trap is screening by label rather than by the turnover metric. Use turnover as a primary filter for taxable-account candidates.

### Overlooking Municipal Bond Tax-Equivalent Yield

Comparing a 3% municipal yield to a 4.5% taxable yield at face value understates the municipal fund's value for a high-bracket taxpayer. The trap is failing to convert to tax-equivalent yield before comparing. Always convert when the taxpayer is in a high bracket.

### Imposing Asset Location Complexity Where The Savings Are Trivial

For a low-bracket investor or a small portfolio, the tax savings from elaborate asset location may be smaller than the cost of the complexity and the risk of distorting the asset allocation. The trap is over-engineering. Scale the effort to the bracket, horizon, and portfolio size.
