# Success Criteria And Student-Facing Targets

An objective written for the teacher is not yet a target students can use. Success criteria are the translation of a learning objective into language students can read, hold, and check themselves against while they work. The recurring failure is writing criteria that restate the objective in adult language, post them once on the board, and assume students have internalized them. Students then work without a clear sense of what good looks like, self-assess against impressions rather than standards, and cannot tell whether they are done. Strong success criteria turn an invisible expectation into a visible tool that students actually use to monitor and improve their own work.

Use this skill when writing student-facing targets, converting objectives into success criteria, designing I-can statements, co-constructing criteria with a class, or making learning visible during a lesson. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating success criteria as decoration and instead make them operational tools that change how students work. This skill concerns the student-facing translation; writing the underlying measurable objective and deconstructing standards are companion skills.

## Core Rules

### Translate The Objective, Do Not Merely Copy It

A success criterion is a translation of the objective into language the student can act on. Copying the objective verbatim usually fails because objectives are written for teachers and assessors, not for the learner mid-task.

Translate deliberately:

- replace jargon and technical phrasing with words students already use;
- convert the teacher's measurable verb into a student's first-person action;
- break a compound objective into separate, checkable criteria;
- keep the cognitive demand intact while simplifying the surface language;
- preserve the conditions and criteria that define quality.

"Students will justify a claim with two pieces of textual evidence" becomes "I can state my claim and point to two places in the text that support it, and explain how each one connects." The demand is preserved; the language becomes usable.

### Write Criteria As I-Can Statements Students Can Self-Monitor

The form of a success criterion matters. "I can" statements invite students to assess themselves, whereas noun phrases or teacher commands do not. The first-person form turns the criterion into a self-check.

Write effective I-can statements:

- begin with "I can" followed by an observable action;
- keep each statement to one checkable behavior;
- make the statement specific enough that a student can tell if it is true;
- include the evidence the student should be able to point to;
- avoid vague closers like "well" or "correctly" without defining them.

"I can write a topic sentence" is checkable; "I can write well" is not. The criterion must let a student answer yes or not yet honestly.

### Make The Cognitive Demand Visible, Not Just The Product

Success criteria often describe only the finished product and hide the thinking that produces it. If the objective requires analysis, the criterion must make the analyzing visible, not just the output.

Surface the thinking:

- include criteria about the reasoning, not only the format;
- name the moves, such as compare, explain why, or connect;
- require students to show the thinking, such as labeling steps or citing evidence;
- avoid criteria that reward surface features when the target is deeper;
- separate what the work looks like from what the thinking must do.

A criterion that lists "has a title, has three paragraphs, has a conclusion" describes a product shell and says nothing about whether the student analyzed anything. Match the criteria to the cognition.

### Co-Construct Criteria With Students When Possible

Criteria handed down without discussion are often ignored. Co-construction, where students help generate or refine the criteria from examples, makes the targets their own and dramatically increases the chance they use them.

Co-construct effectively:

- show an exemplar and ask students what makes it strong;
- collect their observations and organize them into criteria;
- refine their language into clear I-can statements together;
- compare a weak example against the draft criteria to test them;
- revisit and revise the criteria as students encounter the work.

Co-construction takes time but produces criteria students actually understand and own. When time is short, even partial co-construction beats silent posting.

### Make Targets Visible And Referenced Throughout The Work

A target posted once at the start and never referenced is decoration. Criteria must remain visible and be actively used during the work.

Build in reference points:

- post criteria where students can see them throughout the lesson;
- reference the criteria when giving instructions and feedback;
- ask students to point to which criterion they are working on;
- have students self-assess against the criteria midway, not only at the end;
- use the criteria to frame peer feedback.

The test of visibility is whether a visitor could ask any student what they are learning and how they know they are succeeding, and get a real answer.

### Match Language To Age, Proficiency, And Context

Success criteria fail when their language exceeds the students' reading level or language proficiency. The translation must fit the actual learners.

Adapt the language:

- use simpler sentence structures for younger students;
- provide bilingual or glossed versions for multilingual learners;
- pair words with symbols or examples for early readers and some students with disabilities;
- avoid idioms and abstract phrasing that obscure the checkable behavior;
- keep technical terms only when they are part of the learning itself.

A criterion students cannot read cannot guide their work. Translate to the learners, not to a generic student.

### Distinguish Success Criteria From Instructions And Rubrics

Success criteria are easily confused with task instructions and grading rubrics, but they serve different purposes and live at different moments. Conflating them weakens all three.

Keep them distinct:

- instructions tell students what to do; criteria tell them what good looks like;
- rubrics are teacher tools for judging finished work; criteria are student tools during work;
- criteria should be simpler and more immediate than a full rubric;
- a rubric can be derived from criteria, but criteria should not be buried inside rubric language.

If students cannot use the text as a self-check while working, it is probably a rubric or instruction set mislabeled as success criteria.

### Build In Self-And-Peer Assessment Against The Criteria

Criteria realize their value when students use them to judge work, their own and others'. Build the assessment habit into the routine.

Use criteria for self and peer assessment:

- have students rate each criterion as yes, almost, or not yet before submitting;
- require a specific fix tied to a not-yet criterion;
- train peer feedback to reference specific criteria rather than general praise;
- use the criteria to focus teacher conferences on one target at a time;
- track criterion-level data over time to reveal patterns.

When students assess against criteria routinely, the quality of work rises before any grading occurs.

### Keep Criteria Stable Within A Task But Evolving Across A Course

Criteria should be stable enough within a task that students can rely on them, but they should grow across a unit or course as expectations rise. Static criteria that never increase in demand cap student growth.

Manage criterion evolution:

- keep criteria fixed for the duration of a single task or unit;
- add or raise criteria as students gain proficiency, naming what is new;
- retire criteria that have become automatic so attention focuses on new demands;
- make the escalation visible so students see the bar rising legitimately.

A course whose criteria never change signals that growth is not expected. A course whose criteria shift without warning signals that the goalposts are arbitrary. Evolve them deliberately and transparently.

## Common Traps

### Copying The Objective As The Criterion

Pasting the teacher-facing objective does not give students a usable tool. Translate into first-person, checkable language.

### Describing Only The Product

Criteria that list format features hide the thinking the target requires. Make the cognitive moves visible in the criteria.

### Posting Once And Never Referencing

Criteria ignored during the work do not change behavior. Reference and use them throughout the lesson.

### Writing Vague Closers

"I can write well" cannot be self-checked. Define what good looks like concretely.

### Language Above The Learners

Criteria students cannot read cannot guide them. Match the language to age and proficiency.

### Confusing Criteria With Instructions Or Rubrics

Instructions say what to do; rubrics judge finished work; criteria guide work in progress. Keep them separate.

### Handing Down Criteria Without Co-Construction

Imposed criteria are often ignored. Co-construct from exemplars when possible so students own them.

### Never Raising The Bar

Criteria that never evolve cap student growth. Increase demands deliberately and visibly across a course.

## Self-Check

- Are criteria translated into student-facing language rather than copied from the objective?
- Are they written as first-person I-can statements that students can self-check?
- Do the criteria make the cognitive demand visible, not just the product features?
- Are criteria co-constructed with students from exemplars where time allows?
- Are targets visible and actively referenced throughout the work, not only posted once?
- Is the language matched to the age, reading level, and language proficiency of the actual students?
- Are success criteria kept distinct from task instructions and grading rubrics?
- Do students use the criteria for self- and peer-assessment during the work, with specific fixes?
- Are criteria stable within a task but deliberately evolved as demands rise across the course?
- Could a visitor ask any student what they are learning and how they know they are succeeding, and get a real answer?
