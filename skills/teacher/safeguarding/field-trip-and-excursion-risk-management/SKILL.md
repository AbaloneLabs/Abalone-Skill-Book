# Field Trip And Excursion Risk Management

The judgment problem in field trips is that the controlled, predictable school environment — where help is nearby, boundaries are known, and routines are established — is replaced by an unfamiliar setting where hazards are not mapped, where students behave differently, where supervision is harder, and where an emergency is far more logistically difficult to manage. The teacher who would never allow a student to wander unsupervised in a classroom can lose track of a student at a crowded museum; the teacher who has medical information at their desk may not have it at a remote trailhead. Most field trip incidents are not caused by exotic dangers but by ordinary failures amplified by the off-site setting: a lost student, an allergic reaction without medication, a behavior escalation without a quiet space, a transport delay without contact. The skilled teacher treats the excursion as a risk-management exercise that begins long before departure — with site visits, consent, medical planning, supervision design, and contingency plans — and recognizes that the educational value of the trip must justify the residual risk, and that cancellation or modification is always a legitimate decision.

This is a high-stakes domain. Off-site incidents can involve serious injury, a lost or missing child, a medical emergency far from help, or a safeguarding failure in an environment with public access. The teacher carries legal duty-of-care that does not pause at the school gate.

## Core Rules

### Conduct a pre-visit risk assessment based on a physical site visit, not assumptions

A risk assessment built from a website, a brochure, or last year's memory misses the hazards that actually matter: the busy road crossed on foot, the unfenced water feature, the area with no mobile signal, the exit that locks, the crowd density at the time of day you will visit. Whenever possible, visit the site in person at the planned time, walk the route students will take, identify hazards, and document them. Where a physical visit is impossible, obtain current detailed information from the venue and from recent visitors, and document the basis of your assessment. A risk assessment that was never grounded in the actual site is not a risk assessment.

### Design supervision around ratios, zones, and accountability, not headcounts

A supervision ratio (adults to students) is a minimum, not a plan. Effective supervision assigns each adult a specific group of named students for whom they are responsible, divides the site into zones with assigned coverage, and establishes a system for knowing where every student is at all times — buddy pairs, regular check-ins, roll calls at transitions. The failure mode is the diffuse "everyone watch all the students," which means no one is accountable for any specific child. Define who is with whom, check at every transition, and have a protocol for a missing student that does not require first discovering the absence.

### Secure informed parental consent and full medical and behavioral information

Consent is not a permission slip signed once at the start of the year; it is specific, informed consent for this trip, this destination, these activities, and these risks. The consent process is also your opportunity to surface the information that will matter in an emergency or a behavior incident: allergies, asthma, medication, seizures, mobility limits, anxiety, behavioral triggers, and anything else that changes how the student must be supervised. Ask directly and specifically; parents will not always volunteer critical information on a generic form. Ensure that the supervising adults carry this information and the student's emergency medication, not just the lead teacher.

### Plan the medical and emergency response before departure

Before the trip, know the nearest medical facility, the local emergency number, whether mobile signal is reliable at the site, and what the institution's emergency-contact and incident-escalation protocol is. Ensure at least one supervising adult has current first-aid training appropriate to the activity and environment, and that emergency medication (inhalers, epinephrine, glucose) is carried by the adult responsible for that student, not stored in a bag on a bus. Define who calls whom, in what order, if something goes wrong, and ensure supervising adults know the plan rather than improvising under stress.

### Treat transportation as a risk in itself

Many field trip incidents occur in transit, not at the destination. Verify the safety and licensing of any transport provider, the appropriateness of the vehicle for the group, seatbelt or restraint requirements, and the route. For walking or public transit, scout the route for road crossings, traffic, and safe rest stops. Never overload a vehicle, never use unlicensed or uninsured transport, and have a plan for the student who becomes separated during transit. The supervision plan must cover the journey, not just the arrival.

### Build in contingency and the authority to cancel or modify

Conditions change: weather deteriorates, a venue becomes unsafe, a key supervisor is absent, a student has a crisis, traffic makes the schedule impossible. Before departure, define the decision-maker, the thresholds that would trigger cancellation or modification (weather warnings, staff absence below safe ratio, site closure), and the communication plan to families if the trip changes. The decision to cancel or shorten a trip that has already been planned and announced is uncomfortable, and that discomfort is exactly why the threshold must be decided in advance rather than rationalized away in the moment. A trip that proceeds into foreseeable danger because cancellation is inconvenient is a failure of duty of care.

### Match the activity and environment to the actual students, not the ideal class

The risk that matters is the risk to the specific students attending: the student with severe asthma on a strenuous hike, the student with elopement risk in an open public space, the student with anxiety in a crowded venue, the student with a mobility difference on uneven terrain. Review the roster against the activity and environment, and modify the plan, add support, or, if a student cannot be safely included, address that honestly and early rather than hoping it works out. Excluding a student is a serious decision that requires consultation with the family and specialists; forcing an unsafe inclusion is worse.

### Maintain safeguarding vigilance in public-access environments

Off-site venues are open to the public, which introduces risks absent in a closed school: strangers, uncontrolled interactions, students encountering content or people, and the difficulty of maintaining a safe boundary. Supervising adults must be alert not only to the students but to the environment and the people in it. Brief students age-appropriately on boundaries, what to do if approached, and where to go for help. Never allow students to be alone with a single adult in a non-visible setting; the two-adult rule that applies in school applies on the trip.

## Common Traps

### Skipping the site visit and assessing from memory or marketing

Last year's site is not this year's site: venues change, construction begins, hazards appear, and crowd patterns shift with time of day and season. A risk assessment built from assumption rather than a current physical visit misses the hazards most likely to cause harm.

### Diffuse supervision where no adult is accountable for specific students

"Everyone keep an eye on the kids" produces the exact condition in which a child goes unnoticed missing — because each adult assumes another is watching. Assign named groups to named adults and check at every transition.

### Generic consent forms that fail to surface critical medical and behavioral information

A single annual permission slip does not capture the allergy that developed this term, the new medication, or the behavioral plan that matters off-site. Ask specifically, per trip, and carry the information with the supervising team.

### Proceeding into foreseeable risk because cancellation is embarrassing or inconvenient

Once families have been told, transport booked, and students excited, the pressure to proceed is immense. That pressure is the reason to set cancellation thresholds in advance. Foreseeable danger — a weather warning, an unsafe ratio, a closed venue — must override the discomfort of canceling.

### Storing emergency medication in a central bag rather than with the responsible adult

An inhaler or epinephrine device stored in the lead teacher's backpack on a bus is useless to a student in distress at the far end of a site. Emergency medication travels with the adult responsible for that student.

### Treating the destination as the risk and ignoring transit

The journey — roads, crossings, public spaces, vehicle safety, separation — is where many incidents occur. Supervision and risk planning must cover the entire excursion, door to door.

### Including a student whose needs cannot be safely met, hoping it works out

Hope is not a supervision plan. If a student's medical, behavioral, or physical needs cannot be safely accommodated in the planned activity and environment, that must be addressed through modification, added support, or honest consultation — not by proceeding and trusting luck.

### Losing safeguarding discipline in a "fun" setting

The relaxed atmosphere of a trip can erode the boundaries that protect students: the two-adult rule, visibility, and appropriate physical contact. Off-site public environments actually require greater vigilance, not less.

## Self-Check

- Is the risk assessment based on a current physical site visit (or documented current information), identifying specific hazards at the time and place of the trip?
- Does the supervision plan assign named students to named adults, with a check-in system at every transition and a protocol for a missing student?
- Did the consent process secure specific, informed consent for this trip and surface medical, allergy, and behavioral information for each student?
- Is emergency medication carried by the responsible adult, and does at least one adult have appropriate first-aid training?
- Have I identified the nearest medical facility, confirmed mobile signal reliability, and defined who contacts whom in an emergency?
- Does the supervision and risk plan cover transportation door to door, not just the destination?
- Did I define, in advance, the thresholds and decision-maker for canceling or modifying the trip, and am I willing to use them?
- Did I review the specific student roster against the activity and environment, and modify the plan for any student whose needs require it?
- Am I maintaining two-adult and visibility safeguards in a public-access environment, rather than relaxing them because the setting feels informal?
- Is the educational value of the trip sufficient to justify the residual risk after mitigation?
