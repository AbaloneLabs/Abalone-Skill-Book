# Authentic Audience And Public Product

The public product is what separates project-based learning from a long assignment. When the only audience is the teacher and the only outcome is a grade, students rationally invest the minimum effort that earns the desired mark. A real audience changes the math: the work has to be good enough to matter to someone who actually cares, and that pressure produces more revision, more care, and more learning. But authenticity is easy to fake and easy to get wrong. A staged audience that has no genuine stake teaches students the work is performative, and a public setting handled carelessly can expose student data, embarrass a learner, or create safety risks the teacher did not anticipate.

This skill is for the audience and product design phase of PBL: choosing a real audience, matching the product to that audience, planning the exhibition, and protecting students when their work goes public. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a public product that is public in name only, an audience that is performative, or an exhibition that creates privacy and equity harms.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish A Real Audience From A Fake One

An authentic audience is a person or group that genuinely cares about, would use, or would be affected by the answer or product. A fake audience is one assembled for the exercise with no actual stake. Students can tell the difference, and a fake audience produces the same minimum-effort dynamic as no audience at all.

Strengthen authenticity by asking who in the world would actually want or use this. Real audiences include community members affected by a local issue, a younger cohort who needs the explanation, a professional who works in the relevant field, a decision-maker who could act on a proposal, or a public that would encounter the published work. Weaken it by inventing a panel of adults pretending to be stakeholders, or by telling students to imagine an audience.

Test the audience: if no one outside the classroom would ever read, watch, hear, or use the product, the audience is not authentic. Either find a real one or honestly frame the work as a simulation, which can still be valuable but is a different design choice.

### Match The Product Format To The Audience And Question

The product is the natural answer to the driving question, delivered in a form the audience can actually use. A mismatch confuses the work and wastes effort. If the audience is a city council member, a concise proposal with evidence is more useful than a decorative poster. If the audience is younger students, a clear explainer or model beats a technical report.

Choose format by asking what the audience needs and can consume:

- a proposal or recommendation for a decision-maker;
- an explainer, guide, or tutorial for a learner;
- a published article, podcast, or video for a public;
- a prototype or design for a user or client;
- a performance or exhibition for a community;
- a dataset, map, or model for a practitioner.

Do not let a flashy format override function. A video is not automatically better than a written brief; choose the form that best delivers the answer to the people who need it.

### Make The Stakes Genuine But Appropriate

A real audience raises stakes, which raises effort, but the stakes must be appropriate to the age and context. Younger students should not be put in situations where public failure is humiliating. Older students can handle higher-stakes settings, but the teacher is still responsible for ensuring the risk is intellectual, not emotional or social.

Genuine stakes mean the audience might disagree, ask hard questions, or choose not to use the product. That is healthy. But prepare students for it: rehearse the presentation, anticipate questions, and frame critique as part of learning rather than judgment of the person.

### Plan The Exhibition Deliberately

An exhibition is not the day the project is due; it is a designed event. Plan the venue, the audience invitation, the format of presentation, the time each team has, the role of the audience, and how feedback is collected. A poorly run exhibition undermines the work; a well-run one amplifies it.

Decide:

- where and when the exhibition happens, and who attends;
- the format, such as a gallery walk, formal presentation, panel pitch, or published release;
- how long each team or student has and what they must cover;
- what the audience does, such as asking questions, giving feedback, or voting;
- how feedback is captured for students to use in reflection;
- the backup plan if attendance is low or technology fails.

Rehearse. Students who present cold often underperform and learn less than those who have practiced and anticipated questions.

### Protect Student Privacy And Safety In Public Settings

Public products create real privacy and safety obligations, especially for minors. Once work is public, it can be photographed, shared, searched, and preserved. The teacher must decide what is shared, under what name, with what consent, and on what platform.

Address before going public:

- school and district policy on publishing student work and names;
- parental or guardian consent for public display, recording, or publication;
- whether to use full names, first names only, or pseudonyms;
- whether photos, video, or recordings of students are permitted;
- what personal data the product itself might reveal, such as location, family details, or opinions;
- platform choice and who controls the account and the content;
- the right to remove or take down work later.

A product that reveals a student's identity, location, or sensitive views without consent is a FERPA and safeguarding problem, not just a design choice. Default to the most conservative sharing that still achieves the authentic audience goal.

### Ensure Equity Of Access To The Audience

Public exhibitions can advantage students with more resources, confidence, or family support. A team whose parents can drive them to an evening event, buy supplies, or coach their pitch has an edge. Design the audience experience so access is equitable.

Provide in-class rehearsal time, supply materials and devices at school, schedule exhibitions during the school day when possible, and offer alternative formats for students who cannot attend outside hours. Do not let the public product become a measure of family resources.

### Use Audience Feedback As Learning, Not Just Applause

The point of a real audience is not praise; it is feedback that students can learn from. Design how the audience responds: structured feedback forms, question-and-answer sessions, or a follow-up reflection where students analyze what the audience understood, questioned, or challenged. If the audience only claps, the learning opportunity is mostly motivational, which is fine but limited.

### Decide When Public Means Truly Public Versus Classroom-Public

Not every project needs to be published to the internet. Classroom-public, sharing with peers and invited guests, is often authentic enough and far safer than open publication. Match the level of publicity to the learning goal and the maturity of the students. Escalate to wider publication only when the audience genuinely requires it and consent and safeguards are in place.

## Common Traps

### The Imagined Audience

Telling students to write or present as if for a real audience, when no such audience exists, produces performative work. Find a real audience or call it a simulation.

### Performative Panel Of Adults

Adults role-playing stakeholders with no actual stake is detected quickly and reduces effort. Bring in people with a genuine interest whenever possible.

### Format Over Function

Choosing a flashy product first and forcing the content to fit it inverts the design. Match the format to the question and the audience's actual need.

### Public Without Consent

Publishing student names, images, or work without guardian consent violates policy and can harm students. Confirm consent and default to conservative sharing.

### Revealing Sensitive Data In The Product

A community project can inadvertently reveal a student's address, family situation, or personal views. Review products for what they expose before going public.

### Equity Gap In Exhibition

Evening events, costly supplies, or required family involvement advantage some students. Provide access at school and during the day.

### Applause Instead Of Feedback

An audience that only praises teaches little. Design the audience response to produce usable feedback and student reflection.

### Over-Publishing Minors

Open internet publication of minors' identifiable work is rarely necessary and often risky. Prefer classroom-public unless the audience truly demands wider reach.

## Self-Check

- Is the audience genuinely authentic, meaning real people who care about, use, or are affected by the work, rather than an imagined or staged panel?
- Does the product format match the driving question and what the audience can actually consume and use?
- Are the stakes genuine but appropriate to the students' age, with rehearsal and framing that makes critique about the work, not the person?
- Is the exhibition designed as an event, with venue, audience role, timing, feedback capture, and a backup plan?
- Are privacy and safety handled, including consent, naming, recording, platform control, take-down rights, and what the product itself reveals?
- Does the design avoid revealing personal data such as location, family details, or sensitive opinions without consent?
- Is access to the audience equitable, with in-class rehearsal, school-supplied materials, and options for students who cannot attend outside hours?
- Does the audience produce usable feedback and student reflection, not only applause?
- Is the level of publicity matched to the learning goal, with classroom-public preferred over open internet publication for minors unless genuinely required?
- If the audience is staged or simulated, is that framed honestly rather than presented as authentic?
