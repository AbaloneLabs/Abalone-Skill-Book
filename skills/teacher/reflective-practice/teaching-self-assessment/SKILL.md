# Teaching Self-Assessment

Self-assessment is the practice of a teacher honestly evaluating their own effectiveness in order to improve. It sounds simple and is consistently hard, because the two failure modes pull in opposite directions. One is defensiveness: explaining away evidence of weak practice, attributing student struggles to the students, and concluding that everything is basically fine. The other is harsh self-criticism: blaming oneself for every student outcome, ignoring structural factors, and concluding that one is a bad teacher. Both prevent growth, the first by denying there is a problem and the second by making the problem feel personal and overwhelming. Useful self-assessment sits in the uncomfortable middle, where evidence is faced honestly without being turned into identity.

This skill covers the judgment needed to self-assess productively: using multiple sources of evidence, distinguishing what is and is not in your control, identifying specific growth areas, setting goals from the assessment, and avoiding both defensiveness and self-flagellation. The goal is an honest, useful account of your practice that drives improvement.

## Core Rules

### Use Multiple Sources Of Evidence, Not Just Feeling

Self-assessment based on how you feel about your teaching is unreliable, because feeling is shaped by mood, the last difficult class, and the comparison you happen to be making. Triangulate from multiple sources: student work and assessment results, student feedback, peer observation, your own reflective notes, and patterns over time. Each source is imperfect on its own; together they form a more accurate picture. A teacher who feels a unit "went well" may find, on checking assessment results, that key students did not learn the key idea; a teacher who feels a lesson "flopped" may find evidence of real learning. Evidence checks feeling in both directions.

### Separate What Is And Is Not In Your Control

Student outcomes are influenced by many factors outside a teacher's control: prior preparation, home circumstances, health, attendance, district policy, class composition. Taking full personal credit for every success and full personal blame for every failure is both inaccurate and corrosive. The useful question is not "did the students learn" in aggregate but "given who these students are and what they brought, what did my teaching add or fail to add." Look for evidence of your specific contribution: did students learn what I taught more than they would have otherwise, did gaps narrow, did struggling students improve. This framing is honest about constraints while keeping responsibility where it belongs.

### Look For Patterns, Not Just Incidents

A single bad lesson is data; a pattern of similar bad lessons is a growth area. Self-assessment that reacts to the most recent incident misses the larger picture, while assessment that looks across a unit or term surfaces recurring strengths and recurring weaknesses. Review a span of work, ask what shows up repeatedly, and treat patterns as the priority for growth. An isolated weakness may not warrant investment; a pattern almost certainly does.

### Examine Both Strengths And Growth Areas

Self-assessment that only hunts for weaknesses produces a distorted, demoralizing picture and misses the chance to build on strengths. Equally, assessment that only affirms strengths produces complacency. A balanced self-assessment names what you do well, because strengths that are understood can be deepened and shared with colleagues, and names what needs growth, because weaknesses that are named can be addressed. Both are necessary, and a teacher who cannot identify either their strengths or their weaknesses is not assessing honestly.

### Use Student Feedback As Evidence, Not As Verdict

Students see things about your teaching that no one else does, and well-collected feedback is among the most valuable evidence available. But student feedback is also imperfect: it can reflect preference rather than learning, can be shaped by factors unrelated to teaching quality, and can be skewed by a few loud voices. Use feedback as one source among several, look for patterns rather than over-weighting individual comments, and pay special attention to feedback that surprises you, because surprise often signals a blind spot. Do not dismiss uncomfortable feedback, and do not collapse into self-criticism over a negative comment.

### Move From Assessment To A Specific Goal

Self-assessment that produces no change is introspection. After identifying a growth area, translate it into a specific, actionable goal: not "be a better questioner" but "increase wait time after questions to at least five seconds and check whether more students respond." The goal should be specific enough to know whether you achieved it, within your control, and tied to evidence you can track. Assessment without a goal produces reflection; assessment with a goal produces improvement.

### Avoid Both Defensiveness And Self-Flagellation

The two great enemies of honest self-assessment are defensiveness, which denies problems, and self-flagellation, which exaggerates them. Both feel different but produce the same result: no useful change. Defensiveness says "the students just don't care" or "the test was unfair"; self-flagellation says "I'm a terrible teacher." Neither engages with the actual evidence. The disciplined alternative is to face evidence calmly, treat it as information about practice rather than about identity, and decide what to do. Your worth as a person is not on the table in self-assessment; your teaching practice is.

### Re-Assess After Acting On A Goal

Closing the loop means checking whether the change you made actually worked. After pursuing a growth goal, return to the evidence and assess whether the targeted aspect of practice improved. This re-assessment is what turns self-assessment from a one-time exercise into an ongoing improvement cycle, and it prevents the common failure of setting goals, making changes, and assuming improvement without verifying it.

## Common Traps

### Self-Assessment Based On Feeling Alone

Mood distorts the picture. Triangulate from multiple evidence sources.

### Taking Full Credit Or Full Blame For Outcomes

Many factors shape outcomes. Look for evidence of your specific contribution.

### Reacting To Incidents Instead Of Patterns

A single bad lesson is an incident; recurring ones are a growth area. Review spans of work.

### Hunting Only For Weaknesses

Strengths understood can be deepened. Assess both what you do well and what needs growth.

### Dismissing Or Collapsing Over Student Feedback

Feedback is imperfect but valuable. Look for patterns and attend to what surprises you.

### Assessment With No Goal

Reflection without action changes nothing. Translate growth areas into specific goals.

### Defensiveness Or Self-Flagellation

Both avoid the evidence. Face it calmly as information about practice, not identity.

### Assuming Improvement Without Re-Assessing

Changes need verification. Close the loop by re-assessing after acting.

## Self-Check

- Is my self-assessment based on multiple evidence sources rather than feeling alone?
- Have I separated what is and is not within my control when reading outcomes?
- Am I looking at patterns across a unit or term rather than reacting to incidents?
- Have I honestly named both strengths and growth areas?
- Am I using student feedback as one source, attending to patterns and surprises?
- Did I translate each growth area into a specific, evidence-trackable goal?
- Did I avoid both defensiveness and self-flagellation, treating evidence as information about practice?
- Did I plan to re-assess after acting on the goal to verify improvement?
