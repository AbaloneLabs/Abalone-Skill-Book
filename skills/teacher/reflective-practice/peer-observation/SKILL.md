# Peer Observation

Peer observation is one of the most powerful and most misused tools for professional growth. Done well, it exposes a teacher to new approaches, surfaces blind spots in their own practice, and builds a culture of collaborative improvement. Done badly, it becomes performative, with the observed teacher putting on a showcase lesson and the observer filing a polite report, or it becomes evaluative, with the observer acting as a judge and the observed teacher growing defensive. The difference between observation that transforms practice and observation that wastes everyone's time is almost entirely in how it is designed.

This skill covers the judgment needed to make peer observation productive: setting a clear focus, observing and recording accurately, giving feedback that helps rather than wounds, receiving feedback without defensiveness, and keeping observation separate from evaluation. The goal is observation that genuinely improves teaching rather than observation that fulfills a requirement.

## Core Rules

### Agree On A Specific Focus Before The Observation

Observation without a focus produces either an overwhelming catalog of everything or a vague impression of nothing. Before observing, agree with the colleague on what they want observed: a specific aspect of practice they are working on, a question they have about their teaching, a particular group of students, or a moment in the lesson they are unsure about. A focused observation gathers useful data on the question that matters to the teacher; an unfocused one gathers scattered notes that serve no one. The focus also constrains the observer, preventing the temptation to evaluate everything.

### Observe And Record Descriptively, Not Evaluatively

The observer's job during the lesson is to record what happens, not to judge it. Describe what the teacher and students do in neutral, specific language: "seven of nine students attempted the problem within thirty seconds," not "engagement was good." Descriptive records give the observed teacher accurate data to reflect on; evaluative records impose the observer's interpretation and trigger defensiveness. The discipline of descriptive observation is harder than it sounds, because the mind constantly wants to jump to judgment, but it is what makes the data usable.

Record evidence the teacher cannot see from the front of the room: which students participated, where confusion appeared, how time was distributed.

### Give Feedback That Is Specific, Evidence-Based, And Owned

Feedback after observation should be grounded in the agreed focus and the descriptive evidence collected. Offer observations rather than verdicts, and frame interpretations as your perspective rather than objective truth: "I noticed that the same three students answered most of the questions; I wondered whether others had a chance," rather than "you didn't include enough students." Own your perspective with "I noticed" and "I wondered," which invites dialogue rather than closing it. Balance what you observed with genuine questions, because the teacher knows things about the class that you do not.

### Separate Observation From Evaluation

Peer observation dies the moment it becomes covert evaluation. If observation feeds into formal evaluation, grading, or personnel decisions, teachers will perform rather than teach, hide rather than reveal, and the entire practice becomes useless for growth. Keep peer observation strictly formative and confidential, with clear agreements about what is shared and with whom. This separation must be explicit and protected, because the moment it blurs, trust collapses and observation stops producing honest data.

### Receive Feedback Without Defensiveness

Being observed is vulnerable, and the instinct when receiving feedback is to explain, justify, or defend. The effective move is to listen fully, ask clarifying questions, and sit with the feedback before responding. Most feedback contains at least partial truth even when imperfect, and defensiveness prevents you from hearing it. You do not have to agree with everything, but you should understand it before deciding. Treat feedback as data about how your teaching lands with a thoughtful observer, not as a verdict on your worth.

### Use Observation To Learn, Not Just To Be Judged

Observing others is as valuable as being observed. Watching a colleague teach exposes you to different approaches, surfaces assumptions you did not know you held, and gives you concrete alternatives to borrow or adapt. Approach observing as a learner rather than a critic, and look for what you can take from the lesson regardless of whether you would teach it the same way. A culture where teachers regularly observe each other as learners grows far faster than one where each teacher works in isolation.

### Make Observation Reciprocal And Ongoing

One-off observations rarely change practice; reciprocal, ongoing observation relationships do. When colleagues observe each other regularly over time, trust deepens, feedback becomes more honest and useful, and the focus can shift from surface issues to deeper questions of practice. Build observation into the rhythm of the year rather than treating it as an event. Reciprocity, observing and being observed, also balances the vulnerability and prevents the dynamic where one teacher is always the judge and the other always the judged.

### Connect Observation To A Specific Change

Observation that does not lead to a change in practice is observation wasted. After each cycle, identify one specific thing to try differently, and let the next observation examine whether it worked. This closes the loop between observation and improvement and prevents observation from becoming a ritual that produces reflection without action.

## Common Traps

### Observation Without A Focus

Unfocused observation produces scattered or overwhelming notes. Agree on a specific question first.

### Evaluative Recording

Judgments impose interpretation and trigger defensiveness. Record descriptively.

### Feedback As Verdict

"You didn't include enough students" closes dialogue. Use "I noticed" and "I wondered."

### Observation Tangled With Evaluation

The moment observation feeds evaluation, performance replaces honesty. Keep it formative and confidential.

### Defensiveness When Observed

Explaining before listening blocks learning. Sit with the feedback before responding.

### Observing As A Critic Rather Than A Learner

Watching to judge teaches nothing. Watch to learn and borrow.

### One-Off Observation With No Follow-Through

Single observations rarely change practice. Build reciprocal, ongoing relationships and connect each cycle to a change.

## Self-Check

- Did we agree on a specific focus before the observation?
- Did I record descriptively rather than evaluatively, capturing what the teacher cannot see?
- Is my feedback specific, evidence-based, and framed as my perspective rather than verdict?
- Is the observation strictly formative and separate from evaluation?
- When receiving feedback, did I listen fully before responding?
- Do I approach observing others as a learner rather than a critic?
- Is observation reciprocal and ongoing rather than a one-off event?
- Did each observation cycle lead to a specific change to try next?
