# Cooperative Task And Structure Design

Putting students in a group and giving them a task does not produce cooperative learning; it produces group-adjacent individual work, in which the fastest student does the thinking, the others copy or wait, and everyone gets the same grade for unequal contribution. Genuine cooperative learning is engineered: the task must be structured so that students cannot succeed unless they cooperate, the structure must distribute participation, and the composition and size must fit the cognitive and social demands. The agent's job is to design the interdependence deliberately, choose a structure that enforces it, and reject tasks that merely happen to be assigned to multiple people.

This applies when the work is to design a cooperative task, choose a structure (jigsaw, think-pair-share, numbered heads together, etc.), engineer positive interdependence, or decide group size and composition. The recurring failure is assuming proximity produces cooperation, then being surprised by freeloading and dominance.

## Core Rules

### Make The Task Genuinely Require Cooperation

The first test of a cooperative task is whether one motivated student could complete it alone. If they could, the task does not require cooperation; it merely permits it, and the predictable result is that one student does it while others watch. Design the task so cooperation is structurally necessary.

Build in requirement for cooperation by:

- giving each member a distinct, necessary piece of information or resource (resource interdependence) so no one person has the whole picture;
- assigning roles whose functions are all required to complete the product (role interdependence);
- setting a task complex enough that one student cannot finish it well in the time, but a coordinated group can;
- requiring a product whose components each depend on the others (task interdependence);
- imposing a constraint, such as one shared answer sheet or a single collective product, that forces consensus.

If you can remove the group and the task is unchanged, the task was never cooperative.

### Engineer Positive Interdependence Explicitly

Positive interdependence is the structural property that students sink or swim together: one cannot succeed unless all do. It is not a feeling or a hope; it is built into the task design. State, for each activity, what kind of interdependence is operating and how it is enforced.

Common forms:

- goal interdependence: a single shared product or answer the group must produce together;
- resource interdependence: information or materials distributed so each member holds a part no one else has;
- role interdependence: complementary roles (reader, recorder, checker, encourager) whose functions are all required;
- reward interdependence: a group bonus contingent on every member reaching a threshold, not on the group average.

Pair positive interdependence with individual accountability (see the companion skill); interdependence without accountability produces a comfortable group in which some members still do not learn.

### Choose A Structure That Matches The Purpose

Cooperative structures are tools with specific effects; choosing one because it is familiar rather than because it fits the purpose produces weak results. Match the structure to the cognitive and social goal.

- Think-pair-share: every student thinks alone, refines with a partner, then shares to the class. Best for broad participation and rehearsal of reasoning before public exposure; low setup cost.
- Jigsaw: each member becomes expert on one piece, then teaches it to the group. Best when the content divides into complementary chunks and you want every member essential; requires that the pieces genuinely interlock.
- Numbered heads together: the group discusses to ensure every member can answer, then one random member is called. Best for review and accountability; forces the group to prepare everyone.
- Group investigation or project structures: the group plans, divides labor, and synthesizes. Best for extended, complex work; demands the most social skill.

A jigsaw with pieces that do not interlock, or a think-pair-share used for a task that needed division of labor, is a structure mismatch that undermines the learning.

### Size The Group To The Task

Group size is a design variable, not a default. Larger groups dilute individual accountability and invite social loafing; smaller groups increase participation but reduce the range of resources and perspectives.

Use size deliberately:

- pairs for maximum individual talk time and lowest freeloading risk;
- triads or quads for tasks requiring role diversity or jigsaw division;
- rarely exceed four for academic tasks, because the fifth and sixth members tend to disengage;
- larger groups only for genuinely complex projects with clearly divided roles and individual accountability mechanisms.

The right size is the smallest group that can do the task well, not the largest that fits the furniture.

### Compose Groups For The Cognitive And Social Goal

Composition should serve the learning goal, and different goals call for different mixes. Decide what mix the task needs, rather than defaulting to friendship or convenience.

- Heterogeneous grouping (mixed readiness, language, or background) supports peer explanation, exposes students to diverse reasoning, and benefits the explainer as much as the explained; best for most academic tasks.
- Homogeneous grouping (similar readiness) lets the teacher target a small group at the same level, or lets advanced students extend without dominating others; use selectively, not as a permanent track.
- Balance language learners with fluent peers who can provide comprehensible input, but do not isolate a single language learner per group repeatedly.
- Vary composition across tasks so no student is permanently the weakest or strongest in their group, which damages both motivation and status.

Permanent ability tracking via seating is not cooperative learning; it is stratification with tables.

### Sequence Individual, Pair, And Group Work

The strongest cooperative sequences begin with individual thinking, not with group talk. A student who has not yet formed a thought arrives at the group with nothing to contribute and defaults to copying or silence.

Sequence:

- individual first: each student thinks, writes, or attempts alone for a short, defined time;
- pair or small group: members compare, explain, and reconcile;
- whole group or class: synthesized product or shared reporting.

The individual phase protects against freeloading and ensures every member brings something to the group. Skipping it is the most common sequencing error.

### Teach And Rehearse The Structure

Students do not inherently know how to execute a jigsaw or a numbered-heads-together; they will improvise, and the improvisation usually collapses into one student doing the work. Teach the structure explicitly the first time, rehearse it with low-stakes content, and only then run it with the target material. A structure that is never taught will not produce the participation patterns it was designed for.

### Keep The Task At The Right Cognitive Level

If the task is too easy, cooperation is unnecessary and the group finishes in two minutes with no one stretched. If it is too hard, the group cannot make progress and the strongest member either does it all or everyone stalls. Aim for tasks in the productive band where the work is genuinely challenging for an individual but achievable by a coordinated group, so that cooperation is both necessary and sufficient.

## Common Traps

### Proximity Mistaken For Cooperation

Seating students together on a task one could do alone produces parallel individual work, not cooperation. Engineer interdependence into the task.

### Interdependence Without Accountability

A comfortable group where some members coast still produces unequal learning. Pair positive interdependence with individual accountability.

### Structure Chosen By Familiarity

Using think-pair-share for a task that needed jigsaw, or vice versa, undermines the purpose. Match the structure to the cognitive and social goal.

### Groups Too Large

Groups of five or six invite the disengagement of peripheral members. Use the smallest group that can do the task.

### Defaulting To Friendship Or Convenience

Composing groups by who sits near whom or who is friends ignores the learning goal. Compose for the cognitive and social purpose.

### Skipping The Individual Phase

Starting with group talk lets unprepared students copy or stay silent. Begin with individual thinking.

### Never Teaching The Structure

Assuming students know how to execute a structure leads to improvised collapse. Teach and rehearse it first.

### Task Too Easy Or Too Hard

A trivial task needs no cooperation; an impossible one invites dominance or stall. Calibrate to the band where cooperation is necessary and sufficient.

## Self-Check

- Could one motivated student complete the task alone, and if so, has the task been redesigned so cooperation is structurally required?
- Is positive interdependence engineered explicitly, with the form (goal, resource, role, reward) stated and enforced?
- Is the structure (think-pair-share, jigsaw, numbered heads, project) chosen to match the cognitive and social purpose, not by familiarity?
- Is the group size the smallest that can do the task well, with a stated reason for the size chosen?
- Is composition matched to the learning goal, with heterogeneous or homogeneous mixing chosen deliberately and varied across tasks?
- Does the sequence begin with individual thinking before group talk, to protect against freeloading?
- Has the structure been explicitly taught and rehearsed with low-stakes content before use with target material?
- Is the task in the productive band where it is challenging for an individual but achievable by a coordinated group?
- Is individual accountability paired with the interdependence so all members must learn, not just cooperate?
