# Test Preparation Coaching

Most students study badly, and most study advice makes it worse. The strategies that feel productive, rereading notes, highlighting, cramming the night before, are among the least effective known to cognitive science, while the strategies that work, retrieval practice, spacing, interleaving, feel harder and less satisfying and so are avoided. The result is that students who "study hard" still underperform, conclude they are not smart, and never learn that the problem was method. Coaching test preparation is the work of replacing ineffective habits with effective ones, while also addressing the anxiety, motivation, and planning problems that derail preparation regardless of method.

This skill covers the judgment needed to coach effective test preparation: teaching evidence-based study strategies, helping students plan and pace their review, addressing test anxiety, designing review that teaches how to study, and using practice results to focus effort. The goal is students who can prepare effectively and independently for any assessment.

## Core Rules

### Teach Retrieval Practice As The Core Study Strategy

The single most evidence-supported study method is retrieval practice: actively recalling information from memory rather than passively reviewing it. Self-testing with flashcards, practice questions, blank-page recall, and explaining concepts aloud all force retrieval and produce far stronger memory than rereading. The reason most students avoid retrieval is that it feels difficult and exposes what they do not know, which is exactly why it works. Teach retrieval explicitly, normalize the productive difficulty, and help students understand that the feeling of effort is the learning happening, not a sign of failure.

Rereading and highlighting, by contrast, produce familiarity that masquerades as mastery and collapses under assessment pressure.

### Teach Spacing And Interleaving Over Massed Cramming

Cramming produces short-term recall that fades quickly; spacing study across multiple sessions produces durable memory. Interleaving, mixing different problem types or topics within a study session, is harder and feels less productive than blocking one topic at a time, but it produces better discrimination and transfer, especially in mathematics and skills-based subjects. Teach students to spread study over days rather than compressing it into one night, and to mix topics rather than finishing one before starting the next. These strategies feel less efficient in the moment and are substantially more effective over time, which is a hard sell worth making repeatedly.

### Help Students Plan And Pace Their Preparation

Effective study requires planning: working backward from the assessment, allocating time across topics based on difficulty and importance, and scheduling spaced sessions. Most students do not plan and instead study whatever is in front of them in the order it appears. Teach a simple planning routine: list what will be assessed, rank by confidence and importance, schedule spaced sessions, and build in retrieval and review. A student with a plan studies the right things; a student without one studies whatever feels easiest.

### Use Practice And Feedback To Direct Study

The most useful information for focusing study is what the student does and does not already know, which practice reveals and rereading conceals. Have students attempt practice questions early, before they feel ready, and use the results to target their study. Studying what you already know feels good and wastes time; studying what you got wrong feels bad and produces growth. Coach students to treat practice errors as the map of where to spend effort, not as verdicts on ability.

### Address Test Anxiety As A Skill, Not Just A Feeling

For many students, anxiety, not knowledge, is what determines assessment performance. Anxiety consumes working memory, causes students to freeze or rush, and makes prepared students perform below their actual ability. Treat anxiety as something students can learn to manage rather than as a fixed trait: teach grounding and breathing techniques, normalize anxiety as a normal response, build familiarity with the assessment format through practice under similar conditions, and help students reframe the physical sensations of arousal as readiness rather than threat. Severe anxiety may warrant referral to counseling; do not treat it as something the student just needs to push through.

### Teach Students To Interpret Assessment Formats And Task Demands

Part of preparation is understanding what kind of thinking an assessment requires: recall, application, analysis, synthesis, or extended writing. Different demands call for different preparation. Coach students to read the assessment structure, identify what each section is really asking, and prepare accordingly. A student who studies only content but not the application of that content will be surprised by an assessment that asks them to use what they know in a new context.

### Make Review Teach Study Skill, Not Just Content

Review activities in class are an opportunity to model effective study methods, not just to re-cover content. Run a retrieval practice routine, demonstrate how to build a study schedule, or have students interleave problems and reflect on the experience. If review is just another lecture, students learn nothing about how to study independently. Treat review as apprenticeship in study skill.

### Avoid Encouraging Strategies That Feel Good But Do Not Work

Be honest with students about which strategies work and which do not, even when the ineffective ones are popular. Rereading, highlighting, and cramming feel productive and are weak; retrieval, spacing, and interleaving feel harder and are strong. Students will drift back to the comfortable ineffective methods unless the case for the effective ones is made repeatedly and credibly.

## Common Traps

### Rereading And Highlighting As Default Study

Both feel productive and produce little learning. Teach retrieval practice instead.

### Cramming Praised As Dedication

Massed cramming produces fragile short-term recall. Teach spacing and interleaving.

### Studying What Is Already Known

It feels good and wastes time. Use practice results to target gaps.

### Treating Anxiety As A Character Flaw

Anxiety is manageable skill, not weakness. Teach techniques and refer severe cases.

### Review As Re-Lecture

Re-covering content teaches nothing about how to study. Use review to apprentice study skill.

### Ignoring Assessment Format In Preparation

Content study alone fails application questions. Coach students to read task demands.

### Letting Students Default To Comfortable Ineffective Methods

The strategies that feel easy do not work. Make the case for harder, better methods repeatedly.

## Self-Check

- Am I teaching retrieval practice as the core strategy, and normalizing its productive difficulty?
- Am I teaching spacing and interleaving over massed cramming?
- Am I helping students plan and pace preparation by working backward from the assessment?
- Do I use practice and feedback to direct study toward gaps rather than comfort?
- Do I treat test anxiety as a learnable skill and refer severe cases?
- Do I coach students to interpret assessment formats and prepare for the right kind of thinking?
- Does my in-class review teach study skill, not just re-cover content?
- Am I honest about which strategies work and which only feel productive?
