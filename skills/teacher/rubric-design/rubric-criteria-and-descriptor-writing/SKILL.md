# Rubric Criteria And Descriptor Writing

The criteria and descriptors are the substance of a rubric; everything else is layout. A rubric with the right structure but poorly written criteria is worse than useless because it creates the illusion of objectivity while actually grading on impression. The recurring failure is language that looks precise but communicates nothing: level descriptions that differ only by adjectives like good, fair, and poor; criteria that bundle three separate skills into one score; descriptors that confuse how much work was done with how well it was done; and levels so similar that two graders reading the same words reach different scores. Writing strong criteria and descriptors is a craft of making invisible expectations observable, parallel, and distinct.

Use this skill while drafting or revising the criteria rows and performance-level cells of any rubric, proficiency scale, or scoring guide. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing rubric text that is grammatically correct, professionally formatted, and yet unusable for consistent scoring or actionable feedback.

## Core Rules

### Define Each Criterion As A Dimension Of Quality

A criterion should name a distinct dimension of performance that contributes to quality, not a step in the task or a feature of the format. "Introduction, body, conclusion" is a map of the assignment; "claim clarity, use of evidence, reasoning, organization" is a map of quality. The test is whether two pieces of work could be strong on one criterion and weak on another. If the criteria cannot vary independently, they are not separate dimensions and should be merged or rewritten.

Strong criteria for academic work often include content understanding or accuracy, use of evidence or source quality, reasoning or argument, structure or coherence, technique or craft, and conventions appropriate to the discipline. For performances, criteria might include technique, interpretation, and audience engagement. Choose only the dimensions that genuinely matter to the learning target; every added criterion multiplies the writing and grading burden.

### Make Descriptors Observable, Not Evaluative

A descriptor should describe features of the work that a reader can point to, not a judgment of the student. "Uses two or more relevant sources and explains how each supports the claim" is observable. "Shows good understanding of sources" is evaluative and tells the grader nothing about where the boundary falls. The test is whether two people looking at the same paper could locate the described feature. If the descriptor only restates a quality judgment, it cannot guide consistent scoring.

Replace evaluative adjectives with concrete markers: counts, named features, described relationships, specified structures. "Cites evidence for each major claim" beats "supports claims well." Where a quality judgment is unavoidable, anchor it with at least one observable example so the judgment has a handle.

### Distinguish Quantity From Quality Explicitly

A common collapse is treating amount of work as evidence of quality. A paragraph with five pieces of evidence is not necessarily better argued than one with two well-chosen pieces. Decide for each criterion whether quantity matters, and if it does, state it separately from quality rather than letting the two blur.

For example, "includes three sources" is a quantity statement; "selects sources that directly address the counterargument" is a quality statement. A descriptor that reads "includes many relevant sources" confuses the two because a reader cannot tell whether a student who included two highly relevant sources should score above or below one who included five marginally relevant ones. Separate the dimensions, or within a single descriptor make the quality requirement explicit and primary.

### Write Levels That Differ On The Same Dimension

Each performance level for a given criterion must describe a different amount or quality of the same feature. The most common failure is levels that differ by changing the subject: the top level says "evidence is well integrated," the middle says "evidence is present," and the bottom says "evidence is missing." These levels actually describe three different things, integration, presence, and absence, so a paper with present but poorly integrated evidence fits nowhere cleanly.

Keep the dimension constant across levels and vary the degree. If the dimension is integration of evidence, then every level should describe integration: "evidence is woven into the reasoning with explicit links," "evidence is included but loosely connected to the reasoning," "evidence appears as dropped quotations with no connection." Now each level is a point on the same scale.

### Use Parallel Structure Across Levels

Within a criterion, the descriptors at each level should follow the same grammatical structure and address the same sub-features. Parallel structure lets a grader scan across levels and see exactly what changes. If the top level mentions evidence, reasoning, and organization but the middle level mentions only evidence, the grader cannot tell how reasoning or organization should be scored at the middle level.

Write each level as a sentence or phrase that names the same components in the same order, changing only the degree or quality. This discipline also exposes when a criterion is secretly two criteria bundled together: if you cannot describe all levels on the same components, the criterion may need splitting.

### Define The Boundary Between Adjacent Levels

Graders spend most of their uncertainty on the border between two levels. A descriptor that does not specify where one level ends and the next begins forces arbitrary calls. Build boundary language into the descriptors: "uses two or more relevant sources" versus "uses one relevant source" gives a clear line; "uses several sources" versus "uses some sources" does not.

Where a strict count is artificial, use qualitative thresholds that are still distinguishable, such as "reasoning connects evidence to claim throughout" versus "reasoning connects evidence to claim in the strongest section only." The point is that a reader can decide which side of the line a paper falls on without guessing.

### Keep The Number Of Levels To The Minimum Defensible

Every additional level requires a distinct descriptor for every criterion and increases the grader's discrimination burden. Three or four levels are enough for most developmental and feedback purposes. Five or more should be used only when fine discrimination is genuinely required and can be described concretely, such as in external certification scoring.

Decide whether the top level represents a realistic target students can reach or a rare ideal. If the top level is unreachable, students stop aiming for it and the rubric's motivational function collapses. Similarly, decide whether the bottom level represents a genuine lowest quality of attempt or a separate "no evidence" category; conflating missing work with weak work distorts scores.

### Separate Achievement From Behavior, Effort, And Format

Descriptors often smuggle in non-achievement factors: "turned in on time," "shows effort," "is neatly presented." These conflate what a student knows with how they behaved or how the work looks. If behavior, timeliness, or presentation matters, report it separately rather than letting it inflate or depress the achievement score.

The same applies to language proficiency: do not let descriptor language penalize content mastery for surface errors unless language is the learning target. Keep separate what is separate, so the score honestly reports the dimension it claims to report.

### Write In Language The Audience Can Use

If students are expected to read the rubric before working, the descriptors must be in language they understand. Technical or vague terms defeat the rubric's function as a guide to the work. After drafting, read each descriptor as a student would and ask whether it tells them what to do or produce. Where a term is necessary but specialized, define it once or pair it with a plain-language gloss.

## Common Traps

### Adjective Ladders

Levels that differ only by excellent, good, fair, and poor communicate nothing and guarantee inconsistent scoring. Replace adjectives with observable markers.

### Bundling Multiple Skills Into One Criterion

Rolling evidence, reasoning, and organization into a single "argument" criterion hides which skill is weak and makes the score unactionable. Split dimensions that can vary independently.

### Confusing Quantity With Quality

Rewarding amount of work rather than quality of work inflates scores for verbose or lengthy submissions and penalizes concise, strong work. State quality requirements explicitly.

### Levels That Describe Different Things

If the top level describes integration, the middle describes presence, and the bottom describes absence, the levels are not on the same scale and work will fit nowhere cleanly. Hold the dimension constant across levels.

### Non-Parallel Descriptors

When levels address different sub-features, graders cannot compare across levels and students cannot see what to improve. Use parallel structure naming the same components.

### Fuzzy Boundaries

Descriptors without a clear line between levels force arbitrary scoring on borderline work and invite disputes. Build explicit thresholds into the language.

### Too Many Levels With Thin Distinctions

Five or six levels that differ marginally are impossible to apply consistently and exhausting to write well. Use only as many levels as you can distinctly describe and defend.

### Mixing Behavior Into Achievement Descriptors

Penalizing achievement for neatness, timeliness, or effort makes the score misrepresent learning. Keep behavior and format in separate reporting.

## Self-Check

- Does each criterion name a distinct dimension of quality that can vary independently of the others?
- Are descriptors written as observable features of the work rather than evaluative judgments of the student?
- Where quantity matters, is it stated separately from and subordinate to quality?
- Do the levels within each criterion differ on the same dimension rather than describing different things?
- Do level descriptors use parallel structure so a grader can scan across levels and see exactly what changes?
- Is the boundary between adjacent levels explicit enough that a grader can place borderline work without guessing?
- Is the number of levels the minimum that the feedback or scoring purpose requires?
- Are achievement, behavior, effort, and format kept separate so the score reports what it claims to report?
- Can the intended student audience read and act on the descriptors in plain language?
