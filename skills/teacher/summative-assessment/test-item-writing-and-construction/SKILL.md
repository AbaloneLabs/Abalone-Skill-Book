# Test Item Writing And Construction

A blueprint tells you what to assess; item writing is where the assessment actually lives or dies. A well-aligned blueprint can still produce an invalid test if the items are ambiguous, clueing, loaded with tricks, or biased. Item writing is a craft with concrete rules, and most item flaws are invisible to the person who wrote them. A stem that seems clear to the author may be unreadable to a student; a distractor that seems obviously wrong to an expert may be the most attractive option to a learner with a partial misconception; a constructed-response prompt that seems open may be answered in wildly different ways because it never specified the required reasoning. The discipline of item writing is to make each item measure the intended cognition and nothing else, so that a correct answer reflects understanding and an incorrect answer reflects a gap, rather than confusion, test-wiseness, or fatigue.

Use this skill whenever test items are being written or reviewed. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing items that look professional but are ambiguous, clueing, tricky, unfair, or misaligned with the cognitive demand of the objective.

## Core Rules

### Write Each Item To Measure One Specific Objective At One Demand

Every item should have a single, clear purpose: to measure one objective at one cognitive demand. Items that try to do too much become uninterpretable, because a wrong answer could reflect a gap in any of several skills.

Before writing, confirm:

- which objective the item measures;
- what cognitive demand it places on the student;
- that the item requires that demand and not a lower-level shortcut;
- that the item is independent of other items, so one answer does not give away another.

If you cannot state the single objective and demand an item measures, the item is not yet ready.

### Make The Stem Clear, Complete, And Self-Contained

The stem is the most important part of a selected-response item. A confusing stem turns a content item into a reading-comprehension item, introducing construct-irrelevant variance.

Write stems that:

- present a complete problem or question so the student can answer without reading the options;
- use clear, direct, grade-appropriate language;
- avoid unnecessary detail that increases reading load without adding to the construct;
- put shared wording in the stem rather than repeating it in every option;
- avoid negative phrasing where possible, and emphasize negatives such as "EXCEPT" or "NOT" when they must be used.

A student should know what is being asked before looking at the options. If the stem only makes sense after reading the options, rewrite it.

### Build Distractors That Are Plausible And Diagnostic

Distractors are not filler; they are diagnostic instruments. A good distractor reflects a specific misconception, partial understanding, or common error, so that the pattern of chosen distractors tells the teacher what students are thinking.

Build distractors that:

- are plausible to a student who has not mastered the objective;
- reflect common, specific misconceptions or mistakes;
- are clearly wrong to a student who has mastered the objective;
- are homogeneous in length, structure, and format with the correct option;
- avoid absurd, humorous, or obviously throwaway options that no student would choose;
- are mutually exclusive where the item calls for one best answer.

Map each distractor to the misconception it represents. A distractor with no plausible reasoning behind it teaches you nothing when students choose it.

### Avoid Clueing And Test-Wiseness Cues

Students who are skilled at taking tests can find the right answer without knowing the content, by reading cues the author left by accident. These cues make the item measure test-wiseness rather than learning.

Eliminate common clues:

- specific determiners such as "always" or "never" in options, which are usually wrong;
- making the correct option noticeably longer or more qualified than the distractors;
- grammatical mismatches between stem and distractors that reveal the wrong options;
- repeating a key word from the stem only in the correct option;
- using "all of the above" or "none of the above" in ways that let students eliminate or confirm by partial knowledge;
- placing the correct answer in a predictable position across items.

Review each item as if you did not know the content, asking whether the answer could be guessed from structure alone.

### Ensure One Unambiguously Best Answer

A selected-response item must have one best answer, not several defensible ones. Ambiguous items punish knowledgeable students who can argue for a second option.

Ensure one best answer by:

- confirming the correct option is clearly and defensibly the best;
- confirming every distractor is wrong for a specific, statable reason;
- avoiding "trick" items whose difficulty comes from ambiguity rather than demand;
- having a colleague try to defend each distractor as correct, and revising any that survive;
- when more than one response is acceptable, changing the format to "select all that apply" and stating it explicitly.

An item that depends on a trick or a fine, unstated distinction measures the author's cleverness, not the student's learning.

### Write Constructed-Response Prompts That Specify The Required Reasoning

Constructed-response items are powerful because they let students show thinking, but they are also easy to write vaguely. A prompt that does not specify what reasoning is required produces answers that cannot be scored consistently.

Write constructed-response prompts that:

- state precisely what the student must do, explain, justify, compare, analyze;
- specify the audience, purpose, and any required format or length;
- name the evidence or criteria the response must use;
- make the cognitive demand explicit, so a list is not accepted where an argument is required;
- include any necessary context without burying the actual task.

Pair every constructed-response prompt with a rubric or scoring guide that defines what a complete response looks like. The prompt and the rubric should be written together.

### Allocate Points By The Demand And Importance, Not By Convenience

Point values communicate what matters. Items should be worth points proportionate to their demand, importance, and the evidence they provide.

Allocate points so that:

- higher-demand items, such as extended reasoning or transfer, are worth more than recall;
- essential objectives carry more total points than minor ones;
- no single item is worth so much that one mistake dominates the score;
- partial credit is planned for multi-step constructed responses;
- the total aligns with the blueprint's weighting.

Points assigned by habit, such as everything worth one point, distort the meaning of the score when items vary in demand.

### Review Items For Bias And Construct-Irrelevant Variance

Item review for bias is a required step, not optional. Bias is usually invisible to the author, so a deliberate review with fresh eyes is necessary.

Review each item for:

- names, contexts, and scenarios that represent diverse groups and avoid stereotypes;
- cultural, socioeconomic, or geographic references that assume shared background;
- vocabulary and reading load that exceed what the content construct requires;
- timed conditions that disproportionately penalize some students;
- language that could be interpreted differently by multilingual learners;
- double-jeopardy structures where one error cascades into many lost points.

Have a colleague review when possible. The author is often the last person to see an item's bias.

### Pilot Items And Use Post-Administration Data and follow Accessibility And Accommodation Requirements

Items improve with evidence. Whenever possible, pilot new items or examine how they performed after administration.

Use item data to:

- flag items that were answered correctly or incorrectly by nearly everyone, which may be too easy, too hard, or misaligned;
- examine whether distractors attracted students as intended, which validates their diagnostic design;
- watch for differential performance across groups, which can signal bias;
- retire or revise items that performed poorly before reusing them;
- build a bank of validated items over time rather than writing every assessment from scratch.

An item is a hypothesis about what students know. Treat post-administration data as the test of that hypothesis.

Items must be usable by all students, including those with documented accommodations. Accessibility is part of item quality, not an add-on.

Ensure items:

- are compatible with required accommodations such as read-aloud, extended time, or alternate format;
- use clear layout, adequate white space, and legible formatting;
- avoid relying solely on color or visual features inaccessible to some students;
- follow universal design principles that improve access for everyone;
- have equivalent alternate forms available if make-ups are needed.

An item that cannot be accessed by a student with a required accommodation does not measure that student's learning.

## Common Traps

### Stems That Require The Options To Make Sense

A stem that only becomes clear after reading the options turns the item into a puzzle. Make the stem self-contained.

### Throwaway Distractors

Options so absurd no student would choose them teach nothing and lower the item's discrimination. Make every distractor plausible and diagnostic.

### Clueing From Structure

Length, grammar, determiners, or position patterns that let test-wise students guess correctly. Review as a non-expert would.

### Trick Items Whose Difficulty Is Ambiguity

Items that depend on a fine unstated distinction punish knowledgeable students. Aim for one unambiguously best answer.

### Vague Constructed-Response Prompts

Prompts that do not specify the required reasoning produce unscorable answers. Pair every prompt with a rubric.

### Points By Habit

Assigning uniform point values regardless of demand distorts the score. Allocate by demand and importance.

### Skipping The Bias Review

Bias is invisible to the author. Schedule a deliberate review with fresh eyes.

### Reusing Unvalidated Items Forever

Items that were never piloted or analyzed may be flawed without anyone knowing. Use post-administration data to revise and retire.

## Self-Check

- Does each item measure one specific objective at one intended cognitive demand, with no lower-level shortcut?
- Is every stem clear, complete, and self-contained so the student knows what is asked before reading the options?
- Are distractors plausible, diagnostic of specific misconceptions, and clearly wrong to a master of the objective?
- Has the item been checked for clueing, length cues, determiners, grammatical mismatches, and predictable answer positions?
- Is there one unambiguously best answer, with every distractor wrong for a specific, statable reason?
- Do constructed-response prompts specify the required reasoning, audience, evidence, and demand, with a matching rubric?
- Are point values allocated by demand and importance, with no single item dominating the score and partial credit planned?
- Has a deliberate bias review checked names, contexts, vocabulary, reading load, timing, and language for fairness?
- Are items compatible with required IEP, 504, and language accommodations, and do they follow universal design principles?
- Is there a plan to use pilot or post-administration data to validate, revise, or retire items over time?
