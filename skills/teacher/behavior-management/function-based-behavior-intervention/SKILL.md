# Function-Based Behavior Intervention

Behavior is not random. It serves a function for the student: to get something, such as attention, an object, an activity, or sensory stimulation, or to escape or avoid something, such as a difficult task, an aversive social situation, an overwhelming environment, or an internal state. Two students can show the exact same behavior, calling out, leaving the room, refusing work, for completely different functions, and the same consequence that helps one will make the other worse. Function-based intervention is the discipline of identifying what the behavior actually accomplishes for the student and designing support that teaches a more appropriate way to achieve the same function. The most common failure is responding to the form of behavior, what it looks like, rather than its function, what it achieves, and then being surprised when a consequence that should work makes things worse. A second failure is assuming function without data, projecting an explanation onto the student rather than investigating.

Use this skill when analyzing persistent behavior, conducting or contributing to a functional behavior assessment, or designing a function-based intervention. The goal is to identify the function through data, teach a replacement behavior that serves the same function, and align responses so they do not accidentally reinforce the very behavior being targeted.

## Core Rules

### Understand That Behavior Serves A Function

All persistent behavior does something for the student. The two broad categories are obtaining, attention, tangibles, activities, sensory stimulation, and escaping or avoiding, tasks, people, environments, internal states. Within these, the specific function matters: attention from peers is different from attention from the teacher, escape from a hard task is different from escape from a noisy room. Before designing intervention, identify the function the behavior actually serves.

### Use Data, Not Assumption, To Identify Function

Function is identified through data, not projection. Conduct a functional behavior assessment that gathers antecedent-behavior-consequence data across settings and times, interviews with the student, family, and staff, direct observation, and review of records and prior interventions. Look for patterns: when does the behavior occur, what reliably precedes it, what reliably follows it, when does it not occur, what interventions have been tried and what happened. The function emerges from the pattern, not from a first guess.

### Distinguish Function From Form

The form of a behavior, what it looks like, tells you little about its function. Calling out might serve attention from peers, escape from a task the student finds too hard, or access to a preferred activity. Refusing to work might escape an overwhelming demand, avoid public failure, or obtain adult one-on-one attention. The same consequence, such as sending the student to the office, might provide escape for one student and attention for another, accidentally reinforcing exactly the function driving the behavior. Respond to function, not form.

### Teach A Replacement Behavior That Serves The Same Function

The most effective interventions teach a replacement behavior that lets the student achieve the same function through appropriate means. If the function is escape from hard tasks, the replacement might be a taught help-request routine, a break card, or scaffolded task entry. If the function is peer attention, the replacement might be taught ways to gain peer attention through contribution, humor within limits, or leadership. The replacement must be as efficient and reliable as the problem behavior at achieving the function, or the student will return to the behavior that works.

### Align Antecedents And Consequences To Support The Plan

A function-based plan adjusts both antecedents and consequences. Adjust antecedents to prevent the behavior: pre-teach, scaffold, reduce triggers, build in choice, prime transitions, modify tasks. Adjust consequences so the problem behavior no longer achieves its function and the replacement behavior does. If escape has been the function, do not let the problem behavior produce escape; instead, let the replacement behavior produce a legitimate break. Misaligned consequences that accidentally reinforce the problem function are the most common reason plans fail.

### Make The Replacement Behavior Efficient And Reinforced

A replacement behavior that is harder, slower, or less reliable than the problem behavior will not be adopted. Make the replacement efficient: it should require less effort and produce the function at least as quickly as the problem behavior did. Reinforce the replacement heavily at first, fade reinforcement as it becomes established, and ensure the function is genuinely achieved through it. A help-request that is ignored teaches the student that asking does not work.

### Address Setting Events And Antecedents, Not Only Consequences

Behavior is shaped by setting events, the conditions that make the behavior more or less likely, such as hunger, fatigue, conflict, prior failure, or disrupted routine. Address setting events proactively where possible: breakfast, sleep, predictable routine, social support, modified demands on hard days. The strongest plans reduce the conditions that set up the behavior in addition to responding after it occurs.

### Collect Data To Evaluate The Plan

A function-based plan is a hypothesis to be tested. Collect data on the target behavior before, during, and after implementation, and on the replacement behavior's use. If the behavior decreases and the replacement increases, the function hypothesis is supported. If not, revise the hypothesis and the plan. Plans that are never evaluated cannot improve and often quietly fail while appearing to work.

### Coordinate With The Team And Follow Individual Plans

Function-based intervention for significant behavior is a team process involving the special educator, behavior specialist, counselor, family, and often the student. Implement IEP behavior intervention plans exactly as written, contribute data and observations to team meetings, and escalate concerns through proper channels. Do not improvise interventions that conflict with or duplicate the plan, and follow safety protocols for serious behavior.

### Include The Student's Voice

The student is often the best source of information about the function of their own behavior, especially older students. Ask them, in a calm moment, what was happening before, what they were trying to get or avoid, and what would help. Student voice improves the accuracy of the functional assessment and the buy-in for the plan. A plan designed with the student is more likely to work than one imposed on them.

## Common Traps

### Responding To Form Instead Of Function

Same behavior, different function, different right response. Identify the function before designing intervention.

### Projecting Function Without Data

Function emerges from antecedent-behavior-consequence patterns, not first guesses. Investigate.

### Consequences That Accidentally Reinforce The Target Behavior

Office referral that provides escape or attention strengthens the behavior. Align consequences to function.

### Replacement Behavior Less Efficient Than The Problem

If the replacement is harder or slower, the student returns to what works. Make it efficient and reinforced.

### Ignoring Setting Events And Antecedents

Address the conditions that set up the behavior, not only the consequences after it.

### Plans Never Evaluated

Collect data before, during, and after. Revise the hypothesis when the plan does not work.

### Improvising Around The IEP Behavior Plan

Implement the plan exactly and coordinate with the team.

### Designing Plans Without The Student

The student often knows the function. Include their voice for accuracy and buy-in.

## Self-Check

- Do you understand that persistent behavior serves a function of obtaining or escaping, and that function must be identified before intervention?
- Is the function identified through antecedent-behavior-consequence data, observation, interviews, and records rather than assumption?
- Are you responding to the function the behavior serves rather than its surface form?
- Does the intervention teach a replacement behavior that achieves the same function through appropriate means?
- Are antecedents adjusted to prevent the behavior and consequences aligned so the problem no longer achieves its function?
- Is the replacement behavior at least as efficient and reliable as the problem behavior, and heavily reinforced at first?
- Are setting events and antecedents addressed proactively, not only consequences after the fact?
- Is data collected before, during, and after implementation to test the hypothesis and revise the plan?
- Are IEP behavior intervention plans implemented exactly, with the team coordinated and safety protocols followed for serious behavior?
- Is the student's voice included in identifying the function and designing the plan?
