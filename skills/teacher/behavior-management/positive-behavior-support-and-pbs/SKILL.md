# Positive Behavior Support And PBS

Positive Behavior Support is a multi-tiered framework for improving behavior and learning by teaching, acknowledging, and reinforcing expected behavior, providing targeted support to students who need more, and delivering intensive individualized support to the few who need most. It is the opposite of a punishment-first system in both philosophy and effect. Punishment-first systems are dramatic and visible but produce avoidance, resentment, escalation, and disproportionate impact on the very students, often by race, disability, and trauma history, whom schools most need to engage. PBS systems are quieter and more preventive: they install clear expectations, teach them explicitly, acknowledge them consistently, and reserve intensive intervention for the students whose data shows they need it. The framework rests on the recognition that behavior, like academics, is learned and can be taught, and that the goal is competent, self-regulated students rather than compliant, fearful ones.

Use this skill when designing or revising a behavior support system, whether classroom-level or schoolwide. The goal is to build a multi-tiered preventive framework that teaches and reinforces expected behavior, identifies students who need more, and delivers support proportionate to need while monitoring equity.

## Core Rules

### Treat Behavior As Learned And Teachable

Behavior is not a fixed trait to be punished into compliance; it is a set of skills and responses that can be taught, practiced, reinforced, and corrected like any other learning. A student who calls out has not been taught and reinforced for the alternative behavior of raising a hand and waiting. A student who shuts down has not been taught coping and help-seeking strategies. Reframe behavior problems as teaching opportunities, and invest in explicit instruction of expected behavior rather than only consequences for its absence.

### Establish Three To Five Schoolwide Expectations

A small number of clear, positive, broadly applicable expectations, often three to five, provide the backbone of a PBS system. Common formulations include be safe, be respectful, be responsible, or care for self, others, and the environment. Define each expectation specifically for the settings where it applies: what does respectful look like in the hallway, the cafeteria, the classroom, the assembly. Fewer, clearer expectations taught deeply beat many rules enforced shallowly.

### Teach Expected Behavior Explicitly

Expectations that are only posted on the wall are decorations. Teach them directly: name the behavior, explain why it matters, model it, show examples and non-examples, have students practice, give feedback, and reteach after breaks and problems. Use lesson plans for behavior just as for academics. The first weeks of school and the points after disruption are the highest-leverage times for this teaching, but it continues throughout the year as routines are refined and new settings arise.

### Acknowledge Expected Behavior Consistently

Acknowledgment, not punishment, is the engine of a PBS system. Students who demonstrate expected behavior should receive specific, genuine acknowledgment that names what they did, not generic praise. Acknowledgment can be verbal, social, or part of a token or recognition system, but the key is consistency, specificity, and a ratio that favors acknowledgment of positive behavior over correction. Aim to catch students doing the right thing far more often than the wrong, and distribute acknowledgment equitably across all students, not only the easy ones.

### Use Multi-Tiered Support Proportional To Need

PBS organizes support into tiers. Universal, Tier 1, is the strong core instruction and reinforcement all students receive; about eighty percent of students should succeed at this level with no additional support. Targeted, Tier 2, is small-group or check-in support for students whose data shows they need more, perhaps fifteen percent. Intensive, Tier 3, is individualized, function-based support for the small number, perhaps five percent, whose needs are significant. Use data to identify who needs more, deliver support proportional to need, and avoid both under-serving students who struggle and over-serving students who do not.

### Use Data To Identify Needs And Monitor Effectiveness

Behavior decisions should be driven by data, not impression. Track office referrals, suspensions, acknowledgment distribution, attendance, tardies, and minor behavior incidents, and disaggregate by student group to detect disproportionality. Use the data to identify students who need targeted or intensive support, to evaluate whether interventions are working, and to surface equity problems. A PBS system without data is a set of good intentions; with data it becomes a accountable, improvable system.

### Monitor And Address Disproportionality

Discipline data consistently shows disproportionate impact on students by race, disability, gender, and language status, often driven by subjective categories such as defiance and disruption that reflect adult perception as much as student behavior. Monitor disaggregated data, examine the subjective categories where bias enters, audit who receives acknowledgment versus correction, and intervene on patterns rather than treating disproportionality as coincidence. Equity is a core PBS responsibility, not an optional add-on.

### Match Consequences To Function And Severity

When consequences are needed, they should match the function the behavior served and the severity of the behavior, not the adult's frustration. Logical consequences that teach the alternative behavior, restore harm, and return the student to learning are more effective than exclusionary punishment, which removes students from instruction, damages relationship, and predicts further problems. Reserve exclusion for genuine safety, and always plan reentry, repair, and recovery of missed instruction.

### Coordinate With Specialists And Follow Individual Plans

Many students with significant behavior needs have IEPs with behavior intervention plans, 504 plans, or safety plans. Implement these exactly as written, coordinate with the special educator, counselor, behavior specialist, and family, and bring data to team meetings. Do not improvise interventions in place of the plan, and escalate through proper channels when the plan seems insufficient. Serious behavior involving safety, threats, self-harm, abuse, or weapons requires school protocols, not classroom improvisation.

### Build Prevention Into The Environment And Instruction

PBS is preventive at its core. Most behavior is prevented by strong instruction, clear routines, engaging tasks, strong relationships, accessible materials, and well-managed transitions. The behavior support system rests on the quality of the academic and social environment. Do not treat PBS as separate from instruction; the strongest behavior support is a classroom where students are learning successfully and feel known.

## Common Traps

### Punishment-First Systems

Visible consequences feel strong but produce avoidance, escalation, and disproportionality. Teach and acknowledge instead.

### Expectations Only Posted, Never Taught

Wall posters are decorations. Teach, model, practice, and reteach expectations explicitly.

### Generic Praise Instead Of Specific Acknowledgment

Name what the student did. Distribute acknowledgment equitably, not only to easy students.

### Skipping Tiers Or Over-Serving

Use data to match support to need. Avoid both under-serving strugglers and over-serving those who do not need it.

### Data-Free Good Intentions

Track referrals, acknowledgment, and disproportionality. Decisions need evidence.

### Ignoring Disproportionality

Discipline falls hardest on students by race, disability, and gender. Audit and intervene on patterns.

### Exclusionary Consequences For Non-Safety Issues

Removal from instruction predicts further problems. Use logical, restorative consequences and plan reentry.

### Improvising Around Individual Plans

Implement IEP and 504 behavior plans exactly. Coordinate with specialists.

### Treating PBS As Separate From Instruction

The strongest behavior support is strong, accessible instruction and relationship.

## Self-Check

- Is behavior treated as learned and teachable rather than punished into compliance?
- Are there three to five clear, positive expectations defined specifically for each setting?
- Are expectations explicitly taught, modeled, practiced, and retaught as curriculum, not only posted?
- Is expected behavior acknowledged specifically, genuinely, consistently, and equitably, with a ratio favoring acknowledgment over correction?
- Is support organized into universal, targeted, and intensive tiers, with data used to match support to need?
- Are behavior decisions driven by disaggregated data, including referrals, acknowledgment, attendance, and minor incidents?
- Is disproportionality by race, disability, gender, and language status monitored and addressed rather than treated as coincidence?
- Do consequences match function and severity, favoring logical and restorative responses over exclusion except for genuine safety?
- Are IEP, 504, behavior intervention, and safety plans implemented exactly, with specialists coordinated and serious behavior handled through school protocols?
- Is prevention built into instruction, routines, relationships, and environment rather than treated as separate from behavior support?
