# Mathematical Problem Solving Instruction

Problem solving in mathematics is what a student does when the path to a solution is not already known and laid out. It is distinct from exercises, where a learned procedure is applied to a familiar form. Genuine problem solving requires the student to make sense of the situation, choose a representation, select or invent a strategy, monitor whether it is working, and justify the result. This is where mathematics becomes a discipline of thinking rather than a catalogue of procedures, and it is also where instruction most often collapses into its opposite: the teacher demonstrates a problem type, students practice identical problems, and the whole enterprise is called problem solving even though no actual problem solving occurred because the path was given.

The recurring failure is assigning problems that are really exercises and then wondering why students cannot solve anything unfamiliar. The second failure is the opposite: assigning genuinely open problems with no support, watching students flounder, and concluding they cannot problem-solve. The design challenge is to offer problems whose solution path is not pre-specified, while teaching the heuristics, representations, and habits that let students make progress. This skill applies whenever the work is to design, sequence, or evaluate mathematical problem-solving instruction.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Problems From Exercises And Use Each Purposefully

An exercise applies a known procedure to a familiar form; its purpose is consolidation and fluency. A problem requires deciding what to do; its purpose is to develop thinking. Both have a place, but they are not interchangeable, and labeling exercises as problems denies students the thinking that problems exist to develop.

Be honest about which you are assigning and why. A unit of pure exercises produces procedural fluency without transfer; a unit of pure unsupported problems produces frustration without growth. Balance them deliberately, with exercises building the tools and problems requiring their strategic use.

### Teach Problem-Solving Heuristics Explicitly

Problem solving is not an unteachable talent; it is the deployment of heuristics that can be named, taught, and practiced. Teach students a repertoire of moves to try when stuck: draw a picture or diagram, make an organized list or table, act it out, work backwards, solve a simpler version, look for a pattern, guess and check, identify a subgoal. These are not gimmicks; they are the actual strategies competent problem solvers use.

Teach heuristics explicitly, model them, and have students name which they used and why. The goal is students who, when stuck, have moves to make rather than giving up.

### Make Sense Of The Problem Before Calculating

The most common reason students fail problems they could solve is that they begin calculating before they understand what the problem is asking. Teach an explicit sense-making phase: read the problem, restate it in your own words, identify what is given and what is asked, identify what a reasonable answer would look like, and only then choose a strategy.

Students who skip sense-making grab numbers and perform an operation, often the wrong one. The habit of pausing to understand before calculating is the single most teachable problem-solving behavior.

### Require Multiple Representations

Mathematical understanding deepens when a problem is represented in more than one way: a situation described in words, modeled with a picture, recorded in a table, expressed as an equation. Different representations reveal different aspects of the problem, and translating among them is itself mathematical thinking.

Encourage students to represent a problem in a way that makes sense to them, then to consider how another representation might show the same structure. A student who can only solve a problem one way has a fragile grasp; a student who can move among representations has a robust one.

### Use Non-Routine And Open-Ended Tasks To Build Real Problem Solving

Routine problems, however numerous, do not build problem solving because the path becomes routine. To develop genuine problem-solving ability, students need non-routine tasks whose structure they have not seen dozens of times, and open-ended tasks with multiple valid approaches or answers. These tasks force the sense-making and strategy selection that routine problems allow students to bypass.

Introduce non-routine tasks regularly, in a low-stakes culture where struggle is expected and supported, not penalized. Reserve them for after the relevant tools are available, so students are problem-solving with mathematics, not drowning without it.

### Teach Persistence And Productive Struggle As Part Of The Discipline

Problem solving is uncomfortable; being stuck is the normal state of a mathematician at work. Students who have only ever met exercises experience being stuck as failure and give up. Teach that productive struggle is the work, that being stuck is a signal to try a different heuristic rather than to stop, and that persistence is a mathematical virtue.

Normalize struggle by sharing your own thinking when you are stuck, by valuing partial progress, and by refusing to rescue students the moment they hesitate. Support without short-circuiting the thinking.

### Make Reasoning Public And Justify Solutions

A solution is not complete until it is justified. Require students to explain their reasoning, defend their strategy, and convince a skeptic. Making reasoning public through discussion, written explanation, and critique of others' solutions develops the mathematical practice of argumentation and exposes gaps that a bare answer hides.

Use structures that require justification: present a problem, have students solve and explain, then compare strategies and debate which is most efficient or general. The explanation is where the learning consolidates.

### Sequence Problems To Build Toward Generalization

A well-designed problem set is not random; it sequences problems so that patterns emerge, strategies extend, and students are led toward generalization. A sequence might begin with a concrete case, add complexity, reveal a pattern, and invite a general rule. Sequencing for emergence turns a set of problems into a curriculum of thinking.

Avoid problem sets that are merely collections of unrelated exercises. Design them so the order itself teaches.

## Common Traps

### Labeling Exercises As Problems

Assigning familiar-form practice and calling it problem solving develops no problem-solving ability. Reserve genuine problems for genuine thinking.

### Calculating Before Sense-Making

Students who grab numbers and operate without understanding choose the wrong operation. Teach an explicit sense-making phase first.

### Demonstrating Then Having Students Mimic

Modeling a problem type and having students copy the method is not problem solving; it is procedure teaching. Let students attempt before you demonstrate.

### Rescuing Students At The First Sign Of Struggle

Stepping in the moment a student hesitates denies them the thinking that produces growth. Support without short-circuiting.

### Problems Without The Needed Tools

Asking students to solve problems for which they lack the necessary concepts or skills produces drowning, not problem solving. Ensure tools are available first.

### Only One Representation Accepted

Requiring a single canonical method denies the thinking that multiple representations develop. Welcome and compare diverse approaches.

### Bare Answers Without Justification

Accepting answers without reasoning hides gaps and skips the argumentation that consolidates learning. Require justification.

### Random Problem Sets

Collections of unrelated problems teach nothing cumulatively. Sequence problems so patterns and generalizations emerge.

## Self-Check

- Are genuine problems, whose path is not pre-specified, distinguished from exercises and used purposefully alongside them?
- Are problem-solving heuristics taught explicitly, modeled, and named by students when they use them?
- Is an explicit sense-making phase required before students begin calculating?
- Are multiple representations encouraged and compared, so students can translate among word, picture, table, and equation?
- Are non-routine and open-ended tasks used regularly to develop thinking beyond familiar forms?
- Is productive struggle normalized and supported, with persistence taught as part of the discipline?
- Are students required to make their reasoning public and justify their solutions to peers?
- Are problem sets sequenced so that patterns emerge and students are led toward generalization?
- Is the balance between procedural practice and genuine problem solving deliberate rather than accidental?
- Could you name, for a student who failed a problem, whether the failure was sense-making, strategy selection, execution, or justification?
