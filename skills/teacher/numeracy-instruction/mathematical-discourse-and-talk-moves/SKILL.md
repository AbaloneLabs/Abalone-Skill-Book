# Mathematical Discourse And Talk Moves

Mathematical discourse is the structured classroom talk through which students make their thinking visible, encounter others' reasoning, and build shared mathematical understanding. It is not the same as asking questions and collecting answers. A classroom in which the teacher asks a question, a student answers, and the teacher evaluates the answer as right or wrong is conducting recitation, not discourse. Discourse is what happens when students explain, justify, compare, build on, and critique each other's mathematical ideas, with the teacher orchestrating rather than evaluating. The shift matters because mathematical understanding is constructed through the articulation and examination of reasoning, not through the receipt of correct answers.

The recurring failure is the teacher-centered pattern of initiate, respond, evaluate, in which the teacher asks, a student answers, and the teacher judges, then moves on. This pattern produces no discourse and develops no mathematical reasoning. The second failure is loose, unstructured discussion in which students talk but never engage each other's ideas, so the talk is social rather than mathematical. This skill applies whenever the work is to design, facilitate, or evaluate mathematical classroom discourse.

## Core Rules

### Shift From Evaluate-And-Move-On To Orchestrate-And-Connect

The default classroom pattern, teacher asks, student answers, teacher says good and moves on, shuts down thinking. Replace it with a pattern in which the teacher's role is to connect student ideas to each other and to the mathematics: ask another student to restate, ask whether anyone solved it differently, ask which strategies agree, ask what makes this strategy work. The teacher's questions become about the mathematics of the ideas, not about their correctness.

This shift requires resisting the impulse to immediately confirm or correct. When a teacher instantly labels an answer right or wrong, every other student stops thinking about the reasoning and waits for the verdict. Withhold judgment and turn the reasoning back to the class.

### Use Talk Moves That Make Reasoning Visible

Specific, named talk moves reliably deepen discourse. Teach and use them deliberately:

- revoicing: restating a student's contribution and asking whether you got it right, which clarifies and values the thinking;
- asking a student to restate another's idea, which forces active listening;
- pressing for reasoning: asking why, how do you know, can you explain your strategy, which moves from answers to justification;
- wait time: pausing after a question and after a response, which dramatically increases the quality and quantity of thinking;
- turn-and-talk: briefly pairing students to articulate thinking before whole-class sharing, which gives every student practice explaining.

These moves are not tricks; they are the mechanics of discourse. Use them consistently rather than occasionally.

### Anticipate And Sequence Student Solutions Before Discussion

Powerful mathematical discussion is not improvised; it is planned. Before the lesson, anticipate the strategies students are likely to use, including partial and incorrect ones. During independent work, monitor what students actually do. Then sequence which solutions to share and in what order, so the discussion builds from concrete to abstract, from common to efficient, or from partial toward complete.

Sequencing is what turns a set of student answers into a curriculum. Sharing randomly loses the arc; sharing in a planned order lets the class see how strategies relate and how understanding deepens.

### Press For Mathematical Justification, Not Just Answers

The currency of mathematical discourse is reasoning, not answers. Press students to justify: why does that work, how do you know, what is the mathematics behind your strategy, would this always work, when would it fail. A classroom that accepts answers without justification is doing arithmetic, not mathematics.

Make justification the norm by asking for it consistently, not only when an answer is wrong. Correct answers also require justification; otherwise students learn that reasoning matters only when you are in trouble.

### Make Disagreement Productive

Disagreement is among the most valuable events in mathematical discourse, because it forces examination of reasoning. Teach students to disagree with ideas, not people, and to engage a classmate's reasoning specifically: I disagree because, I see it differently because, I agree with your answer but got there a different way. Productive disagreement requires a culture in which being wrong is safe and revising thinking is celebrated.

Do not rush to resolve disagreement by announcing the correct answer. Let the disagreement surface the reasoning, let students examine each other's arguments, and let the mathematics settle the question when possible.

### Use The Board To Make Collective Thinking Visible

As students share strategies, record them publicly so the class can see and compare them. A board filled with diverse solution strategies, labeled with their authors, becomes a shared artifact the class can examine. Recording thinking externalizes it, makes it available for comparison, and honors the contributors.

Record faithfully, including partial and incorrect strategies when they are instructive, and use the recorded strategies as the basis for comparison and connection.

### Distribute Participation Deliberately

Discourse collapses when the same few students always speak. Use structures that distribute participation: turn-and-talk so every student rehearses thinking, random or strategic calling, no-hands-up norms where the teacher selects respondents, and small-group discussion before whole-class sharing. The goal is not equal airtime but broad participation in mathematical thinking.

Be aware of who is silent and why. Silence may reflect lack of access, language, status, or confidence; address the cause rather than forcing speech.

### Connect To The Mathematical Goal

Discourse is in service of a mathematical idea. Keep the discussion pointed toward the learning goal: the strategy you want everyone to take up, the generalization you want to emerge, the misconception you want to surface. Without a mathematical throughline, discussion becomes a show-and-tell of strategies with no takeaway. Decide what the discussion should leave students understanding, and steer toward it.

## Common Traps

### Initiate-Respond-Evaluate Pattern

Teacher asks, student answers, teacher judges and moves on. This recitation produces no discourse. Withhold judgment and connect ideas.

### Instant Confirmation Or Correction

Labeling an answer right or wrong the moment it is offered stops everyone else's thinking. Turn the reasoning back to the class.

### Random Sharing Without Sequencing

Calling on students arbitrarily loses the arc of building understanding. Anticipate, monitor, select, and sequence solutions.

### Answers Without Justification

Accepting correct answers without why does arithmetic, not mathematics. Press for reasoning consistently.

### Rushing To Resolve Disagreement

Announcing the correct answer the moment students disagree denies them the examination of reasoning. Let the mathematics settle it.

### Same Few Students Always Speak

Uneven participation leaves most students as spectators. Use structures that distribute who thinks and who speaks.

### Show-And-Tell Without A Mathematical Throughline

Sharing strategies with no connection to a goal produces entertainment, not learning. Steer toward the mathematical takeaway.

### Honoring Only Correct Strategies

Sharing only right answers hides the reasoning that produces growth. Share partial and incorrect strategies when they are instructive.

## Self-Check

- Has the initiate-respond-evaluate pattern been replaced with orchestration that connects student ideas?
- Are named talk moves, revoicing, restating, pressing, wait time, turn-and-talk, used consistently?
- Are student solutions anticipated, monitored, selected, and sequenced to build an arc of understanding?
- Is justification required for correct answers as well as incorrect ones, making reasoning the norm?
- Is disagreement treated as productive, with students taught to engage ideas rather than people?
- Are strategies recorded publicly so the class can compare and connect them?
- Is participation distributed through structures that broaden who thinks and who speaks?
- Does the discussion have a clear mathematical throughline that it steers toward?
- Are partial and incorrect strategies used as instructive material, not hidden?
- Could a visitor identify the mathematical idea the discussion was building toward, not just the problems it covered?
