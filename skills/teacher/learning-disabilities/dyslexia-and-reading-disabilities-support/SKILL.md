# Dyslexia And Reading Disabilities Support

Dyslexia is a specific, neurobiological language-based learning disability that affects the phonological component of reading. It is not seeing letters backwards, not a visual problem, not a sign of low intelligence, and not curable by colored overlays or by waiting for the student to mature. It is also not rare: estimates place its prevalence somewhere between five and fifteen percent of the population, which means every teacher will teach many dyslexic students regardless of whether they are identified. The most damaging classroom failures are not malice but misinformation: assuming a bright student who cannot decode must be lazy, forcing dyslexic readers to read aloud in front of peers, denying audiobooks on the grounds that they are cheating, or relying on incidental exposure to print when the student needs explicit, systematic, sequential instruction in the structure of language. A second failure is conflating decoding with comprehension: many dyslexic students have strong listening comprehension and deep understanding that print cannot yet reveal, and assessing them only through what they can decode under-measures what they know.

Use this skill when planning reading instruction, selecting texts, choosing interventions, or implementing accommodations for students with dyslexia or related reading disabilities. The goal is to provide the explicit structured instruction these students need while preserving access to grade-level content, comprehension, and dignity.

## Core Rules

### Understand Dyslexia As Phonological, Not Visual

The core difficulty in dyslexia is mapping the sounds of language, phonemes, onto letters and letter patterns, not a visual problem of reversed letters. Decoding, spelling, and reading fluency are affected; listening comprehension and reasoning are typically intact and often strong. This means a dyslexic student can understand a text read aloud that they cannot yet read independently, and instruction must target the phonological deficit through structured phonics rather than vision-based interventions that lack evidence.

### Use Explicit, Systematic, Sequential Phonics Instruction

Students with dyslexia do not absorb the code of written language through incidental exposure, immersion, or guessing from context. They need explicit, systematic, sequential instruction in phonemic awareness, phonics, decoding, spelling, and morphology, taught to mastery with cumulative review. Programs grounded in structured literacy or the Orton-Gillingham approach, multisensory, explicit, sequential, diagnostic, and cumulative, have the strongest evidence base for this population. Coordinate with the reading specialist or special educator on the specific program and its frequency, since intensity and dosage matter.

### Separate Decoding From Comprehension In Assessment

A dyslexic student's listening comprehension often far exceeds their reading comprehension because decoding is the bottleneck. Assess each separately so you know what the student understands from text when decoding is removed. Use read-aloud, audiobooks, or text-to-speech to reveal comprehension that independent reading masks. Do not conclude a student lacks understanding when the real barrier is decoding.

### Provide Assistive Technology As Access, Not Cheating

Audiobooks, text-to-speech, speech-to-text, dictation software, and word prediction are access tools that let dyslexic students engage with grade-level content and produce work matching their thinking. They are not cheating, not a crutch to be removed when the student tries harder, and not a substitute for direct decoding instruction. The student should receive both: explicit structured-literacy instruction to build decoding, and assistive technology to access content and produce work now. Denying assistive technology while waiting for decoding to mature denies the student years of grade-level learning.

### Reduce Incidental Reading Load Without Lowering Cognitive Demand

Dyslexic students tire quickly from decoding, and that fatigue consumes cognitive capacity that should go to thinking. Reduce incidental reading load by limiting unnecessary text, providing directions orally and in writing, pre-teaching key vocabulary, and chunking longer texts. Do not, however, lower the cognitive demand of the task itself. The student should still grapple with grade-level concepts, evidence, and reasoning; only the unnecessary decoding burden is reduced.

### Protect The Student During Oral Reading

Cold reading aloud in front of the class is one of the most damaging practices for a dyslexic student, producing shame, anxiety, and avoidance that can last for years. Do not call on dyslexic students to read unfamiliar text aloud. If oral reading is required for assessment or instruction, use alternatives such as letting the student prepare a passage in advance, reading a passage they have rehearsed, or using a different student for the cold-read role. A student who volunteers to read may do so; a student who does not should not be forced.

### Teach Spelling Explicitly And Accommodate It In Production

Spelling is consistently difficult for dyslexic students and does not improve through more reading exposure alone. Teach spelling explicitly through structured approaches that address phonics, morphology, and the patterns of English orthography. In content-area production, accommodate spelling through spell-check, word banks, dictation, and grading that separates spelling from ideas, unless spelling is itself the learning target. Do not lower a student's grade for spelling errors in work where spelling is incidental to the objective.

### Build Fluency Through Repeated Reading, Not Pressure

Fluency, reading with appropriate rate, accuracy, and expression, is a persistent challenge for dyslexic students even after decoding improves, because it depends on rapid automatic word recognition. Build fluency through repeated reading of practiced passages, modeled reading, choral reading, and reader's theater, with feedback and graphing of progress. Avoid timed reading pressure that produces anxiety and undermines comprehension; fluency is built through supported practice, not stress.

### Address The Emotional And Motivational Damage

By the time many dyslexic students are identified, they have absorbed a narrative of stupidity, laziness, or failure that can be harder to repair than the reading deficit itself. Address this directly: explain dyslexia in age-appropriate terms as a difference in how the brain processes language, not a measure of intelligence; connect the student with role models and peers; celebrate strengths; and ensure that accommodations are framed as access, not remediation. Motivation collapse is a real and serious consequence of unsupported dyslexia.

### Coordinate With Specialists And Implement The IEP

Dyslexia support is a team effort. Coordinate with the reading specialist, special educator, speech-language pathologist where relevant, and the family. Implement the IEP or 504 plan accommodations exactly as written, document fidelity, and bring data to team meetings. Do not improvise interventions in place of the student's plan, and escalate through proper channels if the plan seems insufficient.

## Common Traps

### Treating Dyslexia As Visual Or Curable By Overlays

Dyslexia is phonological, not visual. Evidence-based intervention is structured literacy, not colored lenses or vision therapy.

### Relying On Incidental Exposure To Teach Decoding

Dyslexic students do not absorb the code through immersion. They need explicit, systematic, sequential phonics instruction.

### Conflating Decoding With Comprehension

Strong listening comprehension often masks behind weak decoding. Assess both separately to know what the student understands.

### Denying Assistive Technology As Cheating

Audiobooks and text-to-speech are access tools, not crutches. Provide them alongside, not instead of, decoding instruction.

### Forcing Oral Reading Of Unfamiliar Text

Cold reading aloud produces lasting shame. Use alternatives and never force a reluctant dyslexic student to read cold.

### Lowering Cognitive Demand Along With Reading Load

Reduce incidental decoding, not the thinking. The student still grapples with grade-level concepts and reasoning.

### Grading Spelling In Work Where It Is Incidental

Spelling is persistently hard for dyslexic students. Separate spelling from ideas unless spelling is the target.

### Using Timed Reading Pressure For Fluency

Timed pressure raises anxiety and undermines comprehension. Build fluency through supported repeated reading.

### Ignoring The Emotional Damage Of Unsupported Dyslexia

The shame narrative can outlast the reading deficit. Address it directly and frame accommodations as access.

### Improvising Instead Of Following The IEP

Coordinate with specialists and implement the plan exactly. Escalate concerns through proper channels.

## Self-Check

- Do you understand dyslexia as a phonological, language-based disability rather than a visual problem or low intelligence?
- Is decoding instruction explicit, systematic, sequential, and cumulative, coordinated with the reading specialist, rather than left to incidental exposure?
- Are decoding and comprehension assessed separately so that strong listening comprehension is not masked by weak decoding?
- Are audiobooks, text-to-speech, and dictation provided as access tools alongside, not instead of, direct decoding instruction?
- Is incidental reading load reduced without lowering the cognitive demand of grade-level tasks?
- Are dyslexic students protected from forced oral reading of unfamiliar text, with alternatives used for assessment and instruction?
- Is spelling taught explicitly and accommodated in production, with grading that separates spelling from ideas where spelling is incidental?
- Is fluency built through supported repeated reading rather than timed pressure?
- Is the emotional and motivational damage of unsupported dyslexia addressed through honest explanation, strengths, and access framing?
- Are interventions coordinated with specialists and the IEP or 504 plan implemented exactly, with documented fidelity and proper escalation?
