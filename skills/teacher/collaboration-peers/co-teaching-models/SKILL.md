# Co-Teaching Models

Co-teaching, two or more educators sharing responsibility for the same students, has enormous potential and a predictable failure mode. Done well, it gives students two skilled adults, allows for flexible grouping and targeted support, and lets teachers learn from each other in real time. Done badly, it becomes one teacher teaching while the other watches, a dynamic often called "one teach, one observe," which wastes a professional, signals to students that one adult is auxiliary, and breeds resentment that destroys the partnership. Co-teaching fails not because the teachers lack skill but because the relationship, the planning, and the deliberate choice of models are neglected.

This skill covers the judgment needed to co-teach effectively: choosing the right model for the purpose, planning together, dividing responsibility genuinely, communicating through conflict, and ensuring both teachers are visibly and meaningfully engaged. The goal is a co-taught classroom where students experience two full teachers and both educators grow.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Co-Teaching Model Deliberately For The Purpose

Co-teaching is not one model but a set of them, each suited to different purposes. In "one teach, one observe," one leads while the other gathers data on students; useful occasionally but corrosive as a default. In "station teaching," each teacher leads a station and students rotate; excellent for differentiated small-group work. In "parallel teaching," the class is split and both teachers teach the same content to half the group; strong for lowering student-to-teacher ratio. In "alternative teaching," one teacher works with a small pre-selected group for targeted support or enrichment while the other teaches the rest. In "team teaching," both teachers deliver instruction together, often taking turns within the same segment; demanding but powerful. Deliberately choose the model that fits the lesson's purpose, and vary models across a unit rather than defaulting to one.

### Never Default To One Teach, One Observe

The single most common co-teaching failure is the collapse into "one teach, one observe" as the default, where the general education teacher teaches and the specialist, often a special education or language specialist, watches or circulates. This model, used occasionally for specific data collection, is fine; used as the norm, it signals to students that one teacher is the real teacher and the other is an assistant, sidelines a professional, and forfeits the benefits of having two teachers. If you find yourself defaulting to this model, deliberately plan for models that engage both teachers in instruction.

### Plan Together, Not In Isolation

Co-teaching requires shared planning, which is the part most often skipped under time pressure. Without shared planning, the teachers arrive with different understandings of the lesson, the goals, and their roles, and the co-teaching collapses into one teacher improvising around the other's plan. Build protected planning time into the schedule, agree on the lesson's goals and structure together, and explicitly decide who does what in each segment. Planning together is what makes the in-class partnership possible; planning in isolation makes co-teaching performative.

### Divide Responsibility Genuinely, Not Symbolically

Both teachers should be visibly responsible for meaningful instruction, not for symbolic tasks. The division should reflect each teacher's expertise while ensuring both are engaged in teaching, not just present. A special education teacher who knows the students' needs should help shape how content is scaffolded and assessed, not merely circulate to support individual students. An English language specialist should shape how academic language is taught, not just translate for individuals. Genuine division uses both teachers' expertise to improve the lesson for all students, rather than using one teacher to patch up the other's lesson for a few.

### Communicate Roles To Students Explicitly

Students read the classroom dynamics quickly, and if they sense one teacher is auxiliary, they will route all questions and authority to the other. Explicitly communicate to students that both teachers are equally responsible for the class, both can be approached for help, and both are involved in assessment and decisions. Model this by referring to each other, handing off instruction visibly, and avoiding contradicting or undermining each other in front of students. The message that there are two teachers, not a teacher and a helper, must be deliberate and repeated.

### Address Conflict Between Co-Teachers Directly

Co-teaching creates friction: disagreements about pedagogy, about students, about division of labor, about whose approach prevails. Unaddressed friction accumulates into resentment that destroys the partnership. Address disagreements directly, in private, with a focus on the students' needs rather than on winning. Distinguish substantive disagreements, where the approach really matters, from preference differences, where either choice is fine, and invest your conflict energy in the former. Most co-teaching conflict is resolvable through honest conversation; what destroys partnerships is the conflict that is never named.

### Build On Each Other's Strengths In Front Of Students

Co-teaching is most powerful when each teacher's strengths are visible to students. If one teacher is exceptional at modeling a complex skill and the other at questioning that deepens thinking, design segments where each leads from their strength. This is not about ego; it is about giving students the best of both educators and modeling professional collaboration. Students who see two teachers genuinely building on each other learn something about collaboration itself, in addition to the content.

### Co-Assess And Co-Reflect

Assessment and reflection are part of co-teaching, not an afterthought. Grade and analyze student work together, discuss what the evidence shows about the instruction, and decide together what to change. Co-assessment prevents the dynamic where one teacher owns the academic outcomes and the other owns the "support," and it produces shared accountability for all students' learning. Co-reflection after a unit is where the partnership deepens and the next cycle improves.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To One Teach, One Observe

It sidelines a professional and signals one teacher is auxiliary. Vary the models deliberately.

### Planning In Isolation

Different understandings collapse the partnership. Build protected shared planning time.

### Symbolic Division Of Labor

Circulating to help individuals is not co-teaching. Divide genuine instructional responsibility.

### Letting Students Route All Authority To One Teacher

Explicitly communicate that both are teachers and model shared authority.

### Unaddressed Friction Accumulating Into Resentment

Name conflicts privately and focus on students' needs rather than winning.

### One Teacher Owning Academics, The Other Owning Support

Co-assess and co-reflect so both are accountable for all students' learning.

### Treating Co-Teaching As A Mandate Rather Than A Partnership

The relationship is the work. Invest in communication, planning, and mutual respect.

## Self-Check

- Do we deliberately choose the co-teaching model for each lesson's purpose?
- Do we avoid defaulting to one teach, one observe, and vary models across the unit?
- Do we plan together in protected time rather than in isolation?
- Is responsibility divided genuinely, using both teachers' expertise for all students?
- Have we explicitly communicated to students that both teachers are equally responsible?
- Do we address conflict directly and privately, focusing on students' needs?
- Do we build on each other's strengths visibly in front of students?
- Do we co-assess student work and co-reflect on the instruction together?
