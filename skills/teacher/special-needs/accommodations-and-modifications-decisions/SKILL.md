# Accommodations And Modifications Decisions

Selecting supports for a student is a decision with two opposite failure modes, and both are serious. The first failure is to remove a needed support, leaving a capable student unable to show what they know because a barrier stood in the way. The second failure is to quietly lower the learning expectation, dressing it up as help, so that a student is permanently excused from grade-level rigor and capped in their growth. The line between these two failures runs exactly through the distinction between an accommodation, which changes how a student accesses or shows learning without changing the expectation, and a modification, which changes what the student is expected to learn. Getting that line right, supportively and deliberately, is the whole judgment problem. It is not a paperwork detail; it determines what a student is entitled to, what they actually learn, and what their grades and credentials mean.

Use this skill before choosing or revising supports for a student, designing accessible tasks, selecting assistive technology, or deciding whether a change preserves or alters the learning target. The goal is to help the agent match supports to genuine barriers through a universal design lens, preserve the learning target whenever possible, authorize a modification only through the proper team process, and document the distinction clearly. Defer always to the student's plan, the IEP or 504 team, and school policy.

## Core Rules

### Define The Learning Target Before Choosing Any Support

You cannot decide whether a support preserves or changes the target until the target is explicit. The same support can be an accommodation for one objective and a modification for another.

Before selecting supports, state precisely:

- the standard or competency the task addresses;
- the essential understanding or skill that defines mastery;
- what evidence will demonstrate mastery;
- which features of the task are essential and which are flexible;
- whether the skill being assessed is the very thing the support would bypass.

The last point is the crucial test. If the objective is reading comprehension, reading the text aloud to the student may be an accommodation because it removes a decoding barrier to the comprehension target. If the objective is decoding itself, reading aloud is a modification because it removes the very skill being measured. The identical act changes meaning depending on the target. Never choose a support without first naming the target.

### Use Universal Design For Learning As The Default Frame

Before individualizing supports, remove unnecessary barriers for everyone. Universal design reduces the number of students who need individualized retrofitting and benefits many learners beyond those with identified disabilities.

Apply UDL across three principles:

- multiple means of representation, offering content through text, visuals, audio, demonstration, and interaction, so no student is shut out by a single format;
- multiple means of engagement, offering choice, relevance, varied challenge, and supports for sustained attention;
- multiple means of action and expression, allowing students to show learning through writing, speaking, building, recording, or performing when the objective allows.

Universal design separates the goal from the means of reaching it. It asks, for each barrier, whether the barrier is essential to the learning or merely incidental. Build out the incidental barriers for everyone, then reserve individualized supports for genuine, student-specific needs.

### Match Supports To Specific Barriers, Not To Labels

A support should answer a specific barrier a specific student faces. Supports chosen because a student "has a disability" or "is an English learner," without naming the barrier, tend to be either excessive or insufficient.

For each support, articulate the barrier and the match:

- the specific obstacle, such as decoding difficulty, slow processing, attention regulation, fine-motor challenge, language demand, sensory access;
- how the support removes or bypasses that obstacle;
- why the support does not remove the essential learning demand;
- how the student will still demonstrate the target skill.

If you cannot name the barrier, the support is not yet justified. If the support removes the target skill, it is a modification, not an accommodation, and must be handled accordingly.

### Distinguish Accommodation, Modification, And Scaffold Precisely

These three are often blurred, and the blur causes both legal and instructional harm.

- An accommodation changes how a student accesses content or demonstrates learning without changing the expectation. Examples include extended time, preferential seating, a quiet setting, read-aloud when decoding is not the target, a scribe when handwriting is not the target, enlarged print, captions, and breaks.
- A modification changes what the student is expected to learn or demonstrate. Examples include reducing grade-level content to a lower-level standard, substituting an easier text for a grade-level one when the grade-level complexity is the point, or excusing a student from a component of the standard.
- A scaffold is temporary support toward the same goal, designed to be faded as independence grows, such as sentence frames, graphic organizers, or worked examples.

The decisive question is whether the expectation has changed. If yes, it is a modification; if no, it is an accommodation or scaffold. Only the IEP team can authorize a modification that changes grade-level expectations. A teacher must not quietly convert an accommodation into a modification by lowering the goal.

### Preserve The Learning Target Whenever Possible

The default posture is to preserve the grade-level target and remove barriers to it, not to lower the target. Students are entitled to rigorous content with the supports that make it accessible, and permanently easier work caps growth and segregates students from the curriculum.

Preserve the target by:

- using accommodations and scaffolds that remove barriers while keeping the skill intact;
- teaching the prerequisite or strategy the student lacks so they can reach the target;
- fading temporary supports as competence grows;
- monitoring progress toward the grade-level standard, not only a lowered one;
- resisting the kindness trap of permanently easier work.

When a student consistently cannot reach the grade-level target despite strong, sustained, well-implemented supports, that is a signal for the team to consider a modification deliberately, through the proper process, with documentation.

### Authorize Modifications Only Through The Team Process

A modification is never a solo teacher decision. It changes what a student is learning, what their grades represent, and often their path to a standard diploma, so it carries weight that one person should not carry alone.

If you believe a modification is appropriate:

- bring data showing the student's response to high-quality accommodations and scaffolds over time;
- raise the concern with the case manager and request a team meeting;
- let the team, including the family, decide whether to modify and how;
- document the modified expectation, the rationale, and the data in the plan;
- implement the modification consistently and review it regularly.

Do not pre-empt this process by silently giving a student easier work and calling it an accommodation. That denies the family their role and the student a coherent plan.

### Select Assistive Technology By Function And Fit

Assistive technology ranges from low-tech pencil grips and slant boards to text-to-speech, speech-to-text, word prediction, communication devices, and screen readers. Selection should be functional and evidence-based, not driven by novelty or vendor claims.

When considering assistive technology:

- name the specific barrier the tool addresses;
- confirm the tool does not bypass the learning target;
- check that the student can use the tool independently or can be taught to;
- verify the tool is available, working, and consistent across settings;
- ensure staff are trained to support it;
- monitor whether the tool actually improves access and learning;
- coordinate with the assistive technology specialist when available.

A tool that the student cannot operate, that breaks constantly, or that staff cannot support will not be used and becomes a barrier itself. Trial the tool, gather data, and keep what works.

### Document The Distinction Clearly

Because accommodations and modifications carry different legal and instructional meanings, documentation must make the distinction explicit. Vague notes invite drift and disputes.

For each support, record:

- whether it is an accommodation, modification, or scaffold;
- the specific barrier it addresses;
- the learning target it preserves or, for a modification, the changed expectation;
- who provides it and how often;
- how fidelity and effectiveness will be monitored.

Documentation protects the student's access, clarifies the team's intent, and gives future teachers an accurate picture.

### Coordinate With Required Plans And Specialists

Support decisions must align with the student's IEP, 504 plan, English learner plan, and behavior plan, and with the specialists who serve the student. Do not add, remove, or change supports unilaterally if they are governed by a plan. Bring proposed changes to the team and follow the plan as written in the meantime.

## Common Traps

### Modifying While Calling It Accommodating

Quietly lowering the expectation without team authorization is an unauthorized modification. Name it honestly and route it through the team.

### Removing A Support That Removes A Barrier

Withholding a needed accommodation, such as read-aloud or extended time, leaves a capable student unable to show learning. Match supports to real barriers.

### Choosing Supports By Label, Not By Barrier

Supports chosen because of a disability or language label, without naming the specific obstacle, tend to miss the mark. Articulate the barrier.

### Bypassing The Target Skill By Accident

A support that is an accommodation for one objective can be a modification for another. Check the target every time.

### Over-Scaffolding That Never Fades

Permanent crutches prevent independence. Plan from the start how scaffolds will be removed as competence grows.

### The Kindness Trap Of Permanently Easier Work

Lowering expectations in the name of help caps growth and segregates students from grade-level rigor. Preserve the target and remove barriers instead.

### Assistive Technology Without Fit Or Training and ignoring Universal Design

A tool the student cannot use or staff cannot support becomes its own barrier. Trial, train, and monitor.

Jumping straight to individualized supports, when many barriers could be removed for everyone, multiplies your workload and stigmatizes some students. Build access in first.

### Undocumented Or Vague Supports and acting Alone On A Plan-Governed Support

If the record does not clearly state whether a support is an accommodation or modification, drift and disputes follow. Document the distinction explicitly.

Adding, removing, or changing supports that are governed by an IEP or 504 without the team is not yours to decide. Follow the plan and bring changes through the team.

## Self-Check

- Is the learning target stated precisely before any support is chosen, with the essential skill and evidence of mastery identified?
- Is the decisive test applied, whether the proposed support bypasses the very skill being measured, making it a modification rather than an accommodation?
- Is universal design for learning used first to remove incidental barriers for everyone, before layering individualized supports?
- Does each support match a specific, named barrier rather than a label, and does it preserve the essential learning demand?
- Are accommodation, modification, and scaffold distinguished correctly, with the change in expectation as the deciding line?
- Is the grade-level target preserved whenever possible, with barriers removed rather than the goal lowered?
- Are any modifications authorized only through the IEP or 504 team, with data, rationale, and documentation, never unilaterally?
- Is assistive technology selected by function and fit, trialed, trained, and monitored for actual effect on access and learning?
- Does documentation clearly state, for each support, whether it is an accommodation, modification, or scaffold, the barrier it addresses, and the target it preserves or changes?
- Do all support decisions align with required plans and specialist guidance, with changes routed through the team rather than made alone?
