# Collaboration With Special Education

Supporting a student with a disability is never one person's job, and the structure meant to share that job, the partnership between general and special education, is where support most often silently fails. The general educator assumes the special educator owns the student; the special educator assumes the general educator will implement accommodations; the paraprofessional hovers without a defined instructional role; the family hears from no one until there is a problem; and the student falls through the gap between adults who each thought someone else was responsible. The central judgment problem in this work is not whether to collaborate, but how to make collaboration concrete: who does what, when, with what information, under what boundaries, and with what shared accountability. Vague goodwill is not a plan. Shared ownership requires explicit roles, scheduled communication, respect for expertise on every side, and clear lines of authority over the student's legally binding plan.

Use this skill before co-planning or co-teaching with a special educator, assigning paraprofessional roles, preparing for an IEP meeting, communicating with a family, or navigating a disagreement about a student's supports. The goal is to help the agent build genuine shared ownership while respecting role boundaries, confidentiality, family voice, and the legal authority of the IEP team. Defer to the case manager, the plan, and school policy whenever authority is unclear.

## Core Rules

### Treat Shared Ownership As A Structure, Not A Feeling

The phrase "shared ownership" is meaningless without structure. Real collaboration is built from explicit agreements about who does what, and it decays without them.

Make shared ownership concrete by:

- naming the case manager or primary special education contact for each student;
- agreeing on who plans, who delivers, who assesses, and who documents each component;
- scheduling regular co-planning time and protecting it;
- deciding how decisions get made and who has final say on plan-governed matters;
- establishing how and when you will communicate about the student;
- clarifying what each adult will do when the other is absent.

Without these structures, collaboration collapses into assumptions, and assumptions produce gaps. Write the agreements down when the stakes are high.

### Choose A Co-Teaching Model Deliberately

Co-teaching is not one thing. It is a family of arrangements, each with different demands, benefits, and failure modes, and the choice should fit the lesson, the students, and the adults' expertise.

Common models include:

- one teach, one observe, where one leads while the other gathers data on specific students;
- station teaching, where students rotate and each teacher runs a station;
- parallel teaching, where the class splits and each teacher teaches the same content to half;
- alternative teaching, where one teacher works with a small pre-teach or reteach group while the other leads the rest;
- teaming, where both teachers share instruction dynamically in front of the whole group.

Each model has tradeoffs. Station and parallel teaching multiply adult attention but require strong routines; alternative teaching targets needs but risks stigmatizing the small group; teaming is powerful but demands trust and planning time. Choose the model that serves the learning target and the students, not the model that is easiest, and never let the special educator default to only working with the students with IEPs, which recreates segregation inside an inclusive classroom.

### Co-Plan, Do Not Just Co-Teach

Co-teaching without co-planning produces two adults running two separate lessons in the same room. The single highest-leverage practice is shared planning time before instruction.

In co-planning, agree on:

- the learning target and how all students will reach it;
- which accommodations and modifications apply and who provides them;
- the instructional model for each segment of the lesson;
- how each teacher will contribute their expertise;
- how grouping, materials, and access supports will be handled;
- how understanding will be checked and by whom;
- what each teacher will do for students who need more support or more challenge.

If co-planning time is not formally provided, advocate for it. Without it, co-teaching degrades into the general educator leading while the special educator assists, which wastes expertise and shortchanges students.

### Define The Paraprofessional's Instructional Role

Paraprofessionals are often the least clearly defined adults in the room, and unclear roles waste their skill and can even hinder student independence. A paraprofessional is not a personal attendant glued to one student, and over-support can prevent the student from developing independence.

Define the role by:

- clarifying whether the paraprofessional supports one student, several students, or the class;
- distinguishing instructional support from personal care and behavior support;
- specifying what the paraprofessional may and may not do, such as implementing a behavior plan versus modifying instruction;
- planning how the paraprofessional will fade proximity and prompts to build student independence;
- ensuring the paraprofessional is included in planning and communication;
- directing their work as the teacher, while respecting their expertise and input.

Coordinate with the special educator on the paraprofessional's assignment, since it is often tied to a student's IEP services, but remember that classroom instruction is led by the certified teacher.

### Participate As A Required IEP Team Member

The general education teacher is a legally required member of the IEP team and holds information no one else has: how the student performs under grade-level expectations, with peers, in the mainstream setting, across a full day.

Prepare for and contribute to meetings by:

- bringing current data, work samples, and specific observations;
- reporting on what the student can do, not only deficits;
- describing how current accommodations are working and where they fall short;
- raising concerns about access, behavior, or progress through the meeting;
- contributing to goals that are meaningful in the general education setting;
- implementing the team's decisions consistently afterward;
- following up in writing when you need clarity or a change.

Do not treat the meeting as the special educator's show. Your presence and input are required by law because your perspective is irreplaceable.

### Communicate With Families With Respect And Regularity

Families are core members of the team and hold legal and moral authority in their child's education. Silence until there is a problem erodes trust; reactive, deficit-only communication damages the partnership.

Communicate well by:

- establishing positive contact early, before any concern;
- sharing successes and progress, not only problems;
- being honest and specific while preserving the student's dignity;
- listening to the family's knowledge of their child;
- following the school's rules about who communicates what, since some matters belong to the case manager;
- respecting confidentiality in every channel;
- documenting significant communications;
- bringing concerns to the team rather than acting unilaterally.

Families of students with disabilities often navigate a stressful, bureaucratic system. Consistency, honesty, and respect from you materially affect their child's outcomes.

### Respect Role Boundaries And Lines Of Authority

Collaboration thrives when each adult respects what the others bring and stays within their authority. Confusion about authority is especially dangerous because the IEP is a legal document.

Respect boundaries by:

- recognizing that only the IEP team can change goals, services, accommodations, or placement;
- letting the case manager lead on plan-governed decisions;
- letting related service providers, speech, occupational, physical therapy, behavioral, own their clinical expertise;
- letting the general educator lead on grade-level curriculum and classroom instruction;
- raising disagreements through the team process rather than overriding each other;
- not asking paraprofessionals to perform work outside their role and training;
- not making promises to families that exceed your authority.

When authority is unclear, default to the plan and the case manager, and document the question.

### Share Information Under Confidentiality

Collaboration requires sharing information, but disability and plan information is confidential under FERPA and district policy. Balance the need to share with the duty to protect.

Share information by:

- limiting disclosure to staff with a legitimate educational interest;
- using secure channels, not open hallways or uncontrolled group chats;
- never disclosing a student's disability, plan, or services to peers or other families;
- briefing substitutes, paraprofessionals, and co-teachers on a need-to-know basis;
- storing plan documents and notes securely.

### Navigate Disagreements Through The Team Process

Adults will disagree about supports, placement, behavior, and expectations. The healthy path is not to avoid disagreement but to route it through the team and the data.

When disagreement arises:

- return to the student's data and the plan;
- separate the issue from the person;
- raise the concern with the case manager first;
- request a team meeting for substantive disputes;
- document the concern and the responses;
- follow the district's process for formal resolution if needed;
- keep the student's interests at the center, not adult convenience or ego.

Do not let a disagreement drift into silent non-implementation of a plan. Follow the plan while you resolve the dispute.

## Common Traps

### Each Adult Assuming The Other Owns The Student

When general and special educators each think the other is responsible, the student falls through the gap. Make ownership explicit.

### Co-Teaching Without Co-Planning

Two adults running two lessons in one room wastes expertise. Plan together before you teach together.

### The Special Educator Working Only With IEP Students

Defaulting the special educator to only the labeled students recreates segregation inside inclusion. Use models where expertise reaches all learners.

### The Paraprofessional As Permanent Shadow

Gluing a paraprofessional to one student can stigmatize and prevent independence. Define an instructional role and plan for fading.

### Silence With Families Until There Is A Problem

Reactive, deficit-only communication erodes trust. Establish positive, regular contact.

### Overstepping Or Understepping Authority

Changing plan-governed supports unilaterally, or abdicating your own role, both harm the student. Respect the lines and the team process.

### Breaching Confidentiality In The Name Of Coordination

Sharing disability information beyond need-to-know violates FERPA. Use secure channels and limit disclosure.

### Letting Disagreements Drift Into Non-Implementation and treating The IEP Meeting As Someone Else's Meeting

Silently not following a plan while a dispute festers denies the student their rights. Follow the plan and resolve disputes through the team.

The general educator's input is legally required and practically essential. Prepare, attend, and contribute.

## Self-Check

- Is shared ownership made concrete through named roles, scheduled co-planning, and clear communication channels, rather than left as goodwill?
- Is the co-teaching model chosen deliberately to fit the lesson and students, avoiding defaulting the special educator to only IEP students?
- Is there protected co-planning time in which the target, accommodations, model, grouping, and assessment are agreed before instruction?
- Is the paraprofessional's role defined instructionally, with attention to fading support and building student independence?
- Are you participating as a required IEP team member, bringing data, observations, and a strengths-based perspective?
- Is family communication positive, regular, honest, dignified, and aligned with school rules about who communicates what?
- Are role boundaries and lines of authority respected, with plan-governed decisions left to the team and the case manager?
- Is disability and plan information shared only with staff who have a legitimate educational interest, through secure channels?
- Are disagreements routed through the team and the data, with the plan followed while disputes are resolved?
- Have you avoided both overstepping and abdicating your role, deferring to the plan, the case manager, and school policy when authority is unclear?
