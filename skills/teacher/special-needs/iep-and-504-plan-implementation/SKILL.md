# IEP And 504 Plan Implementation

An IEP and a 504 plan are not suggestions, preferences, or helpful hints. They are legally binding documents that operationalize a student's civil right to a free appropriate public education, and the general education teacher is legally and ethically responsible for implementing them in the mainstream setting where most of these students spend most of their day. The most common failure is not hostility; it is passive drift. A teacher means to provide the read-aloud, forgets on a busy day, assumes the special educator is handling it, or quietly skips an accommodation because it is inconvenient, and over weeks the student is quietly denied what they are owed. The second failure is confusion: many teachers cannot clearly distinguish an IEP from a 504 plan, do not know which document governs which student, and do not know what to do when a plan seems to conflict with how the class normally runs. This skill provides conservative, procedural judgment for an area where mistakes carry legal, academic, and human consequences.

Use this skill before planning instruction, materials, grouping, behavior support, or assessment for any student with an IEP or 504 plan. The goal is to help the agent implement required supports faithfully, document them, protect confidentiality, and escalate concerns through proper channels rather than improvising or silently overriding a plan. When in doubt, defer to the plan, the case manager, and school policy.

## Core Rules

### Know The Legal Difference Between An IEP And A 504 Plan

These are distinct instruments with different origins, scope, and weight, and confusing them leads to wrong implementation.

An IEP, created under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, is for a student who, because of one of the recognized disability categories, needs specialized instruction and related services to access and progress in the general curriculum. It is a detailed, team-developed document with annual goals, services, accommodations, modifications, placement in the least restrictive environment, and transition planning. It carries the strongest procedural protections.

A 504 plan, created under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, is for a student with a disability that substantially limits a major life activity and who needs accommodations or services to access school, but who may not need specialized instruction. It is typically a shorter list of accommodations and services without annual goals. It carries serious legal weight but fewer procedural steps than an IEP.

The practical consequence: both are mandatory, both must be implemented as written, but an IEP implies specialized instruction and goals that the special educator leads, while a 504 plan is usually implemented by the general education teacher through accommodations. Know which document you are holding for each student.

### Read The Actual Plan Before Teaching The Student

Implementation begins with reading the student's actual, current plan, not your memory of last year's version or a colleague's summary. Before or at the start of the year, obtain and read each plan for the students on your roster.

From each plan, identify:

- the present levels of performance and baselines;
- the annual goals and any short-term objectives;
- every instructional accommodation the student receives;
- every assessment or testing accommodation, including state and district provisions;
- any modifications to content or expectations;
- related services, who provides them, and when;
- the behavior intervention plan if one exists;
- the placement and least restrictive environment details;
- the people responsible for each service and the case manager;
- the next review or annual meeting date.

If a plan is unclear, missing, or appears outdated, contact the case manager immediately. Do not guess and do not wait.

### Implement Accommodations Proactively And Consistently

An accommodation works only if it is actually used, every day, in every relevant setting, not only on test day. Reactive or inconsistent implementation denies the student their right and sets them up to fail.

Implement proactively by:

- building each accommodation into lesson plans and materials from the start;
- preparing accessible versions of texts, slides, and handouts in advance;
- arranging seating, grouping, and environment to match the plan;
- setting reminders for supports that are easy to forget, such as breaks or read-aloud;
- coordinating with co-teachers, substitutes, and paraprofessionals so supports continue when you are absent;
- not waiting for the student to request the support, since many will not ask, especially if asking marks them as different.

If an accommodation is genuinely impractical in a specific lesson, that is a problem to solve with the case manager, not a reason to silently omit it.

### Document Fidelity

In this domain, if it is not documented, it did not happen. Documentation protects the student, protects you, and gives the team the data it needs to make good decisions.

Document by:

- keeping a simple log of when and how accommodations were provided;
- noting any instance where a support could not be provided and why;
- collecting work samples that show the student's response to supports;
- recording observations relevant to the student's goals and needs;
- bringing this documentation to IEP or 504 meetings and progress updates.

Documentation should be factual and professional. Record what happened and what you observed, not judgments about the family or labels about the student.

### Handle Conflicts With Class Routine Through Proper Channels

Sometimes a plan's requirements seem to conflict with how you normally run class: a testing accommodation that disrupts your schedule, a behavior plan that limits a consequence you usually use, a service that pulls the student during your instruction. The correct response is never to override the plan silently.

Instead:

- follow the plan as written while you raise the concern;
- bring the conflict to the case manager or 504 coordinator promptly;
- request a team meeting if the conflict is ongoing or significant;
- propose alternatives through the team process, not unilateral changes;
- remember that only the team can change an IEP, and 504 changes follow the school's process;
- never let convenience for the class override a student's legal right.

A plan is the product of a team including the family. One teacher cannot unilaterally revise it.

### Protect Confidentiality Under FERPA And District Policy

Disability, plan status, accommodations, and related services are confidential education records. Mishandling them violates federal law and harms students.

Protect confidentiality by:

- storing plan documents securely, not in open view or shared drives without controls;
- sharing information only with staff who have a legitimate educational interest;
- never disclosing a student's disability, plan, or accommodations to peers or other families;
- implementing supports discreetly so the student is not visibly singled out where avoidable;
- avoiding casual discussion of students' needs in hallways, staff rooms, or unsecured channels;
- following your district's rules for substitutes, volunteers, and paraprofessionals.

A student's dignity is part of their education. Public identification as "the IEP kid" is itself a harm.

### Distinguish Accommodations, Modifications, And Services

These are not interchangeable, and confusing them changes what the student is entitled to and what they are learning.

- An accommodation changes how a student accesses or shows learning without changing the expectation.
- A modification changes what the student is expected to learn, and only the IEP team can authorize it.
- A related service, such as speech or occupational therapy, is delivered by a qualified provider, usually outside your room.

Implement exactly what the plan specifies. Do not quietly turn an accommodation into a modification by lowering the goal, and do not withhold a service because it is inconvenient.

### Participate As A Core IEP Or 504 Team Member

The general education teacher is a required and essential member of the IEP team. You hold unique information about how the student performs in the mainstream setting, with peers, under grade-level expectations.

Contribute by:

- bringing current data, work samples, and observations to meetings;
- reporting on how accommodations are working and where they are not;
- sharing what the student can do, not only deficits;
- raising concerns about access, behavior, or progress through the meeting;
- implementing the team's decisions consistently afterward;
- following up in writing when you need clarity or a change.

Do not treat the meeting as something the special educator runs without you. Your voice is legally required and practically essential.

### Know The Escalation Path When Something Is Wrong

If you believe a plan is not working, a service is not being delivered, or a student is being denied supports, follow the chain rather than acting alone.

Typical escalation:

- raise the concern with the case manager or 504 coordinator;
- if unresolved, bring it to the special education lead or administrator;
- request a team meeting to review or amend the plan;
- document the concern and the responses;
- follow district policy for formal disputes or compliance complaints.

Do not let a problem drift, and do not solve a legal problem with an informal workaround.

## Common Traps

### Treating Accommodations As Optional Or Convenient

Required supports are not suggestions. Omitting them because they are inconvenient denies the student their right and may violate law.

### Confusing IEP And 504 Plans

The two have different scope and weight. Know which document governs each student and what each requires of you.

### Implementing From Memory Or Summary

Plans change. Read the current actual document each year and whenever updated.

### Inconsistent, Test-Day-Only Implementation

Supports used only sometimes or only on tests fail the student. Build them in proactively and consistently.

### Silently Overriding A Plan That Conflicts With Routine

When a plan disrupts your normal practice, raise it through the team; do not quietly set it aside.

### Modifying Without Team Authorization

Quietly lowering expectations is an unauthorized modification. Only the team can change what a student is expected to learn.

### Breaching Confidentiality and assuming The Specialist Handles Everything

Disclosing disability or plan status to peers or in unsecured channels violates FERPA and harms the student.

The general education teacher implements supports in the mainstream setting. Do not abdicate your role.

### Drift Without Documentation and letting Concerns Drift Instead Of Escalating

Undocumented implementation cannot be proven or improved. Keep factual records of what was provided and observed.

Unresolved problems compound. Use the team and escalation process promptly.

## Self-Check

- Can you clearly distinguish an IEP from a 504 plan, and do you know which document governs each student on your roster?
- Have you read the current, actual plan for each student, including goals, accommodations, modifications, services, and responsible parties?
- Are accommodations built into instruction and materials proactively and consistently, not only on assessment day?
- Are you keeping factual documentation of when and how supports were provided, including any gaps and the reasons?
- When a plan conflicts with class routine, are you following the plan while raising the concern through the case manager and team rather than overriding it?
- Are disability, plan, and accommodation information kept confidential under FERPA and district policy, and shared only with staff who have a legitimate educational interest?
- Are accommodations, modifications, and related services implemented exactly as written, with no unauthorized changes to expectations?
- Are you participating as a required team member, bringing data and observations to IEP or 504 meetings and implementing decisions afterward?
- Do you know and use the escalation path when a plan is not working or a service is not delivered?
- Have you avoided informal workarounds for legal and procedural problems, deferring instead to the plan, the case manager, and school policy?
