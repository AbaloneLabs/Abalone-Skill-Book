# Co Teaching And Inclusive Practice

Inclusive education places students with disabilities and diverse needs in general education settings alongside their peers, supported by the collaboration of general and special educators. Done well, inclusion benefits all students, building community, raising expectations, and providing access to rich curriculum. Done poorly, it places students in settings where their needs are not met, where co-teachers work in parallel rather than together, and where inclusion becomes physical placement without instructional substance. The judgment problem is that inclusion is a pedagogical practice, not a placement decision, and it succeeds only when co-teachers deliberately design instruction for the range of learners in the room. The most common failure is one-teach-one-assist, where the special educator is relegated to a support role and the instruction is not genuinely co-designed. The skill is structuring co-teaching so that both educators contribute substantively, designing instruction for diverse learners, and making inclusion work for every student in the room.

Use this skill before co-teaching in an inclusive classroom, before planning collaboration between general and special educators, before choosing a co-teaching model, or before diagnosing why inclusion is not working. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating inclusion as mere placement, from relegating one teacher to a subordinate role, or from designing instruction for the mythical average student rather than for the actual range.

## Core Rules

### Treat Inclusion As Pedagogy, Not Placement

Inclusion is not achieved by placing students with disabilities in a general education room; it is achieved by designing instruction so that those students can genuinely learn and participate. Placement without pedagogical design produces physical inclusion without educational inclusion. The pedagogy must match the placement.

Treat as pedagogy by:

- designing instruction for the range of learners actually in the room;
- embedding accommodations and supports into the core instruction;
- ensuring all students have meaningful access to the curriculum;
- measuring whether included students are learning, not just present;
- rejecting the idea that placement alone constitutes inclusion.

### Co-Plan Substantively And Regularly

Co-teaching fails without co-planning. Two teachers cannot coordinate instruction if they do not plan it together, and without shared planning time, the special educator is reduced to reacting to the general educator's plan. Substantive, regular co-planning is the foundation of effective co-teaching.

Co-plan by:

- securing dedicated, regular co-planning time;
- planning instruction together, not sequentially or separately;
- both teachers contributing expertise to the design;
- planning for the range of learners, not just the majority;
- documenting plans so both teachers share ownership.

### Choose Co-Teaching Models Deliberately

Co-teaching is not one model; it is several, including one-teach-one-assist, station teaching, parallel teaching, alternative teaching, and team teaching. Each has strengths and limits, and the choice should match the lesson's goals and the students' needs. Defaulting to one-teach-one-assist wastes the second teacher.

Choose deliberately by:

- matching the model to the lesson's goals and structure;
- varying models across lessons rather than defaulting to one;
- using station and parallel teaching to reduce student-teacher ratio;
- using alternative teaching to provide pre-teaching or re-teaching;
- reserving team teaching for lessons that benefit from two instructors.

### Avoid The One-Teach-One-Assist Default

One-teach-one-assist, where one teacher leads and the other circulates, is the easiest model and the most overused. It relegates the special educator to a support role, fails to use both teachers' expertise, and does not change the fundamental structure of instruction. It should be one option among many, not the default.

Avoid the default by:

- recognizing one-teach-one-assist as a limited, not primary, model;
- using models where both teachers instruct substantively;
- rotating the lead role between teachers;
- ensuring the special educator teaches content, not just supports;
- designing lessons that genuinely require two teachers.

### Design Instruction For The Range Of Learners

An inclusive classroom contains a wide range of learners, and instruction designed for the average leaves out both ends. Effective inclusive instruction uses universal design, differentiation, and flexible grouping to reach the full range, ensuring that students with disabilities are not an afterthought.

Design for the range by:

- using universal design to build in access from the start;
- differentiating process, product, and content as needed;
- using flexible grouping to provide targeted instruction;
- embedding accommodations into core instruction, not as add-ons;
- planning extensions for advanced learners and supports for struggling ones.

### Share Ownership Of All Students

In effective co-teaching, both teachers share ownership of all students, not just the special educator owning the students with disabilities. When ownership is divided, students sense it and the class fragments. Shared ownership signals that all students belong to both teachers.

Share ownership by:

- both teachers taking responsibility for all students' learning;
- avoiding the division where one teacher owns "regular" and one "special" students;
- both teachers grading, communicating with families, and leading;
- presenting as equal partners to the class and families;
- treating the class as one community, not two populations.

### Define And Respect Roles Without Hierarchy

Co-teaching requires clear roles, but roles must not become hierarchical, with the general educator as the real teacher and the special educator as the assistant. Both teachers bring essential expertise, and roles should reflect complementary contributions, not rank.

Define roles by:

- clarifying each teacher's contributions to planning and instruction;
- valuing content expertise and learning-expertise equally;
- rotating roles so neither teacher is permanently subordinate;
- addressing hierarchy and status issues openly;
- presenting both teachers as equals to students and families.

### Use Data To Guide Instruction For All Learners

Inclusive instruction must be informed by data on how all students, especially those with disabilities, are progressing. Without data, inclusion proceeds on assumption and students who are not learning go unnoticed until they have fallen far behind.

Use data by:

- tracking progress for all students, with attention to included students;
- using data to form groups and target instruction;
- sharing data between co-teachers to coordinate support;
- adjusting instruction based on what the data shows;
- catching and addressing learning gaps early.

### Build A Culture Of Belonging For All Students

Inclusion is not only about instruction; it is about community. Students with disabilities must be genuinely included in the social and academic life of the class, not just physically present. A culture of belonging benefits all students and is the foundation of inclusive practice.

Build belonging by:

- establishing norms of respect and inclusion;
- structuring collaborative work that includes all students;
- avoiding practices that single out or stigmatize students with disabilities;
- teaching all students about difference and inclusion;
- ensuring students with disabilities have friends and roles in the class.

### Support Co-Teaching Through Systems

Co-teaching succeeds or fails partly through systems: scheduling, planning time, caseloads, and administrative support. Without systemic support, co-teachers are set up to fail. Advocating for and building supportive systems is part of inclusive practice.

Support through systems by:

- securing protected co-planning time in the schedule;
- keeping caseloads and class sizes manageable;
- ensuring administrative understanding and support for co-teaching;
- providing professional development on co-teaching;
- addressing systemic barriers rather than blaming teachers.

## Common Traps

### Placement As Inclusion

Placing students in the room without designing instruction for them.

### No Co-Planning

Teachers planning separately or sequentially, preventing coordination.

### One-Teach-One-Assist Default

Relegating the special educator to a support role in every lesson.

### Instruction For The Average

Designing for the mythical average student and leaving out the range.

### Divided Ownership

One teacher owning the regular students and one the special students.

### Role Hierarchy

Treating the general educator as the real teacher and the special educator as assistant.

### Assumption-Based Inclusion

Proceeding without data on whether included students are learning.

### Social Isolation

Physical inclusion without genuine social belonging.

## Self-Check

- Is inclusion treated as pedagogy designed for the range of learners, not just as placement?
- Do co-teachers co-plan substantively and regularly, with shared ownership of plans?
- Are co-teaching models chosen deliberately to match lesson goals, not defaulted?
- Is the one-teach-one-assist model used sparingly rather than as the constant default?
- Is instruction designed for the full range through universal design and differentiation?
- Do both teachers share ownership of all students, not divided by disability status?
- Are roles defined clearly without hierarchy, valuing both teachers' expertise?
- Is data used to guide instruction and catch gaps for all learners, especially included students?
- Is a culture of belonging built so all students are genuinely included socially and academically?
- Are systemic supports, planning time, caseloads, and development, in place to sustain co-teaching?
