# Reading Comprehension Instruction

Reading comprehension is the construction of meaning from text. It is not a single transferable skill exercised equally on any passage; it is the product of two things working together: the reader's ability to decode the words fluently and the reader's knowledge of the topic, the vocabulary, and how the kind of text being read works. A student who decodes well but knows nothing about photosynthesis will comprehend a passage about photosynthesis poorly, regardless of how many comprehension strategies they have practiced. This is the central insight that casual comprehension instruction misses: comprehension is knowledge-bound, and teaching a battery of generic strategies while neglecting knowledge building produces students who can execute strategies on simple text but stall on anything demanding.

The recurring failure in comprehension planning is treating comprehension as a skill to be drilled with worksheets and strategy lessons on random passages, rather than as understanding that emerges from decoding fluency, rich knowledge, vocabulary, and active engagement with coherent text. This skill applies whenever the work is to design, sequence, or evaluate comprehension instruction.

## Core Rules

### Establish Decoding Fluency As The Prerequisite

Comprehension cannot occur if decoding is effortful and slow, because the cognitive resources consumed by sounding out words are no longer available for constructing meaning. A student who labors over every word loses the thread of the sentence before reaching its end. Before assigning comprehension work, confirm that students can decode the text at a reasonable rate; if they cannot, the need is fluency and word recognition, not more comprehension strategy.

For students whose decoding is weak, comprehension instruction on grade-level text is built on sand. Address the decoding gap first or provide accessible versions so comprehension itself can be taught.

### Build Background Knowledge Deliberately And Coherently

Because comprehension is knowledge-bound, the most powerful long-term comprehension instruction is the deliberate building of knowledge about the world. A curriculum that jumps from random topic to random topic, each passage unrelated to the last, denies students the accumulating knowledge that makes subsequent passages comprehensible. Coherent, knowledge-building units, in which texts within a topic reinforce and extend each other, compound comprehension over time.

Before students read a demanding text, assess and activate the prior knowledge the text assumes. If the text assumes knowledge students do not have, teach that knowledge first, with images, discussion, a simpler text, or direct explanation. Comprehension instruction that skips this step and then asks comprehension questions is really testing prior knowledge and calling it reading skill.

### Teach Vocabulary That The Text Assumes

Vocabulary is the sharpest edge of background knowledge. Words a student does not know become holes in comprehension, and a single unknown key word can collapse understanding of a sentence or paragraph. Identify the words critical to the meaning of a text before students read it, and teach those that are both unknown and essential.

Distinguish three tiers: everyday words students likely know, general academic words that appear across domains, and domain-specific terms. Teach the general academic and domain-specific words the text depends on, with student-friendly definitions, examples, and opportunities to use the words. Do not pre-teach every unfamiliar word; teach the load-bearing ones and let context support the rest.

### Teach A Small Set Of Strategies, Explicitly And Then Independently

A handful of comprehension strategies, taught explicitly and practiced until students use them independently, reliably improve comprehension. These include: monitoring understanding and repairing when it breaks down, generating questions about the text, summarizing, making inferences from evidence, and visualizing. The evidence supports teaching these strategies well, not teaching many strategies superficially.

Teach each strategy with explicit explanation and modeling, then guided practice, then independent use, fading teacher support until the strategy becomes a habit the student deploys silently while reading. The goal is not students who can name the strategies on a worksheet; it is students who actually stop, reread, and question while reading difficult text.

The most important strategy is monitoring: recognizing when understanding has broken down and taking action, rereading, slowing down, looking back, or seeking clarification. Students who do not monitor will read past their own confusion and answer questions from miscomprehension.

### Use Text-Dependent Questions That Require Returning To The Text

Good comprehension questions force students back into the text rather than allowing them to answer from memory, opinion, or vague impression. Text-dependent questions ask what the text says, how it says it, and why it matters, requiring students to locate evidence, interpret language, and connect parts.

Avoid questions answerable without reading the text, questions that invite personal opinion ungrounded in the passage, and questions so literal they require only recalling a single sentence. A mix of literal, inferential, and evaluative questions, all anchored in textual evidence, builds real comprehension.

### Distinguish Narrative And Informational Comprehension

Narrative and informational text make different demands. Narrative comprehension relies on story structure, character, plot, setting, and theme; teaching students to track story elements and notice how narratives are built supports fiction comprehension. Informational comprehension relies on text features, main idea and supporting detail, cause and effect, comparison, and the structure of argument; teaching students to recognize how informational text is organized helps them navigate it.

Teach the structures specific to each kind of text rather than applying a single generic comprehension approach to everything.

### Make Thinking Visible Through Discussion And Writing

Comprehension is strengthened when students must articulate their understanding, defend it with evidence, and revise it in response to others. Discussion structured around the text, and writing that requires students to organize and express their understanding, reveals and deepens comprehension in ways that multiple-choice questions cannot.

Require students to cite specific textual evidence for their claims. The habit of grounding statements in the text is the core of analytical reading.

### Teach Inference As Reasoning From Evidence, Not Guessing

Inference is the construction of meaning the text implies but does not state explicitly, built by connecting textual evidence with background knowledge. Teach inference as a reasoning process: what does the text say, what do I know, what follows from both. Students who treat inference as guessing produce unsupported interpretations; students who ground inferences in evidence produce defensible ones.

## Common Traps

### Strategy Worksheets On Random Passages

Practicing strategies on disconnected passages while neglecting knowledge building produces students who execute strategies on easy text but cannot comprehend demanding text. Build knowledge coherently.

### Ignoring The Decoding Fluency Prerequisite

Comprehension instruction for students who cannot decode fluently is built on sand. Address decoding or provide accessible text.

### Testing Prior Knowledge And Calling It Comprehension

Asking comprehension questions about a text whose topic students know nothing about really tests what they already knew. Teach the assumed knowledge first.

### Pre-Teaching Every Unknown Word

Pre-teaching the entire vocabulary list exhausts students before reading and steals the chance to use context. Teach only the load-bearing words.

### Teaching Many Strategies Superficially

A long menu of strategies lightly touched produces no habit. Teach a few strategies deeply until students use them independently.

### Questions Answerable Without The Text

If students can answer without reading, the question tests nothing about comprehension. Make questions text-dependent.

### Treating Inference As Opinion

Inference without evidence is guessing. Require textual evidence for every inferential claim.

### Personal Opinion Substituting For Textual Analysis

"What do you think?" questions ungrounded in the passage invite students to talk about themselves rather than the text. Anchor responses in textual evidence.

## Self-Check

- Is decoding fluency confirmed or addressed before comprehension instruction is expected on a given text?
- Is background knowledge built coherently across the curriculum and activated before demanding texts?
- Are the load-bearing vocabulary words identified and taught before reading, rather than every word or none?
- Is a small set of comprehension strategies taught explicitly and faded to independent use, including monitoring of understanding?
- Are questions text-dependent, requiring students to return to the passage and cite evidence, with a mix of literal, inferential, and evaluative levels?
- Are narrative and informational text structures taught distinctly rather than with a single generic approach?
- Do students articulate and defend understanding through discussion and writing grounded in textual evidence?
- Is inference taught as reasoning from textual evidence plus knowledge, not as unsupported guessing?
- Is comprehension instruction building knowledge over time rather than drilling strategies on disconnected passages?
- Could you explain, for a specific student who comprehended a text poorly, whether the cause was decoding, knowledge, vocabulary, monitoring, or attention?
