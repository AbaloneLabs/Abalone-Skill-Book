# Professional Conduct

Professional conduct is the broad domain of how a teacher's behavior, both in and out of school, reflects on the profession and affects students, colleagues, and the community. Teaching carries a public ethical dimension that many professions do not: teachers are held to standards of conduct in their private lives, their online presence, their financial dealings, and their community behavior, because all of these affect their fitness to serve students. Conduct failures are rarely failures of not knowing the rules; they are failures of judgment, of recognizing how an action will be perceived, how a conflict will compromise a role, or how a private decision will become a public issue. The skill is anticipating the ethical and reputational dimensions of decisions before they become problems.

This skill covers the judgment needed to maintain professional conduct: recognizing conflicts of interest, managing public and private behavior, handling social media, addressing colleague misconduct, and upholding the profession's standards. The goal is conduct that honors the trust placed in teachers and avoids the predictable failures that harm students, colleagues, and the profession.

## Core Rules

### Recognize And Disclose Conflicts Of Interest

Conflicts of interest arise whenever your personal interests, relationships, or outside commitments could compromise your professional judgment. Tutoring your own students for pay, grading a relative, hiring a friend, accepting vendor gifts, or holding a second role that competes with your teaching duties are all conflicts, and many are subtle. The governing practice is recognition and disclosure: identify the conflict, disclose it to the appropriate party, and recuse where necessary. Most conflicts are not themselves misconduct; failing to disclose or manage them is. When in doubt, disclose, because transparency protects you and the institution.

### Treat Your Private Conduct As Connected To Your Professional Role

Teaching is a profession in which private conduct affects professional fitness, and this is a reality to navigate rather than resent. Behavior that would be unremarkable in another context, public intoxication, inflammatory social media posts, a dispute that becomes public, can affect a teacher's standing and their students' trust. This does not mean teachers surrender all private life; it means exercising judgment about how private behavior may become visible and how it may be perceived by students, families, and the community. The standard is not perfection but awareness, recognizing that the teacher's role carries into how they are seen in the community.

### Manage Social Media With Professional Care

Social media has created vast new surfaces for conduct issues. Maintain clear separation between professional and personal presence, set privacy settings thoughtfully while understanding they are not guarantees, avoid connecting with current students on personal accounts, and assume that anything you post could become visible to students, families, and employers. Be cautious about posting about students, even anonymously, about school matters, or about content that could undermine your professional standing. The permanence and shareability of online content mean that a moment's poor judgment can have lasting consequences. Treat social media as a professional context, because for a teacher it is one.

### Maintain Honesty And Integrity In All Representations

Honesty in professional contexts includes accurate representation of credentials, honest reporting of student progress, truthful communication with families and colleagues, and integrity in assessment and grading. Misrepresenting credentials, inflating or deflating grades for non-academic reasons, falsifying records, or being dishonest with families are serious conduct failures that harm students and violate the trust on which the profession depends. The standard is not just avoiding outright lies but avoiding the subtle dishonesties, the evasive communication, the convenient omission, the grade adjusted for reasons other than learning, that erode integrity over time.

### Address Colleague Misconduct Through Proper Channels

Discovering misconduct by a colleague, whether ethical, professional, or involving student safety, places an obligation to act through proper channels. The failure mode is silence: not reporting because it feels disloyal, because the misconduct seems minor, or because the consequences feel disproportionate. The professional obligation is to report through the appropriate channel, whether that is a supervisor, a compliance officer, or, for certain safety matters, an external authority. Addressing misconduct protects students and the profession, and silence in the face of it makes one complicit in the harm. Distinguish minor interpersonal issues, which may be addressed directly, from misconduct that requires formal reporting.

### Avoid Financial And Commercial Exploitation Of The Teacher Role

The teacher role carries access to students and families, and exploiting that access for financial gain is a serious conduct violation. This includes selling products or services to your own students, requiring students to purchase materials from which you benefit, accepting vendor gifts that influence purchasing decisions, or using the teacher platform to recruit for outside commercial ventures. Maintain clear boundaries between your role as a teacher and any commercial activity, and when in doubt, decline the opportunity or disclose it. The trust families place in teachers is not a resource to monetize.

### Uphold The Profession's Standards Publicly And Privately

Professional conduct includes upholding the standards of the profession, treating colleagues, students, and families with respect, avoiding public disparagement of the school or colleagues, and representing the profession honorably. This does not mean suppressing legitimate disagreement or critique, which has proper channels, but it means conducting oneself with the dignity the role requires. How teachers speak about the profession, their school, and their colleagues in public shapes public perception of teaching and affects the profession's standing. Private cynicism that becomes public behavior harms the whole field.

### Seek Guidance When Facing Ethical Uncertainty

Ethical situations are often ambiguous, and the right course is not always clear. The professional practice when facing uncertainty is to seek guidance: from a mentor, supervisor, ethics officer, professional standards body, or legal counsel as appropriate. Seeking guidance is not weakness; it is the disciplined response to situations where the stakes are high and the right answer is unclear. Attempting to resolve serious ethical questions alone risks both error and the appearance of impropriety. Build the habit of consulting early, before a decision hardens into action.

## Common Traps

### Undisclosed Conflicts Of Interest

Most conflicts are manageable if disclosed. Failing to disclose is the misconduct.

### Assuming Private Conduct Has No Professional Bearing

For teachers, it does. Exercise judgment about how private behavior may be perceived.

### Careless Social Media Use

Privacy settings are not guarantees, and current students should not be on personal accounts. Treat social media as professional.

### Subtle Dishonesty Eroding Integrity

Evasive communication and convenient omissions are dishonesties. Maintain honesty in all representations.

### Silence In The Face Of Colleague Misconduct

Silence makes one complicit. Report through proper channels.

### Monetizing The Teacher Role

Selling to your students or accepting influential gifts exploits trust. Maintain clear commercial boundaries.

### Public Disparagement That Harms The Profession

Legitimate critique has channels. Public cynicism harms the whole field.

### Resolving Serious Ethical Questions Alone

Seek guidance when stakes are high and answers unclear. Consulting is professional practice.

## Self-Check

- Do I recognize and disclose conflicts of interest rather than hoping they go unnoticed?
- Do I treat my private conduct as connected to my professional fitness?
- Do I manage social media with professional care, assuming visibility?
- Do I maintain honesty in credentials, grading, and communication?
- Would I report colleague misconduct through proper channels rather than staying silent?
- Do I avoid financial or commercial exploitation of my teacher role?
- Do I represent the profession honorably in public, reserving critique for proper channels?
- Do I seek guidance when facing ethical uncertainty rather than deciding alone?
