# Bullying, Harassment, And Target Protection

Bullying and harassment are not ordinary conflict, and treating them as such causes real harm. Bullying is unwanted, aggressive behavior that involves a real or perceived power imbalance and is repeated or has the potential to be repeated. Harassment is targeted mistreatment based on a protected characteristic such as race, sex, disability, religion, sexual orientation, or gender identity, and it carries specific legal obligations. Both differ from ordinary peer conflict in the imbalance of power, the targeted nature of the harm, and the inability of the target to defend themselves, and both require a different response than mediation or conflict resolution. The most damaging failures are inaction: dismissing reports as drama, telling targets to toughen up, mediating situations that have a clear perpetrator and target, or failing to recognize bias-based harassment as the legal issue it is. The opposite failure is over-labeling ordinary conflict as bullying, which pathologizes normal behavior and dilutes the response to genuine cases. The skilled response recognizes bullying and harassment when they occur, protects targets, holds those who cause harm accountable, mobilizes bystanders, and follows the legal and policy obligations that govern these situations.

Use this skill when identifying, preventing, or responding to bullying or harassment. The goal is to recognize these patterns, protect targets, address those who cause harm, work with bystanders, and follow mandatory reporting and anti-harassment obligations.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Bullying By Its Defining Features

Bullying is defined by unwanted aggressive behavior, a real or perceived power imbalance, and repetition or the potential for repetition. The power imbalance may be physical, social, verbal, or based on group dynamics, and it is what makes bullying different from ordinary conflict between relative equals. Forms include physical, verbal, social or relational, and, increasingly, cyberbullying through digital channels that follows students beyond school. Recognize the pattern: a target who cannot easily defend themselves, repeated or escalating harm, and a power differential that mediation cannot address.

### Recognize Harassment As A Legal Category

Harassment based on race, color, national origin, sex, disability, religion, sexual orientation, or gender identity is not only bullying; it is discrimination that triggers specific legal obligations under federal civil rights laws and district policy. Harassment creates a hostile environment when it is severe, pervasive, or persistent enough to interfere with a student's education, and it requires formal response, investigation, and remediation. Recognize bias-based mistreatment as the legal category it is, and follow the formal obligations it triggers, not only the interpersonal response.

### Distinguish Bullying And Harassment From Ordinary Conflict

Ordinary conflict involves mutual engagement between relative equals over a specific issue and can often be mediated because both parties have something to repair. Bullying and harassment involve a target and a perpetrator, a power imbalance, and repeated or patterned harm, and they are not appropriate for mediation, which implies shared responsibility. Misidentifying bullying as conflict leaves the target unprotected and implies they are partly to blame; misidentifying conflict as bullying pathologizes normal behavior. Learn the distinction and respond accordingly.

### Take All Reports Seriously And Investigate

Take every report of bullying or harassment seriously, investigate promptly, and document findings. Dismissing reports as drama, telling targets to ignore it, or assuming both sides must be at fault fails targets and allows harm to continue. Investigate discreetly to protect privacy, interview the target, the alleged aggressor, and witnesses separately, gather evidence, and follow district procedures. Do not promise confidentiality you cannot guarantee, but protect privacy to the extent possible.

### Protect The Target First And Throughout

The first priority is the target's safety and wellbeing. Assess immediate risk, develop a safety plan, ensure the target is not penalized for the harm done to them, and provide ongoing support through counseling and check-ins. Do not require the target to face the aggressor in mediation, do not separate the target from their support network as a solution, and do not make the target's daily life more restrictive while the aggressor's stays the same. The burden of change falls on the behavior causing harm, not on the target's freedom.

### Hold Those Who Cause Harm Accountable

Those who cause bullying or harassment must be held accountable through consequences matched to the severity and pattern, with attention to the function the behavior serves, the impact on the target, and the prevention of recurrence. Accountability is not only punishment; it includes understanding impact, developing empathy and alternative behaviors, making repair where appropriate and wanted by the target, and changing the conditions that allowed the harm. Coordinate with administrators, families, and where relevant, behavior specialists and law enforcement.

### Mobilize Bystanders As The Key Intervention

Bullying thrives or dies with bystanders. Most students are not aggressors or targets but bystanders whose response, joining in, passively watching, or intervening, shapes whether bullying continues. Teach bystanders to recognize bullying, to use safe intervention strategies such as distraction, redirection, reporting, and support for the target, and to shift the social norms that reward cruelty. Bystander intervention is one of the most effective long-term strategies because it changes the climate that allows bullying to flourish.

### Address Bias-Based Harassment Specifically

When harassment is based on a protected characteristic, address the bias explicitly. Name the behavior as discrimination, address the attitudes and climate that produced it, support the targeted student and the broader community of students who share that characteristic, and follow the formal anti-harassment obligations the situation triggers. Do not treat bias-based harassment as merely interpersonal conflict; it is a civil rights issue with specific legal and educational responsibilities.

### Follow Mandatory Reporting And Policy

Bullying, harassment, threats, violence, and abuse disclosures trigger mandatory reporting duties, anti-harassment policy, and district procedures. Know your obligations, follow them promptly, document thoroughly, and involve administrators and where relevant, law enforcement and child protective services. Do not investigate alone what policy requires to be handled through formal channels, and do not let loyalty to a student or fear of consequences lead you to suppress a report.

### Build A Preventive Climate

The strongest response to bullying is prevention through a climate in which cruelty is not rewarded, differences are respected, targets are supported, and bystanders intervene. Build this climate through explicit teaching of respect and inclusion, strong relationships, clear expectations, consistent response to lower-level cruelty before it escalates, and attention to the social norms that govern peer behavior. A preventive climate reduces the conditions that allow bullying to take root.

### Address Cyberbullying Across Contexts

Cyberbullying follows students beyond the school day and the school grounds through digital channels. Know your school's policy on off-campus behavior that affects school, document evidence, involve families, and address the behavior even when it originates outside school hours when it affects the school environment. Coordinate with administrators and, where relevant, platform reporting and law enforcement.

### Coordinate With Families, Specialists, And Administration

Bullying and harassment responses involve families of both target and aggressor, administrators, counselors, and where relevant, behavior specialists, law enforcement, and community organizations. Coordinate with the team, communicate with families according to policy, document thoroughly, and follow up to ensure the harm has stopped and the target is recovering. These situations rarely resolve through one intervention; sustained follow-up matters.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Reports As Drama Or Toughening Up

Reports must be taken seriously and investigated. Dismissal allows harm to continue.

### Mediating Bullying As Mutual Conflict

Bullying has a target and a perpetrator. Mediation implies shared responsibility and blames the target.

### Penalizing The Target Through The Solution

Do not restrict the target's freedom while the aggressor's stays the same. The burden falls on the harm-causing behavior.

### Treating Bias-Based Harassment As Ordinary Conflict

Harassment based on protected characteristics triggers legal obligations. Address the bias explicitly.

### Failing To Mobilize Bystanders

Bystanders shape whether bullying continues. Teach and empower intervention.

### Suppressing Reports Out Of Loyalty Or Fear

Mandatory reporting and policy override personal loyalty. Follow your obligations.

### Assuming One Intervention Resolves The Pattern

Bullying requires sustained follow-up. Check that harm has stopped and the target is recovering.

### Ignoring Cyberbullying Because It Originated Off Campus

Address behavior that affects the school environment regardless of where it originated.

### Over-Labeling Ordinary Conflict As Bullying

Pathologizing normal behavior dilutes response to genuine cases. Learn the distinction.

### Investigating Alone What Policy Requires Formally

Follow district procedures and involve administrators for formal matters.

## Self-Check

- Can you recognize bullying by its defining features of aggression, power imbalance, and repetition or potential for it?
- Do you recognize harassment based on protected characteristics as a legal category triggering specific obligations?
- Can you distinguish bullying and harassment from ordinary conflict, and respond to each appropriately without mediating bullying?
- Are all reports taken seriously, investigated promptly, documented, and handled with privacy protected to the extent possible?
- Is the target protected first and throughout, with safety planning, support, and no penalty for the harm done to them?
- Are those who cause harm held accountable with consequences, impact understanding, and prevention of recurrence?
- Are bystanders mobilized through teaching recognition and safe intervention strategies?
- Is bias-based harassment addressed explicitly as discrimination, with climate and community attention?
- Are mandatory reporting, anti-harassment policy, and district procedures followed and documented?
- Is a preventive climate built through teaching, relationships, expectations, and attention to social norms?
- Is cyberbullying addressed across contexts, with evidence documented and policy followed?
- Are families, administrators, counselors, and specialists coordinated, with sustained follow-up to confirm harm has stopped?
