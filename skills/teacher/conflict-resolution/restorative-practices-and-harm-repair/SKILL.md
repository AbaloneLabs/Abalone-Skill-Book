# Restorative Practices And Harm Repair

Restorative practices offer an approach to harm, conflict, and rule-breaking that focuses on repairing the harm done to people and relationships rather than on applying punishment to the rule-breaker. Where traditional discipline asks what rule was broken, who broke it, and what they deserve, restorative practice asks what happened, who was affected, what needs to be done to repair the harm, and what needs to happen to prevent recurrence. The approach is neither soft nor permissive; it often demands more of the person who caused harm than a suspension would, because genuine accountability requires facing those affected, understanding the impact, and taking action to repair. Restorative practices are powerful when used well, but they are widely misunderstood and misapplied. The most common failure is using the language of restoration without the substance, forcing victims into mediated encounters they do not want, or substituting a circle for accountability in ways that let harm continue. The opposite failure is rejecting restoration as soft and defaulting to exclusion, which removes students from instruction, damages relationship, and predicts further harm.

Use this skill when responding to harm, conflict, or rule-breaking through restorative practices. The goal is to use restoration to repair harm, build accountability, and preserve relationships and safety, while avoiding the traps of coerced encounters, superficial process, and restoration used to avoid accountability.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Restorative Shift From Rule To Relationship

Restorative practice reframes harm as a violation of people and relationships rather than of rules. The response focuses on repairing the harm, addressing the needs of those affected, and rebuilding relationship, rather than on assigning and punishing guilt. This shift does not eliminate accountability; it relocates it. The person who caused harm is held accountable not through suffering but through understanding impact, making repair, and changing behavior, which is often harder than enduring a punishment.

### Use Restorative Practices Proactively, Not Only Reactively

Restorative practices are not only a response to harm; they are a proactive approach to building community and relationship that prevents much harm from occurring. Use circles, check-ins, community-building protocols, and affective communication as regular features of classroom life. A classroom with strong relationships and community can absorb and repair conflict far more easily than one held together only by rules. The proactive use is the foundation that makes reactive use possible.

### Ensure Voluntary Participation, Especially For Those Harmed

Restorative encounters, conferences, and circles must be voluntary, especially for those who have been harmed. Forcing a target to face the person who harmed them re-victimizes them and violates the principle that restoration must serve those affected. Offer the process, respect refusal, and have alternatives ready. The readiness and willingness of participants, particularly the harmed party, determines whether a restorative encounter is appropriate or harmful.

### Hold The Person Who Caused Harm Genuinely Accountable

Restoration is not avoidance of accountability. The person who caused harm must understand the impact of their actions, take responsibility, and engage in meaningful repair. This is often more demanding than passive punishment, because it requires facing those affected, listening to impact, and acting to make things right. Do not use restorative language to let harm continue or to avoid holding the responsible party accountable; that is not restoration, it is evasion.

### Use The Right Restorative Practice For The Situation

Restorative practices range along a continuum from informal to formal. Informal practices include affective questions, impromptu conversations, and small-group problem-solving. More structured practices include circles for classroom-level issues and formal conferences for serious harm involving preparation, multiple participants, and a trained facilitator. Match the practice to the situation: a minor conflict may need only an affective conversation, while a serious harm may require a formal conference with preparation and support. Using too heavy a process for minor issues wastes effort; using too light a process for serious harm fails those affected.

### Prepare Participants Thoroughly For Formal Encounters

Formal restorative conferences require preparation. Meet with each participant in advance, help them understand the process, prepare them for what they will hear and say, assess their readiness, and ensure the encounter is safe and appropriate. Preparation is where most of the work happens; an unprepared conference can do more harm than good. Do not rush to the encounter; invest in readiness.

### Focus On Impact, Needs, And Repair

A restorative conversation focuses on what happened, who was affected and how, what they need to repair the harm, and what the person who caused harm will do to make things right and prevent recurrence. Use affective questions: what happened, what were you thinking at the time, who has been affected, what have you thought about since, and what do you need to make things right. Keep the conversation on impact and repair rather than on debate about facts or excuses.

### Plan And Support Reintegration

When a student has been removed from the classroom or school, plan and support their reintegration. Restorative reintegration addresses what the student needs to return successfully, what the community needs to receive them, and how relationships will be rebuilt. A return without reintegration support often fails, producing repeat harm and further removal. Reintegration is part of the restorative process, not an afterthought.

### Preserve Safety And Use Restoration Within Policy

Restoration does not override safety. Where there is ongoing risk of harm, threats, violence, or patterns despite intervention, safety and school policy take precedence, and restoration may need to wait until safety is established or may be inappropriate. Restoration is not a substitute for safety planning, mandatory reporting, or formal responses to serious harm. Use restoration within, not instead of, the policies that protect students.

### Avoid Disproportionate And Exclusionary Default

Restoration is partly a response to the well-documented failure of exclusionary discipline, which removes students from instruction, predicts further problems, and falls disproportionately on students by race, disability, and other factors. Use restoration to interrupt the cycle in which removal produces disengagement and further harm. Monitor discipline data for disproportionality, and use restorative alternatives where they can address harm without the documented harms of exclusion.

### Coordinate With The Team And Document

Restorative responses to significant harm are a team process involving administrators, counselors, families, and where relevant, specialists and safety officers. Coordinate with the team, follow school policy and any individual behavior plans, document incidents and agreements, and follow up to ensure repair occurred. Restorative practice is not improvisation; it is a structured approach that benefits from coordination and documentation.

## Common Traps

### Restorative Language Without Substance

Using circles and affective questions without genuine accountability is evasion, not restoration.

### Coerced Encounters For Those Harmed

Forcing targets to face those who harmed them re-victimizes. Participation must be voluntary.

### Restoration Used To Avoid Accountability

The person who caused harm must understand impact and repair it. Restoration is not soft.

### Using The Wrong Practice For The Situation

Match informal, circle, and formal conference practices to the severity and readiness of the situation.

### Skipping Preparation For Formal Encounters

Preparation is where most of the work happens. Unprepared conferences can harm.

### Reintegration Without Support

Return without reintegration support often fails. Plan reintegration as part of the process.

### Restoration Overriding Safety

Safety, threats, and policy take precedence. Restoration may need to wait or be inappropriate.

### Defaulting To Exclusion When Restoration Could Work

Exclusion removes instruction and predicts further harm. Use restorative alternatives where appropriate.

### Restorative Practice As Improvisation

Coordinate with the team, follow policy, document, and follow up.

### Ignoring Disproportionality

Exclusion falls hardest on particular groups. Monitor data and use restoration to interrupt cycles.

## Self-Check

- Do you understand the restorative shift from rule-and-punishment to harm-and-relationship, and does it preserve rather than eliminate accountability?
- Are restorative practices used proactively through circles, check-ins, and community-building, not only reactively?
- Is participation voluntary, especially for those harmed, with alternatives ready and refusal respected?
- Is the person who caused harm held genuinely accountable through understanding impact and engaging in repair?
- Is the restorative practice matched to the situation, from informal affective conversations to formal conferences?
- Are formal conference participants prepared thoroughly in advance, with readiness and safety assessed?
- Does the conversation focus on impact, needs, and repair using affective questions rather than factual debate?
- Is reintegration planned and supported after removal, not treated as an afterthought?
- Are safety, threats, mandatory reporting, and school policy preserved, with restoration used within rather than against them?
- Is restoration used to interrupt exclusionary and disproportionate discipline patterns, with data monitored?
- Are significant restorative responses coordinated with the team, documented, and followed up?
