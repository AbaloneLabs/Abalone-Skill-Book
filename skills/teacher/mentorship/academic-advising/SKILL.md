# Academic Advising

Academic advising is the work of helping a student make decisions about their own learning path: which courses to take, how hard to push, how to balance ambitions with reality, what to do after this stage, and how to recover from a setback. It fails when the advisor simply signs off on whatever the student wants, when they impose a path the student does not own, when they steer students toward or away from options based on narrow assumptions about who can succeed, or when they focus on credentials and prestige while ignoring whether the path fits the actual student. Advising also fails when it happens too late, after a student has already closed off options through choices made without guidance.

This skill covers the judgment needed to advise well: grounding decisions in the student's goals and evidence, balancing aspiration with realism, equity in steering, helping students recover from academic difficulty, and treating advising as an ongoing conversation rather than a single sign-off. The goal is a student who makes informed decisions about their own path.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Student's Goals And Values, Not From A Default Path

Effective advising begins by understanding what this student actually wants and values, which may not match the advisor's assumptions or the school's default track. Ask about interests, constraints, what they imagine for themselves, and what their family and financial context requires. A student headed toward a trade, a student aiming for a selective university, and a student unsure of anything are all owed serious advising, and the right path differs for each. Defaulting every student to the most academically intense program, or quietly steering some students away from challenging paths, are both failures of advising. The student's goals are the starting point; the advisor's job is to help them get there with eyes open.

### Balance Aspiration With Realistic Evidence

Students often either overestimate or underestimate what they can handle. Advising well means holding both the student's ambitions and the evidence of their preparation, work habits, and outside commitments in view, and helping them choose a path that stretches without breaking. Pushing a student to attempt something just beyond their current reach can be exactly right; pushing them into a load that predictably overwhelms sets up failure. Equally, holding a capable student back out of caution closes doors they could have opened. The judgment is in calibrating stretch to the individual, using evidence rather than optimism or fear.

### Examine Your Own Steering For Bias

Advisors carry assumptions about who belongs in advanced courses, who is "college material," who can handle rigor, and those assumptions are often shaped by race, class, gender, language, and disability in ways the advisor does not consciously intend. Audit your own patterns: whom do you tend to encourage toward challenging paths, and whom do you tend to steer toward easier ones, and do those patterns correlate with student characteristics rather than with evidence of readiness? Equitable advising means offering the most ambitious appropriate path to every student who can succeed in it, and actively inviting in the students who have been historically excluded. Steering based on bias, however well-intentioned, narrows students' futures.

### Make Options And Tradeoffs Visible

Students often choose from a narrow slice of what is possible because they cannot see the full landscape. A core advising task is making options visible: different course sequences, different post-secondary pathways, the consequences of choosing or not choosing certain courses now, the difference between credentials, and the financial and time implications of each path. Lay out the tradeoffs honestly rather than prescribing a single answer. A student who understands what they are choosing and what they are giving up makes a better decision than one following a recommended script, even if their final choice is unconventional.

### Advise Early Enough To Keep Options Open

Many academic decisions are quietly irreversible. Dropping a course sequence in middle school can close off advanced options years later; missing a prerequisite locks out a path. Advise early, before students make choices that foreclose futures, and explicitly name which choices preserve flexibility and which narrow it. Students who do not yet know what they want should be advised toward paths that keep the most doors open, because certainty about the future is rare and narrowing prematurely is costly.

### Help Students Interpret Their Own Assessment Evidence

Students often read their grades as verdicts on their ability rather than as information about their current standing and next steps. Advising includes helping them interpret results accurately: what a grade does and does not mean, what growth over time shows, what a single low mark implies and what it does not, and how to use feedback to decide next steps. A student who reads evidence instrumentally, as input to a decision, makes better choices than one who reads it as identity.

### Support Recovery From Academic Difficulty Without Lowering Expectations

When a student struggles, two responses are both wrong: writing them off as unable to handle the work, and lowering expectations to make the struggle disappear. The right response is to diagnose what went wrong, build a plan to address it, and hold the student to a realistic but demanding standard while supporting them toward it. Recovery is possible and common when the supports are right, and an advisor who treats a setback as information rather than destiny helps the student recover their trajectory.

### Treat Advising As An Ongoing Conversation

A single advising meeting rarely produces a durable decision, because students' goals and circumstances change. Build advising as an ongoing relationship in which decisions are revisited, evidence is reviewed, and the student's evolving sense of themselves shapes the path. The conversation over years is what produces good outcomes, not a single appointment.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting Every Student To The Same Track

One-size-fits-all advising ignores individual goals and contexts. Start from what this student actually wants.

### Over-Or-Under-Calibrating Stretch

Pushing into predictable failure or holding a capable student back both harm. Calibrate stretch to evidence.

### Steering Based On Bias

Assumptions about who belongs in rigor narrow futures. Audit your patterns and invite in excluded students.

### Prescribing Instead Of Laying Out Tradeoffs

A single recommended answer denies the student agency. Make options and their consequences visible.

### Advising Too Late To Keep Options Open

Irreversible choices made without guidance foreclose futures. Advise early toward paths that preserve flexibility.

### Letting Grades Become Identity

A mark is information, not a verdict. Help students read evidence as input to decisions.

### Writing Off Or Rescuing A Struggling Student

Both deny the student growth. Diagnose, plan, support, and hold a realistic standard.

## Self-Check

- Did I start from the student's own goals, values, and constraints rather than a default path?
- Did I calibrate stretch using evidence of preparation and outside commitments?
- Have I audited my own steering for bias tied to student characteristics?
- Did I make options and tradeoffs visible rather than prescribing a single answer?
- Did I advise early enough to keep the most doors open for an undecided student?
- Did I help the student interpret assessment evidence as information rather than identity?
- For a struggling student, did I diagnose, plan, and support without lowering expectations?
- Is advising an ongoing conversation rather than a single sign-off?
