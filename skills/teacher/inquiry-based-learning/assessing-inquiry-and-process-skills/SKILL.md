# Assessing Inquiry And Process Skills

If you teach inquiry but assess only the final answer, students learn within a week that the investigation was theater and the real game is producing the correct conclusion. The practices of inquiry, questioning, hypothesizing, designing a fair test, marshaling evidence, constructing reasoning, and collaborating, are the durable outcomes, and they are invisible to a test that scores only the result. The challenge is that process skills are harder to assess than products: they unfold over time, they are partly collaborative, and a clean final answer can be produced by a student who contributed none of the thinking. This skill is about designing assessment that captures the practices without becoming so burdensome that the teacher stops doing inquiry.

This applies when the work is to assess an inquiry unit, design an investigation rubric, score process skills, assess collaboration, or align grades with what the inquiry actually taught. The recurring failure is defaulting to grading the product because it is easy, thereby signaling that the process did not matter.

## Core Rules

### Assess The Practices, Not Only The Product

The final product (a conclusion, a model, a report) is the easiest thing to grade and the least informative about what was learned. A correct conclusion can be copied, guessed, or supplied by one group member. The practices of inquiry are the transferable outcomes and must be assessed directly.

Assess, separately and explicitly:

- the quality of the question or hypothesis (is it testable, specific, grounded in prior knowledge);
- the design of the investigation (are variables controlled, is the procedure fair, is the data sufficient);
- the use of evidence (does the reasoning cite the data, distinguish correlation from causation, address anomalous results);
- the construction of the explanation (is the claim supported, is reasoning linked to evidence, are alternatives considered);
- the revision of thinking in response to data.

Grade these on their own scales. A student whose conclusion was wrong but whose evidence and reasoning were sound has learned more than one who guessed right.

### Use A Rubric That Separates Dimensions

A single holistic score for an inquiry project hides whether the student could investigate well but reason poorly, or vice versa. Use an analytic rubric that scores each dimension independently so feedback can target the specific weak practice.

A strong inquiry rubric:

- names each dimension (question, design, evidence, reasoning, communication) as a separate row;
- describes concrete observable performance at each level, not vague quality words;
- distinguishes the practice from the correctness of the conclusion;
- is shared with students before the investigation so the criteria shape their work;
- is calibrated across graders when multiple teachers score.

Avoid rubric language like "good analysis" that two graders would score differently. Describe what is observable: "cites at least three data points and explains how each supports the claim."

### Capture Process Through Artifacts And Observation

Process skills leave traces if you collect them. Design the investigation so the thinking is recorded, then assess those artifacts.

Collect:

- a pre-investigation plan or hypothesis with stated reasoning;
- a data table or observation log in the student's own hand;
- a written claim-evidence-reasoning response;
- a revision note showing how thinking changed after the data;
- a reflection on what the investigation did not answer.

Supplement artifacts with observation: a simple checklist of practices seen during facilitation (asked a testable question, controlled a variable, cited data in discussion) captures what no final product can. Rotate which groups you observe closely so every student is sampled over a unit.

### Assess Collaboration Without Conflating It With Content

Collaboration is a legitimate inquiry outcome, but it is a different construct from scientific reasoning and should be assessed as such, not folded into the content grade. A student can reason brilliantly while dominating a group, or contribute steadily while reasoning weakly.

Assess collaboration through:

- defined roles and the observed fulfillment of them;
- peer input on contribution, gathered in a way that surfaces freeloading and dominance;
- observable behaviors (building on a peer's idea, distributing tasks, checking consensus);
- a self-assessment of what the student contributed and what they learned from others.

Keep the collaboration score separate from the content and reasoning scores so a weak collaborator is not mislabeled a weak scientist, and vice versa.

### Make Individual Accountability Visible In Group Inquiry

Group inquiry tempts the freeloader and hides the contribution. Even when the investigation is collaborative, the assessment of each student's understanding must be individual. Pair the group product with an individual artifact: each student writes their own claim-evidence-reasoning, defends the group's conclusion in a brief individual conference, or completes a transfer task alone. This protects both fairness and the integrity of the grade, and it deters social loafing. See the companion skill on individual accountability.

### Distinguish The Quality Of Reasoning From The Correctness Of The Answer

A well-reasoned investigation can yield a wrong conclusion because of ambiguous data, equipment limits, or a reasonable but mistaken interpretation. A poorly reasoned investigation can yield a correct conclusion by luck or copying. The grade should reward the former and not the latter. Score the reasoning on its own merits: was the evidence marshaled well, were alternatives considered, was the conclusion appropriate to the data, even if later evidence would revise it. This teaches students that how you think matters more than landing on the magic answer, which is the entire point of inquiry.

### Align The Assessment With The Level Of Inquiry

A structured inquiry, where the teacher provided the question and procedure, should not assess students on their ability to design an investigation, because they were not asked to. Match the assessed practices to the practices the level of inquiry actually required. If you want to assess question-generation or procedure-design, the unit must be guided or open enough to demand it. Mismatch breeds unfair grades and student cynicism.

### Use Assessment To Improve Practice, Not Only To Rank

Because inquiry skills develop slowly over many units, a single investigation's score is less important than the trajectory. Use the rubric data to identify which practice the class (or a student) most needs next, and feed that into the next unit's design. Assessment that only ranks without informing the next cycle of teaching wastes the diagnostic power of inquiry artifacts.

## Common Traps

### Grading Only The Final Answer

Scoring the conclusion because it is easy signals that the process did not matter and rewards copying and guessing. Assess the practices directly.

### Holistic Scores That Hide The Weak Dimension

A single grade for an inquiry project obscures which practice is weak. Use an analytic rubric with separate, observable dimensions.

### Vague Rubric Language

"Good analysis" or "strong reasoning" is scored differently by different graders. Describe observable performance at each level.

### Conflating Collaboration With Content

Folding teamwork into the science grade mislabels weak collaborators as weak thinkers. Assess collaboration as its own dimension.

### Group Grade With No Individual Check

A pure group product hides contribution and invites freeloading. Pair group work with an individual artifact or conference.

### Penalizing Well-Reasoned Wrong Conclusions

Grading only correctness punishes the sound reasoning that ambiguous data could not resolve. Reward the quality of the reasoning on its own merits.

### Assessing Practices The Level Did Not Require

Scoring procedure-design in a structured inquiry where the teacher gave the procedure is unfair. Match assessed practices to the inquiry level.

### Observation That Never Reaches All Students

Watching only the loudest groups skews the data. Rotate observation and sample every student across the unit.

## Self-Check

- Does the assessment score inquiry practices (question, design, evidence, reasoning, revision) separately from the correctness of the final conclusion?
- Is the rubric analytic, with each dimension described by observable performance at each level rather than vague quality words?
- Are process artifacts (plans, data logs, claim-evidence-reasoning responses, revision notes) collected and assessed, not only the final product?
- Is collaboration assessed as a distinct dimension, separate from content and reasoning?
- Is there an individual accountability mechanism (individual artifact or conference) paired with any group product?
- Can a student with a well-reasoned but incorrect conclusion earn a strong reasoning score, and can a lucky correct conclusion earn a weak one?
- Are the assessed practices matched to the level of inquiry the unit actually required (structured, guided, open)?
- Is observation rotated so every student's practices are sampled across the unit, not only the loudest groups?
- Does the assessment data feed the design of the next unit, identifying which practice to target, rather than only ranking students?
