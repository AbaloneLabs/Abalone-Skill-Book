# Peer Mentoring Among Teachers

Peer mentoring is a relationship between colleagues, without the formal structure or authority of coaching or induction, in which teachers support each other's growth. It is valuable because it draws on the expertise distributed across a faculty and because it operates outside evaluative hierarchies, enabling honesty that formal structures sometimes cannot. It also fails in predictable ways: when it is unequal, with one teacher always the giver and the other always the receiver; when it drifts into complaint rather than growth; when feedback is avoided to preserve the friendship; or when it lacks the structure to actually produce change. Peer mentoring requires design as much as formal mentoring does.

This skill covers the judgment needed to make peer mentoring productive: building a balanced relationship, giving honest feedback without authority, sharing expertise without imposing, and sustaining the relationship over time. The goal is a collegial relationship that produces real growth for both teachers rather than a friendship that avoids the work.

## Core Rules

### Build Reciprocity Into The Relationship From The Start

The healthiest peer mentoring is reciprocal, with both teachers giving and receiving rather than one always mentoring the other. Reciprocity prevents the dynamic where one teacher is positioned as the expert and the other as the novice, which breeds resentment or dependency, and it draws on the fact that every teacher has strengths worth sharing. Establish reciprocity explicitly: both will observe, both will be observed, both will give and receive feedback, both will share practice. A reciprocal relationship sustains itself; an unequal one erodes.

### Agree On Purpose, Norms, And Confidentiality

Peer mentoring without explicit agreements drifts. Agree early on what the relationship is for, what norms will govern it, and what confidentiality applies. Will you observe each other's practice, examine student work together, plan together, or some combination. How often. What is shared with others and what stays between you. These agreements feel formal for a friendship but they are what prevent the relationship from collapsing into either complaint sessions or unfocused chat. Revisit the agreements as the relationship develops.

### Give Honest Feedback Despite The Lack Of Authority

The hardest part of peer mentoring is honesty. Without the authority of a formal coaching role, it is tempting to avoid uncomfortable feedback to preserve the relationship, but avoidance defeats the purpose. Develop the skill of honest, caring feedback: specific, evidence-based, framed as your perspective, and focused on practice rather than person. Use language that invites dialogue, "I noticed" and "I wondered," rather than pronouncements. Equally, honor the relationship by not softening feedback so much that it carries no information. Honesty delivered with care strengthens rather than threatens a peer relationship.

### Share Expertise Without Imposing It

Peer mentors have expertise worth sharing, and sharing it well is a skill. Offer your experience as a perspective and a resource, not as the correct answer: "when I faced that, here is what I tried, and here is what I found," rather than "you should do this." This framing respects your colleague's professional judgment and their different context, and it lets them take what is useful rather than comply with a directive. Imposed expertise produces resistance; offered expertise produces consideration. The difference is largely in how the expertise is framed.

### Avoid The Drift Into Complaint

Peer mentoring relationships, especially between trusted colleagues, can drift into complaint sessions about students, administration, or the system. Some venting is normal and bonding, but when complaint becomes the substance of the relationship, growth stops. Notice when conversations have drifted into complaint and redirect toward practice and problem-solving. The test is whether the conversation ends with something to try or with shared grievance. A peer mentoring relationship that becomes primarily a complaint venue serves neither teacher's growth.

### Use Structure To Keep The Relationship Productive

Without structure, peer mentoring becomes whatever happens to come up. Build in light structure: a focus for each meeting, a rotation through observing, planning, and examining work, and occasional reflection on whether the relationship is serving both teachers' growth. Structure does not mean formality; it means intentionality. A small amount of structure prevents the relationship from coasting and ensures it actually produces development.

### Protect The Relationship From Evaluation Dynamics

Even in peer relationships, evaluation dynamics can creep in: one teacher becomes the judge, comparison enters, or feedback starts to feel like grading. Protect against this by keeping the relationship explicitly formative, by maintaining reciprocity, and by avoiding comparisons to other colleagues. If the relationship starts to feel evaluative to either party, name it and rebalance. Peer mentoring works only as long as both teachers experience it as mutual support rather than judgment.

### Sustain The Relationship Through Difficulty And Change

Peer mentoring relationships encounter the same strains as any sustained collaboration: busy periods, disagreements, changing roles, one teacher's growth outpacing the other's. Sustaining the relationship through these requires attention: renegotiate the agreements when circumstances change, address tensions directly rather than letting them accumulate, and allow the relationship to evolve rather than forcing it to stay fixed. A peer mentoring relationship that adapts to change can last for years and compound in value; one that cannot adapt ends at the first strain.

## Common Traps

### One-Way Mentoring Dynamic

Always-giver and always-receiver breeds resentment and dependency. Build reciprocity.

### No Agreements, Drifting Conversation

Without purpose, norms, and confidentiality, the relationship drifts. Agree explicitly.

### Avoiding Honest Feedback To Preserve Friendship

Avoidance defeats the purpose. Develop caring, specific, evidence-based honesty.

### Imposing Expertise As The Correct Answer

Imposed expertise produces resistance. Offer experience as perspective and resource.

### Drifting Into Complaint Sessions

Complaint replaces growth. Notice the drift and redirect toward practice.

### Coast Without Structure

Unstructured peer mentoring becomes whatever comes up. Build in light intentionality.

### Evaluation Dynamics Creeping In

Comparison and judgment poison peer mentoring. Keep it explicitly formative and reciprocal.

### Inability To Adapt To Change

Fixed relationships end at the first strain. Renegotiate and let the relationship evolve.

## Self-Check

- Is the relationship reciprocal, with both teachers giving and receiving?
- Did we agree on purpose, norms, and confidentiality at the start?
- Can I give honest, caring, evidence-based feedback rather than avoiding it?
- Do I share expertise as perspective rather than imposing it as the answer?
- Do I notice and redirect drift into complaint?
- Is there light structure keeping the relationship intentional?
- Have I protected the relationship from evaluation dynamics and comparison?
- Can the relationship adapt to changing circumstances and tensions?
