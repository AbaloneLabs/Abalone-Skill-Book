# Instructional Coaching

Instructional coaching is the work of helping a teacher improve specific aspects of their practice through a sustained, partnership relationship. It is powerful and frequently mishandled. Coaching fails when it becomes covert evaluation, when the coach imposes their own approach rather than building on the teacher's goals, when feedback is vague or overwhelming, or when the relationship collapses into either dependency or resistance. Coaching an adult learner is also fundamentally different from teaching a student, and coaches who apply classroom teaching instincts, directing and correcting, often produce resistance rather than growth.

This skill covers the judgment needed to coach instruction effectively: building a partnership, setting goals, observing and giving feedback to adults, using coaching moves well, and keeping coaching separate from evaluation. The goal is a teacher whose practice measurably improves through a relationship they experience as supportive.

## Core Rules

### Build A Partnership Grounded In The Teacher's Goals

Effective coaching is partnership, not prescription. It begins with what the teacher wants to improve, identified through their own goals, their students' needs, or evidence from their practice, and it works toward changes the teacher owns. A coach who imposes their own agenda, however well-intentioned, produces compliance at best and resistance at worst, because adults grow toward goals they set, not goals set for them. Establish the teacher's goals early, revisit them, and let them drive the work. The coach's expertise serves the teacher's agenda, not the reverse.

### Keep Coaching Strictly Separate From Evaluation

Coaching dies the moment it feeds evaluation. If coaching input influences formal evaluation, grading, or personnel decisions, teachers will hide rather than reveal, perform rather than practice, and the coaching relationship becomes useless for growth. Maintain strict confidentiality and a clear boundary between the formative coaching role and any evaluative role. This separation must be explicit and protected, and where it blurs, the coach must advocate for clarity. Without it, coaching cannot produce the honesty that growth requires.

### Use A Coaching Cycle Of Goal, Evidence, Action, Reflection

Productive coaching follows a cycle: identify a specific goal, gather baseline evidence, plan and implement a change, and reflect on the results before setting the next goal. This cycle prevents the common failures of coaching that drifts without focus or that accumulates feedback without action. Each cycle should target a specific, bounded aspect of practice, so that progress is visible and the teacher experiences success. Cycles build on each other over time, producing sustained growth rather than scattered effort.

### Give Feedback That Is Specific, Evidence-Based, And Bounded

Feedback to an adult learner must be specific enough to act on, grounded in observed evidence rather than impression, and bounded to a manageable amount. Vague feedback, "work on engagement," produces nothing; overwhelming feedback, a catalog of every observed issue, produces paralysis. Choose the one or two highest-leverage changes, frame them in evidence, and connect them to the teacher's goal. Equally important, name what is working, because teachers need to understand their successes to reproduce them. Feedback that only corrects demoralizes; feedback that also affirms accurately builds the confidence to change.

### Use The Full Range Of Coaching Moves, Not Just Feedback

Coaching is more than observing and giving feedback. The full range includes co-planning lessons, modeling a strategy for the teacher to observe, co-teaching, examining student work together, analyzing assessment data, and rehearsing specific moves before trying them in class. Different moves serve different purposes: modeling when the teacher needs to see an approach, co-planning when the design is the issue, rehearsal when a skill needs practice before live use. Match the move to the need rather than defaulting to the observe-and-feedback cycle alone.

### Observe Descriptively And Record Evidence The Teacher Cannot See

During observation, record what happens descriptively rather than evaluatively, and capture evidence the teacher cannot see from the front of the room: which students participated, where confusion appeared, how time was distributed, what specific students did. This evidence is the raw material of productive feedback, because it shows the teacher their lesson through a lens they cannot access alone. Descriptive, specific evidence also lowers defensiveness, because it is data rather than judgment, and it lets the teacher draw their own conclusions alongside the coach's.

### Respect The Adult Learner's Autonomy And Experience

Coaching an adult is different from teaching a child. Adult learners bring experience, established practice, and professional identity, and they resist being treated as novices to be corrected. Respect what the teacher already does well, build on their existing strengths, and frame changes as refinements to their practice rather than corrections of failure. Adults grow when their competence is acknowledged and their autonomy is respected, even as they are challenged to develop. A coach who treats a veteran teacher like a beginner produces resentment; a coach who partners with the teacher's experience produces growth.

### Close Each Cycle With Evidence Of Whether The Change Worked

Coaching without evidence of impact is activity without improvement. At the end of each cycle, examine whether the targeted change produced the intended effect on student learning or on the aspect of practice in question. This closes the loop, validates the work, and informs the next goal. A coaching relationship that never checks whether changes worked cannot demonstrate its value or improve its own practice.

## Common Traps

### Coaching As Covert Evaluation

The moment coaching feeds evaluation, honesty dies. Keep the roles strictly separate.

### Imposing The Coach's Agenda

Adults grow toward their own goals. Build partnership around what the teacher wants to improve.

### Drifting Without A Cycle

Ungoaled coaching accumulates feedback without action. Use goal-evidence-action-reflection cycles.

### Vague Or Overwhelming Feedback

Both produce nothing. Choose one or two evidence-based, high-leverage changes.

### Defaulting To Observe-And-Feedback Alone

Coaching includes modeling, co-planning, rehearsal, and data analysis. Match the move to the need.

### Evaluative Observation That Triggers Defensiveness

Record descriptive evidence the teacher cannot see. Let data, not judgment, drive the conversation.

### Treating The Teacher As A Novice

Adults resist being corrected. Respect experience, build on strengths, frame changes as refinements.

### Never Checking Whether Changes Worked

Activity without impact evidence cannot demonstrate value. Close each cycle with evidence.

## Self-Check

- Is the coaching partnership grounded in the teacher's own goals?
- Is coaching strictly separate from evaluation, with explicit confidentiality?
- Do we use a goal-evidence-action-reflection cycle for each focus area?
- Is my feedback specific, evidence-based, bounded, and accurate about what works?
- Do I use the full range of coaching moves matched to the need?
- Do I observe descriptively and capture evidence the teacher cannot see?
- Do I respect the teacher's autonomy and build on their existing strengths?
- Does each cycle close with evidence of whether the change improved student learning?
