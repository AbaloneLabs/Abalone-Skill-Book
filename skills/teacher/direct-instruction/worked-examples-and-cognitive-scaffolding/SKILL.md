# Worked Examples And Cognitive Scaffolding

A worked example is not a correct answer written out. It is a scaffold that lets a novice study an expert solution without having to generate one from scratch, freeing working memory to learn the structure of the strategy. Used well, examples dramatically accelerate early skill acquisition. Used poorly, they either overload the learner with extraneous detail, teach a single surface form that never generalizes, or stay present so long that students never do the cognitive work themselves. The agent's job is to design the example set, the pairing with practice, and the schedule of fading so that support is maximal when the skill is new and withdrawn as soon as the schema can carry the load.

This skill applies when the work is to design worked examples, example-problem pairs, completion problems, or scaffold-fading sequences, or to anticipate where cognitive load will break a lesson. The recurring failure is treating examples as illustrations rather than as load-management tools: a beautiful example that is too rich, too varied, or never faded produces mimicry and false fluency.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Example As A Load-Management Tool

The purpose of a worked example is to reduce cognitive load during early learning so the learner can study the strategy without simultaneously having to generate it. Every design decision should be evaluated against whether it adds germane load (schema construction) or extraneous load (distraction).

Reduce extraneous load by:

- keeping the example free of irrelevant detail, decorative context, or tangential asides;
- integrating text next to the step it explains rather than separating labels from diagrams (split-attention effect);
- avoiding redundant presentation of the same information in two modalities that the learner must reconcile;
- using consistent notation and layout across examples so format is not a variable;
- isolating one principle per example rather than bundling several.

If a novice must work to parse the example before they can study the strategy, the example has failed its purpose.

### Use Example-Problem Pairs, Not Examples Alone

Studying a worked example followed immediately by attempting an isomorphic problem produces stronger learning than studying examples alone or solving problems alone. The example builds the schema; the paired problem forces the learner to retrieve and apply it before it fades.

Structure the pair so:

- the worked example and the practice problem share the same deep structure and very similar surface features early on;
- the learner studies the example, then covers or sets it aside and attempts the problem;
- the problem is close enough that success is likely but requires active reconstruction, not copying;
- feedback follows promptly so errors do not consolidate.

Sequence several pairs, then begin fading the example as fluency builds.

### Fade Scaffolds On A Deliberate Schedule

Scaffolding that never fades prevents the learner from doing the work that builds durable skill. Plan the withdrawal explicitly so support is high when the schema is fragile and tapers as it strengthens.

A typical fading ladder:

1. full worked example, then isomorphic problem;
2. completion problem: the example is partially worked, the learner finishes the remaining steps;
3. the learner attempts a problem with only a labeled strategy cue, not a worked solution;
4. the learner attempts a problem independently with no support;
5. the learner selects which strategy to apply among several.

Move a learner down the ladder based on demonstrated success, not on completing a fixed number of items. A learner who stalls at the completion stage needs another full example, not pressure to go independent.

### Vary Surface Features To Force Generalization

Novices naturally encode surface features (the story context, the specific numbers, the object names) alongside deep structure. If every example shares the same surface form, students learn to match the surface and appear fluent until they meet a new context.

Across the example set, hold the deep structure constant while systematically varying surface features:

- change the cover story, quantities, units, or format;
- swap the objects, names, or settings;
- present the same structure in symbolic, verbal, and graphical form;
- intersperse examples whose surface is similar but whose deep structure differs, to force discrimination.

The goal is for the learner to recognize the strategy across disguises, which requires the disguises to actually vary.

### Include Contrast And Boundary Cases

A single correct example teaches what the strategy does. It does not teach where the strategy applies and where it breaks. Pair examples with contrast to reveal the boundary conditions.

Use:

- two examples that differ in exactly one feature, so the learner sees what that feature controls;
- a correct solution beside a common flawed solution for error analysis;
- a case where the strategy works and a near case where it does not, with the learner explaining why;
- a nonexample that looks plausible but fails, to sharpen the concept.

Contrast is what teaches the limits, which a parade of correct examples cannot.

### Anticipate The Load Breakdown Points

Some steps in a procedure are far more demanding than others, and load breakdowns are predictable. Identify the high-load steps in advance and design support specifically for them.

For each procedure, ask:

- which step requires holding the most intermediate results in working memory;
- which step involves a subgoal that novices lose track of;
- which step has a notation or convention that is unfamiliar;
- which step is where the most common error occurs.

Add scaffolding precisely at those points: a subgoal label, a worked sub-step, a checklist, or a cue. Remove scaffolding from the easy steps first, the hard steps last.

### Align The Example With The Target Performance

The example must model the performance you will eventually assess. If the assessment requires students to choose their own strategy, the example set must include strategy-selection, not only execution of a named strategy. If the assessment requires explanation, the example must model the explanation. An example that shows execution only will leave students unable to do what is later demanded.

### Make The Schema Explicit, Not Implicit

Novices do not automatically abstract the schema from examples; they tend to remember the specifics. Name the strategy, label the subgoals, and state the conditions under which it applies. The labeling converts a memorable example into a transferable schema. Without explicit labeling, students remember "the one about the trains" rather than "a rate problem solved by separating the shared quantity."

## Common Traps

### Rich, Beautiful, Overloaded Examples

An example packed with real-world texture, multiple representations, and elegant extensions feels exemplary but overloads the novice. Strip extraneous detail; richness is for later.

### Examples That Never Fade

If the worked example stays in front of the student throughout practice, the student copies rather than reconstructs. Fade on a schedule.

### Uniform Surface Features

A set of examples that all use the same context teaches surface matching. Vary the surface to force generalization.

### Skipping The Completion Stage

Jumping from full examples to independent problems removes the intermediate scaffold where partial support lets fragile learners succeed. Use completion problems as the bridge.

### Equating Speed With Fluency

Fast correct answers on near-transfer items look like mastery but may be surface imitation. Test with far-transfer and discrimination items before declaring fluency.

### Bundling Multiple Principles Per Example

An example that illustrates three concepts at once teaches none of them cleanly to a novice. Isolate one principle per example.

### Split Attention Between Diagram And Labels

Placing labels in a legend far from the diagram forces the learner to hold and reconcile two sources. Integrate labels next to what they label.

### Assuming The Schema Was Abstracted

Students remember specifics; they do not automatically generalize. Name the strategy and state its conditions explicitly.

## Self-Check

- Does each example minimize extraneous load, with labels integrated and irrelevant detail removed?
- Are examples paired with isomorphic practice problems that require active reconstruction, not copying?
- Is there a deliberate fading schedule from full example to completion problem to independent attempt, gated on success?
- Do surface features vary across the example set while deep structure stays constant, forcing generalization?
- Are contrast and boundary cases included so students learn where the strategy applies and where it breaks?
- Have the high-load steps of the procedure been identified and given targeted scaffolding?
- Does the example model the full target performance, including strategy selection or explanation if those will be assessed?
- Is the schema named and its conditions stated explicitly, rather than left implicit for students to abstract?
- Is fluence verified with far-transfer and discrimination items, not only fast near-transfer performance?
