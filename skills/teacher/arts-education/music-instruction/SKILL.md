# Music Instruction

Music education develops the ability to perform, create, and respond to music with understanding. It encompasses singing, instrumental playing, listening, moving, composing, and analyzing, and it rests on musical concepts (rhythm, melody, harmony, form, timbre, dynamics, tempo) and skills (audiation, music literacy, technique, ensemble). The recurring failure in music instruction is rote performance: students learn to sing or play specific pieces by imitation, perform them at a concert, and learn them so temporarily that nothing transfers to a new piece, because the underlying concepts and skills were never taught. The class sounds good in the moment but produces no lasting musicianship.

The opposite failure is music taught only as abstract concepts and notation with little actual music-making, which is lifeless and misses the point that music is something you do. The design challenge is to build genuine musical understanding and skill through active music-making, balancing performance, creation, and response. This skill applies whenever the work is to design or evaluate music instruction.

## Core Rules

### Make Active Music-Making The Core

Music is learned by making music. The bulk of instructional time should be spent in active music-making: singing, playing, moving, creating, listening with engagement. A music class that is mostly lecture about music, or mostly silent worksheet work on notation, is not developing musicianship, however correct the content. Concept instruction serves the music-making; it does not replace it.

Protect active music-making as the core. Students should be making music in most of every lesson.

### Teach Audiation As The Foundation

Audiation is the ability to hear and comprehend music internally, to know what a sound will be before making it, and to make sense of music one hears. It is the musical equivalent of thinking in language, and it is the foundation on which all other musical skills rest. Students who sing or play without audiating are manipulating sound mechanically; students who audiate make music with understanding.

Develop audiation through singing, moving, listening, inner-hearing exercises, and the consistent connection between sound and symbol. Before students read or perform notation, they should be able to hear it internally.

### Teach Music Literacy Through Sound Before Symbol

Music literacy, reading and writing notation, is important, but it must be taught in connection with sound, not as abstract marks on a page. The sequence is sound to symbol: students first experience and internalize a musical pattern by singing, playing, or moving; then they learn to read the notation that represents what they already know; then they use notation to perform and create. Teaching notation before students have the sound in their ears produces decoding without meaning.

Connect every notation concept to sound. Students should be able to hear what they read and read what they hear.

### Teach Musical Concepts Through Diverse Repertoire

Rhythm, melody, harmony, form, timbre, dynamics, and tempo are not abstractions; they are what music is made of. Teach them through varied repertoire so students encounter each concept in many musical contexts, including diverse genres, traditions, cultures, and historical periods. Concept-plus-repertoire builds transferable understanding; concept taught through a single tradition builds narrow musicianship.

Choose repertoire that is musically rich, culturally respectful, age-appropriate, and representative of the diversity of musical traditions. Avoid tokenistic or stereotypical treatment of music of cultures.

### Balance Performance, Creation, And Response

A complete music education engages students as performers, creators, and responders. Performance develops technique and ensemble; creation, through improvisation and composition, develops musical thinking and ownership; response, through listening and analysis, develops perception and the language to discuss music. A program that is only performance produces skilled but dependent musicians; one that is only listening produces informed but inactive ones.

Include all three across a year. Improvisation and composition, often neglected, are where students make musical decisions and develop the deepest understanding.

### Teach Technique In Service Of Musical Expression

Vocal and instrumental technique matters because it enables musical expression. Teach technique explicitly: breath support, posture, hand position, embouchure, bowing, stick technique, depending on the medium. But teach it always in connection with the music it serves, so students understand that technique is a means to expression, not an end.

Avoid technique drills disconnected from music, which students experience as tedium, and avoid demanding expression without the technique to achieve it, which produces frustration.

### Design Ensemble Rehearsal That Builds Understanding, Not Just Repertoire

Ensemble rehearsal is where individual skills come together, but rehearsal that only runs pieces for the next concert produces performance without lasting learning. Rehearsal should also build conceptual understanding: why does this passage sound this way, what is the form, what is the harmonic structure, what listening or technical skill does this section demand. Rehearsals that teach, not just rehearse, develop musicians who can transfer to new repertoire.

Balance the pressure of upcoming performance with the slower work of building understanding. A concert is one event; musicianship is the long-term goal.

### Teach Listening As Active Engagement

Listening in music class is not passive background; it is active engagement with what is heard. Teach students to listen for specific elements: the form, the instruments, the rhythm, the changes, the expressive choices. Guided listening with focus questions builds the perception that informs both performance and creation.

Connect listening to the concepts and repertoire students are learning, so listening reinforces and extends what they make.

## Common Traps

### Rote Performance With No Transfer

Learning pieces by imitation for a concert produces temporary performance and no lasting musicianship. Teach the underlying concepts and skills.

### Notation Before Sound

Teaching reading as abstract marks before students have the sound in their ears produces decoding without meaning. Sound to symbol.

### Lecture Instead Of Music-Making

A class of talk about music develops no musicianship. Make active music-making the core.

### Technique Without Expression

Drills disconnected from music are tedious. Teach technique in service of musical expression.

### Performance-Only Programs

Programs with no creation or response produce skilled but dependent musicians. Balance performance, creation, and response.

### Narrow Repertoire

Teaching concepts through a single tradition builds narrow musicianship. Use diverse, respectful repertoire.

### Rehearsal That Only Runs Pieces

Rehearsal for the concert without teaching produces no transfer. Rehearse to build understanding, not just repertoire.

### Passive Listening

Background listening teaches nothing. Teach active, guided listening with focus.

## Self-Check

- Is active music-making the core of instruction, occupying most of each lesson?
- Is audiation, hearing and comprehending music internally, developed as the foundation?
- Is music literacy taught sound to symbol, with notation connected to sound students already know?
- Are musical concepts taught through diverse, respectful repertoire that builds transferable understanding?
- Does the program balance performance, creation, and response across the year?
- Is technique taught explicitly but in service of musical expression, not as disconnected drill?
- Does ensemble rehearsal build conceptual understanding, not just prepare repertoire?
- Is listening taught as active engagement with focus, not passive background?
- Could students explain a musical choice using the vocabulary of musical concepts?
- Is instruction building lasting musicianship, not just temporary performance?
