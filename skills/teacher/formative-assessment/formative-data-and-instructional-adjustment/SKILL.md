# Formative Data And Instructional Adjustment

Collecting formative data is the easy part. The hard part, and the part that actually matters, is deciding what to do with it. A teacher can administer exit tickets, run polls, circulate with a clipboard, and grade short responses every day, and still teach no differently the next morning. When that happens, the data was decoration, not formative assessment. Formative assessment is defined by its effect on instruction: evidence becomes formative only when it changes what the teacher or the student does next. The discipline this skill covers is the "so what" that must follow the data. It is the practice of reading patterns in student evidence, forming a hypothesis about what is causing them, choosing an instructional response proportionate to the pattern, and then checking whether the response worked. Without this loop, data collection becomes a burden that exhausts teachers and delivers nothing to students.

Use this skill whenever formative evidence has been collected and a decision must be made about what to do next. The goal is to prevent the agent from stopping at "here is what the data shows" and to force the harder question of "what instruction changes because of it."

## Core Rules

### Treat Data As A Signal For A Decision, Not As A Record

The first question after any formative data is not "what did students score," but "what will I do differently." If the data cannot change a decision, collecting it wasted class time.

Before analyzing, restate the decision the data is meant to inform:

- do I proceed to the next skill or reteach first;
- do I regroup students for targeted support or extension;
- do I adjust pacing for the whole class;
- do I change the model, examples, or representations I use;
- do I refer specific students for additional support.

If no decision is plausible, the data is recordkeeping. The most common failure of data-rich classrooms is that the data never reaches a decision.

### Read Patterns, Not Individual Scores

A pile of exit tickets or a poll result is most useful when read as a pattern across the class, not as a column of individual grades. The pattern tells you what to teach next; the individual scores tell you whom to pull aside.

Look for patterns such as:

- a single misconception shared by many students, which calls for whole-class reteach;
- a split between students who are secure and students who are not, which calls for regrouping;
- scattered errors with no common cause, which may indicate task confusion rather than learning gaps;
- a language or vocabulary pattern, where multilingual learners missed items for language reasons;
- a procedural pattern, where the strategy is sound but execution slips;
- a confidence pattern, where students are correct but unsure, or wrong but confident.

Sorting responses into pattern piles, secure, partial, not-yet, with a note on the misconception in each, is usually more actionable than a scored spreadsheet.

### Hypothesize The Cause Before Choosing A Response

Different causes require different responses. More practice will not fix a misconception if the underlying model is wrong; vocabulary support will not fix a procedural slip. Form a hypothesis about why the pattern exists before acting.

Consider causes such as:

- missing prerequisite skill that the current lesson assumed;
- a misconception, a wrong mental model that produces consistent errors;
- a careless or procedural slip on an otherwise sound strategy;
- a language barrier, where the student understood the concept but not the wording;
- task confusion, where the directions or format caused the error;
- an inaccessible material or accommodation gap;
- insufficient practice or too little time for consolidation.

Match the response to the cause. Reteaching the same way to fix a misconception caused by that very teaching simply repeats the failure.

### Match The Response To The Scope Of The Pattern

The proportion of students affected should drive the scope of the response. Over-responding to a small gap wastes the time of students who are ready; under-responding to a widespread gap leaves the class stranded.

Use scope as a guide:

- widespread shared misconception: whole-class reteach with a different model or representation;
- a sizable subgroup struggling while others are secure: regroup for targeted reteach while others extend or practice;
- a few individuals with specific gaps: small-group or individual conference while others work independently;
- scattered errors suggesting task confusion: clarify the task and recheck rather than reteach content;
- a language pattern: provide vocabulary and language support rather than content reteach.

The response should fit the pattern. A blanket reteach for a two-student gap punishes the ready students; a worksheet for a class-wide misconception abandons the students who needed new teaching.

### Plan The Adjustment Before The Next Lesson Begins

The instructional adjustment must be planned concretely, not left as a vague intention to "address it." A plan that is not specific will not survive the rush of the next morning.

Specify the adjustment:

- what will be retaught and with what different model, example, or representation;
- which students will be regrouped and for how long;
- what the secure students will do while others receive support;
- what pacing change results, and how it affects the unit calendar;
- what targeted practice or extension will be provided;
- when and how the learning will be rechecked to confirm the adjustment worked.

An adjustment that cannot be stated in a sentence is not yet a plan.

### Reteach Differently, Not Just Again

If the first teaching produced a misconception, repeating it identically will reproduce the misconception. The adjustment must change something about the instruction.

Change one or more of:

- the representation, moving from abstract to concrete, symbolic to visual, or vice versa;
- the example, using a clearer or more contrasting case;
- the model or analogy, replacing one that misleads with one that illuminates;
- the sequence, breaking the skill into smaller steps or building the prerequisite first;
- the context, using a situation closer to students' experience;
- the cognitive load, reducing extraneous demand so the target concept is foregrounded.

"Reteach" without "differently" is not an adjustment; it is a repeat.

### Recheck To Confirm The Adjustment Worked

An adjustment is a hypothesis, and hypotheses must be tested. After reteaching or regrouping, check whether the gap actually closed before declaring victory and moving on.

Recheck by:

- administering a short, focused check on the same objective within a day or two;
- comparing the new pattern to the original one to confirm improvement;
- paying attention to the students who were previously not-yet, not just the class average;
- watching for the misconception resurfacing later in a transfer context, where it often hides;
- adjusting again if the first response did not work.

Without rechecking, the teacher cannot know whether the data loop closed or simply moved on.

### Use Data To Adjust For Individuals, Not Just The Class

Class-level patterns drive whole-group moves, but formative data also reveals individual needs that require individual responses. A class average can hide students who are far ahead or far behind.

Use individual signals to:

- pull a small group for a prerequisite they alone are missing;
- extend a student who is secure with a more complex transfer task;
- conference with a student whose errors suggest a specific misconception;
- provide language support to a multilingual learner whose errors are linguistic;
- refer a student for additional support services when the gap exceeds what in-class adjustment can close.

The class move and the individual move often need to happen together.

### Avoid Collecting More Than You Can Act On

A teacher can generate more data than any human can act on. Collecting evidence that will never reach a decision is a cost with no benefit, and it exhausts the teacher.

Constrain collection by:

- choosing checks whose results can be analyzed and acted on within the available time;
- favoring quick, pattern-readable checks over elaborate assessments for daily use;
- reserving deeper data collection for moments when a bigger decision is at stake;
- being honest about capacity; three exit tickets you actually use beat ten you do not.

The test of a good formative check is not how much data it produces but whether the data changes the next lesson.

## Common Traps

### Data Collection Without A Decision

The defining failure. If the data does not change instruction, it was not formative. Name the decision first.

### Reading Scores Instead Of Patterns

A column of numbers hides the misconception. Sort responses into pattern piles to see what to teach.

### Responding Before Hypothesizing The Cause

More practice will not fix a misconception. Match the response to the likely cause.

### Blanket Reteach For A Small Gap

Reteaching the whole class for a two-student problem bores the ready students. Scale the response to the pattern.

### Reteaching Identically

Repeating the lesson that produced the gap reproduces the gap. Change the representation, example, or model.

### Moving On Without Rechecking

An unverified adjustment is a guess. Recheck to confirm the gap closed.

### Over-Collecting Data

More data than you can act on exhausts without helping. Collect only what will reach a decision.

### Treating The Average As The Class

A class average can hide individuals far above or below it. Adjust for individuals alongside the class.

## Self-Check

- Is there a named instructional decision the data is meant to inform, with a plan for each likely result?
- Is the data read as patterns across the class, not just as individual scores in a column?
- Has a hypothesis been formed about the cause of the pattern before a response was chosen?
- Is the scope of the response, whole-class, subgroup, or individual, matched to the scope of the pattern?
- Is the adjustment specified concretely enough to survive the next morning, including materials and grouping?
- Does reteaching change the representation, example, or model rather than repeating the original instruction?
- Is there a plan to recheck the same objective within a day or two to confirm the adjustment worked?
- Are individual needs, extension for the secure and support for the struggling, addressed alongside the class move?
- Has data collection been constrained to what can actually be analyzed and acted on in the available time?
- Can you point to a specific change in instruction that resulted from recent formative data, and if not, why was it collected?
