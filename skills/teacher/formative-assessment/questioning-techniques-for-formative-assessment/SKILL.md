# Questioning Techniques For Formative Assessment

Questioning is the most immediate and most flexible form of assessment a teacher has, capable of surfacing student thinking in real time and reshaping instruction within the same lesson. But questioning as formative assessment only works when it is designed to reveal thinking rather than to perform it. The questions that most often fill classrooms, those with a single right answer, called on volunteers, answered in unison, produce the appearance of engagement while revealing almost nothing about what the class actually understands. The judgment problem is that effective questioning is a design problem: questions must be chosen to expose specific thinking, posed to elicit honest evidence, and used to adjust instruction. The most common failure is asking questions that produce performative rather than diagnostic evidence, then proceeding as if understanding were confirmed. The skill is designing questioning that genuinely surfaces thinking and using it to steer the lesson.

Use this skill before designing questions to check understanding, before planning questioning sequences, before deciding how to elicit and use student responses, or before diagnosing why questioning produced shallow evidence. The goal is to prevent the agent from asking questions that perform rather than reveal understanding, from sampling only confident volunteers, or from treating answers as confirmation rather than as evidence to be interpreted.

## Core Rules

### Ask Questions That Reveal Thinking, Not Just Answers

A question that has a single right answer reveals whether one student knows it; a question that asks how, why, or what makes you think that reveals the reasoning beneath. Formative questioning must target reasoning, not just recall, or it will miss the misconceptions that live in the process.

Reveal thinking by:

- asking why and how, not just what;
- asking for the reasoning behind an answer, including correct ones;
- using questions that have multiple defensible answers to expose reasoning;
- asking students to compare and evaluate different approaches;
- probing the partial understandings that underlie errors.

### Sample The Class, Not Just The Confident

If questioning always reaches the volunteers, it samples the students most likely to be right and conceals the rest. Formative questioning must sample the full range of the class, including the silent and the struggling, or it will produce falsely optimistic evidence.

Sample the class by:

- using cold call and no-hands-up routines to spread participation;
- using all-class responses, whiteboards, signals, or digital tools;
- tracking who has been called on and who has not;
- sampling deliberately across ability and confidence levels;
- treating volunteers' answers as one data point, not a class summary.

### Use Wait Time To Allow Thinking

The silence after a question is where thinking happens, and teachers typically wait less than a second before speaking again. Without adequate wait time, questions elicit reflex rather than reasoning, and the evidence they produce is shallow. Wait time must be deliberately protected.

Use wait time by:

- waiting three to five seconds after a question before calling on anyone;
- waiting again after a response to allow elaboration and others' thinking;
- resisting the urge to fill silence with rephrasing or answers;
- teaching students that silence means thinking is expected;
- using pair discussion before whole-class sharing to extend thinking time.

### Pose Before Naming, So All Think

When a teacher asks a question and names a student simultaneously, only that student thinks. When the teacher poses the question first, gives thinking time, then names a student, all students think. This small sequencing change transforms the cognitive engagement of the class.

Pose before naming by:

- posing the question to the whole class first;
- providing wait time for all to think;
- then naming the student to respond;
- using this sequence consistently for diagnostic questions;
- varying with no-opt-out routines so every student is accountable.

### Use Sequences That Build Understanding

Single questions produce single data points; sequences of questions build understanding across the class. A planned sequence, from accessing prior knowledge to probing reasoning to advancing thinking, produces richer evidence and deeper learning than isolated questions.

Use sequences by:

- planning strings of questions that build toward a key idea;
- moving from access to probe to advance within a questioning episode;
- using funneling questions that converge on a concept;
- using scaffolding questions that build student reasoning step by step;
- connecting one student's response to the next through deliberate linking.

### Use No-Opt-Out To Ensure Accountability

When a student says "I don't know," the interaction often ends, signaling that non-response is an exit. No-opt-out routines return to that student after a hint, a partner's answer, or a rephrase, so that every student is accountable for the learning and non-response is not an escape.

Use no-opt-out by:

- returning to a student who passed after providing a scaffold or cue;
- having the student repeat or build on a peer's answer;
- ensuring the student ultimately produces the response;
- signaling that every student is accountable for thinking;
- balancing accountability with psychological safety.

### Use All-Class Responses For Honest Evidence

Individual questioning samples one student at a time; all-class responses sample everyone simultaneously. Choral responses, whiteboards, signals, and digital tools produce evidence from the whole class at once, revealing the distribution of understanding rather than a single point.

Use all-class responses by:

- using whiteboards or mini-slates for every student to show an answer;
- using hand signals or cards for multiple choice or agree/disagree;
- using digital tools for instant aggregation of responses;
- scanning the full distribution, not just the majority;
- following up on the variation the all-class response reveals.

### Interpret And Act On The Evidence

Questioning produces evidence only if it is interpreted and acted upon. The point of formative questioning is not to confirm that learning has happened but to discover where it has and has not, and to adjust. Questioning that does not change instruction is not formative.

Interpret and act by:

- listening for the reasoning, not just the correctness, of responses;
- identifying patterns across the class, not just individual answers;
- adjusting pace, examples, or scaffolding in response to evidence;
- reteaching when evidence shows widespread misunderstanding;
- advancing when evidence shows readiness, rather than covering planned content.

### Avoid Questions That Perform Rather Than Reveal

Some questioning routines, rapid-fire recall, choral "yes?" responses, leading questions, produce the appearance of engagement and understanding while revealing nothing. These routines must be recognized and replaced with questioning that produces honest evidence.

Avoid by:

- recognizing leading questions that telegraph the answer;
- replacing choral affirmation with individual or all-class responses;
- avoiding rapid-fire recall that rewards speed over thinking;
- watching for the false confirmation of confident volunteers;
- preferring fewer, deeper questions over many shallow ones.

## Common Traps

### Single-Answer Recall

Questions with one right answer that reveal whether one student knows, not how the class thinks.

### Sampling Volunteers Only

Questioning that reaches the confident and conceals the struggling.

### No Wait Time

Filling silence immediately, eliciting reflex rather than reasoning.

### Naming Before Posing

Calling on a student simultaneously with the question, so only one thinks.

### Isolated Questions

Single questions without sequences that build understanding.

### Accepting Non-Response

Letting "I don't know" end the interaction and signal an exit.

### Performative Questioning

Routines that produce the appearance of engagement without honest evidence.

### Not Acting On Evidence

Questioning that confirms rather than adjusts, failing to be formative.

## Self-Check

- Do questions target reasoning through why and how, not just recall through what?
- Does questioning sample the full class, including silent and struggling students, not just volunteers?
- Is wait time of three to five seconds protected after questions and after responses?
- Are questions posed to the whole class before a student is named, so all think?
- Are questioning sequences planned to build from access to probe to advance?
- Does a no-opt-out routine ensure every student is accountable for thinking?
- Are all-class responses used to reveal the distribution of understanding?
- Is the evidence from questioning interpreted and used to adjust instruction?
- Are performative questioning routines recognized and replaced with diagnostic ones?
- Does questioning genuinely surface thinking rather than perform engagement?
