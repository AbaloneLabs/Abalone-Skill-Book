# Feedback That Advances Learning

Feedback is one of the most powerful influences on learning, but only when it is designed to move learning forward rather than to judge what has already happened. Feedback that tells a student whether they are right or wrong, or assigns a grade, may inform but rarely advances. Feedback that identifies the gap between current and desired performance and points toward closing it changes learning. The judgment problem is that effective feedback is not about quantity or praise but about quality and timing: it must be specific, actionable, and aimed at the next step, and it must be used by the learner. The most common failure is giving feedback that describes or judges the work rather than directing the learner, producing information without improvement. The skill is designing feedback that closes the gap between current and desired performance and ensuring it is acted upon.

Use this skill before designing feedback for student work, before deciding feedback form and timing, before planning how students will use feedback, or before diagnosing why feedback did not improve learning. The goal is to prevent the agent from giving feedback that judges rather than advances, from overwhelming students with too much feedback, or from assuming that giving feedback is the same as producing learning.

## Core Rules

### Aim Feedback At The Gap, Not At The Grade

The function of formative feedback is to close the gap between current understanding and the desired goal. Feedback that evaluates, grades, or praises may inform but does not by itself close the gap. Effective feedback identifies where the learner is, where they need to be, and how to get there.

Aim at the gap by:

- referencing the success criteria or learning goal explicitly;
- identifying specific gaps between current work and the standard;
- directing attention to what to do next, not just what was wrong;
- separating evaluative from descriptive feedback;
- ensuring feedback points forward to improvement, not backward to judgment.

### Make Feedback Specific And Actionable

Generic feedback, "good job," "needs work," "be more detailed," gives the learner nothing to act on. Effective feedback is specific enough that the learner knows exactly what to do and actionable enough that they can do it. Specificity turns feedback from comment into instruction.

Make specific and actionable by:

- naming the specific feature of the work, not a general quality;
- providing a concrete next step, not a vague direction;
- focusing on one or two high-leverage changes, not every flaw;
- showing what improvement would look like;
- ensuring the learner has the knowledge to act on the feedback.

### Time Feedback To Be Used

Feedback has its greatest effect when it is given while the learner can still act on it. Feedback after a unit is information; feedback during a draft, a practice, or a revision cycle is instruction. Timing determines whether feedback informs future work or improves current work.

Time feedback by:

- giving feedback during the work, not only after it is finished;
- providing feedback early enough for revision and improvement;
- using rapid feedback during practice to shape learning as it happens;
- avoiding feedback delays that make it irrelevant to current work;
- connecting feedback to immediate opportunities to apply it.

### Distinguish Task, Process, And Self-Regulation Feedback

Feedback can address the task itself, the processes used, or the learner's self-regulation. Different gaps require different levels. Task feedback fixes the immediate work; process feedback improves how the learner approaches similar work; self-regulation feedback builds the learner's ability to monitor and adjust. Matching the level to the gap makes feedback efficient.

Distinguish levels by:

- using task feedback for specific errors in the current work;
- using process feedback when the learner's approach needs improvement;
- using self-regulation feedback to build monitoring and strategy use;
- escalating from task to process to self-regulation as the learner develops;
- avoiding feedback stuck at the task level when the gap is in process.

### Avoid Feedback That Targets The Self

Feedback directed at the person, "you're so smart," "good girl," either pleases without advancing or threatens without informing. Self-referential feedback shifts attention from the task to the ego and undermines the focus on improvement. Feedback should address the work, not the learner.

Avoid self-feedback by:

- addressing the work and the thinking, not the person;
- avoiding praise that attributes success to ability or talent;
- avoiding criticism that implies fixed deficiency;
- focusing on what the work shows and what it needs;
- keeping attention on the gap and how to close it.

### Ensure Feedback Is Used, Not Just Given

Feedback only advances learning if the learner acts on it. Feedback that is read and filed changes nothing. Building in time, structures, and expectations for using feedback, through revision, correction, or application, converts feedback from comment into learning.

Ensure use by:

- building time for acting on feedback into the workflow;
- requiring revision or correction in response to feedback;
- having students set goals based on feedback;
- checking that feedback was understood and applied;
- treating feedback as a step in learning, not a conclusion.

### Calibrate Feedback Volume To The Learner And Task

Too much feedback overwhelms and paralyzes; too little leaves the learner without direction. The right volume depends on the learner's capacity, the task's complexity, and the timing. Calibrating volume ensures feedback is digestible and acted upon.

Calibrate volume by:

- focusing on one or two high-leverage points, not every issue;
- prioritizing feedback that addresses the most important gaps;
- matching volume to the learner's capacity to use it;
- using feedback that builds rather than overwhelms;
- reserving comprehensive feedback for summative moments.

### Use Praise Sparingly And Strategically

Praise feels supportive but can crowd out the feedback that advances learning and can shift attention to the self. When praise is used, it should be specific, effort- and strategy-oriented, and secondary to the descriptive feedback that directs improvement.

Use praise by:

- keeping praise specific and tied to effort, strategy, or progress;
- avoiding empty or generic praise that pleases without informing;
- ensuring praise does not replace descriptive feedback;
- avoiding praise that attributes success to ability;
- using praise to reinforce productive effort and strategy, not to reward.

### Build Students' Capacity To Seek And Use Feedback

The ultimate goal is learners who seek, interpret, and use feedback independently. Instruction should build this capacity by teaching students to request feedback, to self-assess, and to use feedback to set goals. This shifts feedback from a service provided to a skill developed.

Build capacity by:

- teaching students to self-assess against criteria before feedback;
- having students request specific feedback on areas of concern;
- teaching students to interpret and prioritize feedback;
- having students set goals based on feedback received;
- developing students' ability to generate their own feedback.

## Common Traps

### Evaluative Not Descriptive

Feedback that grades or judges rather than describing the gap and the next step.

### Vague Feedback

Generic comments that give the learner nothing specific to act on.

### Poorly Timed

Feedback given too late for the learner to act on it.

### Wrong Level

Task feedback when the gap is in process, or self-regulation feedback when the gap is in the task.

### Self-Referential

Praise or criticism aimed at the person, shifting attention to ego.

### Given But Not Used

Feedback that is read and filed without time or expectation to act.

### Overwhelming Volume

So much feedback that the learner cannot process or act on it.

### Praise Instead Of Feedback

Praise that pleases without advancing or directing improvement.

## Self-Check

- Does feedback aim at closing the gap between current work and the success criteria?
- Is feedback specific and actionable, naming concrete next steps rather than vague directions?
- Is feedback timed so the learner can act on it during the work, not only after?
- Is the feedback level, task, process, or self-regulation, matched to the gap?
- Does feedback address the work rather than the person?
- Is time built in for students to act on feedback through revision or application?
- Is the volume calibrated to what the learner can digest and use?
- Is praise specific, effort- and strategy-oriented, and secondary to descriptive feedback?
- Are students being taught to seek, interpret, and use feedback independently?
- Does feedback advance learning rather than merely describe or judge it?
