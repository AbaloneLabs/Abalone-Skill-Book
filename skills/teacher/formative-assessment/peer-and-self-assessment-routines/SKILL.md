# Peer And Self-Assessment Routines

Peer and self-assessment are not shortcuts for the teacher; they are learning activities in their own right. When a student applies criteria to a piece of work, their own or a classmate's, they internalize what quality looks like, notice gaps in their own understanding, and develop the self-regulation that lets them improve without a teacher standing over them. But these routines fail predictably when they are launched without structure. Untrained peer reviewers default to empty praise, "good job," or unkind criticism, "this is bad," neither of which helps anyone improve. Students asked to self-assess without clear criteria either inflate, to protect themselves, or deflate, because they lack confidence. The difference between a routine that builds independent learners and one that wastes class time is preparation: clear criteria, explicit training, structured protocols, and a teacher who treats feedback as a skill to be taught, not a chore to be assigned.

Use this skill before launching any peer review, self-assessment, reflection, or goal-setting routine. The goal is to prevent the agent from handing students a rubric and assuming useful feedback will follow, and instead to build the scaffolding that makes peer and self-assessment genuinely instructive.

## Core Rules

### Make Criteria Public And Concrete Before Any Assessment Happens

Students cannot assess against criteria they cannot see or do not understand. The criteria must be made explicit, in student-friendly language, before the work begins, not revealed only at grading time.

Establish criteria by:

- sharing the rubric, success criteria, or checklist when the task is assigned;
- translating teacher language into student-friendly descriptions of quality;
- anchoring abstract criteria with exemplars at different levels;
- having students paraphrase the criteria in their own words to confirm understanding;
- referring to the criteria consistently during instruction, not only at assessment time.

Peer and self-assessment built on vague or hidden criteria produces feedback that reflects taste, not the learning target.

### Teach Feedback As A Skill, Not Assume It

Giving useful feedback is a learned skill. Students default to praise or blame because they have not been taught otherwise. Treat feedback as content to be explicitly instructed, modeled, and practiced.

Teach feedback through:

- modeling the difference between praise, judgment, and descriptive feedback;
- showing examples of weak feedback, "good job," "I liked it," and rewriting them into useful feedback;
- giving sentence stems or frames, such as "your claim is strong because; to improve, you could";
- practicing on anonymous or teacher-created samples before live peer work;
- debriefing what made feedback useful or not after each round.

A class that has never been taught how to give feedback will not magically produce it because you assigned peer review.

### Use Structured Protocols That Constrain The Feedback

Open-ended peer review invites drift. A structured protocol narrows attention to specific criteria and forces concrete, criteria-referenced comments.

Use protocols such as:

- glow and grow: one strength tied to a criterion, one area to improve tied to a criterion;
- TAG: tell something you liked, ask a question, give a suggestion;
- two stars and a wish: two specific positives, one specific change;
- claim-evidence-reasoning check: does the work have each, and where;
- PQP: praise, question, polish;
- specific protocol rounds, such as first read for ideas, second read for evidence, third read for conventions.

The structure should match the learning target. A protocol that asks for general reactions will produce general reactions.

### Anchor Every Comment In The Criteria

The single most important rule of useful feedback is that it references the criteria, not the reviewer's preference. "I liked it" is about the reviewer; "your evidence supports the claim but you do not explain how" is about the work against the standard.

Require reviewers to:

- name the specific criterion they are responding to;
- point to the exact place in the work;
- describe what they see, not just judge it;
- suggest a concrete next step tied to the criterion;
- avoid comments on things outside the criteria, such as neatness when the target is reasoning.

When feedback is criteria-referenced, it becomes actionable. When it is preference-referenced, it becomes noise.

### Build Self-Assessment As A Multi-Stage Habit

Self-assessment is most powerful when it happens throughout the work, not only at the end. A student who checks their work against criteria while drafting catches problems the teacher would otherwise mark later.

Build self-assessment into the process:

- before starting, have students predict what will be hard and set a goal tied to a criterion;
- during drafting, have them self-check against a student-friendly checklist;
- before submitting, have them highlight where they met each criterion and flag where they are unsure;
- after feedback, have them write a brief reflection on what they will revise and why;
- over time, have them track patterns in their own strengths and growth areas.

Self-assessment at only the end becomes a performance for the teacher. Self-assessment throughout becomes a thinking habit.

### Prevent Empty Praise And Harshness

The two failure modes of peer feedback are praise that communicates nothing and criticism that wounds without helping. Both must be actively countered.

Counter empty praise by:

- banning pure evaluative phrases, "good," "nice," "I liked," unless followed by a specific reason;
- requiring every positive comment to name the criterion and the evidence;
- modeling how to find genuine strengths even in weak work.

Counter harshness by:

- framing feedback as help toward a shared standard, not judgment of the person;
- requiring suggestions to be specific and actionable, not global labels;
- teaching the difference between describing a problem and attacking the author;
- establishing norms that feedback is about the work, never about the writer;
- monitoring early rounds closely and intervening when tone slips.

A peer review culture that tolerates either emptiness or cruelty will not produce learning.

### Protect Dignity, Privacy, And Safety

Peer review exposes students to each other's judgment, which can be socially risky, especially for struggling students, multilingual learners, and students with anxiety or disabilities. The routine must be safe or students will not be honest.

Protect students by:

- using anonymized or de-identified work where possible, especially early in the year;
- allowing students to opt out of sharing sensitive personal writing;
- training the class in respectful, criteria-focused response;
- avoiding public reading of weak work in a way that identifies the author;
- following school policy and FERPA or local equivalent on what may be shared;
- being alert to social dynamics, such as who is paired with whom, and adjusting pairings to prevent harm.

Safety is a precondition for honest self-assessment and useful peer feedback.

### Use The Teacher To Calibrate And Coach, Not To Replace

The teacher's role in peer and self-assessment is to train, calibrate, coach, and quality-check, not to step in and do the feedback whenever it is imperfect.

The teacher should:

- model and teach the feedback skill explicitly;
- circulate during peer review to coach the reviewer, not to fix the work;
- spot-check peer feedback for accuracy and usefulness;
- calibrate student interpretations of criteria by discussing disagreements;
- provide their own feedback alongside peer feedback, especially for high-stakes work;
- resist the temptation to override peer feedback that is criteria-referenced even if imperfect, using it instead as a teaching moment.

Peer feedback does not replace teacher feedback; it multiplies the feedback a student receives and builds the student's own evaluative skill.

### Close The Loop With Revision

Peer and self-assessment that are not followed by revision teach students that feedback is a ritual, not a tool for improvement. Build revision into the routine.

Ensure that:

- students have time and a clear expectation to act on the feedback;
- revision is tied to specific criteria, not a generic "make it better";
- students can show what changed and why;
- the teacher acknowledges growth that results from acting on feedback;
- over time, students set their own revision goals based on patterns in feedback.

Without revision, the entire routine collapses into busywork that students learn to perform without believing.

## Common Traps

### Handing Out A Rubric And Hoping

Distributing criteria without training produces feedback that reflects taste, not the target. Teach the criteria and the feedback skill explicitly.

### Empty Praise

"Good job" communicates nothing and lets the reviewer off the hook. Require criteria-referenced, evidence-based comments.

### Harsh Or Personal Criticism

Global negative labels wound without helping and destroy safety. Require specific, actionable, work-focused suggestions.

### Self-Assessment Only At The End

End-only self-assessment becomes a performance for the teacher. Build it into drafting and revision.

### No Time To Revise

Feedback without revision teaches students that feedback is a ritual. Always build in time to act on it.

### Public Exposure Of Weak Work

Identifying the author of struggling work shames students and poisons future honesty. Anonymize and protect dignity.

### Letting The Teacher Replace Peer Feedback

Stepping in to fix every imperfection prevents students from developing evaluative skill. Coach the reviewer instead.

### Unstructured "Give Each Other Feedback"

Open-ended review invites drift and off-target comments. Use a structured protocol matched to the learning target.

## Self-Check

- Are the criteria public, concrete, and in student-friendly language before any peer or self-assessment begins?
- Has feedback been explicitly taught and modeled, with weak examples rewritten into useful ones?
- Does a structured protocol constrain feedback to specific criteria rather than general reactions?
- Is every required comment anchored in a criterion and supported by evidence from the work?
- Is self-assessment built into the work process, not only performed at the end?
- Are empty praise and harshness actively countered through norms, stems, and monitoring?
- Are dignity, privacy, FERPA or local policy, and social safety protected, especially for vulnerable students?
- Is the teacher coaching reviewers and calibrating criteria rather than replacing peer feedback?
- Is there protected time for students to revise based on the feedback they receive?
- Can students point to growth that resulted from acting on peer or self-assessment feedback?
