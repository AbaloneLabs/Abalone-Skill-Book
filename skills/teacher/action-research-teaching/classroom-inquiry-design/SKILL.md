# Classroom Inquiry Design

Classroom inquiry, or teacher action research, is the practice of systematically studying your own teaching to improve it. It is powerful because it positions the teacher as an investigator of their own practice rather than a consumer of others' research, and because it produces insight grounded in the specific context that matters most: your own students. But inquiry fails predictably when the question is poorly framed. A question too broad cannot be studied, a question too vague cannot be answered, a question that is really a complaint cannot generate change, and a question with no connection to actual practice produces a report that changes nothing. Designing the inquiry well is most of the work.

This skill covers the judgment needed to design classroom inquiry effectively: framing a meaningful question, scoping it to be studiable, connecting it to practice, planning the cycle, and avoiding the common failures of question design. The goal is an inquiry that actually produces usable insight rather than an exercise that consumes effort and changes nothing.

## Core Rules

### Start From A Genuine Problem Of Practice, Not From A Topic

The strongest inquiry questions emerge from a real tension in your teaching: a lesson that consistently fails, a group of students who are not learning, a practice you suspect is not working, a gap between what you intend and what happens. Start by identifying a specific problem you actually face, not by selecting a topic that sounds researchable. Topic-first inquiry produces studies that feel academic and change little; problem-first inquiry produces studies that address something real. Ask yourself what is genuinely puzzling or frustrating in your practice, and let that drive the question.

### Frame The Question So It Can Actually Be Studied

A researchable question is specific, bounded, and answerable with evidence you can realistically gather. "How can I improve student engagement" is not researchable; it is a wish. "Does using think-pair-share before whole-class discussion increase the number of students who contribute orally" is researchable, because you can observe contribution rates before and after the change. Test your question by asking what evidence would answer it: if you cannot imagine concrete evidence, the question is not yet researchable. Refine toward specificity and observability until the question points to data you can actually collect.

### Scope The Inquiry To What Is Feasible In A Real Classroom

Teacher research happens inside a full teaching load, not in a laboratory, and over-scoped inquiries collapse under their own ambition. Scope the inquiry to what you can realistically sustain: a focused question, a manageable data collection method, a bounded timeframe, a specific class or group. A small inquiry completed well produces more insight than a large one abandoned halfway. Resist the temptation to study everything about a problem; choose the slice that is both meaningful and feasible, and design the inquiry to fit your actual life.

### Connect The Question To A Specific Change You Can Make

Inquiry that only describes a problem without connecting to a change you can make produces understanding without improvement. Frame the question so that the answer points toward action: if I learn X, I will try Y. This action-orientation is what distinguishes action research from generic research, and it ensures the inquiry serves practice rather than merely documenting it. A question like "why do my students struggle with word problems" is descriptive; "what happens to word-problem success if I teach an explicit comprehension routine first" is action-oriented and connects insight to a change you control.

### Plan The Full Cycle Before Starting To Collect Data

A complete inquiry cycle includes the question, the baseline evidence, the change or intervention, the evidence after the change, the analysis, and the decision about what to do next. Plan the whole cycle before starting, so that data collection is purposeful rather than incidental. Knowing what you will compare against what determines what you need to collect, and planning it in advance prevents the common failure of reaching the end with the wrong data or no baseline. The plan can evolve, but starting with a full-cycle design keeps the inquiry coherent.

### Triangulate Data Sources To Avoid Misleading Conclusions

Any single data source can mislead: assessment results may reflect factors other than your change, student self-reports may reflect preference rather than learning, your own impressions may be biased. Triangulate across multiple sources, such as assessment data, observation, student work, and student feedback, so that conclusions rest on converging evidence rather than a single possibly-anomalous source. Triangulation is especially important when the inquiry touches on student perceptions or engagement, which are easy to misread from any one angle.

### Distinguish What Your Evidence Can And Cannot Show

Teacher inquiry produces evidence about your specific context and is not a controlled experiment. Be honest about the limits of what your evidence can show: it can suggest that a change worked for your students in your context, but it cannot prove generalizable causation the way a controlled study can. This honesty matters both for interpreting your own results and for sharing with colleagues. Overclaiming from a small inquiry undermines credibility; appropriately scoped claims, "this worked for my students and may be worth your trying," are both more honest and more useful.

### Build Inquiry Into Practice Rather Than Adding It On Top

Inquiry that is added on top of a full teaching load is abandoned under pressure. Design inquiry to integrate with practice you already do: gather evidence through assessments you already give, observations you already make, and conversations you already have, rather than inventing entirely new data collection. Integrated inquiry is sustainable; bolted-on inquiry is not. The aim is a practice of inquiry that persists across years, not a single heroic study.

## Common Traps

### Topic-First Instead Of Problem-First Inquiry

Topic-first inquiry feels academic and changes little. Start from a genuine problem of practice.

### Questions Too Vague To Be Studied

"How can I improve engagement" is a wish. Refine toward specificity and observability.

### Over-Scoped Inquiry That Collapses

Real classrooms cannot sustain over-ambitious studies. Scope to what is feasible.

### Descriptive Questions With No Action Connection

Understanding without improvement serves little. Connect the question to a change you can make.

### Collecting Data Without Planning The Full Cycle

Without baseline and comparison plans, data cannot answer the question. Plan the whole cycle first.

### Relying On A Single Data Source

Any one source can mislead. Triangulate across assessment, observation, work, and feedback.

### Overclaiming From A Small Context-Bound Study

Teacher inquiry suggests, it does not prove generalizable causation. Scope claims honestly.

### Inquiry Added On Top Of An Already Full Load

Bolted-on inquiry is abandoned under pressure. Integrate it with existing practice.

## Self-Check

- Does my question emerge from a genuine problem of practice rather than a researchable-sounding topic?
- Is the question specific, bounded, and answerable with evidence I can realistically gather?
- Is the inquiry scoped to what I can sustain inside a real teaching load?
- Does the question connect to a specific change I can make in my practice?
- Did I plan the full cycle, including baseline and comparison, before collecting data?
- Am I triangulating across multiple data sources rather than relying on one?
- Am I honest about what my context-bound evidence can and cannot show?
- Is the inquiry integrated with practice I already do rather than added on top?
