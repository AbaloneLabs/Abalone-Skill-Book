# Inquiry Cycles And Analysis

Collecting data is only the middle of an inquiry; what gives inquiry its power is the analysis that turns data into insight and the cycle that turns insight into the next change. This is where inquiry most often stalls. Teachers gather data, glance at it, form a quick impression, and move on, treating the data as confirmation of what they already believed rather than as evidence to be examined. Or they run one cycle, declare success or failure, and stop, when the real value of inquiry comes from successive cycles that compound learning over time. Analysis and cycling are what separate inquiry that transforms practice from inquiry that produces a folder of unused data.

This skill covers the judgment needed to analyze inquiry data well, draw honest conclusions, run successive cycles, share findings responsibly, and convert each cycle into the next improvement. The goal is inquiry that compounds into sustained growth rather than inquiry that produces a single report.

## Core Rules

### Analyze The Data Before Forming Conclusions

The natural sequence is backwards: form an impression, then look at the data to confirm it. The disciplined sequence is to examine the data openly first, asking what it actually shows, before deciding what it means. This means sitting with the data, looking for patterns, comparing baseline to post-change, examining surprises and contradictions, and only then interpreting. Analysis that serves a pre-formed conclusion is not analysis; it is rationalization. Approach the data as if you do not already know the answer, because the value of inquiry is precisely in what you did not expect.

### Look For Patterns, Outliers, And Differences Across Groups

Useful analysis looks at multiple levels: the overall pattern, differences across groups of students, and the outliers that complicate the pattern. An intervention that improved results on average may have helped some students and not others, or helped some and harmed others, and the group-level view reveals this. Outliers, the students for whom the change did not work or worked dramatically, often contain the most useful insight about when and for whom the approach works. Analysis that looks only at the average misses the texture that makes the finding actionable.

### Distinguish What The Evidence Supports From What You Hope

Inquiry is vulnerable to motivated reasoning: teachers want their changes to work, and they read ambiguous evidence favorably. Discipline yourself to distinguish what the evidence clearly supports from what it might suggest from what you hope is true. State the limits honestly: "the evidence suggests improvement for most students but is inconclusive for the lowest-performing group" is more useful than "it worked." Honest, bounded conclusions are more credible and more useful for deciding next steps than enthusiastic overclaims.

### Use The Analysis To Decide The Next Change, Not Just To Judge The Last One

The point of analyzing a cycle is not to declare the change a success or failure; it is to decide what to do next. A change that worked suggests deepening or extending it; a change that worked for some students suggests adapting it for others; a change that did not work suggests a different approach. Frame the analysis around the question "given what I learned, what should I try next," which keeps inquiry moving forward rather than ending in a verdict. Inquiry that produces a verdict but no next change has produced reflection without improvement.

### Run Successive Cycles That Compound Learning

A single inquiry cycle produces a single insight; successive cycles compound into deep understanding of a problem and its solutions. After each cycle, build on what was learned: refine the question, adjust the change, collect new evidence. Over several cycles, you develop a far richer understanding than any single study could produce, and the practice itself becomes more skilled. Plan inquiry as a sequence of cycles rather than a one-off study, because the compounding across cycles is where the real growth happens.

### Share Findings With Colleagues Responsibly

Sharing inquiry findings contributes to the profession and invites useful challenge, but it must be done responsibly. Be explicit about the context and limits of your findings, so colleagues do not over-generalize from your specific situation. Share the method as well as the conclusion, so others can judge the quality of the evidence and adapt the approach. Present findings as "what I tried and what I found" rather than as universal prescriptions. Responsible sharing spreads useful practice without overclaiming, and it invites the collegial challenge that strengthens your own thinking.

### Use Inquiry To Question, Not Just To Confirm

The most valuable inquiry disrupts rather than confirms. If your inquiries consistently confirm that what you were already doing is working, the inquiry is probably not rigorous enough to be useful. Design inquiry to genuinely test your assumptions, including the possibility that your practice is not working as you believe. Inquiry that could change your mind is inquiry that could change your practice; inquiry that can only confirm what you already believe is a ritual. Welcome findings that surprise or unsettle, because they are where real learning lives.

### Connect Inquiry Findings To Broader Research And Practice

Your classroom inquiry does not happen in isolation; it connects to a broader body of research and practice. Connect your findings to what is known: does your evidence align with or challenge existing research, does it add texture to general findings, does it raise questions worth pursuing. This connection both strengthens your interpretation and contributes your context-bound insight to the larger conversation. Inquiry that ignores the broader field risks reinventing or contradicting without awareness; inquiry that connects to it becomes part of accumulated knowledge.

## Common Traps

### Confirming A Pre-Formed Conclusion

Reading data to support what you already believe is rationalization. Examine data openly first.

### Looking Only At The Average

Averages hide group differences and outliers. Analyze patterns across groups and exceptions.

### Overclaiming From Ambiguous Evidence

Hope drives favorable reading. Distinguish what the evidence supports from what you hope.

### Ending In A Verdict Instead Of A Next Step

Judging the last change without deciding the next produces reflection without improvement.

### One-Off Inquiry With No Successive Cycles

Single cycles produce single insights. Run successive cycles that compound.

### Sharing Findings As Universal Prescriptions

Your context is specific. Share method, limits, and context responsibly.

### Inquiry That Only Confirms

Inquiry that cannot change your mind cannot change your practice. Welcome unsettling findings.

### Ignoring The Broader Research Field

Your inquiry connects to accumulated knowledge. Relate findings to existing research and practice.

## Self-Check

- Did I examine the data openly before forming conclusions?
- Did I analyze patterns across groups and outliers, not just the overall average?
- Did I distinguish what the evidence supports from what I hope is true?
- Does my analysis lead to a next change, not just a verdict on the last one?
- Am I planning successive cycles that compound learning rather than a one-off study?
- When sharing findings, do I present context, method, and limits responsibly?
- Is my inquiry designed to genuinely test assumptions rather than only confirm them?
- Do I connect my findings to broader research and practice rather than treating them in isolation?
