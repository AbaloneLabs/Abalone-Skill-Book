# Mayer Principles Of Multimedia Learning

Mayer's principles of multimedia learning are a research-based set of guidelines for designing instructional materials that combine words and images, such as videos, animations, slideshows, and interactive media. They rest on a well-supported finding: people learn better from words and relevant images together than from words alone, but only when the multimedia is designed to manage the learner's limited cognitive capacity. The recurring failure in multimedia design is to add elements that seem to enhance, decorative images, background music, on-screen text matching the narration, interesting tangents, that actually overload the learner and reduce learning. The result is a polished, engaging presentation that teaches less than a simpler one would have.

The opposite failure is to abandon multimedia altogether, missing its genuine power for teaching content that is visual, spatial, dynamic, or otherwise hard to convey in words alone. The design challenge is to apply Mayer's principles to build multimedia that uses images and words together in ways that manage cognitive load and produce learning. This skill applies whenever the work is to design or evaluate instructional multimedia.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Multimedia Principle: Words And Relevant Images Beat Words Alone

People learn better from words and relevant images than from words alone, because words and images are processed in different channels and together they build richer mental representations. Use visuals that illustrate the content, such as diagrams, charts, illustrations, or animations, alongside your verbal explanation, rather than words alone. The key word is relevant: the image must support the content, not merely decorate it.

An abstract concept explained with words plus a relevant diagram is learned better than the same concept in words alone. But a relevant diagram replaced by a stock photo of smiling people adds nothing and may distract.

### Apply The Coherence Principle: Exclude Interesting But Irrelevant Material

People learn better when extraneous material is excluded rather than included. Decorative images, background music, interesting but irrelevant details, and entertaining tangents that do not serve the learning goal all impose cognitive load without contributing to learning, and they actively reduce it. The temptation to add engaging extras is strong because they make the material feel richer, but the research is clear: they hurt learning.

Be ruthless about coherence. Every element, every image, every word, every sound, should earn its place by serving the learning goal. Anything that does not serve it should be cut, however attractive or interesting.

### Apply The Signaling Principle: Highlight Essential Material

People learn better when essential material is highlighted or signaled, because cues direct the learner's limited attention to what matters and away from what does not. Use signals such as headings, arrows, color coding, emphasis, or spoken cues ("note this part") to direct attention to the key elements. Without signals, learners may attend to the wrong things or miss the essential relationships.

Use signals sparingly and consistently; over-signaling creates noise. The goal is to guide attention, not to decorate with arrows.

### Apply The Modality Principle: Present Animation With Narration, Not On-Screen Text

People learn better from graphics with narration than from graphics with on-screen text, when the graphic is dynamic or complex, because narration enters through the auditory channel while the visual channel processes the graphic, avoiding overload of the visual channel. When the same graphic is accompanied by on-screen text, the learner must split visual attention between the text and the graphic, overloading the visual channel and reducing learning.

Present complex or dynamic visuals with spoken narration rather than on-screen text. Reserve on-screen text for key terms, labels, or content that must be referenced precisely.

### Apply The Redundancy Principle: Avoid Narration Plus Duplicate On-Screen Text

People learn worse when on-screen text duplicates the narration, especially with a complex visual, because the learner must process the same words twice, in text and in speech, while also processing the visual, overloading the system. The common instinct to put the script on screen so learners can read along actually reduces learning for complex visual content.

Avoid duplicating narration as on-screen text. If text is needed, use it for labels, key terms, or organization, not for the full spoken script.

### Apply The Spatial Contiguity Principle: Place Text Near The Image It Describes

People learn better when corresponding text and images are presented near each other rather than far apart. When a label or explanation is separated from its image, the learner must expend cognitive effort holding one in mind while searching for the other, which is extraneous load. Place labels on or immediately next to the elements they label, and place explanations near the relevant images.

Avoid the common design of an image on one part of the screen and its explanation elsewhere, which forces the learner to integrate across distance.

### Apply The Temporal Contiguity Principle: Present Corresponding Words And Images Simultaneously

People learn better when corresponding narration and animation are presented simultaneously rather than successively. When the narration comes first and the animation after, or vice versa, the learner must hold one in mind while waiting for the other, which overloads memory. Synchronize the narration with the relevant visual moment.

In an instructional video, the spoken explanation of a visual element should occur as that element appears and is demonstrated, not before or after.

### Apply The Segmenting And Pre-Training Principles For Complex Content

People learn better from a complex multimedia lesson when it is broken into learner-paced segments rather than presented as a continuous whole (segmenting), and when they already know the names and characteristics of key components (pre-training). A continuous complex presentation overwhelms; breaking it into segments the learner can control allows processing at a manageable pace, and pre-training on key terms prevents the learner from struggling with vocabulary while also learning the concept.

For complex content, segment the presentation, allow learner pacing, and pre-teach the key terms and components so the main lesson can focus on the relationships and processes.

### Apply The Personalization And Voice Principles Cautiously

People often learn better when multimedia uses conversational rather than formal language (personalization) and a friendly human voice rather than a machine voice, because these create social presence that encourages effort. However, apply these cautiously and do not over-personalize to the point of distraction. The core load-management principles, coherence, modality, signaling, matter more than voice and style.

Do not let personalization become entertainment or violate coherence. A clear, somewhat conversational narration in a real voice serves; excessive informality or distracting delivery does not.

## Common Traps

### Decorative Elements That Distract

Stock photos, background music, and interesting tangents feel engaging but reduce learning. Apply coherence.

### On-Screen Text Duplicating Narration

Reading-along text with a complex visual overloads the visual channel. Apply modality and redundancy principles.

### Labels Separated From Images

Text far from its image forces integration across distance. Apply spatial contiguity.

### Narration Out Of Sync With Visuals

Words and images presented successively overload memory. Apply temporal contiguity.

### Over-Signaling

Too many arrows and highlights become noise. Signal sparingly and consistently.

### Continuous Complex Presentations

A long unbroken complex animation overwhelms. Segment and allow learner pacing.

### Assuming Engagement Means Learning

Polished multimedia can captivate without teaching. Measure learning, not engagement.

### Abandoning Multimedia For Words Alone

Missing multimedia's power for visual and dynamic content is the opposite error. Use words and relevant images together.

## Self-Check

- Does the material combine words and relevant images rather than words alone or images that merely decorate?
- Has extraneous material, decorative images, music, tangents, been excluded to preserve coherence?
- Are essential elements signaled to guide attention without over-signaling?
- For complex or dynamic visuals, is narration used rather than on-screen text, to manage the visual channel?
- Is on-screen text kept to labels and key terms rather than duplicating the narration?
- Are text labels placed near the images they describe, avoiding distant integration?
- Is narration synchronized with the corresponding visual moments?
- Is complex content segmented and learner-paced, with key terms pre-taught?
- Is personalization applied cautiously, without violating coherence or becoming entertainment?
- Could you defend each multimedia element as serving learning rather than as engaging decoration?
