# Feedback Timing And Feedback Loops

The power of feedback depends less on what it says than on when it arrives and whether the learner can still act on it. Feedback delivered at the end of a unit, after a grade is final, or after the work can no longer be revised is feedback that cannot be used; it becomes a record of judgment rather than a cause of learning. Conversely, feedback given too early, before the learner has wrestled with the task, can short-circuit the thinking that produces durable learning. The art of feedback timing is placing comments at the points in a work cycle where they can change what the student does next, while balancing that against the real constraint that a teacher cannot give rich individual feedback on every draft of every assignment without burning out. Designing feedback as a loop, with revision built in, rather than as a one-time delivery at the end, is what makes feedback actually move learning.

Use this skill when planning the rhythm of feedback across a unit, task, or revision cycle, and when deciding how often, how deeply, and at what points to give feedback. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating feedback as a single end-of-task event and from ignoring the workload reality that makes unsustainable feedback plans collapse.

## Core Rules

### Deliver Feedback While Revision Is Still Possible

The single most important timing rule is that feedback must arrive before the window for using it closes. If a student receives comments only when the essay is returned with a final grade, the comments describe what was wrong but offer no chance to improve, and the learning effect is minimal. Plan feedback at points in the work cycle where the student can still revise: after a first draft, during a practice attempt, before a performance, between iterations of a project.

This requires designing tasks with feedback points built into them, not appending feedback after a finished product. A task that has only one submission, the final graded version, has no place for usable feedback by construction. Build drafts, checkpoints, or practice rounds into the task structure so that feedback has somewhere to land.

### Build Revision Loops, Not Single Deliveries

A feedback loop is a cycle: the student produces work, receives feedback, revises, and produces improved work. A single delivery of feedback at the end is not a loop; it is a terminus. Loops are what produce growth because the student experiences the connection between feedback and improvement. Design at least one revision opportunity into major tasks, and make the revision a real event with time and expectation attached, not an optional afterthought.

The loop also changes how students receive feedback. When they know they will revise, they read comments as instructions for the next version rather than as final judgment, which lowers defensiveness and increases use. The expectation of revision is itself a motivator to attend to feedback.

### Avoid Feedback So Early It Removes The Thinking

Timing is not simply "earlier is better." Feedback given before a learner has struggled with the task can prevent the productive difficulty that builds understanding. If a teacher corrects every misconception the moment it appears, the student never practices detecting and repairing their own errors, which is a core part of learning. Allow a productive attempt first, then intervene with feedback that addresses what the attempt revealed.

The balance is task- and learner-dependent. Novices working on complex or high-stakes tasks may need earlier scaffolding; more proficient learners benefit from being left to wrestle longer before feedback arrives. Calibrate the timing to the learner's readiness and the task's demands, not to a fixed rule.

### Match Feedback Frequency To What Is Sustainable

A teacher cannot give rich, individualized, written feedback on every draft of every assignment for every student without collapsing under the load. Unrealistic feedback plans fail not because the feedback is bad but because it cannot be sustained, and unsustainable plans get abandoned or degraded into perfunctory comments. Be honest about the workload and design feedback frequency that can actually be maintained across a term.

Strategies to keep frequency sustainable include rotating which assignments receive deep individual feedback, using whole-class feedback on common patterns rather than individual comments on every paper, employing peer feedback for some rounds, using focused single-criterion feedback rather than comprehensive marking, and using self-assessment routines that shift part of the feedback load to the student. The goal is not less feedback but feedback placed where it does the most good at a cost the teacher can bear.

### Separate Feedback From Grading So Improvement Precedes Judgment

When feedback arrives only as part of a grade, students tend to read the number and ignore the comments, and the chance to improve is gone. Where possible, give feedback on a draft or practice attempt, allow revision, and grade the revised version. This lets the student experience feedback as a cause of improvement rather than as a final verdict, and it often produces stronger final work and more honest grades that reflect what the student ultimately learned rather than their first attempt.

This is not always possible for every task, but it should be the default for major assignments. Reserve end-only feedback for genuinely terminal assessments where revision is not part of the learning goal.

### Use Whole-Class Feedback For Common Patterns

Not every useful piece of feedback needs to be individual. When several students make the same error or share the same misconception, whole-class feedback addressed to the pattern is efficient and often more effective than repeating the same comment on many papers. Identify the two or three most common issues across a set of work, address them to the whole class with examples, and reserve individual comments for what is unique to each student.

Whole-class feedback also normalizes struggle: when students hear that many classmates faced the same challenge, the issue becomes a learning target rather than a personal failing. Pair it with a brief individual follow-up so each student knows how the pattern applies to their own work.

### Close The Loop By Checking Whether Feedback Was Used

A feedback loop is incomplete until the teacher sees whether the feedback changed the next version of the work. Build in a quick check: did the student act on the starred comment, did the revision address the named issue, did the misconception persist? This check tells the teacher whether the feedback was understood and whether reteaching is needed, and it signals to students that feedback is expected to be used, not merely received.

Closing the loop does not require re-grading everything. A brief look at whether the targeted change was made is often enough to confirm that the loop functioned and to plan the next move.

### Sequence Feedback Across A Unit, Not Just Within A Task

Think about feedback timing across an entire unit, not only within a single assignment. Early in a unit, feedback should be frequent, low-stakes, and focused on foundational skills so that errors are caught before they consolidate. Mid-unit, feedback can address more complex integration as students combine skills. Late in a unit, feedback should focus on transfer and refinement as students approach summative performance. Sequencing feedback to the arc of learning ensures that the right kinds of comments arrive at the moments they can do the most good.

## Common Traps

### Feedback Only At The End

Comments delivered with the final grade cannot be used and become recordkeeping. Build feedback into the work cycle while revision is possible.

### No Built-In Revision Opportunity

A task with only a final submission has no place for usable feedback. Design drafts or checkpoints into major assignments.

### Feedback So Early It Removes Productive Struggle

Intervening before the learner attempts the task prevents the thinking that builds understanding. Allow a real attempt first.

### Unsustainable Feedback Loads

Planning rich individual feedback on every draft of every assignment guarantees collapse into perfunctory comments. Match frequency to what is sustainable.

### Feedback Fused With Grading

When comments come only with a grade, students read the number and ignore the words. Separate feedback from grading where possible.

### Repeating The Same Comment On Many Papers

When an error is common, whole-class feedback on the pattern is more efficient and often more effective than individual repetition.

### Never Checking Whether Feedback Was Used

An open loop teaches students that feedback is optional. Close the loop by checking whether the next version reflects the comment.

### Uniform Timing Regardless Of Learner Or Task

The right timing depends on the learner's readiness and the task's demands. Calibrate rather than applying a fixed rule.

## Self-Check

- Is feedback scheduled to arrive while students can still revise, rather than only at the final grade?
- Does at least one major task per unit include a real revision loop with time and expectation attached?
- Is feedback withheld long enough to allow productive struggle, especially for more proficient learners?
- Is the feedback frequency realistic for the teacher's load across the full term, sustained by rotation, peer, whole-class, or self-assessment strategies?
- Where possible, is feedback separated from grading so students experience improvement before judgment?
- Are common patterns addressed through whole-class feedback rather than repeated individually?
- Is there a step that closes the loop by checking whether students acted on the feedback in their next version?
- Is feedback sequenced across the unit to match the arc from foundational skills to integration to transfer?
