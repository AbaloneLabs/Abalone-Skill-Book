# Peer Feedback Routines

Peer feedback can be one of the most efficient ways to get specific, timely responses to student work, and also one of the most counterproductive activities a classroom can attempt. The difference is structure. Untrained students told to "give each other feedback" default to vague praise ("I liked it"), vague criticism ("it was confusing"), or opinion presented as fact, and the exercise wastes time or, worse, damages trust and confidence. Peer feedback works when it is built on a protocol, when students have been trained and shown what useful comments look like, when comments are anchored to shared criteria rather than personal taste, and when the classroom culture makes giving and receiving critique safe. Designing peer feedback as a routine, with explicit scaffolds and norms, is what turns it from a risky activity into a reliable learning engine.

Use this skill before planning any peer review, peer response, or peer feedback session, and when training students to give and use feedback from classmates. The goal is to prevent the agent from launching a peer feedback activity without the protocol, modeling, criteria anchor, and safety conditions that make it productive.

## Core Rules

### Never Launch Peer Feedback Without A Protocol

A protocol is a structured routine that tells students exactly what to say, in what order, and how. Without one, students improvise, and improvisation produces inconsistent, often unhelpful, sometimes hurtful comments. Choose or design a protocol that fits the task and the developmental level.

Common protocols include praise-question-polish, in which each responder names one thing that works, asks one clarifying question, and suggests one improvement; glow and grow, which separates strengths from areas to develop; and TAG, tell something you like, ask a question, give a suggestion. Each protocol constrains the response into named moves, which lowers the cognitive and social load on the responder and raises the quality and consistency of the feedback.

Match the protocol's complexity to the students. Younger or less experienced students need a simple, highly constrained protocol; older students can handle richer routines that ask for evidence-based critique. State the protocol steps on a visible anchor or handout so students can refer to them during the activity.

### Anchor All Peer Comments To Shared Criteria

Peer feedback becomes opinion the moment it detaches from criteria. "I think your essay is good" is opinion; "against the criterion of using evidence, your second paragraph works because the source directly supports the claim" is criteria-based feedback. Give students the rubric, success criteria, or a focused feedback guide before the session, and require that every comment reference a specific criterion.

A focused feedback guide narrows the task further by asking students to comment on only one or two criteria during a given session. This prevents overwhelm, deepens attention to the chosen dimensions, and makes the feedback more usable. Rotate the focus across sessions so that over time students practice giving feedback on every dimension of quality.

### Train Students Before Expecting Useful Feedback

Students are not born knowing how to give useful feedback, and most have received more bad feedback than good. Training is required, and it should happen before the first real peer feedback session, not be discovered missing during one. Model the protocol on a sample piece of work, thinking aloud as you form each comment against the criteria. Then have students practice on an anchor paper or exemplar, compare their comments to a model set, and discuss what made some comments more useful than others.

Treat the first few sessions as practice rather than high-stakes critique. Debrief after each session: which comments were most helpful and why, which were vague or unkind, and what the group can do better next time. Skill at giving feedback grows through deliberate practice and reflection, just like any other skill.

### Model What Useful Feedback Looks And Sounds Like

Students need concrete examples of strong and weak feedback. Show a weak comment, "this is boring," and revise it into a strong one, "the introduction lists facts but does not state a claim; adding a claim sentence would tell the reader your position." Have students sort a set of sample comments into useful and not-useful and justify their sort. The goal is to build a shared internal standard for what counts as a comment worth giving.

Make the features of useful feedback explicit: it references a criterion, it is specific about where in the work the issue occurs, it describes rather than merely judges, and it offers or invites a next step. Post these features as norms and return to them in every debrief.

### Build Safety And Respect Into The Routine

Peer feedback fails when students fear being shamed or when they feel licensed to be harsh. Establish norms before the first session: feedback addresses the work, never the person; comments are descriptive and criteria-based, not evaluative or personal; the receiver listens before responding; and the goal is to help the work improve, not to rank the writer. Reinforce these norms consistently and address violations immediately.

Structure also creates safety. Anonymous exchange can lower risk for sensitive work, though it must be managed to prevent cruelty behind anonymity. Small stable groups build trust over time better than random pairings for complex critique. Giving the receiver a script for responding, "thank you, I will consider that," reduces defensiveness. The teacher's role during the session is to circulate, coach, and intervene when comments drift from criteria to opinion or personality.

### Ensure The Receiver Can And Will Use The Feedback

Peer feedback that is given but not used teaches students that the activity is performative. Build in a step where the receiver decides which comments to act on and how. A simple routine is to have the receiver star the one or two comments they will use in revision and briefly note why, or to write a short revision plan based on the feedback. This closes the loop and signals that feedback is meant to change the work.

Do not require students to act on every comment. Part of learning to use feedback is judging which comments are most useful, and peers, like any reviewers, can be wrong. The skill of discerning useful from less useful feedback is itself worth teaching.

### Match Pairing And Grouping To The Purpose

How students are paired changes what the feedback can accomplish. Pairing by similar skill level can produce accessible, relatable feedback; pairing across levels can stretch both but risks imbalance if not scaffolded. Interest-based or topic-based grouping lets students give content-specific feedback. Random pairing builds flexibility but may not serve a focused goal. Decide the pairing logic based on what the session is meant to achieve, and vary it across the year so students learn to give and receive feedback with many different peers.

Consider equity and access in pairing. Students with language differences, accommodations, or anxiety may need thoughtful pairing and may benefit from receiving feedback in a structured written form rather than oral critique. Protect privacy regarding any disclosed needs.

## Common Traps

### Launching Without A Protocol

Telling students to "give feedback" without a structured routine produces vague praise, vague criticism, or hurt feelings. Always use a named protocol.

### Feedback Detached From Criteria

Opinion-based comments cannot guide revision. Anchor every comment to a visible criterion or focused feedback guide.

### Skipping Training And Modeling

Students give only as well as they have been taught. Model and practice before expecting useful real feedback.

### Vague Praise Or Vague Criticism

"I liked it" or "this is confusing" tells the receiver nothing actionable. Require specificity about where and what.

### Harsh Or Personal Comments

Feedback aimed at the person damages trust and safety. Establish and enforce norms that address the work, not the writer.

### Feedback Given But Never Used

If receivers do not act on comments, the activity becomes performative. Build in a revision step that uses the feedback.

### Random Pairing For Every Session

Random pairing has value but does not always serve a focused goal, and it can create inequitable or anxiety-producing matches. Choose pairing logic deliberately.

### Treating Peer Feedback As A Time-Saver For The Teacher

Peer feedback is not a way to offload grading; it is a learning activity for both giver and receiver. If the teacher treats it as grading outsourcing, students will too, and quality collapses.

## Self-Check

- Is there a named protocol, such as praise-question-polish or glow-grow, that structures every comment?
- Is every peer comment required to reference a specific criterion from the rubric or success criteria?
- Have students been trained and shown modeled examples of useful versus weak feedback before the first real session?
- Are the features of useful feedback, criteria-referenced, specific, descriptive, actionable, made explicit and posted?
- Are safety and respect norms established, reinforced, and enforced so feedback addresses the work and never the person?
- Is there a built-in step where the receiver decides which comments to use and revises accordingly?
- Is the pairing or grouping logic chosen to serve the session's purpose and to protect equity and access?
- Is peer feedback framed as a learning activity for giver and receiver, not as a shortcut for teacher grading?
