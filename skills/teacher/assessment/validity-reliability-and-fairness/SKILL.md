# Validity, Reliability, And Fairness

An assessment produces a score, and that score travels as if it were a fact about a student. It influences placement, grades, self-image, family conversations, and future opportunity. Three questions must be answered before any score is trusted: does the assessment measure what it claims to measure, is the measurement consistent enough to depend on, and is it fair across the students who take it. These are validity, reliability, and fairness. They are not abstract technical concerns for testing companies; they are the difference between evidence and noise. A test can be well-formatted, on time, and aligned to a standard, and still be invalid because it tests reading instead of science, unreliable because one extra item flips the proficiency call, or unfair because its language assumes background that not all students share. Teachers who ignore these properties hand out numbers that look authoritative but mislead everyone, including themselves.

Use this skill whenever an assessment is being designed, reviewed, or defended. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating validity, reliability, and fairness as afterthoughts or jargon, and instead to make them concrete checks that shape the assessment before students ever see it.

## Core Rules

### Treat Validity As The First Question, Always

Validity is the single most important property of an assessment. A reliable, fair test that measures the wrong thing is still useless. Validity asks: for the intended use, does the evidence support the interpretation of the score?

Build validity by checking, in order:

- content validity: does the assessment sample the actual taught objectives at the cognitive demand they were taught;
- construct validity: does the assessment measure the intended construct, or is it contaminated by reading load, background knowledge, test-wiseness, or other irrelevant factors;
- cognitive validity: does each item require the thinking the objective demands, or does it allow a lower-level shortcut;
- consequential validity: do the decisions made from the score produce consequences that align with the learning goals, or do they distort instruction and harm students.

If any of these fail, the score cannot support the inferences being drawn from it, no matter how clean the formatting.

### Map Every Item To A Taught Objective At The Taught Demand

The most common validity failure is content misalignment. An assessment is invalid if it samples objectives that were not taught, or tests taught objectives at a lower demand than instruction required.

For each item, confirm:

- it maps to a specific taught objective or standard;
- the cognitive demand matches the demand of instruction;
- the format allows the target cognition to be demonstrated;
- no objective is over- or under-represented relative to its importance;
- nothing on the assessment requires knowledge students could only have from outside the course.

A blueprint or table of specifications makes this mapping visible. Without it, alignment is a hope, not a check.

### Eliminate Construct-Irrelevant Variance

Construct-irrelevant variance is anything that affects the score but is not part of the intended construct. It is the chief enemy of validity and fairness, and it is usually invisible to the person who wrote the item.

Hunt for sources of irrelevant variance:

- reading difficulty that turns a math, science, or social studies item into a reading test;
- vocabulary or cultural references that assume specific background;
- test-wiseness, where knowing how tests work helps more than knowing the content;
- formatting, layout, or directions that confuse some students more than others;
- timing that rewards speed rather than understanding;
- motivation or engagement that varies by student and inflates or deflates scores.

When an irrelevant factor shifts the score, the assessment is measuring that factor as much as the target. Rewrite, replace, or compensate.

### Make Reliability A Property Of Decisions, Not Just Scores

Reliability is consistency. A reliable assessment gives similar results across occasions, equivalent forms, and scorers. But reliability matters only in service of a decision: how much error can the decision tolerate?

Match reliability effort to the stakes:

- low-stakes formative checks can tolerate more error because the decision is reversible and the next check will come soon;
- summative unit tests need enough items per objective that a single slip does not flip the proficiency call;
- high-stakes decisions, placement, retention, course grades, need multiple independent pieces of evidence and enough items to reduce noise;
- constructed-response and performance scoring needs rubrics, anchor papers, and calibration to reach acceptable inter-rater reliability.

Ask, for each assessment: if the score were a few points higher or lower, would the decision change? If yes, the assessment is not reliable enough for that decision.

### Use Enough Independent Items To Reduce Noise

Reliability rises with the number of independent, aligned items. One item per objective is almost always too few for a summative judgment, because a single mistake, misread, or lucky guess dominates the result.

Strengthen reliability by:

- allocating several items per essential objective;
- ensuring items are independent, so that knowing one answer does not give away another;
- avoiding near-duplicate items that inflate count without adding information;
- spreading items across the demand levels the objective requires;
- combining formats where a single format would under-sample the construct.

More items help only when they are aligned and independent. Ten clones of one weak item are not more reliable than one.

### Build Fairness Into The Assessment From The Start

Fairness means that the assessment gives every student an equivalent opportunity to demonstrate the intended construct, regardless of background, language, disability, or identity. Fairness is not achieved by treating everyone identically; it is achieved by removing barriers that are irrelevant to the construct.

Build fairness by:

- reviewing items for cultural, linguistic, and socioeconomic assumptions;
- controlling reading load so it does not exceed what the construct requires;
- providing required accommodations for students with IEP, 504, and language plans, such as extended time, read-aloud, alternate format, or glossed vocabulary;
- considering universal design improvements that help all students, such as clearer directions and uncluttered layout;
- avoiding double jeopardy, where one error cascades into many lost points;
- piloting items when possible and watching for differential item performance across groups.

A fair assessment is one where differences in scores reflect differences in the construct, not differences in access.

### Review Items Specifically For Bias

Bias review is a concrete, scheduled step, not a vague aspiration. Bias can be overt, such as stereotyping, or subtle, such as assuming shared experiences.

During item review, check:

- do names, contexts, and scenarios represent diverse groups and avoid stereotypes;
- do any items assume prior experience, travel, technology, or home resources that not all students have;
- is vocabulary controlled so that language proficiency does not confound content mastery for multilingual learners;
- are topics chosen that might distress or alienate particular students avoided or handled with care;
- do timed conditions disproportionately penalize students with certain disabilities or anxiety;
- could any item be interpreted differently by students from different backgrounds.

Have a colleague review when possible. The author of an item is often the last person to see its bias.

### Distinguish Validity, Reliability, And Fairness, But Treat Them As Interdependent

The three properties are distinct but reinforcing. An unfair assessment is invalid for the disadvantaged group. An unreliable assessment cannot support valid high-stakes interpretations. An invalid assessment is not worth making reliable.

When reviewing an assessment, ask all three questions:

- validity: does it measure what it claims, for this purpose, for these students;
- reliability: is it consistent enough for the decision being made;
- fairness: does it give every student an equivalent opportunity to show the construct.

A weakness in any one undermines the others. Do not trade them off against each other casually.

### Document The Evidence For Each Property

Validity, reliability, and fairness are arguments backed by evidence, not certificates. The evidence should be recorded so the assessment can be defended and improved.

Keep records of:

- the blueprint mapping items to objectives and demands;
- item review notes on bias and construct-irrelevance;
- accommodation plans and universal design choices;
- reliability signals, such as items-per-objective counts and, where available, inter-rater agreement;
- post-administration review of items that performed unexpectedly, which may signal bias or misalignment.

Documentation turns a private judgment into a reviewable professional product.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Alignment Equals Validity

Mapping items to standards is necessary but not sufficient. Validity also requires the right cognitive demand and freedom from construct-irrelevant variance.

### Ignoring Reading Load As A Source Of Invalidity

A content assessment with heavy reading demands measures reading as much as the target. Control language load.

### Treating Reliability As A Number Rather Than A Decision Property

Reliability only matters relative to the decision. A reliable low-stakes check and a reliable high-stakes exam need very different evidence.

### One Item Per Objective On A Summative

A single item makes the proficiency call a coin flip. Use enough independent items for the stakes.

### Confusing Equal Treatment With Fairness

Giving every student the identical timed test is not fair if the test contains irrelevant barriers. Fairness removes construct-irrelevant obstacles.

### Skipping A Deliberate Bias Review

Bias is invisible to the item author. Schedule a concrete review step with fresh eyes.

### Letting Scoring Drift On Constructed Response

Without rubrics, anchor papers, and calibration, constructed-response scores become unreliable and unfair. Calibrate before grading.

### Trusting A Score Because The Assessment Looks Professional

Clean formatting and on-time administration do not establish validity, reliability, or fairness. Examine the evidence, not the appearance.

## Self-Check

- Does the assessment measure what it claims, at the cognitive demand instruction required, for the intended use?
- Is every item mapped to a taught objective at the taught demand, with no untaught content?
- Have sources of construct-irrelevant variance, reading load, background, test-wiseness, timing, been identified and reduced?
- Are there enough independent, aligned items per objective to support the reliability the decision requires?
- For constructed response and performance tasks, are rubrics, anchor papers, and calibration in place to reach acceptable inter-rater reliability?
- Are required accommodations for students with IEP, 504, and language plans built in, and have universal design improvements been considered?
- Has a deliberate bias review checked names, contexts, vocabulary, timing, and assumptions for fairness across groups?
- Have post-administration signals, such as unexpectedly easy or hard items, been examined for bias or misalignment?
- Are validity, reliability, and fairness treated as interdependent, with no one sacrificed for another?
- Is there documented evidence, blueprint, review notes, accommodation plans, reliability signals, supporting the score's defensibility?
