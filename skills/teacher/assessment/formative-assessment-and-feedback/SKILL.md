# Formative Assessment And Feedback

Formative assessment is not a quiz category. It is a cycle: gather evidence of student thinking, interpret it against the learning target, give feedback or adjust instruction, and let students use that information to improve. A check for understanding is weak if the teacher collects evidence but does not use it. Feedback is weak if it is too late, too vague, too judgmental, or unrelated to the success criteria.

Use this skill before creating exit tickets, short quizzes, rubrics, progress checks, feedback comments, peer review routines, student work analysis, grading guidance, or reteaching plans. The goal is to keep assessment connected to instruction rather than turning it into recordkeeping.

## Core Rules

### Define The Evidence Needed

Before writing questions, decide what evidence would show progress toward the learning target. Different targets require different evidence.

Match evidence to objective:

- recall requires accurate retrieval;
- concept understanding requires explanation or classification;
- procedural skill requires correct steps and accuracy;
- reasoning requires justification;
- writing requires criteria-based review;
- collaboration requires observable behaviors;
- speaking requires performance evidence;
- problem solving requires strategy and reflection.

Do not assess a complex objective with only a low-level question unless that question is only a quick prerequisite check.

### Keep Formative Assessment Low Stakes

Students need to reveal confusion without fear. If every check feels like a grade, students hide misunderstanding.

Use formative evidence for:

- reteaching;
- grouping;
- feedback;
- pacing;
- extension;
- intervention;
- student reflection;
- teacher planning.

If a formative check contributes to a grade, make the purpose clear and avoid punishing students for early learning. Separate practice evidence from final mastery evidence where possible.

### Ask Questions That Expose Thinking

A correct answer can hide guessing. An incorrect answer can reveal a productive strategy. Ask for reasoning when the objective depends on understanding.

Use:

- "explain your answer";
- "show two ways";
- "choose the better claim and justify";
- "find the error";
- "what evidence supports this?";
- "what would change if...";
- "write a question you still have";
- "rate your confidence and explain why."

Avoid only asking "Do you understand?" Students may not know, may not want to say, or may be wrong about their understanding.

### Sample Broadly And Equitably

Do not let volunteers define the class picture. Collect evidence from many students and from predictable groups, not only the fastest or loudest students.

Plan:

- all-student response;
- random or structured calling;
- pair share before public response;
- teacher circulation checklist;
- quick written response;
- digital poll with privacy protections;
- targeted check for students with prior difficulty;
- extension check for advanced students.

Look for patterns by concept, group, language need, accommodation, and task type. Avoid using evidence to label students permanently.

### Interpret Errors Before Correcting Them

Not all errors are the same. A student may misunderstand vocabulary, miss a prerequisite skill, use a partial strategy, make a calculation slip, misread directions, or hold a misconception.

Classify errors:

- missing prerequisite;
- misconception;
- careless slip;
- language barrier;
- task confusion;
- incomplete strategy;
- overgeneralization;
- attention or working memory issue;
- inaccessible material;
- lack of practice.

The response should match the cause. More practice will not fix a misconception if the model is wrong.

### Give Feedback That Students Can Use

Feedback should be timely, specific, tied to criteria, and actionable. It should tell students what is working, what needs adjustment, and what to do next.

Effective feedback:

- references success criteria;
- focuses on the task or strategy;
- identifies a next step;
- avoids vague praise only;
- avoids overwhelming students with too many corrections;
- arrives while revision is possible;
- preserves student dignity;
- encourages self-monitoring.

"Good job" is not enough. "Your claim is clear; add evidence from paragraph 3 and explain how it supports the claim" is usable.

### Build Time To Use Feedback

Feedback that students cannot act on becomes teacher labor without learning impact. Plan revision time.

Provide:

- correction routine;
- rewrite opportunity;
- targeted practice;
- conference;
- peer response;
- self-assessment;
- error log;
- goal setting;
- reteach group;
- extension challenge.

Do not return feedback at the end of a unit with no chance to apply it.

### Protect Student Privacy And Dignity

Assessment data can expose academic, disability, behavior, language, health, and family information. Treat it as sensitive.

Check:

- school privacy policy;
- FERPA or local equivalent;
- who can see grades;
- public display of scores;
- peer grading rules;
- use of student names in examples;
- screenshots of student work;
- third-party tools;
- parent communication;
- secure storage;
- de-identification when sharing examples.

Do not shame students through public ranking, visible low scores, or comments that expose private needs.

### Use Data For Instructional Decisions

After collecting evidence, decide what changes.

Possible decisions:

- proceed;
- reteach whole class;
- reteach small group;
- adjust materials;
- change grouping;
- provide vocabulary support;
- assign targeted practice;
- give enrichment;
- contact support staff;
- revise next lesson.

Write the decision. If evidence does not change instruction, ask why it was collected.

## Common Traps

### Collecting Too Much Data To Use

A teacher does not need a spreadsheet for every check. Collect evidence that can drive a decision within the available time.

### Grading Every Practice Attempt

Heavy grading can discourage risk-taking. Practice should help learning, not only document errors.

### Confusing Fast With Fluent

Fast responses may reflect confidence, guessing, or prior knowledge. Check reasoning and accuracy.

### Treating Percent Correct As Diagnosis

A score tells how many items were correct, not why. Review item patterns and student explanations.

### Giving Feedback Only To The Final Product

Feedback is most powerful while students can still improve. Build it into drafting, practice, and revision.

### Letting Rubrics Replace Judgment

Rubrics clarify criteria, but teachers still need to examine evidence, student growth, and task fit.

### Publicly Exposing Student Weakness

Class discussion of errors can be useful if anonymized and respectful. Never make a student the example without care and consent according to policy.

## Self-Check

- Does the assessment gather evidence aligned to the exact learning target?
- Is the formative check low stakes and usable for instruction?
- Do prompts reveal reasoning, misconceptions, or strategy rather than only answers?
- Is evidence gathered from all students or a representative sample, not only volunteers?
- Are errors interpreted by likely cause before selecting a response?
- Is feedback specific, timely, criteria-based, respectful, and actionable?
- Is time built in for students to use feedback?
- Are student privacy, dignity, FERPA or local policy, and secure tool use considered?
- Is assessment data used to decide reteaching, grouping, pacing, support, or extension?
- Does the plan avoid collecting more evidence than the teacher can actually use?
