# Captions, Transcripts, And Accessible Media Production

Accessible media is instructional content, video, audio, images, slides, documents, and web pages, designed so that learners with sensory, perceptual, and cognitive differences can perceive and understand it without waiting for a retrofit. The two most common and most consequential accessibility features are captions for video and audio, and transcripts for audio-only content, because they address the single largest access gap in classrooms: students who cannot hear, cannot process auditory information in noise, are deaf or hard of hearing, or are English learners who need the written word alongside speech. The recurring failure in media production is to treat captions and transcripts as optional polish added at the end if there is time, when in fact they are baseline infrastructure that benefits far more learners than the disability community alone. Captions improve comprehension and retention for all learners, support English learners, aid attention in noisy environments, and make content searchable and reviewable. A video without captions excludes and degrades learning for a large share of any classroom.

The opposite failure is to produce captions that exist but are inaccurate, auto-generated garbage, unedited, mistimed, or missing speaker identification and meaningful non-speech audio. Bad captions can be worse than none, because they mislead, distract, and signal that accessibility is performative rather than real. Accessible media is not a checkbox; it is a craft. This skill applies whenever the work is to produce, select, adapt, or evaluate instructional media for accessibility.

## Core Rules

### Treat Captions As Baseline Infrastructure, Not Optional Polish

Every instructional video and audio recording used in a classroom should have accurate, synchronized captions, without exception and without needing a request. Build captions into the production workflow from the start, not as a final step that gets dropped when time runs out. Auto-generated captions are a starting draft, never a finished product; they routinely misplace proper nouns, technical terms, numbers, and punctuation in ways that change meaning. Plan time to edit them. A video that lacks accurate captions is an inaccessible video, full stop, regardless of how engaging its content is.

### Produce Accurate, Synchronized, And Meaningful Captions

Good captions are verbatim or equivalent-meaning, synchronized to the speech within roughly a second, readable (two to three lines, not blocking important visuals), and they include meaningful non-speech audio that carries information (sound effects, music cues, tone indicators like [sighs], [applause], [ominous music]). They identify speakers when ambiguous. They preserve punctuation and sentence boundaries so the meaning is intact. They do not paraphrase away nuance or technical vocabulary. When you edit auto-captions, fix every error, not just the obvious ones; learners rely on every word.

### Provide Transcripts For Audio-Only And Long-Form Content

Captions require synchronized video; transcripts are the text version of audio content, useful for podcasts, lectures, interviews, and any content a learner may want to read, search, or review at their own pace. Transcripts benefit not only deaf and hard-of-hearing learners but also those who process text faster than speech, English learners, and anyone reviewing for notes. Provide a downloadable, searchable transcript alongside audio-only media. For video, a transcript complements captions by enabling search and review.

### Write Meaningful Alt Text For Images And Graphics

Every informational image (diagrams, charts, photographs that convey content) needs alternative text that conveys its meaning to a learner who cannot see it. Decorative images should be marked as decorative (empty alt text) rather than described. Alt text is not a filename or a caption; it is a concise description of what the image communicates: "Bar chart showing rising global temperature from 1880 to 2020, with 2020 marked as the warmest year." For complex graphics (detailed diagrams, data visualizations), a short alt-text summary plus a longer text description or data table is better than cramming everything into one alt attribute. Charts that convey their meaning only through color require additional text or patterns.

### Provide Audio Description For Visual-Only Information

When a video communicates essential information through visuals that are not described in the narration (a demonstration, an on-screen label, a facial expression, a chart), learners who cannot see the video miss that information. Audio description is an additional narration track (or integrated description) that fills these gaps. At minimum, ensure that the spoken narration carries the essential visual information; if it does not, add description. This is often overlooked because sighted producers do not notice what they are conveying only visually.

### Ensure Sufficient Color Contrast And Readable Typography

Accessibility extends beyond captions to visual perception. Text must have sufficient contrast against its background; small or low-contrast text excludes learners with low vision, color vision differences, or simply those at the back of the room or on a small screen. Use readable fonts at adequate sizes, avoid all-caps blocks for body text, avoid relying on color alone to convey meaning (use labels, patterns, or text as well), and ensure that links and interactive elements are distinguishable. These choices help every learner, not only those with diagnosed needs.

### Make Documents And Slides Structurally Accessible

Use real headings, lists, and styles in documents and slides rather than formatting text to look like a heading. Structural elements let screen readers navigate, let learners using keyboards jump through content, and let outline views work. Add alt text to slide images, use readable layouts, and ensure reading order is logical. Tag PDFs so that they are navigable rather than flat images of text. A visually attractive document that is a flat image is inaccessible to a screen reader and unsearchable for everyone.

### Caption And Describe Live Instruction And Media Intentionally

Recorded media is the easier case; live instruction and live media (streamed lessons, guest speakers, live video) also need real-time captioning or interpretation when any participant needs it. Plan for live captioning platforms, sign-language interpretation, or CART services for events and streamed lessons when required, and do not assume "no one here needs it" without checking.

## Common Traps

### Treating Auto-Captions As Finished

Publishing unedited auto-generated captions because they "look mostly right." Auto-captions routinely mangle proper nouns, numbers, technical terms, and punctuation, changing meaning. They are a draft to edit, not a finished product.

### Captioning Only When Requested

Waiting for a deaf or hard-of-hearing student to enroll before captioning media. This is reactive, excludes learners who have not disclosed, and misses the broad benefit to all learners. Caption proactively.

### Decorative Alt Text Or Filename Alt Text

Writing alt text like "image1.jpg" or "students" for a photo that conveys content, or describing decorative images in detail while leaving informational images undescribed. Alt text must convey meaning; decorative images should be marked decorative.

### Relying On Color Alone

Using red versus green to distinguish categories on a chart, or color alone to indicate required fields, excluding learners with color vision differences and those on monochrome or small displays. Pair color with labels, patterns, or text.

### Low Contrast For Aesthetics

Choosing pale gray text on white because it looks elegant, at the cost of legibility for low-vision learners and anyone in a bright room. Accessibility and aesthetics can coexist; choose accessible color combinations from the start.

### Flat Image Documents And Slides

Exporting a slide deck or document as images, or building slides with text boxes that have no structural order. Screen readers cannot read them, keyboard users cannot navigate them, and search fails. Use real structure.

### Ignoring Audio Description

Assuming that because the narration exists, the visuals are covered. When a demonstration, on-screen label, or chart carries meaning the narration does not state, learners who cannot see it are excluded. Describe essential visual information.

### Forgetting Live And Real-Time Media

Captioning recorded videos but not planning for live streamed lessons, guest speakers, or announcements where real-time access is needed. Plan live captioning or interpretation in advance for events.

## Self-Check

- Does every instructional video and audio file have accurate, synchronized, edited captions, not raw auto-generated text?
- Do the captions include meaningful non-speech audio, speaker identification where needed, and correct punctuation and technical terms?
- Is there a downloadable transcript for audio-only content and long-form video?
- Does every informational image have concise, meaningful alt text, and are decorative images marked as decorative?
- For complex graphics and charts, is there a text summary or data table in addition to short alt text?
- Does essential visual information in videos get conveyed through narration or audio description, not visuals alone?
- Does text meet contrast standards, avoid color-only meaning, and use readable fonts and sizes?
- Do documents and slides use real headings, lists, and styles with logical reading order, rather than flat formatting or image-only exports?
- Have I planned for live captioning or interpretation for streamed lessons and events where real-time access is needed?
