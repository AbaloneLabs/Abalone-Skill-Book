# Assistive Technology Selection And Implementation

Assistive technology (AT) is any item, piece of equipment, or software system that is used to increase, maintain, or improve the functional capabilities of a learner with a disability. It ranges from low-tech pencil grips and slant boards to mid-tech calculators and audio recorders to high-tech speech-to-text, text-to-speech, screen readers, word prediction, eye-gaze communication, and switch-access devices. The recurring failure in AT work is to lead with the tool: a teacher or team hears about an exciting new app, recommends it for a student, and only afterward asks whether it actually addresses the learner's specific barrier in their specific environment for their specific tasks. That order produces abandoned devices, frustrated students, and wasted money. The tool is the answer to a question, and if the question was never asked clearly, the answer will not fit.

The opposite failure is to under-consider AT altogether, assuming that a student who struggles simply needs more effort, more practice, or lower expectations, when a well-matched tool could remove the barrier entirely and reveal genuine capability. A student who cannot produce legible handwriting may have rich ideas that speech-to-text unlocks; a student who cannot decode grade-level text may comprehend far above grade level when the text is read aloud. AT is not cheating or a crutch; it is an on-ramp to the curriculum. This skill applies whenever the work is to select, recommend, document, implement, or evaluate assistive technology for a learner.

## Core Rules

### Lead With The Learner, Not The Tool: Use A Decision Framework

Never start with the device. Start with a structured framework that forces you to define the problem before proposing a solution. The SETT framework (Student, Environment, Tasks, Tools) is the most widely used: describe the Student (strengths, needs, abilities, challenges), the Environment (classroom layout, expectations, culture, supports, constraints), and the Tasks (the specific activities the student must do to participate and progress) before considering the Tools. Only after Student, Environment, and Tasks are clear should you generate and evaluate tool options. This sequence prevents the common error of fitting the student to the tool rather than the tool to the student.

### Define The Barrier Precisely Before Selecting A Tool

A vague need ("he struggles with writing") leads to a vague solution. Pin down the barrier: is the barrier ideation (generating ideas), transcription (getting words onto the page), spelling, handwriting fluency, organization, or self-monitoring? Each barrier points to a different tool. Speech-to-text addresses transcription barriers but not ideation; graphic organizers address organization but not handwriting; word prediction helps with spelling and fluency but not with idea generation. A tool matched to the wrong barrier will not help and will be blamed on the student.

### Match Tool Features To The Specific Task And Environment

A tool that works in a quiet resource room may fail in a noisy general-education classroom; a tool that works on a desktop may not transfer to a tablet the student actually carries. Evaluate the tool in the real environment for the real tasks. Consider noise, privacy, battery life, connectivity, logins, sharing of work with the teacher, and whether the tool integrates with the platforms the class already uses. A brilliant tool that the student is embarrassed to use or that cannot submit work to the teacher's workflow will be abandoned.

### Start Low-Tech And Escalate Only As Needed

The principle of least restrictive intervention applies: begin with the simplest tool that removes the barrier and escalate only if it is insufficient. A slant board, pencil grip, lined paper, or printed graphic organizer may solve the problem at a fraction of the cost and complexity of a high-tech solution. High-tech tools carry training burden, failure points, and stigma; do not deploy them when a low-tech option works. Conversely, do not let "low-tech first" become an excuse to deny a student a high-tech tool they genuinely need; escalate promptly when the low-tech option is inadequate.

### Plan For Implementation, Not Just Acquisition

Selecting the tool is roughly ten percent of the work; implementation is the rest. A device handed to a student with no training, no routine, and no teacher buy-in will sit unused. Plan for: who trains the student (and the staff), how the tool is integrated into daily routines, how work is saved and shared, what happens when the tool breaks or the battery dies, how peers will be oriented (to reduce stigma and questions), and how progress will be monitored. Without an implementation plan, even a perfectly matched tool fails.

### Document AT In The IEP With Specificity

If AT is required for a student to access the curriculum, it must be documented in the IEP, not left to informal practice. Vague documentation ("student will use technology as needed") is unenforceable and disappears under staff turnover. Specify the tool, who provides it, when and where it is used, who trains and supports it, and how its effectiveness will be reviewed. AT consideration is a required part of the IEP process; treat it as such rather than as an afterthought.

### Build In Trials And Data Collection Before Finalizing

Do not commit to an expensive tool on a hunch. Run a structured trial: define the baseline, set the tool up properly, implement with fidelity for a defined period, and collect data on whether the barrier is reduced and the student's participation and performance improve. Compare to a baseline or to an alternative tool. Trial data protects the student from being stuck with a poor match and protects the team from sunk-cost commitment to a tool that is not working.

### Consider The Student's Voice And Stigma

A tool the student refuses to use is not a useful tool, regardless of how well it matches the barrier. Involve the student in selection where developmentally appropriate, address stigma directly, and normalize a range of tools for all learners (universal design) so that AT does not mark the user as different. Older students in particular may abandon a visibly "special" tool to avoid social cost; design for acceptability.

## Common Traps

### Tool-First Selection

Hearing about a popular app and recommending it before analyzing the student, environment, and tasks. The result is a mismatch between the tool's features and the actual barrier, and the student is blamed for "not using it."

### Conflating Different Writing Barriers

Treating "writing difficulty" as one problem and prescribing one tool. Ideation, transcription, spelling, organization, and self-monitoring are distinct barriers needing distinct tools. Diagnose the barrier before prescribing.

### Ignoring The Environment And Workflow

A tool that works in isolation but not in the live classroom, that cannot save or submit work, that requires logins the student cannot manage, or that is too noisy or stigmatizing will be abandoned regardless of its theoretical fit.

### No Implementation Plan

Acquiring the device and considering the job done. Without training, routines, staff buy-in, technical support, and a plan for breakdowns, the tool will not be used with fidelity, and the team will wrongly conclude "AT doesn't work for this student."

### Skipping Trials And Data

Committing to an expensive tool without a structured trial and baseline data. The team then cannot tell whether the tool is helping, and sunk-cost bias keeps a poor match in place.

### Denying AT As "Cheating" Or "A Crutch"

Withholding a tool that would remove a barrier because of a belief that the student should "just learn to do it the normal way." This confuses the target skill with the barrier: a student learning to decode should not have content-area learning blocked by decoding, and a student with a permanent motor barrier needs the tool, not more handwriting practice.

### Letting Low-Tech-First Become Denial

Using "start low-tech" as a pretext to deny a student a high-tech tool they clearly need. The principle is least restrictive intervention, not indefinite minimization; escalate when the low-tech option is insufficient.

## Self-Check

- Did I use a structured framework (such as SETT) and define Student, Environment, and Tasks before generating tool options?
- Have I pinpointed the specific barrier (ideation, transcription, spelling, organization, motor, sensory) rather than a vague "difficulty"?
- Does the selected tool's features directly address that specific barrier, and have I ruled out tools that address a different barrier?
- Did I evaluate the tool in the real classroom environment and workflow (noise, privacy, logins, saving, sharing, battery), not just in isolation?
- Did I start with the least restrictive option and escalate based on evidence rather than assumption?
- Is there a concrete implementation plan covering training, routines, technical support, peer orientation, and breakdown contingencies?
- Is the AT documented in the IEP with specificity (tool, provider, setting, training, review) rather than vague language?
- Is there a structured trial with baseline data and a defined review point before the tool is considered finalized?
- Have I involved the student and addressed stigma, so the tool is something they will actually use?
