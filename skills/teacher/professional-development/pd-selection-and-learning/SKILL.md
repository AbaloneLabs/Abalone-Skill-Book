# Professional Development Selection

Professional development is abundant and unevenly valuable, and teachers are routinely sent to workshops that consume time and change nothing. The failure of PD selection is not choosing bad options; it is choosing without a clear sense of what would actually help, chasing whatever is trendy, accumulating clock hours without a learning goal, or defaulting to whatever the school sends you to. Effective PD selection treats professional learning as a scarce resource to be invested toward specific growth, not as a buffet to be sampled. A teacher who selects PD strategically grows faster than one who attends everything and integrates nothing.

This skill covers the judgment needed to select professional development well: diagnosing your own learning needs, evaluating PD quality, aligning choices to goals, avoiding fads, and integrating formal and informal learning. The goal is professional learning that actually changes practice rather than learning that fills a transcript.

## Core Rules

### Start From A Specific Learning Need, Not From Available Options

The most common PD selection error is browsing what is available and then choosing, which produces learning shaped by marketing rather than by need. Invert the process: identify a specific aspect of your practice you want to improve, grounded in evidence from your self-assessment, student results, or peer feedback, and then look for the learning that addresses it. A teacher who knows they need to improve questioning will find and evaluate questioning-specific PD; a teacher who browses the PD catalog will end up wherever the catalog points them. Need-first selection produces focused growth; option-first selection produces scattered attendance.

### Evaluate PD Quality Before Investing Time

Not all PD is worth the time it takes, and some is actively harmful, entrenching debunked practices or wasting effort on activities with no evidence. Before committing, evaluate the PD's quality: who is leading it and what is their expertise, what evidence supports the approach, is it grounded in research or in anecdote, does it include actual practice and feedback or only passive listening, and does it connect to your context. High-quality PD includes active practice, feedback, and follow-up; low-quality PD is a passive presentation that research consistently shows changes little. Treat the time cost as real and hold PD to a standard before investing it.

### Be Wary Of Trendy But Unsupported Approaches

Education has a steady stream of fashionable approaches, often marketed with brain labels, learning-style claims, or novelty, that lack evidence or have been debunked. Learning styles, brain gym, discovery-only math, and uncritical applications of neuroscience are examples of approaches that sound compelling and do not hold up. Before adopting a trendy approach from PD, check the actual evidence, look for independent review rather than promoter claims, and be skeptical of silver bullets. A teacher who protects their practice from fads has more time for approaches that actually work.

### Align PD Choices To A Multi-Year Growth Plan

Single PD events rarely change practice; sequences of related learning do. Build a multi-year sense of where you want to grow and select PD that advances it, so that each experience compounds rather than standing alone. A teacher who spends two years deepening formative assessment practice, with related PD, reading, peer observation, and experimentation, grows far more than one who attends six unrelated workshops. Alignment across time is what turns PD from events into development.

### Balance Formal And Informal Learning

Formal PD, workshops, courses, conferences, is visible and counts toward requirements, but much of the most valuable professional learning is informal: reading research and practitioner writing, observing colleagues, trying practices and reflecting, engaging in online professional communities, and solving real problems with peers. Treat informal learning as real development and invest in it deliberately rather than treating only formal PD as learning. The strongest teachers combine both, using formal PD to acquire new approaches and informal learning to integrate and refine them.

### Choose PD That Includes Practice And Feedback, Not Just Information

PD that consists only of being told things changes behavior very little, because knowing is not the same as being able to do. The most effective PD includes opportunities to practice the new approach, receive feedback, and adjust, ideally with follow-up support after the initial session. When choosing among options, prefer those with active practice, coaching, or sustained community over one-shot presentations. If only passive PD is available, plan your own practice and feedback loop afterward, because without it the information will likely not transfer to your classroom.

### Connect PD To Classroom Experimentation

PD that is not connected to actual classroom change is learning that decays. For each significant PD experience, plan a specific experiment: what will I try, with whom, when, and how will I know whether it worked. This connection is what converts PD input into practice change, and it is the step most often skipped. A teacher who attends PD and then tries nothing has consumed learning without producing growth; a teacher who attends PD and immediately experiments, even imperfectly, converts it into development.

### Evaluate PD Afterward Honestly

After PD, assess honestly whether it was worth the investment: did it address the need, was the quality as expected, did it lead to classroom change, would you recommend it. Honest post-hoc evaluation sharpens future selection, because teachers who never evaluate their PD choices keep choosing the same kinds of options regardless of results. Share useful and poor PD with colleagues, so the team's collective selection improves.

## Common Traps

### Browsing Available Options Before Identifying Need

Option-first selection produces scattered learning. Start from a specific growth need.

### Treating All PD As Equally Valuable

Quality varies enormously. Evaluate evidence, practice opportunities, and follow-up before investing.

### Chasing Trendy Unsupported Approaches

Fads waste time and can entrench debunked practice. Check the evidence independently.

### Single Events With No Follow-Up Plan

One-shot PD rarely changes practice. Align PD to a multi-year growth sequence.

### Treating Only Formal PD As Real Learning

Informal learning is often more valuable. Invest in reading, observation, and peer problem-solving.

### Passive PD With No Practice Or Feedback

Being told things does not build ability. Prefer active PD with coaching and follow-up.

### Attending PD But Trying Nothing In Class

Learning without experimentation decays. Plan a specific classroom experiment after each PD.

## Self-Check

- Did I identify a specific learning need before browsing available PD?
- Did I evaluate the PD's quality, evidence, and practice opportunities before committing?
- Am I wary of trendy approaches and checking independent evidence?
- Is this PD aligned to a multi-year growth plan rather than a standalone event?
- Am I balancing formal PD with deliberate informal learning?
- Does the PD I choose include practice, feedback, and follow-up, not just information?
- Did I plan a specific classroom experiment to convert the PD into practice change?
- Do I honestly evaluate PD afterward and share what I learn with colleagues?
