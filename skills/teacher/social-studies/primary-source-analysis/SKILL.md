# Primary Source Analysis

A primary source is an artifact from the time and place being studied: a letter, diary, photograph, speech, treaty, map, song, advertisement, or object. It is the raw material of history, the evidence historians use to construct accounts of the past. Teaching with primary sources matters because it lets students do history rather than only receive it: they encounter the past directly, weigh evidence, consider perspective, and construct interpretations, developing the historical thinking that a textbook summary cannot provide. The recurring failure is treating primary sources as illustrated textbooks, mining them for facts without examining who produced them, why, under what constraints, and with what biases, which collapses historical thinking into information extraction.

The opposite failure is using sources so complex that students cannot access them and so learn nothing. The design challenge is to choose sources rich enough to think with, scaffold them so students can engage, and teach the analytical moves, sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, close reading, and using background knowledge, that turn a document into historical evidence. This skill applies whenever the work is to select, scaffold, or teach with primary sources.

## Core Rules

### Teach Sourcing Before Content

Sourcing asks who created a source, when, where, for whom, and why, and how those factors shape what the source can and cannot tell us. It is the first move of historical thinking because it determines how much weight to place on a document and how to read it. A diary entry, a propaganda poster, and a government report about the same event tell us very different things and must be read differently.

Teach students to source every document before extracting content: identify the author, the date, the audience, the purpose, and the type of source, and ask how these shape its reliability and perspective. A student who reads a source without sourcing is treating all documents as equally authoritative, which is unhistorical.

### Choose Sources Rich In Perspective And Tension

The most teachable sources are those with evident perspective, voice, or tension, not neutral summaries. A passionate speech, a conflicted diary, a propaganda poster, a dissenting opinion, or a photograph framed to make an argument gives students something to analyze. Sources that simply report information teach sourcing poorly because there is little to source.

Select sources that represent diverse perspectives on an event, including those of people whose voices were often excluded from official records. The set of sources students examine should itself represent the contestedness of the past.

### Scaffold Complex Texts So Students Can Engage

Historical sources are often linguistically and conceptually demanding, written in older English, full of unfamiliar references, and assuming knowledge students lack. Handing students an unmodified eighteenth-century document and asking them to analyze it produces frustration, not historical thinking. Scaffold access through chunking, vocabulary support, modernized paraphrases alongside the original, guiding questions, and the teaching of necessary background.

The goal is not to dumb down the source but to make it penetrable so students can do the analytical work. Provide enough support that students can engage the source's meaning and perspective without drowning in its difficulty.

### Teach Contextualization As Essential To Interpretation

A source cannot be understood apart from its context: the time, place, circumstances, and conventions in which it was produced. A statement that reads one way to a modern student may have meant something quite different in its original context. Teach students to ask what was happening when the source was created, what conventions governed this kind of document, and what the author's world was like.

Without contextualization, students commit presentism, judging the past by the standards and assumptions of the present, and misread sources whose meaning depended on circumstances they do not know. Build the context before or alongside the source analysis.

### Teach Corroboration Across Sources

No single source tells the whole truth. Corroboration asks how sources agree, disagree, and complicate each other, and what a defensible account looks like when sources conflict. Teach students to read sources in conversation: where do they agree, where do they diverge, how can the differences be explained, and what conclusion is best supported by the weight of evidence?

Corroboration is where historical argument is born. A student who has read three conflicting accounts and must construct a defensible synthesis is doing history.

### Design Document-Based Questions That Require Argument

A document-based question asks students to construct an argument supported by evidence from multiple sources, not to extract and report information. Frame questions that the sources can help answer but that require interpretation, weighing, and synthesis. Require students to cite specific sources as evidence, acknowledge conflicting evidence, and reach a defensible conclusion.

Avoid questions answerable from a single source or from prior knowledge alone, and avoid questions so vague that any answer works. The question should demand the analytical moves the sources make possible.

### Teach Close Reading For Language And Subtext

Historical sources often mean more than their surface. Teach close reading that attends to word choice, tone, what is emphasized and omitted, framing, and subtext. A source may claim one thing while its language reveals another. The skill of reading what a document says, what it implies, and what it leaves unsaid is central to historical thinking.

Model close reading aloud, showing how a historian notices a telling word or a revealing absence, and have students practice on short passages.

### Use Visual And Material Sources, Not Only Written Ones

Photographs, paintings, maps, cartoons, objects, and buildings are primary sources too, and they are often more accessible than written documents while teaching the same analytical moves. A photograph can be sourced, contextualized, and corroborated as rigorously as a letter. Visual and material sources also engage students who struggle with complex text and broaden the kinds of evidence students learn to weigh.

Apply the same analytical framework, sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, close reading, to visual sources, adapting the questions to the medium.

## Common Traps

### Sources As Illustrated Textbooks

Mining primary sources for facts without examining their perspective collapses historical thinking into information extraction. Source every document first.

### Sources Too Complex To Access

Handing students unmodified difficult documents produces frustration. Scaffold access so students can do the analytical work.

### Single Source Treated As The Truth

No one source tells the whole truth. Teach corroboration across multiple sources.

### Presentism

Judging the past solely by present standards misreads sources whose meaning depended on their context. Teach contextualization.

### Questions Answerable From One Source Or Prior Knowledge

If a question does not require weighing multiple sources, it is not a document-based question. Demand synthesis and argument.

### Neutral Summaries Instead Of Voiced Sources

Sources without evident perspective teach sourcing poorly. Choose rich, voiced, contested sources.

### Ignoring Visual And Material Evidence

Limiting primary sources to written documents narrows the evidence students learn to use. Include photographs, maps, cartoons, and objects.

### Reading Only Surface Meaning

Historical sources often mean more than their surface. Teach close reading for language, tone, and subtext.

## Self-Check

- Do students source every document before extracting content, asking who, when, where, for whom, and why?
- Are sources chosen for richness of perspective and tension, representing diverse voices on contested events?
- Are complex texts scaffolded so students can engage their meaning without drowning in difficulty?
- Is contextualization taught as essential, so students avoid presentism and read sources in their time and place?
- Do students corroborate across sources, weighing agreement and disagreement to construct defensible accounts?
- Do document-based questions require argument supported by multiple sources, not information extraction?
- Is close reading taught for language, tone, emphasis, and subtext, not only surface meaning?
- Are visual and material sources included alongside written ones, analyzed with the same framework?
- Could students explain how a source's perspective and context shape what it can and cannot tell us?
- Are students doing history, weighing evidence and constructing interpretations, rather than receiving conclusions?
