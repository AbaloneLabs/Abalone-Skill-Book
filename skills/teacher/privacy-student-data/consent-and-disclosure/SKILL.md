# Consent and Disclosure of Student Information

Between the extremes of total secrecy and careless sharing lies the everyday work of deciding when and how student information may be disclosed, and when consent is required. The judgment problem is that teachers often operate on intuition about what feels appropriate to share, when in fact disclosure is governed by a layered set of rules: federal law such as FERPA, state and local policy, professional ethics, and the student's own developmental right to privacy and autonomy. Getting this wrong in either direction causes harm. Over-disclosure exposes students to stigma, breaches of trust, and legal liability. Under-disclosure can deny students services they need, isolate families from information that belongs to them, or fail to meet safety obligations.

The hardest cases involve sensitive information that a student has confided, requests from families to see or control information, and pressure from colleagues or outside parties for details. The skilled teacher knows the difference between information that may be shared under an exception, information that requires consent, and information that should not be shared at all, and handles each with clarity and care.

## Core Rules

### Classify the Information Before You Act

Before sharing any student information, classify what kind it is, because the rules differ. Directory information, such as name and enrollment status per your district's list, may be disclosed under conditions unless the family opted out. General education records, such as grades and attendance, may be shared with school officials who have a legitimate educational interest and with parents and eligible students. Sensitive records, including disability and accommodation records, health information, counseling notes, and disciplinary records, carry stricter handling and often require consent for outside disclosure. Legally protected or safety-critical information, such as allegations of abuse or threats of harm, triggers mandatory reporting and specific protocols. When you classify first, you avoid the common error of treating all information the same.

### Know When Consent Is Required Versus Permitted

Consent is required to disclose non-directory education records to outside parties who lack a specific exception, and to release records for purposes beyond the school's educational function. Consent must be specific, informed, and documented, meaning the signer knows what will be released, to whom, and for what purpose. Blanket consent forms that ask families to authorize all future disclosures are not genuine consent. Within the school, the legitimate educational interest exception permits sharing without consent, but only with those who need the information to perform their duties. When you are unsure whether an exception applies, default to seeking consent or consulting the records officer rather than disclosing and hoping it was permitted.

### Handle Student Self-Disclosure with Care

When a student shares sensitive information about themselves, their family, or their home life, the disclosure often carries an expectation of confidentiality that you cannot fully honor. Be honest with the student, in age-appropriate terms, about the limits of confidentiality before they disclose if possible, and certainly before you act on what they tell you. Explain that you may need to share certain information to keep them or others safe, and that you cannot promise secrecy. Listen first, validate, and then follow the appropriate protocol. Do not promise to keep secrets, do not investigate allegations yourself, and do not share the information more broadly than the situation requires.

### Distinguish What Families May Access from What They May Control

Parents and eligible students have the right to inspect and review education records and to request amendment of records they believe are inaccurate or misleading, but they do not have an unlimited right to control every piece of information about the student, and some records, such as certain counselor notes and law enforcement records, may be handled under specific rules. When a family requests records, route the request through the proper process and respond within the required timeframe. When a family asks you to alter or remove a record, follow the amendment procedure rather than unilaterally editing. Do not withhold information a family is entitled to, and do not disclose information they are not entitled to without basis.

### Apply the Safety and Mandatory Reporting Exceptions Correctly

Some information must be disclosed regardless of consent because the law requires it. Suspected child abuse or neglect must be reported to child protective authorities, typically immediately and by the person who suspects it, following your state's mandated reporter rules. Threats of serious harm to self or others must be communicated to the appropriate school officials and, where required, to authorities. These obligations override ordinary confidentiality. The key is to disclose only to the parties who need the information for the safety purpose, to document what you disclosed and why, and to involve the administration promptly. Do not use the safety exception as a pretext for broad sharing.

### Manage Outside Provider and Third-Party Requests

Requests from outside therapists, doctors, tutors, coaches, or community programs for student information require written consent from the parent or eligible student before you disclose. Conversely, information you receive from outside providers should be handled according to school policy and shared only with those who have a legitimate educational interest. Be cautious about informal information sharing that bypasses the consent process, and route unusual requests through the records officer or administration.

### Respond to Subpoenas and Legal Requests Through Proper Channels

If you receive a subpoena, court order, or law enforcement request for student records, do not respond directly. Notify your administration and the appropriate district office immediately, as these requests carry specific notice and compliance rules under FERPA and state law. Do not destroy records in response to a request, and do not disclose more than the request lawfully requires.

### Document Consent and Disclosure

Keep clear records of what consent was obtained, what was disclosed, to whom, when, and under what authority. Documentation protects the student, the family, the school, and you, and it is essential if a disclosure is later questioned. Maintain these records according to school policy and store them securely.

## Common Traps

### Promising Confidentiality You Cannot Keep

Telling a student that what they share will stay between you sets up a breach of trust the moment a mandatory reporting or safety obligation kicks in. Establish the limits of confidentiality up front and honestly, and never promise absolute secrecy.

### Treating All Student Information as Equally Shareable

Applying a single standard to all information leads to over-sharing sensitive data or under-sharing routine data. Classify information first and match the disclosure rule to the category.

### Disclosing to Be Helpful Without a Legitimate Basis

Sharing information with a colleague, an outside provider, or a family member because it seems helpful, without a legitimate educational interest or proper consent, is an unauthorized disclosure. Helpful intent is not a legal basis. When unsure, ask before you share.

### Using the Safety Exception Too Broadly

The health or safety emergency and mandatory reporting exceptions permit necessary disclosure, not blanket communication. Disclose to the parties who need the information for the safety purpose, and avoid turning a specific obligation into a general announcement.

### Letting Family Pressure Override the Rules

Families may press for information about other students, for changes to records, or for restrictions on what may be shared with colleagues. Respond with empathy but follow the actual rules, route requests through proper channels, and do not make exceptions based on insistence or status.

### Failing to Document

Undocumented consent and disclosure become impossible to verify later and leave you and the school vulnerable. Build documentation into your routine for any non-routine disclosure.

## Self-Check

- Before sharing any student information, have I classified it as directory, general education, sensitive, or safety-critical, and matched the disclosure rule to that category?
- Where consent is required, is the consent specific, informed, and documented, rather than a blanket authorization?
- Have I been honest with students about the limits of confidentiality, and avoided promising secrecy I cannot legally or ethically keep?
- When handling a safety or mandatory reporting situation, did I disclose only to the parties who needed the information, document the basis, and involve administration promptly?
- For requests from outside providers, families seeking records, or legal requests, have I routed them through the proper school process rather than responding informally?
- Am I resisting the temptation to disclose out of helpfulness without a legitimate educational interest or proper consent, and consulting when unsure?
- Have I documented what was consented to and what was disclosed, so that the decision could be reviewed and justified if questioned later?
