# FERPA Compliance for Teachers

The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA) protects the privacy of student education records and governs how schools may disclose them. For a classroom teacher, FERPA is not an abstract legal concern handled by the front office; it is a daily set of decisions about what you write down, where you store it, who you show it to, what you say aloud, and what you post publicly. The judgment problem is that the most common FERPA violations in classrooms are not dramatic data breaches but ordinary, well-intentioned acts: leaving a gradebook open on a desk, discussing a student's performance in the staff lounge, emailing a colleague about a student without a legitimate educational interest, posting student work with names visible, or forwarding an IEP to someone who does not need it. Teachers also frequently misunderstand the difference between directory information, which can be disclosed under certain conditions, and non-directory records, which generally cannot.

Compounding the difficulty, school technology has multiplied the surfaces on which student data lives: learning management systems, shared drives, messaging apps, video conferencing, and third-party edtech tools. Each is a potential disclosure point. The teacher's responsibility is to treat student information as a trust, to know the boundaries of legitimate educational interest, and to default to the minimum disclosure necessary.

## Core Rules

### Know What Counts as an Education Record

Under FERPA, an education record is broadly defined as any record directly related to a student and maintained by the school or a party acting for the school. This includes far more than the official cumulative file: gradebooks, attendance logs, discipline records, health information kept by the nurse, counseling notes maintained by the school, individualized education programs, 504 plans, responses to intervention data, behavioral incident reports, and even emails and notes you write about a student if they are maintained. If you write it down and keep it, and it relates to a student, assume it is an education record subject to FERPA. This means casual notes, sticky notes, and personal spreadsheets about students are covered if retained.

### Apply the Legitimate Educational Interest Test Before Sharing

Within the school, student records may be shared with school officials who have a legitimate educational interest, meaning they need the information to perform their duties. Before sharing with a colleague, ask whether they genuinely need the information to do their job for that student. A teacher who has the student in class has a legitimate interest; a teacher who is merely curious does not. A counselor or administrator involved in the student's services has a legitimate interest; a colleague in another department who wants background generally does not. When in doubt, share the minimum necessary and route requests through the appropriate administrator or records officer. Never share records with outside parties, including outside therapists, coaches, or family friends, without proper authorization.

### Distinguish Directory Information from Non-Directory Records

Directory information is data that would not generally be considered harmful or an invasion of privacy if disclosed, such as name, enrollment status, and dates of attendance, though the exact list is set by the district. Schools may disclose directory information without consent but must give families the chance to opt out. Non-directory information, including grades, discipline, disability status, health information, and most behavioral data, generally requires consent for disclosure outside the legitimate educational interest exception. Do not assume something is directory information because it seems harmless; check your district's specific directory list and opt-out records before using any student information publicly, and remember that a student whose family opted out of directory information must be excluded from programs, publications, and lists.

### Protect Records in All Physical and Digital Forms

Physical records should not be left visible on desks, in open mailboxes, on printers, or in unlocked rooms. Digital records should be stored on approved, secured systems, not on personal devices, personal cloud accounts, or unencrypted portable drives. Lock your screen when you step away. Use school-approved communication channels rather than personal email or messaging apps for any student information. Be especially careful with shared drives: check the permissions on any folder containing student data and remove access for those who no longer need it. When disposing of records, shred paper and securely delete digital files rather than tossing them.

### Be Careful in Public and Semi-Public Spaces

Conversations about students in hallways, staff lounges, parking lots, and social settings are a frequent source of inadvertent disclosure. Even if the listener has a legitimate educational interest, bystanders may not. Avoid naming students or discussing identifiable details where others can overhear, and redirect colleagues who try to draw you into such conversations. The same caution applies to written communication: assume any email could be forwarded and write accordingly.

### Handle Parent and Student Rights Correctly

Under FERPA, parents and eligible students have the right to inspect and review education records, the right to request amendment of inaccurate or misleading records, and the right to some control over disclosure. When a parent asks to see records, route the request through the proper school process rather than handing over your own notes piecemeal. When a parent or student challenges the accuracy of a record, follow the amendment procedure rather than unilaterally altering or deleting entries. Do not promise confidentiality you cannot guarantee, and do not agree to keep a record off the books.

### Apply the Health or Safety Emergency Exception Narrowly

FERPA allows disclosure of records without consent in a health or safety emergency, but only to parties who need the information to protect the student or others, and only for the duration of the emergency. This exception does not authorize routine sharing with law enforcement or general community notification. If you believe an emergency exception applies, involve the administration immediately and document the basis.

## Common Traps

### The Visible Gradebook and Seating Chart

Leaving a gradebook, IEP at-a-glance, or seating chart with accommodations visible on a desk or projector is one of the most common violations. Anyone walking by can read it. Close or cover records when not actively using them, and never display any accommodation or disability information where peers can see it.

### Discussing Students in Shared Spaces

The staff lounge and hallway conversations about a difficult student may feel like professional venting, but they expose the student's information to anyone in earshot, including visitors, volunteers, and students. Save such discussions for private, need-to-know settings.

### Emailing Records to the Wrong Audience

Forwarding an IEP, a behavior report, or a grade summary to a colleague who lacks a legitimate educational interest, or to a personal account for convenience, is a disclosure. Use the minimum necessary recipient list, double-check the recipients before sending, and use secure channels.

### Assuming Digital Tools Are Compliant by Default

Many attractive edtech tools have not signed the data privacy agreements your district requires, and using them with student data can constitute an unauthorized disclosure. Do not enter student names or records into any tool, app, or AI service that has not been approved by your district. When in doubt, check before you type.

### Posting Student Work with Identifiers

Displaying student work is valuable, but names, photos, and other identifiers can turn a celebration into a disclosure, especially for students whose families opted out of directory information. Follow school policy on what may be displayed and how, and obtain consent where required.

### Treating Your Own Notes as Exempt

Personal notes that you alone keep and never share may, in some interpretations, fall outside FERPA, but the moment you share them, place them in a student's file, or use them to make decisions about the student, they become education records. The safest practice is to assume any note you keep about a student is covered.

## Self-Check

- Before I share any student information with a colleague, have I confirmed they have a legitimate educational interest and limited the disclosure to what they actually need?
- Are all physical records I handle secured when not in use, and are all digital records stored only on approved systems rather than personal devices or accounts?
- Have I checked my district's directory information list and opt-out records before using any student name or information in a public display, program, or publication?
- Am I avoiding discussions of identifiable students in hallways, lounges, and social settings where bystanders without a legitimate interest could overhear?
- Have I confirmed that any edtech tool, app, or AI service I use with student data has been approved through my district's privacy review?
- When a parent or student requests to inspect or amend records, am I routing the request through the proper school process rather than handling it informally?
- If an administrator reviewed my practices around gradebooks, emails, shared drives, and displayed work, would they find anything that constitutes an inadvertent disclosure?
