# Selecting And Evaluating Edtech Tools

Educational technology is adopted for many reasons, learning outcomes, engagement, efficiency, trend-following, administrative pressure, and the reason shapes whether the tool helps or harms. A tool chosen because it fits a learning goal and is well-implemented can deepen learning; a tool chosen because it is novel or mandated, without alignment to pedagogy or evaluation of its effects, consumes money and time while producing little. The judgment problem is that edtech evaluation is often driven by features and demos rather than by evidence of learning, and that adoption decisions are frequently irreversible once teachers and students have invested in a platform. The most common failure is selecting tools for their novelty or feature list rather than for their fit with learning goals and their demonstrated effect on outcomes. The skill is evaluating tools against learning goals, evidence, and implementation realities before committing to adoption.

Use this skill before selecting an edtech tool, before evaluating a tool's fit, before deciding among competing platforms, or before diagnosing why an adopted tool failed to improve learning. The goal is to prevent the agent from choosing tools for novelty or features, from adopting without evidence of learning effects, or from ignoring the implementation and equity realities that determine whether a tool succeeds.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Learning Goal, Not The Tool

The most important rule of edtech selection is that the tool serves the goal, not the reverse. Beginning with a tool and searching for a use inverts the logic and produces adoption that is solution-seeking-a-problem. Beginning with the learning goal and asking what, if anything, technology adds keeps the tool in service of learning.

Start with the goal by:

- defining the learning goal and the problem to be solved first;
- asking whether technology adds value over non-technological approaches;
- evaluating tools against the specific goal, not against generic criteria;
- rejecting tools that do not serve a defined learning purpose;
- being willing to conclude that no tool is the right answer.

### Demand Evidence Of Learning Effects

Features and engagement are not evidence of learning. A tool can be engaging and feature-rich while producing no improvement in outcomes, or even undermining them. The question is whether the tool, implemented as intended, improves learning relative to alternatives, and the answer should be sought in evidence, not in marketing.

Demand evidence by:

- asking for research or evaluation data on learning outcomes;
- distinguishing engagement metrics from learning metrics;
- looking for independent evidence, not just vendor studies;
- piloting tools and measuring effects before full adoption;
- treating claims of transformation skeptically until evidenced.

### Evaluate Fit With Pedagogy And Curriculum

A tool that fits one pedagogy may undermine another. A drill-and-practice tool conflicts with inquiry-based learning; an open creation tool may not serve direct instruction. Fit with the classroom's pedagogy and with the curriculum's content and sequence determines whether the tool integrates or conflicts.

Evaluate fit by:

- checking alignment with the classroom's pedagogical approach;
- verifying alignment with curriculum content and standards;
- assessing whether the tool's structure supports or conflicts with teaching;
- considering how the tool fits into existing routines and workflows;
- rejecting tools that require pedagogy to bend to the tool.

### Assess Implementation Realities

A tool that works in a demo may fail in a real classroom. Implementation realities, devices, connectivity, time, teacher capacity, technical support, determine whether a tool can actually be used as intended. Ignoring these realities produces adoption that never reaches its potential.

Assess realities by:

- checking device availability, platform compatibility, and connectivity;
- evaluating the time and training required for teachers and students;
- assessing the availability and quality of technical support;
- considering the burden on IT and administration;
- piloting under real conditions before full adoption.

### Evaluate Data Privacy And Security

Edtech tools collect significant data on students, often including minors, and the privacy and security of that data is a legal and ethical obligation. Tools that cannot demonstrate responsible data practices must not be adopted, regardless of their learning benefits. Privacy is a threshold criterion, not a secondary consideration.

Evaluate privacy by:

- reviewing the tool's data collection, storage, and sharing practices;
- checking compliance with relevant privacy laws for minors;
- verifying whether data is sold, used for advertising, or shared with third parties;
- assessing the vendor's security practices and breach history;
- refusing tools that cannot meet privacy and security thresholds.

### Consider Equity And Access

Edtech can widen or narrow equity gaps. A tool that requires devices or connectivity at home advantages students who have them and disadvantages those who do not. Equity and access must be evaluated before adoption, or the tool will deepen the gaps it was meant to close.

Consider equity by:

- assessing whether the tool requires resources not all students have;
- checking accessibility for students with disabilities;
- ensuring language and cultural appropriateness;
- providing alternatives or support for students with limited access;
- monitoring whether adoption widens or narrows achievement gaps.

### Evaluate Total Cost Of Ownership

The price of a tool is not just its license fee. Total cost includes training, support, integration, device requirements, and the opportunity cost of teacher time. A cheap tool that demands extensive training and support may cost more than a pricier tool that integrates cleanly.

Evaluate total cost by:

- accounting for training, support, and integration costs;
- considering device and infrastructure requirements;
- including the opportunity cost of teacher time;
- comparing total cost across competing tools;
- weighing cost against demonstrated learning benefit.

### Plan For Sustainability And Exit

Adoption is not just a beginning; tools are eventually replaced, merged, or abandoned. A tool that locks data, content, or workflows into a proprietary format creates dependence and makes exit costly. Planning for sustainability and exit at adoption prevents future lock-in.

Plan for sustainability by:

- checking whether data and content can be exported in standard formats;
- assessing the vendor's stability and roadmap;
- avoiding tools that create proprietary lock-in;
- planning for how the tool will be evaluated and potentially replaced;
- treating adoption as reversible, not permanent.

### Pilot Before Full Adoption

Full adoption without piloting is a gamble. Piloting under real conditions, with measurement of effects, reveals whether the tool delivers before the investment is irreversible. Pilots should be structured to produce evidence, not just impressions.

Pilot by:

- piloting with a representative subset of teachers and students;
- defining what the pilot will measure before it begins;
- collecting evidence on learning, implementation, and burden;
- gathering honest feedback from pilot participants;
- deciding on full adoption based on pilot evidence, not enthusiasm.

### Monitor Effects After Adoption

Adoption is not the end of evaluation. Tools should be monitored for their actual effects on learning, engagement, equity, and burden, and those that do not deliver should be revised, replaced, or dropped. Without monitoring, ineffective tools persist by inertia.

Monitor by:

- tracking learning outcomes after adoption;
- gathering regular feedback from teachers and students;
- monitoring usage patterns and equity effects;
- reviewing whether the tool still fits as goals and context evolve;
- being willing to drop tools that do not deliver.

## Common Traps

### Solution Seeking A Problem

Adopting a tool and searching for a use rather than starting from a learning goal.

### Confusing Engagement With Learning

Treating student engagement with a tool as evidence of learning outcomes.

### Ignoring Implementation Realities

Adopting tools that cannot be used as intended given devices, time, or support.

### Neglecting Privacy

Adopting tools without verifying responsible data practices for minors.

### Widening Equity Gaps

Adopting tools that advantage students with resources and disadvantage those without.

### Hidden Costs

Ignoring training, support, and opportunity costs in favor of license fees alone.

### Proprietary Lock-In

Adopting tools that make future exit costly through data and workflow lock-in.

### Adopt And Forget

Failing to pilot or monitor, allowing ineffective tools to persist by inertia.

## Self-Check

- Does selection start from the learning goal, with the tool serving the goal rather than the reverse?
- Is evidence of learning effects demanded, distinguishing engagement from outcomes?
- Does the tool fit the classroom's pedagogy and curriculum content and sequence?
- Are implementation realities, devices, time, support, evaluated before adoption?
- Are data privacy and security verified as a threshold criterion for adoption?
- Does the tool advance rather than widen equity and access gaps?
- Is total cost of ownership, including training and support, evaluated?
- Is sustainability and exit planned to avoid proprietary lock-in?
- Is the tool piloted under real conditions with measurement before full adoption?
- Are effects monitored after adoption, with ineffective tools revised or dropped?
