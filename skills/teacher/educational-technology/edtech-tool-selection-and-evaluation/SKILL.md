# EdTech Tool Selection And Evaluation

Choosing an educational technology tool is a decision with real consequences for learning, privacy, equity, time, and money, and it should be made deliberately rather than by trend. The recurring failure in edtech adoption is chasing the new: a tool appears, generates buzz, and gets adopted because it is engaging or innovative, without anyone asking whether it serves a learning goal the previous tools did not, whether it is safe for students, whether all students can access it, or whether its cost in money, time, and attention is justified. Schools accumulate shelves of unused licenses and students accumulate exposure to tools that took their data and taught them little.

The opposite failure is rejecting all technology reflexively, missing tools that genuinely serve learning. The design challenge is to evaluate tools against the learning goal first, then against safety, equity, and practicality, and to adopt only those that earn their place. This skill applies whenever the work is to select, evaluate, recommend, or reject an educational technology tool.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Learning Goal, Not The Tool

The first question of tool selection is never "is this tool cool" but "what learning goal am I trying to serve, and does this tool serve it better than the alternatives." Begin by defining the instructional need: what students should learn or do, what the current approach lacks, and what a tool would need to provide. Only then evaluate candidate tools against that need.

A tool adopted because it impressed at a conference, without a clear instructional purpose, becomes shelfware or distraction. A tool adopted to serve a defined need has a chance to earn its place. If you cannot state the learning goal a tool serves and why it serves it better than non-digital alternatives, do not adopt it.

### Evaluate Whether The Tool Actually Improves Learning

Engagement is not learning. A tool that captivates students with gamified rewards may produce time-on-task without learning, or even undermine intrinsic motivation. Ask for evidence, not testimonials: does the tool rest on sound learning principles, does it enable something pedagogically valuable that was not possible before, does it support the specific learning outcomes at stake.

Look past the demo. A polished demo shows the tool at its best; the question is how it performs with real students, real content, and real constraints. Pilot before adopting widely, and measure whether the learning goal is better served than before.

### Scrutinize Privacy, Safety, And Data Practices

Educational tools collect data on students, often extensive data, and that data has real value and real risk. Before adopting any tool, examine what data it collects, how it is stored, with whom it is shared, whether it is sold or used for advertising, whether it complies with relevant student privacy laws (such as FERPA and COPPA in the United States, and equivalent laws elsewhere), and whether minors' data is specially protected. Prefer tools with clear, restrictive privacy policies and avoid those that monetize student data.

Do not assume a tool is safe because a school adopted it or because it markets to education. Read the privacy policy, check independent reviews, and consult district policy. The cost of a privacy failure, to students who may live with compromised data for years, is far higher than the benefit of a flashy tool.

### Ensure Equitable Access For All Students

A tool that only some students can use, because of cost, device access, internet at home, language, or disability, worsens inequity rather than reducing it. Before adopting, confirm that all students can access the tool fully: that it runs on the devices they have, that it works for students without home internet if homework depends on it, that it supports the languages your students speak, and that it is accessible to students with disabilities.

Equity is not an afterthought to check at the end; it is a selection criterion from the start. A tool that excludes any group of students is not acceptable, however impressive for the rest.

### Consider Total Cost In Money, Time, And Attention

The price of a tool is not only its license fee. It includes the time to learn it, the time to set it up and manage accounts, the ongoing administrative burden, the class time spent teaching students the tool rather than content, the cognitive load the tool's interface imposes, and the opportunity cost of what else that money and time could fund. A free tool that consumes hours of teacher time is not free.

Weigh total cost against benefit. Sometimes the non-digital alternative, a discussion, a paper activity, a physical manipulative, serves the goal at lower cost and should be preferred.

### Prefer Tools That Fit The Existing Ecosystem

Tools that integrate with the systems already in use, the learning management system, single sign-on, gradebook, and devices, impose far less burden than tools that require separate accounts, logins, and workflows. Each additional separate system is friction for teachers and students and a new place for data to live. Prefer tools that fit, and be skeptical of tools that demand their own parallel infrastructure.

Account fatigue is real. Students juggling a dozen separate logins spend cognitive energy on access rather than learning.

### Plan For Sustainability And Exit

Tools come and go; companies are acquired, shut down, or change terms. Before adopting, consider sustainability: will the tool be available and affordable next year, what happens to student work and data if it is not, and how hard is it to migrate away. Prefer tools that allow export of student work and avoid those that lock data in.

Avoid building curriculum around a single proprietary tool whose loss would break the program. Build around learning goals and standard formats, with tools as interchangeable servants.

### Pilot Before Broad Adoption

Adopt new tools through a pilot with a small group first, measure whether they serve the learning goal, gather feedback from teachers and students, and decide on broader adoption based on evidence rather than enthusiasm. Pilots catch the problems, technical, pedagogical, equity, privacy, that demos and marketing conceal.

A tool that fails a pilot has cost a small amount; a tool adopted widely and then abandoned has cost a great deal.

## Common Traps

### Adoption By Trend

Adopting tools because they are new or buzzed, without a learning goal, produces shelfware. Start with the need.

### Confusing Engagement With Learning

Gamified captivation is not learning. Demand evidence of learning outcomes.

### Ignoring Privacy And Data

Tools that collect and monetize student data carry real, lasting risk. Read the privacy policy.

### Tools That Exclude Some Students

A tool inaccessible to any group worsens inequity. Access for all is a selection criterion.

### Counting Only License Cost

Time, attention, and opportunity cost matter. Weigh total cost.

### Account And System Fatigue

Each separate login and workflow is friction. Prefer tools that fit the ecosystem.

### Lock-In To Proprietary Tools

Building curriculum around one tool whose loss breaks the program is fragile. Plan for exit.

### Broad Adoption Without Pilot

Adopting widely on enthusiasm catches problems too late. Pilot and measure first.

## Self-Check

- Is the tool selected to serve a defined learning goal that non-digital alternatives do not serve as well?
- Is there evidence, beyond testimonials and demos, that the tool improves the target learning outcomes?
- Have privacy, safety, and data practices been scrutinized against student privacy law and good practice?
- Can all students access the tool fully, regardless of device, internet, language, or disability?
- Has total cost in money, time, attention, and opportunity been weighed against benefit?
- Does the tool fit the existing ecosystem, or does it impose parallel infrastructure and account fatigue?
- Is there a plan for sustainability and exit, including export of student work and avoidance of lock-in?
- Has the tool been piloted with a small group and measured before broad adoption?
- Could you defend the adoption of this tool to a skeptical parent or auditor on learning, privacy, and equity grounds?
- Is the tool a servant of the learning goal, or has the learning goal been bent to fit the tool?
