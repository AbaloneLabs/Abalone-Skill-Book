# Scope Sequence Adaptation And Contingency

No curriculum survives a real school year intact. Snow days close school for a week, a flu outbreak empties half the desks, a sudden shift to remote learning breaks the activities you had planned, or a unit that should have taken five days takes nine because students did not understand it the first time. The question is never whether you will have to adapt, but how. The danger is that adaptation becomes panic: cutting whatever falls last on the calendar, racing through what remains, and quietly dropping the synthesis unit that actually mattered most. Good contingency planning decides the adaptation logic in advance, protects core learning under pressure, and compresses or sacrifices only what can be lost without breaking the chain of prerequisites that everything else depends on.

Use this skill when a class has fallen behind, when interruptions have consumed planned time, when a pivot to remote or hybrid is forced, when an entire unit must be restructured mid-stream, or when a teacher must choose what to preserve and what to let go. The goal is to prevent the agent from making reactive cuts that sacrifice the most important learning, or from insisting on full coverage in a way that guarantees shallow treatment of everything.

## Core Rules

### Separate Core From Sacrificial Before The Crisis Hits

The single most important adaptation decision is what you will protect and what you will give up. This decision must be made calmly, before pressure arrives, because under pressure teachers cut whatever is closest to the end of the calendar, which is often the most important synthesis and transfer work.

Define and record three tiers for every unit:

- core learning, the transfer goals and priority standards that later work depends on, which must be preserved under almost any time pressure;
- compressible learning, supporting standards that can be taught more efficiently, combined, or moved to independent work without major loss;
- sacrificial learning, enrichment, optional depth, or isolated supporting content that can be dropped or deferred.

When time is lost, cut from the bottom tier first, compress the middle, and protect the top absolutely. Never let the crisis decide the cut order for you.

### Diagnose The Cause Before Adapting

Different causes of lost time demand different adaptations. Adapting the wrong way wastes the limited time you have.

Match adaptation to cause:

- lost days from closures require compression or cutting, not just shifting;
- mass absence requires reteach and consolidation, not new content;
- slower-than-expected learning requires slowing down and protecting depth, not racing ahead;
- a remote pivot requires redesigning activities for the new medium, not replicating in-person plans online;
- a shortened year requires aggressive prioritization and explicit sacrifice.

A blanket response such as "move everything back a week" usually fails because it ignores why the time was lost and what the new constraints actually are.

### Protect The Prerequisite Chain Above All

When you compress or cut, the prerequisite chain is the thing you cannot break. Cutting a foundational unit to save time destroys every unit that depends on it for the rest of the year.

Before cutting anything, check:

- what later units or grades depend on this learning;
- whether this is a hard prerequisite that gates future work;
- whether cutting it now guarantees failure later;
- whether the cut can be recovered through spiral return or homework.

Never cut a hard prerequisite to hit a date. If you must cut, cut content that nothing else depends on, even if it feels painful to lose.

### Compress By Combining And Streamlining, Not By Speeding Up

Compression does not mean talking faster. It means achieving the same learning in less time by removing redundancy, combining objectives, and streamlining activities.

Compress effectively by:

- merging two related lessons that share a conceptual core;
- converting a multi-day exploration into a more guided experience when time is short;
- moving practice and review to homework or independent stations;
- cutting decorative activities that do not serve the learning target;
- using formative data to skip content students already show they know;
- replacing several small assessments with one cumulative check.

Compression that preserves the core learning target is legitimate. Compression that removes the thinking students need to do is just coverage.

### Decide Explicitly Between Depth And Breadth Under Pressure

When time is short, you cannot have both depth and breadth. The choice must be conscious, not the accidental result of racing.

Choose depth when:

- the unit contains priority standards and transfer goals;
- later learning hard-depends on this understanding;
- shallow treatment would produce misconception;
- the content is conceptually difficult and resists quick coverage.

Choose breadth only when:

- the content is supporting and isolated;
- exposure rather than mastery is the genuine goal;
- the content will be spiraled and developed again later.

Default to depth on priority standards and breadth on supporting ones. Never sacrifice depth on the core to maintain breadth on the periphery.

### Build Reteach And Consolidation Into Recovery

When learning has been disrupted, students arrive at the next unit with shaky foundations. Racing into new content guarantees compounding confusion. Build recovery into the adaptation.

Recover by:

- inserting a diagnostic to reveal what was actually learned during disruption;
- reteaching only the specific gaps the diagnostic shows, not the whole unit;
- consolidating key skills through interleaved practice before moving on;
- using small-group instruction for students who fell further behind;
- spiraling the disrupted content into later units rather than abandoning it.

Recovery is faster when it is targeted. A blanket reteach of everything wastes the little time you have.

### Redesign, Do Not Relocate, For A Remote Pivot

A pivot to remote or hybrid learning is not a relocation of in-person plans. Activities that work in a classroom often fail online, and vice versa. Trying to replicate the in-person day remotely exhausts everyone and produces little learning.

Redesign for the medium by:

- identifying which objectives can be met asynchronously and which need synchronous time;
- converting discussion to structured online discourse with clear prompts;
- replacing hands-on activities with simulations, home materials, or video models;
- shortening synchronous sessions to respect screen-time limits;
- building in more frequent formative checks since you cannot read the room;
- ensuring accessibility for students with limited devices, connectivity, or home support;
- protecting equity, since remote learning widens gaps along existing lines of advantage.

Plan the pivot as a redesign problem, not a logistics problem. The learning targets stay; the path to them changes.

### Communicate Adaptations To Students, Families, And Colleagues

Adaptation that is invisible to stakeholders creates confusion and erodes trust. Students need to know what changed and why; families need to know what to expect; colleagues need to know so they can align.

Communicate:

- what is being prioritized and what is being adjusted, in plain language;
- why the change was made and what it means for assessment and grades;
- what students and families should focus on and what can be let go;
- how the change affects later units and the rest of the year;
- to colleagues, which standards are being compressed so vertical alignment holds.

Silent adaptation leaves everyone guessing. Named adaptation keeps the community aligned.

### Record Adaptations For Next Year's Planning and preserve Assessment Integrity While Adapting

Every adaptation is data about where the original plan was unrealistic. If you do not record it, next year repeats the same crisis.

Record:

- which units ran long and which had slack;
- which interruptions actually occurred and how you responded;
- which cuts preserved learning and which you regretted;
- which compressions worked and which damaged understanding;
- what you would sequence or weight differently next time.

Next year's scope and sequence should be built from this year's reality, including the adaptations you were forced to make.

When you compress or cut, the assessments must change too, or they will test content you no longer taught. An assessment that covers sacrificed content is unfair and produces meaningless data.

Adjust assessment by:

- aligning summative tasks to what was actually prioritized and taught;
- removing or down-weighting items on sacrificed content;
- adding items that check the prerequisite foundations you protected;
- keeping common assessments comparable across sections if colleagues adapted differently;
- being transparent with students about what will and will not be assessed.

Adapt the assessment to the adapted curriculum, never the reverse.

## Common Traps

### Cutting Whatever Is Last On The Calendar

The last unit is often the synthesis and transfer work that matters most. Decide the cut order by priority in advance, not by position.

### Protecting Coverage Over Core Learning

Racing to "get through everything" produces shallow treatment of all of it. Choose depth on priority standards under pressure.

### Breaking The Prerequisite Chain

Cutting a foundational unit to save time destroys every unit that depends on it. Never cut a hard prerequisite.

### Compressing By Speeding Up

Talking faster does not compress learning; it removes processing. Compress by combining, streamlining, and cutting redundancy.

### Replicating In-Person Plans Online

A remote pivot requires redesign for the medium, not relocation of activities. Trying to copy the classroom day remotely fails.

### Racing Past Shaky Foundations

After disruption, students arrive unprepared. Diagnose, reteach gaps, and consolidate before pushing into new content.

### Silent Adaptation and testing Content You Did Not Teach

When students, families, and colleagues do not know what changed, trust erodes and alignment breaks. Name the adaptation plainly.

Compressing the curriculum without adjusting the assessment is unfair. Align summative tasks to what was actually prioritized.

### Forgetting To Record What Happened and treating Adaptation As Failure

Unrecorded adaptations force next year to repeat the same crisis. Annotate reality so future planning is honest.

Adapting is the job, not a confession of weakness. A rigid plan that ignores reality harms learning more than a thoughtful cut.

## Self-Check

- Are units tiered as core, compressible, and sacrificial, with a cut order decided before pressure arrives?
- Is the adaptation matched to the actual cause of lost time rather than applied as a blanket shift?
- Is the prerequisite chain protected so that no hard prerequisite is cut to save time?
- Is compression achieved by combining and streamlining, not by speeding up instruction?
- Has the depth-versus-breadth choice been made consciously per standard under pressure?
- Is recovery built in through diagnosis, targeted reteach, and consolidation of disrupted content?
- For a remote pivot, has the curriculum been redesigned for the medium rather than relocated?
- Have adaptations been communicated clearly to students, families, and colleagues?
- Have adaptations been recorded to inform next year's scope and sequence?
- Have assessments been adjusted to align with what was actually prioritized and taught?
