# Prerequisite And Skills Sequencing

Sequencing is not arranging topics in the order a textbook lists them. It is the deliberate construction of a learning path in which each new idea attaches to something already built, and in which no skill is required before the skill it depends on has been established. When sequencing is wrong, the damage is invisible to the teacher and obvious to the student: a lesson "doesn't make sense," students appear to lack ability when they actually lack a prerequisite, and reteach cycles multiply because the foundation was never poured. Good sequencing is mostly an act of dependency analysis, asking constantly what must be true before this can be learned, and then refusing to teach anything whose prerequisites are not yet solid.

Use this skill before ordering objectives within a unit, before sequencing units across a year, before diagnosing why a lesson is failing, or when reviewing whether a curriculum's order is defensible. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a sequence that looks logical on the surface but breaks the prerequisite chain, teaches abstractions before their concrete grounding, or assumes mastery that was never confirmed.

## Core Rules

### Map The Dependency Chain Before Ordering

Before you decide what comes first, you must know what depends on what. Most sequencing errors come from never doing this analysis at all and instead copying the order of a published resource or the order topics were learned historically.

Build a dependency map by asking of each skill:

- what must a student already be able to do before this skill is learnable;
- what conceptual understanding must already exist;
- what vocabulary must already be owned;
- what procedural fluency is assumed;
- what would make this skill impossible to learn if missing.

Record the prerequisites explicitly. If skill C requires skill B which requires skill A, the order is fixed by the chain, not by preference. Sequencing decisions are only free where no dependency constrains them.

### Distinguish Hard From Soft Prerequisites

Not every prerequisite is equally binding. Confusing the two leads either to rigid over-sequencing or to reckless jumps.

Hard prerequisites are skills or concepts without which the new learning is genuinely impossible or will produce deep misconception. For example, you cannot learn to add fractions with unlike denominators without understanding equivalent fractions. These must be solid before moving on.

Soft prerequisites are skills that make the new learning easier or faster but are not strictly required. For example, fluency with multiplication facts speeds fraction work but does not gate conceptual understanding. These can be developed in parallel or revisited.

Mark each prerequisite as hard or soft. Protect the hard ones ruthlessly; allow flexibility on the soft ones.

### Sequence Concrete Before Abstract, Known Before Unknown

Cognitive development moves from concrete to representational to abstract, and from the familiar to the unfamiliar. Sequencing that reverses this forces students to memorize symbols whose meaning they do not have.

Apply the progression:

- begin with concrete objects, real contexts, or manipulable models;
- move to representations such as drawings, diagrams, or tables;
- move to symbolic or abstract notation;
- connect back so the abstract is understood as a compression of the concrete.

Likewise, anchor new content to what students already know. Introduce a new concept through a familiar context, then generalize. Teaching the abstract rule first and hoping students infer the concept produces procedural mimicry without understanding.

### Choose Between Mastery And Spiral Sequencing Deliberately

There are two broad sequencing logics, and they suit different content and different goals. Choosing without thinking produces the worst of both.

Mastery sequencing teaches a skill to a target proficiency before moving on. It suits procedural skills with clear prerequisites, content where later learning hard-depends on earlier mastery, and foundational skills that must be automatic. Its risk is that some students never reach mastery and stall, and that long gaps before return produce forgetting.

Spiral sequencing revisits concepts repeatedly at increasing depth over time. It suits conceptual understanding, content that matures with repeated exposure, and curricula that value long-term retention. Its risk is shallow treatment on each pass and students never reaching depth if the spiral moves too fast.

Decide per standard which logic fits. Many strong curricula use mastery for procedural foundations and spiral for conceptual big ideas, with spiral returns reinforcing earlier mastery.

### Check For Out-Of-Order Teaching Against Assessment Data

A sequence can look logical and still be wrong for your students, because their actual prior knowledge differs from the assumed prior knowledge. Use diagnostic data to check.

Look for signals of sequencing failure:

- a lesson that consistently confuses the majority of students;
- students who can do the procedure but cannot explain it;
- wide variation in readiness that suggests missing foundations;
- error patterns that point to a specific prerequisite gap;
- a unit where reteach consumes most of the time.

When most of a class struggles, suspect a prerequisite gap before suspecting effort or ability. Diagnose what is missing and decide whether to teach it now, resequence for next time, or both.

### Sequence Vocabulary And Concepts Alongside Skills

Academic vocabulary is often a hidden prerequisite. A lesson that uses terms students do not own will fail regardless of how well the skills are sequenced, because students cannot follow the reasoning built on those words.

Sequence language by:

- identifying terms essential to each skill before teaching it;
- front-loading or pre-teaching critical vocabulary;
- using the term consistently across the sequence;
- requiring students to use the term in their own explanations;
- revisiting terms as they recur in later units.

Treat vocabulary as part of the dependency chain, not as decoration added at the end.

### Sequence For Conceptual Coherence, Not Topic Convenience

It is tempting to group topics by surface similarity or by textbook chapter, but conceptual coherence matters more. Skills that share a deep structure should be sequenced together even if they look different, and skills that look similar but rest on different foundations should be separated.

Ask:

- do these skills share a conceptual structure that should be taught together;
- are these superficially similar skills actually built on different prerequisites;
- does teaching them adjacent create confusion or reinforce understanding;
- would interleaving them later strengthen discrimination.

For example, area and perimeter look similar but rest on different concepts; teaching them back to back without care produces persistent confusion. Sequence for the underlying logic, not the surface appearance.

### Build Vertical Coherence Across Grades

Sequencing within a year is only half the problem. A skill taught this year assumes skills from prior years and feeds skills in future years. A sequence that ignores the vertical chain creates gaps and redundancies across grade bands.

Check vertically:

- what the prior grade actually taught and to what depth;
- what the next grade expects students to arrive knowing;
- whether this year's sequence bridges the two without repetition;
- whether vocabulary and models are consistent across grades;
- whether priority standards are introduced, developed, or mastered at this level.

Coordinate with the grades above and below. A unit that reteaches what students already know wastes time; a unit that assumes what was never taught guarantees failure.

### Reserve Free Sequence Decisions For Purposeful Choice and make The Sequence Transparent And Defensible

Where the dependency chain does not constrain order, you have freedom. Use it purposefully rather than arbitrarily.

Use free decisions to:

- align with seasonal or community context that makes content relevant;
- build toward a culminating performance or integration point;
- balance cognitive load across the year so heavy units are not adjacent;
- connect to cross-curricular work happening in other classes;
- match the developmental readiness of your specific students.

Freedom in sequencing is not an excuse to be random. It is an opportunity to optimize for context the dependency chain does not capture.

A sequence should be explainable to anyone who asks why this comes before that. If you cannot defend the order, the order is probably inherited rather than designed.

Document for each unit:

- the prerequisites it assumes and where they were taught;
- what it enables in later units;
- why it is placed here and not elsewhere;
- which sequencing logic, mastery or spiral, it follows;
- where the hard prerequisites are and where flexibility exists.

A transparent sequence survives staff turnover, supports substitutes and specialists, and lets the next teacher continue rather than rebuild.

## Common Traps

### Copying A Textbook Order Without Analysis

Published orders are often defensible but sometimes driven by tradition or marketing. Map the dependencies yourself rather than inheriting the order uncritically.

### Teaching Abstract Before Concrete

Introducing the symbolic rule before the concrete model produces memorization without meaning. Move from concrete to representational to abstract.

### Assuming Mastery That Was Never Confirmed

A prerequisite listed as taught is not the same as a prerequisite students actually own. Verify with data before building on it.

### Treating All Prerequisites As Equal

Hard prerequisites must be solid before moving on; soft ones can develop in parallel. Confusing the two produces either rigidity or recklessness.

### Choosing Spiral Or Mastery By Habit

Each suits different content. Decide deliberately per standard rather than defaulting to one logic for everything.

### Grouping By Surface Similarity

Skills that look alike may rest on different foundations and confuse students when adjacent. Sequence by deep structure, not appearance.

### Ignoring Vocabulary As A Prerequisite

A lesson built on unowned terms fails regardless of skill sequencing. Treat language as part of the dependency chain.

### Siloed Sequencing Across Grades and inherited Order With No Defense

A unit that ignores what came before and after repeats content or assumes foundations never taught. Coordinate vertically.

If you cannot explain why this comes before that, the sequence is accidental. Document the reasoning so it can be reviewed and improved.

## Self-Check

- Has a dependency map been built that records what each skill requires before it can be learned?
- Are prerequisites distinguished as hard, requiring solid mastery, or soft, allowing parallel development?
- Does the sequence move from concrete to representational to abstract, and from known to unknown?
- Is the choice between mastery and spiral sequencing made deliberately per standard, not by habit?
- Has assessment data been used to check whether assumed prior knowledge is actually present?
- Is essential vocabulary sequenced and front-loaded as part of the dependency chain?
- Are skills grouped by conceptual coherence rather than surface similarity?
- Is the sequence coordinated vertically with the prior and next grades to avoid gaps and redundancy?
- Are unconstrained sequencing decisions used purposefully for context, not arbitrarily?
- Can the order of every unit be defended and explained to a colleague or successor?
