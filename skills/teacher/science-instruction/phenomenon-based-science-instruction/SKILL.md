# Phenomenon-Based Science Instruction

Phenomenon-based instruction organizes a science unit around an observable event or process that students must explain: why do shadows change length over a day, why does a coast have tides, why did a particular bridge collapse, why do some ice cubes melt faster. The phenomenon is not a hook to be enjoyed and discarded; it is the problem that drives the entire unit, and the unit is complete when students can explain it using the science they have built. The premise is that learning science in order to explain something real produces more durable, transferable understanding than learning the same content as isolated facts, because the content is acquired as the tools needed to solve a problem the student has a stake in.

The recurring failure is the decorative phenomenon: a striking image or video shown at the start to engage students, then abandoned as the unit reverts to textbook content with no further reference to the phenomenon. The second failure is the phenomenon too abstract or remote for students to investigate, so it functions as a riddle they cannot gain traction on. The design challenge is to choose phenomena that are observable, puzzling, and explainable with the target science, and to weave them through the unit so every lesson contributes to the explanation. This skill applies whenever the work is to design or evaluate phenomenon-based science instruction.

## Core Rules

### Choose Phenomena That Are Observable, Puzzling, And Explainable

A strong anchoring phenomenon has three qualities. It is observable: students can see it, experience it, or examine authentic data about it, so it is concrete rather than abstract. It is puzzling: it raises a genuine why or how question that students cannot immediately answer, creating the need for investigation. And it is explainable: the target science of the unit can actually account for it, so building the science builds the explanation.

Weak phenomena fail one of these tests. A phenomenon too familiar is not puzzling. A phenomenon too abstract or remote cannot be investigated. A phenomenon the target science cannot explain will leave the unit unresolved. Test candidate phenomena against all three criteria.

### Use The Phenomenon As The Unit-Long Problem, Not A One-Day Hook

The defining feature of phenomenon-based instruction is that the phenomenon drives the whole unit. Each lesson should contribute a piece of the explanation: a concept, a practice, a piece of evidence that moves students toward being able to account for the phenomenon. The unit is complete when students can construct the explanation, not when the textbook content is covered.

Return to the phenomenon repeatedly. Begin lessons by recalling what we still need to explain, develop a piece of science, and connect it back to the phenomenon. If a lesson has no connection to the phenomenon, either it should or the unit is not actually phenomenon-based.

### Build A Coherent Storyline Across Lessons

A storyline is a planned sequence in which each lesson answers a question raised by the previous one, building toward the full explanation of the phenomenon. Rather than a list of topics, the unit becomes a narrative of investigation: students notice something about the phenomenon, generate questions, investigate to answer them, develop concepts, and apply them back to the phenomenon, progressively closing the gap between what they observed and what they can explain.

Design the storyline by working backward from the full explanation: what concepts and evidence are needed, in what order, so that each piece is motivated by the questions the previous piece raised. A disconnected sequence of lessons, each touching the phenomenon independently, is not a storyline.

### Integrate The Three Dimensions Intentionally

Three-dimensional instruction weaves together disciplinary core ideas (the science concepts), science and engineering practices (the doing of science), and crosscutting concepts (the big ideas like cause and effect, systems, patterns, energy and matter that span disciplines). The phenomenon is the context in which all three are engaged: students use practices to investigate, develop core ideas to explain, and apply crosscutting concepts to see the structure beneath the phenomenon.

Plan deliberately for all three dimensions. Name the practices a lesson engages, the core ideas it develops, and the crosscutting concepts it surfaces. A lesson that addresses only the core idea, treating practices and crosscutting concepts as incidental, is one-dimensional instruction with a phenomenon attached.

### Let Student Questions Shape The Pathway

While the storyline is planned, it should respond to the questions students actually raise about the phenomenon. Elicit, surface, and honor student questions; use them to prioritize which pieces of the explanation to develop next. Students who see their own questions driving the investigation are invested in a way that teacher-imposed questions cannot produce.

This does not mean abandoning the plan; it means using student questions as the route through the planned concepts, navigating flexibly while keeping the destination in view.

### Require Students To Construct The Explanation

The unit culminates not in a teacher-delivered summary but in students constructing their own explanation of the phenomenon, using the science they have built and citing the evidence they gathered. The explanation is the assessment: can students account for the phenomenon with the core ideas, supported by evidence, and structured by the crosscutting concepts.

If the teacher tells the explanation at the end, students learn that the investigation was theater and the real answer comes from authority. Require students to do the explaining.

### Use Investigative Phenomena To Build Pieces Of The Explanation

The anchoring phenomenon is often too complex to investigate directly. Use smaller, investigable phenomena throughout the unit to build the pieces: a simple experiment that reveals one concept, a data set that shows a pattern, a model that makes a mechanism visible. Each investigative phenomenon contributes a piece that students assemble into the explanation of the anchoring phenomenon.

Connect each investigative phenomenon explicitly to the anchor. Students should see how the piece they just learned helps explain the bigger puzzle.

### Make The Crosscutting Concepts Visible As Thinking Tools

Crosscutting concepts are not vocabulary to memorize; they are lenses through which scientists see structure across phenomena. Make them visible as tools: ask students to look for cause and effect, to identify the system and its boundaries, to trace energy and matter, to notice patterns. Over a year, students should internalize these lenses and apply them to new phenomena independently.

Name the crosscutting concept when students use it, so it becomes a deliberate tool rather than an accident.

## Common Traps

### The Decorative Phenomenon

A striking image shown once and then abandoned as the unit reverts to textbook content. The phenomenon must drive the whole unit.

### Phenomenon Too Abstract Or Remote

A phenomenon students cannot observe or investigate becomes a riddle they cannot gain traction on. Choose observable, investigable phenomena.

### Phenomenon The Science Cannot Explain

If the target science cannot account for the phenomenon, the unit ends unresolved. Match phenomenon to the science to be built.

### Disconnected Lessons Touching The Phenomenon Independently

A list of topics each referencing the phenomenon is not a storyline. Build a coherent sequence where each lesson answers the previous.

### One-Dimensional Instruction With A Phenomenon Attached

Engaging only the core idea while practices and crosscutting concepts are incidental is not three-dimensional. Plan all three.

### Teacher Delivers The Final Explanation

If the teacher tells the answer at the end, the investigation was theater. Students must construct the explanation.

### Ignoring Student Questions

Imposing all questions from the plan denies students investment. Use their questions to navigate the pathway.

### Crosscutting Concepts As Vocabulary

Treating patterns or systems as terms to memorize misses their role as thinking tools. Make them visible as lenses students use.

## Self-Check

- Is the anchoring phenomenon observable, puzzling, and explainable with the target science?
- Does the phenomenon drive the entire unit, returned to in each lesson, rather than appearing only as a hook?
- Is the unit organized as a coherent storyline where each lesson answers a question raised by the previous?
- Are all three dimensions, core ideas, practices, and crosscutting concepts, planned and engaged intentionally?
- Do student questions shape the pathway through the planned concepts?
- Do students construct the final explanation themselves, citing evidence and using the science they built?
- Are investigative phenomena used to build pieces that assemble into the explanation of the anchor?
- Are crosscutting concepts made visible as thinking tools students apply, not vocabulary they memorize?
- Could a student explain, at the end of the unit, how the phenomenon works and what evidence supports their explanation?
- Is the unit genuinely phenomenon-based, or is it a conventional unit with a phenomenon pasted on?
