# Differentiation By Readiness

Differentiating by readiness means responding to where students actually are in relation to a specific learning goal, not sorting children into permanent ability tracks. The most common failure is to confuse readiness, which is task-specific and changes over time, with fixed intelligence or general worth. A student who struggles with fraction addition may be advanced in geometric reasoning; a student who reads below grade level may reason far above it. Readiness differentiation that quietly locks the same students into the low group every day, every subject, every year becomes tracking, and tracking tends to harden inequity rather than fix it. The second common failure is to lower the goal for struggling students and merely add volume for advanced ones, which denies both groups real learning.

Use this skill before designing tiered tasks, pre-assessments, flexible groups, scaffolds, or extensions. The goal is to help the agent keep the same meaningful learning target in view for every student while varying the difficulty, scaffolding, and pace needed to reach it, and to do so in ways that preserve dignity and avoid stigma.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Every Differentiation Decision To A Single Clear Learning Target

Readiness differentiation is impossible without a crisp learning target. If you cannot state what students should know and be able to do by the end, you cannot decide who needs more support or more challenge, and you cannot tell whether a variation is an accommodation, a scaffold, or an accidental lowering of the goal.

Before tiering anything, write down:

- the specific standard or competency being addressed;
- the essential understanding or skill that is non-negotiable;
- what mastery looks like, concretely, in student work;
- which parts of the task are flexible and which define the learning;
- how you will know each student reached the target.

A vague target like "understand fractions" cannot be tiered honestly. A target like "add and subtract fractions with unlike denominators by finding a common denominator" can. Do not differentiate a lesson whose goal you have not first made precise.

### Base Grouping On Evidence, Not Labels Or Impressions

Readiness groups must be formed from current evidence about this specific skill, not from last year's data, a deficit label, behavior, or a hunch. Students move between groups as evidence changes.

Use as evidence:

- a short pre-assessment or diagnostic focused on the target;
- exit tickets from prior lessons;
- analysis of recent student work;
- a quick check for understanding at the start of the lesson;
- observation of strategy use during a task.

Avoid forming groups from cumulative labels like "the low group" or from composite measures that do not speak to this skill. A student labeled struggling overall may have strong readiness for this particular concept. Re-sort frequently so that grouping reflects current learning, not identity.

### Tier Tasks Up And Down Around The Same Goal

Tiering means offering parallel routes to the same destination. The struggling route adds scaffolding toward the same target; the advanced route increases depth, complexity, or independence without sending the student to a different, unrelated destination.

For learners who need more support, tier by:

- providing worked examples and partially completed models;
- chunking the task into smaller steps with checkpoints;
- offering manipulatives, visuals, or graphic organizers;
- front-loading key vocabulary and prerequisite skills;
- giving sentence frames or strategy prompts;
- reducing the number of practice items while keeping the same skill.

For learners who demonstrate readiness for more, tier by:

- asking open-ended or transfer problems that apply the same skill in a new context;
- adding constraints that increase complexity;
- requiring justification, generalization, or proof;
- offering problems with multiple valid solution paths;
- inviting independent inquiry that extends the same concept;
- asking students to critique, compare, or create examples.

The test of a good tier is that every student is working on the same essential learning. If the top group is doing algebra while the bottom group is still on whole-number operations, that is tracking, not tiering, unless the team has deliberately decided on a modification through the proper process.

### Keep Tasks Respectful

Respectful tasks are equally interesting, equally important, and do not signal who is smart and who is not. The single fastest way to wound a classroom is to hand the struggling group a visibly easier, uglier, more childish worksheet while the advanced group gets the "fun" challenge.

Make tasks respectful by:

- ensuring all tiers look similarly appealing and substantial;
- using the same rich context or real-world situation across tiers;
- avoiding labels like "easy," "hard," "below," or "challenge" on student-facing materials;
- letting any student attempt a higher tier if they choose;
- giving each group work that looks and feels like real thinking, not busywork.

Respect also means not using advanced students as unpaid assistant teachers. Occasional peer explanation can deepen the explainer's learning, but sustained tutoring in place of their own learning is exploitative and denies them challenge.

### Use Flexible, Fluid Grouping

Groups should form, dissolve, and reform based on the lesson's purpose. The same student might be in a support group for decimals, a standard group for geometry, and an extension group for data analysis within the same week.

Principles for flexible grouping:

- name groups by the task, the station, or a neutral label, never by ability;
- change membership as evidence shifts;
- use whole-class, small-group, partner, and individual structures across a unit;
- avoid always placing the same students together or always separating particular students;
- let students sometimes choose their own level of challenge once trust is built.

Fixed, public, ability-ranked groups stigmatize students and cap their growth. If a student is always in the lowest group, they internalize a ceiling.

### Raise The Target For Advanced Learners Without Just Adding More

Advanced readiness is not a reward to be paid out in extra problems. More of the same teaches bright students that learning faster just means more labor, which breeds resentment and coasting.

Challenge advanced learners with:

- depth, probing why a method works and where it fails;
- complexity, problems with more variables or interrelated parts;
- abstraction, moving from concrete to general cases;
- transfer, applying the skill to unfamiliar contexts;
- creation, designing problems, proofs, or models;
- critique, evaluating others' reasoning or flawed examples;
- pace, compacting already-mastered content to free time for extension.

Compacting is a powerful tool: pre-assess, excuse students from content they have already mastered, and use the freed time for genuine extension rather than additional drill.

### Scaffold Struggling Learners Toward The Same Goal

Support should be a ladder to the target, not a lower target. The aim is fading, not permanence.

Scaffold by:

- identifying the specific prerequisite gap blocking progress;
- providing temporary supports such as prompts, models, or organizers;
- teaching the missing strategy explicitly;
- checking for understanding at each step;
- planning how and when supports will be removed;
- celebrating progress toward the same standard, not a lowered one.

If a student consistently cannot reach the grade-level target even with strong scaffolding over time, that is a signal to consult specialists and possibly the IEP team about a deliberate, documented modification. It is not a license to silently lower expectations on your own.

### Plan For Logistics And Time

Differentiation fails in practice when the logistics collapse. A teacher cannot run four simultaneous lessons.

Manage logistics by:

- designing tasks students can do independently or with peers while you teach a small group;
- using stations, menus, or choice boards so students are never idle;
- keeping teacher-led time focused on the students who most need direct guidance;
- preparing all materials in advance, including the extension;
- building routines so students know what to do when stuck, finished, or absent;
- budgeting time realistically; over-ambitious tiering leads to rushed, unfinished lessons.

### Coordinate With Required Plans And Specialists

Readiness decisions must not contradict a student's IEP, 504 plan, English learner plan, or behavior plan. When a student has a required plan, follow it and coordinate with the case manager or specialist before changing grouping, supports, or expectations.

## Common Traps

### Tracking Disguised As Differentiation

Permanent, public, same-students-always low groups are tracking. They depress achievement and reinforce inequity. Keep groups fluid, evidence-based, and neutrally labeled.

### Lowering The Goal Instead Of Adding The Ladder

Giving struggling students an easier objective silently denies them grade-level learning. Scaffold toward the same target, and reserve true modifications for team-authorized, documented decisions.

### More Work For Advanced Learners

Extra volume teaches bright students that efficiency is punished. Offer depth, complexity, and transfer instead of more of the same.

### Grouping From Labels Instead Of Current Evidence

Composite labels and last year's data misplace students for this skill. Re-sort from fresh evidence each unit or lesson.

### Disrespectful Task Design

A visibly easier, less appealing task for the struggling group broadcasts who is "smart." Make all tiers look equally substantial and interesting.

### Using Advanced Students As Permanent Tutors

Peer explanation has value, but sustained tutoring in place of a student's own learning is exploitation. Protect advanced learners' right to challenge.

### Differentiating Without A Clear Target

You cannot tier honestly around a fuzzy goal. Make the learning target precise before designing any tiers.

### Over-Scaffolding That Never Fades

Permanent crutches prevent independence. Plan from the start how supports will be removed as competence grows.

## Self-Check

- Is there a single precise learning target that every tier is working toward, stated before any differentiation is designed?
- Are readiness groups formed from current evidence about this specific skill, and will they change as evidence changes?
- Do struggling learners receive scaffolding toward the same goal rather than a lowered goal, with a plan for fading support?
- Do advanced learners receive depth, complexity, and transfer rather than additional volume of the same work?
- Are all tiers respectful, equally appealing, and free of public ability labels on student-facing materials?
- Is grouping flexible and fluid, avoiding permanent same-students-always low groups?
- Are logistics planned so the rest of the class is productively engaged while a small group is taught?
- Do any required plans, IEP, 504, English learner, behavior, take precedence over informal differentiation choices, with specialists consulted?
- Is there a clear way to know whether each student reached the target, regardless of which tier they used?
- Have you avoided silently modifying expectations for a student who needs a true modification without team authorization and documentation?
