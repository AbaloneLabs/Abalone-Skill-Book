# Differentiated Instruction And Accessibility

Differentiated instruction is not making many separate lessons for many separate students. It is planning flexible access to important learning for students who differ in readiness, language, background knowledge, disability, attention, pace, and interest. Accessibility is not an afterthought added after materials are finished. If a student cannot access the directions, text, visuals, discussion, tool, or task format, the lesson may be measuring barriers rather than learning.

Use this skill before adapting a lesson, creating materials for mixed-readiness groups, planning accommodations, supporting English learners, preparing accessible handouts or slides, designing small-group instruction, or deciding whether a change is an accommodation, modification, or enrichment. The goal is to make the agent preserve meaningful learning while removing unnecessary barriers.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Learning Target Before Adapting

Adaptations should protect the core learning target. If the objective is argument writing, supports may help students organize claims and evidence. If the objective is decoding, reading the text aloud may change what is being assessed. Know what must remain intact.

Clarify:

- target skill or concept;
- non-negotiable learning demand;
- flexible parts of the task;
- acceptable supports;
- assessment evidence;
- individual plan requirements;
- grade-level expectation;
- modified expectation if applicable.

Do not simplify the task so much that students lose access to the intended learning.

### Distinguish Accommodation, Modification, And Scaffold

These terms matter. An accommodation changes access without changing the learning expectation. A modification changes the expectation. A scaffold provides temporary support toward the same goal.

Examples:

- extended time is often an accommodation;
- text-to-speech may be an accommodation depending on objective and plan;
- sentence frames can be a scaffold;
- reducing the number of problems may be an accommodation if the same skill is tested;
- changing grade-level content to a lower-level objective is a modification.

Follow IEP, 504, school policy, and specialist guidance. Do not invent changes that conflict with required plans.

### Use Student Data And Required Plans

Differentiation should be based on evidence, not stereotypes or assumptions.

Use:

- IEP or 504 accommodations;
- English learner proficiency data;
- formative assessment;
- student work;
- reading level where relevant;
- prior knowledge;
- behavior support plan;
- assistive technology plan;
- family or specialist input where appropriate;
- student self-report;
- observation.

Protect privacy. Do not disclose disability, language status, or support plans to peers.

### Remove Barriers In Materials

Materials should be accessible before instruction begins. Review the actual handout, slide, video, tool, and platform.

Check:

- readable font size;
- clear layout;
- sufficient contrast;
- alt text for meaningful images;
- captions or transcripts for video;
- headings and structure;
- plain directions;
- uncluttered visuals;
- screen reader compatibility where needed;
- keyboard navigation where needed;
- translation or language support where appropriate;
- print and digital access.

Avoid decorative complexity that makes the task harder without improving learning.

### Provide Multiple Ways To Access Content

Students may need varied representations to understand the same concept.

Use:

- visuals;
- models;
- manipulatives;
- examples and nonexamples;
- oral explanation;
- written explanation;
- demonstration;
- graphic organizer;
- vocabulary preview;
- background knowledge activation;
- worked example;
- peer explanation.

Multiple representations should connect to the same idea. Do not scatter unrelated supports.

### Provide Multiple Ways To Show Learning

When the objective allows, let students demonstrate understanding through different formats.

Options:

- written response;
- oral explanation;
- diagram;
- model;
- recorded explanation;
- annotated work;
- performance;
- conference;
- project component.

Keep the evidence aligned. If writing mechanics are the objective, oral response may not be enough. If science reasoning is the objective, requiring polished essay structure may create an unnecessary barrier.

### Plan Flexible Grouping

Grouping should serve instruction. Fixed low groups can stigmatize students and lower expectations.

Use groups for:

- targeted reteach;
- vocabulary support;
- strategy practice;
- peer discussion;
- enrichment;
- teacher conference;
- collaborative problem solving.

Vary groups over time. Use data, task, and student needs. Avoid assigning the same students to the same low-status roles.

### Support Language Development

English learners and students developing academic language need access to both content and language.

Plan:

- content vocabulary;
- language functions such as compare, justify, infer, argue;
- sentence frames;
- visuals;
- partner rehearsal;
- native language resources where appropriate;
- wait time;
- explicit modeling;
- chances to speak, listen, read, and write.

Do not confuse language development with lower thinking ability. Keep cognitive demand meaningful while supporting language.

### Include Advanced Learners Without Only Adding More Work and coordinate With Specialists And Families

Advanced learners need depth, complexity, choice, and challenge, not simply extra problems.

Use:

- extension questions;
- open-ended tasks;
- transfer problems;
- independent inquiry;
- alternative texts;
- leadership with clear learning purpose;
- creative constraints;
- critique and revision;
- real-world application.

Do not use advanced students as unpaid tutors in a way that replaces their own learning.

Some adaptations require collaboration. Teachers should not work alone when plans, services, or safety issues are involved.

Coordinate with:

- special education teacher;
- English learner specialist;
- counselor;
- occupational therapist;
- speech-language pathologist;
- assistive technology specialist;
- school psychologist;
- family or guardian according to policy;
- administrator when needed.

Document required supports and concerns through the school's process.

## Common Traps

### Lowering Expectations Instead Of Removing Barriers

Students may need access support, not easier work. Preserve the important learning target whenever possible.

### Confusing Equal With Fair

Giving everyone the same material may not be fair if some students cannot access it. Fairness means appropriate access to meaningful learning.

### Treating Accommodations As Optional

Required accommodations are not suggestions. Follow the student's plan and document concerns through proper channels.

### Over-Scaffolding

Too much support can prevent productive thinking. Plan how supports will fade as students gain independence.

### Publicly Identifying Support Needs

Do not announce accommodations, disability status, language level, or intervention group in a way that stigmatizes students.

### Using Technology Without Accessibility Checks

Digital tools can create barriers. Check captions, navigation, compatibility, login access, and privacy.

### Giving Advanced Students More Of The Same

More volume is not the same as deeper learning. Add complexity, choice, and transfer.

## Self-Check

- Is the core learning target clear before adaptations are chosen?
- Are accommodations, modifications, and scaffolds distinguished correctly?
- Are IEP, 504, English learner, assistive technology, and behavior plans followed where applicable?
- Are materials accessible in text, visual, audio, digital, and physical formats?
- Are multiple representations used to support the same concept?
- Can students show learning in varied ways when the objective allows?
- Are groups flexible, purposeful, and non-stigmatizing?
- Are language supports included without lowering cognitive demand?
- Are advanced learners challenged with depth and transfer rather than extra volume?
- Are specialists, families, and school processes involved when required?
