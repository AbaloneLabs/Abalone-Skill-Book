# Small Group And Targeted Instruction

Small-group instruction is one of the highest-leverage moves a teacher can make, and also one of the easiest to execute badly. The promise is direct, responsive teaching of a few students who share a specific need. The reality is that running a focused small group requires simultaneously managing the other twenty-something students, and that is where the practice usually breaks down. A teacher huddled with six students while the rest of the class drifts, misbehaves, or does busywork is not differentiation; it is neglect wearing a costume. The central judgment problem is therefore not only what to teach the small group, but how to engineer the whole room so that every student is learning, the teacher can actually attend to the group, and the grouping is fluid, evidence-based, and non-stigmatizing.

Use this skill before planning small-group lessons, station rotations, intervention blocks, or reteach cycles. The goal is to help the agent design targeted instruction that is purposeful, time-bounded, and embedded in a classroom where everyone, including the students not currently with the teacher, is productively engaged.

## Core Rules

### Define The Specific Purpose Of Each Small Group

A small group without a sharp purpose becomes supervised worksheet time. Before forming any group, name the precise instructional reason it exists.

Purposes may include:

- targeted reteach of a skill missed on recent evidence;
- pre-teaching vocabulary or a concept to students who will need it;
- guided practice with immediate feedback on a new skill;
- enrichment or extension for students who have mastered the core;
- language support for multilingual learners around a specific task;
- strategy instruction, such as a comprehension or problem-solving routine;
- progress monitoring and conferring.

Write the purpose as a single sentence tied to evidence: "These four students added instead of multiplying when finding common denominators, so this group reteaches the concept behind common denominators." If you cannot state the purpose in one evidence-based sentence, the group is not ready to meet.

### Base Membership On Current Evidence And Keep It Fluid

Small groups are not permanent assignments. They form from recent, specific evidence and dissolve or reform as that evidence changes.

Use as evidence:

- exit tickets and quick checks from the current unit;
- analysis of student work for specific error patterns;
- a diagnostic or pre-assessment focused on the target;
- observation of strategy use during independent or partner work;
- conferring notes.

Re-sort frequently. A student who needed reteach yesterday may be ready for extension today. Avoid the trap of the same students always being pulled for "help" and the same students always being left alone, which hardens into tracking and stigma. Use neutral labels, group by the task or station name, and never announce ability publicly.

### Engineer The Rest Of The Class First

The single most important rule: plan what the other students are doing before you plan what the small group will do. If the rest of the class is not independently productively engaged, the small group will not happen.

Design independent and collaborative structures such as:

- station rotations where each station has a clear task, materials, and timer;
- meaningful independent practice tied to already-taught skills;
- partner or cooperative learning with accountable roles;
- choice boards or menus students can navigate without help;
- technology-based practice with clear login and task routines;
- reading, writing, or problem-solving centers with familiar routines.

The independent work must be genuinely valuable, not filler, and students must be able to do it without interrupting you. Build and teach the routines for independent work over weeks before expecting a smooth small-group block.

### Teach And Rehearse The Routines

Small-group time succeeds or fails on routines. Students must know exactly what to do, where to go, what materials they need, what to do when stuck, what to do when finished, and how to get help without disrupting the teacher.

Establish and rehearse:

- transition procedures between stations or tasks;
- voice levels and movement expectations;
- what to do when finished early;
- how to ask for help, such as "ask three before me";
- how to handle technology or material problems;
- what the teacher-led group signal means, such as a do-not-interrupt light.

Practice these routines when you are not running a group, then maintain them with consistent reinforcement. Routines decay without attention.

### Keep Teacher-Led Groups Short, Focused, And Feedback-Rich

A small group is not a mini-lecture. Its power is proximity, observation, and immediate feedback. Keep groups short, often eight to fifteen minutes, and tightly focused on the specific need.

Within the group:

- open with a quick check of the target skill;
- model or reteach the specific gap with a clear example;
- have students practice while you observe and coach;
- give immediate, specific feedback on each student's work;
- close with a quick check for understanding to decide next steps;
- send students off with clear independent practice tied to the group.

Avoid the trap of talking the whole time. The value is in what students do and say while you watch and respond.

### Use Station Rotation To Multiply Teacher Time

Station rotation lets one teacher reach multiple groups in a block by moving students through cycles while one station is teacher-led.

Design rotations by:

- keeping the number of stations manageable, usually three to four;
- making each non-teacher station genuinely independent and valuable;
- timing rotations equally and posting the timer visibly;
- ensuring every student reaches the teacher-led station within the cycle;
- having a clear, practiced transition procedure;
- planning what students who finish early will do.

Watch the math of time. Four groups of fifteen minutes require a full hour plus transitions. Be honest about what fits in your block, and do not overbuild.

### Match Intervention To The Multi-Tiered Framework

Targeted intervention in most schools sits within a multi-tiered system of supports. Know where your small-group instruction fits and what it is not.

- Tier 1 is strong core instruction for all students.
- Tier 2 is targeted small-group support for students not yet successful with Tier 1, time-limited and progress-monitored.
- Tier 3 is intensive, often individualized support, frequently tied to a special education referral or IEP.

If a student is not responding to your Tier 2 group over a reasonable period, that is a signal to use the school's referral and data-review process, not to simply keep pulling the student indefinitely. Document the intervention, its frequency, and the progress data, because this evidence matters for decisions about further support.

### Coordinate With Specialists And Required Plans

Small-group decisions must align with any IEP, 504, English learner, or behavior plan. A student's required services, such as pull-out time with a specialist, take precedence and must not be missed for your informal group. Coordinate with the special educator, interventionist, or English learner specialist so that grouping is complementary, not contradictory. Share what you observe; their data and yours together form the picture of the student's need.

### Monitor Behavior, Engagement, And Dignity and manage Time Realistically

While teaching a small group, you must still monitor the room. A common failure is becoming so absorbed in the group that the rest of the class unravels.

Maintain awareness by:

- positioning yourself to see the room;
- using brief scans and proximity between group turns;
- reinforcing routines with quick, calm redirection;
- never ignoring disruptive or unsafe behavior to finish a group;
- protecting the dignity of students in the targeted group by keeping their needs private.

A student pulled repeatedly for help can feel exposed. Use neutral language, vary who is invited, and never publicly rank students.

Time is the scarce resource. Over-planning is the norm and it sabotages the practice.

Budget time by:

- limiting the block to what your schedule actually allows;
- trimming the number of groups to what you can reach with quality;
- preparing all materials in advance, including independent tasks;
- building in transition and buffer time;
- accepting that some days the group will not happen and having a fallback.

## Common Traps

### Small Group Without A Purpose

Pulling students for generic "help" or supervised worksheets wastes time. Name a specific, evidence-based instructional purpose.

### Neglecting The Rest Of The Class

If the other students are off-task or doing busywork, the small group is failing even if it looks focused. Engineer independent learning first.

### Permanent, Stigmatizing Groups

The same students always pulled, always labeled, always "low," is tracking. Keep groups fluid, neutral, and evidence-based.

### Over-Talking In The Group

A small group is not a lecture. Its value is student practice with immediate feedback, not teacher talking.

### Overbuilding The Rotation

Too many stations or groups for the time available leads to rushed, unfinished cycles. Be honest about what fits.

### Ignoring Behavior To Finish A Group

Absorbed teaching while the room unravels is not acceptable. Maintain awareness and redirect calmly.

### Indefinite Intervention Without Progress Monitoring

Pulling a student forever without data is not intervention. Monitor progress and use the school's referral process when a student does not respond.

### Conflicting With Required Plans and filler Independent Work

Informal groups must not override IEP, 504, or specialist services. Coordinate so supports complement each other.

If the non-teacher tasks are not genuinely valuable, the whole structure loses its purpose. Make every station meaningful.

## Self-Check

- Does each small group have a single, evidence-based instructional purpose stated in one sentence?
- Is group membership based on current evidence, fluid, neutrally labeled, and free of permanent ability tracking?
- Have you planned what the rest of the class will do before planning the small group, and is that work genuinely valuable and independent?
- Are transition, voice-level, help-seeking, and early-finisher routines taught and rehearsed?
- Is each teacher-led group short, focused on the specific gap, and dominated by student practice with immediate feedback rather than teacher talk?
- Does any station rotation fit realistically within the time block, with every student reaching the teacher station?
- Is your targeted instruction correctly situated within the school's multi-tiered framework, with progress monitored and documented?
- Do required plans and specialist services take precedence over informal grouping, with coordination rather than conflict?
- Are you maintaining awareness of the whole room and protecting the dignity and privacy of students in targeted groups?
- Have you budgeted time and materials realistically, with a fallback for days the group cannot run?
