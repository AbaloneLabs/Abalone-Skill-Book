# Relationship Skills And Conflict Resolution

Relationship skills is the CASEL competency concerning the ability to establish and maintain healthy and rewarding relationships with diverse individuals and groups. It includes communicating clearly, listening actively, cooperating, resisting inappropriate social pressure, negotiating conflict constructively, seeking and offering help, and repairing relationships when they are damaged. These skills are foundational to learning, because most classroom learning is social, and to life, because almost everything of consequence is done with other people. The recurring failure is to assume relationship skills are absorbed by proximity: put students in groups and they will "learn to work together," or run a one-off conflict-resolution lesson and consider the competency covered. They will not, and it is not. Relationship skills develop through explicit instruction, structured practice, and, critically, through how the teacher handles conflict and repair in real time. A classroom where conflict is punished rather than repaired teaches students to avoid or escalate conflict, not to resolve it.

The opposite failure is to manage all conflict for students, intervening to fix every dispute so students never develop the skills themselves, or to enforce superficial harmony that suppresses real disagreement. The skill is to teach communication and repair explicitly, design collaboration that builds relationships, handle conflict as a learning opportunity, and build a culture of belonging where healthy relationships can grow. This skill applies whenever the work is to design collaborative learning, respond to peer conflict, teach communication, or build classroom community.

## Core Rules

### Teach Communication And Active Listening Explicitly

Communication and listening are skills, notgivens. Teach them directly: how to state a viewpoint clearly and respectfully, how to disagree without dismissing, how to ask clarifying questions, how to listen to understand rather than to reply, how to read nonverbal cues, and how to adjust communication for different audiences and contexts. Use structured protocols (think-pair-share, conversation moves, sentence stems, active-listening scripts) that make the skills visible and rehearseable. Name and coach the skills in the moment when students use them well or poorly. Communication skills transfer only when taught, practiced, and reinforced across contexts.

### Design Collaboration That Builds Relationships Rather Than Straining Them

Group work can build relationship skills or destroy them, depending on design. Ungrouped, unsupported group work with unclear roles and no accountability often produces conflict, freeloading, and resentment that erode relationships. Design collaboration deliberately: clear task and goals, defined roles and accountability, structures for equal participation, scaffolds for giving and receiving feedback, group contracts or norms co-created by students, and reflection on how the group functioned. Teach cooperation as a content of the lesson, not just the container. When collaboration is well designed, students build relationships through the work; when it is not, they damage them.

### Handle Conflict As A Learning Opportunity, Not Just A Discipline Event

Conflict is inevitable wherever humans work together; the question is whether it becomes a learning opportunity or a punishment event. Treat ordinary peer conflict as a chance to teach resolution skills: help students name what happened from multiple perspectives, identify needs and feelings, generate options, and agree on a repair. Reserve punitive discipline for genuine harm that requires it, and even then pair it with repair. When conflict is only punished, students learn to hide it, escalate it, or avoid the relationships entirely; when it is coached, they learn to navigate it.

### Teach And Use Restorative Practices For Repair

Restorative practices focus on repairing harm and restoring relationships rather than only on assigning punishment. Teach and use restorative language and circles: What happened? What were you thinking at the time? Who has been affected and how? What do you need to make things right? Restorative approaches build the skills of empathy, accountability, and repair, and they reduce recidivism compared with purely punitive approaches. Use them for both minor conflicts and more serious harm, and ensure staff are trained so the practices are genuine rather than performative.

### Build Belonging And Inclusion As The Soil Relationships Grow In

Relationships grow in soil of belonging. Students who feel they belong, that they are known, valued, and that they matter, are far more able to form healthy relationships and far less likely to act out of exclusion or anxiety. Build belonging deliberately: learn students' names and lives, affirm their identities and cultures, create inclusive norms, address exclusion and bias directly, and ensure every student has at least one strong adult relationship and positive peer connections. Belonging is not a soft extra; it is the precondition for the academic and social work of school.

### Teach Perspective-Taking And Empathy As Skills

Perspective-taking, the ability to see a situation from another's point of view, and empathy, the ability to connect with what they feel, are core to relationships and are teachable. Use literature, history, role-play, structured disagreement, and real classroom incidents to practice taking another's perspective, especially across difference. Teach students to distinguish empathy (understanding and caring) from agreement (condoning), so they can empathize without abandoning their own perspective. Perspective-taking reduces conflict and deepens relationships.

### Teach Help-Seeking And Help-Offering As Relational Skills

Asking for help and offering it are relationship skills, not signs of weakness or charity. Teach students how to ask for help productively (specific, timely, appropriate) and how to offer it respectfully (without taking over or condescending). Build structures (peer support, partners, study groups, classroom jobs) that make help-seeking and offering normal and frequent. A classroom where help flows freely is a classroom of strong relationships and stronger learning.

### Model Repair And Healthy Conflict As An Adult

Students learn relationship skills from how the adults around them handle conflict and repair. Model them openly: when you make a mistake or have a conflict with a student, repair it visibly ("I was wrong to speak to you that way; I am sorry, and here is what I will do differently"). Show healthy disagreement with colleagues and students. Avoid suppressing all conflict or pretending relationships are frictionless; show that friction, handled well, is normal and survivable.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Group Work Teaches Collaboration Automatically

Putting students in groups with no structure, roles, or coaching, and assuming they will learn to collaborate. Ungrouped work often produces conflict, freeloading, and resentment. Design and coach collaboration explicitly.

### Punishing Conflict Instead Of Coaching It

Treating every peer conflict as a discipline matter to be punished, so students learn to hide or escalate conflict rather than resolve it. Coach ordinary conflict as a learning opportunity.

### Managing All Conflict For Students

Intervening to fix every dispute so students never develop resolution skills. Step back where safe, coach students through resolution, and reserve direct management for genuine harm.

### Superficial Harmony That Suppresses Disagreement

Enforcing a norm of "everyone gets along" that pushes real disagreement underground, where it festers. Make space for productive disagreement and teach how to do it respectfully.

### Restorative Practices As Performance

Using restorative language without the underlying shift in practice, so circles become compliance rituals rather than genuine repair. Train staff and implement restorative approaches authentically.

### Ignoring Exclusion And Bias

Letting patterns of exclusion, cliques, or bias go unaddressed, which erodes belonging for targeted students and teaches the class that exclusion is tolerated. Address exclusion and bias directly and build inclusive norms.

### Empathy Collapsed With Agreement

Treating empathy as condoning the other's behavior, so students refuse to empathize. Teach that empathy is understanding and caring, distinct from agreement, and that it is possible across disagreement.

### Adults Who Do Not Model Repair

Teachers who never visibly repair their own mistakes or conflicts, teaching students that repair is something only they have to do. Model repair openly to legitimize the practice.

## Self-Check

- Do I teach communication and active listening explicitly, with protocols and stems, and coach them in the moment?
- Is the collaborative learning I design structured (clear roles, accountability, norms, reflection) so it builds rather than strains relationships?
- Do I treat ordinary peer conflict as a learning opportunity for resolution skills rather than only as a discipline event?
- Do I use restorative practices authentically, with trained staff, to repair harm and restore relationships?
- Do I build belonging deliberately (knowing students, affirming identity, addressing exclusion) as the soil in which relationships grow?
- Do I teach perspective-taking and empathy as skills, and distinguish empathy from agreement?
- Do I teach and normalize help-seeking and help-offering as relational skills, with structures that make them frequent?
- Do I model repair and healthy conflict as an adult, including repairing my own mistakes visibly with students?
