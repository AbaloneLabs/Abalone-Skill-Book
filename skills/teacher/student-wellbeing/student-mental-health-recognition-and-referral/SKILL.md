# Student Mental Health Recognition And Referral

Teachers are not clinicians, but they are often the first adult to notice that a student is struggling with their mental health, and they are frequently the bridge to the professional help a student needs. The work here is recognition and referral, not diagnosis or treatment. The recurring failure is one of two opposite errors: either a teacher notices warning signs and does nothing, assuming it is "just teenage behavior" or someone else's job, or a teacher oversteps the role by attempting to diagnose, counsel, or carry a student's mental health burden alone. Both errors harm students. The teacher's job is to notice, to respond with care and within competence, to connect the student to the right help, and to follow mandated reporting duties. Doing this well can be decisive in a young person's life; doing it poorly can miss a crisis or worsen one.

This skill is written for the classroom teacher's scope of practice. It does not train you to be a therapist. It applies whenever the work is to recognize possible mental health concerns, respond supportively, refer to school support staff and families, or decide when a situation requires immediate escalation.

## Core Rules

### Know Your Role: Recognize And Refer, Do Not Diagnose Or Treat

Be precise about the boundary of your competence. You can notice changes, express care, listen within limits, and connect a student to help; you cannot diagnose (depression, anxiety, ADHD, trauma), provide therapy, or promise confidentiality you cannot keep. Telling a student "I think you have anxiety" is outside your role and can mislead, stigmatize, or delay proper assessment. Describe what you observe ("I've noticed you seem down and have stopped turning in work") and route to professionals. Holding the boundary protects the student and protects you from carrying a clinical load you are not equipped for.

### Learn The Warning Signs And Take Changes Seriously

Mental health concerns rarely arrive labeled; they show up as changes from a student's baseline. Watch for sustained changes in mood (persistent sadness, irritability, flatness, anxiety), behavior (withdrawal from friends or activities, drop in performance, loss of interest, increased conflict), physical signs (changes in sleep, appetite, weight, hygiene, frequent somatic complaints like headaches or stomachaches), and talk or content (hopelessness, references to death or self-harm, dark writing or artwork, giving away possessions). A single sign in isolation may be nothing; a cluster, a sustained change, or any mention of self-harm warrants attention. Take changes seriously rather than dismissing them as phases.

### Respond With Care, Calm, And Within Limits

When a student discloses or you initiate a check-in, respond in a way that keeps the door open without overstepping. Listen more than you talk. Validate the feeling without minimizing or rushing to fix ("That sounds really hard; thank you for telling me"). Avoid false reassurance ("Everything will be fine"), judgment, or interrogation. Be honest about confidentiality limits up front or as soon as disclosure begins: you care, and because you care you may need to involve others to keep them safe. Do not promise to keep secrets you cannot keep.

### Know And Follow Mandatory Reporting Duties

In most jurisdictions, educators are mandated reporters for suspected abuse or neglect, and many jurisdictions extend duties around self-harm, harm to others, or threats of violence. Know your specific duties, your school's protocol, and the timeline. When a disclosure or observation triggers a duty, follow the protocol promptly and document it; do not investigate it yourself, do not delay to "gather more evidence," and do not promise confidentiality. Mandatory reporting is a legal and ethical obligation, not a judgment call.

### Know Your Referral Pathway Before You Need It

In a crisis you will not have time to figure out who to call. Before any concern arises, know your school's referral pathway: who the counselor, psychologist, social worker, and administrator are; how to reach them quickly; what information they will want; and what happens after a referral. Build relationships with these colleagues so referral is a warm handoff, not a cold form. Know the after-hours and community resources for concerns that arise outside school.

### Partner With Families Appropriately And Without Surprising Them

Families are central to a student's support, but disclosure to families requires judgment. For most concerns, partner with the family with the student's knowledge and, where possible, their involvement. For mandated-reporting situations, follow protocol, which may mean contacting authorities rather than the family first. Avoid blindsiding a student by calling home without warning when a gentler, prepared conversation is possible, and avoid promising a student you will not tell their parents when safety requires that you do. When you do contact families, describe observable behaviors and connect them to support, rather than delivering a diagnosis.

### Avoid Carrying It Alone: Use Your Team And Self-Care

Supporting students with mental health concerns is emotionally taxing, and teachers who carry it alone risk burnout, secondary stress, and compromised judgment. Use your team: consult counselors, debrief with trusted colleagues, and access employee assistance resources. Maintain the boundary between caring and being a student's sole support. Your wellbeing is part of the system that protects students.

### Document Observations Factually And Promptly

When you notice warning signs, make a referral, or have a concerning conversation, document what you observed and did, factually and without interpretation, in line with school policy. Note dates, observable behaviors, your actions, and who you notified. Documentation protects the student (creating a pattern that triggers support), protects you, and ensures continuity if staff change.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Signs As "Just Adolescence"

Writing off sustained changes in mood, withdrawal, or performance drops as normal teenage behavior. Some changes are normal; clusters or sustained shifts are not. When in doubt, check in and consult; the cost of a needless check-in is far lower than the cost of a missed crisis.

### Diagnosing Or Counseling Outside Competence

Telling a student what they "have," offering therapeutic techniques as treatment, or becoming the student's primary support. This oversteps the role, can mislead or harm, and delays proper care. Describe observations and refer.

### Promising Confidentiality You Cannot Keep

Saying "this stays between us" to encourage disclosure, then being unable to honor it when safety requires escalation. This breaks trust and can endanger the student. Be honest about limits from the start.

### Missing Or Delaying Mandatory Reporting

Hesitating to report suspected abuse or self-harm because you are unsure, want more evidence, or fear being wrong. Mandated reporting does not require certainty; it requires a reasonable suspicion and following protocol. When in doubt, consult the designated reporter immediately.

### No Referral Pathway Known In Advance

Discovering only at the moment of crisis that you do not know who to call or what the protocol is. Learn the pathway before you need it, and build relationships with support staff.

### Blindsiding Students Or Families

Contacting families without preparing the student when a gentler approach was possible, or vice versa, telling a student you will handle it and then surprising them with a call home. Plan disclosures thoughtfully and involve the student where you can.

### Carrying The Load Alone

Trying to be the student's sole support to "be there for them," without consulting counselors or accessing your own support. This risks your wellbeing and produces worse outcomes than a coordinated team approach.

### Documenting Interpretation As Fact

Recording opinions ("student seems depressed") rather than observable behaviors ("student cried in class, declined to participate, mentioned feeling hopeless"). Stick to what you observed and what you did.

## Self-Check

- Am I clear that my role is to recognize and refer, not to diagnose or treat, and have I avoided labeling a student with a clinical term?
- Do I know the warning signs of common concerns (anxiety, depression, trauma, self-harm) and am I taking sustained changes from a student's baseline seriously?
- When a student discloses, do I listen, validate, and stay honest about confidentiality limits rather than promising secrecy I cannot keep?
- Do I know my mandatory reporting duties, my school's protocol, and the timeline, and do I follow it promptly when triggered?
- Have I learned the referral pathway (counselor, psychologist, social worker, administrator, community resources) before I need it?
- When involving families, do I do so thoughtfully, with the student's awareness where possible, and focused on observable behaviors and support?
- Am I using my team and my own self-care rather than carrying a student's mental health burden alone?
- Do I document observable behaviors, my actions, and notifications factually and promptly, in line with school policy?
