# Bilingual And Multilingual Support

Bilingualism is not two monolinguals in one mind, and a multilingual student is not a defective English speaker. The student who mixes languages, reaches for a word in the wrong tongue, or writes a draft in one language before translating is often demonstrating sophisticated linguistic awareness, not confusion. Teachers trained only in monolingual assumptions often misread these behaviors as deficits: they treat code-switching as error, forbid the native language in the name of English immersion, or assume a long-term English learner who speaks fluently but writes weakly is simply lazy. Strong bilingual support treats the student's full linguistic repertoire as a single integrated resource, leverages cross-linguistic transfer, and addresses the specific profiles of students whose paths through language and schooling have been uneven.

Use this skill when planning for bilingual and multilingual learners, choosing translanguaging strategies, supporting newcomers or students with interrupted schooling, working with long-term English learners, or deciding how to honor and use students' languages rather than suppress them.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Full Linguistic Repertoire As One Resource

A multilingual speaker does not keep languages in separate sealed rooms. They draw on an integrated repertoire, and the boundary between languages is fluid and strategic. Translanguaging, the deliberate use of all of a student's languages to make meaning, is a normal and powerful practice: discussing a concept in the native language, reading in English, writing notes bilingually, and producing a final product in the target language. Allow and structure translanguaging rather than policing language boundaries, and recognize that strategic language mixing often reflects advanced metalinguistic awareness.

### Leverage Cross-Linguistic Transfer

Skills, concepts, and strategies developed in one language transfer to another. A student who can summarize in their native language already holds the concept of summarizing; the task is to attach English forms to that existing skill. Cognates, shared structures, literacy strategies, content knowledge, and learning habits all transfer positively when explicitly bridged. Point out cognates, ask students to identify what they already know in L1, and build bridges between languages rather than treating English as a fresh start that ignores prior knowledge.

### Recognize And Address Long-Term English Learners Distinctly

Long-term English learners, students who have been classified as English learners for five or more years, present a distinct profile. They often speak conversational English fluently, have strong cultural identity in English-speaking peer groups, yet lack academic language, read below grade level, struggle with formal writing, and may have gaps in schooling from years of watered-down instruction. They are frequently misidentified as unmotivated or low-ability when the real issue is unmet academic language development. Provide explicit academic language instruction, targeted literacy intervention, and meaningful grade-level content rather than continued low-level placement, and address the affective dimension of students who have spent years feeling stuck in a category.

### Support Newcomer Students With Targeted Onboarding

Newcomer students, recently arrived immigrants and refugees, face layered challenges: new language, new culture, possible trauma, family separation, disrupted schooling, and unfamiliar school systems. Provide a structured onboarding that includes orientation to school routines, a welcoming peer or mentor, assessment of prior schooling and literacy in L1, native-language access to key content where possible, and connection to counseling and family services. Do not assume the student's English level reflects their prior knowledge; assess content knowledge in L1 where feasible. Be alert to signs of trauma and follow school referral procedures.

### Address Limited Or Interrupted Formal Education Explicitly

Students with limited or interrupted formal education may lack foundational literacy and numeracy not because of disability but because schooling was disrupted by war, displacement, migration, or poverty. These students need explicit foundational instruction, often in their native language first, alongside content access. Distinguish limited prior schooling from learning disability, since the supports differ. Coordinate with specialists, allow time for foundational skill growth, and avoid conflating slow English acquisition with cognitive limitation.

### Build On Rather Than Suppress Native-Language Literacy

Native-language literacy is one of the strongest predictors of second-language academic success. A student who reads and writes well in L1 brings transferable skills that accelerate English growth. Where students are literate in L1, build on it through bilingual texts, translation, and explicit comparison of languages. Where students are not yet literate in L1, prioritize either L1 literacy or English literacy strategically depending on age, goals, and available resources, and avoid undermining the L1 in the process.

### Use Code-Switching As A Diagnostic And Instructional Tool

When a student switches languages mid-sentence, they are often signaling which language holds the concept more efficiently, where a vocabulary gap exists, or where the cognitive load is heaviest. Treat code-switching as data: it reveals the student's stronger language for a given concept and points to where bridging is needed. Use it instructionally by asking the student to explain in L1 and then jointly constructing the English version, turning a perceived error into a learning opportunity.

### Honor Linguistic Identity And Counter Deficit Framing

Language is tied to identity, family, culture, and self-worth. Framing a student's native language as a problem to be overcome, or treating bilingualism as a temporary condition to be resolved into English, damages identity and slows learning. Name and value the languages students bring, include bilingual and multicultural materials, invite families to share language and culture, and counter deficit narratives among staff and peers. A student who feels their languages are assets engages more deeply than one who feels they are being remediated.

### Distinguish Bilingual Support From Special Education Needs

Bilingual students are both over- and under-identified for special education, often because language difference is mistaken for disability or disability is masked by language difference. Before referring a bilingual student, ensure that observed difficulties persist in the native language as well as English, that sufficient time and appropriate instruction have been provided, and that the assessment uses appropriate language and accommodations. Coordinate with the ELL specialist and follow district procedures for culturally and linguistically responsive evaluation.

### Coordinate With Families Across Language And Culture

Families of multilingual learners are partners, not obstacles. Communicate in the family's preferred language using qualified interpreters, not student siblings or machine translation for high-stakes messages, and provide translated documents. Understand that families may have different expectations of school, different experiences with authority, or constraints such as work schedules and immigration concerns. Build trust through consistent, respectful, two-way communication that treats families as knowledgeable about their children.

## Common Traps

### Policing Language Boundaries And Forbidding L1

Forbidding the native language cuts students off from their strongest thinking tool, slows acquisition, and damages identity. Structure translanguaging instead.

### Misreading Code-Switching As Error

Strategic language mixing often reflects advanced metalinguistic awareness and reveals where bridging is needed. Use it as data, not as a deficit.

### Treating Long-Term English Learners As Unmotivated

Fluent social English with weak academic language is a profile of unmet need, not laziness. Provide explicit academic language and literacy support.

### Assuming English Level Reflects Prior Knowledge

A newcomer may know advanced content in L1 that English cannot yet reveal. Assess content knowledge in the native language where feasible.

### Conflating Interrupted Schooling With Disability

Limited formal education produces gaps that look like disability but require different support. Investigate schooling history before referring.

### Undermining Native-Language Literacy

Strong L1 literacy accelerates English growth. Build on it rather than treating English literacy as a replacement project.

### Deficit Framing Of Bilingualism

Treating the native language as a problem to overcome damages identity and engagement. Frame languages as assets and honor linguistic identity.

### Inappropriate Special Education Referral

Referring before ruling out language difference, or missing a real disability masked by language, both harm the student. Use culturally responsive evaluation procedures.

### Using Ad Hoc Translation For High-Stakes Communication

Student siblings and machine translation are inappropriate for IEP meetings, discipline, or grades. Use qualified interpreters and translated documents.

## Self-Check

- Do you treat the student's full linguistic repertoire as one integrated resource and structure translanguaging rather than policing language boundaries?
- Do you explicitly leverage cross-linguistic transfer through cognates, shared strategies, and bridges between languages?
- For long-term English learners, do you provide explicit academic language and literacy support rather than continued low-level placement or deficit labels?
- Do newcomers receive structured onboarding, peer support, L1 content access, and trauma-aware referral where needed?
- Are students with limited or interrupted formal education given foundational literacy and numeracy support distinct from, and not conflated with, special education?
- Is native-language literacy built upon as an asset that accelerates English growth rather than undermined?
- Is code-switching used as diagnostic and instructional data rather than corrected as error?
- Is linguistic identity honored through multilingual materials, family involvement, and counter-deficit framing among staff?
- Before any special education referral, have you ruled out language difference, observed difficulty in L1, and used culturally responsive evaluation?
- Is family communication conducted in the preferred language with qualified interpreters and translated documents for all high-stakes matters?
