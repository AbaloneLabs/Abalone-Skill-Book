# Lesson Materials And Resource Curation

Selecting materials for a lesson is not a shopping task. It is a series of judgments about whether a text, task, manipulative, worksheet, or tool will actually carry the intended learning to the specific students in the room. The recurring failure is choosing materials because they look polished, match the topic, or are conveniently free, while ignoring whether they are readable by the students, free of bias that will harm or alienate them, legally usable, accessible to learners with disabilities, and equitably usable given the devices and bandwidth the class actually has. A beautiful resource that is two grade levels above the readers, or that assumes one-to-one devices the class does not have, will quietly sink a lesson whose plan looked perfect.

Use this skill before choosing, adapting, or building any material for a lesson: a reading passage, a problem set, a worksheet, a manipulative, a video, a simulation, an app, or an open educational resource. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating curation as a quick lookup instead of the multi-dimensional evaluation it actually is. This skill concerns the materials themselves; it does not replace the design of the lesson architecture or the learning objective.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Learning Target, Not From The Resource

The most common curation error is finding an appealing resource first and then justifying its use. The target must dictate the material, never the reverse.

Before searching, fix:

- the specific learning objective the material must serve;
- the cognitive demand the material must let students practice;
- what students must produce or do with the material;
- what evidence the material must generate for the teacher.

A resource that is engaging but does not let students practice the target demand is the wrong resource, however well made. Reject the temptation to bend the objective to fit a popular tool.

### Match Readability And Language Load To The Actual Students

A text or task that is too hard to read becomes, in practice, a reading test rather than a test of the intended learning. Readability is an instructional decision, not a cosmetic one.

Evaluate readability against the real class:

- check lexile or other quantitative measures against the grade and the actual readers;
- consider the qualitative complexity, structure, vocabulary density, and knowledge demands;
- account for multilingual learners whose comprehension may lag their oral proficiency;
- distinguish the reading demand that is the target from reading demand that is incidental;
- reduce incidental reading load so it does not become the real barrier.

If the objective is science reasoning, a dense passage should not turn the lesson into a decoding struggle. Adapt the text, provide glossed vocabulary, or choose a more accessible version that preserves the cognitive target.

### Review For Bias, Representation, And Cultural Responsiveness

Materials carry messages about who belongs and whose experience matters. A resource can be technically accurate and still harm students through stereotyping, invisibility, or deficit framing.

Review materials for:

- representation of cultures, genders, abilities, family structures, and contexts;
- stereotyping or tokenism in examples, names, images, and scenarios;
- deficit framing of communities or historical events;
- assumptions about prior knowledge, holidays, or home resources that exclude some students;
- whether students in the class can see themselves and others respectfully represented.

Do not assume a published resource is neutral. Adapt or replace materials that alienate the students they are meant to serve. When in doubt, consult colleagues, families, or community members closest to the affected group.

### Resolve Copyright And Licensing Before Use

A resource found online is not automatically legal to copy, modify, or redistribute. Licensing errors create real liability for teachers and schools and are often invisible until a problem appears.

Verify before use:

- the license, such as all-rights-reserved, Creative Commons, public domain, or school-purchased;
- what the license permits: viewing, copying, modifying, redistributing, commercial use;
- attribution requirements and whether you can satisfy them;
- whether a purchased or subscription resource permits reproduction for a class;
- whether student-facing reuse, such as posting online or in portfolios, is allowed;
- terms of service for digital tools, especially regarding student data.

Open educational resources can reduce cost and allow adaptation, but only if their specific license is respected. When a resource's status is unclear, treat it as unusable until confirmed, not as usable until challenged.

### Ensure Accessibility For Learners With Disabilities

Accessibility is a legal obligation and an instructional necessity. A material that works for most students but blocks a student with a disability fails that student entirely.

Check accessibility:

- digital texts should support screen readers, reflow, and adjustable contrast and size;
- images, diagrams, and videos need alt text, descriptions, or captions;
- color should not be the only way information is conveyed;
- documents should have navigable structure and readable fonts;
- audio and video need transcripts or captions;
- manipulatives and physical materials need tactile or adapted alternatives where required.

Match materials to the specific accommodations in IEPs and 504 plans, and build accessibility in from the start rather than retrofitting it under pressure. Retrofitting is where students get left behind.

### Resolve Device And Bandwidth Equity

A tool that requires one-to-one devices, reliable internet, or accounts will disadvantage students whose access is limited. Equity must be checked at the material level, not assumed.

Ask:

- how many devices are actually available, and are they shared across classes;
- whether the tool works offline or on low bandwidth;
- whether it requires accounts, logins, or personal data from students, raising privacy concerns;
- whether students without home access can complete any assigned portion at school;
- whether a low-tech or no-tech alternative reaches the same learning target.

If a digital tool is central, have a concrete fallback for students who cannot access it. A lesson that silently assumes every student has a device and connection is not yet equitable.

### Prefer Adaptable Over Fixed Materials

Fixed, all-rights-reserved materials force the teacher to accept the resource as-is. Adaptable materials let the teacher adjust readability, examples, length, and cultural fit for the actual class.

Prefer resources that allow:

- editing text to adjust reading level or replace biased examples;
- trimming length to fit the real time budget;
- adding scaffolds such as glossaries, sentence frames, or graphic organizers;
- translating or providing bilingual supports;
- remixing with other sources to fit the target.

When a polished but fixed resource does not fit the students, an adaptable but plainer resource that you can shape is usually the better instructional choice.

### Vet For Accuracy, Currency, And Alignment

A resource can be readable, licensed, and accessible and still be wrong, outdated, or off-target. Accuracy and alignment are independent checks.

Vet:

- factual accuracy against reliable sources, especially for science, history, and health;
- currency, since some fields change quickly and outdated examples mislead;
- alignment of the resource's actual demand to the learning target's demand;
- whether the resource's embedded assessment matches what you intend to measure;
- whether the resource smuggles in unrelated content that adds cognitive load.

A free worksheet that practices the wrong skill is not a bargain. The cost of misalignment is paid in student time and misleading evidence.

### Evaluate Cognitive Load And Clutter and plan For Logistics And Failure Modes

Even a correct, well-licensed resource can fail through visual or structural clutter that overwhelms working memory.

Inspect for load:

- excessive visuals, animations, or side content unrelated to the target;
- long directions that consume attention before the task begins;
- worksheet layouts that split attention across pages or screens;
- embedded ads, links, or interactive elements that distract;
- too many new vocabulary terms introduced at once.

Reduce clutter and chunk directions. The material should make the target the most salient thing on the page or screen.

Materials decisions interact with real classroom logistics. A resource that is perfect on paper can fail in distribution.

Plan:

- printing, copying, and cutting time and cost;
- material distribution and collection routines;
- login and launch time for digital tools;
- what happens when the internet, projector, or devices fail;
- whether absent students can access the material later;
- cleanup and storage for manipulatives.

A material whose logistics eat instructional time is a poor choice regardless of its instructional quality.

## Common Traps

### Choosing The Appealing Resource And Bending The Objective

Starting from a polished tool and rationalizing its fit reverses the correct order. Start from the target and let it reject the resource.

### Ignoring Readability

A text above the students' level turns a reasoning task into a decoding task. Measure and adapt readability, separating target reading from incidental reading.

### Assuming Published Means Unbiased

Commercial and popular resources can stereotype or exclude. Review representation and framing, and adapt or replace as needed.

### Treating Online Resources As Free To Use

Found does not mean legal to copy or modify. Confirm the license and attribution requirements before use.

### Retrofitting Accessibility Under Pressure

Adding captions, alt text, or alternatives at the last minute leaves students waiting. Build accessibility in from the selection stage.

### Assuming One-To-One Device Access

A digital-first plan that ignores real device and bandwidth access disadvantages the students with the least. Provide a genuine low-tech path.

### Preferring Polished-But-Fixed Over Adaptable

A beautiful resource you cannot adjust to your students is less useful than a plain one you can shape. Favor adaptability.

### Keeping Misaligned Free Materials

A free worksheet that practices the wrong skill costs student time and produces misleading evidence. Alignment outranks convenience.

## Self-Check

- Does the resource serve a fixed learning target, or has the objective been bent to fit the resource?
- Is readability matched to the actual students, with incidental reading load reduced where it is not the target?
- Have materials been reviewed for bias, representation, and cultural responsiveness, and adapted or replaced where needed?
- Is the copyright or license confirmed, with attribution and redistribution terms respected?
- Are materials accessible to learners with disabilities, matching specific IEP and 504 accommodations, built in rather than retrofitted?
- Is there a concrete plan for students without reliable devices, accounts, or bandwidth, including a low-tech alternative?
- Are adaptable resources preferred where fixed resources do not fit the students?
- Has the resource been vetted for accuracy, currency, and alignment to the target's cognitive demand?
- Has cognitive load and visual clutter been reduced so the target is most salient?
- Are logistics, cost, failure modes, and absent-student access planned rather than discovered during the lesson?
