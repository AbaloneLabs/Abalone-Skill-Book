# Unit And Multi-Day Sequencing

A unit is not a stack of daily lessons. It is a connected arc in which each lesson changes what the next lesson can assume. The defining risk of multi-day planning is treating the days as independent episodes: each one polished, but with no cumulative logic, no prerequisite discipline, and no mechanism to adjust the back of the unit based on what the front of the unit revealed. A unit that looks complete on a calendar can still leave students unable to do the final task, because no day deliberately built the capability the final day requires. Sequencing is the work of making the dependencies between days explicit and building in the feedback loops that let the arc correct itself.

Use this skill when planning a unit, a multi-day investigation, a sequence of lessons within a unit, or a tutoring arc across several sessions. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a calendar of well-designed days that fail to compound into the intended learning. This skill is distinct from single-lesson architecture, which concerns the internal structure of one meeting, and from whole-course architecture, which concerns the placement of units across a year.

## Core Rules

### Define The Terminal Performance Before Sequencing The Days

Before deciding what happens on day one, fix what students must be able to do by the last day. A unit sequenced without a clear terminal performance drifts into coverage, because there is no destination to measure progress against.

Define the terminal performance:

- the transfer task or performance students must complete at the end;
- the enduring understanding the unit is really about;
- the prerequisite capabilities the terminal performance depends on;
- the misconceptions that typically block the terminal performance.

Only once the destination is fixed can the days be ordered to build toward it. Sequencing forward from day one without a fixed endpoint produces units that cover content but never arrive.

### Map The Capability Chain Backward

Sequence the days by working backward from the terminal performance to the capabilities students must already have, then to what they must have before that. This is the spine of the unit.

Build the chain:

- list the terminal capability;
- identify the sub-capabilities it requires;
- identify what each sub-capability in turn requires;
- continue until you reach what students can already do at the start;
- assign days to the links that genuinely need instruction, not to every topic that could be mentioned.

Not every link needs its own day. Some prerequisites can be checked and activated rather than taught. The chain tells you where instruction is actually required and where a quick diagnostic will do.

### Make Lesson N Depend On Lesson N-1 Explicitly

The test of a real arc is that each day's success changes what the next day can assume. If day three could be taught whether or not day two succeeded, the days are episodes, not a sequence.

For each transition, state:

- what students must be able to do at the end of the prior lesson for the next lesson to begin;
- how the prior lesson's evidence will confirm that readiness;
- what the next lesson does if the readiness is not there.

If you cannot answer what the next lesson does when the prior lesson failed, the arc has no real connection between those days. Either build the dependency or admit the days are independent and sequence them for a different reason.

### Distribute Evidence Across The Arc, Not Only At The End

A unit with one summative at the end discovers failure too late to act on it. Evidence must be distributed so the teacher can adjust the back of the unit based on the front.

Plan a layered evidence system:

- a diagnostic at or near the start to confirm prerequisites are secure;
- short formative checks after each capability link in the chain;
- a mid-unit checkpoint substantial enough to reveal whether the spine is holding;
- a summative or performance task aligned to the terminal performance;
- a cumulative or transfer task that revisits earlier capabilities in a new context.

The mid-unit checkpoint is the most strategically important. It is the last honest chance to reteach before the terminal performance depends on the missing capability.

### Build Reteach And Flex Days Into The Sequence

A unit sequenced with no slack will collapse the first time a lesson runs long or a checkpoint reveals a gap. Build the slack in deliberately.

Plan for reality:

- insert flex days or partial flex days at predictable difficulty points;
- mark which days can compress without loss and which cannot;
- decide in advance which reteach trigger will activate the flex day;
- account for lost time from assemblies, testing, weather, and absences;
- protect the days that build capabilities the terminal performance depends on.

A unit with no flex is a unit that assumes nothing will go wrong, which is the same as assuming it will fail.

### Decide Where Each Lesson Sits On The Learning Curve

Not every lesson in an arc has the same job. Some introduce, some consolidate, some extend, some retrieve. Sequencing should reflect these different roles rather than treating every day as a generic lesson.

Identify the role of each day:

- introduction days that establish a new concept or skill;
- consolidation days that build fluency and surface misconceptions;
- extension days that transfer to new contexts;
- retrieval days that reactivate earlier capabilities to protect retention;
- synthesis days that integrate capabilities toward the terminal performance.

A sequence of only introduction days produces fragile learning with no consolidation. A sequence of only practice days produces fluency without transfer. Balance the roles across the arc.

### Spiral Earlier Capabilities Into Later Lessons

Capabilities taught once and never revisited fade. The arc should return to earlier capabilities in new contexts so retention is protected and transfer is built.

Build spiraling into the sequence:

- use warm-ups that retrieve earlier capabilities;
- design later tasks that require earlier skills in combination;
- revisit essential questions across multiple lessons;
- interleave problem types so students must choose the right approach;
- place a deliberate return to a difficult concept a few lessons after its introduction.

Spaced return is what turns a taught capability into a durable one. A unit that teaches each thing once and moves on produces learning that does not survive the summative.

### Plan The Mid-Unit Adjustment Decision In Advance

The mid-unit checkpoint is only useful if the response to its evidence is pre-decided. Vowing to "adjust as needed" usually results in no adjustment, because the decision is too complex to make well under time pressure.

Pre-decide the branches:

- if most students show the capability is secure, proceed and consider compacting;
- if a subset is missing it, plan a small-group reteach while others extend;
- if many are missing it, decide which flex day absorbs the reteach and what gets cut;
- if a specific misconception dominates, plan a targeted lesson rather than a full reteach.

Writing these branches into the plan makes the mid-unit decision fast and likely to happen.

### Coordinate The Arc With What Came Before And Comes After and document The Sequence So It Survives The Planner

A unit does not begin from nothing. It begins from whatever the prior unit actually established, and it must set up whatever the next unit will assume.

Check vertical context:

- confirm with evidence, not assumption, that prior-unit prerequisites are secure;
- identify capabilities this unit introduces but does not master, and flag them for the next unit;
- align vocabulary, models, and representations with what students already met;
- avoid reteaching what was mastered and skipping what was only introduced.

An arc planned in isolation from its neighbors will either redundantly reteach or fatally assume.

A unit sequence that lives only in one teacher's head is lost at the first staff change. The sequence must be recorded so a co-teacher, substitute, or next-year teacher can reconstruct the logic.

Record:

- the terminal performance and enduring understanding;
- the capability chain and which day builds which link;
- the evidence at each checkpoint and what each result triggers;
- the flex and reteach plan;
- notes on what actually happened, so next year's plan improves.

The sequence is an artifact, not a memory. Its value compounds only if it is written down and revised.

## Common Traps

### Sequencing Days Forward Without A Fixed Destination

Starting from day one and filling the calendar produces coverage without arrival. Fix the terminal performance first and sequence backward.

### Treating Each Day As Independent

If day N does not depend on day N-1 succeeding, the unit is a collection of episodes. Make the dependency explicit or restructure the sequence.

### One Summative At The End

A single final assessment reveals gaps only after it is too late to address them. Distribute evidence, especially a mid-unit checkpoint.

### No Flex Days

A unit with no slack breaks on the first disruption. Build in flex and mark which days can compress.

### Assuming Prerequisites From The Prior Unit Are Secure

Without a diagnostic, the unit may be built on a gap. Confirm readiness with evidence at the start.

### Teaching Each Capability Once

Capabilities taught once and never revisited fade. Spiral earlier learning into later lessons.

### Vowing To Adjust Without A Pre-Decided Branch

"Adjust as needed" rarely happens under pressure. Write the if-then branches before the checkpoint, not after.

### Keeping The Sequence In One Person's Head

A sequence not documented is lost when the planner leaves. Record the logic so it survives and improves.

## Self-Check

- Is the terminal performance fixed before the daily sequence is designed?
- Is the day order derived from a backward capability chain rather than a topic list?
- Does each lesson explicitly depend on the prior lesson's success, with a plan for when it fails?
- Is evidence distributed across the arc, including a diagnostic and a mid-unit checkpoint?
- Are flex and reteach days built in, with triggers that activate them?
- Do lessons play distinct roles, balancing introduction, consolidation, extension, retrieval, and synthesis?
- Are earlier capabilities spiraled into later lessons to protect retention and build transfer?
- Are the mid-unit adjustment branches pre-decided rather than left to improvisation?
- Is the arc coordinated with the prior unit's actual outcomes and the next unit's assumptions?
- Is the sequence documented well enough that a different teacher could reconstruct and improve it?
