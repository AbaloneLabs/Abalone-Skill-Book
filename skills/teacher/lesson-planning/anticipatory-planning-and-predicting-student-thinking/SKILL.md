# Anticipatory Planning And Predicting Student Thinking

The most effective teachers do not merely plan what they will do; they plan for what students will think. Anticipatory planning, predicting how students will approach a task, what misconceptions will arise, what errors will be made, and what questions will surface, allows a teacher to prepare responses, scaffolds, and questions that meet students where their thinking actually is. Without it, teaching is reactive, scrambling to address confusion after it has derailed a lesson. The judgment problem is that anticipatory planning requires deep knowledge of both the content and of how learners typically think about it, and it must be specific rather than generic. The most common failure is planning only the teacher's actions, the explanations and activities, while leaving student thinking to chance. The skill is predicting the cognitive landscape students will inhabit and preparing to navigate it.

Use this skill before planning a lesson or task, before predicting student responses, before designing questions and scaffolds based on anticipated thinking, or before diagnosing why a lesson produced surprises. The goal is to prevent the agent from planning only teacher actions, from being surprised by predictable misconceptions, or from preparing generic responses that do not meet specific predicted thinking.

## Core Rules

### Plan For Student Thinking, Not Just Teacher Action

A lesson plan that details what the teacher will do but not what students will think is incomplete. The heart of teaching is responding to student thinking, and that requires anticipating it. Anticipatory planning shifts the focus from the teacher's performance to the students' cognitive experience.

Plan for thinking by:

- predicting how students will approach the task before teaching it;
- identifying the conceptions and misconceptions likely to arise;
- preparing responses to each anticipated response;
- designing questions that will surface and probe student thinking;
- treating the plan as a set of hypotheses about student cognition.

### Predict Misconceptions Specifically

Generic awareness that "students may struggle" is not enough. Effective anticipatory planning predicts specific misconceptions, the particular flawed conceptions that students typically hold about the content, and prepares specific responses to each. Specificity allows preparation; generality does not.

Predict specifically by:

- drawing on knowledge of common misconceptions for the content;
- predicting the specific errors students will make and why;
- identifying the partial understandings that underlie errors;
- preparing a targeted response for each anticipated misconception;
- prioritizing the misconceptions most likely and most consequential.

### Anticipate Multiple Solution Paths

For many tasks, especially open ones, students will take multiple valid paths and some invalid ones. Anticipatory planning considers the range of approaches students may take, evaluates each for validity and efficiency, and prepares responses that build on correct paths and redirect incorrect ones.

Anticipate paths by:

- solving the task multiple ways before teaching it;
- identifying efficient, inefficient, and incorrect approaches;
- preparing questions that build on each valid approach;
- preparing redirects for anticipated incorrect approaches;
- planning how to use multiple paths to deepen class understanding.

### Use The Task Itself To Predict Thinking

The design of a task shapes the thinking it will elicit. A well-designed anticipatory plan examines the task from the student's perspective, predicting how its features, its wording, its numbers, its context, will guide or mislead student thinking. Task features that will produce unwanted responses should be revised.

Use the task by:

- examining the task from a novice perspective, not an expert one;
- predicting how specific features, wording, numbers, context, will be interpreted;
- revising features that will mislead or misdirect student thinking;
- choosing numbers and contexts that reveal rather than obscure concepts;
- anticipating how the task's representation will shape student approaches.

### Prepare Questions That Probe Predicted Thinking

Anticipatory planning is not complete with predictions alone; it must prepare the questions that will surface, probe, and advance the predicted thinking. Questions prepared in advance are sharper than those improvised in the moment, because they are designed for specific anticipated responses.

Prepare questions by:

- writing questions that will surface anticipated misconceptions;
- preparing probing questions for each predicted response;
- designing advancing questions that move thinking forward;
- planning questions that prompt students to compare and connect approaches;
- sequencing questions from surfacing to probing to advancing.

### Prepare Scaffolds And Extensions For Predicted Responses

Students will respond to a task at different levels, some struggling, some succeeding quickly. Anticipatory planning prepares scaffolds for predicted struggles and extensions for predicted successes, so that all students are challenged appropriately without ad hoc improvisation.

Prepare by:

- preparing scaffolds for the struggles most likely to arise;
- preparing extensions for students who succeed quickly;
- deciding in advance when and how to intervene;
- identifying the minimal support that preserves student thinking;
- planning how to re-engage students who have stalled.

### Plan For The Diagnostic Moment

When students respond, the teacher faces a diagnostic moment: what does this response reveal about their thinking, and what should I do next? Anticipatory planning prepares the teacher to interpret responses quickly by pre-loading the interpretation of anticipated responses, so that the diagnostic work is done in advance.

Plan for diagnosis by:

- pre-loading the interpretation of each anticipated response;
- connecting each response to the underlying conception it reveals;
- deciding in advance what each response implies for next steps;
- preparing to recognize productive partial understandings;
- planning how to use student responses as formative assessment.

### Use Anticipated Responses To Design The Discussion

The class discussion is where individual thinking becomes collective learning, and it should be designed around anticipated responses. Anticipatory planning identifies which responses to share, in what order, and to what end, so that the discussion builds understanding rather than merely sharing answers.

Design discussion by:

- selecting anticipated responses to feature in discussion;
- sequencing shared responses to build toward the key idea;
- planning how to connect and contrast different approaches;
- preparing questions that prompt comparison and generalization;
- ensuring the discussion reaches the target mathematical or conceptual idea.

### Refine Predictions Through Experience

Anticipatory planning improves with experience and with deliberate study of student thinking. Predictions that miss should be examined and refined, and the accumulated knowledge of how students think about specific content should inform future planning.

Refine by:

- comparing predicted responses to actual responses after teaching;
- examining misses to improve future predictions;
- documenting misconceptions and effective responses over time;
- studying research on student thinking in the content area;
- treating each lesson as data that sharpens future anticipation.

## Common Traps

### Planning Only Teacher Actions

Detailing explanations and activities while leaving student thinking to chance.

### Generic Struggle Awareness

Knowing students "may struggle" without predicting specific misconceptions.

### Single Solution Path

Planning only the intended approach and being unprepared for others.

### Ignoring Task Features

Failing to predict how specific task features will shape student thinking.

### Improvised Questions

Relying on in-the-moment questions rather than prepared, targeted ones.

### No Scaffolds Or Extensions

Unprepared for predicted struggles or successes, forcing ad hoc improvisation.

### Reactive Diagnosis

Scrambling to interpret responses in the moment rather than pre-loading interpretation.

### Unstructured Discussion

Sharing answers without a designed sequence that builds understanding.

## Self-Check

- Does the plan predict how students will think, not just what the teacher will do?
- Are misconceptions predicted specifically, with targeted responses prepared for each?
- Are multiple solution paths anticipated, with responses prepared for each?
- Does the plan examine how task features will shape student thinking and revise misleading ones?
- Are questions prepared in advance to surface, probe, and advance predicted thinking?
- Are scaffolds and extensions prepared for predicted struggles and successes?
- Is the teacher prepared to diagnose responses quickly through pre-loaded interpretation?
- Is the class discussion designed around anticipated responses to build collective understanding?
- Are predictions refined through comparison with actual responses after teaching?
- Does anticipatory planning prepare the teacher to meet students where their thinking actually is?
