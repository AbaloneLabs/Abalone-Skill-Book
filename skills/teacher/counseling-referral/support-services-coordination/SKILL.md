# Coordinating Support Services

Some students need more than a single referral. They receive counseling, academic intervention, health services, special education accommodations, and outside therapy, often simultaneously, and the teacher sits at the center of a web they did not design and cannot fully see. Coordination fails when each provider works in isolation: the counselor addresses one issue, the interventionist another, the outside therapist a third, and the teacher adjusts to none of them, so the student experiences a set of disconnected adults each pulling in a different direction. It also fails when coordination collapses into surveillance, with adults over-sharing and the student feeling managed rather than supported.

This skill covers the judgment needed to coordinate multiple services coherently: understanding tiered systems, aligning the adults around a shared plan, connecting families to resources beyond the school, managing accommodations across classrooms, and sustaining support over time without eroding the student's autonomy or dignity. The goal is a coherent support system the student experiences as help, not as a case file.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Tiered Structure Of Support And Where The Student Sits

Most schools operate a tiered intervention model: universal supports for all students, targeted supports for those who need more, and intensive individualized supports for the few with significant needs. Knowing where a student sits in this structure tells you what supports they should be receiving, who is responsible for each, and what the escalation path is if needs change. A student receiving only universal supports who clearly needs targeted intervention is being under-served, and recognizing the gap is the first step to closing it. Understand both the academic and the social-emotional sides of the tiered system, because they often run in parallel.

If your school's tiered structure is unclear, ask. You cannot coordinate what you cannot see.

### Align The Adults Around A Shared Plan, Not A Stack Of Separate Ones

When multiple providers support a student, the risk is that each builds their own plan and the plans conflict or duplicate. Push for a shared understanding of the student's goals, the specific role of each adult, and the strategies being used, so the student experiences coherence rather than contradiction. A shared plan clarifies who communicates with the family, who delivers which intervention, and how progress is reviewed. Without alignment, the student does the integrating work that the adults should be doing, and they usually cannot.

This often requires you to initiate the coordination, because the classroom teacher is the one constant across the student's school day.

### Distinguish Coordination From Surveillance

Coordinating support means the right people have the right information to help the student. It does not mean every adult knows every detail, nor that the student is monitored constantly. Over-sharing can stigmatize, violate privacy, and make the student feel like a problem to be tracked rather than a person to be taught. Share information on a need-to-know basis, aligned to each adult's role, and be transparent with the student about who knows what and why. Coordination serves the student; surveillance serves the adults' anxiety.

### Connect Families To Resources Beyond The School

School-based services are necessary but often insufficient. Families may need community mental health providers, food or housing support, healthcare, language services, tutoring, or recreational programs, and they often do not know what exists or how to access it. Build working knowledge of the local resource landscape, including eligibility and waitlists, and make warm handoffs rather than handing over a phone number. A warm handoff, introducing the family, explaining the process, and following up, dramatically increases the chance the family actually accesses the resource. Connecting a family to real help is one of the most consequential things a teacher can do.

Maintain a current list of vetted resources, because stale referrals erode trust fast.

### Manage Accommodations Consistently Across Classrooms

A student's accommodations are only as good as their consistent implementation. If a student receives extended time in one class and is denied it in another, the accommodation becomes a source of conflict rather than support. Know each of your students' documented accommodations, implement them as a matter of course rather than by request, and coordinate with co-teachers and departments so the student does not have to negotiate their own access in every room. Inconsistent implementation is a common and serious equity failure, because it places the burden of access on the student.

If an accommodation seems unclear or impractical, raise it through the proper channel rather than quietly dropping it.

### Review Progress Periodically And Adjust The Support

Support plans are hypotheses, not prescriptions. A student's needs change, interventions work or do not, and a plan that was right in October may be wrong by February. Build periodic review into the coordination, asking whether the supports are actually working, whether some can be faded, and whether new ones are needed. Plans that are written once and never revisited become stale and can hold a student at a lower level of expectation than they are ready for. Review is what keeps support responsive rather than rigid.

Include the student's own perspective in the review, because they know whether the help is helping.

### Sustain Support Without Eroding Autonomy And Dignity

Long-term support carries a risk: the student comes to feel defined by their needs, dependent on adults, and incapable of doing things they could in fact do. Balance support with genuine opportunities for the student to take age-appropriate responsibility, make choices, and experience success on their own. Fade supports as independence grows, and never let the support system become a message that the student is fragile or broken. The aim of coordination is a student who can eventually navigate their own support, not one who is perpetually managed by adults.

### Document Coordination Decisions And Communications

When multiple adults and services are involved, a record of what was decided, who is doing what, and what was communicated with the family prevents confusion and protects the student. Keep factual, neutral notes of coordination meetings and key contacts, stored according to policy and privacy law. Good documentation is what allows the support to survive staff turnover and transitions between years.

## Common Traps

### Each Provider Working In Isolation

Separate plans conflict and duplicate. Push for a shared plan with clear roles for each adult.

### Over-Sharing In The Name Of Coordination

Need-to-know sharing respects privacy and dignity. Coordination is not surveillance.

### Stale Or Unvetted Referrals

Handing out a phone number that no longer works erodes trust. Maintain current resources and make warm handoffs.

### Inconsistent Accommodation Implementation

Access that depends on the room is an equity failure. Implement accommodations as a default, not by request.

### Writing A Plan Once And Never Revisiting It

Needs change and plans go stale. Review periodically and adjust, fade, or add supports.

### Defining The Student By Their Needs

Long-term support can erode autonomy. Balance help with real responsibility and fade supports as independence grows.

### No Record Of Coordination Decisions

Without documentation, support collapses at staff turnover. Keep factual notes of decisions and communications.

## Self-Check

- Do I know where each supported student sits in the tiered system and what they should be receiving?
- Are the adults around each student aligned on a shared plan with clear roles, rather than separate plans?
- Am I sharing information on a need-to-know basis that serves the student rather than surveillance?
- Can I connect families to vetted community resources through warm handoffs?
- Are accommodations implemented consistently across classrooms as a default, not by request?
- Is the support plan reviewed periodically and adjusted as the student's needs change?
- Does the support preserve the student's autonomy and dignity, with supports faded as independence grows?
- Are coordination decisions and key communications documented and stored according to policy?
