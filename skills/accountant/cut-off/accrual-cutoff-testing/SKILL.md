# Accrual Cutoff Testing

Accrual cutoff testing is the procedure that confirms a period has captured all the expenses it should have, even those not yet invoiced or paid. It is a completeness test. The question is not whether recorded accruals are correct, but whether there are costs incurred before period end that were never recorded at all. This is the gap that most easily distorts period profit, because an unrecorded cost simply disappears from the period and shifts silently into the next. Agents often focus on testing the accruals that exist and forget to search for the ones that should exist but do not, which is exactly where completeness breaks down.

Use this skill before performing accrual cutoff testing, searching for unrecorded liabilities, evaluating period-end expense completeness, or reviewing the accrual population for audit or close. The goal is to confirm the period contains all incurred costs and that no material expense has been omitted.

This skill covers accrual cutoff testing procedures. The scope and depth of testing appropriate for audit versus management purposes differ, and specific completeness procedures depend on the entity's risk profile and the applicable framework. Confirm the testing objective before applying these procedures.

## Core Rules

### Frame The Test As A Completeness Search

Accrual cutoff testing is fundamentally a search for what is missing. Start from the assumption that some incurred costs may not be recorded, and design procedures to find them.

The search looks for:

- goods or services received before period end with no recorded payable or accrual;
- services performed before period end with no accrued expense;
- periodic costs, such as utilities or rent, incurred through period end but not yet billed;
- amounts owed to employees, such as wages or bonuses earned but unpaid;
- interest, fees, and other financial costs accrued through period end.

Testing only the recorded accruals tests existence and accuracy, not completeness. Completeness requires looking outside the books.

### Use Subsequent Events To Find Unrecorded Items

The most effective completeness procedure is examining transactions after period end. Costs incurred before period end often appear as payments or invoices in the following period.

Search the subsequent period for:

- cash disbursements in the first weeks after period end that may relate to prior-period costs;
- supplier invoices received after period end for goods or services delivered before period end;
- payroll payments in the next period for wages earned in the closing period;
- utility and telecom bills covering the period through period end.

When a subsequent payment or invoice relates to a cost incurred before period end, confirm whether an accrual was recorded. If not, the period is incomplete.

### Trace From Source Records To The Accrual

Completeness testing traces from independent source records forward to the accrual, rather than from the accrual backward to support. The direction matters.

Forward-tracing procedures include:

- from open purchase orders or contracts with activity before period end, to a recorded accrual;
- from receiving records for goods received before period end, to a recorded payable or accrual;
- from timesheets or payroll records for wages earned before period end, to a wage accrual;
- from usage or meter data for utilities through period end, to an accrued expense.

If the source record shows activity but no accrual exists, the cost is unrecorded.

### Cover The High-Risk Categories

Some expense categories are more prone to cutoff gaps than others. Focus testing on:

- professional services and consulting, often billed in arrears;
- utilities and telecommunications, billed on cycles that lag the period;
- freight and logistics, where third-party invoices arrive late;
- wages, bonuses, and commissions earned but unpaid at period end;
- interest and financing costs between payment dates;
- warranty and after-sales obligations tied to period sales.

These categories share the trait that the invoice or payment lags the incurrence, creating the gap where a cost is incurred but not yet recorded.

### Evaluate The Materiality Of Unrecorded Items

Not every unrecorded item requires an adjustment. Evaluate identified gaps against materiality to decide whether an accrual is needed.

Consider:

- the individual and aggregate amount of unrecorded items;
- whether the gap is material to the period's expense or profit;
- whether the gap is part of a recurring pattern that accumulates;
- whether the entity's policy requires accrual above a threshold.

A single small unrecorded item may be immaterial, but a pattern of unrecorded items across categories can be material in aggregate and indicates a process weakness.

### Distinguish Timing Differences From True Gaps

Some items appear unrecorded at period end but are legitimate timing differences that will reverse. Others are genuine omissions that misstate the period.

Distinguish by asking:

- is there an accrual that will capture this cost in the correct period;
- is the cost recorded in a later period without reversal, double-counting or omitting;
- is the timing difference expected to clear, or has it become permanent.

A timing difference that never clears is an omission in disguise.

### Document The Testing And Conclusions

Accrual cutoff testing should be documented so the procedures, the items found, the materiality evaluation, and the conclusion are evident. This supports both the close review and any audit.

Document:

- the procedures performed and the population examined;
- the unrecorded items identified and their amounts;
- the materiality assessment and whether adjustments were proposed;
- the conclusion on period completeness.

Undocumented testing cannot be relied on later and provides no evidence that completeness was assessed.

## Common Traps

### Testing Existence Instead Of Completeness

Reviewing the accruals that exist tests whether they are right, not whether all needed accruals exist. Completeness requires searching outside the recorded population.

### Searching Only The Recorded Accruals

Looking at the accrual list and confirming each is supportable misses the unrecorded items entirely. The unrecorded items are, by definition, not on the list.

### Ignoring Subsequent Payments And Invoices

The clearest evidence of an unrecorded prior-period cost is a payment or invoice in the next period for activity before period end. Failing to examine subsequent events misses the most reliable signal.

### Focusing Only On Large Or Obvious Categories

Small recurring categories like utilities or freight accumulate. Testing only the large professional services accrual and ignoring the routine categories leaves aggregate gaps undetected.

### Treating A Timing Difference As Complete

A cost recorded in the next period without an accrual or reversal is not a clean timing difference. It omits the prior period and double-counts or distorts the next.

### Dismissing Small Items That Aggregate

A small unrecorded utility bill may be immaterial alone, but if the same gap appears every period across several categories, the aggregate effect on trends and annual results can be material.

### Failing To Document The Search

Testing that is not documented provides no evidence. The procedures, findings, and conclusion must be recorded for the close and for audit.

## Self-Check

- Is the accrual cutoff test framed as a completeness search for unrecorded incurred costs, not just a review of existing accruals?
- Are subsequent-period payments and invoices examined for costs relating to the closing period?
- Does testing trace forward from independent source records, such as receiving reports, timesheets, and usage data, to confirm a recorded accrual exists?
- Are high-risk categories like professional services, utilities, freight, wages, and interest specifically covered?
- Are identified unrecorded items evaluated for individual and aggregate materiality, with adjustments proposed where needed?
- Are legitimate timing differences distinguished from genuine omissions that misstate the period?
- Is the testing documented with procedures, findings, materiality assessment, and conclusion?
- Has the testing objective, audit versus management, and the applicable framework been confirmed before relying on these procedures?
