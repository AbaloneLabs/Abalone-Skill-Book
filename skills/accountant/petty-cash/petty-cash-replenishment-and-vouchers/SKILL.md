# Petty Cash Replenishment And Vouchers

Replenishment is the moment when petty cash control either holds or fails. It is the point at which vouchers are reviewed, expenses are recorded, and the fund is restored to its authorized level. Done with discipline, replenishment is a genuine control checkpoint that catches unsupported spending, miscoding, personal use, and duplicate reimbursement before they become embedded. Done carelessly, replenishment becomes an automatic top-up where no one examines what the money was spent for, vouchers are accepted without receipts, and the fund turns into an unreviewed conduit for leakage. The voucher is the heart of the process. A voucher is not a formality; it is the evidence that justifies the cash that left the fund, and the document on which the expense is recorded. When vouchers are weak, missing, or approved by the same person who spent the money, the entire replenishment cycle loses its ability to detect abuse. The agent's task is to treat every replenishment as a review event, not a refill.

Use this skill before replenishing a petty cash fund, designing voucher requirements, recording replenishment entries, approving disbursements, or handling missing or incomplete support. The goal is to prevent the agent from processing replenishment as a mechanical top-up that bypasses the review the cycle exists to perform.

Expense-reporting rules, tax-deductibility of small cash expenses, acceptable receipt practices, and reimbursement limits vary by jurisdiction, entity type, and tax framework. Do not state a single universal rule. Treatment of disallowed items, employee reimbursements, and tax-sensitive spending should follow the entity's policy and be confirmed with a qualified accountant. This skill provides operational replenishment and voucher judgment, not a final determination in any specific case.

## Core Rules

### Treat Replenishment As A Review Event, Not A Refill

The purpose of replenishment is not to put cash back in the box. It is to review the spending that occurred, confirm it was legitimate and properly supported, record it in the correct accounts and periods, and only then restore the fund. When replenishment becomes a routine top-up, the review disappears and unsupported spending passes through indefinitely.

At each replenishment:

- count cash and vouchers to confirm the fund equals the authorized float before replenishing;
- review every voucher for completeness, proper approval, and correct coding;
- challenge vouchers that lack receipts, have vague purposes, or look unusual;
- record the expenses in the general ledger by account and period;
- request replenishment for the exact amount of reviewed and approved vouchers;
- reset the voucher file and retain spent vouchers for audit.

If the review is skipped, replenishment becomes the mechanism by which leakage is funded rather than detected.

### Require A Complete Voucher For Every Disbursement

No cash should leave the fund without a voucher, and no voucher should be accepted without the information needed to justify and record the expense. A voucher that is missing fields is not a voucher; it is an incomplete claim that should be returned for completion.

Every voucher must capture:

- a unique, sequentially tracked voucher number;
- the date of the disbursement;
- the amount;
- the payee or recipient;
- a specific business purpose, not just a category;
- the account code for proper recording;
- approval by someone other than the custodian for items above a threshold;
- the original receipt attached wherever the vendor provides one.

A voucher with a purpose of "office supplies" and no receipt is not adequate support. The purpose should explain what was purchased and why, and the receipt should confirm the amount and vendor.

### Enforce Approval Independent Of The Custodian

The custodian should not approve their own spending. For disbursements above a defined threshold, approval must come from someone other than the custodian, and replenishment itself should be approved by an independent reviewer. Without this separation, the fund has no effective control over what is spent.

Separate at minimum:

- the person spending or requesting cash from the person approving it;
- the custodian from the person approving replenishment;
- voucher approval from disbursement for material items;
- replenishment from bank account control where possible.

In very small organizations where perfect separation is impossible, compensating controls such as owner review of every replenishment and surprise counts must fill the gap, and the limitation should be acknowledged explicitly.

### Record Replenishment In The Correct Accounts And Periods

Replenishment is the accounting event that converts vouchers into recorded expenses. The expenses should be coded to the accounts that reflect what was actually purchased, in the periods the spending occurred, not the period of replenishment.

Ensure:

- each voucher is coded to the correct expense account based on substance;
- expenses are recorded in the period the disbursement occurred, not the replenishment date;
- sales tax or input tax is handled per the applicable rules;
- the replenishment check or transfer credits cash and debits the expense accounts;
- the fund balance returns to the authorized float in the general ledger.

Recording all replenishment to a single miscellaneous account defeats the purpose of coding and hides what the fund is actually spent on. Coding should be meaningful even for small amounts.

### Handle Missing Or Incomplete Support Deliberately

Missing receipts are the most common sign of leakage, and a policy that accepts "receipt lost" without consequence invites abuse. At the same time, genuinely lost receipts do happen, and the response should be proportionate rather than rigid. The key is that every exception is documented, reviewed, and limited.

For missing or incomplete support:

- require a written explanation of why the receipt is missing;
- obtain approval above the normal threshold for unsupported vouchers;
- flag repeated missing-receipt patterns from the same person;
- set a limit on the value or frequency of unsupported vouchers accepted;
- deny or delay replenishment for vouchers that exceed policy or look suspicious.

A pattern of missing receipts matters more than any single instance. One lost receipt is normal; a custodian or employee who routinely lacks support is a control failure.

### Replenish Only What Has Been Reviewed And Approved

The replenishment amount should equal the total of reviewed and approved vouchers, no more. If the count reveals a shortage, the shortage is not resolved by replenishing extra cash. It is investigated, documented, and resolved through the proper process before the fund is restored.

Rules for the replenishment amount:

- replenish only the sum of approved vouchers;
- do not plug a shortage by replenishing to the float without explanation;
- investigate and document any difference between cash plus vouchers and the float;
- obtain approval for any shortage write-off before replenishing;
- reset the voucher file so spent vouchers are not reimbursed twice.

Replenishing to the float regardless of the count turns replenishment into a tool that hides shortages rather than reveals them.

### Retain Vouchers And Replenishment Records For Audit

Spent vouchers are the audit evidence for the expenses recorded at replenishment. They should be retained organized by replenishment cycle, with the replenishment request, approvals, and supporting receipts, for the period required by the entity's retention policy and applicable rules.

Retain:

- the spent vouchers in sequence with their receipts attached;
- the replenishment request showing the total and account coding;
- the approval records for the replenishment and for material vouchers;
- any shortage or exception documentation;
- the bank or cash record of the replenishment transfer.

If vouchers cannot be produced later, the expenses they supported cannot be verified, and the fund's integrity is in question regardless of how clean the replenishment looked.

## Common Traps

### Replenishing Without Reviewing Vouchers

When replenishment is a routine top-up, no one checks what the money was spent for. Unsupported, personal, or duplicate expenses pass through the fund indefinitely.

### Accepting Vouchers Without Receipts Or Specific Purpose

A voucher with no receipt and a vague purpose like "supplies" provides no real support. Without specific purpose and evidence, the disbursement cannot be justified or verified.

### Letting The Custodian Approve Their Own Spending

When the custodian can approve, disburse, and replenish without independent review, the fund has no effective control. Trust is not a substitute for segregation.

### Recording All Replenishment To One Miscellaneous Account

Coding every replenishment to a single account hides what the fund is spent on and prevents meaningful review of spending patterns. Coding should reflect the actual nature of each expense.

### Plugging Shortages By Replenishing To The Float

Replenishing extra cash to cover a shortage hides the difference instead of investigating it. Shortages must be documented and approved before the fund is restored.

### Accepting Repeated Missing Receipts Without Escalation

One lost receipt is normal; a pattern is a warning sign. A policy that never escalates repeated missing-receipt instances invites ongoing abuse.

### Recording Expenses In The Replenishment Period

Expenses recorded when the fund is replenished, rather than when the disbursement occurred, distort period comparisons and can misstate month-end results.

### Losing Or Discarding Spent Vouchers

If spent vouchers are not retained and organized, the expenses they supported cannot be audited, and the fund's integrity cannot be verified after the fact.

## Self-Check

- Is replenishment treated as a review event where vouchers are examined, challenged, and recorded before the fund is restored, rather than a routine top-up?
- Does every disbursement have a complete voucher with number, date, amount, payee, specific business purpose, account code, approval, and receipt where available?
- Is approval for material disbursements and for replenishment itself performed by someone independent of the custodian?
- Are expenses coded to meaningful accounts based on substance and recorded in the period the disbursement occurred, not the replenishment date?
- Are missing or incomplete receipts handled with written explanation, elevated approval, pattern tracking, and limits, rather than accepted without consequence?
- Does the replenishment amount equal only reviewed and approved vouchers, with any shortage investigated and approved before the fund is restored?
- Are spent vouchers, replenishment requests, approvals, and exception documentation retained and organized for audit?
- Are repeated missing-receipt patterns and unusual vouchers escalated rather than passed through routinely?
- Have jurisdiction-specific expense-reporting, tax-deductibility, and receipt rules been confirmed with a qualified professional before finalizing voucher and replenishment policy?
