# Payroll Liabilities And Remittance

Every pay run creates a bundle of liabilities that the employer holds temporarily on behalf of others: tax authorities, benefit vendors, retirement plan custodians, courts, and sometimes former employees. These are not ordinary trade payables. Withheld taxes are trust fund money that already belongs to the government, and many deductions are employee money routed through the employer. The accounting job is to record each liability cleanly, hold it in the right account, remit it on the legally required schedule, and prove the balance returns to zero.

Agents miss this work because the payroll system produces the withholdings automatically and the remittances look like routine bill payments. But the moment withholdings are recorded as a liability, a fiduciary obligation begins, and the consequences of late, short, or misapplied remittance are penalties, interest, personal liability for responsible officers in many jurisdictions, and broken benefit promises to employees. The harm this skill prevents is treating payroll liabilities like generic accounts payable.

This is accounting guidance, not legal or tax advice. Remittance schedules, deposit frequencies, penalty regimes, garnishment limits, and escheat rules differ dramatically by jurisdiction, tax type, and deposit size. Route definitive remittance timing, penalty exposure, and garnishment compliance to a qualified payroll provider, HR professional, or tax advisor. The agent's freedom is to structure the liability and remittance accounting correctly and to surface the obligations; it is not to assert deposit dates or legal limits as universal facts.

## Core Rules

### Treat Withholdings As Trust Fund Liabilities, Not Vendor Payables

Employee income tax withholdings and the employee share of social security and medicare are money the employer holds in trust for the tax authority. They are not the employer's money, and they should never be commingled with operating cash or used to fund operations. Record them as distinct current liabilities the moment the payroll runs, and keep them separate from trade payables in the chart of accounts.

Implications:

- these liabilities usually carry fiduciary responsibility and, in many jurisdictions, personal liability for responsible persons if unremitted;
- they should not be netted against expense or settled by discounting vendor bills;
- the account balance should reflect exactly what is owed and not yet remitted;
- shortfalls must be funded by the employer when they occur, not borrowed from the next pay run.

### Record Each Liability Type In Its Own Account

Blending all payroll liabilities into one "payroll taxes payable" account hides which obligation is which, when each is due, and whether each has cleared. Separate the streams so the balance sheet tells the truth.

Typical separate accounts:

- employee income tax withheld;
- employee and employer social security and medicare contributions;
- federal or national unemployment tax;
- state, provincial, or regional unemployment and disability taxes;
- garnishments held for remittance to courts or agencies;
- retirement plan contributions (employee deferral and employer match);
- health, dental, and other benefit deductions;
- union dues or other voluntary deductions.

Each account should reconcile to a schedule showing the per-pay-run accrual, the remittances made, and the residual balance.

### Honor The Correct Remittance Schedule For Each Liability

Different liabilities have different deposit rules. Some are due the next banking day, some semi-weekly, some monthly, some quarterly, and benefit deductions often follow the plan's deposit rule rather than a tax calendar. A single "pay all payroll liabilities on the 15th" rule is almost always wrong for at least some streams.

Decision criteria:

- confirm the deposit frequency assigned to each tax by the relevant authority, which can change as deposit size grows;
- confirm each benefit vendor's required remittance timing, often tied to the pay date;
- confirm garnishment remittance timing, which can be per pay period or per court order;
- record the remittance when the cash leaves, not when the return is filed, because filing and depositing are separate events;
- reconcile the liability account to zero (or to a known in-transit balance) after each scheduled remittance.

### Use Clearing Accounts And Reconcile Every Cycle

A payroll clearing account receives net pay and withholdings when the run posts and releases them as checks clear, deposits settle, taxes deposit, and vendors are paid. Without disciplined reconciliation, clearing becomes a graveyard of stale items: uncashed checks, duplicate postings, unremitted taxes, and reversed entries.

Reconcile clearing every cycle:

- net pay should clear to the payroll bank account within a few days;
- tax liabilities should move to their dedicated accounts and then out on deposit;
- benefit deductions should move to vendor payments on schedule;
- uncashed checks should be tracked toward escheat;
- any residual balance needs a documented explanation and a resolution owner.

### Track Garnishments And Deductions Through To The Beneficiary

Garnishments, levies, and many voluntary deductions create a liability to a third party the moment they are withheld from the employee. The accounting does not stop at recording the deduction; it continues until the money reaches the court, agency, retirement custodian, or benefit vendor.

For each:

- record the withholding as a liability in its own account;
- respect statutory withholding limits and priority ordering where multiple garnishments compete for the same wages;
- remit on the order's schedule, not the employer's convenience;
- retain proof of remittance and the case or employee reference;
- reconcile the liability to zero after remittance.

### Account For Employer Matching And Employer-Only Taxes As Expense

The employer side of payroll is different from the employee side. Employer matching contributions to social security, medicare, retirement, and unemployment taxes are employer expense, recorded when the related wages are earned, with a corresponding liability until remitted. Do not net employer expense against employee withholdings.

- accrue employer tax expense in the same period as the wages;
- record the offsetting liability until the deposit is made;
- allocate the employer burden to the same cost objects as the base wages;
- watch wage base limits that cap certain employer taxes, so expense stops accruing once the ceiling is reached.

### Handle Unclaimed And Stale Wages Deliberately

Net pay that is never claimed, a check that is never cashed, or a direct deposit that is returned does not simply disappear. It remains a liability to the employee until it is reissued or, after a legally defined dormancy period, escheated to the state as unclaimed property.

Track:

- uncashed payroll checks and returned deposits;
- reissue attempts and employee contact;
- the dormancy period after which the funds must be escheated;
- the reporting and remittance to the unclaimed property authority;
- the journal entry that moves the liability from wages payable to escheat payable and then out on remittance.

## Common Traps

### Commingling Trust Fund Taxes With Operating Cash

Using withheld taxes to smooth cash flow is one of the most serious payroll errors. It converts a fiduciary obligation into an unauthorized loan and can trigger personal liability for responsible officers. Keep trust fund money segregated or at least separately tracked at all times.

### Blending All Liabilities Into One Account

A single combined payroll liability account makes it impossible to see what is due when, and small shortfalls hide inside the blended balance. Separate accounts per liability type are what make remittance discipline possible.

### Paying All Liabilities On One Arbitrary Date

Treating every payroll liability as due on the same monthly date guarantees that fast-deposit taxes are late and that some benefit deductions miss vendor deadlines. Each liability has its own rule and must be scheduled individually.

### Recording Remittance At Filing Instead Of At Deposit

Tax returns and tax deposits are separate events. Recording the liability reduction when the return is filed, rather than when the cash was deposited, leaves the liability overstated and can mask a missed or late deposit.

### Letting Clearing Balances Accumulate

A payroll clearing account that never returns near zero is hiding something: an unremitted tax, an uncashed check, a duplicate posting, or a reversal that never happened. Reconcile and explain every cycle.

### Forgetting That Garnishments Are Employee Money

Garnishments and voluntary deductions reduce net pay but create a third-party liability. Failing to remit them on time means the employer is holding the employee's or the court's money beyond the permitted window, which is both a control failure and a compliance risk.

### Ignoring Escheat On Unclaimed Wages

Uncashed payroll checks do not expire into income. After the dormancy period they must be reported and remitted as unclaimed property. Leaving them on the books indefinitely understates the escheat obligation and misstates the liability.

## Self-Check

- Are employee tax withholdings and statutory contributions recorded as separate trust fund liabilities, distinct from trade payables?
- Is each liability type held in its own account so balances, due dates, and remittances are visible?
- Is each liability remitted on its own legally required schedule rather than a single arbitrary date?
- Is the payroll clearing account reconciled every cycle with every residual balance explained and resolved?
- Are garnishments, levies, and voluntary deductions tracked from withholding through to third-party remittance with proof retained?
- Is employer matching and employer-only tax recorded as expense when wages are earned, with a liability until deposit?
- Are wage base limits and taxable ceilings respected so employer tax expense stops accruing at the cap?
- Are unclaimed or stale wages tracked toward reissue or escheat rather than left indefinitely on the books?
- Is each remittance recorded when cash leaves, separate from any return filing date?
- Are jurisdiction-specific deposit frequencies, garnishment limits, and escheat rules confirmed with a qualified payroll provider or tax advisor rather than assumed?
