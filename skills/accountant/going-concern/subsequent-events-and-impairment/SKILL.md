# Subsequent Events And Impairment

Subsequent events are significant events that occur between the reporting date and the date the financial statements are authorized for issuance. Some of these events provide additional evidence about conditions that existed at the reporting date and require adjustment of the amounts recognized; others relate to conditions that arose after the reporting date and require disclosure but not adjustment. The distinction matters because it determines whether the financial statements are revised or merely annotated. Subsequent events are especially important for impairment, because events after period end, such as a sharp decline in a market, the loss of a major customer, or a bankruptcy filing, may provide evidence that an asset was impaired at the reporting date even if the impairment was not yet visible. Failing to consider subsequent events, or misclassifying an adjusting event as non-adjusting, produces financial statements that do not reflect the best available information about conditions at the reporting date.

Use this skill when evaluating subsequent events, distinguishing adjusting from non-adjusting events, assessing post-period impairment indicators, or preparing subsequent event disclosures. The goal is to prevent the agent from ignoring events that require adjustment, from misclassifying events, or from failing to disclose non-adjusting events whose knowledge is important to users.

## Core Rules

### Actively Search For Subsequent Events Through The Issuance Date

Subsequent events do not announce themselves as such. The entity must actively search for them through the date the statements are authorized for issuance.

Search by:

- reviewing subsequent transactions, commitments, and communications for significance;
- inquiring of management and legal counsel about litigation, claims, and contingencies;
- reviewing board minutes and significant agreements executed after period end;
- monitoring market, economic, and industry developments affecting the entity;
- assessing the impact of any identified event on the reported balances.

A passive approach that waits for events to surface misses adjusting events that should change the statements. Active search is required.

### Distinguish Adjusting From Non-Adjusting Events

The central judgment is whether a subsequent event provides evidence about conditions at the reporting date, requiring adjustment, or relates to conditions arising after, requiring disclosure.

Distinguish by:

- classifying as adjusting those events that provide additional evidence of conditions existing at the reporting date, such as a post-period settlement of a pre-period lawsuit;
- classifying as non-adjusting those events that reflect conditions arising after the reporting date, such as a fire or a decline in market value after period end;
- applying the distinction consistently with the applicable framework, which has specific guidance;
- documenting the classification and the reasoning for each significant event.

Misclassification is the core error. An adjusting event treated as non-adjusting leaves the statements uncorrected; a non-adjusting event treated as adjusting changes amounts that should reflect conditions at the reporting date.

### Adjust For Adjusting Events Before Issuance

When an event is adjusting, the financial statements must be revised before issuance, not merely disclosed.

Adjust by:

- updating the affected balances to reflect the additional evidence;
- reassessing related estimates, such as provisions or fair values, in light of the new evidence;
- ensuring the adjustment flows through all affected line items, notes, and disclosures;
- documenting the event, the adjustment, and the basis for treating it as adjusting.

A subsequent settlement of a lawsuit that confirms the obligation at the reporting date is an adjusting event. The provision should reflect the settlement amount, not the original estimate.

### Disclose Non-Adjusting Events That Are Material To Users

Non-adjusting events do not change the reported amounts, but if their non-disclosure would affect users' decisions, they must be disclosed.

Disclose by:

- describing the nature of the event;
- estimating the financial effect, or stating that it cannot be estimated;
- explaining why the event does not result in adjustment;
- placing the disclosure in the subsequent events note.

A major acquisition announced after period end, or a significant decline in asset values due to post-period conditions, is material non-adjusting information that users need. Omitting it deprives users of decision-relevant information.

### Assess Impairment Indicators Arising After Period End

Subsequent events are a critical source of impairment indicators. An event after period end may reveal that an asset was impaired at the reporting date.

Assess by:

- considering whether post-period events indicate that an asset's carrying amount may not be recoverable;
- evaluating declines in market value, loss of key customers, technological obsolescence, or business closures;
- distinguishing evidence of conditions at the reporting date, which may require impairment, from conditions arising after, which may not;
- performing the impairment test and recognizing any loss before issuance.

A sharp post-period decline in a market that had begun declining before period end may be evidence of impairment at the reporting date. The timing of when conditions arose determines whether adjustment is required.

### Define And Document The Authorization Date

The subsequent events period ends on the date the financial statements are authorized for issuance. This date must be defined and documented.

Define by:

- identifying the date the governing body, such as the board or audit committee, authorizes issuance;
- documenting that date in the financial statements;
- ensuring the active search for subsequent events continues through that date;
- understanding who has authority to authorize, as this varies by governance structure.

The authorization date defines the scope of the subsequent events review. Events after that date are not considered for the current statements, though they may be relevant to the next period.

### Coordinate With Auditors On Subsequent Events Review

Auditors are required to perform procedures to identify subsequent events. Coordinate throughout.

Coordinate by:

- informing auditors of significant subsequent events as they are identified;
- sharing the analysis of adjusting versus non-adjusting classification;
- aligning on the treatment of significant events before issuance;
- understanding the auditor's procedures and any concerns.

A disagreement with auditors about whether an event is adjusting, discovered after the statements are drafted, creates rework and delay. Align during the review.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

Subsequent events evaluation and impairment assessment involve complex, framework-specific, and jurisdiction-specific requirements that are the professional domain of qualified accountants and auditors. The adjusting versus non-adjusting distinction, impairment indicator assessment, and disclosure drafting require professional expertise and judgment. This skill provides awareness of what to consider, not definitive conclusions. Confirm all significant subsequent event classifications, adjustments, and disclosures with qualified accounting professionals in the relevant jurisdiction, and align with auditors. Do not treat any summary as authoritative; the standards, regulatory requirements, and professional judgment govern.

## Common Traps

### Passive Approach To Subsequent Events

Waiting for events to surface, rather than actively searching, misses adjusting events that should change the statements.

### Misclassifying Adjusting As Non-Adjusting

Treating an event that provides evidence of reporting-date conditions as non-adjusting leaves the statements uncorrected.

### Failing To Disclose Material Non-Adjusting Events

Omitting non-adjusting events that would affect users' decisions deprives them of decision-relevant information.

### Ignoring Post-Period Impairment Indicators

Events after period end may reveal impairment at the reporting date; ignoring them leaves assets overstated.

### Authorization Date Undefined

Without a defined and documented authorization date, the scope of the subsequent events review is unclear.

### Disagreement With Auditors Discovered Late

Misalignment with auditors on event classification, discovered after drafting, creates rework and delay.

### Adjustments Not Flowing Through All Items

An adjusting event that corrects one balance but not its related estimates, tax effects, or disclosures is incomplete.

### Summaries Treated As Authoritative

Subsequent events and impairment require professional expertise; confirm with qualified professionals.

## Self-Check

- Is there an active search for subsequent events through the date the statements are authorized for issuance?
- Are subsequent events correctly distinguished as adjusting or non-adjusting, with the classification documented?
- Are adjusting events reflected in revised balances, estimates, and disclosures before issuance?
- Are material non-adjusting events disclosed with their nature, estimated financial effect, and the reason for non-adjustment?
- Are post-period events assessed for impairment indicators that may reveal conditions at the reporting date?
- Is the authorization date defined, documented, and used as the scope of the subsequent events review?
- Is the subsequent events review coordinated with auditors, with alignment on significant event treatment?
- Are all significant subsequent event classifications, adjustments, and disclosures confirmed with qualified accounting professionals and aligned with auditors in the relevant jurisdiction?
