# Going Concern Assessment Triggers

The going concern assumption holds that an entity will continue to operate for the foreseeable future, typically at least twelve months from the reporting date, without intention or need to liquidate or cease trading. Financial statements are prepared on a going concern basis unless that assumption is not appropriate. When conditions raise doubt about going concern, management must perform a formal assessment, and the results may require disclosure, a change in basis of preparation, or a qualified audit opinion. Going concern is not a remote, theoretical topic. It becomes acute during downturns, liquidity crises, loss of key customers or financing, litigation, or regulatory action. The danger is twofold: assessing going concern too late, after the entity has already failed or after statements have been issued on an inappropriate basis; and assessing it too optimistically, relying on hopeful forecasts rather than evidence. A rigorous, trigger-based assessment is what protects the entity, its auditors, and its users from the consequences of a delayed or unjustified going concern conclusion.

Use this skill when evaluating going concern, identifying assessment triggers, analyzing liquidity forecasts, or supporting management assessment. The goal is to prevent the agent from missing the conditions that trigger assessment, from relying on unsupported forecasts, or from reaching a going concern conclusion without the evidence the framework and auditors require.

## Core Rules

### Monitor For Conditions That Trigger A Formal Assessment

Going concern assessment should be triggered by conditions and events, not only by a calendar. Monitor for them continuously.

Triggers include:

- recurring operating losses, negative cash flow from operations, or negative working capital;
- inability to meet debt obligations, covenant breaches, or denial of financing;
- loss of a major customer, supplier, or license critical to operations;
- pending litigation, regulatory action, or loss of key personnel;
- default on loan terms or inability to refinance maturing debt;
- significant doubt expressed by auditors, lenders, or regulators;
- indicators of impairment that suggest asset values depend on uncertain future performance.

A trigger does not mean the entity will fail, but it means a formal assessment is required. Ignoring triggers because management is confident is how going concern problems are discovered too late.

### Project Cash Flow And Liquidity Over The Required Horizon

The core of going concern assessment is whether the entity can meet its obligations as they fall due over the required period, typically twelve months from the reporting date.

Project by:

- preparing a detailed cash flow forecast over the assessment period, not just an annual budget;
- incorporating realistic assumptions about revenue, collections, payments, and capital expenditure;
- including committed and available financing, and assessing the likelihood of renewal;
- stress-testing the forecast for adverse but reasonably possible scenarios;
- identifying the timing of obligations, because liquidity is about when cash is needed, not just whether it is sufficient in total.

A forecast that shows adequate cash in total but cannot meet a payment due in month three is a going concern problem. Timing matters as much as amount.

### Base Forecasts On Realistic, Supported Assumptions

The credibility of the going concern assessment depends on the realism of its assumptions. Optimistic assumptions produce unjustified comfort.

Ensure assumptions are:

- based on historical performance adjusted for known changes, not on aspiration;
- supported by evidence, such as backlog, contracts, and committed financing;
- consistent with the entity's actual track record of meeting forecasts;
- reviewed for reasonableness by someone independent of the forecast preparer;
- stress-tested for sensitivity to changes in key drivers.

A forecast that assumes a dramatic turnaround with no supporting evidence is not a basis for a going concern conclusion. Auditors and users will, rightly, challenge it.

### Evaluate Mitigating Plans And Their Feasibility

When conditions raise doubt, management's plans to mitigate are part of the assessment. Evaluate their feasibility, not just their existence.

Evaluate plans such as:

- disposal of assets, assessed for marketability, timing, and expected proceeds;
- restructuring or cost reduction, assessed for achievability and savings timing;
- new financing or refinancing, assessed for commitment and likelihood;
- suspension of dividends or capital expenditure, assessed for cash preservation effect;
- debt forbearance or covenant waivers, assessed for lender agreement.

A plan that is conceptual but not committed, such as financing that has only been discussed, provides less support than a signed agreement. The stage of implementation matters.

### Document The Assessment And Its Conclusion

The going concern assessment must be documented thoroughly, because auditors will test it and users may rely on it.

Document:

- the conditions or events that triggered the assessment;
- the cash flow forecast and its key assumptions, with support;
- the mitigating plans evaluated and their feasibility;
- the conclusion, whether going concern is appropriate, and the basis for that conclusion;
- the involvement of management, the board, and any advisors.

An undocumented assessment cannot withstand audit scrutiny. The documentation is the evidence that the assessment was performed rigorously.

### Align With Auditors On The Assessment

Auditors are required to evaluate going concern and reach their own conclusion. Align throughout the process.

Align by:

- informing auditors as soon as triggers are identified;
- sharing the forecast, assumptions, and mitigating plans;
- discussing the conclusion and any significant judgment areas;
- understanding the auditor's procedures and potential report modifications;
- addressing auditor concerns about assumptions or plan feasibility promptly.

Reaching a going concern conclusion that auditors disagree with, after statements are prepared, creates a crisis. Early alignment allows the assessment to be tested and refined before reporting.

### Understand The Reporting Consequences Of The Conclusion

The going concern conclusion determines the basis of preparation and the required disclosures and audit report modifications.

Understand that:

- if going concern is appropriate without material uncertainty, no specific disclosure may be required, though severe but not material uncertainty may still warrant disclosure;
- if there is material uncertainty, specific disclosures of the uncertainty and management plans are required;
- if going concern is not appropriate, the financial statements must be prepared on a different basis, such as liquidation;
- auditors may issue an unmodified opinion with an emphasis-of-matter, a qualified opinion, or a disclaimer, depending on the circumstances.

The reporting consequences are significant and framework-specific. Misunderstanding them leads to incorrect statements and audit report modifications.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

Going concern assessment involves complex, framework-specific, and jurisdiction-specific requirements that are the professional domain of qualified accountants and auditors. Cash flow forecasting, assumption evaluation, mitigating plan feasibility, and disclosure and audit report implications require professional expertise and judgment. Going concern conclusions can have severe consequences for the entity, its lenders, its investors, and its regulators. This skill provides awareness of what to consider, not definitive conclusions. Confirm all significant going concern assessments, forecasts, and disclosures with qualified accounting professionals in the relevant jurisdiction, and align with auditors. Do not treat any summary as authoritative; the standards, regulatory requirements, and professional judgment govern.

## Common Traps

### Triggers Ignored Until Too Late

Conditions that require assessment, such as losses or covenant breaches, are often dismissed until the entity has already failed or statements are issued on an inappropriate basis.

### Forecasts Based On Aspiration

Optimistic forecasts unsupported by evidence produce unjustified going concern comfort.

### Timing Of Obligations Ignored

A forecast adequate in total but unable to meet a near-term payment is a going concern problem.

### Mitigating Plans Assumed Feasible Without Evidence

Conceptual plans, such as discussed but uncommitted financing, provide less support than management assumes.

### Undocumented Assessment

An assessment without documentation cannot withstand audit scrutiny or support the conclusion.

### Conclusion Reached Without Auditor Alignment

A going concern conclusion that auditors disagree with, discovered after preparation, creates a reporting crisis.

### Reporting Consequences Misunderstood

Incorrect understanding of disclosure and audit report requirements leads to inappropriate statements.

### Summaries Treated As Authoritative

Going concern assessment requires professional expertise; confirm with qualified professionals and align with auditors.

## Self-Check

- Are conditions and events that trigger a formal going concern assessment monitored continuously, not only at period end?
- Is a detailed cash flow forecast prepared over the required horizon, incorporating realistic assumptions, committed financing, and stress testing?
- Are forecast assumptions based on evidence and historical performance, reviewed for reasonableness, and not on aspiration?
- Are mitigating plans evaluated for feasibility, considering the stage of implementation and the strength of commitment?
- Is the assessment documented, including triggers, forecast, assumptions, plans, conclusion, and the basis for the conclusion?
- Is the assessment aligned with auditors throughout, with early notification and sharing of forecast and plans?
- Are the reporting consequences of the conclusion understood, including disclosure, basis of preparation, and audit report modifications?
- Are all significant going concern assessments, forecasts, and disclosures confirmed with qualified accounting professionals and aligned with auditors in the relevant jurisdiction?
