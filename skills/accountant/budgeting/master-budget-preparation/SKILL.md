# Master Budget Preparation

A master budget is the integrated set of operating and financial budgets that translates a business plan into quantified expectations for a period. It is not a collection of independent spreadsheets. The sales budget drives production, production drives materials and labor, those drive cash, and cash plus capital and financing decisions drive the budgeted balance sheet. When the links are broken or guessed, the master budget looks complete but contradicts itself, and managers approve numbers that cannot coexist. The harm appears later, when cash falls short, inventory builds unexpectedly, or departments cannot execute plans that were never funded consistently.

Use this skill before building or revising a master budget, coordinating departmental submissions into a consolidated plan, preparing budgeted financial statements, setting budget assumptions, or reviewing a budget for approval readiness. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a budget that balances on paper but rests on broken links, optimistic assumptions, or missing constraints that will collapse during execution.

Budgeting assumptions, especially around tax, depreciation, capitalization, and financing, are jurisdiction and entity dependent. Tax estimates in a budget are planning figures only and are not a substitute for a tax provision computed under the applicable rules. This skill provides operational judgment only. Where a budget will support lending covenants, investor commitments, regulatory filings, or tax planning, a qualified professional must confirm the relevant assumptions and treatments before reliance.

## Core Rules

### Start From A Clear Sales Forecast And Document Its Basis

The sales budget is the foundation of the master budget because almost every other budget derives from it. A sales forecast built on wishful thinking infects the entire plan. Build the sales forecast from documented drivers such as historical trends, pipeline, market data, seasonality, price assumptions, win rates, and known contract commitments, and separate the base case from stretch and downside scenarios.

State the key assumptions explicitly: volume by product or segment, average selling price, timing of revenue recognition versus cash collection, and any one-time events. A sales budget whose assumptions are not written down cannot be challenged or updated when conditions change, and every downstream budget inherits its weakness.

### Build The Operating Budgets In The Right Sequence

The master budget has a logical sequence, and building out of order creates rework and inconsistency. The typical sequence is sales budget, then production budget, then direct materials purchases budget, then direct labor budget, then manufacturing overhead budget, then ending inventory budget, then cost of goods sold budget, then selling and administrative expense budgets, then the capital expenditures budget, then the cash budget, and finally the budgeted income statement and balance sheet.

Each operating budget should reference the one before it. Production must satisfy sales plus desired ending inventory minus beginning inventory. Materials purchases must satisfy production needs plus desired materials inventory. Labor must satisfy production hours at budgeted rates. When a budget is built in isolation and pasted in, the links break and the master budget stops representing a single coherent plan.

### Reconcile Production To Inventory Targets Explicitly

The production budget must reconcile expected sales, desired ending finished goods inventory, and beginning inventory. If production is set equal to sales without considering inventory targets, the business will either stock out or build unwanted inventory. Make the inventory target explicit and tie it to a service level, lead time, or turnover policy rather than leaving it to guess.

The same logic applies to raw materials and work in process. Each inventory budget should state the target level, the policy behind it, and the effect on purchases and cash. Inventory targets that are too aggressive consume cash and warehouse space; targets that are too low risk stockouts and lost sales.

### Build The Cash Budget From Real Timing, Not Accrual Averages

The cash budget is where many master budgets fail, because it is built from accrual-period averages rather than real cash timing. Revenue recognized in a month is not cash collected in that month. Expenses incurred are not cash paid. The cash budget must reflect collection patterns, payment terms, payroll cycles, tax payment dates, and financing inflows and outflows.

Model the cash conversion cycle explicitly: days sales outstanding for collections, days payable outstanding for supplier payments, and inventory days for production timing. Include scheduled debt principal and interest payments, tax installments, dividend or distribution plans, and capital expenditure outflows by month. A budgeted income statement that shows profit while the cash budget shows a liquidity shortfall is a sign the cash timing was not modeled, and the shortfall is the real constraint.

### Include Capital Expenditures And Financing Decisions

The capital expenditures budget captures planned investment in long-lived assets, and it affects both the cash budget and the budgeted balance sheet through depreciation and asset balances. Omitting or underestimating capital spending understates cash needs and overstates free cash flow.

Link capital projects to the operating plan. A capacity expansion should be supported by the sales and production budgets that justify it. Financing decisions, new debt, equity contributions, lease commitments, and repayments, must flow into the cash budget and the budgeted balance sheet. A master budget that ignores how capital projects and debt will be funded is incomplete and will mislead anyone relying on it for liquidity or solvency decisions.

### Produce Budgeted Financial Statements That Tie Together

The output of a master budget is a set of budgeted financial statements: income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement. These must tie together with the same discipline as actual financial statements. Net income must flow into retained earnings. Cash must reconcile across the cash budget and the balance sheet. The balance sheet must balance.

If the budgeted balance sheet does not balance, there is a missing link, usually in inventory, depreciation, retained earnings, payables, or financing. Do not force it to balance with a plug. Find the broken link, because a plug hides an error that will surface during execution. The discipline of making the statements tie is itself a check on whether the operating budgets are consistent.

### Define Ownership And Review Cadence

A master budget is built by people and approved by people. Assign ownership for each component budget to the function accountable for it: sales owns the sales budget, production owns the production budget, and so on. Without ownership, assumptions cannot be challenged and variances cannot be interpreted later.

Define a review cadence with clear approval gates. Bottom-up submissions should be challenged and reconciled to top-down targets, and the gap should be resolved deliberately, not papered over. Document who approved the final budget and the key assumptions, so that when actuals diverge, the basis for the original plan is recoverable.

### Separate Base Case From Stretch And Contingency

A single point budget hides uncertainty. Build at least a base case, a stretch case, and a downside case, and identify the triggers that would move the business between them. The downside case is especially important for cash, because the cost of a liquidity shortfall is severe and asymmetric. Contingency plans, such as delayed hiring, deferred capital, or a credit facility, should be identified before they are needed, not invented under pressure.

## Common Traps

### Building Budgets In Isolation And Pasting Them Together

When each department builds its budget independently and the pieces are concatenated, the links between sales, production, materials, labor, and cash are broken. The result balances numerically but does not represent a coherent plan. Build in sequence with explicit references between budgets.

### Using Accrual Timing For The Cash Budget

Modeling cash from accrual revenue and expense averages ignores collection lag, payment terms, payroll cycles, and tax dates. The budgeted income statement can show profit while the business runs out of cash. Always build the cash budget from real cash timing.

### Optimistic Sales With No Downside Scenario

A sales budget built only on the hoped-for outcome leaves the business exposed when demand falls short. Without a downside case and contingency plan, the reaction to a shortfall is improvised and late. Always model the downside, especially for cash.

### Forgetting Inventory Reconciliation In Production

Setting production equal to sales ignores the need to build or draw down inventory. The result is either stockouts or excess inventory that consumes cash and warehouse space. Reconcile production to sales plus desired ending inventory minus beginning inventory explicitly.

### Omitting Or Underestimating Capital Expenditures

Treating capital spending as optional or rounding it down understates cash needs and overstates free cash flow. Capital commitments must be in the cash budget and reflected in depreciation and asset balances on the budgeted balance sheet.

### Forcing The Budgeted Balance Sheet To Balance With A Plug

A plug hides a missing link in inventory, depreciation, retained earnings, payables, or financing. It makes the statements appear consistent while concealing an error that will surface during execution. Find the broken link instead.

### Ignoring Tax Timing And Installments

Tax is paid on statutory dates, not evenly through the year, and estimated installments can create large cash outflows in specific months. Omitting tax timing from the cash budget creates false liquidity comfort. Model tax payments on their real dates, and treat budgeted tax as a planning figure subject to professional confirmation.

### Treating The Budget As Fixed Once Approved

Conditions change, and a budget that is never revisited becomes stale and ignored. Define a review and reforecast cadence so the budget stays relevant, and distinguish a fixed performance target from a living planning tool.

## Self-Check

- Is the sales forecast built from documented drivers with base, stretch, and downside scenarios, and are its assumptions written down?
- Are the operating budgets built in the correct sequence with explicit references between sales, production, materials, labor, and overhead?
- Does the production budget reconcile sales, desired ending inventory, and beginning inventory rather than equating production to sales?
- Is the cash budget built from real collection, payment, payroll, tax, and financing timing rather than accrual averages?
- Are capital expenditures and financing decisions included and reflected in both the cash budget and the budgeted balance sheet?
- Do the budgeted income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement tie together without a balancing plug?
- Is each component budget owned by the accountable function, with documented approval and key assumptions?
- Are tax payments modeled on real statutory dates and treated as planning figures subject to professional confirmation?
- Is there a downside case with identified contingency triggers for cash and operations?
- Does the analysis acknowledge jurisdiction and entity limits and flag where a qualified professional must confirm tax, financing, or covenant assumptions?
