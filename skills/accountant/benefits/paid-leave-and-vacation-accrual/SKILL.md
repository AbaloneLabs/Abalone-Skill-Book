# Paid Leave And Vacation Accrual

Paid time off, vacation, and sick leave are forms of deferred compensation. The employee earns the right to paid absence as they render service, and the employer incurs a liability that will be settled either by granting the time off or, in many cases, by paying it out in cash on termination. Agents often treat leave as a cash event, recorded only when the employee takes a day off or resigns, but accrual accounting requires the liability to build as the leave is earned and to be measured at the rate the employer will actually pay.

The harm this skill prevents is understated compensation cost and understated liabilities from failing to accrue earned leave, misstated balances from measuring at historical rather than current rates, and surprise cash outflows when a long-tenured employee resigns with a large bank. The judgment problem is deciding what kind of leave is being earned, whether it vests, how to measure it, and when, if ever, it must be paid out.

This is accounting guidance, not legal or tax advice. Whether unused leave must be paid on termination, whether accruals carry over year to year, and how vesting is defined vary enormously by jurisdiction, by plan policy, and sometimes by the wording of the employee handbook. Route definitive payout and policy compliance questions to a qualified HR professional or employment lawyer. The agent's freedom is to structure the accrual, measurement, and reconciliation accounting correctly and to flag where policy-specific inputs are required; it is not to assert payout rules as universal facts.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Vesting From Non-Vesting Leave

The accounting treatment of paid leave turns on whether the leave vests, meaning the employee retains the right to the benefit, including a cash payout on termination, or whether it is non-vesting, meaning it is forfeited if not used. This distinction drives whether a liability is recognized.

Vesting leave:

- the employee accumulates a right to paid absence that is not forfeited on termination;
- a liability is recognized as the leave is earned;
- the liability is measured at the rate expected to be paid;
- common examples include vacation and many PTO banks.

Non-vesting leave:

- the leave is available only while employed and is forfeited if not used or on termination;
- under many frameworks, no liability is recognized until the leave is taken;
- common examples include sick leave that does not pay out and certain personal leave;
- watch for jurisdictions or frameworks that require accrual even for conditional leave.

Confirm the vesting character from the written policy, because handbook wording can convert an intended non-vesting benefit into a vesting one.

### Recognize Leave As It Is Earned

For vesting leave, the liability builds continuously as the employee renders service, not when the time is taken or the employee resigns. The accrual follows the earning schedule in the policy, typically a per-pay-period or per-month accrual.

Recognition criteria:

- accrue the leave earned each period at the policy rate;
- record the offsetting liability;
- reduce the liability when leave is taken;
- handle accrual caps, where earning stops once a maximum balance is reached;
- restart earning when the balance drops below the cap.

### Measure The Liability At Current Pay Rates

The leave liability should be measured at the compensation rate the employer expects to pay when the leave is taken or paid out, not at the historical rate when the leave was earned. Because pay rates rise over time, the liability must be remeasured periodically.

Measurement principles:

- value the unused leave balance at the employee's current pay rate;
- update the liability when pay rates change, including merit increases and promotions;
- recognize the effect of rate changes as additional compensation cost in the period of the change;
- for large populations, consider an average rate or a statistical remeasurement where the framework permits;
- do not carry the liability at stale rates across multiple annual cycles.

### Handle Sabbatical And Extended Leave Programs

Sabbatical programs, where an employee earns an extended paid absence after a period of service, create a longer-horizon accrual. The cost should be recognized over the qualifying service period, not when the sabbatical is taken.

Sabbatical considerations:

- recognize the cost over the period that earns the sabbatical, often several years;
- if the sabbatical is conditional and not probable, do not accrue until the condition is met;
- measure at the expected pay rate during the sabbatical;
- track vesting and forfeiture if the employee leaves before qualifying.

### Account For PTO Payouts On Termination

When a vesting leave-earning employee terminates, the accumulated balance is typically paid out in cash. The payout settles the liability that was accrued over the employee's tenure, so the accounting should reflect a reduction of the liability rather than a new expense.

Payout accounting:

- pay the vested balance at the termination pay rate;
- reduce the leave liability by the gross payout;
- withhold taxes and record the net pay through payroll;
- forfeit any non-vested balance per policy;
- confirm the payout amount against the leave ledger before payment.

### Apply Jurisdictional Rules On Mandatory Payout

Whether unused leave must be paid on termination is a jurisdiction and policy question, not an accounting choice. Some jurisdictions treat all earned vacation as wages that must be paid out, others permit use-it-or-lose-it policies with conditions, and some distinguish vacation from sick leave.

Compliance considerations:

- confirm whether the jurisdiction treats accrued vacation as earned wages;
- confirm whether the policy, as written, creates a vesting or forfeiture right;
- recognize that ambiguous handbook language can create an unintended payout obligation;
- where payout is mandatory, the leave is vesting and a liability must be accrued;
- where payout is not mandatory and the policy is clearly non-vesting, the treatment follows the non-vesting logic.

### Reconcile The Leave Ledger To The General Ledger

The leave liability in the general ledger must tie to the detailed leave balances tracked in the payroll or time system. A drift signals accrual errors, missed taken-leave entries, or rate-update gaps.

Reconciliation:

- tie the total unused leave hours to the liability balance at current rates;
- reconcile additions, taken leave, and payouts each period;
- investigate variances at the employee or department level;
- confirm accrual caps are enforced in both systems;
- clear any residual after each reconciliation cycle.

## Common Traps

### Recording Leave Only When Taken Or Paid

Recording vacation expense only when an employee takes a day off, or only on termination, understates the period's compensation cost and ignores the accumulating liability. Accrue vesting leave as it is earned.

### Treating All Leave As Non-Vesting

Assuming sick leave or PTO never vests can be wrong where the policy or jurisdiction converts it to a payout obligation. Confirm the vesting character from the written policy and applicable law.

### Measuring The Liability At Historical Rates

Carrying the leave liability at the rate when it was earned, rather than the current rate, understates the obligation as pay rises. Remeasure at current rates and recognize rate-change effects.

### Ignoring Accrual Caps

Earning leave beyond a cap inflates the liability and overstates expense. Apply the cap so earning stops at the maximum and resumes only when the balance drops.

### Missing The Sabbatical Accrual Over The Qualifying Period

Recording the sabbatical cost only when the leave is taken concentrates years of expense in one period. Recognize the cost over the qualifying service period.

### Paying Out Without Reducing The Liability

Treating a termination payout as new expense, rather than as a settlement of the accrued liability, double counts the cost. Reduce the liability by the gross payout.

### Letting The Leave Ledger Drift From The General Ledger

When the detailed leave balances no longer tie to the liability account, the gap reflects accrual, taken-leave, or rate-update errors. Skipping the reconciliation lets the misstatement grow.

## Self-Check

- Is each leave type classified as vesting or non-vesting based on the written policy and applicable jurisdiction?
- Is vesting leave accrued as it is earned, with the liability building over the earning period?
- Is the leave liability measured at current pay rates and remeasured when rates change?
- Are accrual caps enforced so earning stops at the maximum balance and resumes only below the cap?
- Are sabbatical and extended leave costs recognized over the qualifying service period rather than when taken?
- Are termination payouts of vested leave recorded as a reduction of the liability rather than new expense?
- Is any non-vested balance forfeited per policy on termination, with the forfeiture recorded?
- Are jurisdictional rules on mandatory payout of unused leave confirmed before classifying a leave type as non-vesting?
- Does the leave liability in the general ledger tie to the detailed leave balances in the payroll or time system?
- Are policy-specific and jurisdiction-specific payout, carryover, and vesting rules confirmed with a qualified HR professional or employment lawyer rather than asserted as universal facts?
