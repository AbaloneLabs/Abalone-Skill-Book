# Health And Welfare Benefit Accounting

Health and welfare benefits, medical, dental, vision, life, disability, and similar coverages, are a large and growing slice of compensation cost, and they create accounting complexity that a simple premium payment does not capture. Fully insured plans look like a monthly invoice. Self-funded plans look like a stream of claim payments plus an estimate of claims that have been incurred but not yet reported. Agents often apply the fully insured logic to a self-funded plan, or vice versa, and miss the accrual, the employee contribution reconciliation, and the special arrangements such as COBRA, flexible spending accounts, health savings and reimbursement arrangements, and stop-loss coverage.

The harm this skill prevents is understated benefit expense from missing IBNR accruals, misstated liabilities from unreconciled employee contributions, broken FSA and HSA mechanics, and silent exposure when a self-funded plan's claims spike beyond expectations. The judgment problem is recognizing the right expense under the right funding model and reconciling every dollar of employee contribution to the carrier or administrator.

This is accounting guidance, not legal, actuarial, or tax advice. Benefit regulations, continuation coverage rules, FSA and HSA contribution limits, stop-loss terms, and claims reserve methods vary by jurisdiction and plan document. Route definitive plan design, compliance, and actuarial questions to a qualified benefits broker, plan administrator, or tax advisor. The agent's freedom is to structure the expense, liability, accrual, and reconciliation accounting correctly and to flag where specialist input is required; it is not to assert plan-specific rules as universal facts.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Fully Insured From Self-Funded Plans

The accounting follows the funding model. A fully insured plan is a contract: the employer pays a fixed premium and the insurer bears the claims risk. A self-funded plan is a promise: the employer pays claims as they occur and bears the risk, often buying stop-loss insurance to cap exposure. The two produce very different expense patterns and liabilities.

Fully insured:

- expense is the premium billed, recognized over the coverage period;
- the insurer bears claims risk;
- the balance sheet liability is premiums earned but not yet paid;
- reconciliations tie to the carrier invoice.

Self-funded:

- expense is claims paid plus the change in incurred-but-not-reported reserves, plus administrative and stop-loss premiums;
- the employer bears claims risk;
- the balance sheet carries an IBNR liability and any unpaid claims;
- reconciliations tie to the third-party administrator's claims reports.

Confirm the funding model from the plan document before recording, because the same employer can run both.

### Accrue Incurred-But-Not-Reported Claims For Self-Funded Plans

In a self-funded plan, claims are incurred when the medical service is rendered, but the claim may not be reported or paid until weeks or months later. Recognizing only paid claims understates the period's expense and the liability. An IBNR accrual estimates the claims that have been incurred but not yet reported or paid.

IBNR accrual criteria:

- estimate claims incurred through the period-end based on historical run-out patterns;
- record the IBNR as a liability with a corresponding expense;
- true up the estimate each period as actual claims emerge and the run-out completes;
- obtain actuarial support for the estimate where the plan is material;
- document the methodology, the data sources, and the run-out assumptions.

### Account For Employer Premiums And Employee Contributions Separately

Health and welfare cost has two components: the employer-paid portion, which is expense, and the employee-paid portion, which is withheld from pay and remitted to the carrier. Both must be recorded, and the employee portion must be reconciled to the carrier.

Separation principles:

- the employer share is benefit expense, accrued over the coverage period;
- the employee share is withheld from pay and held as a liability until remitted;
- do not net the two into a single premium line;
- the gross premium, employer share, and employee share should all be visible in the entry or schedule.

### Reconcile Employee Contributions To The Carrier

Employee benefit deductions flow from payroll to the carrier, and the amount remitted must equal the amount withheld. A drift signals enrollment mismatches, missed deductions, or remittance errors.

Reconciliation:

- tie the per-pay-period deduction to the enrollment election and rate;
- tie the cumulative deductions to the carrier invoice each billing cycle;
- investigate variances, which often indicate a coverage tier change not reflected in payroll;
- clear any residual liability after remittance;
- reconcile at the employee level when the carrier provides a detail file.

### Handle COBRA And Continuation Coverage

When an employee loses coverage due to termination or reduced hours, many jurisdictions require the employer to offer continuation coverage, often at the employee's expense. The accounting for COBRA premiums is generally straightforward, paid by the participant, but the administration and the 2 percent administrative surcharge must be tracked.

COBRA considerations:

- collect the participant premium, often 102 percent of the full premium;
- record the premium received and remitted to the carrier;
- track the election, grace, and coverage periods;
- recognize that the employer is not subsidizing the coverage unless it chooses to;
- handle nonpayment and coverage termination cleanly.

### Account For Flexible Spending Accounts Correctly

FSAs are prefunded, tax-advantaged accounts with a use-it-or-lose-it rule subject to limited carryover or grace period extensions. The accounting must reflect the annual election, the prefunding, the claims reimbursement, and the forfeitures.

FSA mechanics:

- the full annual election is available on day one of the plan year, creating an early exposure;
- record employee contributions as a liability and employer contributions, if any, separately;
- reimburse claims as submitted and approved;
- forfeit unused balances at year-end subject to carryover or grace period rules;
- reconcile the FSA liability to the administrator's report.

### Track HSA, HRA, And Stop-Loss Distinctly

Health savings accounts, health reimbursement arrangements, and stop-loss insurance each have distinct accounting. HSAs are employee-owned accounts funded by employer or employee contributions; HRAs are employer-funded reimbursements; stop-loss is insurance the employer buys to cap self-funded claims exposure.

Distinct treatment:

- HSA employer contributions are expense when contributed and are not subject to forfeiture;
- HRA contributions fund a reimbursement arrangement and are expensed as claims are reimbursed;
- stop-loss premiums are insurance expense, recognized over the policy period;
- stop-loss recoveries, when claims exceed the attachment point, reduce claims expense when recoverable;
- do not commingle these with the core medical plan liability.

## Common Traps

### Applying Fully Insured Logic To A Self-Funded Plan

Recording only paid claims for a self-funded plan ignores the IBNR accrual and understates both expense and liability. Confirm the funding model and accrue IBNR where self-funded.

### Ignoring The IBNR Accrual

Even when the self-funded model is recognized, skipping the IBNR estimate because claims are not yet reported leaves the period's expense understated. Estimate, record, and true up each period.

### Netting Employer And Employee Premium Shares

Combining the employer expense and the employee withholding into one premium line hides the liability for unremitted employee contributions and distorts compensation cost. Keep them separate.

### Letting Employee Contributions Drift From The Carrier

When withheld deductions no longer tie to the carrier invoice, the gap usually reflects an enrollment or coverage-tier change not updated in payroll. Skipping the reconciliation lets the error compound.

### Treating FSAs Like A Simple Reimbursement Account

FSAs are prefunded with the full annual election available immediately, creating early exposure, and they carry use-it-or-lose-it forfeiture rules. Treating them as a pay-as-you-go reimbursement misstates the liability.

### Mishandling HSA Versus HRA

HSAs are employee-owned and non-forfeitable; HRAs are employer-funded reimbursement arrangements. Confusing the two misstates expense and forfeiture treatment.

### Forgetting Stop-Loss Recoveries

Stop-loss premiums are recorded, but recoveries when claims exceed the attachment point are sometimes missed, overstating net claims expense. Track and recognize recoverable stop-loss recoveries.

## Self-Check

- Is the plan funding model, fully insured or self-funded, confirmed from the plan document before recording?
- For self-funded plans, is an IBNR accrual estimated, recorded, and trued up each period with documented methodology?
- Are employer-paid premiums recorded as expense over the coverage period, separate from employee contributions withheld?
- Are employee benefit contributions reconciled to the carrier invoice each billing cycle with variances investigated?
- Is COBRA or continuation coverage premium collected, remitted, and tracked through its election and coverage periods?
- Are FSA elections, prefunding, reimbursements, and forfeitures recorded with the use-it-or-lose-it or carryover rules applied?
- Are HSA employer contributions expensed when contributed and treated as non-forfeitable, distinct from HRAs?
- Are HRA reimbursements expensed as claims are reimbursed, with the liability tracked to the administrator?
- Are stop-loss premiums expensed over the policy period and recoveries recognized when claims exceed the attachment point?
- Are plan-specific regulations, contribution limits, continuation rules, and reserve methods confirmed with a qualified benefits broker, plan administrator, or tax advisor rather than asserted as universal facts?
