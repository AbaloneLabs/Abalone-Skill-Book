# Retirement Plan Accounting

Employer retirement plans create two kinds of accounting obligation. Defined contribution plans, such as 401(k)-style arrangements, generate employer matching and profit-sharing contributions that must be accrued as the employee earns them and remitted to the custodian on time. Defined benefit plans generate a far more complex obligation: a promise to pay future pensions whose present value must be measured, recognized, and updated each period. Agents often conflate the two or treat the plan as a cash event when the contribution is funded, but the recognition, vesting, forfeiture, and funding-versus-expense distinctions matter for accurate financial statements.

The harm this skill prevents is understated compensation cost from delayed or missing accruals, misstated liabilities from confusing expense with funding, broken vesting and forfeiture accounting, and, for defined benefit plans, a liability that is ignored because it is hard to measure. The judgment problem is recognizing the right expense in the right period under the right condition, not simply recording the cash sent to the plan.

This is accounting guidance, not legal, actuarial, or tax advice. Plan types, contribution limits, vesting rules, nondiscrimination testing, and defined benefit measurement standards vary by jurisdiction and plan document, and defined benefit obligations require an actuary. Route definitive plan design, compliance, and actuarial questions to a qualified plan administrator, actuary, or tax advisor. The agent's freedom is to structure the expense, liability, and reconciliation accounting correctly and to flag where specialist input is required; it is not to assert plan-specific rules as universal facts.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Defined Contribution From Defined Benefit

The accounting starts with plan type, because the two are recognized very differently. Mixing their logic produces materially wrong statements.

Defined contribution:

- the employer's obligation is the contribution, not the eventual benefit;
- expense equals the contribution earned by the employee in the period;
- no ongoing balance sheet liability beyond amounts withheld but not yet remitted;
- investment risk sits with the employee.

Defined benefit:

- the employer's obligation is the future pension promise;
- a projected benefit obligation must be measured, typically by an actuary;
- expense includes service cost, interest cost, expected return on plan assets, and amortization of gains, losses, and prior service costs;
- investment risk sits with the employer.

Confirm the plan type from the plan document before recording anything, because the same employer can sponsor both.

### Accrue Defined Contribution Matching As Earned

Employer matching and profit-sharing contributions are compensation cost, and they should be accrued as the employee earns the right to them, not when the contribution is funded to the custodian. A pay run that straddles a period-end requires the matching on the pre-cutoff wages to be accrued in the closing period.

Accrual criteria:

- accrue the match on wages earned through the period-end date;
- record the offsetting liability until the contribution is remitted;
- reverse or net the accrual when the actual contribution posts;
- respect any per-paycheck or annual true-up in the match formula;
- watch for true-up true-down plans where the year-end match corrects earlier over- or under-matching.

### Apply Vesting Schedules To Employer Contributions

Employer contributions often vest over time, meaning the employee's ownership builds according to a schedule. Vesting affects forfeitures and the eventual cost, but for defined contribution plans it generally does not change the period of expense recognition, which follows the service that earned the contribution.

Vesting considerations:

- identify the vesting schedule: cliff, graded, or immediate;
- track each employee's vested percentage and accumulated balance;
- recognize forfeitures when a non-vested employee terminates;
- distinguish employee deferrals, which are generally immediately vested, from employer contributions, which follow the schedule;
- confirm whether the plan uses a vesting schedule that changes with hours or years of service.

### Account For Forfeitures Deliberately

When a non-vested employee terminates, the unvested employer contribution is forfeited. Forfeitures are not simply ignored; they are tracked and used according to the plan document, often to reduce future employer contributions or to pay plan administrative expenses.

Forfeiture accounting:

- reclassify the forfeited amount out of the employer contribution liability;
- track forfeitures in a separate suspense or forfeiture account;
- apply forfeitures to reduce future employer contributions or pay plan expenses as the plan permits;
- do not double count forfeitures as income and as a contribution reduction;
- reconcile the forfeiture balance to the plan administrator's records.

### Separate Plan Expense From Funding

Expense recognition and funding are different events. Expense is earned when the employee renders service or, for defined benefit, when the obligation accrues. Funding is the cash sent to the plan trustee. The two rarely match in amount or timing.

Separation principles:

- defined contribution expense follows the matching earned each period, regardless of when funded;
- defined benefit expense follows the actuarial measurement, regardless of the contribution deposited;
- the liability or asset on the balance sheet reflects the difference between cumulative expense and cumulative funding;
- a funded status different from the recognized amount requires careful presentation and disclosure.

### Handle Defined Benefit Basics Without Pretending To Be The Actuary

Defined benefit accounting is complex and depends on actuarial assumptions: discount rate, expected return on plan assets, mortality, salary escalation, and amortization methods. The agent's role is to record the actuary's measurement correctly, not to produce it.

Defined benefit handling:

- obtain the actuarial valuation at the required frequency, typically annually with interim roll-forward;
- record service cost in compensation expense and interest cost in financing or operating expense per the applicable framework;
- recognize expected return on plan assets and amortize actuarial gains, losses, and prior service costs in other comprehensive income or expense as the framework requires;
- present the net defined benefit asset or liability on the balance sheet;
- reconcile the recorded obligation and plan assets to the actuarial report each period.

### Reconcile Contributions To The Plan Custodian

Retirement contributions, whether employee deferrals or employer matches, must be reconciled to the plan custodian and to participant accounts. A drift between the ledger and the custodian signals late deposits, missing participants, or posting errors.

Reconciliation:

- tie employee deferral liabilities to the custodian deposit each pay period;
- tie employer contribution liabilities to the custodian deposit on the contribution schedule;
- reconcile participant-level contributions to the custodian statement at least quarterly;
- investigate any residual balance in the contribution liability accounts;
- confirm deposit timing meets the plan's deadline, because late deposits can be a prohibited transaction.

## Common Traps

### Recognizing The Match When Funded Instead Of When Earned

Recording the employer match only when cash is sent to the custodian shifts compensation cost into the next period and understates the closing period's labor cost. Accrue on wages earned, fund on the plan schedule.

### Conflating Defined Contribution And Defined Benefit

Applying defined contribution logic to a defined benefit plan, or vice versa, produces fundamentally wrong expense and liability. Confirm the plan type from the document before recording.

### Ignoring Vesting And Forfeitures

Treating all employer contributions as permanently the employee's ignores that non-vested balances forfeit on termination. Forfeitures must be tracked, applied per the plan, and reconciled.

### Confusing Expense With Funding

Equating the period's expense with the cash contributed hides the accrued liability or asset and misstates the balance sheet. The two are separate events that must be tracked independently.

### Recording Defined Benefit Without The Actuary

Attempting to estimate a defined benefit obligation without an actuarial valuation produces unreliable numbers and disclosure failures. Obtain and record the actuary's measurement.

### Letting Contribution Liabilities Drift From The Custodian

A residual balance in the contribution liability after the deposit cycle usually means a late deposit, a missing participant, or a posting error. Skipping the reconciliation lets the gap grow and can create prohibited transaction exposure.

### Missing The Plan's Deposit Deadline

Employee deferrals generally must be deposited on a tight schedule tied to when they could be segregated from employer assets. Treating the deposit as a monthly convenience can cross into prohibited transaction territory.

## Self-Check

- Is the plan type, defined contribution or defined benefit, confirmed from the plan document before recording?
- Is defined contribution matching and profit-sharing accrued as the employee earns it, including period-end straddle splits?
- Is the vesting schedule, cliff, graded, or immediate, applied to employer contributions and tracked per employee?
- Are forfeitures of non-vested balances recorded, tracked, and applied to reduce future contributions or pay plan expenses as the plan permits?
- Is plan expense kept separate from plan funding, with the difference reflected as a liability or asset on the balance sheet?
- For defined benefit plans, is the actuarial valuation obtained and recorded, including service cost, interest cost, expected return, and amortization?
- Is the net defined benefit asset or liability presented and reconciled to the actuarial report each period?
- Are employee deferral and employer contribution liabilities reconciled to the custodian deposits each cycle?
- Are participant-level contributions reconciled to custodian statements at least quarterly?
- Are plan-specific contribution limits, vesting rules, deposit deadlines, and actuarial assumptions confirmed with a qualified plan administrator, actuary, or tax advisor rather than asserted as universal facts?
