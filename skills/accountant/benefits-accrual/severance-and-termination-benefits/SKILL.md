# Severance And Termination Benefits

Severance and termination benefits are infrequent, large, and easy to mis-time. Unlike ongoing compensation, these benefits are triggered by a specific event: a termination, a restructuring, or an exit decision. The accounting depends on whether the arrangement is voluntary or involuntary, when the entity's obligation begins, and whether the benefits are one-time termination benefits or ongoing special termination benefits. Recognizing severance in the wrong period, or under the wrong recognition model, distorts both the restructuring charge and ongoing operating expense.

This skill covers judgment for recognizing, measuring, and disclosing severance and termination benefits. The accounting is governed by standards such as US ASC 420 for exit and disposal activities, ASC 712 for ongoing benefit arrangements, and international IAS 19, while employment law on notice, severance entitlements, and collective consultation varies materially by jurisdiction and entity. Confirm final plan interpretation, legal entitlements, and tax treatment with a qualified accountant and, where employment law or works-council obligations are involved, legal counsel in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Benefit By Recognition Model

Termination benefits fall into different recognition models depending on their nature. The classification drives when the liability and expense are recognized.

Distinguish:

- involuntary one-time termination benefits, generally recognized when the entity has a present obligation based on a communicated plan and the liability is estimable;
- voluntary termination or early retirement benefits offered to induce employees to leave, recognized when the employees accept the offer;
- special termination benefits that continue over a period after termination, recognized over the future service period if service is required;
- benefits provided under an ongoing benefit arrangement (such as an enhanced pension) that follow the relevant benefit accounting;
- statutory or contractual notice pay owed on termination.

The trap is applying the involuntary recognition trigger to a voluntary offer, or vice versa, which recognizes the cost in the wrong period.

### Establish The Present Obligation For Involuntary Terminations

For involuntary one-time termination benefits, recognition generally requires a present obligation. Confirm the elements that create it.

Confirm:

- a plan of termination that identifies the affected employees, locations, and roles;
- approval by the appropriate level of authority and a commitment to execute;
- communication of the plan to the affected employees or their representatives;
- that the terms are set and the amount is estimable;
- that the obligation exists at the reporting date, not merely an intention to restructure.

An intention to restructure that has not been communicated or approved does not create a present obligation. Recognizing a liability before the plan is communicated overstates the charge and the liability.

### Recognize Voluntary Offers On Acceptance

Voluntary termination benefits are offered to induce employees to leave. The obligation arises as employees accept the offer, not when the offer is made.

Apply:

- estimate the cost based on the expected number of acceptances;
- update the estimate as acceptances are received;
- recognize the cost as the obligation is incurred through acceptance;
- handle withdrawal periods during which employees can revoke acceptance, where applicable.

Recognizing the full offered pool at the offer date, before any acceptance, overstates the liability. Recognizing only on final settlement understates the interim obligation.

### Separate Termination Cost From Ongoing And Asset Costs

A restructuring event generates several types of cost that have different accounting. Do not lump them into one severance line.

Separate:

- one-time termination benefits (the exit cost);
- ongoing benefit arrangements and enhancements (recognized under benefit accounting);
- costs to relocate or retain employees (recognized over the service period);
- asset impairments and write-downs triggered by the exit (recognized under impairment rules);
- contract termination penalties and exit costs (recognized when the obligation exists);
- costs to operate assets until disposal.

Mixing these into a single restructuring charge obscures the different recognition triggers and can misstate both the period and the classification of cost.

### Measure The Liability And Update Estimates

Severance liabilities are estimates that change as plans are executed. Establish a process to measure and true up.

Measure and update:

- the number of affected employees and their individual severance entitlements;
- statutory, contractual, and enhanced amounts;
- the probability of acceptance for voluntary offers;
- the effect of alternative employment, rehiring, or challenges to the termination;
- payments made and remaining obligations;
- the reversal of accruals when terminations do not occur or costs are lower than estimated.

Severance accruals that are never trued up become a dumping ground for estimates that no longer reflect reality.

### Respect Legal Entitlements And Consultation

Severance accounting cannot be separated from employment law. Statutory notice, severance entitlements, and collective consultation obligations often determine both the amount and the timing of the obligation.

Consider:

- statutory minimum notice and severance that may exceed contractual terms;
- works-council, union, or collective consultation requirements that affect when a plan is final;
- the effect of jurisdiction-specific rules on mass layoffs or plant closings;
- whether enhanced benefits create an ongoing obligation rather than a one-time cost;
- tax and social security treatment of severance, which may differ from ordinary pay.

A plan that is not yet final because consultation is incomplete may not create a present obligation at the reporting date.

### Maintain Disclosure For Exit And Disposal Activities

Exit and restructuring activities carry specific disclosure obligations. Confirm the entity captures:

- the nature and timing of the exit plan;
- the components of the charge, separated by type;
- the reconciliation of the accrual from opening to closing balance;
- the segments or locations affected;
- the expected future cash outflows;
- any assumptions that significantly affect the measurement.

## Common Traps

### Recognizing Severance Before The Obligation Exists

Recording a restructuring charge when management merely intends to restructure, before approval and communication, violates the recognition trigger and overstates the liability.

### Applying The Wrong Recognition Model

Treating a voluntary early-retirement offer as an involuntary termination recognizes the cost at the wrong time. Voluntary offers follow acceptance; involuntary benefits follow the communicated plan.

### Bundling All Restructuring Cost As Severance

Asset impairments, contract penalties, relocation, retention, and ongoing benefit enhancements are not severance. Grouping them hides the different recognition rules and misleads users about the nature of the charge.

### Ignoring Statutory Entitlements And Consultation

Contractual severance may be less than statutory minimums, and a plan may not be final until consultation concludes. Accounting that ignores legal entitlements and process understates or mistimes the liability.

### Failing To True Up Severance Estimates

Severance accruals that are established and never revisited become stale. Acceptances differ from estimates, some employees are redeployed, and challenges change outcomes. Without true-up, the liability drifts from reality.

### Forgetting Ongoing Benefit Enhancements

An enhanced pension or continued healthcare for terminated employees is often an ongoing benefit arrangement, not a one-time termination cost. Accounting for it as severance omits the service-period or actuarial recognition it requires.

## Self-Check

- Has each termination benefit been classified as involuntary one-time, voluntary, special termination with future service, or an ongoing benefit enhancement, and matched to the correct recognition model?
- For involuntary terminations, is there a communicated, approved plan with estimable terms that creates a present obligation at the reporting date?
- For voluntary offers, is the cost recognized based on estimated and actual acceptances rather than the full offered pool at the offer date?
- Are severance, ongoing benefit enhancements, relocation, retention, asset impairment, and contract penalties separated by their recognition rules rather than bundled?
- Is the liability measured from statutory, contractual, and enhanced entitlements, and trued up as acceptances, redeployments, and challenges change the estimate?
- Have statutory notice, severance minimums, and collective consultation requirements been considered in determining the amount and timing of the obligation?
- Does disclosure separate the charge by type, reconcile the accrual, and identify the affected segments, expected cash outflows, and key assumptions?
- Has the final classification, measurement, legal entitlements, and tax treatment been confirmed with a qualified accountant and, where employment law is involved, legal counsel for the relevant jurisdiction?
