# Absence And Disability Leave Accounting

Beyond ordinary vacation, employees are absent from work for many compensated reasons: short-term and long-term disability, workers' compensation injuries, paid family and medical leave, parental leave, and salary continuation during illness. Each of these programs has a different payer, a different funding source, a different duration, and a different accounting treatment. Agents often lump them together as "leave" and record a single accrual, but the liability, the expense recognition, and the offsetting recoveries differ materially across program types.

The harm this skill prevents is understated compensation cost and liability from failing to accrue employer-paid disability or salary continuation, misstated recoveries from ignoring reimbursements under insured or state-mandated programs, and broken liability measurement for long-duration obligations. The judgment problem is identifying who bears the cost of each absence, when the expense is incurred, and how recoveries and subsidies are recorded.

This is accounting guidance, not legal, actuarial, or tax advice. Disability benefit terms, workers' compensation rules, paid family and medical leave program structures, and salary continuation policies vary enormously by jurisdiction, by whether the program is state-mandated or voluntary, and by the insurance contract. Route definitive program design, compliance, and claims questions to a qualified benefits broker, HR professional, or tax advisor. The agent's freedom is to structure the accrual, liability, and recovery accounting correctly and to flag where specialist input is required; it is not to assert program rules as universal facts.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Absence By Payer And Funding Source

The accounting follows who pays the employee during the absence and where the money comes from. Classify each program before recording.

Program classifications:

- short-term disability: often employer-paid, insured, or state-mandated, replacing a portion of wages for a non-work illness or injury;
- long-term disability: typically insured, replacing a portion of wages beyond the short-term period, often with an employer-paid or employee-paid premium;
- workers' compensation: a statutory program covering work-related injury or illness, funded through employer-paid insurance or self-insurance;
- paid family and medical leave: may be state-mandated and funded through employee and employer contributions, or voluntary and employer-funded;
- parental leave: may overlay statutory programs with employer-paid salary continuation;
- salary continuation: an employer policy that pays full or partial wages during illness, distinct from insured disability.

The classification determines whether the employer accrues a liability, records an insurance recovery, or simply remits contributions to a state fund.

### Accrue Employer-Paid Disability And Salary Continuation

When the employer bears the cost of disability or salary continuation, the expense and liability are incurred as the employee renders the service that earns the benefit or, for self-insured claims, as the absence occurs. Recording only the cash paid understates the obligation.

Accrual criteria:

- for insured disability, the premium is expense over the coverage period, with claims paid by the insurer;
- for self-insured or employer-paid disability, accrue the expected benefit payments for absences incurred through the period-end;
- for salary continuation, accrue the cost of absences earned or incurred in the period;
- record recoveries from the insurer or state program as a reduction of expense or as a reimbursement receivable;
- true up accruals as actual benefit payments emerge.

### Account For Workers' Compensation By Funding Model

Workers' compensation accounting depends on whether the employer is insured or self-insured. An insured employer records the premium as expense and lets the insurer pay claims; a self-insured employer accrues for claims incurred, including an estimate of incurred-but-not-reported claims.

Funding model handling:

- insured: recognize the premium over the policy period, with experience-rated adjustments when the audit or modification is finalized;
- self-insured: accrue the expected cost of claims incurred through the period-end, including IBNR, often with actuarial support;
- record recoveries or subrogation when applicable;
- track the claims liability and reconcile to the claims administrator's report;
- recognize experience modifications and policy audits as adjustments to expense.

### Separate State-Mandated From Voluntary Paid Leave Programs

Paid family and medical leave programs may be state-mandated, funded through payroll contributions remitted to a state fund, or voluntary, funded by the employer. The accounting differs sharply.

State-mandated programs:

- employee and employer contributions are withheld and remitted to the state fund, recorded as liabilities until remitted;
- benefits are paid by the state, not the employer, so the employer generally does not accrue a benefit liability;
- the contributions are similar to payroll taxes in their accounting;
- watch for jurisdiction-specific employer contribution rates and wage bases.

Voluntary programs:

- the employer funds the benefit, so the expense and liability are accrued as leave is taken or earned;
- any offset from a state program the employer participates in is recorded as a recovery;
- track the liability for ongoing obligations.

### Measure Long-Duration Disability Obligations Appropriately

Long-term disability and long-duration salary continuation create obligations that extend far into the future. The liability should reflect the present value of expected future payments, often requiring actuarial measurement, and should be discounted where the framework requires.

Measurement principles:

- for self-insured long-term disability, measure the present value of expected future benefit payments;
- use a discount rate and actuarial assumptions consistent with the framework;
- remeasure periodically as assumptions and experience change;
- recognize the effect of remeasurement in the appropriate period;
- obtain actuarial support for material long-duration obligations.

### Record Recoveries And Subsidies Correctly

Many absence programs involve recoveries: insurance reimbursements, state program offsets, or social security disability offsets. These reduce the employer's net cost and must be recorded, not ignored.

Recovery handling:

- record insured disability recoveries as a reduction of disability expense or as a reimbursement receivable;
- record state paid leave offsets against the employer-funded portion where applicable;
- record social security or other statutory offsets for long-term disability;
- do not net recoveries against gross wage expense; record them against the related benefit expense;
- reconcile recoveries to the insurer or state program statements.

### Reconcile Liabilities To Claims And Program Reports

Absence and disability liabilities must reconcile to the underlying claims, insurance, or program reports. A drift signals unrecorded claims, missed recoveries, or stale accruals.

Reconciliations:

- tie self-insured disability and workers' compensation liabilities to the claims administrator's incurred claims report;
- tie state-mandated contribution liabilities to the state fund remittances;
- tie insured premium expense to the carrier invoices and experience modifications;
- tie recoveries to the insurer or state program statements;
- investigate any residual balance each cycle.

## Common Traps

### Lumping All Leave Into One Accrual

Disability, workers' compensation, paid family leave, and salary continuation have different payers and funding sources. A single blended accrual misstates expense, liability, and recoveries.

### Recording Only Cash Paid For Self-Insured Claims

For self-insured disability or workers' compensation, recording only claims paid ignores incurred-but-not-reported claims and understates the liability. Accrue IBNR with actuarial support where material.

### Ignoring Recoveries From Insurers Or State Programs

Insured disability and state paid leave programs generate reimbursements that reduce the employer's net cost. Failing to record them overstates benefit expense.

### Treating State-Mandated Contributions As Employer Expense

State-mandated paid leave contributions are remitted to a state fund that pays the benefits. The contributions are liabilities until remitted, similar to payroll taxes, not an employer benefit accrual.

### Missing The Long-Duration Measurement

Long-term disability and long-duration salary continuation extend years into the future. Recording them at undiscounted nominal amounts ignores the time value and overstates the liability.

### Forgetting Experience Modifications On Workers' Compensation

Workers' compensation premiums are experience-rated, and the final premium often differs from the deposit. Failing to true up the audit or modification misstates the period's expense.

### Letting Claims Liabilities Drift From Administrator Reports

When the accrued claims liability no longer ties to the claims administrator's incurred report, the gap reflects unrecorded claims, missed recoveries, or stale estimates. Skipping the reconciliation lets the misstatement grow.

## Self-Check

- Is each absence program classified by payer and funding source before recording, distinguishing insured, self-insured, state-mandated, and employer-paid?
- Is employer-paid disability and salary continuation accrued as the benefit is earned or the absence is incurred, not only when cash is paid?
- For self-insured workers' compensation or disability, is an IBNR accrual recorded with actuarial support where material?
- Are state-mandated paid leave contributions recorded as liabilities until remitted rather than as employer benefit accruals?
- Are voluntary paid leave program benefits accrued as the employer-funded obligation is incurred?
- Are long-duration disability and salary continuation obligations measured at present value with actuarial support and remeasured periodically?
- Are recoveries from insurers, state programs, or statutory offsets recorded as reductions of the related benefit expense?
- Are workers' compensation experience modifications and policy audits trued up as adjustments to expense?
- Do the disability and workers' compensation liabilities reconcile to claims administrator and program reports each cycle?
- Are program-specific terms, funding rules, and statutory requirements confirmed with a qualified benefits broker, HR professional, or tax advisor rather than asserted as universal facts?
