# Vacation And Paid Time Off Accrual

Accruing vacation and paid time off (PTO) looks like a simple multiplication of unused hours by a pay rate. In practice it is a recurring source of misstatement because the obligation depends on company policy, employment law, the distinction between vesting and forfeitable leave, and the rate at which leave should be valued. An accrual that looks reasonable can be wrong because it ignored a legal entitlement to payout on termination, used the wrong pay rate, or double-counted leave that is already paid.

This skill covers judgment for accruing vacation, PTO, holiday, and similar compensated absences. The accounting treatment is influenced by standards such as US ASC 710 and international IAS 19, but the most important driver is often employment law and company policy on vesting, carryover, and termination payout, which vary by jurisdiction and entity. Confirm final policy interpretation, legal entitlements, and tax treatment with a qualified accountant and, where employment law is involved, legal counsel in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether The Leave Obligation Must Be Accrued

The first question is whether unused leave creates an obligation that must be recognized. This depends on both the accounting framework and the substance of the policy.

Establish:

- does the leave vest, meaning employees earn a right to be paid for unused balances even if they leave?
- is the policy use-it-or-lose-it, and is such forfeiture legally enforceable in the relevant jurisdiction?
- does the law require payout of certain leave types on termination regardless of policy?
- is the obligation a present legal or constructive obligation at the reporting date?

Many jurisdictions treat vacation as earned wages that must be paid on termination, making the leave vesting even if the policy says otherwise. If the law overrides the policy, accrue based on the legal entitlement, not the written forfeiture clause.

### Separate Leave Types By Their Accrual Behavior

Not all leave is the same. Mixing leave types into one accrual produces errors.

Distinguish:

- vacation and PTO that vest or carry over (accrue as earned);
- sick leave that vests (accrue) versus sick leave that only pays if illness occurs and does not carry over (generally expense when taken);
- holiday or floating holiday tied to specific dates (generally expense in the period of the holiday);
- sabbatical or extended leave earned over a long service period;
- compensatory time off for overtime workers;
- parental, jury, bereavement, and other statutory leave.

The conditional nature of sick leave is a frequent trap. Sick leave that does not vest and pays only on the occurrence of illness is usually expensed when taken, not accrued for unused balances.

### Value The Accrual At The Right Rate

The rate used to value unused leave affects the liability. Determine the correct basis.

Consider:

- the current pay rate at the reporting date, which is the most common basis;
- whether the framework requires use of the rate expected at payout or the rate in effect when earned;
- the effect of upcoming pay increases if leave is expected to be taken or paid after a raise;
- whether bonuses, shift differentials, or premium pay should be included in the rate for certain leave types;
- payroll taxes and benefits loading on the leave value where required.

Using a stale or average rate understates the liability when pay has risen or when the framework requires the current or expected future rate.

### Estimate The Population And Forfeitures Realistically

The accrual should reflect the leave that will actually be paid or taken, not a gross theoretical maximum.

Estimate:

- the eligible employee population and their leave balances;
- historical patterns of carryover, usage, and payout;
- expected forfeitures for non-vesting leave where the framework permits;
- the probability of termination payouts for vesting leave;
- caps on carryover and the effect of forced year-end use.

A pure gross calculation with no forfeiture or usage estimate overstates the liability for non-vesting leave and can understate it for vesting leave if termination payouts are ignored.

### Reconcile Balances To Payroll And HR Systems

The accrual is only as reliable as the leave balance data. Reconcile before recording.

Reconcile:

- leave balances per the time and attendance or HR system to the accrual calculation;
- earned, taken, and carried-over hours by employee or cohort;
- adjustments for policy changes, balance true-ups, and corrections;
- negative balances and advances that may need separate treatment;
- the leave liability to the payroll clearing and any unpaid leave taken but not yet paid.

Unreconciled leave data is a common cause of large year-end adjustments and audit findings.

### Handle Policy Changes And Rollovers Carefully

Leave policy changes affect the accrual and must be accounted for in the correct period.

Address:

- transitions between separate vacation and sick banks to a combined PTO bank;
- changes to carryover caps and the effect on existing balances;
- buy-back or conversion programs that settle leave for cash;
- mandatory shutdowns and the timing of related holiday pay;
- the effect of a policy change on the accrued liability, recognized in the period of the change.

## Common Traps

### Ignoring Legal Payout Requirements Behind A Use-It-Or-Lose-It Policy

A written forfeiture clause is meaningless if employment law in the jurisdiction treats vacation as earned wages payable on termination. Accruing based on the policy alone understates the liability and can create a legal exposure.

### Accruing All Leave Types The Same Way

Treating sick, holiday, vacation, and statutory leave identically ignores the conditional and vesting differences. Sick leave that does not vest should not be accrued for unused balances, while vesting vacation must be.

### Using A Stale Or Average Pay Rate

Leave earned now will likely be paid at a future, higher rate. Using last year's rate or a blended average understates the liability when the framework requires the current or expected payout rate.

### Gross Accrual With No Forfeiture Or Usage Estimate

Recording every unused hour at full value, with no allowance for usage, forfeiture, or caps, overstates the liability for non-vesting leave and produces volatile year-end swings.

### Failing To Reconcile Leave Balances

When the accrual is built in a spreadsheet disconnected from the time and attendance system, errors accumulate silently. Breaks between recorded leave and system balances signal data integrity problems.

### Double Counting Leave Taken But Unpaid

Leave that employees have already taken but that has not yet been processed through payroll can be counted both in the leave accrual and in the accrued payroll. Ensure each hour is recognized once.

## Self-Check

- Has it been determined, for each leave type, whether the obligation vests and must be accrued, considering both policy and overriding employment law in the jurisdiction?
- Are vacation and PTO, sick leave, holiday, sabbatical, comp time, and statutory leave separated by their accrual behavior?
- Is the accrual valued at the appropriate rate (current, expected payout, or earned rate) per the applicable framework, including required loadings?
- Does the estimate reflect realistic usage, carryover, forfeiture, and termination payout patterns rather than a gross theoretical maximum?
- Are leave balances reconciled between the time and attendance or HR system and the accrual calculation, with negative balances and advances handled?
- Have policy changes, rollover caps, buy-back programs, and mandatory shutdowns been accounted for in the correct period?
- Is each hour of leave recognized only once, with no double counting between the leave accrual and accrued payroll?
- Has the final policy interpretation, legal entitlements, and tax treatment been confirmed with a qualified accountant and, where employment law is involved, legal counsel for the relevant jurisdiction?
