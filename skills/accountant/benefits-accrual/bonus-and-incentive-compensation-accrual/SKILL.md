# Bonus And Incentive Compensation Accrual

Bonuses, profit-sharing, and incentive compensation are variable pay tied to performance, profitability, or discretionary decision. Unlike base salary, the amount is often uncertain until late in the period, and the recognition question is whether and how much to accrue before the final number is known. Agents tend to wait until the bonus is approved or paid, but accrual accounting requires the cost to be recognized when the obligation becomes probable and reasonably estimable, which can be well before the formal payout. The judgment problem is deciding when the obligation exists, how to estimate it, over what service period to spread it, and when to reverse it if the payout never comes.

The harm this skill prevents is understated compensation cost from delaying bonus accruals until payment, misstated expense from wrong service-period attribution, and stale accruals left on the books when a bonus is cancelled or revised. The judgment problem is probabilistic: recognize enough, early enough, without recognizing obligations that are not yet real.

This is accounting guidance, not legal or tax advice. Bonus plan terms, tax treatment of bonuses and gross-ups, and the recognition thresholds for probable and estimable vary by jurisdiction, plan document, and applicable framework. Route definitive plan interpretation, tax, and compliance questions to a qualified compensation specialist or tax advisor. The agent's freedom is to structure the accrual, estimation, and reversal accounting correctly and to flag where specialist input is required; it is not to assert recognition thresholds or tax treatments as universal facts.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Discretionary From Contractual Plans

The recognition treatment turns on whether the bonus is discretionary or contractual, formula-driven. A discretionary bonus is recognized when the employer communicates the intent to pay; a contractual or formula-driven bonus is recognized as the performance condition is met and the obligation becomes probable.

Plan classification:

- discretionary: no formula, no prior commitment; the employer decides at or near year-end; recognize when communicated;
- contractual or formula-driven: defined formula or plan document; recognize as the underlying performance is achieved and probable;
- target-based: tied to measurable company or individual targets; recognize as the target becomes probable of achievement;
- retention or sign-on: tied to continued service; recognize over the requisite service period.

Confirm the plan type from the document, because the same employer can run several types simultaneously.

### Recognize When The Obligation Is Probable And Estimable

For contractual and target-based bonuses, the accrual begins when the obligation is probable and the amount is reasonably estimable. Waiting for board approval or payment understates the period's compensation cost.

Recognition criteria:

- assess whether the performance condition is probable of achievement at each reporting date;
- estimate the payout using the plan formula and the latest performance data;
- recognize the estimated amount over the relevant service period;
- update the estimate each period as performance and probability change;
- if the condition becomes improbable, reverse previously recognized cost.

### Spread Cost Over The Relevant Service Period

Bonus cost is compensation for service, so it should be recognized over the period the employee renders the service that earns the bonus, not concentrated at the payout date. The service period depends on the plan's design.

Service period determination:

- for an annual performance bonus, the service period is generally the performance year, even if payout occurs after year-end;
- for a multi-year incentive, spread the cost over the multi-year performance or service period;
- for a retention bonus, spread over the retention period;
- for a sign-on bonus with a clawback period, spread over the clawback service period;
- do not front-load the entire bonus into the payout period.

### Estimate Reliably Or Disclose The Limitation

When the amount cannot be estimated reliably, the framework may permit or require deferring recognition until estimation becomes possible. Forcing a number when the inputs are too uncertain produces unreliable financial statements.

Estimation handling:

- use the plan formula and the best available performance data;
- where the formula has ranges or discretion, estimate using the most likely or expected amount;
- where estimation is not reliable, document the limitation and defer recognition;
- reassess at each reporting date as new information arrives;
- disclose the nature of the uncertainty where material.

### Handle Gross-Ups And Tax Effects

Some bonuses are grossed-up so the employee receives a stated net amount after tax. The gross-up increases the total compensation cost and the employer's payroll tax burden, and it must be accrued alongside the bonus.

Gross-up handling:

- compute the gross bonus and the additional gross-up needed to cover the employee's tax;
- accrue the total, bonus plus gross-up, as compensation cost;
- accrue the employer payroll tax on both the bonus and the gross-up;
- withhold and remit on the grossed-up amount at payout;
- watch for supplemental tax rate rules that apply to bonus payments.

### Set A Clear Accrual Cutoff At Period-End

At each period-end, decide what bonus has been earned through the cutoff and accrue it. The cutoff should reflect performance through the period-end date and the service rendered through that date, not the full-year projection unless the plan accelerates recognition.

Cutoff principles:

- accrue the portion of the annual bonus earned by the service and performance through period-end;
- for year-end, accrue the full estimated annual bonus if performance is probable, even though the formal calculation follows;
- reverse the accrual in the following period or net it against the actual payout;
- document the cutoff date, the estimate, and the basis;
- reconcile the accrual to the actual payout and true up the difference.

### Reverse Accruals When Payouts Do Not Occur

If a bonus is cancelled, reduced, or becomes improbable, the previously recognized accrual must be reversed. Leaving a stale accrual on the books overstates the liability and the cumulative compensation cost.

Reversal triggers:

- the performance condition becomes improbable;
- the plan is cancelled or amended downward;
- the employee terminates before the service condition is met;
- the discretionary bonus is not communicated;
- the estimated amount is revised downward.

Reverse promptly and document the reason, the amount, and the period of reversal.

## Common Traps

### Waiting Until Payment To Accrue

Recording the bonus only when it is paid shifts compensation cost into the next period and understates the closing period's labor cost. Accrue when probable and estimable, not when funded.

### Treating A Formula Bonus As Discretionary

A formula-driven or contractual bonus is not discretionary. Deferring recognition until communication understates the period's expense and ignores the obligation that builds as performance accrues.

### Concentrating Cost At Payout

Even when accrued, bonus cost is sometimes dropped into the payout period rather than spread over the service period. This distorts period-to-period margins and the matching of cost to service.

### Forcing A Number When Estimation Is Unreliable

When the performance inputs are too uncertain, forcing an estimate produces unreliable statements. Document the limitation and defer recognition until estimation becomes reliable.

### Missing The Gross-Up

A net-of-tax bonus promise requires a gross-up that increases both the bonus and the payroll tax. Accruing only the net amount understates compensation cost and the tax liability.

### Leaving Stale Accruals When Payouts Change

If a bonus is cancelled, reduced, or becomes improbable, the accrual must be reversed. Leaving it in place overstates the liability and cumulative expense.

### Ignoring Reversal When The Employee Terminates

A bonus tied to continued service that is forfeited on termination must be reversed for the unvested portion. Failing to reverse overstates compensation cost.

## Self-Check

- Is each bonus classified as discretionary, contractual, target-based, or retention, with recognition treatment matched to the type?
- Are contractual and target-based bonuses accrued when the obligation is probable and the amount is reasonably estimable?
- Is bonus cost spread over the relevant service period rather than concentrated at the payout date?
- Where estimation is unreliable, is the limitation documented and recognition deferred until estimation becomes reliable?
- Are gross-ups computed and accrued alongside the bonus, including the employer payroll tax on both?
- Is a clear period-end cutoff applied, accruing the portion earned by service and performance through the cutoff date?
- Is the accrual reversed or netted against the actual payout, with the difference trued up?
- Are accruals reversed promptly when a bonus is cancelled, reduced, becomes improbable, or is forfeited on termination?
- Is the accrual reconciled to the actual payout and the variance explained each cycle?
- Are plan-specific terms, tax treatments, and recognition thresholds confirmed with a qualified compensation specialist or tax advisor rather than asserted as universal facts?
