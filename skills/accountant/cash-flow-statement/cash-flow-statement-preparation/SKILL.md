# Cash Flow Statement Preparation

The statement of cash flows reports the cash inflows and outflows of an entity during a period, classified into operating, investing, and financing activities. It is the statement that connects the income statement (accrual-based performance) to the balance sheet (ending cash), and it is the one users scrutinize when they distrust accruals. Preparing it requires classifying every cash flow into the correct activity, choosing between the direct and indirect methods for operating activities, and reconciling the net change to the actual change in the cash balance. Agents often misclassify items (interest paid as financing instead of operating, dividends received as investing instead of operating), apply the indirect method mechanically without understanding the working-capital adjustments, or fail to reconcile to the balance sheet cash movement.

Use this skill before drafting a cash flow statement, classifying cash flows, choosing between direct and indirect methods, or reconciling the statement. The goal is to prevent the agent from misclassifying activities, omitting non-cash adjustments, or producing a statement that does not tie to the cash balance.

Cash flow statement rules are broadly consistent between US GAAP (ASC 230) and IFRS (IAS 7), with key differences in interest and dividends classification and the treatment of bank overdrafts. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For audited or filed statements, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Classify Cash Flows Into Operating, Investing, And Financing Activities

Operating activities are the principal revenue-producing activities and other activities not classified as investing or financing — cash receipts from customers, cash payments to suppliers and employees, and other operating cash flows. Investing activities are the acquisition and disposal of long-term assets and other investments not included in cash equivalents — purchases and sales of PP&E, intangibles, and investments in other entities. Financing activities are activities that result in changes in the size and composition of contributed equity and borrowings — issuing shares, repurchasing shares, borrowing, and repaying debt.

The classification is based on the nature of the activity, not the form of the transaction. When an item could fit multiple categories, classify based on the predominant economic characteristic. Apply the classification consistently across periods.

### Classify Interest And Dividends According To The Framework

Under IFRS (IAS 7), interest paid and interest received, and dividends paid and dividends received, may be classified consistently into operating, investing, or financing, provided the classification is disclosed and applied consistently. A common choice is: interest paid as financing (or operating), interest received as investing (or operating), dividends received as investing (or operating), dividends paid as financing.

Under US GAAP (ASC 230), the classification is more prescriptive: interest paid and interest received, and dividends received, are operating activities; dividends paid are financing activities. Confirm which framework applies, because the classification differs and affects the operating cash flow total.

### Choose Between The Direct And Indirect Method For Operating Activities

The direct method reports major classes of gross cash receipts and payments (cash received from customers, cash paid to suppliers, cash paid to employees), resulting in net cash from operating activities. The indirect method starts with net income and adjusts for non-cash items (depreciation, amortization, deferred tax, unrealized gains and losses) and changes in working capital (receivables, inventory, payables), arriving at net cash from operating activities.

Under both IFRS and US GAAP, entities are encouraged to use the direct method but the indirect method is permitted and is far more common. Under US GAAP, if the direct method is used, a reconciliation of net income to operating cash flow (essentially the indirect method) must also be presented. Under IFRS, the reconciliation is encouraged but not required for the direct method.

### Apply The Indirect Method Reconciliation Correctly

The indirect method reconciliation starts with net income (or profit before tax, then adjusts for tax separately). Add back non-cash expenses (depreciation, amortization, impairment losses, share-based payment expense, deferred tax expense). Subtract non-cash income (gains on disposal, unrealized gains, deferred tax income). Adjust for investing and financing items that were included in net income but are not operating (gains and losses on disposal of non-current assets — subtract gains, add losses, because the disposal proceeds are in investing). Adjust for changes in working capital: increases in current assets (receivables, inventory, prepayments) decrease cash; decreases increase cash; increases in current liabilities (payables, accruals) increase cash; decreases decrease cash.

The working-capital adjustments must use the change in the operating working-capital balances, not the ending balance. If a receivable increased, cash was not collected, so subtract the increase. Verify each adjustment against the comparative balance sheets.

### Reconcile The Net Change In Cash To The Balance Sheet Movement

The sum of net cash from operating, investing, and financing activities, plus the effect of exchange rate changes on cash (for foreign currency cash balances), equals the net change in cash and cash equivalents for the period. This net change, plus the opening cash balance, equals the closing cash balance, which must tie to the balance sheet.

A cash flow statement that does not reconcile to the balance sheet cash movement has an error. This reconciliation is the final check before finalizing.

### Define Cash And Cash Equivalents Consistently

Cash and cash equivalents comprise cash on hand and demand deposits, plus short-term highly liquid investments that are readily convertible to known amounts of cash and subject to an insignificant risk of changes in value (generally investments with original maturities of three months or less). Bank overdrafts integral to cash management may be included (IFRS). Apply the definition consistently across periods and disclose the components of cash and cash equivalents.

Do not include short-term investments with maturities longer than three months, or investments with material value risk, in cash equivalents. These are investing activities.

### Report Cash Flows Gross Unless The Net Basis Is Permitted

Cash flows are reported on a gross basis (gross cash receipts and payments), not netted. Exceptions where the net basis is permitted include: cash flows on behalf of customers (e.g., a bank accepting deposits); items with quick turnover, large amounts, and short maturities (credit card receipts and payments, purchase and sale of investments); and cash flows for an intermediary handling customer cash. Under IFRS, the net basis is permitted for these; under US GAAP, certain items (such as debt prepayments and securitizations) may be reported net with disclosure.

Netting cash flows obscures the entity's gross cash activity and is generally not permitted. When in doubt, report gross.

### Disclose Material Non-Cash Transactions Separately

Material investing and financing transactions that do not involve cash (acquiring an asset by assuming a liability, converting debt to equity, acquiring an asset via a finance lease) are excluded from the cash flow statement but disclosed in the notes. This ensures users see the entity's investing and financing activity even when no cash changed hands.

## Common Traps

### Misclassifying Interest Or Dividends Across Frameworks

Under US GAAP, interest paid/received and dividends received are operating; dividends paid are financing. Under IFRS, the classification is an election but must be consistent. Applying the wrong framework's classification misstates operating cash flow.

### Omitting Non-Cash Adjustments In The Indirect Method

Depreciation, amortization, share-based payment, deferred tax, and impairment must be added back. Omitting them overstates or understates operating cash flow. Identify all non-cash items in the income statement.

### Using Ending Working-Capital Balances Instead Of Changes

The indirect method uses the change in working capital, not the ending balance. Using ending balances produces an incorrect adjustment. Verify each change against comparative balance sheets.

### Netting Cash Flows That Should Be Gross

Reporting cash receipts and payments net (e.g., netting borrowings and repayments) obscures activity. Report gross unless the net basis is specifically permitted.

### Including Non-Three-Month Investments In Cash Equivalents

Investments with maturities longer than three months, or with value risk, are not cash equivalents. Including them overstates cash and misclassifies investing activity.

### Failing To Reconcile To The Balance Sheet Cash

The net change in cash plus opening cash must equal the closing balance sheet cash. A statement that does not reconcile has an error. Always perform this final check.

### Omitting Non-Cash Transaction Disclosure

Material non-cash investing and financing transactions must be disclosed in the notes. Omitting them leaves users unaware of significant activity.

## Self-Check

- Are cash flows classified into operating, investing, and financing based on the nature of the activity, applied consistently?
- Are interest and dividends classified according to the applicable framework (US GAAP prescriptive: interest operating, dividends paid financing; IFRS elected but consistent)?
- Is the direct or indirect method chosen for operating activities, with the reconciliation presented where required (US GAAP direct method requires reconciliation)?
- Does the indirect method reconciliation add back non-cash expenses (depreciation, amortization, impairment, share-based payment, deferred tax) and adjust for gains/losses on disposal and changes in working capital?
- Does the net change in cash (operating plus investing plus financing plus FX effect) reconcile to the movement in the balance sheet cash balance?
- Is the cash and cash equivalents definition (three-month maturity, insignificant value risk) applied consistently, with components disclosed?
- Are cash flows reported gross, with netting only where specifically permitted (customer cash, quick-turnover items)?
- Are material non-cash investing and financing transactions disclosed in the notes, and has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed the statement?
