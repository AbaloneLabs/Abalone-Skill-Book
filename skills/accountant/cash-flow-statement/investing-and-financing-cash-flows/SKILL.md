# Investing And Financing Cash Flows

The investing and financing sections of the cash flow statement report the cash flows from acquiring and disposing of long-term assets (investing) and from changes in equity and borrowings (financing). While operating cash flows are the focus of most analysis, investing and financing reveal the entity's capital allocation, growth investment, and funding strategy. The classification judgments here are consequential: a lease payment, a share buyback, a tax refund on an asset disposal, an intercompany loan — each has specific classification rules. Agents often misclassify items (capital expenditures versus operating maintenance, principal versus interest on lease payments), net transactions that should be gross, or omit the cash element of acquisitions.

Use this skill before classifying investing or financing cash flows, preparing these sections, handling lease and acquisition cash flows, or resolving ambiguous classification questions. The goal is to prevent the agent from misclassifying activities, netting where gross is required, or omitting acquisition-related cash flows.

Classification rules follow ASC 230 (US GAAP) and IAS 7 (IFRS), with differences in interest, dividends, and bank overdrafts. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For complex transactions, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Classify Investing Activities As Acquisition And Disposal Of Long-Term Assets And Investments

Investing activities include: cash payments to acquire and cash receipts from the sale of property, plant, and equipment, intangibles, and other long-term assets; cash payments to acquire and receipts from the sale of equity or debt instruments of other entities (unless held for trading or classified as cash equivalents); advances and loans made to and collected from other parties; and cash received from insurance settlements for property losses.

The test is whether the cash flow relates to acquiring or disposing of productive assets or investments intended for long-term use. Capital expenditures (purchasing PP&E) are investing; maintenance and repair expenses are operating. Distinguish capital projects (investing) from routine maintenance (operating) based on whether the expenditure extends the asset's life or improves it versus maintaining it.

### Classify Financing Activities As Changes In Equity And Borrowings

Financing activities include: cash proceeds from issuing shares or other equity instruments; cash payments to owners to acquire or redeem the entity's shares (treasury share repurchases, share buybacks); cash proceeds from issuing bonds, loans, notes, and other borrowings; cash repayments of amounts borrowed; and cash payments by a lessee for the principal portion of lease liabilities (under IFRS 16 and ASC 842).

The test is whether the cash flow changes the size or composition of contributed equity or interest-bearing borrowings. Dividends paid are financing under US GAAP; under IFRS, they may be financing or operating (with consistent classification). Interest paid is operating under US GAAP; under IFRS, it may be operating or financing.

### Report Capital Expenditure And Asset Disposal Cash Flows Gross

Cash paid for capital expenditures and cash received from asset disposals are reported separately and gross, not netted. An entity that spent 10 million on new equipment and received 2 million from selling old equipment reports 10 million outflow and 2 million inflow, not an 8 million net outflow. Netting obscures the entity's investment and divestment activity.

### Separate The Principal And Interest Components Of Lease Payments

Under IFRS 16 and ASC 842, lease payments are split: the principal repayment reduces the lease liability (financing cash outflow) and the interest portion is classified as operating (US GAAP) or as an elected category (IFRS). Do not report the entire lease payment as a single cash flow — the principal and interest have different classifications.

For short-term leases, variable lease payments, and low-value asset leases (IFRS), the payments are operating cash flows because they are expensed as incurred rather than recognized as a lease liability.

### Report Borrowings And Repayments Gross

Cash proceeds from new borrowings and cash repayments of borrowings are reported separately and gross. An entity that drew 50 million on a credit facility and repaid 30 million reports a 50 million inflow and a 30 million outflow, not a 20 million net. Netting obscures the entity's refinancing activity.

The net basis is permitted only for specific items (debt prepayments with rapid turnover, certain securitizations) with disclosure. When in doubt, report gross.

### Report The Cash Element Of Acquisitions Separately

In a business acquisition, the cash paid (including cash acquired) is an investing outflow. The acquiree's cash and cash equivalents are included in the consideration and also added to the entity's cash — disclose the gross cash paid and the cash acquired. The acquiree's operating, investing, and financing cash flows are consolidated from the acquisition date, which affects period-over-period comparability.

Disclose the cash flow effect of acquisitions and disposals separately so users can see the impact on each category and adjust for comparability.

### Classify Tax Cash Flows As Operating Unless Specifically Identifiable With Investing Or Financing

Cash flows from taxes are generally classified as operating, unless the tax can be specifically identified with a financing or investing transaction (in which case it is classified accordingly). In practice, most tax payments are operating. A tax refund on an asset disposal may be investing; tax on a share issuance may be financing. Apply the specific-identification test and default to operating when the tax cannot be specifically identified.

### Classify Dividends And Interest According To The Framework

Under US GAAP: interest paid is operating, interest received is operating, dividends received are operating, dividends paid are financing. Under IFRS: interest paid may be operating or financing; interest received may be operating or investing; dividends received may be operating or investing; dividends paid may be operating or financing. The IFRS election must be consistent and disclosed.

Confirm which framework applies. The classification of interest and dividends is a common source of cross-framework differences.

## Common Traps

### Netting Capital Expenditure And Asset Disposals

Capital expenditures and disposal proceeds must be reported gross. Netting them obscures the entity's investment and divestment activity.

### Reporting The Entire Lease Payment As One Cash Flow

Lease payments are split into principal (financing) and interest (operating or elected). Reporting the full payment as operating or as financing misclassifies one component.

### Netting Borrowings And Repayments

New borrowings and repayments must be reported gross. Netting obscures refinancing activity and the entity's reliance on rolling debt.

### Misclassifying Maintenance As Capital Expenditure

Routine maintenance is operating; capital improvements are investing. Misclassifying maintenance as investing overstates investing outflow and understates operating outflow (flattering operating cash flow).

### Omitting The Cash Element Of Acquisitions

The cash paid in an acquisition (including cash acquired) must be reported as an investing outflow. Omitting it understates investing activity and distorts comparability.

### Defaulting All Tax To Operating Without Specific Identification

Most tax is operating, but tax specifically identifiable with investing or financing (a disposal tax refund, a share issuance tax) should be classified accordingly. Defaulting everything to operating is acceptable but may miss specific-identification opportunities.

### Applying US GAAP Interest And Dividend Classification Under IFRS

The classification of interest and dividends differs between frameworks. Applying the US GAAP classification (interest operating, dividends paid financing) under IFRS without considering the entity's election is incorrect.

## Self-Check

- Are investing activities limited to acquisition and disposal of long-term assets and investments, with capital expenditures distinguished from operating maintenance?
- Are financing activities limited to changes in equity and borrowings, including share issuance, repurchases, borrowings, and repayments?
- Are capital expenditure and asset disposal cash flows reported gross, not netted?
- Are lease payments split into principal (financing) and interest (operating or elected), with short-term and variable lease payments as operating?
- Are borrowings and repayments reported gross, with netting only where specifically permitted and disclosed?
- Is the cash element of acquisitions (including cash acquired) reported as an investing outflow, with the impact on comparability disclosed?
- Are tax cash flows classified as operating unless specifically identifiable with investing or financing?
- Are interest and dividends classified according to the applicable framework (US GAAP prescriptive, IFRS elected and consistent), and has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed the classification?
