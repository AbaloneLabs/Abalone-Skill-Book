# Operating Cash Flow: Indirect Method

The indirect method is the most common approach to reporting cash flow from operating activities. It starts with net income and adjusts for non-cash items and changes in working capital to arrive at the cash generated by operations. While it appears mechanical, the indirect method requires identifying every non-cash item in the income statement, correctly analyzing every working-capital change, and handling unusual items (deferred tax, share-based payment, pension remeasurements, unrealized gains and losses, acquisitions and disposals affecting working capital). Agents often omit non-cash adjustments, misread working-capital changes, or fail to adjust for items that entered working capital through non-operating transactions (acquisitions, foreign currency translation, reclassifications).

Use this skill before preparing the indirect method operating section, troubleshooting a reconciliation that does not balance, or handling unusual non-cash adjustments. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying the indirect method as a formula without identifying the specific items that require adjustment.

The indirect method follows ASC 230 (US GAAP) and IAS 7 (IFRS). This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For complex reconciliations, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Start With Net Income And Add Back All Non-Cash Expenses

The indirect method begins with net income (or profit before tax, with tax cash flows shown separately). Add back all non-cash expenses recognized in the income statement: depreciation and amortization of non-current assets; impairment losses on receivables, inventory, and non-current assets; share-based payment expense (the non-cash compensation cost); deferred tax expense (the portion of tax that did not use cash); provisions charged to expense (pension expense net of contributions, warranty provisions, restructuring provisions); and unrealized foreign exchange losses.

Subtract non-cash income: gains on disposal of non-current assets (the gain is non-cash; the disposal proceeds are in investing); unrealized gains (on financial instruments, foreign exchange); deferred tax income; and reversals of provisions or impairments.

The test is whether the item affected net income but did not use or generate cash. Every such item requires an adjustment. Review the income statement line by line to identify non-cash items.

### Adjust For Investing And Financing Items Included In Net Income

Some items in net income belong to investing or financing activities, so they must be removed from the operating section. Gains and losses on disposal of non-current assets are removed (subtract gains, add losses) because the cash proceeds are classified as investing. Investment income accounted for under the equity method (the share of associate's profit) is non-cash and must be removed; only dividends received from the associate are operating (or investing, depending on classification).

Unrealized gains and losses on available-for-sale or FVOCI investments are non-cash and are removed (they are in OCI, not net income, but verify the treatment). Interest income and expense may require separate presentation depending on the framework's classification.

### Analyze Working-Capital Changes Using The Change In Balances

Adjust for the change in each working-capital balance between the opening and closing balance sheet. Increases in operating current assets (accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses) decrease operating cash flow (cash was tied up). Decreases increase operating cash flow (cash was freed). Increases in operating current liabilities (accounts payable, accrued expenses, deferred revenue) increase operating cash flow (cash was retained). Decreases decrease operating cash flow (cash was used to pay down liabilities).

The adjustment is the change (closing minus opening), not the ending balance. Verify each change against the comparative balance sheets. If a balance moved for a non-operating reason (acquisition, disposal, foreign currency translation, reclassification), adjust the change to isolate the operating portion.

### Adjust Working-Capital Changes For Non-Operating Movements

Working-capital balances can change for reasons other than operating activity. An acquisition brings in the acquiree's receivables and payables — this increase is not operating cash flow and must be removed from the working-capital adjustment. A foreign currency translation adjusts receivable and payable balances without any cash flow — remove the FX effect. A reclassification (moving a receivable to a long-term loan) changes balances without cash — adjust for it.

Failing to adjust for these non-operating movements is a common source of reconciliation errors. Analyze the rollforward of each working-capital balance: opening balance plus operating activity plus non-operating movements equals closing balance. Use the operating activity portion for the cash flow adjustment.

### Handle Deferred Tax As A Non-Cash Adjustment

The deferred tax expense (or income) in the income statement is non-cash — it represents tax that will be paid or recovered in future periods. Add back deferred tax expense (or subtract deferred tax income) in the reconciliation. The current tax paid (or received) is the cash element, shown separately as taxes paid in operating activities (or as a separate line).

Verify the deferred tax adjustment against the deferred tax liability/asset rollforward. The change in the deferred tax balance should equal the deferred tax expense (adjusted for items recognized outside profit or loss, such as OCI or business combinations).

### Handle Share-Based Payment Expense As A Non-Cash Addition

Share-based payment expense (stock options, RSUs) recognized in the income statement is non-cash. Add it back in the reconciliation. The tax benefit from share-based payment (if any) is handled separately — the excess tax benefit (over the book expense) may be classified as financing under US GAAP.

### Handle Provisions And Pension Remeasurements

Provisions charged to expense (warranty, restructuring, environmental) are non-cash when recognized; the cash outflow occurs when the provision is settled. Add back the provision charge and subtract the cash paid to settle provisions. For pensions, the service cost is an operating expense (already in net income); the remeasurement (actuarial gains and losses) is non-cash and in OCI (under IAS 19) — it does not affect net income, so no adjustment is needed in the operating section for the remeasurement. The employer contribution is a cash outflow, shown as a reduction of operating cash flow.

### Reconcile To Net Cash From Operating Activities

After all adjustments (non-cash items, investing/financing items removed, working-capital changes, tax), the result is net cash from operating activities. This figure, plus net cash from investing and financing, plus the FX effect on cash, equals the net change in cash. Reconcile to the balance sheet cash movement as the final check.

## Common Traps

### Omitting A Non-Cash Item From The Add-Back

Every non-cash item in the income statement requires an adjustment. Missing depreciation, share-based payment, or deferred tax understates or overstates operating cash flow. Review the income statement line by line.

### Using Ending Balances Instead Of Changes For Working Capital

The adjustment uses the change (closing minus opening), not the ending balance. Using ending balances produces a grossly incorrect figure.

### Failing To Adjust Working-Capital Changes For Acquisitions Or FX

An acquisition inflates receivables and payables without operating cash flow. FX translation changes balances without cash. Failing to remove these effects distorts the working-capital adjustment.

### Not Removing The Equity-Method Share Of Associate Profit

The share of an associate's profit is non-cash income. Leaving it in overstates operating cash flow. Remove it; only dividends received are cash.

### Treating Deferred Tax Income As An Addition

Deferred tax income is subtracted (it reduced the tax expense but did not generate cash). Adding it back understates operating cash flow.

### Double-Counting Provision Charges And Settlements

The provision charge is added back; the cash settlement is shown as a reduction. Doing only one (adding back the charge but not subtracting settlements, or vice versa) misstates the figure.

### Not Reconciling To The Balance Sheet Cash

The operating cash flow plus investing plus financing must reconcile to the cash movement. A reconciliation that does not balance has an error in a non-cash adjustment or working-capital change.

## Self-Check

- Does the reconciliation start with net income and add back all non-cash expenses (depreciation, amortization, impairment, share-based payment, deferred tax, provisions)?
- Are non-cash income items (gains on disposal, unrealized gains, deferred tax income, provision reversals) subtracted?
- Are investing and financing items included in net income (gains/losses on disposal, equity-method share of profit) removed from the operating section?
- Are working-capital adjustments based on the change in balances (closing minus opening), verified against comparative balance sheets?
- Are working-capital changes adjusted for non-operating movements (acquisitions, FX translation, reclassifications) to isolate the operating portion?
- Is deferred tax expense added back (or income subtracted), reconciled to the deferred tax balance rollforward?
- Are share-based payment expense, provision charges and settlements, and pension items handled correctly?
- Does the net cash from operating activities reconcile (with investing and financing) to the balance sheet cash movement, and has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed the reconciliation?
