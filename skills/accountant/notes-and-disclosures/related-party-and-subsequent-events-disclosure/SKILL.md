# Related Party And Subsequent Events Disclosure

Related party transactions and subsequent events are two disclosure areas that users and auditors scrutinize heavily because they reveal information outside the normal course of business and after the reporting date. Related party disclosures expose transactions with owners, management, affiliates, and other controlled entities that may not be at arm's length. Subsequent events disclose developments after the reporting date that affect the financial statements (adjusting events) or require disclosure (non-adjusting events). Agents often fail to identify all related parties, omit key management compensation detail, or misclassify subsequent events as adjusting when they are non-adjusting (or vice versa).

Use this skill before drafting related party disclosures, identifying related parties, classifying subsequent events, or deciding whether a post-reporting-date development requires adjustment or disclosure. The goal is to prevent the agent from missing related parties, providing incomplete compensation disclosure, or misclassifying subsequent events.

Related party rules are in ASC 850 (US GAAP) and IAS 24 (IFRS); subsequent events rules are in ASC 855 (US GAAP) and IAS 10 (IFRS). This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For filed financial statements, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Identify All Categories Of Related Parties

Related parties include: the entity's parent and ultimate controlling party; subsidiaries and fellow subsidiaries; associates and joint ventures; key management personnel (directors and senior officers) and their close family members; entities controlled, jointly controlled, or significantly influenced by key management personnel or their close family members; and post-employment benefit plans for the entity's employees. Under IFRS, a related party also includes parties that control or jointly control the entity.

Identify all categories before drafting disclosure. A common gap is failing to identify the ultimate controlling party, or omitting transactions with close family members of key management. Maintain a related party register and review it annually.

### Disclose Related Party Transactions And Balances

Disclose material related party transactions and outstanding balances: the nature of the relationship; the nature and amount of each material category of transactions; the amount of outstanding balances (receivables, payables, loans) and commitments and guarantees; provisions for doubtful related party receivables; and the expense recognized for bad debts. For material transactions, disclose enough detail for users to understand the effect on the financial statements.

Under IFRS (IAS 24), disclosure is required for transactions with related parties regardless of whether a price was charged. Under US GAAP (ASC 850), disclose material related party transactions. The level of detail should allow users to assess whether transactions were at arm's length and their effect on the financial statements.

### Disclose Key Management Personnel Compensation In Total By Category

Under IFRS (IAS 24), disclose key management personnel compensation in total by category: short-term employee benefits; post-employment benefits; other long-term benefits; termination benefits; and share-based payment. The disclosure is by category in total, not by individual. Under US GAAP, disclose related party transactions including compensation arrangements with key management, though the categorical breakdown is less prescriptive than IFRS.

Key management compensation is a high-interest disclosure because it reveals the cost of management and potential conflicts of interest. Disclose the total by category and ensure it reconciles to the amounts recognized in the financial statements.

### Distinguish Adjusting From Non-Adjusting Subsequent Events

Subsequent events (events after the reporting date) are classified as adjusting or non-adjusting. Adjusting events provide evidence of conditions that existed at the reporting date (a customer bankruptcy filed after year-end that confirms a receivable was impaired at year-end; a court judgment after year-end that confirms a provision amount; a fire after year-end that destroys inventory, but only if conditions causing the fire existed at year-end — generally not adjusting). Adjust the financial statements for adjusting events.

Non-adjusting events are indicative of conditions that arose after the reporting date (a major business combination announced after year-end; a fire or natural disaster after year-end; a major decline in market value after year-end). Do not adjust the financial statements, but disclose material non-adjusting events (nature and estimated financial effect) so users can assess the post-reporting-date developments.

### Adjust For Conditions Existing At The Reporting Date

The test for an adjusting event is whether it provides evidence of conditions that existed at the reporting date. If yes, adjust. Examples: a receivable confirmed as uncollectible by a customer's post-year-end bankruptcy (the customer's financial difficulty existed at year-end); a litigation judgment after year-end that confirms the obligation existed at year-end; an inventory write-down confirmed by post-year-end net realizable value evidence; a fraud discovered after year-end that occurred before year-end.

Do not adjust for conditions that arose after the reporting date. A business combination signed after year-end, a fire after year-end, or a share price decline after year-end are non-adjusting.

### Disclose Material Non-Adjusting Events With Estimated Financial Effect

For material non-adjusting events, disclose the nature of the event and an estimate of its financial effect (or a statement that the effect cannot be estimated). The disclosure helps users understand post-reporting-date developments that could affect their assessment of the entity. Examples: a major acquisition announced after year-end; a major restructuring plan announced after year-end; a significant decline in market value of investments after year-end; a natural disaster affecting operations after year-end.

If the event is immaterial, no disclosure is required. Apply materiality judgment — a material non-adjusting event is one whose effect could influence users' decisions.

### Disclose Dividends Declared After The Reporting Date

Dividends declared after the reporting date but before the financial statements are authorized for issue are not recognized as a liability at the reporting date (because no obligation exists at that date) but are disclosed. Under IFRS (IAS 10), disclose the amount per share. Under US GAAP (ASC 855), subsequent-event disclosure of dividends declared after the reporting date is required.

### Define The Authorization Date And The Subsequent Events Period

The subsequent events period extends from the reporting date to the date the financial statements are authorized for issue. Disclose the authorization date and who gave the authorization (the board of directors, the owners). Events after the authorization date are not subsequent events and are not disclosed in those financial statements.

Under US GAAP (ASC 855), the entity evaluates events through the date the financial statements are issued (a slightly different concept from authorization under IFRS). Confirm the applicable framework's date.

## Common Traps

### Failing To Identify All Related Party Categories

Omitting the ultimate controlling party, close family members, or entities controlled by key management leaves the related party disclosure incomplete. Maintain a comprehensive related party register.

### Omitting Key Management Compensation By Category

Under IFRS, key management compensation must be disclosed by category. Providing only a total, or omitting it, is non-compliant.

### Misclassifying A Subsequent Event As Adjusting When It Is Non-Adjusting

Adjusting for a post-year-end event that reflects conditions arising after the reporting date (a fire, a business combination) overstates or understates the financial statements. Apply the conditions-existing-at-reporting-date test.

### Failing To Adjust For Genuine Adjusting Events

Not adjusting for a customer bankruptcy or litigation judgment that confirms a year-end condition leaves the financial statements misstated. Adjust for evidence of conditions existing at the reporting date.

### Omitting The Estimated Financial Effect Of Non-Adjusting Events

Stating that a non-adjusting event occurred without disclosing the estimated financial effect leaves users unable to assess the impact. Disclose the nature and the estimated effect.

### Not Disclosing Dividends Declared After The Reporting Date

Dividends declared after year-end are not a liability but must be disclosed. Omitting the disclosure leaves users unaware of the distribution.

### Not Disclosing The Authorization Date

The authorization date defines the subsequent events period. Omitting it leaves users unable to determine the period covered.

## Self-Check

- Are all categories of related parties identified (parent, ultimate controlling party, subsidiaries, associates, joint ventures, key management, close family, controlled entities)?
- Are material related party transactions and balances disclosed with nature, amounts, outstanding balances, commitments, and guarantees?
- Is key management personnel compensation disclosed in total by category (short-term, post-employment, other long-term, termination, share-based payment) under IFRS?
- Are subsequent events classified as adjusting (conditions existed at reporting date — adjust) or non-adjusting (conditions arose after — disclose)?
- Are adjusting events (customer bankruptcy, litigation judgment, inventory NRV evidence, pre-year-end fraud) reflected in the financial statements?
- Are material non-adjusting events disclosed with nature and estimated financial effect (or a statement that estimation is not possible)?
- Are dividends declared after the reporting date disclosed (not recognized as a liability)?
- Is the authorization date and authorizing body disclosed, and has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed the related party and subsequent events disclosures?
