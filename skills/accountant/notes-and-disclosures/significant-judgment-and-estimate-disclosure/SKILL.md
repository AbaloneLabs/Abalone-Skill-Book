# Significant Judgment And Estimate Disclosure

Many accounting amounts rely on estimates and judgments that are inherently uncertain: the useful life of an asset, the recoverable amount of a cash-generating unit, the fair value of a level 3 financial instrument, the outcome of litigation, the measurement of a provision, the timing of revenue recognition. Users need to understand these uncertainties to assess the reliability of the reported amounts. The disclosure requirements focus on estimates that have a significant risk of material adjustment within the next financial year (IFRS) or estimates that are particularly sensitive (US GAAP). Agents often omit these disclosures, provide generic statements ("estimates are used"), or fail to identify the specific assumptions that drive the most sensitive amounts.

Use this skill before drafting judgment and estimate disclosures, identifying the sensitive estimates, or applying the material-adjustment threshold. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing generic disclosure that does not help users understand which estimates drive the financial statements and what could change.

Judgment and estimate disclosure rules are in IAS 1 (IFRS, with the specific "significant risk of material adjustment within the next financial year" requirement) and ASC 275 (US GAAP, with the "particularly sensitive" requirement). This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For filed financial statements, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Identify Estimates With A Significant Risk Of Material Adjustment

Under IFRS (IAS 1), disclose information about the assumptions and estimation uncertainties that have a significant risk of resulting in a material adjustment to the carrying amounts of assets and liabilities within the next financial year. Under US GAAP (ASC 275), disclose estimates that are particularly sensitive because the nature of the estimates is significant due to uncertainties and the possible effects on the financial statements.

The focus is on estimates where reasonably possible changes would materially affect the reported amount. Not every estimate qualifies — only those where the uncertainty is high and the amount is material. Identify these by reviewing the significant estimates (impairment, fair value level 3, provisions, revenue variable consideration, deferred tax valuation allowance, pension assumptions) and testing which have both high uncertainty and material carrying amounts.

### Disclose The Nature Of The Estimate And The Key Assumptions

For each qualifying estimate, disclose: the nature of the estimate (what is being estimated and why); the key assumptions and other sources of estimation uncertainty (the inputs that drive the estimate); the carrying amount of the affected assets and liabilities; and an explanation of how the estimate is made. The disclosure should help users understand what drives the number and where the uncertainty lies.

For a goodwill impairment test, disclose the cash-generating unit, the recoverable amount basis (fair value less costs of disposal or value in use), the discount rate, the terminal growth rate, and the headroom. For a litigation provision, disclose the nature of the claim, the basis of the estimate, and the range of possible outcomes (without prejudicing the case). For revenue variable consideration, disclose the constraint methodology.

### Disclose The Sensitivity Of The Estimate To Changes In Assumptions

Where an estimate is highly sensitive to a specific assumption, disclose the sensitivity — how the carrying amount would change if the assumption changed by a reasonably possible amount. For goodwill impairment, disclose the effect of a 1% change in the discount rate or terminal growth rate on the recoverable amount. For fair value level 3, disclose the effect of changes in unobservable inputs.

The sensitivity disclosure helps users assess the margin of safety and the risk of a future write-down or adjustment. Without it, users cannot assess how close the estimate is to a threshold that would trigger a material change.

### Distinguish Significant Judgments From Estimation Uncertainties

Significant judgments (applying accounting policies) and estimation uncertainties (assumptions about the future) are related but distinct. Significant judgments include decisions about whether control exists (consolidation), whether an arrangement contains a lease, whether a performance obligation is distinct, and whether an instrument is equity or liability. Estimation uncertainties include assumptions about future cash flows, discount rates, useful lives, and outcomes of litigation.

Under IFRS, IAS 1 requires disclosure of both: the judgments made in applying accounting policies (separate disclosure) and the key sources of estimation uncertainty. Address both, because users need to understand both the policy-application judgments and the forward-looking estimates.

### Disclose The Range Of Possible Outcomes For Provisions

For provisions measured at the best estimate of the expenditure required to settle the obligation, disclose the range of possible outcomes and the major assumptions. For litigation, disclose the nature of the claim and the estimated range (without disclosing the entity's legal strategy). For environmental provisions, disclose the nature of the obligation, the basis of estimation, and the expected timing. For restructuring, disclose the nature and the major categories of cost.

The range disclosure helps users assess the uncertainty around the provision. A single point estimate without a range obscures the potential variability.

### Disclose Fair Value Hierarchy And Valuation Techniques For Fair Value Items

For items measured at fair value, disclose the fair value hierarchy level (Level 1 quoted prices, Level 2 observable inputs, Level 3 unobservable inputs), the valuation techniques used, and the significant unobservable inputs for Level 3 items. For Level 3 items, also disclose a reconciliation of opening to closing balances (purchases, sales, settlements, transfers, gains and losses in profit or loss, gains and losses in OCI).

The fair value disclosure helps users assess the reliability of the fair value measurement (Level 1 is most reliable; Level 3 is most uncertain) and the sensitivity to unobservable inputs.

### Apply Consistency And Comparability To Judgment Disclosures

Present judgment and estimate disclosures consistently across periods so users can compare. If the key assumptions change (a new discount rate, a revised useful life), disclose the change and the reason. Do not reorganize the disclosures each period without reason, as this impairs comparability.

## Common Traps

### Providing Generic "Estimates Are Used" Disclosure

Stating that estimates are used, without identifying the specific sensitive estimates and their assumptions, is non-compliant and unhelpful. Identify and disclose the specific estimates.

### Omitting The Sensitivity Disclosure For Highly Sensitive Estimates

For goodwill impairment, fair value level 3, and similar estimates, the sensitivity to assumptions is the key disclosure. Omitting it leaves users unable to assess the risk of material adjustment.

### Disclosing Every Estimate Regardless Of Materiality

Not every estimate qualifies for the detailed disclosure — only those with a significant risk of material adjustment (IFRS) or that are particularly sensitive (US GAAP). Disclosing every estimate clutters the notes and obscures the significant ones.

### Failing To Distinguish Judgments From Estimation Uncertainties

Significant judgments (policy application) and estimation uncertainties (forward-looking assumptions) are distinct disclosures. Addressing only one leaves the disclosure incomplete.

### Omitting The Range Of Outcomes For Provisions

A single point estimate for a litigation or environmental provision, without a range, obscures the uncertainty. Disclose the range of possible outcomes.

### Not Disclosing The Fair Value Hierarchy For Fair Value Items

Fair value measurements require hierarchy-level disclosure and valuation technique disclosure. Omitting the hierarchy leaves users unable to assess reliability.

### Changing Disclosure Structure Each Period

Frequent reorganization of judgment disclosures without reason impairs comparability. Present consistently and disclose changes.

## Self-Check

- Are estimates with a significant risk of material adjustment within the next financial year (IFRS) or particularly sensitive estimates (US GAAP) identified and disclosed?
- For each qualifying estimate, are the nature, key assumptions, affected carrying amounts, and estimation approach disclosed?
- Is the sensitivity of highly sensitive estimates (goodwill impairment, fair value level 3) to changes in key assumptions disclosed?
- Are significant judgments (policy application) distinguished from estimation uncertainties (forward-looking assumptions), with both addressed?
- For provisions, is the range of possible outcomes and the major assumptions disclosed?
- For fair value items, are the hierarchy level, valuation techniques, and significant unobservable inputs (Level 3) disclosed, with a Level 3 reconciliation?
- Are judgment and estimate disclosures presented consistently across periods, with changes in assumptions disclosed?
- Has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed the disclosures for compliance with the applicable standard?
