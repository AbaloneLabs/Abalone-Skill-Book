# Accounting Policy Disclosure

Accounting policy disclosures tell users how the entity has applied recognition, measurement, presentation, and disclosure requirements to its transactions. Well-drafted policies focus on the entity's specific judgments and material choices; poorly drafted policies reproduce generic standard summaries that add no entity-specific information. Under IFRS, only material accounting policy information is disclosed (per IAS 1 amendments), shifting the focus from boilerplate to relevance. Agents often copy generic policy templates, disclose every policy regardless of materiality, or fail to disclose the judgments that actually affect the entity's numbers.

Use this skill before drafting or reviewing accounting policy disclosures, selecting which policies to present, or applying the materiality filter. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing boilerplate policy disclosures that do not help users understand the entity's specific accounting choices.

Accounting policy disclosure rules are in ASC 235 (US GAAP) and IAS 1 / IAS 8 (IFRS), with significant IFRS changes emphasizing materiality. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For filed financial statements, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Disclose Only Material Accounting Policy Information

Under IFRS (IAS 1 as amended), an entity discloses material accounting policy information only. Accounting policy information is material when, together with other information, it can reasonably be expected to influence decisions of users. Generic policy information that simply restates the standard is not material and should not be disclosed. Focus on policies that involve significant judgment, choices between alternatives, or that are specific to the entity's business.

Under US GAAP (ASC 235), the description of accounting policies should identify and describe the accounting principles followed and the methods of applying them, with emphasis on policies where alternatives exist or where the policy is particularly significant. While US GAAP does not impose the same explicit materiality filter as the IFRS amendment, the principle of focusing on significant policies applies.

### Focus On Entity-Specific Policy, Not Standard Summary

A useful accounting policy disclosure explains how the entity applies the standard to its specific transactions, not what the standard says in general. For revenue, describe the performance obligations, the timing of revenue recognition, and the significant judgments — not a restatement of the five-step model. For leases, describe the entity's accounting for lease and non-lease components, the incremental borrowing rate approach, and short-term lease elections — not a summary of the lease standard.

Review each policy and ask: does this tell the user something specific about how this entity accounts for its transactions? If the answer is no (the policy is a generic restatement), revise or remove it.

### Disclose Policies For Material And Significant Items

Disclose policies for items that are material to the financial statements or that involve significant judgment. Common material policies include: revenue recognition (the performance obligations, timing, methods); income taxes (the method for deferred tax, treatment of losses); leases (lessee and lessor accounting, elections); financial instruments (classification, measurement, impairment approach); inventories (cost flow method, valuation basis); property, plant, and equipment (measurement basis, depreciation methods); intangibles and goodwill (recognition, amortization, impairment testing); provisions (measurement approach); and employee benefits (defined benefit or contribution).

Do not disclose policies for immaterial items. A policy for biological assets is irrelevant to a software company; a policy for extractive activities is irrelevant to a retailer. Tailor the policy set to the entity.

### Disclose The Measurement Basis And Significant Judgments

Accounting policy disclosures should include the measurement basis used (historical cost, fair value, amortized cost) for each material class of asset and liability, and the significant judgments made in applying the policies. For fair value measurements, describe the valuation techniques and inputs. For impairment tests, describe the key assumptions and the cash-generating unit. For revenue, describe the timing of revenue recognition and the methods for measuring progress.

The judgment disclosures help users assess the reliability of the reported amounts. Under IFRS (IAS 1), disclosure of the assumptions and estimation uncertainties that have a significant risk of resulting in material adjustment is required.

### Present Policies In A Logical Order, Generally Following The Statements

Present accounting policies in a logical order, often following the order of the primary statements (revenue, then expense-related policies, then asset and liability policies, then equity policies). Alternatively, group by topic (operating policies, investing policies, financing policies). Maintain a consistent order across periods unless the structure genuinely improves.

Cross-reference policies to the relevant notes so users can navigate. A well-organized policy section helps users find the information relevant to specific line items.

### Disclose Changes In Accounting Policies, Estimates, And Errors

Disclose any changes in accounting policies (with the nature, reason, and transition method — usually retrospective), changes in accounting estimates (with the nature and effect, applied prospectively), and corrections of errors (with the nature and the retrospective restatement effect). These disclosures are required under both IFRS (IAS 8) and US GAAP, and they are high-interest because they affect comparability.

A change in accounting policy is applied retrospectively (restating comparatives) unless a transition relief or future-date amendment applies. A change in estimate is applied prospectively. An error correction is applied retrospectively. Disclose which treatment applies and the financial effect.

### Review Policies Annually For Continued Relevance

Review the accounting policy disclosures annually. Remove policies that are no longer relevant (a discontinued business line, an immaterial item). Add policies for new transactions (a new revenue stream, a new financial instrument). Update policies when standards change or when the entity's application changes. Stale policies that no longer match the entity's transactions mislead users.

## Common Traps

### Reproducing Generic Standard Summaries As Policy

A policy that restates the standard (the five-step revenue model, the lease definition) adds no entity-specific information. Replace generic summaries with descriptions of how the entity applies the standard to its transactions.

### Disclosing Every Policy Regardless Of Materiality

Under IFRS, only material policy information is disclosed. Disclosing policies for immaterial items clutters the notes and obscures the significant policies. Apply the materiality filter.

### Failing To Disclose Significant Judgments And Estimates

Policies that involve significant judgment (fair value measurement, impairment testing, revenue timing) should disclose the key assumptions. Omitting the judgments leaves users unable to assess the reliability of the amounts.

### Not Disclosing Changes In Policies, Estimates, Or Errors

Changes in accounting policies, estimates, and errors are high-interest disclosures. Omitting them impairs comparability and may be non-compliant.

### Keeping Stale Policies That No Longer Match The Entity

Policies from prior years that no longer reflect the entity's transactions (a discontinued product line, a changed business model) mislead users. Review and update annually.

### Disclosing Policies In A Random Or Inconsistent Order

Policies presented in random order or reordered each period without reason impair usability. Present in a logical, consistent order.

### Omitting The Measurement Basis

The measurement basis (cost, fair value, amortized cost) for material asset and liability classes should be disclosed. Omitting it leaves users guessing about the valuation approach.

## Self-Check

- Are only material accounting policies disclosed (IFRS materiality filter), focused on entity-specific application rather than generic standard summaries?
- Does each policy explain how the entity applies the standard to its specific transactions, not what the standard says in general?
- Are policies disclosed for material items and significant judgments, tailored to the entity's business?
- Are the measurement basis and significant judgments (fair value techniques, impairment assumptions, revenue timing) disclosed for relevant policies?
- Are policies presented in a logical, consistent order, cross-referenced to relevant notes?
- Are changes in accounting policies (retrospective), estimates (prospective), and errors (retrospective) disclosed with nature, reason, and financial effect?
- Have policies been reviewed annually for continued relevance, with stale policies removed and new policies added?
- Has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed the policy disclosures for compliance with the applicable standard?
