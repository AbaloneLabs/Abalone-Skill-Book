# Impairment Reversal And Disclosure

Impairment is not always permanent. Under IFRS, impairment losses on most assets must be reversed when the assumptions that led to the loss have changed. Under US GAAP, reversal is prohibited for long-lived assets held and used, creating a fundamental difference between the frameworks. The reversal decision and the related disclosures are where many errors occur, because the rules differ by asset category and framework, and because reversal must not create a carrying amount higher than would have existed absent impairment.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during impairment reversal and disclosure. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist valuation advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 350 and 360, IFRS IAS 36) and consult qualified professionals for material reversals.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether Reversal Is Permitted For The Asset Category

Reversal eligibility depends on the asset category and the framework. Do not assume symmetry between recognition and reversal.

- Under IFRS, impairment losses on assets other than goodwill must be reversed when there has been a change in the estimates used to determine the recoverable amount since the last impairment loss was recognized.
- Under IFRS, goodwill impairment losses are never reversed, even if the reasons for the loss no longer apply.
- Under US GAAP, impairment losses on long-lived assets held and used are never reversed, even if the recoverable amount subsequently increases.
- Under US GAAP, assets held for sale can have recoveries recognized, but only up to the cumulative impairment previously recognized, not above the original carrying amount.
- For investments measured at fair value, changes in fair value are not impairment reversals but fair value remeasurements, regardless of framework.

### Apply The IFRS Reversal Rules For Long-Lived Assets

Under IFRS, reversal is required, not optional, when the conditions are met.

- Assess at each reporting date whether there is any indication that an impairment loss recognized in prior periods may no longer exist or may have decreased.
- Indicators that reversal may be needed include external sources such as market decline reversal, market, economic, legal, or technology environment improvements, and interest rate decreases, and internal sources such as evidence of improved performance or use, and improved economic performance.
- If an indicator exists, estimate the recoverable amount. The recoverable amount is the higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use.
- Recognize a reversal only if the recoverable amount exceeds the current carrying amount. The reversal is recognized in profit or loss, unless the asset is carried at revalued amount, in which case the reversal is handled under the revaluation rules.

### Cap The Reversal At The Notional Carrying Amount

The reversal cannot create a carrying amount higher than the carrying amount that would have existed, net of depreciation, had no impairment been recognized in prior years.

- Calculate the notional carrying amount by depreciating the original carrying amount from the impairment date, as if the impairment had not occurred.
- The reversal is limited to the lower of the recoverable amount and the notional carrying amount.
- This cap prevents the reversal from creating a gain that exceeds the cumulative effect of the prior impairment.

### Allocate Reversals Across Assets In The Group

When a reversal relates to a cash-generating unit, allocate it across the assets in the unit.

- Allocate the reversal to the assets in the unit, except goodwill, pro rata, but do not increase any asset above the lower of its recoverable amount and the notional carrying amount.
- Goodwill is allocated last and cannot be increased by a reversal.
- Document the allocation and the caps applied to each asset.

### Distinguish Reversal From Revaluation And Fair Value Changes

Do not confuse impairment reversal with revaluation increases or fair value remeasurement.

- For property, plant, and equipment carried at revalued amount under IFRS, a reversal of impairment is treated as a revaluation increase to the extent it reverses a prior revaluation decrease, with the excess recognized in profit or loss.
- For investment property carried at fair value, changes in fair value are recognized in profit or loss or OCI according to policy, not as impairment reversals.
- For financial assets measured at fair value, changes in value are fair value remeasurements, not impairment reversals.

### Apply The US GAAP Non-Reversal Rule Consistently

Under US GAAP, the non-reversal rule for long-lived assets held and used is absolute.

- Do not reverse impairment losses on long-lived assets held and used, regardless of subsequent recovery in value.
- Reassess the remaining useful life and depreciation method after impairment, but do not write the asset back up.
- For assets held for sale, recognize recoveries up to the cumulative impairment recognized, not above the original carrying amount.
- For goodwill, once impaired, the loss is permanent and cannot be reversed.

### Adjust Depreciation After Reversal Or Impairment

Whether recognizing impairment or reversal, adjust the depreciation prospectively.

- After an impairment loss, depreciate the reduced carrying amount over the remaining useful life.
- After a reversal under IFRS, depreciate the increased carrying amount over the remaining useful life.
- Reassess the useful life and residual value as part of the reversal assessment.

### Disclose Impairment And Reversal Adequately

Disclosures must give users enough information to understand the amounts, the assumptions, and the sensitivity of the results.

- Disclose the amount of impairment losses and reversals recognized in profit or loss and in OCI, by class of asset and by reportable segment.
- For material individual impairments or reversals, disclose the events and circumstances, the asset or CGU affected, the amount, and the method of determining fair value or recoverable amount.
- Disclose the key assumptions used, including discount rates, growth rates, and the period of cash flow projections, for which a reasonably possible change would eliminate headroom or trigger reversal.
- Disclose sensitivity analysis showing the effect of reasonably possible changes in key assumptions.
- Disclose the carrying amount of goodwill by reportable segment and the basis of allocation.

## Common Traps

### Reversing Impairment Under US GAAP

The non-reversal rule for long-lived assets held and used under US GAAP is absolute. Recognizing a recovery, even when value has clearly recovered, is prohibited and misstates the financial statements.

### Reversing Goodwill Under IFRS

Goodwill impairment is never reversible under IFRS, unlike other assets. Treating goodwill like other long-lived assets and reversing its impairment is a clear error.

### Exceeding The Notional Carrying Amount Cap

The reversal cannot create a carrying amount higher than would have existed net of depreciation had impairment not been recognized. Recognizing the full recoverable amount without applying the cap overstates assets and income.

### Failing To Reassess Useful Life And Residual Value

After impairment or reversal, the remaining useful life and residual value should be reassessed. Continuing to depreciate on the old basis misstates subsequent expense.

### Confusing Reversal With Fair Value Remeasurement

For assets measured at fair value, value changes are not impairment reversals. Treating a fair value increase as a reversal applies the wrong accounting and the wrong disclosures.

### Not Allocating Reversals Correctly Across A CGU

When a reversal relates to a cash-generating unit, it must be allocated across the assets, with caps applied, and goodwill excluded. Recognizing the entire reversal in one asset or including goodwill overstates that asset.

### Inadequate Disclosure Of Assumptions And Sensitivity

Impairment and reversal measurements depend on assumptions. Failing to disclose discount rates, growth rates, and sensitivity analysis hides the uncertainty from users and is a common audit finding.

### Treating Reversal As Optional Under IFRS

Under IFRS, reversal is required when indicators exist and the recoverable amount exceeds the carrying amount. Treating it as optional understates assets and income.

## Self-Check

- Has the asset category and framework been confirmed to determine whether reversal is permitted, with goodwill and US GAAP held-and-used assets excluded from reversal?
- Under IFRS, has an indicator assessment been performed at each reporting date, and is reversal recognized when the recoverable amount exceeds the carrying amount?
- Is the reversal capped at the notional carrying amount that would have existed, net of depreciation, had impairment not been recognized?
- For reversals relating to a cash-generating unit, is the reversal allocated across assets pro rata, with caps applied and goodwill excluded?
- Under US GAAP, are impairment losses on long-lived assets held and used never reversed, with only held-for-sale recoveries recognized up to the cumulative impairment?
- Is depreciation adjusted prospectively after both impairment and reversal, with useful life and residual value reassessed?
- Are reversal and revaluation increases distinguished, with reversal of impairment on revalued assets treated under the revaluation rules?
- Are disclosures complete, including amounts by class and segment, events and circumstances, key assumptions, and sensitivity analysis?
- For material balances, has a valuation specialist been engaged, and are assumptions current and supportable?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for complex reversals or multi-asset CGUs?
