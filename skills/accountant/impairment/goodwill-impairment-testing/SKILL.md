# Goodwill Impairment Testing

Goodwill is not amortized under US GAAP or IFRS, but it must be tested for impairment at least annually and whenever indicators exist. Because goodwill testing relies on fair value measurements of reporting units or cash-generating units, the assumptions drive the outcome. Small changes in discount rates, growth rates, or cash flow projections can swing the result. This is an area where unsupported optimism defers losses and unsupported pessimism writes down assets unnecessarily.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during goodwill impairment testing. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist valuation advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 350, IFRS IAS 36) and engage valuation specialists for material goodwill balances.

## Core Rules

### Identify And Define Reporting Units Or Cash-Generating Units

Goodwill is allocated to the units that are expected to benefit from the synergies of the business combination, and testing is performed at that level.

- Under US GAAP, goodwill is allocated to reporting units. A reporting unit is an operating segment or one level below an operating segment (a component) if discrete financial information is available and segment management regularly reviews the component's operating results.
- Under IFRS, goodwill is allocated to cash-generating units (CGUs) or groups of CGUs, being the smallest group that generates cash inflows largely independent of other units, and that benefits from the synergies of the combination.
- Allocate goodwill promptly after a business combination and update the allocation when the organization of reporting units or CGUs changes.
- The level of the unit is critical. Testing at too high a level dilutes impairment signals, while testing at too low a level can trigger premature impairment.

### Determine The Timing And Frequency Of Testing

Goodwill must be tested at least annually and more frequently if indicators of impairment exist.

- Under US GAAP, the annual test may be performed at any time during the fiscal year, as long as it is performed at the same time each year for each reporting unit. Different reporting units may be tested at different times.
- Under IFRS, annual testing is required for goodwill and for intangible assets with indefinite useful lives or not yet available for use, regardless of whether indicators exist. Other goodwill allocations to CGUs require annual testing.
- Test between annual tests whenever an indicator of impairment exists, such as a significant decline in market capitalization below the aggregate carrying amount of net assets, significant adverse changes in the business climate, regulatory or competitive changes, or a decision to restructure.

### Apply The Qualitative Assessment Under US GAAP

Under US GAAP, an entity has the option to perform a qualitative assessment to determine whether it is more likely than not that a reporting unit's fair value is less than its carrying amount.

- The qualitative assessment considers events and circumstances such as macroeconomic conditions, industry and market considerations, cost factors, overall financial performance, entity-specific events, reporting unit events, and other relevant factors.
- If the qualitative assessment concludes it is more likely than not that fair value is less than carrying amount, proceed to the quantitative test. If not, no further testing is required.
- Document the qualitative assessment thoroughly. An undocumented or superficial qualitative assessment is a common audit finding.
- The entity may elect to bypass the qualitative assessment and proceed directly to the quantitative test.

### Perform The Quantitative Test

The quantitative test compares the fair value of the reporting unit or CGU to its carrying amount, including goodwill.

- Under US GAAP, perform a one-step quantitative test. Compare the fair value of the reporting unit to its carrying amount. If fair value is less than carrying amount, recognize an impairment loss for the difference, limited to the goodwill balance.
- Under IFRS, the test compares the recoverable amount of the CGU to its carrying amount. The recoverable amount is the higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use. If carrying amount exceeds recoverable amount, recognize an impairment loss, allocated first to goodwill and then to other assets pro rata.
- Under both frameworks, once goodwill is written down, the loss cannot be reversed.

### Measure The Fair Value Or Recoverable Amount

The fair value or recoverable amount measurement is the heart of the test and depends on assumptions.

- The most common technique is a discounted cash flow (income approach), using projected cash flows, a discount rate reflecting the time value of money and risk, and a terminal value.
- Market approaches use multiples from comparable companies or transactions.
- Use current, supportable assumptions. Discount rates should reflect the risk profile of the unit, not the entity as a whole. Growth rates in the terminal period should not exceed the long-term growth rate of the markets in which the unit operates.
- Document the valuation methodology, key assumptions, and sensitivity analysis. Engage valuation specialists for material goodwill balances.

### Understand And Apply Headroom

Headroom is the excess of the fair value or recoverable amount of a unit over its carrying amount. Headroom absorbs future declines before impairment is triggered.

- Under IFRS, when goodwill is allocated to a CGU, the carrying amount of the unit includes goodwill. Headroom is the buffer before impairment.
- When a portion of a CGU is reorganized or a CGU is realigned, any impairment loss previously absorbed by headroom must be recognized before reallocating goodwill, to prevent moving headroom to mask impairment.
- Under US GAAP, when a reporting unit is reorganized, any goodwill previously impaired must be considered, and goodwill is reallocated using a relative fair value approach.

### Allocate And Reallocate Goodwill

When the organization of reporting units or CGUs changes, goodwill must be reallocated.

- Under US GAAP, when a reporting unit is reorganized, reallocate goodwill using a relative fair value approach if the change constitutes a change in reporting structure, or using a transfer approach if a portion of a reporting unit is transferred.
- Under IFRS, when a CGU is reorganized, reallocate goodwill using a relative value approach, and test the affected CGUs for impairment before and after the realignment.
- Do not move goodwill to mask impairment in a unit. The reallocation must reflect the benefits expected from the goodwill.

### Recognize And Disclose Impairment Losses

Recognize impairment losses in profit or loss, generally within operating expenses, and disclose the facts and circumstances, the amount, the method of determining fair value or recoverable amount, and the segment affected.

- Disclose the key assumptions used in the measurement, including discount rates, growth rates, and the period of cash flow projections.
- Disclose sensitivity analysis showing the effect of reasonably possible changes in assumptions.
- Under IFRS, disclose the carrying amount of goodwill by reportable segment and by CGU, and the basis of allocation.

## Common Traps

### Using An Unsupported Or Stale Discount Rate

The discount rate has an outsized effect on the fair value result. Using a rate that is too low inflates fair value and avoids impairment. The rate must reflect the risk of the specific unit and current market conditions.

### Assuming Growth Rates Above The Market

Terminal growth rates above the long-term growth of the relevant market imply the unit will grow faster than the economy forever, which is not supportable. Use growth rates consistent with the market in which the unit operates.

### Testing At The Wrong Unit Level

Testing at too high a level (such as the consolidated entity) dilutes impairment signals. Testing at too low a level can trigger premature impairment. The unit must reflect the level at which goodwill is monitored.

### Skipping The Qualitative Assessment Documentation

Under US GAAP, a qualitative assessment that concludes no impairment without documented analysis of events and circumstances is not defensible. Document the assessment or bypass it and perform the quantitative test.

### Failing To Test Between Annual Tests When Indicators Exist

Market capitalization below net asset carrying amount, adverse business changes, or restructuring decisions are indicators. Waiting until the annual test date when indicators exist mid-year defers recognition.

### Ignoring Market Capitalization Versus Carrying Amount

For listed entities, if market capitalization is below the aggregate carrying amount of net assets, that is a strong indicator. Dismissing it as a temporary market fluctuation without analysis is not defensible.

### Reallocating Goodwill To Mask Impairment

Reorganizing reporting units or CGUs to move headroom and avoid impairment is not permitted. Reallocations must be substantive and tested before and after the change.

### Using Optimistic Cash Flow Projections Without Sensitivity

Cash flow projections that ignore current conditions, assume aggressive margin recovery, or exclude downside scenarios are not supportable. Include sensitivity analysis to show the effect of changes.

## Self-Check

- Is goodwill allocated to the appropriate reporting units or cash-generating units, and is the allocation updated when the organization changes?
- Is the annual test performed at the same time each year under US GAAP, and is testing performed between annual tests when indicators exist?
- Under US GAAP, if a qualitative assessment is performed, is it documented with analysis of relevant events and circumstances?
- Is the quantitative test performed by comparing fair value or recoverable amount to carrying amount, including goodwill?
- Are discount rates, growth rates, and cash flow projections current, supportable, and documented, with sensitivity analysis?
- Is the terminal growth rate consistent with the long-term growth of the relevant market?
- For listed entities, is the relationship between market capitalization and aggregate carrying amount assessed as an indicator?
- When reporting units or CGUs are reorganized, is goodwill reallocated using the appropriate approach, and are affected units tested before and after the realignment?
- Are impairment losses recognized in profit or loss, limited to the goodwill balance, and not reversed in subsequent periods?
- Are disclosures complete, including key assumptions, sensitivity analysis, and carrying amounts by segment, and has a valuation specialist been engaged for material balances?
