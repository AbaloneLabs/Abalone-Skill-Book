# Long-Lived Asset Impairment Testing

Long-lived assets must be tested for impairment when events or circumstances indicate that the carrying amount may not be recoverable. The testing involves identifying triggering events, grouping assets, measuring recoverability, and recognizing losses. The judgments are significant: which assets to group, what cash flows to include, and what assumptions to use. Casual assumptions can defer loss recognition, overstate assets, or write down assets unnecessarily.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during long-lived asset impairment testing. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist valuation advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 360 for long-lived assets to be held and used, IFRS IAS 36) and consult qualified professionals for material impairment assessments.

## Core Rules

### Identify Triggering Events And Indicators Of Impairment

Impairment testing begins with identifying whether events or changes indicate that the carrying amount may not be recoverable. Do not test on a fixed schedule unless the asset is goodwill, an indefinite-lived intangible, or another asset that requires annual testing.

- Common triggers include a significant decrease in market price, a significant adverse change in the manner or extent of use or physical condition, a significant adverse change in legal factors or the business climate, accumulation of costs significantly in excess of the amount originally expected, a current period loss combined with a history of losses, or a current expectation that an asset will be sold or disposed of significantly before the end of its useful life.
- Under IFRS, indicators include external sources such as market decline, technology, economic, legal, or market environment changes, and interest rate increases, and internal sources such as evidence of obsolescence or physical damage, asset use changes, economic performance worse than expected, and plans to discontinue or restructure.
- Document the triggering event assessment at each reporting date. If no triggers exist, no testing is required for assets held and used.

### Group Assets At The Lowest Level Of Identifiable Cash Flows

Long-lived assets are tested at the asset group level, not the individual asset level, when cash flows from individual assets are not independent.

- Under US GAAP, the asset group is the lowest level for which identifiable cash flows are largely independent of the cash flows of other groups. Identify the asset group before testing.
- Under IFRS, the cash-generating unit (CGU) is the smallest identifiable group of assets that generates cash inflows largely independent of those from other assets or groups.
- Include in the asset group or CGU all assets that generate the related cash flows, including goodwill allocated to the group, working capital, and other assets.
- Do not include financial assets, deferred tax assets, or assets related to postretirement benefits in the asset group unless their cash flows are part of the group.

### Apply The Recoverability Test Under US GAAP

Under US GAAP, for assets held and used, apply a two-step test once a trigger is identified.

- Step 1 is the recoverability test. Estimate the undiscounted future cash flows expected to result from the use and eventual disposition of the asset group. If the carrying amount exceeds the undiscounted cash flows, the asset is not recoverable and step 2 applies.
- Step 2 is the measurement of impairment. Measure the fair value of the asset group. The impairment loss is the excess of the carrying amount over fair value.
- Use cash flow estimates that are consistent with the assumptions marketplace participants would use, excluding interest and income tax effects, and excluding cash flows from restructuring, future capital improvements, and cash inflows or outflows from other asset groups.

### Apply The Recoverable Amount Test Under IFRS

Under IFRS, for assets held and used, measure the recoverable amount once an indicator exists.

- The recoverable amount is the higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use.
- Value in use is the present value of the future cash flows expected to be derived from the asset or CGU. It includes estimates of future cash flows, expectations of possible variations, the time value of money, the price for bearing uncertainty, and assumed liability assumptions.
- Fair value less costs of disposal is the price to sell an asset in an orderly transaction between market participants less disposal costs.
- If either amount exceeds the carrying amount, the asset is not impaired. If the recoverable amount is lower than the carrying amount, recognize the impairment loss.

### Measure And Allocate Impairment Losses

The impairment loss reduces the carrying amount of the asset group to its fair value or recoverable amount.

- Under US GAAP, allocate the impairment loss to the assets in the group pro rata, but do not reduce any asset below its individual fair value, if determinable, or to zero.
- Under IFRS, allocate the impairment loss first to goodwill allocated to the CGU, then to other assets pro rata, but do not reduce any asset below its individual recoverable amount or fair value less costs of disposal.
- After impairment, the reduced carrying amount becomes the new basis. Depreciation is adjusted prospectively over the remaining useful life.

### Handle Reversal Of Impairment Differently By Framework

The reversal rules differ significantly between US GAAP and IFRS.

- Under US GAAP, impairment losses on long-lived assets held and used are not reversed in subsequent periods, even if the recoverable amount later increases.
- Under IFRS, an impairment loss recognized in prior periods must be reversed, except for goodwill, if the assumptions have changed. The reversal is recognized in profit or loss, capped at the carrying amount that would have existed had no impairment been recognized, net of depreciation.
- Reversal is prohibited for goodwill under both frameworks.

### Account For Assets Held For Sale Differently

Assets held for sale are measured at the lower of carrying amount or fair value less costs to sell, and are not depreciated.

- A long-lived asset is classified as held for sale when the sale is probable, available for immediate sale, and an active plan to sell is in place, among criteria.
- Once classified as held for sale, the asset is tested for impairment using fair value less costs to sell, and subsequent increases in fair value less costs to sell are recognized as a recovery, but not in excess of the cumulative impairment previously recognized.

### Use Supportable, Documented Assumptions

Impairment testing depends heavily on cash flow and discount rate assumptions. Unsupported or stale assumptions undermine the test.

- Use cash flow projections based on reasonable and supportable assumptions, reflecting the most recent budgets and forecasts, typically covering a maximum of five years unless a longer period can be justified.
- Use a discount rate that reflects the time value of money and risks specific to the asset, consistent with market participants.
- Document the methodology, key assumptions, sensitivity analysis, and the involvement of valuation specialists for material balances.

## Common Traps

### Failing To Identify Or Document Triggering Events

Without documented trigger assessment, impairment testing may be skipped when it is required, or performed when it is not. The assessment must be performed at each reporting date and documented.

### Grouping Assets At The Wrong Level

Grouping too broadly dilutes impairment signals by mixing strong and weak assets. Grouping too narrowly can trigger premature impairment. The asset group or CGU must reflect the lowest level of independent cash flows.

### Using Discounted Cash Flows In The US GAAP Recoverability Test

The US GAAP step 1 test uses undiscounted cash flows. Using discounted cash flows in step 1 makes the test too sensitive and recognizes impairment too early.

### Including Inappropriate Cash Flows

Including cash flows from restructuring, future capital improvements, or other asset groups inflates the recoverability estimate and defers impairment. Exclude these from the test.

### Failing To Reassess Useful Lives And Residual Values After Impairment

After an impairment loss, the remaining useful life and residual value should be reassessed. Continuing to depreciate on the old basis misstates subsequent expense.

### Reversing Impairment Under US GAAP

Under US GAAP, impairment losses on assets held and used cannot be reversed. Recognizing a recovery misstates income. Under IFRS, reversal is required, except for goodwill.

### Using Stale Or Aggressive Cash Flow Assumptions

Cash flow projections that are outdated, ignore current market conditions, or use unsupported growth rates can avoid recognizing impairment that should be recognized. Assumptions must be current and supportable.

### Overlooking Held For Sale Classification And Measurement

Assets that meet the held for sale criteria must be reclassified and measured at the lower of carrying amount or fair value less costs to sell, and depreciation ceases. Continuing to depreciate or test under the held and used model is incorrect.

## Self-Check

- Has a triggering event or indicator assessment been performed at each reporting date and documented, with testing performed when indicators exist?
- Are assets grouped at the lowest level of independent cash flows, the asset group under US GAAP or the cash-generating unit under IFRS?
- Under US GAAP, is the recoverability test performed using undiscounted future cash flows, and is the impairment loss measured as the excess of carrying amount over fair value?
- Under IFRS, is the recoverable amount measured as the higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use?
- Are cash flow estimates excluding restructuring, future capital improvements, and other asset groups, and consistent with market participant assumptions?
- Is the impairment loss allocated to assets in the group without reducing any asset below its individual fair value or recoverable amount?
- Under IFRS, are impairment losses (except goodwill) reversed when assumptions change, capped at the depreciated carrying amount that would have existed absent impairment?
- Under US GAAP, are impairment losses on assets held and used not reversed?
- Are assets held for sale classified and measured at the lower of carrying amount or fair value less costs to sell, with depreciation ceased?
- Are assumptions current and supportable, documented with sensitivity analysis, and supported by valuation specialists for material balances, and has the applicable framework been confirmed?
