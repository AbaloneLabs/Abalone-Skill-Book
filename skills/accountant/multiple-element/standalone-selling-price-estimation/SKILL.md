# Standalone Selling Price Estimation

Standalone selling price is the price at which an entity would sell a promised good or service separately to a customer. It is the foundation for allocating the transaction price across performance obligations. When prices are directly observable, estimation is straightforward, but many obligations are never sold separately, or are sold only in bundles, or are sold at prices that vary widely. In those cases the price must be estimated, and the method, inputs, and assumptions determine whether the allocation is defensible. Agents frequently grab the most convenient number, apply a method without justifying it, or use the residual approach as a shortcut to avoid estimating a difficult price.

Use this skill before concluding what standalone selling prices to use for allocation. The goal is to force the agent to prefer observable evidence, choose an estimation method that fits the situation, document the inputs, and apply methods consistently.

This guidance is general and educational. Estimation approaches and their acceptability differ between ASC 606 and IFRS 15, across industries, and by entity policy, and all vary by jurisdiction. Final determinations for a specific entity should be made by a qualified accounting professional with access to pricing data and entity policy. This skill is not legal, tax, or audit advice and is not a substitute for the governing standard.

## Core Rules

### Prefer Directly Observable Prices

The best standalone selling price is the price at which the entity sells the same good or service separately under similar circumstances at or near the contract date. Before estimating, look for directly observable evidence.

Search for:

- current standalone price lists and published rates;
- recent standalone sales of the same item to similar customers;
- prices charged for the item when sold separately, even if infrequent;
- prices for closely similar goods or services that can be adjusted.

When observable prices exist, use them. Estimation is a fallback for when observation is not possible, not a default starting point.

### Choose An Estimation Method That Fits The Situation

When a price is not directly observable, estimate it using a method appropriate to the nature of the good or service. The method must maximize the use of observable inputs and reflect what the entity would charge in a standalone sale.

Common methods:

- adjusted market assessment, using market conditions, competitor pricing, and the entity's market position;
- expected cost plus a reasonable margin, using cost data and a margin that reflects the entity's pricing strategy;
- residual approach, permitted only in narrow circumstances.

Choose based on what best predicts the standalone price. A method that fits software licenses may not fit professional services, and a method that fits a mature product may not fit a new offering with no market history.

### Use Adjusted Market Assessment With Real Market Data

Adjusted market assessment uses external market evidence adjusted for the entity's specific circumstances. It works well when there is an active market for similar goods or services, even if the entity's exact offering differs.

For this method, gather:

- competitor prices for similar offerings;
- published market rates and industry benchmarks;
- the entity's historical discounting and pricing strategy;
- differences between the entity's offering and the market comparable, such as quality, scope, or brand.

Document the market data and the adjustments. An adjusted market assessment without documented market evidence is just an assertion.

### Use Expected Cost Plus Margin When Cost Data Is Reliable

Expected cost plus margin estimates the price from the cost to provide the good or service plus a margin consistent with the entity's pricing. It works well when costs are predictable and the entity's margin strategy is stable.

For this method, gather:

- expected direct and indirect costs to deliver the obligation;
- the entity's typical margin for similar goods or services;
- evidence that the margin reflects what the market would bear, not just an internal target.

The margin must be reasonable and supportable. A cost-plus estimate with an unsupported margin produces an allocation that will not survive scrutiny.

### Restrict The Residual Approach

The residual approach estimates one obligation's price by subtracting the observable standalone prices of the other obligations from the total transaction price. It is permitted only when one or more of the obligations have highly variable or uncertain standalone selling prices and the others have observable prices. It is not a general-purpose method.

Do not use the residual approach:

- when standalone prices are observable for all obligations;
- to avoid estimating a difficult price;
- when the residual amount is negative or implausible, which signals the method is inappropriate.

When the residual produces a price that is out of line with any reasonable expectation, switch to a different method.

### Maximize Observable Inputs

Whatever method is chosen, it should maximize the use of observable inputs and minimize unobservable assumptions. The more an estimate rests on internal targets and unsupported assumptions, the less defensible it is. Prefer external market data, historical transaction data, and verifiable cost data over guesses.

### Apply Methods Consistently To Similar Arrangements

Use the same estimation method for the same or similar goods or services across contracts. Switching methods contract to contract without justification undermines comparability and signals that the method is being chosen to produce a desired allocation rather than to estimate the true price. Document the policy for when each method is used.

### Estimate At contract inception And Reassess When Required

Standalone selling prices are generally estimated at contract inception for the purpose of initial allocation. For variable consideration and changes in scope or price, reassess as required by the standard. Do not silently change standalone prices period to period to smooth revenue; changes must be supported by evidence and recognized appropriately.

### Document The Method, Inputs, And Rationale

For every estimated price, record the method, the inputs, the assumptions, and the reason the method was chosen. Documentation is what makes the estimate auditable and consistent. An undocumented estimate cannot be reliably revisited, defended, or applied consistently to similar contracts.

## Common Traps

### Skipping The Search For Observable Prices

Agents often move straight to estimation because it is faster, missing directly observable prices that would be more defensible. Always search for observable evidence first. Estimation without that search is premature.

### Using The Residual Approach As A Shortcut

The residual approach is tempting because it avoids estimating the hardest price, but it is restricted to narrow circumstances. Using it broadly produces allocations that depend entirely on the prices of the other obligations and can yield implausible or negative results.

### Unsupported Margins In Cost-Plus Estimates

Expected cost plus margin is only as good as the margin. An internal target margin with no evidence that the market would bear it produces a price that looks precise but is not supportable. The margin must reflect market reality.

### Adjusted Market Assessment Without Market Data

Calling an estimate an adjusted market assessment does not make it one. Without documented competitor prices, benchmarks, or market evidence, the estimate is an assertion. The label must match the evidence.

### Inconsistent Methods Across Similar Contracts

Switching methods for the same good or service, contract to contract, signals that the method is being chosen to produce a desired allocation. Consistency is required unless a documented reason justifies a change.

### Changing Prices Period To Period To Smooth Revenue

Silently adjusting standalone selling prices each period to smooth or shift revenue is not permitted. Prices are estimated at inception and reassessed only when the standard requires. Ad hoc changes distort revenue and are a serious error.

### Ignoring Highly Variable Or Uncertain Prices

When a price is genuinely highly variable, a point estimate may mislead. Understand the range and the drivers of variability, and where the residual approach is permitted, use it appropriately. Ignoring variability produces false precision.

### Relying On Internal Targets Instead Of Market Evidence

Estimates built solely on internal cost or margin targets, without reference to what the market would pay, do not reflect a standalone selling price. The price is what a customer would pay separately, not what the entity would like to earn.

## Self-Check

- Has a search for directly observable standalone prices been conducted before resorting to estimation?
- Where prices are estimated, is the chosen method, whether adjusted market assessment, expected cost plus margin, or residual, appropriate to the nature of the good or service?
- For adjusted market assessment, is there documented market data such as competitor prices, benchmarks, and adjustments for the entity's offering?
- For expected cost plus margin, is the margin reasonable and supported by evidence that the market would bear it, rather than an unsupported internal target?
- Is the residual approach used only where permitted, with highly variable or uncertain prices and observable prices for the other obligations, and never where it produces implausible results?
- Does the estimate maximize observable inputs and minimize unobservable assumptions?
- Is the same method applied consistently to the same or similar goods or services across contracts, with any change documented and justified?
- Are standalone selling prices estimated at contract inception and reassessed only when the standard requires, not changed period to period to smooth revenue?
- Are the method, inputs, assumptions, and rationale documented for each estimated price so the conclusion is auditable and revisitable?
- Have material or borderline estimation judgments been flagged for review by a qualified accounting professional with access to pricing data and entity policy?
