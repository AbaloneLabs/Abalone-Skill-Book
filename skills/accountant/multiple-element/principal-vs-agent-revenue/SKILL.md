# Principal Versus Agent Revenue

When an entity arranges for a third party to provide goods or services to a customer, it must decide whether to report revenue gross, as a principal, or net of the amount paid to the third party, as an agent. This decision does not change profit in many cases, but it changes reported revenue and gross margin dramatically, which affects how the business is valued, how it compares to peers, and whether it meets revenue-based covenants or metrics. The determination turns on control: a principal controls the specified good or service before it is transferred to the customer, while an agent merely arranges for that transfer. An agent that reports gross inflates revenue; a principal that reports net understates it. Both mislead users about the scale and nature of the business.

Use this skill before deciding gross versus net presentation, while applying the control indicators, while evaluating marketplace, consignment, or drop-ship arrangements, while setting revenue policy for intermediary models, or while auditing principal versus agent classification. The goal is to make the agent determine presentation from the substance of control rather than from which number looks better, and to prevent gross reporting from being chosen to inflate top-line metrics.

Revenue recognition standards vary by jurisdiction and reporting framework. The dominant converged frameworks are US GAAP ASC 606 and IFRS 15, which share the control-based approach to principal versus agent, but entities may also be subject to industry-specific guidance, local GAAP, tax-basis rules, or sector regulation. Some older guidance used different indicator-based tests, and the transition to control-based guidance changed outcomes for some arrangements. Do not state a single universal rule. Where the arrangement is complex or the control indicators conflict, route the question to a qualified accountant or auditor. This skill offers operational judgment, not a definitive accounting opinion.

## Core Rules

### Determine Presentation From Control, Not From Preference

The single decisive question is whether the entity controls the specified good or service before it is transferred to the customer. If it does, it is a principal and reports gross. If it does not, and merely arranges the transfer, it is an agent and reports net, recognizing only its commission. Control is the primary criterion; everything else is evidence about control.

Establish control by determining whether the entity:

- has the ability to direct the use of, and obtain substantially all the remaining benefits from, the good or service;
- is primarily responsible for fulfilling the promise to the customer;
- has inventory risk before or after the customer order;
- has discretion in establishing the price.

No single indicator is automatically determinative. Weigh them together to infer whether the entity controls the specified good or service.

### Apply The Control Indicators As Evidence, Not As A Checklist

The indicators help infer control, but they must be weighed in context. An entity may show some principal indicators and some agent indicators, and the conclusion follows from the overall weight of evidence.

Consider:

- primary obligor, whether the entity is primarily responsible for providing the product or service and for fulfillment;
- inventory risk, whether the entity bears the risk of loss before or after the customer order, including in consignment or stock held;
- pricing discretion, whether the entity sets the price and can retain the margin, or passes through a fixed amount;
- discretion in supplier selection, whether the entity determines which supplier provides the good or service;
- exposure to credit risk, whether the entity bears the customer's credit risk or the supplier bears it;
- the nature of the promise, whether the entity provides the good or service itself or arranges for another party to do so.

Strong indicators of inventory risk and pricing discretion generally point to principal. A pure pass-through with no inventory risk, fixed margins, and no pricing discretion generally points to agent. Mixed indicators require judgment about which reflects the substance of control.

### Evaluate Inventory Risk Before And After The Order

Inventory risk is one of the strongest signals of control. An entity that obtains or controls the good before the customer order, or that retains the good after and bears the risk of it not selling, generally controls the good and is a principal.

Assess:

- does the entity purchase or hold inventory before a customer order is placed?
- does the entity bear loss if the good is damaged or becomes obsolete while held?
- after the order, does the entity retain risk if the customer returns or refuses the good?
- in drop-ship, does the entity ever control the good, or does it flow directly from supplier to customer?

An entity with no inventory risk at any point, where goods flow directly from supplier to customer and the entity never holds or controls them, is more likely an agent. But the absence of physical inventory alone does not decide the question; consider the other indicators.

### Assess Pricing Discretion And The Right To Set Price

Pricing discretion reflects whether the entity controls the economic benefits of the good. A principal typically sets the price to the customer and retains the full margin. An agent typically earns a fixed commission or percentage and has no ability to set the customer price.

Distinguish:

- the entity sets the customer price and bears the margin, indicating principal;
- the entity earns a fixed fee or percentage regardless of the customer price, indicating agent;
- the entity has limited discretion within a supplier-set range, which requires judgment about the degree of control.

Some agents have a narrow band of pricing flexibility for service or marketing reasons; this does not by itself make them principals. Focus on whether the entity captures the substantial risks and rewards of the price.

### Identify The Primary Obligor And Responsibility For Fulfillment

The primary obligor indicator asks whether the entity is responsible for fulfilling the promise to the customer, including providing the product or service and handling issues. A principal is typically the customer's primary contact and bears responsibility for fulfillment. An agent's responsibility is limited to arranging the transaction.

Assess:

- is the entity the party the customer looks to for fulfillment and resolution?
- does the entity handle returns, warranties, and customer service?
- if the supplier fails, is the entity obligated to the customer to provide a substitute?

Being the primary obligor supports principal classification, but it is one indicator among several and must be weighed with inventory risk and pricing discretion.

### Handle Marketplace, Consignment, And Drop-Ship Models Carefully

Intermediary business models often sit at the boundary, and the substance varies by arrangement. Apply the control test to the specific facts rather than assuming a model-wide classification.

For these models:

- marketplaces may be principals for goods they hold and control and agents for third-party listings they merely facilitate, requiring segment-by-segment analysis;
- consignment, where the entity holds another party's goods to sell on its behalf, requires determining who controls the goods during consignment;
- drop-ship, where goods ship directly from supplier to customer, requires determining whether the entity ever controls the good and bears inventory risk.

A single entity can be a principal for some transactions and an agent for others. Classify each arrangement on its own control facts.

### Present And Disclose The Basis For Gross Or Net Treatment

Because gross versus net materially affects reported revenue, the basis for the determination should be documented and, where required, disclosed. Users need to understand whether revenue is gross or net and why.

Document:

- the control conclusion and the indicators that support it;
- the segments or arrangements treated as principal versus agent;
- the effect on reported revenue and margin;
- any changes in classification from period to period.

Inconsistent or undocumented classification undermines comparability and invites audit challenge.

## Common Traps

### Choosing Gross Reporting To Inflate Revenue

Gross reporting flatters top-line revenue and apparent scale. Choosing gross because it looks better, rather than because the entity controls the good, misleads users and may breach reporting requirements.

### Treating Any Single Indicator As Decisive

No single indicator, including primary obligor or inventory risk, automatically decides principal versus agent. The conclusion follows from weighing the indicators together to infer control.

### Ignoring Inventory Risk In Drop-Ship Arrangements

In drop-ship, the entity may never control the good. Assuming principal because the entity bills the customer, without assessing whether it ever bore inventory risk, overstates revenue.

### Classifying An Entire Marketplace As Principal Or Agent

A marketplace may be principal for some transactions and agent for others. Applying one classification to the whole platform misstates revenue for the segments that differ.

### Confusing Limited Pricing Flexibility With Control

A narrow band of pricing discretion for service reasons does not make an agent a principal. Control of the price, not a marketing margin, is the relevant test.

### Failing To Disclose Or Document The Basis

Gross versus net materially changes reported revenue. An undocumented or inconsistently applied classification undermines comparability and audit defensibility.

### Applying Legacy Indicator Tests Without The Control Overlay

Older guidance relied more heavily on indicator checklists. Under current control-based guidance, the indicators are evidence about control, not a standalone scoring system.

## Self-Check

- Is gross versus net presentation determined from whether the entity controls the specified good or service before transfer, rather than from which number looks better?
- Are the control indicators, primary obligor, inventory risk, pricing discretion, supplier selection, and credit risk, weighed together rather than treated as a checklist?
- Is inventory risk assessed both before and after the customer order, including in drop-ship and consignment arrangements?
- Does pricing discretion reflect control of the margin rather than a narrow service or marketing band?
- Is the entity's responsibility for fulfillment, returns, and customer service assessed as part of the primary obligor indicator?
- Are marketplace, consignment, and drop-ship models classified on their specific control facts, with segment-by-segment analysis where they differ?
- Where the entity is principal for some transactions and agent for others, is each arrangement classified separately?
- Is the basis for gross or net treatment documented, with the supporting indicators and the effect on revenue and margin?
- Does the analysis confirm the applicable control-based framework and recommend qualified professional review where indicators conflict or the arrangement is complex?
