# Bundle And Discount Allocation

When a contract bundles multiple goods or services at a combined price, the transaction price must be allocated across the performance obligations. Discounts, free items, vouchers, and variable amounts all complicate the allocation. The temptation is to allocate proportionally and move on, but the rules require evidence before assigning any discount or variable amount to specific obligations. Agents frequently allocate discounts by convenience, treat free items as having zero value, misallocate variable consideration, and fail to reassess when bundles change. These errors shift revenue between obligations and between periods.

Use this skill before concluding how to allocate a bundled price and any discounts. The goal is to force the agent to allocate on relative standalone selling prices, to handle discounts and variable amounts with evidence, and to flag where the allocation requires professional judgment.

This guidance is general and educational. Allocation rules differ between ASC 606 and IFRS 15, across industries, and by entity policy, and all vary by jurisdiction. Final determinations for a specific contract should be made by a qualified accounting professional with access to the full arrangement and pricing data. This skill is not legal, tax, or audit advice and is not a substitute for the governing standard.

## Core Rules

### Allocate On Relative Standalone Selling Price

The default method for allocating the transaction price across bundled obligations is the relative standalone selling price method. Each obligation receives a share of the transaction price proportional to its standalone price relative to the total of all standalone prices. This method applies unless a specific exception, such as observable evidence for a discount, justifies a different allocation.

Compute:

- the standalone selling price of each obligation;
- the total of all standalone prices;
- each obligation's share as its standalone price divided by the total;
- the allocated transaction price as that share times the total transaction price.

Document the standalone prices, especially when they are estimated rather than observed.

### Handle Discounts With Evidence

A discount, meaning the sum of standalone prices exceeds the transaction price, generally allocates across all performance obligations proportionally. A discount may be allocated to specific obligations only when there is observable evidence supporting that treatment.

Observable evidence includes:

- the entity regularly sells each obligation in the bundle on a standalone basis;
- the entity also sells the obligation with the discount on a standalone basis;
- the discount is consistent with the entity's standalone pricing for that obligation.

Without such evidence, allocate the discount proportionally. Do not assign a discount to the largest line, the most observable price, or the obligation where it produces a convenient result.

### Distinguish Standalone Sales Evidence From Convenience

The test for assigning a discount to specific obligations is whether the entity's actual standalone sales support it. Convenience is not evidence. Ask whether the entity genuinely sells the item separately at the discounted price, not whether assigning the discount there would be tidy.

### Allocate Variable Consideration Appropriately

Variable consideration generally allocates the same way as the transaction price, proportionally across all obligations. Variable consideration may allocate to specific obligations when it relates specifically to one or more of them.

Examples where specific allocation may apply:

- a performance bonus tied to a single deliverable;
- a refund right that relates only to one item;
- a contingent payment triggered by a specific obligation's outcome.

Without a specific link, allocate variable consideration proportionally. Document the reason for any specific allocation.

### Treat Free Items And Vouchers As Material Rights Where Applicable

Free items, vouchers, coupons, and loyalty points can be material rights that are themselves performance obligations. A free item is not automatically zero value. If the customer receives a benefit it would not otherwise receive, allocate a portion of the transaction price to that right and recognize it when exercised or expired.

Ask:

- does the customer receive something it would not otherwise receive?
- is there a discount beyond the range normally given?
- is the option or voucher exercisable without significant additional cost?

If yes, the right is an obligation requiring its own allocation.

### Reassess Allocation When The Bundle Changes

Contract modifications, added obligations, changed prices, and resolved variable consideration all require reassessment of the allocation. A change may be treated as a separate contract, a cumulative catch-up, or a combination, depending on the nature of the additional goods or services and their pricing. Do not carry forward the original allocation when the bundle has materially changed.

### Reconcile Allocated Amounts To The Transaction Price

The sum of amounts allocated to all obligations must equal the transaction price. After allocation, confirm the total ties. A mismatch signals an arithmetic error, a misestimated standalone price, or a discount or variable amount that was not fully allocated.

### Apply Consistency Across Similar Bundles

Use the same allocation approach for similar bundles. Switching between proportional and specific discount allocation, or changing standalone price methods, without justification undermines comparability and signals that the method is chosen to produce a desired result.

### Document The Allocation Reasoning

Record the standalone prices, the discount or variable amount analysis, the evidence supporting any specific allocation, and the reassessment triggers. Documentation is what makes the allocation defensible and revisitable.

## Common Traps

### Allocating Discounts To The Largest Line

Assigning a discount to the obligation with the largest standalone price, or to the one with the most observable price, is only correct when standalone sales evidence supports it. Without evidence, proportional allocation is required. Convenience allocations distort revenue across obligations and periods.

### Treating Free Items As Zero Value

A free item, voucher, or loyalty point can be a material right requiring its own allocation. Recording it at zero ignores a real obligation and overstates revenue for the other items. The question is whether the customer receives a benefit it would not otherwise receive.

### Misallocating Variable Consideration

Variable consideration is not automatically tied to the obligation that triggered it. Unless it relates specifically to one or more obligations, it allocates proportionally. Assigning a performance bonus or refund to a single item without a specific link misstates the allocation.

### Failing To Reassess After Modifications

When a bundle changes through modification, added scope, or resolved contingencies, the original allocation may no longer hold. Carrying it forward produces cumulative errors across the remaining obligations.

### Skipping The Reconciliation

If the allocated amounts do not sum to the transaction price, something is wrong. Forcing the entries without reconciling hides an arithmetic error, a misestimated price, or an unallocated discount.

### Using Inconsistent Methods Across Similar Bundles

Switching allocation methods for similar bundles, without justification, signals that the method is chosen to produce a desired allocation. Consistency is required unless a documented reason justifies a change.

### Assuming Bundle Price Equals Sum Of Components

The bundle price is the amount to allocate, not the sum of standalone prices. The discount, if any, is the difference and must be allocated. Treating the bundle as if each component sold at its standalone price ignores the discount entirely.

### Overlooking Material Rights In Promotions

Promotional vouchers, renewal discounts, and loyalty rewards can be material rights even when they look like marketing. Overlooking them misses an obligation and misallocates the transaction price.

## Self-Check

- Is the transaction price allocated across obligations on a relative standalone selling price basis, with each standalone price observed or estimated by a documented method?
- Are discounts allocated proportionally unless observable standalone sales evidence supports assignment to specific obligations?
- Has the evidence for any specific discount allocation been documented, distinguishing genuine standalone sales evidence from convenience?
- Is variable consideration allocated proportionally unless it relates specifically to one or more obligations, with any specific allocation documented?
- Have free items, vouchers, coupons, and loyalty points been evaluated as material rights requiring their own allocation where the customer receives a benefit it would not otherwise receive?
- Has the allocation been reassessed when the bundle changed through modification, added scope, or resolved contingencies?
- Do the allocated amounts reconcile exactly to the transaction price, with any difference investigated rather than plugged?
- Is the same allocation approach applied consistently to similar bundles, with any change documented and justified?
- Are the standalone prices, discount analysis, variable consideration analysis, and reassessment triggers documented so the allocation is auditable?
- Have material or borderline allocation judgments been flagged for review by a qualified accounting professional familiar with the entity's framework and pricing data?
