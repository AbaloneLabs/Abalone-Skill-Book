# Licensing And Royalty Revenue

Licensing intellectual property is a distinct revenue problem because the promise in a license can take two fundamentally different forms. A right-to-use license grants the customer the ability to use the IP as it exists at a point in time, and revenue is recognized when control of that license transfers. A right-to-access license grants the customer access to the entity's IP as it exists throughout the license period, including future updates and enhancements, and revenue is recognized over time. Getting this distinction wrong moves revenue between a single moment and an entire period. On top of this, royalties tied to sales or usage follow a special exception that delays recognition until the later of the sale occurring or the performance being complete. An agent that applies the default rules to royalties, or that confuses a right-to-use license with a right-to-access license, materially misstates the timing of revenue.

Use this skill before recognizing license or royalty revenue, while distinguishing license types, while applying the royalty exception, while accounting for IP bundled with other goods or services, or while auditing licensing revenue. The goal is to make the agent determine the nature of the license and the royalty terms from substance, and to prevent default recognition rules from being applied where a special exception or a different pattern governs.

Revenue recognition standards vary by jurisdiction and reporting framework. The dominant converged frameworks are US GAAP ASC 606 and IFRS 15, which share the right-to-use versus right-to-access distinction and the sales-based and usage-based royalty exception, but entities may also be subject to industry-specific guidance, local GAAP, tax-basis rules, or sector regulation. Some industries, such as pharmaceuticals, entertainment, and franchising, have additional interpretive guidance. Do not state a single universal rule. Where the license nature or the royalty exception is unclear, route the question to a qualified accountant or auditor. This skill offers operational judgment, not a definitive accounting opinion.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether The License Is Right-To-Use Or Right-To-Access

The first and most important determination is the nature of the license. A right-to-use license lets the customer use the IP as it stands at the point in time the license is granted. A right-to-access license lets the customer use the IP throughout the license period, including changes the entity makes to the IP during that time. The recognition pattern follows directly from this distinction.

A right-to-use license is indicated when:

- the customer obtains the right to use the IP as it exists at grant, with no expectation of changes;
- the IP is static, such as completed software, a finished film, or a finalized design;
- the contract does not promise updates, enhancements, or continued access.

A right-to-access license is indicated when:

- the customer expects the IP to change during the license period and to benefit from those changes;
- the entity is obligated to undertake activities that affect the IP to which the customer has rights;
- the license is to a body of IP that the entity continues to develop, such as a brand, a platform, or ongoing content.

Right-to-use revenue is recognized at the point in time control transfers. Right-to-access revenue is recognized over the license period. This single determination drives the entire recognition pattern.

### Recognize Right-To-Use Licenses At A Point In Time

For a right-to-use license, revenue is recognized at the point in time the customer obtains control of the license, which is typically when the license period begins and the customer can use the IP. The customer receives the benefit of the IP as it exists at that moment.

Confirm point-in-time recognition by checking:

- the customer can direct the use of and obtain the benefits from the IP;
- the license period has begun;
- there is no obligation to provide ongoing changes to the IP;
- the standard point-in-time control indicators support the timing.

Do not spread right-to-use license revenue over the license period unless the license is actually a right-to-access license. Spreading a static license over time defers revenue that was earned at grant.

### Recognize Right-To-Access Licenses Over Time

For a right-to-access license, revenue is recognized over the license period because the customer benefits from the IP as it exists throughout, including the entity's ongoing activities. The measure of progress is typically time elapsed, since the customer receives access ratably.

Confirm over-time recognition by checking:

- the customer has rights to the IP as it changes during the period;
- the entity undertakes activities that affect the IP to which the customer has rights;
- the customer simultaneously receives the benefit over the period.

Recognize over the license term using a method that reflects the transfer of access, generally straight-line over time unless another method better depicts performance.

### Apply The Sales-Based And Usage-Based Royalty Exception

When a license is for a fixed fee, the standard recognition rules apply. But when the consideration for a license is a sales-based or usage-based royalty, a special exception applies: revenue is recognized only when, or as, the later of the following events occurs:

- the later of when the related sale occurs, or when the performance obligation is satisfied.

This means royalty revenue is not estimated and recognized over time based on expected sales. It is recognized only when the underlying sale that generates the royalty actually happens, and only after the license performance obligation itself is satisfied.

Apply the exception when:

- the license is of IP;
- the consideration is a royalty based on sales or usage of the IP.

The exception does not apply to fixed license fees, to non-IP goods or services, or to consideration that is not sales-based or usage-based. Misapplying the exception, either by recognizing estimated royalties early or by deferring fixed fees, distorts revenue.

### Distinguish Fixed From Variable Royalties

The royalty exception applies only to sales-based and usage-based royalties, which are variable. Fixed license fees, even for IP, follow the standard recognition rules based on whether the license is right-to-use or right-to-access. Some licenses combine a fixed minimum with a variable royalty above it; each component is treated according to its nature.

Treat components separately:

- the fixed minimum fee under the standard rules, point in time or over time based on license type;
- the variable sales-based or usage-based royalty under the exception, recognized when the later of sale or performance occurs.

Do not blend a fixed fee and a variable royalty into a single stream that ignores the exception, and do not apply the exception to the fixed portion.

### Account For IP Licenses Bundled With Other Goods And Services

An IP license is frequently bundled with other promises, such as implementation, support, hosting, or additional goods. Each distinct promise is a separate performance obligation, and the transaction price is allocated across all of them. The royalty exception applies only to the IP license component, not to the bundled non-IP services.

Decompose the bundle by:

- identifying the IP license and each other distinct good or service;
- allocating standalone selling price to each;
- recognizing each according to its own pattern and rules;
- applying the royalty exception only to the sales-based or usage-based IP license component.

A support or service component tied to the license is not itself subject to the royalty exception and is recognized under the standard rules.

### Reassess License Type And Royalty Terms When The Contract Changes

The license type and the royalty terms are assessed at inception, but contract modifications, changes in the entity's activities, or changes in how the customer uses the IP can require reassessment. A license that began as right-to-use may effectively become right-to-access if the entity begins providing meaningful updates, and royalty structures may change.

Reassess when:

- the contract is modified to add or remove updates and access;
- the entity's activities toward the IP change materially;
- the royalty structure shifts between fixed and variable.

Treat material changes as modifications and reevaluate the recognition pattern and the application of the royalty exception.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Right-To-Use With Right-To-Access

A static license recognized over time defers revenue improperly, and an access license recognized at a point overstates revenue. The license nature, determined from whether the customer benefits from ongoing changes, drives the pattern.

### Spreading A Right-To-Use License Over The License Period

A right-to-use license is recognized at the point control transfers. Spreading it over time, simply because the license has a term, misapplies the rules.

### Recognizing Estimated Royalties Before The Sale Occurs

The royalty exception requires recognition when, or as, the later of the sale or performance occurs. Estimating and recognizing expected royalties early violates the exception and overstates revenue.

### Applying The Royalty Exception To Fixed Fees Or Non-IP Services

The exception applies only to sales-based or usage-based royalties for IP licenses. Applying it to fixed fees or to bundled services misstates the recognition pattern for those components.

### Blending Fixed Minimums And Variable Royalties

A license with a fixed minimum plus a variable royalty has two components with different rules. Blending them ignores the exception for the variable portion or misapplies it to the fixed portion.

### Treating Bundled Services As Part Of The License

Support, implementation, and hosting bundled with a license are distinct obligations with their own recognition. Treating them as part of the license misallocates price and misapplies the royalty exception.

### Failing To Reassess When Activities Toward The IP Change

A license can shift from right-to-use to right-to-access if the entity begins providing meaningful updates. Failing to reassess keeps the wrong recognition pattern in place.

## Self-Check

- Is the license classified as right-to-use or right-to-access based on whether the customer benefits from the IP as it exists at grant or as it changes over the period?
- Is a right-to-use license recognized at the point in time control transfers, rather than spread over the license period?
- Is a right-to-access license recognized over time using a method that reflects the transfer of access?
- Where consideration is a sales-based or usage-based royalty, is revenue recognized only when, or as, the later of the sale or performance occurs?
- Are fixed license fees recognized under the standard rules, separate from any variable royalty subject to the exception?
- Where a license combines a fixed minimum and a variable royalty, is each component treated according to its nature?
- Are bundled goods and services, such as support, implementation, and hosting, identified as distinct obligations and recognized under their own rules?
- Is the royalty exception applied only to the IP license component and not to non-IP services?
- Are license type and royalty terms reassessed when the contract is modified or the entity's activities toward the IP change materially, and does the analysis recommend qualified professional review where the determination is judgmental?
