# Impairment Of Long-Lived Assets

Impairment testing forces an entity to confront the possibility that an asset's carrying amount exceeds the economic benefits it can still generate. Unlike depreciation, which spreads cost systematically, impairment captures a sudden or gradual loss of value that the depreciation pattern did not anticipate — obsolescence, damage, a declining market, a closed facility. The judgment is substantial: identifying when to test, choosing between fair value less costs of disposal and value in use, estimating future cash flows, and deciding whether to recognize the loss. Agents often defer impairment because it is painful (it reduces profit and equity) or apply it mechanically without testing whether the indicators genuinely exist.

Use this skill before assessing impairment indicators at a reporting date, testing an asset or cash-generating unit after a triggering event, recognizing an impairment loss, classifying an asset as held for sale, or reversing a previously recognized impairment. The goal is to prevent the agent from ignoring impairment indicators, using optimistic cash flow assumptions to avoid a write-down, or recognizing impairment without proper measurement.

Impairment rules differ significantly between US GAAP (ASC 360 for long-lived assets held and used, ASC 280 for held for sale) and IFRS (IAS 36), particularly in the measurement models, the role of cash-generating units, and the reversal of impairment losses. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. Impairment measurement requires significant judgment and often valuation expertise — involve a qualified accountant, valuation specialist, or auditor for material write-downs. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific asset.

## Core Rules

### Assess Impairment Indicators At Each Reporting Date

At each reporting date, assess whether there is any indication that an asset may be impaired. If any indication exists, estimate the recoverable amount. Indicators come from external sources (market decline, technological change, market interest rate increases, market capitalization below net assets) and internal sources (obsolescence or physical damage, asset use or expected use changes, worse-than-expected economic performance, plans to discontinue or restructure).

Under US GAAP, long-lived assets held and used are tested only when events or changes in circumstances indicate the carrying amount may not be recoverable (a two-step model). Under IFRS, the indicator assessment triggers an estimate of recoverable amount. Do not test every asset every period unless required (goodwill and indefinite-life intangibles require annual testing regardless of indicators); test when indicators exist.

### Identify The Correct Unit Of Account: Asset Or Cash-Generating Unit

Test impairment at the lowest level for which independent cash flows can be identified. If the asset generates cash inflows largely independent of other assets, test it individually. If not (most manufacturing equipment, shared facilities), test it as part of a cash-generating unit (IFRS terminology) or asset group (US GAAP terminology) — the smallest group of assets that generates cash inflows largely independent of other groups.

Identifying the cash-generating unit is itself a judgment. It should be consistent over time and based on how management monitors the assets. An incorrectly defined unit (too large or too small) distorts the impairment test. Document the CGU definition and keep it stable across periods.

### Measure Recoverable Amount As The Higher Of Fair Value Less Costs Of Disposal And Value In Use

Recoverable amount is the higher of fair value less costs of disposal (the amount obtainable from selling the asset in an orderly transaction, less disposal costs) and value in use (the present value of future cash flows expected from continued use and eventual disposal). Under US GAAP for held-and-used assets, the test is whether the carrying amount is recoverable from undiscounted future cash flows, and if not, the impairment is measured as the excess of carrying amount over fair value.

Fair value less costs of disposal is often determined by market prices (Level 1) or valuation techniques (Levels 2 and 3). Value in use requires estimating future cash inflows and outflows, applying a discount rate that reflects current market assessments of the time value of money and asset-specific risks. Use the method that gives the higher, more reliable figure.

### Build Value In Use Cash Flow Projections On Reasonable And Supportable Assumptions

Value in use projections should be based on reasonable and supportable assumptions that represent management's best estimate of the economic conditions over the remaining useful life. Cash flow projections are typically for a maximum of five years unless a longer period can be justified. Beyond the explicit forecast period, estimate a terminal value using a stable or declining growth rate that does not exceed the long-term average growth rate of the market.

The discount rate should be a pre-tax rate that reflects current market assessments of the time value of money and the risks specific to the asset for which the future cash flow estimates have not been adjusted. Do not double-count risk by both reducing cash flows for risk and using a risk-adjusted discount rate. Document every key assumption, because auditors and reviewers will challenge optimistic projections used to avoid a write-down.

### Recognize The Impairment Loss Down To Recoverable Amount

If the carrying amount exceeds the recoverable amount, recognize an impairment loss immediately: reduce the carrying amount to recoverable amount and recognize the loss in profit or loss (generally within the same line as the related expense or as a separate impairment line). For a cash-generating unit, allocate the loss to the assets in the unit pro rata, but do not reduce any asset below the higher of its fair value less costs of disposal and value in use — allocate the residual loss to the other assets.

After impairment, the reduced carrying amount becomes the new basis for future depreciation. Revise the useful life and depreciation method if appropriate.

### Account For Held-For-Sale Assets Differently

When an asset (or disposal group) meets the held-for-sale criteria — available for immediate sale in its present condition, the sale is highly probable, and an active program to locate a buyer has begun — it is reclassified as held for sale and measured at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell. No depreciation is taken on held-for-sale assets. If the criteria are not met, do not reclassify; if they cease to be met, reclassify back to held and used and resume depreciation.

The held-for-sale classification is powerful (it stops depreciation and can trigger a write-down) but restrictive (the criteria are specific). Do not classify an asset as held for sale merely because management intends to sell it eventually.

### Reverse Impairment Under IFRS But Not For Held-And-Used Assets Under US GAAP

Under IFRS, an impairment loss recognized in prior periods for an asset other than goodwill must be assessed for reversal at each reporting date. If the indicators that led to the impairment no longer exist or have decreased, the impairment loss is reversed (up to the carrying amount that would have been determined had no impairment been recognized, net of depreciation). Under US GAAP, impairment losses on held-and-used long-lived assets are not reversed. This is a significant difference between the frameworks — confirm which applies.

### Disclose Material Impairment

Disclose the amount of impairment losses recognized and reversed, the events and circumstances leading to them, the method of determining recoverable amount, the discount rate, and the cash-generating unit where relevant. Users rely on this to understand why the asset's value declined and to assess the reliability of the remaining carrying amount.

## Common Traps

### Deferring Or Avoiding Impairment To Protect Profit

Recognizing impairment reduces profit and equity, which creates pressure to defer it. Using optimistic cash flow projections, an inappropriately low discount rate, or an overstated terminal value to keep recoverable amount above carrying amount produces a misstated balance sheet. Build projections on supportable assumptions.

### Using Optimistic Cash Flows To Avoid A Write-Down

Value in use is only as reliable as its assumptions. Growth rates above the market long-term average, overly long explicit forecast periods, or cash flows that ignore expected declines all inflate value in use and avoid a write-down. Challenge every assumption.

### Testing At The Wrong Unit Of Account

Testing a shared asset individually (when it cannot generate independent cash flows) or testing an overly broad asset group distorts the result. Define the cash-generating unit based on independent cash inflows and keep it stable.

### Double-Counting Risk In Cash Flows And Discount Rate

Adjusting cash flows downward for risk and also using a risk-adjusted discount rate double-counts risk and understates value in use. Either adjust cash flows or the discount rate, not both, for the same risk.

### Recognizing Impairment Without Proper Measurement

Writing an asset down to a round number or a management-determined value, rather than to a measured recoverable amount, is not compliant. Measure recoverable amount using fair value less costs of disposal or value in use with documented assumptions.

### Classifying As Held For Sale Without Meeting The Criteria

The held-for-sale criteria are specific (immediate availability, highly probable sale, active program). Classifying an asset as held for sale merely because of a vague intent to sell stops depreciation inappropriately and may trigger an incorrect write-down.

### Reversing Impairment Under US GAAP

Under US GAAP, impairment losses on held-and-used long-lived assets cannot be reversed. Reversing them is incorrect. Under IFRS, reversal is required when indicators improve, but only up to the depreciated carrying amount that would have existed.

## Self-Check

- Have impairment indicators from external and internal sources been assessed at each reporting date, triggering a recoverable amount estimate when they exist?
- Is the asset tested individually if it generates independent cash inflows, or as part of a correctly defined and stable cash-generating unit if it does not?
- Is recoverable amount measured as the higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use, using the more reliable figure?
- Are value in use cash flow projections based on reasonable, supportable assumptions, with a discount rate that reflects asset-specific risks without double-counting?
- Is the impairment loss recognized to reduce carrying amount to recoverable amount, with the loss allocated correctly within a cash-generating unit?
- After impairment, are depreciation, useful life, and method revised on the new carrying amount?
- Are held-for-sale assets measured at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell, with depreciation stopped, only when the specific criteria are met?
- Under IFRS, are prior impairment losses (other than goodwill) assessed for reversal when indicators improve, reversed only up to the depreciated carrying amount?
- Under US GAAP, are impairment losses on held-and-used assets not reversed?
- Are material impairment losses, methods, discount rates, and key assumptions disclosed, and has a qualified accountant, valuation specialist, or auditor reviewed the measurement?
