# Useful Life And Residual Value Estimation

Useful life and residual value are the two estimates that drive depreciation, and both are genuinely uncertain. Useful life is the period over which the asset is expected to be available for use, not the asset's total physical life. Residual value is the estimated amount the entity would currently obtain from disposal, less estimated disposal costs, if the asset were already of the age and condition expected at the end of its useful life. Setting these too long understates depreciation and overstates the asset; setting them too short overstates depreciation and understates the asset. Because they are estimates, they must be reviewed at least annually and revised when circumstances change, with the revision applied prospectively.

Use this skill before setting up a new asset's life and residual, reviewing existing estimates at year-end, responding to changed operating conditions, or deciding whether an asset's life should be shortened or extended. The goal is to prevent the agent from using generic industry averages or the previous year's estimates without testing whether they still reflect the entity's actual expected use and disposal value.

Useful life and residual value rules are broadly consistent between US GAAP (ASC 360) and IFRS (IAS 16), though IFRS requires annual review of both. Tax lives are entirely separate. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For large or unusual assets, or when estimates change materially, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific asset.

## Core Rules

### Estimate Useful Life Based On Expected Use, Not Physical Life

Useful life is the period over which the entity expects to use the asset, which may be shorter than the asset's physical or economic life. It is influenced by: expected usage of the asset (intensity, shifts per day, maintenance level); expected physical wear and tear; technical or commercial obsolescence; legal or similar limits on use (lease term, regulatory licenses); and the entity's asset replacement policy.

An asset the entity plans to replace every five years has a five-year useful life even if it could physically last ten. An asset used in two shifts wears out faster than one used in one shift. An asset subject to rapid technological change has a shorter life than one in a stable technology environment. Document the factors considered for each material asset class, not just a single number.

### Consider All Drivers Of Useful Life

When estimating useful life, weigh: the entity's historical experience with similar assets; industry data and manufacturer specifications; the planned maintenance program; the expected usage pattern; technological trends that may make the asset obsolete; and legal or contractual limits. Do not rely on a single source. A manufacturer's recommended life may be longer than the entity's replacement cycle; industry averages may not reflect the entity's specific intensity of use.

For assets with legal or contractual limits (leasehold improvements, licensed software), the useful life is limited by the legal or contractual term, adjusted for any renewal options that are reasonably certain to be exercised and any shorter economic life.

### Set Residual Value As The Current Disposal Estimate At End Of Life

Residual value is the estimated amount the entity would currently obtain from disposal of the asset, less estimated disposal costs, if the asset were already of the age and condition expected at the end of its useful life. It is not the expected proceeds at the actual disposal date (which is in the future), but the amount obtainable today for an asset of that age and condition.

For many assets, residual value is negligible or zero because an asset at the end of its life has little market value and disposal costs (removal, transport) may exceed gross proceeds. For assets with active secondhand markets (vehicles, certain machinery), residual value may be material. For land, residual value is generally the current fair value because land does not depreciate. Estimate residual value at acquisition and review it at each reporting date.

### Review Useful Life And Residual Value At Least Annually

Under IFRS, residual value and useful life must be reviewed at least at each financial year-end. Under US GAAP, they are reviewed when events or changes in circumstances indicate the current estimates are no longer appropriate. In practice, both require an annual review for material asset classes. If expectations differ from previous estimates, the change is applied prospectively: the carrying amount at the date of revision is depreciated over the revised remaining useful life, down to the revised residual value.

Do not restate prior periods. The prospective application means current and future depreciation changes; past depreciation stands. Disclose the effect of material changes.

### Shorten Useful Life When Obsolescence Or Changed Use Accelerates Consumption

When an asset becomes obsolete faster than expected (new technology, changed product line, reduced demand), shorten the useful life and accelerate depreciation prospectively. This may coincide with an impairment test if the carrying amount exceeds recoverable amount. Shortening the life without testing for impairment may understate the write-down needed.

Signs that useful life should be shortened: the asset is being used less or differently than planned; a replacement is being brought forward; the market for the asset's output has declined; technology has advanced; or the asset has physical damage reducing its service potential.

### Extend Useful Life Only With Supporting Evidence

Extending useful life reduces annual depreciation and increases profit, so it is scrutinized. Extend only when there is clear evidence that the asset will be used longer than originally estimated — a deferred replacement program, a major overhaul that extends service life, or changed operating conditions. Document the evidence. Extending life to manage earnings, without changed circumstances, is inappropriate and will be challenged.

### Apply Residual Value Conservatively For Assets With Uncertain Markets

For assets with thin or volatile secondhand markets, estimate residual value conservatively. A residual value based on a peak-market price may not be realizable at disposal. If residual value is revised upward (because market prices rose), depreciation is reduced prospectively; if revised downward, depreciation is increased. Both are changes in estimate applied prospectively.

### Separate Finite-Life From Indefinite-Life Assets

Fixed assets (property, plant, and equipment) have finite lives and are depreciated. Some assets — land, and certain intangible assets with indefinite lives — are not depreciated or amortized but are tested for impairment annually. Do not depreciate an indefinite-life asset. Confirm the life classification before applying depreciation.

## Common Traps

### Using Generic Industry Averages Without Adjusting For The Entity's Use

Industry averages do not reflect the entity's specific intensity of use, maintenance program, or replacement cycle. An asset used harder or replaced sooner than the industry average needs a shorter life.

### Setting Residual Value At Expected Future Proceeds

Residual value is the amount obtainable today for an asset of the end-of-life age and condition, not the proceeds expected at the future disposal date. Using future expected proceeds overstates residual value and understates depreciation.

### Never Reviewing Or Revising Estimates

Useful life and residual value are estimates that change. Failing to review them annually leaves depreciation based on stale assumptions, overstating or understating the asset and expense.

### Extending Useful Life To Manage Earnings

Extending life reduces depreciation and increases profit. Doing so without genuine evidence of longer use is earnings management and will be challenged by auditors.

### Shortening Life Without Testing For Impairment

When obsolescence shortens useful life, the carrying amount may also exceed recoverable amount. Shortening the life accelerates depreciation but does not capture an immediate impairment write-down that may be required.

### Applying Residual Value Changes Retrospectively

A residual value revision is a change in estimate applied prospectively. Restating prior periods is incorrect.

### Using Tax Lives For Book Depreciation

Tax depreciation lives are set by statute and often differ from the economic useful life. Using tax lives for book purposes misstates the depreciation pattern. Maintain separate schedules.

## Self-Check

- Is useful life estimated based on the entity's expected use, considering usage intensity, wear and tear, obsolescence, legal limits, and replacement policy — not just physical life?
- Are multiple drivers of useful life considered (historical experience, manufacturer specs, maintenance, technology trends, legal limits)?
- Is residual value set as the current disposal estimate for an asset of end-of-life age and condition, less disposal costs, not the expected future proceeds?
- Are useful life and residual value reviewed at least annually (IFRS) or when circumstances change (US GAAP)?
- Is any revision applied prospectively as a change in estimate, with the carrying amount depreciated over the revised remaining life down to the revised residual?
- When useful life is shortened for obsolescence, is an impairment test also performed?
- Is useful life extended only with documented evidence of genuinely longer use, not to manage earnings?
- Are finite-life assets depreciated and indefinite-life assets (land, certain intangibles) excluded from depreciation and tested for impairment?
- Are book and tax lives maintained separately, and have material estimate changes been reviewed by a qualified accountant or auditor?
