# Depreciation Method Selection

Depreciation is the systematic allocation of an asset's depreciable amount over its useful life. The method chosen determines the expense pattern, and the pattern should reflect how the entity consumes the asset's economic benefits. Straight-line produces constant expense; declining-balance produces front-loaded expense; units-of-production ties expense to usage. Selecting a method because it is the default in the software, or because it produces a desired profit number, rather than because it matches consumption, produces a misstated expense pattern and violates the matching principle. The method is an accounting estimate that must be reviewed at least annually.

Use this skill before selecting a depreciation method for a new asset, reviewing whether an existing method is still appropriate, calculating periodic depreciation, or considering a change in method. The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to straight-line for everything or choosing a method to manage earnings, without testing whether the pattern reflects how the asset is consumed.

Depreciation rules are broadly consistent between US GAAP (ASC 360) and IFRS (IAS 16), but the review requirement and the treatment of method changes differ in detail. Tax depreciation is entirely separate and follows statutory schedules. This skill provides the general framework for book depreciation; the applicable standard governs the detail. For large or unusual asset portfolios, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific asset.

## Core Rules

### Match The Method To The Consumption Pattern Of Economic Benefits

The depreciation method should reflect the pattern in which the asset's future economic benefits are expected to be consumed by the entity. If benefits are consumed evenly over time (a building, office furniture), straight-line is appropriate. If benefits are consumed more heavily in early years (vehicles, rapidly obsoleting technology), an accelerated method is appropriate. If benefits are consumed based on usage rather than time (machinery running hours, mine extraction), units-of-production is appropriate.

The test is economic, not administrative. Do not select a method because it is the system default, because it is simplest, or because it smooths earnings. Document the rationale linking the method to the expected consumption pattern, and revisit it when circumstances change.

### Apply Straight-Line When Benefits Are Consumed Evenly

Under straight-line depreciation, the depreciable amount (cost minus residual value) is allocated equally over the useful life. Annual depreciation equals depreciable amount divided by useful life. This produces constant annual expense and a linear decline in carrying amount. It is the most common method and is appropriate for assets whose benefits decline roughly evenly — buildings, fixtures, and equipment with steady usage.

The simplicity of straight-line makes it a reasonable default, but it should be chosen because the consumption pattern is even, not because it is easy. For assets that lose value faster early, straight-line understates early expense and overstates late expense.

### Apply Accelerated Methods When Benefits Are Front-Loaded

Declining-balance and sum-of-the-years'-digits methods produce higher depreciation in early years and lower depreciation in later years. Under declining-balance, a constant percentage is applied to the declining carrying amount (often double the straight-line rate, hence double-declining-balance). The method switches to straight-line on the remaining balance at some point, or the asset is depreciated down to residual value in the final periods.

Accelerated methods are appropriate for assets that lose economic benefit rapidly — vehicles, computers, and equipment subject to fast technological change. The pattern matches the reality that the asset is most productive and most valuable early and becomes less so as it ages. Under IFRS, declining-balance is acceptable; under US GAAP, declining-balance and sum-of-the-years'-digits are both acceptable. Switching from declining-balance to straight-line on the remaining balance is a computational refinement, not a change in accounting policy.

### Apply Units-Of-Production When Consumption Tracks Usage

Under units-of-production depreciation, the depreciable amount is allocated based on actual usage — machine hours, units produced, miles driven, or tons extracted. Depreciation per unit equals depreciable amount divided by total estimated production. Periodic depreciation equals the per-unit rate times actual production in the period. When no production occurs, no depreciation is recorded (though an impairment or idle-capacity review may be needed).

This method is appropriate for machinery whose wear correlates with usage, vehicles whose value declines with mileage, and extractive assets whose depletion tracks output. It requires a reliable estimate of total lifetime production, which is itself a judgment. If the estimate is revised, the remaining depreciable amount is spread over the revised remaining production prospectively.

### Depreciate Down To Residual Value, Not Below

Depreciation must not reduce the carrying amount below the residual value. Under declining-balance, ensure the method does not depreciate below residual — adjust the final periods or switch to straight-line on the gap between carrying amount and residual. The residual value is reviewed at each reporting date; if it changes, the depreciation is adjusted prospectively.

### Treat A Change In Method As A Change In Accounting Estimate

A change in depreciation method is generally treated as a change in accounting estimate (prospectively), not a change in accounting policy (retrospectively), under both IFRS and US GAAP. The remaining depreciable amount at the date of change is depreciated over the remaining useful life using the new method. Disclose the nature and effect of the change.

However, changing the method must be justified by a change in the expected consumption pattern or new information, not by a desire to manage earnings. A method change that lacks an economic rationale is a red flag for auditors and may be challenged.

### Review The Method At Least Annually

Under IFRS, the depreciation method must be reviewed at least at each annual reporting date, and changed if the pattern of consumption has changed. Under US GAAP, useful lives and methods are reviewed when events or changes in circumstances suggest they are no longer appropriate. A shift from straight-line to units-of-production (because the entity began measuring usage), or from accelerated to straight-line (because the asset stabilized), is appropriate when supported by changed circumstances.

### Maintain A Depreciation Schedule Per Asset

Each asset's register record should show: cost, residual value, useful life, depreciation method, accumulated depreciation, carrying amount, and the current-period depreciation. The schedule must reconcile to the general ledger. For units-of-production, track actual production against the total estimate. For declining-balance, track the rate and the switch point.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Straight-Line For Every Asset

Straight-line is appropriate only when benefits are consumed evenly. Applying it to assets that lose value rapidly understates early expense and overstates early profit. Test the consumption pattern, not just the system default.

### Choosing A Method To Smooth Or Manage Earnings

Selecting or changing a method to hit a profit target violates the matching principle and the requirement that the method reflect consumption. A method change without an economic rationale is an audit red flag and may need to be reversed.

### Depreciating Below Residual Value

Declining-balance methods can mechanically reduce carrying amount below residual value. The method must be adjusted so depreciation stops at residual. Depreciating below residual understates the asset and overstates expense.

### Failing To Switch From Declining-Balance To Straight-Line

Pure declining-balance leaves a large residual balance that is never fully depreciated. Switching to straight-line on the remaining balance (the optimal switch point) produces a smoother and more accurate expense pattern in the later years.

### Treating A Method Change As Retrospective

A change in depreciation method is generally prospective, not retrospective. Restating prior periods is incorrect and overstates the complexity of the change.

### Using Book Depreciation For Tax Purposes

Book depreciation follows the consumption pattern; tax depreciation follows statutory schedules (such as MACRS in the US or capital allowances in other jurisdictions). Using the book method for tax, or the tax method for book, produces incorrect figures in both. Keep separate schedules.

### Not Reviewing The Method When Circumstances Change

An asset moved from steady use to heavy use, or from active production to standby, may need a method change. Failing to review and adjust produces a depreciation pattern that no longer matches consumption.

## Self-Check

- Does the selected depreciation method reflect the expected pattern in which the asset's economic benefits are consumed (even, front-loaded, or usage-based)?
- Is straight-line used only where benefits are consumed evenly, and accelerated or units-of-production used where the pattern differs?
- For declining-balance, is the method adjusted so depreciation does not reduce carrying amount below residual value, with a switch to straight-line where appropriate?
- For units-of-production, is there a reliable estimate of total lifetime production, with prospective adjustment when the estimate changes?
- Is any change in depreciation method treated prospectively as a change in estimate, with disclosure of the nature and effect?
- Is the method reviewed at least annually (IFRS) or when circumstances change (US GAAP), with changes supported by changed consumption patterns?
- Does each asset's depreciation schedule reconcile to the general ledger, with cost, residual, life, method, accumulated depreciation, and carrying amount tracked?
- Are book and tax depreciation maintained on separate schedules?
- Has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed method selection for large or unusual assets and any method changes?
