# Component Accounting And Major Maintenance

Large fixed assets are rarely single monolithic units. A building has a roof, an HVAC system, and a structural shell with very different useful lives. An aircraft has engines, airframe, and landing gear that are replaced on different cycles. A manufacturing line has major sub-assemblies that are overhauled periodically. Treating the whole asset as one depreciation unit produces distorted expense: the asset's carrying amount is depreciated over an average life, and when a major component is replaced the accounting either double-counts the new part or fails to remove the old one. Component accounting forces the entity to break the asset into parts that are depreciated and replaced on their own economic schedules.

Use this skill before recording a major overhaul or inspection, replacing a significant part of an asset, setting up a complex asset with parts of different lives, or deciding the accounting treatment of a recurring maintenance program. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating every major repair as a simple expense or every replacement as an addition to the whole asset, when the correct treatment depends on whether the part is a separate component and whether it enhances the asset.

Component accounting is required under IFRS (IAS 16) and permitted but less prescriptive under US GAAP (ASC 360). The treatment of major inspections differs between the two frameworks. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For assets with many components or unusual overhaul cycles, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific asset.

## Core Rules

### Identify Components Based On Different Useful Lives Or Benefit Patterns

Under IFRS, an entity must split the cost of an item of property, plant, and equipment into its significant parts (components) when those parts have different useful lives or provide economic benefits on a different pattern. Each component is depreciated separately over its own useful life. A component does not need to be physically separable; it needs to have a distinguishable economic life. Typical components include a building roof, HVAC, elevators, and structural shell; an aircraft's engines, airframe, and interior; a ship's hull and engine.

Under US GAAP, componentization is permitted but not required; many entities depreciate the whole asset over one life. However, even under US GAAP, when a major part is replaced the accounting must avoid double-counting, and many entities adopt component tracking for large assets as a practical matter.

### Set Up Each Component With Its Own Cost, Life, And Method

When an asset is acquired or constructed, allocate the total cost to each significant component using a reasonable basis (relative fair value, engineering estimates, or appraisal). Each component then has its own useful life, residual value, and depreciation method in the fixed-asset register. The remaining cost not allocated to specific components is depreciated over the life of the main asset.

This produces more accurate expense recognition: a roof with a 20-year life is depreciated over 20 years while the building shell depreciates over 50 years, rather than averaging everything over 40 years. The depreciation schedule must reflect each component separately.

### Derecognize The Replaced Component When A Part Is Replaced

When a significant component is replaced, the carrying amount of the old component must be derecognized (removed from the register) and any gain or loss on disposal recognized. The new component is then capitalized and depreciated over its own useful life. This is required under IFRS and is the correct conceptual treatment under US GAAP.

The practical challenge is that the entity may not track the old component's carrying amount separately. Under IFRS, if the entity does not track components, it must estimate the carrying amount of the replaced component — typically as the cost of the replacement at current prices, adjusted for the difference between current and historical cost, or by using a deemed cost approach. Failing to remove the old component double-counts the asset and overstates depreciation.

### Capitalize Major Inspection Costs As A Separate Component

Under IFRS, the cost of a major inspection or overhaul may be capitalized as a separate component if the inspection is a condition for continuing to operate the asset and the entity can identify the cost of the previous inspection. When the next major inspection occurs, the carrying amount of the previous inspection component is derecognized and replaced with the new inspection cost. This spreads the cost of each major inspection over the interval until the next one.

Under US GAAP, major maintenance costs are generally expensed as incurred unless they meet the betterment criteria, and the carrying amount of any undepreciated prior overhaul is not typically tracked as a separate component. Some industries (airlines, utilities) have specific industry practice. Determine the applicable standard's treatment before recording.

### Distinguish A Capitalized Component From An Expensed Repair

Not every maintenance event creates a capitalized component. Routine repairs, minor part replacements, cleaning, lubrication, and periodic servicing that merely keep the asset in working order are expensed as incurred. A cost is capitalized as a component (or betterment) only when it enhances the asset beyond its original condition, adds a new significant part, or constitutes a major inspection that is a condition for continued operation.

The decision framework: Does the expenditure add a new component with its own economic life? Capitalize it and depreciate separately. Does it replace an existing significant component? Derecognize the old, capitalize the new. Does it restore the asset to operating condition after a major inspection cycle? Capitalize as an inspection component under IFRS, expense under typical US GAAP unless it is a betterment. Does it merely maintain the asset? Expense it.

### Document The Component Structure And Replacement Cycle

For each complex asset, document the component breakdown: which parts are tracked as separate components, their cost allocation, useful lives, residual values, and expected replacement cycles. This documentation supports the derecognition estimates when a component is replaced and provides the audit trail for the inspection-component accounting. Without it, the entity cannot reliably estimate the carrying amount of a replaced component.

### Reconcile Component Schedules To The Ledger

The sum of all component carrying amounts plus the residual main-asset carrying amount must equal the total fixed-asset balance in the general ledger. Reconcile each period. Component derecognition, additions, and depreciation must all flow through the ledger correctly.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Whole Asset As One Depreciation Unit When Components Have Different Lives

Depreciating a building with a 20-year roof and a 50-year shell over a single 40-year life misstates expense in every period. The roof is under-depreciated early and over-depreciated late, and the shell is depreciated too fast.

### Capitalizing A Replacement Without Derecognizing The Old Component

Adding the cost of a new roof or new engine to the asset without removing the carrying amount of the old one double-counts the asset. The balance sheet is overstated and depreciation is wrong until the error is found.

### Estimating The Old Component's Carrying Amount As Zero

When the entity does not track components, it must estimate the replaced component's carrying amount. Setting it to zero (so the entire replacement is a gain-free addition) overstates the asset. Use a reasonable estimate based on the replacement cost and the depreciation that would have been taken.

### Capitalizing Every Major Repair As A Betterment

A major repair that merely restores the asset to its original operating condition is not a betterment. Capitalizing it defers expense and overstates the asset. Test against the enhancement criteria, not the dollar size of the invoice.

### Applying IFRS Inspection-Component Treatment Under US GAAP

Capitalizing major inspection costs as a separate component is an IFRS approach. Applying it under US GAAP without meeting the betterment criteria overstates the asset. Confirm the applicable standard before recording.

### Failing To Track The Prior Inspection Component For Derecognition

Under IFRS, the inspection component is derecognized at the next inspection. If the entity cannot identify the cost of the prior inspection, it cannot reliably capitalize the new one. Maintain the inspection-component record from the first capitalized inspection.

### Losing The Component Detail In A System Migration

When fixed-asset data moves between systems, component breakdowns are often collapsed into a single asset record. This destroys the ability to derecognize components correctly and produces audit findings. Preserve component detail in any migration.

## Self-Check

- Has each significant part of a complex asset been identified as a component where it has a different useful life or benefit pattern?
- Is the total cost allocated to components on a reasonable basis, with each component depreciated over its own life?
- When a component is replaced, is the carrying amount of the old component derecognized (using a reasonable estimate if not separately tracked) and the new component capitalized?
- Are major inspection costs treated correctly under the applicable standard (capitalized as a component under IFRS with prior inspection derecognized; expensed or capitalized under US GAAP based on betterment criteria)?
- Is each maintenance expenditure tested against the enhancement and component criteria rather than capitalized based on dollar size alone?
- Is the component structure documented with cost allocation, lives, residual values, and replacement cycles for each complex asset?
- Does the sum of component carrying amounts reconcile to the general ledger fixed-asset balance each period?
- Under IFRS, can the entity identify the cost of the previous inspection to support derecognition at the next inspection?
- Has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed the component estimates, derecognition methodology, and inspection-component treatment?
