# Work In Process And Finished Goods

Manufacturing inventory moves through stages: raw materials become work in process, work in process becomes finished goods, and finished goods become cost of goods sold. Each stage has its own valuation, reconciliation, and cutoff challenges. Work in process is especially difficult because it captures partially completed units whose cost depends on an estimate of completion. Finished goods looks simpler but carries its own risks around valuation, obsolescence, and lower of cost or net realizable value. When these balances are wrong, both the balance sheet and the income statement are wrong, often invisibly until an audit or a write-off exposes the gap.

Use this skill before valuing work in process, recording production completions, reconciling manufacturing subledger to ledger, testing inventory for obsolescence or net realizable value, or closing manufacturing accounts at period end. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing inventory balances that reconcile internally but misstate the stage, completion, or recoverability of the underlying goods.

## Core Rules

### Value Work In Process Using A Defensible Completion Method

Work in process valuation requires estimating how complete the units in process are. The method chosen drives the cost assigned and must be applied consistently.

Choose and document:

- the costing method, such as weighted average, FIFO, or standard cost;
- the equivalent unit calculation for materials added at the start versus conversion costs added throughout;
- the stage at which materials are introduced;
- the treatment of beginning and ending work in process using a method such as weighted average or FIFO equivalent units;
- the normal loss and abnormal loss treatment consistent with the applicable framework.

Estimating completion is inherently judgmental. Use production data, routing progress, or physical observation where possible, and document the basis. Do not default to a round percentage without evidence.

### Record Material And Conversion Costs To The Correct Stage

Costs must be recorded in the inventory stage where they are consumed. Misclassifying a cost between raw materials, work in process, and finished goods misstates each balance.

Verify:

- material issues move raw material to work in process at the correct cost;
- labor and overhead are absorbed into work in process as operations are performed;
- completed production transfers work in process to finished goods at the full standard or actual cost;
- subcontracted operations are capitalized into the correct stage;
- variances are posted to the appropriate accounts.

Costs left in work in process that should have moved to finished goods, or vice versa, distort both balances and margin.

### Reconcile The Manufacturing Subledger To The General Ledger

The production system and the general ledger must agree. A manufacturing subledger that does not tie to the ledger produces inventory balances no one can trust.

Reconcile each period:

- raw material, work in process, and finished goods subledger totals to ledger control accounts;
- production order costs to work in process additions;
- shipment and issue activity to reductions;
- variance accounts to production activity;
- interplant or intercompany movements.

Investigate reconciling items promptly. Persistent small differences accumulate into material misstatements.

### Apply Cutoff Discipline At Period End

Period end is where manufacturing inventory is most often misstated. Goods in transit between stages, between plants, or to customers must be recorded in the correct period.

Apply cutoff checks:

- goods received into raw materials are recorded in the period of receipt;
- production completed before period end is transferred to finished goods;
- goods shipped before period end are recorded as cost of goods sold;
- goods in transit between locations are recorded in the receiving location when title passes;
- subcontracted goods in transit are correctly capitalized.

Cutoff errors shift cost between periods and distort margin, especially for high-value or high-volume items.

### Test Finished Goods For Recoverability

Finished goods must be carried at the lower of cost or net realizable value. Holding obsolete, slow-moving, or impaired inventory at full cost overstates assets and profit.

Assess:

- aging of finished goods by item;
- slow-moving and obsolete stock identified and reserved;
- net realizable value compared to cost for items at risk;
- damaged, returned, or condemned stock written down;
- the basis for any net realizable value estimate, including selling price, cost to complete, and cost to sell.

Document the obsolescence and net realizable value policy and apply it consistently. Reserves that are never adjusted, or adjusted only in good years, misstate inventory.

### Handle Scrap, Rework, And By-Products Consistently

Manufacturing generates scrap, rework, and by-products. Their accounting treatment must be consistent and framework-compliant.

Address:

- normal scrap absorbed in product cost or accounted for at the point of generation;
- abnormal scrap or spoilage expensed as a period cost under most frameworks;
- rework cost capitalized or expensed consistent with policy;
- by-product net realizable value credited to cost of production or recognized as other income.

Inconsistent treatment period to period distorts margin and comparability.

### Respect Framework And Jurisdictional Limits

Inventory valuation is heavily governed by framework and jurisdiction. What costs are inventoriable, how overhead is absorbed, how net realizable value is measured, how scrap and idle capacity are treated, and whether methods such as LIFO are permitted all depend on the applicable GAAP, IFRS, tax, or regulatory standard. The rules differ materially between frameworks and between jurisdictions. Confirm the valuation, reserve, and disclosure approach against the relevant standard before relying on the balances externally, and escalate uncertain or material treatments to a qualified professional.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting Completion To A Round Percentage

Estimating work in process completion as a fixed percentage without production evidence produces arbitrary valuations that cannot be defended.

### Leaving Costs In The Wrong Stage

Costs that belong in finished goods but sit in work in process, or vice versa, misstate both balances and the period margin.

### Failing To Reconcile Each Period

Small unreconciled differences accumulate. A subledger that drifts from the ledger eventually requires a large, embarrassing adjustment.

### Weak Cutoff At Period End

Goods in transit, late production completions, and early shipments are common cutoff errors that shift cost between periods.

### Ignoring Obsolescence Until Forced

Carrying finished goods at full cost when they are obsolete or impaired overstates inventory and profit. Reserves must be assessed regularly, not only when convenient.

### Inconsistent Scrap And Rework Treatment

Changing the treatment of scrap, rework, or by-products period to period, without disclosure, distorts comparability.

### Assuming Inventory Cost Is Framework-Neutral

Inventory rules differ by framework and jurisdiction. Applying one set of assumptions to all reporting contexts can create material errors.

## Self-Check

- Is work in process valued using a documented completion method with equivalent units calculated for materials and conversion costs?
- Are material, labor, overhead, subcontract, and variance costs recorded in the correct inventory stage?
- Does the manufacturing subledger reconcile to the general ledger for raw material, work in process, and finished goods each period?
- Are period-end cutoffs applied to receipts, production completions, shipments, and goods in transit?
- Is finished goods tested for obsolescence and lower of cost or net realizable value using a documented and consistently applied policy?
- Are scrap, rework, and by-products accounted for consistently and in line with the applicable framework?
- Are reconciling items investigated promptly rather than allowed to accumulate?
- Does the valuation acknowledge framework and jurisdictional limits, with material or uncertain treatments escalated to a qualified professional?
