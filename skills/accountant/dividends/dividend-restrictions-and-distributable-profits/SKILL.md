# Dividend Restrictions And Distributable Profits

The ability to pay dividends is constrained by legal capital requirements, solvency tests, statutory reserves, contractual covenants, and the availability of distributable reserves. Confirming dividend capacity is not simply checking the cash balance; it requires assessing multiple layers of restriction and the composition of equity. Errors here can result in unlawful distributions, covenant breaches, or misleading disclosures about the entity's ability to return capital to shareholders.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during dividend restriction and distributable profit assessment. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist legal advice. Confirm the applicable jurisdictional rules and consult a qualified professional for complex capital structures or cross-border entities.

## Core Rules

### Determine The Legal Framework Governing Distributions

The rules governing distributions vary by jurisdiction and by the entity's legal form. Identify the applicable legal framework first.

- In incorporation-based jurisdictions, such as Delaware in the United States, distributions are generally governed by a solvency test and a surplus test. The entity must be solvent and must not have a deficit in surplus beyond stated capital.
- In some civil law jurisdictions, distributions are limited to distributable reserves, which may include retained earnings less statutory reserves and other restrictions.
- In jurisdictions with a balance sheet test, distributions are limited to the excess of assets over liabilities and legal capital, with additional solvency requirements.
- Confirm the specific rules of the jurisdiction of incorporation, including any requirements for director certification or shareholder approval.

### Apply The Legal Capital And Surplus Test

Legal capital and surplus tests determine the maximum distributable amount.

- Legal capital typically comprises the par value of issued shares, plus share premium in some jurisdictions. Distributions cannot reduce equity below legal capital.
- In Delaware-style jurisdictions, the surplus is the excess of net assets over the statutory capital. Distributions can be made from surplus, subject to solvency.
- Confirm whether the jurisdiction uses par value, stated capital, or a different measure of legal capital, and calculate the surplus accordingly.
- For entities with no surplus due to accumulated losses, distributions are prohibited until surplus is restored.

### Apply The Solvency Test

Many jurisdictions require a solvency test in addition to the surplus test.

- The solvency test typically requires that the entity can pay its debts as they become due in the usual course of business (equity solvency or cash flow solvency), and that the entity's assets exceed its liabilities (balance sheet solvency).
- Directors may be required to certify solvency, and may face personal liability for unlawful distributions if the entity is insolvent at the time of distribution or becomes insolvent as a result.
- Assess solvency using cash flow forecasts and balance sheet data, not just historical financials.

### Identify And Apply Statutory Reserve Requirements

Some jurisdictions require transfers to statutory reserves that reduce distributable profits.

- In jurisdictions such as China, France, and other civil law countries, a percentage of profit must be transferred to a statutory reserve until it reaches a threshold, typically a percentage of registered capital.
- The statutory reserve is not distributable until the legal conditions for release are met.
- Deduct the statutory reserve from distributable profits when calculating dividend capacity.
- Confirm the specific requirements of the jurisdiction, as thresholds and percentages vary.

### Evaluate Contractual And Covenant Restrictions

Debt agreements, loan covenants, and other contracts may restrict dividends beyond legal requirements.

- Review all debt agreements for dividend restrictions, including covenants on minimum net worth, maximum debt-to-equity ratios, working capital requirements, and restrictions on distributions while debt is outstanding.
- Some covenants restrict distributions to a formula based on earnings or a restricted payment basket.
- Breaching a covenant by paying a dividend can trigger a default, accelerating debt repayment.
- Disclose the nature and amount of contractual restrictions on dividends.

### Measure Distributable Reserves

Distributable reserves are the portion of equity available for distribution after legal, statutory, and contractual restrictions.

- Start with retained earnings, which generally represent accumulated distributable profits.
- Deduct statutory reserves that are not distributable.
- Deduct or exclude revaluation reserves, which are generally not distributable until realized through use or disposal.
- Deduct or exclude translation reserves, which are recycled only on disposal of the foreign operation.
- Consider any other restrictions, such as committed but unpaid capital expenditures or contingent obligations.

### Handle Negative Equity Or Accumulated Deficits

Entities with accumulated deficits or negative equity face strict limitations on distributions.

- An accumulated deficit in retained earnings means there are no distributable earnings from which to pay dividends, unless the jurisdiction allows capitalization of reserves or a capital reduction to eliminate the deficit.
- Some jurisdictions allow a capital reduction to eliminate accumulated deficits, creating distributable capacity, subject to court approval or solvency tests.
- Negative total equity generally prohibits distributions entirely under solvency-based tests.
- Disclose the accumulated deficit and the restrictions on distributions.

### Account For Interim Dividends And Final Dividends

The treatment of interim and final dividends differs by framework.

- Under IFRS, dividends declared after the reporting period are non-adjusting subsequent events, disclosed but not recognized as a liability. Interim dividends declared and paid during the period are recognized.
- Under US GAAP, dividends declared after the reporting period are also non-adjusting subsequent events.
- Confirm the framework's treatment of dividends declared between the balance sheet date and the issuance date.

### Disclose Dividend Restrictions Adequately

Disclose all restrictions on dividends, including legal, statutory, contractual, and regulatory restrictions.

- Disclose the nature and amount of statutory reserve requirements.
- Disclose contractual restrictions from debt agreements and other contracts, including the covenants and the headroom.
- Disclose the distributable reserves and the accumulated deficit, if any.
- Disclose any regulatory restrictions for regulated entities, such as insurance or banking entities.
- Disclose dividends declared after the reporting date as subsequent events.

## Common Traps

### Paying Dividends Based On Cash Without Checking Distributable Reserves

Cash availability does not equal distributable capacity. Paying dividends without confirming distributable reserves can result in unlawful distributions.

### Ignoring Covenant Restrictions

Debt covenants often restrict dividends more tightly than legal requirements. Paying a dividend that breaches a covenant triggers a default, even if the distribution is legally permissible.

### Distributing Revaluation Or Translation Reserves

Revaluation and translation reserves are generally not distributable until realized. Distributing them violates the restrictions and misstates distributable capacity.

### Not Applying Statutory Reserve Requirements

In jurisdictions with statutory reserves, failing to deduct the reserve from distributable profits overstates dividend capacity and may result in unlawful distributions.

### Overlooking Solvency Test Requirements

Even when surplus exists, the solvency test must be met. Paying a dividend that renders the entity insolvent as to the solvency test exposes directors to liability.

### Not Disclosing Restrictions

Legal, statutory, contractual, and regulatory restrictions must be disclosed. Omitting the disclosures hides the entity's true dividend capacity from users.

### Treating Subsequent Event Dividends As Liabilities

Dividends declared after the reporting period are non-adjusting subsequent events. Recognizing them as liabilities at the balance sheet date overstates liabilities.

### Failing To Assess Distributable Capacity In Negative Equity

Entities with accumulated deficits or negative equity face strict distribution limits. Assuming distributions are permissible without addressing the deficit can result in unlawful distributions.

## Self-Check

- Has the legal framework governing distributions been identified for the jurisdiction of incorporation, including legal capital, surplus, and solvency tests?
- Is the legal capital and surplus calculated correctly, and does the proposed distribution comply with the surplus test?
- Has the solvency test been assessed using cash flow forecasts and balance sheet data, with director certification if required?
- Are statutory reserve requirements identified and deducted from distributable profits?
- Have all debt covenants and contractual restrictions been reviewed, and does the proposed distribution comply with them?
- Are distributable reserves measured correctly, excluding revaluation and translation reserves that are not distributable?
- For entities with accumulated deficits or negative equity, has the restriction on distributions been assessed and disclosed?
- Are interim and final dividends accounted for correctly, with subsequent event dividends disclosed but not recognized as liabilities?
- Are disclosures complete, including statutory reserves, contractual restrictions, distributable reserves, accumulated deficits, regulatory restrictions, and subsequent event dividends?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction been confirmed, and has specialist legal advice been obtained for complex capital structures or cross-border distribution rules?
