# Dividend Declaration And Payment

Dividends are distributions of earnings to shareholders. The accounting involves recognizing a liability at declaration, reducing equity, and settling the liability at payment. While the mechanics are straightforward, the judgments lie in distinguishing dividends from returns of capital, handling preferred dividends and accumulation, and confirming that distributions comply with legal and contractual restrictions. Errors misstate equity, liabilities, and the entity's distributable capacity.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during dividend declaration and payment accounting. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist legal advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 505, IFRS IAS 10) and consult a qualified professional for complex capital structures or cross-border distributions.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Liability At The Declaration Date

A dividend liability is recognized when the dividend is declared by the appropriate authority.

- At the declaration date, the board of directors or equivalent authority formally declares the dividend, creating a legal obligation. Recognize dividends payable and reduce retained earnings.
- Between the declaration date and the payment date, the dividends payable is a current liability on the balance sheet.
- At the payment date, settle the liability by reducing cash and eliminating dividends payable.
- The record date determines which shareholders receive the dividend but does not affect the accounting. The ex-dividend date is the date on which the shares trade without the right to the dividend.

### Distinguish Dividends On Common And Preferred Stock

Different classes of stock have different dividend rights.

- Common stock dividends are declared by the board and paid pro rata to common shareholders.
- Preferred stock dividends may be cumulative or non-cumulative, participating or non-participating, and may have a fixed or floating rate.
- Deduct preferred dividends from net income when calculating earnings available to common shareholders for EPS, whether or not declared for cumulative preferred, and only if declared for non-cumulative.

### Account For Cumulative Preferred Dividends

Cumulative preferred dividends accumulate if not declared.

- For cumulative preferred stock, the dividend accumulates each period whether or not declared. Deduct the periodic dividend from earnings available to common shareholders for EPS, even if not declared.
- Disclose cumulative preferred dividends in arrears, the accumulated undeclared dividends, in the notes, even though they are not recognized as a liability until declared.
- Do not recognize dividends payable for undeclared cumulative preferred dividends. They are disclosed, not accrued.

### Account For Non-Cumulative Preferred Dividends

Non-cumulative preferred dividends do not accumulate if not declared.

- For non-cumulative preferred stock, the dividend is earned only if declared. If not declared in a period, the right to that dividend is lost.
- Deduct non-cumulative preferred dividends from earnings available to common shareholders for EPS only if declared.
- Do not disclose dividends in arrears for non-cumulative preferred, because they do not accumulate.

### Confirm Compliance With Legal And Contractual Restrictions

Distributions must comply with legal capital requirements, statutory restrictions, and contractual covenants.

- Confirm that the entity has sufficient distributable reserves to pay the dividend. Legal capital requirements vary by jurisdiction and may restrict distributions to retained earnings or a solvency test.
- Check debt covenants and contractual restrictions that may limit or prohibit dividend payments. Breaching a covenant can trigger a default.
- For regulated entities, confirm compliance with regulatory capital requirements before declaring dividends.
- Disclose any restrictions on the ability to pay dividends, including the nature and amount of the restrictions.

### Distinguish Dividends From Returns Of Capital

The classification of a distribution as a dividend or a return of capital affects the accounting and the tax treatment.

- A dividend is a distribution of earnings. It reduces retained earnings.
- A return of capital is a distribution of paid-in capital. It reduces additional paid-in capital or the stated capital account.
- Some jurisdictions classify distributions based on source: distributions from earnings are dividends, and distributions in excess of earnings are returns of capital.
- Confirm the legal and accounting classification of the distribution based on the jurisdiction's rules and the entity's distributable reserves.

### Handle Property And Scrip Dividends

Dividends can be paid in forms other than cash.

- For a property dividend, distribute assets other than cash. Recognize the asset at fair value, with a gain or loss on the difference between the fair value and the carrying amount of the asset distributed, and then recognize the dividend payable at fair value.
- For a scrip dividend, issue a promissory note or additional shares in lieu of cash. If a promissory note is issued, recognize a liability and accrue interest if the note bears interest. If shares are issued, account for it as a stock dividend.
- Confirm the fair value of the distributed assets and the terms of the scrip.

### Account For Liquidating Dividends

A liquidating dividend is a return of capital, not a distribution of earnings.

- Liquidating dividends occur when an entity distributes more than its accumulated earnings, returning paid-in capital to shareholders.
- Reduce additional paid-in capital or stated capital, not retained earnings.
- Disclose that the distribution is a return of capital, as the tax treatment differs from ordinary dividends.

### Disclose Dividends Adequately

Disclose the dividends declared per share, the total amount, the dividends payable at the balance sheet date, and any restrictions.

- Disclose the dividends declared per share for each class of stock, the aggregate amount, and the dividends payable at the reporting date.
- Disclose cumulative preferred dividends in arrears.
- Disclose any restrictions on dividends, including debt covenants, legal capital requirements, and regulatory restrictions.
- Disclose subsequent events, including dividends declared after the balance sheet date but before the financial statements are issued, which are non-adjusting and disclosed.

## Common Traps

### Recognizing Dividends Payable Before Declaration

A dividend liability exists only after the board declares the dividend. Recognizing dividends payable before declaration overstates liabilities and understates equity.

### Accruing Undeclared Cumulative Preferred Dividends As A Liability

Cumulative preferred dividends in arrears are disclosed, not recognized as a liability, until declared. Accruing them as a liability overstates liabilities.

### Not Deducting Cumulative Preferred Dividends For EPS

Cumulative preferred dividends are deducted from earnings available to common shareholders for EPS whether or not declared. Failing to deduct them overstates basic EPS.

### Distributing In Violation Of Legal Or Contractual Restrictions

Distributing dividends without confirming compliance with legal capital, covenant, or regulatory restrictions can trigger defaults, regulatory action, or unlawful distributions. Confirm compliance before payment.

### Misclassifying A Return Of Capital As A Dividend

A distribution in excess of earnings is a return of capital. Classifying it as a dividend misstates retained earnings and additional paid-in capital, and misleads users about the source of the distribution.

### Not Disclosing Dividends In Arrears

Cumulative preferred dividends in arrears must be disclosed. Omitting the disclosure hides the accumulated obligation from users.

### Failing To Recognize Gain Or Loss On Property Dividends

Property dividends require recognizing the distributed asset at fair value, with a gain or loss on the difference from carrying amount. Recording the dividend at carrying amount omits the gain or loss.

### Not Disclosing Subsequent Event Dividends

Dividends declared after the balance sheet date are non-adjusting subsequent events. Failing to disclose them hides a significant post-balance sheet event.

## Self-Check

- Is the dividend liability recognized at the declaration date, reducing retained earnings, and settled at the payment date?
- Are common and preferred dividends accounted for separately, with cumulative preferred dividends deducted from EPS earnings whether or not declared?
- Are cumulative preferred dividends in arrears disclosed but not recognized as a liability until declared?
- Are non-cumulative preferred dividends deducted from EPS earnings only if declared?
- Has compliance with legal capital, statutory, covenant, and regulatory restrictions been confirmed before payment?
- Are returns of capital distinguished from dividends, with the correct equity account reduced?
- Are property dividends recognized at fair value with gain or loss on the distributed asset, and scrip dividends accounted for based on their form?
- Are liquidating dividends classified as returns of capital, reducing paid-in capital?
- Are disclosures complete, including dividends per share, aggregate amounts, dividends payable, arrears, restrictions, and subsequent event declarations?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for complex capital structures or cross-border distributions?
