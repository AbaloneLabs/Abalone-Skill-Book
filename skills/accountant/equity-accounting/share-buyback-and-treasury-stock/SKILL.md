# Share Buyback And Treasury Stock

When an entity repurchases its own shares, the accounting depends on whether the shares are held as treasury stock or retired, and on the method elected. Unlike most transactions, gains and losses on treasury stock transactions do not flow through the income statement. Errors in method selection, retirement accounting, or the treatment of differences between repurchase price and original issue price distort equity and the equity per share calculation.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during share buyback and treasury stock accounting. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist legal advice on buyback authorizations. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 505, IFRS IAS 32) and consult a qualified professional for complex repurchase programs or cross-border buybacks.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether Repurchased Shares Are Held As Treasury Or Retired

The first decision is whether the repurchased shares are held in treasury for potential reissuance or retired upon repurchase.

- Under the treasury stock method, the repurchased shares are held as treasury stock, a contra-equity account, reducing total equity. The shares remain issued but are not outstanding.
- Under the retirement method, the repurchased shares are treated as retired, reducing the number of issued shares and adjusting the par value, additional paid-in capital, and retained earnings.
- Under IFRS, treasury shares are deducted from equity as a contra-equity balance. IFRS does not permit recognizing a gain or loss in profit or loss on treasury stock transactions; all differences are recognized in equity.

### Apply The Cost Method Or Par Value Method Under US GAAP

Under US GAAP, treasury stock held for reissuance can be accounted for using the cost method or the par value method.

- Under the cost method, record treasury stock at the repurchase cost as a contra-equity balance. Upon reissuance, remove the treasury stock at cost, record the proceeds at par and additional paid-in capital, and recognize any excess of proceeds over cost in additional paid-in capital, or any excess of cost over proceeds as a reduction of additional paid-in capital, limited to the existing balance from previous treasury transactions, then retained earnings.
- Under the par value method, record treasury stock at par value, with the excess of repurchase price over par value charged to additional paid-in capital for the original premium, and any remaining excess to retained earnings. This method effectively retires the shares from an equity presentation standpoint while keeping them in a treasury account.
- Apply the chosen method consistently. The cost method is more common in practice.

### Record The Repurchase At Cost

When shares are repurchased, reduce cash and increase treasury stock at the repurchase price.

- Under the cost method, debit treasury stock for the total repurchase cost and credit cash.
- The treasury stock balance is a contra-equity, reducing total stockholders' equity. It does not appear on the income statement.
- Do not recognize a gain or loss on the repurchase. The repurchase is a capital transaction, not an operating event.

### Handle Reissuance Of Treasury Stock

When treasury shares are reissued, remove the treasury stock at cost and record the proceeds.

- Under the cost method, credit treasury stock at the original repurchase cost. Debit cash for the reissue proceeds.
- Allocate the proceeds to common stock at par and additional paid-in capital for the excess over par.
- If reissue proceeds exceed the treasury cost, recognize the excess in additional paid-in capital from treasury stock transactions, not in income.
- If reissue proceeds are less than the treasury cost, reduce additional paid-in capital from treasury stock transactions first, then retained earnings if the additional paid-in capital balance is insufficient. Do not recognize a loss in income.

### Account For Retirement Of Treasury Stock

When treasury shares are retired, remove the treasury stock and adjust the equity accounts.

- Under the cost method, upon retirement, remove the treasury stock at cost, reduce common stock at par value for the shares retired, reduce additional paid-in capital for the original premium received on those shares, and charge any remaining difference to retained earnings.
- If the repurchase cost is less than the original proceeds, the difference increases additional paid-in capital.
- Retirement reduces the number of issued shares, while treasury stock holding does not.

### Apply Constructive Retirement When Appropriate

Constructive retirement applies when an entity repurchases shares with the intent to retire them, even if the formal retirement has not occurred.

- If the entity's stated intent and actions indicate retirement, account for the repurchase as a retirement rather than as treasury stock.
- Under constructive retirement, reduce common stock at par, reduce additional paid-in capital for the original premium, and charge any excess of repurchase price over the original proceeds to retained earnings.
- Reassess the intent if circumstances change. If the entity later decides to reissue the shares, the accounting may need to be revised.

### Handle IFRS Requirements

Under IFRS, treasury shares are deducted from equity, and no gain or loss is recognized in profit or loss.

- Record treasury shares at cost as a deduction from equity.
- Upon reissuance or disposal, recognize the difference between the reissue proceeds and the carrying amount in equity, not in profit or loss.
- If the entity reacquires and holds its own shares, the consideration paid including directly attributable costs is recognized as a deduction from equity.

### Disclose Treasury Stock And Buyback Activity

Disclose the treasury stock balance, the number of shares held, the repurchase program details, and the effect on equity.

- Disclose the number of treasury shares held at the beginning and end of the period, and the movements during the period.
- Disclose the repurchase program authorization, including the number of shares authorized and the amount.
- Disclose the carrying amount of treasury shares and the effect on equity per share.

## Common Traps

### Recognizing A Gain Or Loss On Treasury Stock In The Income Statement

Treasury stock transactions are capital transactions. Gains and losses are recognized in equity, not in profit or loss. Recognizing a gain inflates income, and recognizing a loss understates it.

### Inconsistent Application Of The Cost Or Par Value Method

Switching between the cost and par value methods without justification distorts comparability. Apply the chosen method consistently.

### Failing To Reduce Additional Paid-In Capital Before Retained Earnings On Reissuance Below Cost

When reissue proceeds are below cost, the difference reduces additional paid-in capital from treasury transactions first, then retained earnings. Charging it directly to retained earnings overstates additional paid-in capital.

### Not Retiring Shares When Intent Is To Retire

If the entity intends to retire the repurchased shares, constructive retirement should be applied. Holding them as treasury stock when retirement is the intent misstates the equity accounts.

### Recording Treasury Stock As An Asset

Treasury stock is a contra-equity, not an asset. Recording it as an asset overstates total assets and misleads users about the entity's resources.

### Ignoring The Effect On Earnings Per Share

Treasury shares reduce the number of shares outstanding, affecting earnings per share. Failing to exclude treasury shares from the weighted average shares outstanding overstates EPS.

### Not Disclosing The Repurchase Program

Buyback programs and the shares repurchased must be disclosed. Omitting the disclosure hides the scale and effect of repurchases on capital structure.

### Confusing Constructive Retirement With Treasury Stock Holding

The accounting differs based on intent. Constructive retirement adjusts par and additional paid-in capital, while treasury stock holding maintains a contra-equity balance. Applying the wrong treatment misstates equity.

## Self-Check

- Has the entity determined whether repurchased shares are held as treasury stock or retired, based on intent and actions?
- Under US GAAP, is the cost or par value method applied consistently, with treasury stock recorded as a contra-equity?
- Are gains and losses on reissuance recognized in equity, not in profit or loss, with additional paid-in capital reduced before retained earnings on reissuance below cost?
- For retirement or constructive retirement, are common stock at par, additional paid-in capital for the original premium, and retained earnings adjusted correctly?
- Under IFRS, are treasury shares deducted from equity with no gain or loss in profit or loss?
- Is treasury stock presented as a contra-equity and not as an asset?
- Are treasury shares excluded from the weighted average shares outstanding for EPS calculations?
- Are disclosures complete, including the number of treasury shares, the repurchase program authorization, and the carrying amount?
- Is the intent to hold or retire reassessed when circumstances change, with the accounting revised if needed?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for large or complex repurchase programs?
