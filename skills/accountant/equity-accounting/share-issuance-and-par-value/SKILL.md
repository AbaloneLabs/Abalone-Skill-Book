# Share Issuance And Par Value

Share issuance is the primary way an entity raises equity capital. The accounting requires allocating proceeds between the par or stated value of the shares and additional paid-in capital, handling issuance costs, and dealing with non-cash consideration. While the mechanics appear simple, errors in allocation, classification between debt and equity, and treatment of costs distort equity and mislead users about the entity's capital structure.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during share issuance accounting. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist legal and securities advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 480 and 505, IFRS IAS 32 and IFRS 2) and consult a qualified professional for complex instruments with embedded features.

## Core Rules

### Allocate Proceeds Between Par Or Stated Value And Additional Paid-In Capital

When shares are issued for cash, the proceeds are allocated between the par or stated value and additional paid-in capital.

- Record the par or stated value of the shares in the common stock or preferred stock account at the par value multiplied by the number of shares issued.
- Record the excess of the issue price over par or stated value in additional paid-in capital, also called share premium.
- If shares have no par value, record the full proceeds in the common stock account, or in the stock account and additional paid-in capital according to jurisdictional rules.
- Some jurisdictions require a stated or nominal par value for legal and registration purposes. Confirm the par value per the articles of incorporation.

### Account For Issuance Costs Correctly

Costs directly attributable to the issuance of shares reduce the proceeds and are netted against additional paid-in capital.

- Direct costs include registration fees, legal fees, underwriting fees, printing costs, and other incremental costs of the issuance.
- Under both US GAAP and IFRS, issuance costs are deducted from the proceeds, reducing additional paid-in capital. They are not expensed.
- Do not capitalize issuance costs as an asset. They are a reduction of equity.
- Separate issuance costs from ongoing operating costs. Only costs directly attributable to the specific issuance qualify.

### Record Shares Issued For Non-Cash Consideration

When shares are issued for consideration other than cash, measure the transaction at fair value.

- Measure the shares issued and the non-cash consideration received, and record the transaction at the more reliably measurable fair value, typically the fair value of the consideration received.
- If the fair value of the consideration is not reliably measurable, use the fair value of the shares issued, based on the market price at the issuance date or a valuation.
- Allocate the recorded amount between par value and additional paid-in capital as with cash issuances.
- Common examples include shares issued for property, equipment, intellectual property, or services. For services, recognize the expense or asset corresponding to the service received.

### Account For Shares Issued To Employees Or Suppliers For Services

When shares are issued in exchange for services, the accounting depends on whether the arrangement is a share-based payment.

- If the shares are granted as compensation to employees, apply share-based payment accounting, measuring the fair value of the shares or options and recognizing expense over the service period.
- If shares are issued to non-employees for goods or services, apply the guidance for non-employee share-based payments, generally measuring at the fair value of the goods or services received or the equity instruments granted.
- Distinguish share issuance for services from a capital raising. Services-based issuances are compensation or expense, not a capital transaction recorded at net proceeds.

### Distinguish Between Common And Preferred Stock

Different classes of shares have different rights and are recorded separately.

- Common stock carries voting rights and residual claims to earnings and assets. Record at par value with excess in additional paid-in capital.
- Preferred stock may carry preferential dividend rights, liquidation preferences, conversion features, or redemption features. Record separately from common stock.
- Assess whether preferred stock is actually equity or contains features that make it a liability, such as mandatory redemption or dividend features that are not discretionary. Redeemable preferred stock with mandatory redemption is classified as a liability under US GAAP and may be a financial liability under IFRS.

### Handle Stock Subscription And Forward Issuance

When investors subscribe to shares before issuance, account for the subscription.

- Record subscriptions receivable and common stock subscribed at par, with the excess in additional paid-in capital, if the subscription is binding and collectibility is reasonably assured.
- Upon collection and issuance, reclassify to the permanent equity accounts.
- If the subscription is non-binding or collectibility is uncertain, defer recognition until the shares are issued.

### Apply Legal And Regulatory Capital Requirements

The par value and legal capital requirements vary by jurisdiction and affect what can be distributed.

- Confirm the legal capital requirements of the jurisdiction of incorporation. Some jurisdictions require a minimum par value or stated capital that cannot be distributed as dividends.
- Maintain records of legal capital separately from distributable reserves if required by the jurisdiction.
- For entities in jurisdictions with solvency-based distribution tests, such as the United Kingdom, track distributable reserves separately from total equity.

### Disclose Share Issuance Adequately

Disclose the number and class of shares issued, the par value, the proceeds, the costs deducted, and the rights attached to each class.

- Disclose the authorized, issued, and outstanding shares by class.
- Disclose any shares reserved for issuance under options, warrants, or convertible instruments.
- Disclose any restrictions on dividends or distributions, including legal capital requirements and contractual restrictions.

## Common Traps

### Expensing Issuance Costs Instead Of Netting Against Proceeds

Issuance costs are a reduction of equity, not an expense. Expensing them understates equity and overstates expense in the issuance period.

### Recording Shares At Par Value Only

The full proceeds must be allocated between par and additional paid-in capital. Recording only par value understates additional paid-in capital and total equity.

### Misclassifying Preferred Stock With Mandatory Redemption As Equity

Preferred stock that must be redeemed or that has dividend features that are not at the issuer's discretion may be a liability, not equity. Classifying it as equity misleads users about leverage and capital structure.

### Using Book Value Instead Of Fair Value For Non-Cash Consideration

Shares issued for non-cash consideration must be measured at fair value. Using the book value of the asset received understates or overstates the transaction.

### Failing To Separate Common And Preferred Stock

Different classes have different rights and risks. Combining them into a single equity balance hides the preferential rights of preferred holders.

### Not Recognizing Expense For Shares Issued For Services

Shares issued for employee or supplier services are compensation or expense, not a pure capital transaction. Recording only the equity entry without the corresponding expense understates compensation expense.

### Ignoring Legal Capital And Distributable Reserve Requirements

Distributing amounts below the legal capital minimum or outside distributable reserves violates jurisdictional rules and may be unlawful. Track legal capital and distributable reserves.

### Inadequate Disclosure Of Share Classes And Rights

Failing to disclose the rights, preferences, and restrictions of each share class hides the capital structure from users.

## Self-Check

- Are proceeds from share issuance allocated between par or stated value and additional paid-in capital, with the par value confirmed against the articles of incorporation?
- Are direct issuance costs netted against additional paid-in capital and not expensed?
- For non-cash consideration, is the transaction measured at fair value, using the more reliably measurable of the shares or the consideration?
- Are shares issued for services accounted for as share-based payment with expense recognized over the service period?
- Are common and preferred stock recorded separately, with preferred stock assessed for liability classification based on redemption and dividend features?
- Are stock subscriptions accounted for based on whether they are binding and collectible?
- Are legal capital and distributable reserve requirements confirmed for the jurisdiction and tracked separately?
- Are disclosures complete, including authorized, issued, and outstanding shares by class, par value, rights, and restrictions?
- Are shares reserved for options, warrants, or convertibles disclosed?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for complex instruments or cross-border issuances?
