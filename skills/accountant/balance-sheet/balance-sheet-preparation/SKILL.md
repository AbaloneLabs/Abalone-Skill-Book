# Balance Sheet Preparation

The balance sheet (statement of financial position under IFRS) is a snapshot of an entity's assets, liabilities, and equity at a reporting date. It is the statement users rely on to assess liquidity, solvency, leverage, and capital structure. Preparing it is not merely dropping trial-balance balances into a template — it requires classifying each balance as current or noncurrent, grouping items into the correct line categories, confirming offsetting is appropriate, and ensuring the statement articulates with the income statement and statement of changes in equity. Agents often misclassify items (operating cycle vs. twelve-month threshold), net balances that should be presented gross, or produce a statement that does not tie to the equity rollforward.

Use this skill before drafting a balance sheet from a trial balance, reviewing a prepared statement for classification errors, converting between current/noncurrent and liquidity/order-of-permanence presentation, or deciding presentation format under IFRS versus US GAAP. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating the balance sheet as a mechanical sorting exercise and missing the classification, offsetting, and articulation judgments that determine whether the statement is correct.

Balance sheet presentation rules differ between US GAAP (ASC 210, classified current/noncurrent unless an industry exception applies) and IFRS (IAS 1, with a choice of current/noncurrent or liquidity presentation and specific minimum line items). This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For audited or filed statements, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Classify Current Versus Noncurrent Based On The Operating Cycle Or Twelve Months

Under a classified presentation, an asset is current when it is expected to be realized within the normal operating cycle, held primarily for trading, or expected to be realized within twelve months after the reporting period. A liability is current when it is expected to be settled within the normal operating cycle, held primarily for trading, or due within twelve months. The operating cycle is the time between acquiring materials for processing and realizing cash from the sale of goods or services; for most entities it is shorter than twelve months, but for some (distilleries, construction, defense contracting) it is longer, and the operating cycle governs classification for those items.

Items not meeting the current criteria are noncurrent. When the operating cycle is not clearly identifiable, assume twelve months. Do not classify a liability as noncurrent merely because management intends to refinance it — refinancing must be completed or have a firm agreement by the reporting date (under IFRS, the criteria for refinancing are specific).

### Apply The Minimum Line Items Required By The Framework

Under IFRS (IAS 1), the balance sheet must include, at a minimum, line items for: property, plant, and equipment; investment property; intangible assets; financial assets (excluding amounts shown under other lines); investments accounted for using the equity method; biological assets; inventories; trade and other receivables; cash and cash equivalents; assets held for sale; trade and other payables; provisions; financial liabilities; current tax assets and liabilities; deferred tax assets and liabilities; liabilities included in disposal groups; noncontrolling interests; and issued capital and reserves. Additional line items are added when relevant to understanding the entity's financial position.

Under US GAAP (ASC 210), a classified balance sheet presents current assets, noncurrent assets, current liabilities, and noncurrent liabilities, with sufficient detail for users to assess liquidity and capital structure. The specific line items are not prescribed to the same degree as IFRS, but consistency with industry practice is expected.

### Present Assets In Order Of Liquidity (Or Permanence) Consistently

Assets are generally presented in descending order of liquidity (most liquid first: cash, receivables, inventory, then noncurrent assets). Some jurisdictions and industries present assets in order of permanence (least liquid first). Choose one approach and apply it consistently. Within current assets, the order typically follows: cash and equivalents, short-term investments, receivables, inventories, prepaid expenses. Within noncurrent assets: PP&E, intangibles, investments, deferred tax assets.

Liabilities are generally presented in order of maturity (most current first). Equity is presented after liabilities, with share capital, additional paid-in capital, retained earnings, and accumulated other comprehensive income shown separately.

### Do Not Offset Assets And Liabilities Unless Criteria Are Met

Assets and liabilities, and income and expenses, must not be offset unless required or permitted by a standard. A financial asset and a financial liability are offset only when the entity has a legally enforceable right to set off and intends to settle on a net basis (or to realize the asset and settle the liability simultaneously). Cash overdrafts may be netted against other cash balances at the same bank only when there is a right of setoff.

Offsetting understates both assets and liabilities and obscures the entity's gross exposures. When in doubt, present gross. Tax assets and liabilities are offset only when there is a legal right and the intent to settle on a net basis.

### Distinguish Equity From Liabilities Carefully

The classification of an instrument as equity or liability drives whether it appears above or below the equity line and whether distributions are interest or dividends. A redeemable preferred share (mandatory redemption) is a liability; a perpetual non-redeemable preferred share may be equity. A compound instrument (convertible bond) is split into liability and equity components. Put this classification judgment at the top of the preparation checklist, because it affects multiple lines and the equity total.

Under IFRS (IAS 32), the classification is based on substance (the presence of a contractual obligation to deliver cash or another financial asset). Under US GAAP, the classification follows ASC 480 for mandatorily redeemable instruments and ASC 815 for derivatives with equity features. The two frameworks can classify the same instrument differently.

### Present Noncontrolling Interest Within Equity, Separately From Owners

Noncontrolling interest (the equity in a subsidiary not attributable to the parent) is presented within equity in the consolidated balance sheet, separately from the equity attributable to the parent's owners. This applies under both IFRS and US GAAP. Do not present NCI as a mezzanine item (between liabilities and equity) or as a liability.

### Ensure The Statement Articulates With The Income Statement And Equity Rollforward

The balance sheet must articulate with the other primary statements. Retained earnings at period end equals opening retained earnings plus net income (from the income statement) minus dividends. Accumulated OCI at period end equals opening AOCI plus OCI for the period (from the statement of comprehensive income). Total equity at period end equals opening equity plus comprehensive income minus distributions plus or minus capital transactions (from the statement of changes in equity). Cash at period end ties to the cash flow statement.

A balance sheet that does not articulate with these rollforwards has an error. Reconcile before finalizing.

### Disclose Material Classification Judgments And Offsetting

Disclose the basis of presentation, significant accounting policies affecting classification, material judgments about whether items are current or noncurrent, and any offsetting arrangements. Users rely on this to understand the statement's structure and to compare entities.

## Common Traps

### Misclassifying Current Versus Noncurrent Using The Wrong Threshold

Using twelve months when the operating cycle is longer (or vice versa) misclassifies items. Identify the operating cycle for entities with long production or collection cycles.

### Netting Balances That Should Be Presented Gross

Offsetting a receivable against a payable from the same counterparty without a legal right of setoff understates both. Present gross unless offsetting criteria are met.

### Classifying A Refinancing Intention As Noncurrent

A current liability is noncurrent only if refinancing is completed or under a firm agreement by the reporting date. An intention to refinance is insufficient.

### Treating A Redeemable Instrument As Equity

A mandatorily redeemable preferred share is a liability, not equity. Misclassifying it overstates equity and understates liabilities.

### Presenting Noncontrolling Interest Outside Equity

NCI is part of consolidated equity, presented separately. Placing it as a mezzanine item or liability is incorrect under current standards.

### Failing To Reconcile To The Equity Rollforward And Cash Flow

A balance sheet that does not tie to retained earnings rollforward, OCI movement, or cash flow ending balance has an error. Reconcile before finalizing.

### Using Inconsistent Ordering Across Periods

Switching between liquidity and permanence ordering, or changing line-item grouping, without disclosure impairs comparability. Apply presentation consistently.

## Self-Check

- Is each asset and liability classified as current or noncurrent based on the operating cycle (if identifiable) or the twelve-month threshold?
- Are the minimum line items required by the applicable framework (IFRS minimum lines or US GAAP classified presentation) included?
- Are assets presented in a consistent order (liquidity or permanence), with liabilities by maturity and equity components shown separately?
- Are assets and liabilities presented gross, with offsetting applied only when there is a legal right of setoff and intent to settle net?
- Are compound and redeemable instruments classified as liability or equity based on substance, with equity instruments not shown as liabilities?
- Is noncontrolling interest presented within equity, separately from the parent's owners' equity?
- Does the balance sheet articulate with the income statement (retained earnings), statement of comprehensive income (AOCI), statement of changes in equity, and cash flow statement (ending cash)?
- Are the basis of presentation, classification judgments, and offsetting arrangements disclosed, and has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed the statement?
