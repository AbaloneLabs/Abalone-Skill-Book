# Working Capital And Liquidity Disclosure

The balance sheet is the primary source of liquidity information, but the raw current/noncurrent split is often insufficient. Users need to understand restrictions on cash, available credit facilities, net asset restrictions in subsidiaries, covenant constraints, and the going concern assessment. Agents often present the current ratio mechanically without disclosing the items that materially affect liquidity — restricted cash, undrawn committed facilities, net assets locked in subsidiaries, or covenants that restrict dividend capacity. Under IFRS, explicit liquidity and going concern disclosures are required; under US GAAP, similar information is expected through risk and liquidity disclosures.

Use this skill before finalizing balance sheet liquidity disclosures, assessing going concern, disclosing cash restrictions and credit facilities, or analyzing whether working capital presentation is transparent. The goal is to prevent the agent from presenting a clean current ratio while omitting the restrictions, commitments, and going concern uncertainties that determine actual liquidity.

Liquidity disclosure rules differ between US GAAP and IFRS (IFRS has specific going concern and liquidity risk disclosure requirements in IAS 1 and IFRS 7). This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For going concern assessments, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Disclose Material Restrictions On Cash And Cash Equivalents

Cash and cash equivalents presented in the balance sheet should be available without restriction. If any portion is restricted (legally, contractually, or practically), disclose the nature and amount of the restriction and classify the restricted amount separately if material. Restrictions include: compensating balances supporting borrowings; escrow funds; cash in jurisdictions with exchange controls or transfer restrictions; cash pledged as collateral; and cash subject to regulatory requirements.

Failing to disclose restrictions overstates the entity's available liquidity. Users assume cash is freely available unless told otherwise.

### Disclose Available Credit Facilities And Their Drawn Amounts

Disclose the amount of committed and uncommitted credit facilities, the drawn and undrawn amounts, and any terms that affect availability (covenants, expiration dates, security). Committed facilities (where the lender is contractually obligated) are more reliable liquidity than uncommitted facilities (where the lender can withdraw). The undrawn portion of committed facilities is a liquidity source that users need to see.

Disclose whether facilities are secured or unsecured, their maturity, and any conditions that could reduce availability (financial covenants, material adverse change clauses).

### Assess And Disclose Going Concern Uncertainty

The financial statements are prepared on a going concern basis unless management intends to liquidate or cease trading, or has no realistic alternative. If material uncertainty exists that may cast significant doubt on the entity's ability to continue as a going concern, disclose that uncertainty clearly. Under IFRS (IAS 1), if the going concern basis is appropriate despite material uncertainty, disclose the uncertainty and the basis for the going concern assumption; if going concern is not appropriate, prepare the statements on a different basis and disclose that basis.

Under US GAAP (ASC 205), if substantial doubt exists, disclose the principal conditions and events, management's evaluation, and plans to mitigate. The going concern assessment considers at least twelve months from the reporting date (under both frameworks, with some timing differences). Do not prepare statements on a going concern basis when liquidation is intended or inevitable.

### Disclose Net Asset Restrictions In Subsidiaries

If a material portion of the entity's net assets is held in subsidiaries and is restricted (cannot be distributed to the parent without cost, tax, or regulatory consequence), disclose the amount and nature of the restriction. Net assets locked in subsidiaries reduce the parent's practical access to liquidity and dividend capacity.

This is particularly relevant for groups with regulated subsidiaries (insurance, banking), foreign subsidiaries with exchange controls, or subsidiaries with minority shareholders whose consent is needed for distributions.

### Disclose Financial Covenants And Their Status

Disclose financial covenants attached to debt facilities, the entity's compliance status at the reporting date, and the headroom (margin by which the covenant is met). If a covenant is breached or headroom is narrow, disclose the breach or the sensitivity. Covenant breaches can trigger acceleration of debt, which would reclassify long-term debt to current and materially affect liquidity.

Do not omit covenant information because it is uncomfortable. Users and lenders rely on it to assess refinancing risk.

### Present Bank Overdrafts As Financing Or As Cash Depending On The Basis

Bank overdrafts that are integral to the entity's cash management (fluctuating, repayable on demand, forming part of overall cash management) may be included in cash and cash equivalents under IFRS. Overdrafts that are not integral (standing facilities used as a financing source) are classified as financing activities and presented as borrowings. Under US GAAP, overdrafts are generally classified as liabilities (financing), not as negative cash.

The classification affects the cash and cash equivalents balance on the balance sheet and the cash flow statement. Apply the integral-to-cash-management test and disclose the basis.

### Disclose Contractual Commitments And Contingencies

Disclose material contractual commitments that affect future liquidity: capital expenditure commitments, operating lease commitments (the total undiscounted future lease payments, separate from the lease liability), purchase obligations, and guarantees. These are often disclosed in the notes rather than recognized on the balance sheet, but they affect future cash outflows.

Contingent liabilities (litigation, guarantees, environmental obligations) are disclosed unless remote. The disclosure helps users assess potential future liquidity drains.

### Reconcile The Liquidity Presentation Across Statements

The cash and cash equivalents balance on the balance sheet must tie to the ending balance on the cash flow statement. Restricted cash, if presented separately, should be reconciled. Short-term borrowings and the current portion of long-term debt should be identifiable. The working capital presentation (current assets minus current liabilities) should be consistent with the line-item classification.

## Common Traps

### Presenting Restricted Cash As Unrestricted

Restricted cash is not freely available. Including it in unrestricted cash and equivalents overstates liquidity. Disclose and classify separately.

### Omitting Credit Facility Disclosure

Undrawn committed facilities are a liquidity source. Omitting them understates available liquidity and prevents users from assessing refinancing capacity.

### Ignoring Going Concern Uncertainty

Preparing statements on a going concern basis when material uncertainty exists, without disclosure, misleads users. Assess going concern and disclose uncertainty.

### Failing To Disclose Net Asset Restrictions In Subsidiaries

Net assets locked in regulated or foreign subsidiaries reduce practical liquidity. Omitting this overstates the parent's access to resources.

### Hiding Covenant Breaches Or Narrow Headroom

Covenant breaches can trigger debt acceleration. Omitting them leaves users unaware of refinancing risk. Disclose breaches and narrow headroom.

### Classifying Overdrafts Inconsistently With The Cash Management Test

Overdrafts integral to cash management may be in cash equivalents; others are financing. Misclassifying affects both the balance sheet and cash flow statement.

### Omitting Commitment And Contingency Disclosure

Capital commitments, lease commitments, and contingencies affect future liquidity. Omitting them from notes leaves users with an incomplete picture.

## Self-Check

- Are material restrictions on cash and cash equivalents disclosed, with restricted amounts classified separately if material?
- Are committed and uncommitted credit facilities, drawn and undrawn amounts, terms, and security disclosed?
- Has a going concern assessment been performed for at least twelve months, with material uncertainty disclosed (or a different basis applied if going concern is not appropriate)?
- Are material net asset restrictions in subsidiaries (regulated, foreign, minority-consent) disclosed?
- Are financial covenants, compliance status, headroom, and any breaches disclosed?
- Are bank overdrafts classified based on whether they are integral to cash management (cash equivalents) or a financing source (borrowings)?
- Are contractual commitments (capital, lease, purchase) and contingent liabilities disclosed in the notes?
- Does the cash and equivalents balance tie to the cash flow statement, and is the working capital presentation consistent with line-item classification?
- Has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed the going concern assessment and liquidity disclosures?
