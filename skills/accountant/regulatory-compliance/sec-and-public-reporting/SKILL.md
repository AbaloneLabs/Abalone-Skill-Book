# SEC And Public Reporting

Public companies in the United States are subject to the reporting requirements of the Securities and Exchange Commission, which go well beyond the preparation of financial statements under GAAP. Annual reports on Form 10-K, quarterly reports on Form 10-Q, current reports on Form 8-K, and proxy statements each have specific content, format, and timing requirements. The SEC also imposes disclosure controls and procedures, internal control over financial reporting requirements under Sarbanes-Oxley, insider trading restrictions, and a continuous disclosure obligation for material information. The stakes are high: filing errors, missed deadlines, and inadequate disclosures can trigger SEC comment letters, restatements, enforcement actions, and loss of investor confidence. Public reporting is a disciplined, deadline-driven process that demands accuracy, completeness, and a clear understanding of what the rules require and when.

Use this skill when preparing SEC filings, drafting MD&A and footnotes, evaluating disclosure controls, or supporting public company compliance. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating SEC reporting as routine GAAP reporting, from missing form-specific requirements and deadlines, or from underestimating the rigor of disclosure controls and SOX obligations.

## Core Rules

### Know The Form Requirements, Content, And Deadlines For Each Filing

Each SEC form has specific requirements. Know them before drafting, not during review.

Know for each form:

- the required content and the specific items that must be addressed;
- the format and presentation requirements, including interactive data in XBRL;
- the filing deadline, which depends on filing status and form type;
- the signatures and certifications required, including CEO and CFO certifications;
- the incorporation by reference relationships with other filings.

A 10-K is not just annual financial statements; it includes MD&A, risk factors, controls evaluations, and numerous specific disclosures. Drafting without a checklist of required items guarantees omissions.

### Apply Disclosure Controls And Procedures Rigorously

Public companies must maintain disclosure controls and procedures designed to ensure that information required to be disclosed is recorded, processed, summarized, and reported on time.

Apply by:

- maintaining a process that identifies material information and routes it to decision-makers;
- certifying the effectiveness of disclosure controls with each quarterly and annual filing;
- evaluating disclosure controls for any material weakness or significant deficiency;
- ensuring the certification process involves the CEO and CFO personally, not just staff;
- documenting the controls and the evaluation evidence.

Disclosure controls are broader than ICFR; they cover all information that must be disclosed, not just financial statement amounts. A process that produces accurate financials but fails to surface material non-financial information is a disclosure control failure.

### Comply With Sarbanes-Oxley Internal Control Requirements

Section 404 of SOX requires management to assess and report on the effectiveness of internal control over financial reporting, and the external auditor to attest to that assessment.

Comply by:

- maintaining documentation of all significant controls;
- testing the design and operating effectiveness of controls annually;
- identifying and remediating control deficiencies, and disclosing any material weakness;
- obtaining management's assessment and the auditor's attestation;
- ensuring changes to controls are documented and tested.

A material weakness in ICFR must be disclosed and attracts scrutiny. SOX compliance is an ongoing program, not a year-end exercise, and late preparation guarantees deficiencies.

### Draft MD&A That Explains, Not Just Reports

Management's Discussion and Analysis is where management explains the financial results, their drivers, and the outlook. It is among the most-read sections and the most scrutinized by the SEC.

Draft MD&A that:

- discusses liquidity, capital resources, and known trends and uncertainties;
- explains the drivers of revenue, expense, and margin changes, not just the amounts;
- addresses critical accounting estimates and their sensitivity;
- compares the current period to the prior period with specific explanation of changes;
- avoids boilerplate, generic risk discussion, and forward-looking statements without basis.

The SEC reviews MD&A closely and issues comment letters requesting specific, quantitative explanation of changes. Vague MD&A invites comment and rework.

### Meet The Continuous Disclosure Obligation For Material Information

Public companies must disclose material information on a current basis, not only in periodic reports, through Form 8-K.

Meet the obligation by:

- maintaining a process to identify material events as they occur;
- filing 8-K within the required timeframe, typically four business days, for triggering events;
- understanding the specific items that require 8-K filing, such as material agreements, executive changes, and financial restatements;
- coordinating disclosure with any Regulation FD obligations to avoid selective disclosure;
- avoiding leaks or selective disclosure before public filing.

A material event disclosed late, or selectively disclosed to analysts before public filing, is a violation. The continuous disclosure process must be ready at all times, not only during reporting season.

### Coordinate With Legal, Investor Relations, And Auditors

Public reporting involves multiple functions. Coordination is essential to accuracy and timeliness.

Coordinate by:

- engaging legal counsel on disclosure obligations, risk factors, and litigation disclosure;
- coordinating with investor relations on consistency between filings and public communications;
- aligning with external auditors on financial statements, controls, and review or audit timelines;
- ensuring the audit committee reviews significant disclosures before filing;
- managing the review and approval workflow so all parties sign off before filing.

A filing prepared by accounting without legal, IR, and audit committee input risks inconsistent disclosure, missed obligations, and governance failures. Build the coordination into the filing calendar.

### Prepare For SEC Review And Comment Letters

The SEC reviews filings and may issue comment letters requesting clarification, additional disclosure, or restatement. Be prepared to respond.

Prepare by:

- anticipating areas of SEC focus, such as revenue recognition, non-GAAP measures, and segment reporting;
- maintaining support for all significant disclosures and estimates;
- responding to comment letters thoroughly and on time, with the involvement of counsel and auditors;
- assessing whether a comment response requires amendment or restatement.

A comment letter is not necessarily an accusation, but it requires a substantive response. Dismissing or inadequately responding to comments escalates the matter.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

SEC reporting and public company compliance involve complex, framework-specific, and jurisdiction-specific legal and regulatory requirements that are the professional domain of qualified accountants, securities lawyers, auditors, and disclosure specialists. Filing requirements, disclosure obligations, SOX compliance, and SEC interactions require professional expertise and involve legal liability. This skill provides awareness of what to consider, not definitive conclusions or legal advice. Confirm all significant SEC reporting decisions, disclosures, and filings with qualified accounting professionals and securities counsel in the relevant jurisdiction, and align with auditors. Do not treat any summary as authoritative; the SEC rules, regulations, and professional and legal judgment govern.

## Common Traps

### SEC Reporting Treated As Routine GAAP Reporting

SEC requirements go well beyond GAAP financials, including form-specific content, MD&A, and controls certifications.

### Missing Form Requirements And Deadlines

Drafting without a checklist of required items and deadlines guarantees omissions and late filings.

### Disclosure Controls Treated As A Formality

Disclosure controls cover all material information, not just financials; a perfunctory certification is a control failure.

### SOX Treated As A Year-End Exercise

SOX compliance is an ongoing program; late preparation guarantees deficiencies and material weaknesses.

### Vague Or Boilerplate MD&A

Generic MD&A without specific, quantitative explanation of changes invites SEC comment letters and rework.

### Late Or Selective Disclosure Of Material Events

Failing to file 8-K on time or selectively disclosing to analysts is a violation of continuous disclosure obligations.

### Filing Without Cross-Functional Coordination

A filing without legal, IR, audit committee, and auditor input risks inconsistent disclosure and governance failures.

### Summaries Treated As Authoritative

SEC reporting requires professional and legal expertise; confirm with qualified professionals and securities counsel.

## Self-Check

- Are the content, format, deadline, signature, and incorporation requirements known for each form before drafting?
- Are disclosure controls and procedures applied rigorously, with CEO and CFO certification and documented evaluation?
- Is SOX section 404 complied with through ongoing documentation, testing, deficiency remediation, and auditor attestation?
- Does MD&A explain the drivers of results, liquidity, trends, and critical estimates with specific quantitative comparison?
- Is the continuous disclosure obligation met through prompt 8-K filing and avoidance of selective disclosure?
- Is the filing coordinated with legal, investor relations, auditors, and the audit committee before submission?
- Is the entity prepared for SEC review, with support for disclosures and a process for thorough, timely comment response?
- Are all significant SEC reporting decisions, disclosures, and filings confirmed with qualified accounting professionals and securities counsel, and aligned with auditors?
