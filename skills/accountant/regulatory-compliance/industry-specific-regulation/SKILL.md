# Industry Specific Regulation

General-purpose accounting frameworks such as GAAP and IFRS apply across industries, but many industries are also subject to specialized accounting and regulatory requirements that supplement, modify, or override general rules. Banks face capital adequacy and call report requirements. Insurance companies face statutory accounting principles and solvency regulation. Healthcare entities face reimbursement, cost reporting, and fraud-and-abuse rules. Government contractors face cost accounting standards and allowable-cost rules. Utilities face rate regulation. Each of these specialized regimes reflects the unique risks, public interest, and economic characteristics of the industry. An entity in a regulated industry that applies only general accounting, without understanding and complying with its specialized requirements, produces incomplete reporting, breaches regulatory obligations, and exposes itself to enforcement. Industry-specific regulation is not an optional layer; for regulated entities, it is often the primary reporting and compliance framework.

Use this skill when evaluating industry-specific requirements, addressing specialized reporting rules, or ensuring compliance with sector-specific regulators. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying only general accounting to a regulated entity, from confusing general-purpose and regulatory reporting, or from underestimating the specialized knowledge required for the industry.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Specific Regulatory Frameworks That Apply To The Entity

The first step is identifying which specialized frameworks apply. This depends on the entity's industry, activities, and jurisdiction.

Identify by:

- determining the entity's regulated status, such as bank, insurer, utility, or government contractor;
- identifying the primary regulator and the reporting framework it requires;
- assessing whether the entity is subject to multiple regimes, such as general GAAP plus statutory accounting;
- confirming the jurisdiction-specific requirements, which vary even within an industry;
- documenting the applicable frameworks and their interaction.

An entity may prepare general-purpose financial statements for investors and separate regulatory reports for its regulator, on different bases. Failing to identify a applicable regime is a compliance failure from the start.

### Understand How Industry Rules Modify General Accounting

Specialized rules often modify how general accounting is applied. Understand the modifications.

Understand modifications such as:

- statutory accounting principles for insurance, which differ from GAAP in asset admission and reserve treatment;
- regulatory accounting for banks, which may modify loan loss and capital treatment;
- cost accounting standards for government contractors, which govern cost allocation and allowability;
- rate regulation for utilities, which affects the recognition of regulatory assets and liabilities;
- reimbursement rules for healthcare, which affect revenue recognition and cost reporting.

A bank that applies general GAAP without considering call report requirements, or a contractor that ignores CAS, produces reporting that fails its regulatory framework. The modifications are not optional.

### Maintain Parallel Reporting Where Required

Many regulated entities maintain parallel reporting: general-purpose statements for investors and regulatory reports for the regulator, on different bases.

Maintain parallel reporting by:

- identifying the differences between the general-purpose and regulatory bases;
- maintaining the records and adjustments needed to produce both sets of reports;
- reconciling between the bases to ensure consistency and identify differences;
- ensuring both sets are prepared on time and filed with the appropriate parties;
- disclosing the basis of preparation for each set of reports.

Parallel reporting is complex and error-prone. A common failure is letting the regulatory report drift from the general-purpose statements without reconciliation, producing inconsistent information.

### Address Capital, Solvency, And Prudential Requirements

Financial services and insurance entities face capital, solvency, and prudential requirements that constrain their operations and reporting.

Address by:

- monitoring capital adequacy ratios and solvency margins against regulatory minimums;
- understanding the regulatory capital treatment of assets, liabilities, and off-balance-sheet items;
- reporting capital and solvency positions to the regulator on the required schedule;
- assessing the impact of business decisions on regulatory capital before execution;
- planning for capital actions, such as dividends or acquisitions, that require regulatory approval.

A decision that is sound under general accounting, such as a distribution, may breach a regulatory capital requirement. Prudential requirements constrain management action and must be considered in decisions.

### Comply With Cost Allowability And Reimbursement Rules

Government contractors, healthcare entities, and others subject to cost reimbursement must comply with specific cost allowability rules.

Comply by:

- understanding which costs are allowable, unallowable, and allocable under the applicable rules;
- maintaining the cost accounting structure and practices required, such as CAS compliance;
- segregating unallowable costs and excluding them from billing and claims;
- preparing and submitting required cost reports, such as incurred cost submissions;
- supporting costs with documentation that withstands audit and investigation.

An unallowable cost billed to the government, or a cost report that cannot be supported, leads to disallowance, recovery demands, and potential fraud allegations. Cost allowability is a high-stakes compliance area.

### Engage With The Regulator Proactively

Regulated entities benefit from proactive engagement with their regulators, rather than waiting for examination or enforcement.

Engage by:

- maintaining open communication with the regulator on significant matters;
- preparing thoroughly for examinations and providing complete, accurate information;
- responding promptly to regulator inquiries and requests;
- self-reporting material issues when required, rather than waiting for discovery;
- understanding the regulator's priorities and areas of focus.

A regulator that discovers an undisclosed issue reacts far more severely than one informed proactively. Engagement builds credibility and can mitigate consequences.

### Stay Current With Regulatory Change

Regulatory requirements change, sometimes significantly. Stay current.

Stay current by:

- monitoring regulatory developments, proposed rules, and guidance from the regulator;
- assessing the impact of changes on the entity's reporting and operations;
- implementing required changes by the effective date;
- participating in industry associations that track and influence regulation;
- training staff on regulatory changes that affect their responsibilities.

A regulatory change ignored until after its effective date is a compliance failure. Regulated entities must have a process to monitor and implement change.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

Industry-specific regulation involves complex, framework-specific, jurisdiction-specific, and legally binding requirements that are the professional domain of qualified accountants, industry specialists, regulatory experts, and legal counsel. Specialized accounting, regulatory reporting, prudential requirements, and cost allowability rules require professional expertise and involve legal liability and regulatory consequences. This skill provides awareness of what to consider, not definitive conclusions or legal advice. Confirm all significant industry-specific accounting and compliance decisions with qualified accounting professionals, industry specialists, and legal counsel in the relevant jurisdiction, and engage with the relevant regulator. Do not treat any summary as authoritative; the regulatory rules, standards, and professional and legal judgment govern.

## Common Traps

### Applying Only General Accounting To A Regulated Entity

General-purpose GAAP or IFRS, without the specialized industry rules, produces incomplete and non-compliant reporting.

### Confusing General-Purpose And Regulatory Reporting

Failing to distinguish investor reporting from regulatory reports, and letting them drift without reconciliation, produces inconsistency.

### Ignoring Prudential Constraints On Decisions

A decision sound under general accounting may breach capital, solvency, or allowability requirements.

### Unallowable Costs Billed Or Claimed

Billing unallowable costs or submitting unsupported cost reports leads to disallowance and potential fraud allegations.

### Reactive Rather Than Proactive Regulator Engagement

Waiting for examination or enforcement, rather than engaging proactively, worsens the consequences of issues.

### Regulatory Change Ignored Until Too Late

A regulatory change ignored until after its effective date is a compliance failure with potential enforcement consequences.

### Parallel Reporting Without Reconciliation

Letting regulatory reports drift from general-purpose statements without reconciliation produces inconsistent information.

### Summaries Treated As Authoritative

Industry-specific regulation requires professional, industry, and legal expertise; confirm with qualified professionals.

## Self-Check

- Are the specific regulatory frameworks that apply to the entity identified, including regulator, reporting basis, and jurisdiction?
- Are the ways industry rules modify general accounting understood and applied, such as statutory principles, call reports, CAS, or rate regulation?
- Is parallel reporting maintained with reconciliation between general-purpose and regulatory bases?
- Are capital, solvency, and prudential requirements monitored, with decisions assessed for regulatory impact?
- Are cost allowability and reimbursement rules complied with, with unallowable costs segregated and cost reports supported?
- Is the regulator engaged proactively, with thorough examination preparation and prompt, complete responses?
- Is the entity current with regulatory change, with monitoring, impact assessment, implementation, and training?
- Are all significant industry-specific accounting and compliance decisions confirmed with qualified accounting professionals, industry specialists, and legal counsel, with regulator engagement?
