# KPI Design And Dashboards

Key performance indicators and dashboards translate raw accounting and operational data into the measures that managers use to run the business. A well-designed KPI focuses attention on what drives value and reveals problems early. A poorly designed one does the opposite. It can optimize the wrong behavior, obscure real performance through misleading ratios, or present a number that looks precise but is built on inconsistent definitions. The trap is that KPIs feel objective because they are numbers, but every KPI embeds choices about what to include, how to calculate it, and what to compare it against. Agents often grab conventional metrics like EBITDA margin or customer acquisition cost without checking whether the definition fits the business, whether the components are consistently measured, or whether the metric incentivizes the right behavior.

Use this skill before designing KPIs, building a dashboard, defining a non-GAAP measure, or reviewing reported metrics for clarity and consistency. The goal is to ensure KPIs measure what matters, are calculated consistently, are presented honestly, and drive the right decisions.

This skill covers KPI and dashboard design judgment. Specific non-GAAP or alternative performance measures may be subject to regulatory disclosure rules depending on the audience and jurisdiction. Confirm any applicable reporting requirements before publishing metrics externally.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Decision The KPI Should Inform

A KPI exists to support a decision. Before defining a metric, identify the question it answers. A KPI without a clear decision behind it is noise on a dashboard.

Ask for each proposed KPI:

- what decision will this metric inform;
- who will act on it and how;
- what behavior does measuring this drive;
- what would a change in this metric trigger.

If no one would change a decision based on the metric, it does not belong on the dashboard. Crowding a dashboard with metrics that no one acts on dilutes attention from the ones that matter.

### Define Each KPI Precisely And Consistently

Every KPI needs a precise, written definition that is applied consistently. Vague definitions produce numbers that cannot be compared across periods or segments.

A complete KPI definition includes:

- the numerator and denominator or the exact formula;
- what is included and excluded;
- the time period and the unit, such as per month or per customer;
- the data source and the calculation timing;
- any adjustments, such as excluding one-time items.

For example, customer acquisition cost means nothing without a definition of what counts as a customer, what costs are included, and over what period. Document the definition so different people calculate it the same way.

### Choose Leading Over Lagging Indicators Where Possible

Lagging indicators, like net income or annual revenue, tell you what happened. Leading indicators, like pipeline conversion or churn signals, tell you what is likely to happen. A dashboard of only lagging indicators reports results too late to change them.

Balance the dashboard with:

- lagging indicators that confirm outcomes;
- leading indicators that predict future outcomes;
- operational indicators that show current state.

A dashboard that is all backward-looking financials gives no early warning. One that is all operational metrics with no financial tie-in loses connection to value.

### Avoid Misleading Ratios And Definitions

A ratio can look precise and still mislead. Common pitfalls include ratios with mismatched periods, denominators that are not meaningful, or adjustments that flatter the result.

Watch for:

- annualized rates computed from a partial period without disclosure;
- margins computed on inconsistent revenue bases across periods;
- growth rates from a tiny base that produce huge percentages;
- non-GAAP measures that exclude normal recurring costs;
- per-customer or per-unit metrics with inconsistent definitions of the unit.

A metric that is technically calculated but definitionally misleading is worse than no metric, because it creates false confidence.

### Handle Non-GAAP And Alternative Measures Carefully

Non-GAAP or alternative performance measures, like adjusted EBITDA or free cash flow, can be useful but are prone to selective adjustment. Each exclusion should be justified and applied consistently.

For non-GAAP measures:

- start from the most directly comparable GAAP measure;
- disclose each adjustment and why it is excluded;
- apply the same adjustments consistently across periods;
- avoid excluding normal recurring costs just because they are inconvenient;
- reconcile clearly to the GAAP measure.

A non-GAAP measure that changes definition period to period, or that excludes core operating costs, is not a performance measure, it is marketing.

### Present Dashboards With Context And Honesty

A number without context is hard to interpret. A dashboard should show each KPI with enough context to act on it.

Include for each KPI:

- the current value and the trend over time;
- the target or benchmark for comparison;
- the prior period and year-to-date comparison;
- any caveat about data quality or definition changes.

Avoid visual tricks that exaggerate trends, like truncated axes or inconsistent scales. A dashboard that is designed to make things look good rather than to reveal truth undermines decision-making.

### Review KPIs Periodically For Relevance

A KPI that was critical two years ago may no longer matter. Review the dashboard periodically to confirm each metric still informs a decision and still reflects the business strategy.

Retire metrics that no longer drive action, and add metrics when the strategy or business model shifts. A stale dashboard measures the old business, not the current one.

### Ensure Data Quality And Single Source Of Truth

A KPI is only as reliable as the data behind it. If different teams calculate the same metric from different sources, the numbers will disagree and trust erodes.

Establish:

- a single authoritative source for each data element;
- defined data refresh timing;
- controls over data entry and changes;
- reconciliation between dashboard numbers and the source systems.

Conflicting numbers across reports destroy credibility faster than any single error.

## Common Traps

### Metrics Without A Decision Behind Them

A dashboard full of metrics that no one acts on wastes attention. Every KPI should connect to a decision or it should be removed.

### Vague Or Shifting Definitions

A KPI calculated differently each period, or differently by different people, cannot be compared. Document and lock the definition.

### All Lagging Indicators

A dashboard of only historical financials reports results too late to influence them. Add leading and operational indicators.

### Misleading Ratios And Adjustments

Annualized partial periods, inconsistent bases, and selective non-GAAP exclusions produce numbers that look precise but mislead. Define and adjust honestly.

### Visual Tricks That Exaggerate

Truncated axes, inconsistent scales, and cherry-picked periods make trends look better or worse than they are. Present honestly.

### Stale Dashboards Measuring The Old Business

A KPI set defined years ago may not reflect the current strategy. Review and refresh periodically.

### Multiple Conflicting Sources

When each team reports a different number for the same metric, trust collapses. Establish a single source of truth.

### Optimizing The Metric Instead Of The Outcome

When a KPI becomes a target, people optimize the metric, sometimes at the expense of the real outcome it was meant to reflect. Watch for behavior that games the measure.

## Self-Check

- Does each KPI on the dashboard connect to a specific decision and drive the right behavior?
- Is every KPI defined precisely in writing, with formula, inclusions, exclusions, period, source, and adjustments, and applied consistently?
- Does the dashboard balance lagging, leading, and operational indicators rather than relying only on backward-looking financials?
- Are ratios and definitions free of misleading elements like mismatched periods, inconsistent bases, or selective exclusions?
- For non-GAAP measures, is each adjustment justified, consistent across periods, and reconciled to the closest GAAP measure?
- Does the dashboard present each KPI with target, trend, prior-period comparison, and honest visuals rather than tricks that exaggerate?
- Are KPIs reviewed periodically for continued relevance to the current strategy?
- Is there a single authoritative data source for each metric, with controls and reconciliation to source systems?
- Have any external disclosure or regulatory requirements for non-GAAP measures been confirmed before publishing metrics outside the company?
