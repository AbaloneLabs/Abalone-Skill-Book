# Board And Management Package

A board or management reporting package is the regular financial and operational report that leadership uses to oversee the business. It typically includes the income statement, balance sheet, cash flow, KPIs, variance analysis, forecasts, and narrative commentary. The trap is that these packages often grow by accretion. New slides get added each period and never removed, until the package is a hundred pages of detail that no one reads. Or it swings the other way, a few high-level numbers with no explanation of what drove them. A good package is curated. It leads with the few things that matter, supports them with the right detail, and connects the numbers to the decisions leadership must make. Agents often assemble the package by pulling every available report rather than designing it around what the audience needs to know.

Use this skill before assembling a board or management package, selecting its content, designing the reporting deck, or reviewing an existing package for effectiveness. The goal is to produce a package that is accurate, focused, and decision-oriented rather than exhaustive or empty.

This skill covers reporting package design. The specific governance requirements, confidentiality obligations, and regulatory disclosure rules for board materials vary by jurisdiction and entity type. Confirm the applicable requirements before finalizing and distributing the package.

## Core Rules

### Design The Package Around The Audience And Its Decisions

The package exists to help leadership govern and decide. Its content, depth, and format should match what the audience needs, not what is easy to produce.

Determine for the specific audience:

- what decisions will this package inform, such as approving a budget, assessing a covenant, or directing resources;
- what level of financial literacy does the audience have;
- how much time will they spend on it;
- what questions are they likely to ask.

A board package for financially sophisticated directors differs from a management package for operational leaders. Tailor the content, or the same numbers will be either too dense or too shallow.

### Lead With The Few Things That Matter

Attention is scarce. The opening of the package should highlight the handful of results, trends, and issues that matter most this period, not bury them on page forty.

A strong executive summary includes:

- the key results versus plan and prior period;
- the main variances and their causes;
- the critical issues or risks requiring attention;
- the decisions or approvals being requested.

If a reader only reads the first two pages, they should understand the period's story. The detail exists to support and allow drilling down, not to carry the message.

### Provide The Core Financials With Variance Context

The package should include the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow, each with comparison and variance context.

For each core statement:

- show actual versus budget, forecast, and prior period;
- highlight material variances with brief explanation;
- show year-to-date as well as the current period where useful;
- present key ratios and trends, not just raw numbers.

Bare financial statements without variance context force the reader to do the analysis themselves. The package should do that work for them.

### Balance Summary And Detail

The package needs both a clear summary and the supporting detail, but they must be layered so the summary is not lost. Lead with summary, then provide detail in appendices or backup.

Structure the package so that:

- the executive summary and dashboard come first;
- the core financials and KPIs follow;
- detailed schedules, segment breakouts, and supporting analysis are in later sections or appendices;
- every detailed section can be traced back to the summary.

A package that is all detail has no message. One that is all summary has no support. Layer them.

### Include Forward-Looking Information

Historical results tell what happened. Leadership also needs to see where the business is heading. Include forecast or outlook information where appropriate.

Forward-looking content may include:

- the updated forecast versus plan;
- cash flow projections and liquidity outlook;
- key risks and opportunities on the horizon;
- commitments and contingencies requiring future attention.

Clearly label forecasts and assumptions so readers understand what is projected versus actual. Avoid presenting forecasts with false precision.

### Connect Numbers To Narrative

Numbers alone do not tell the story. Each major result or variance should be accompanied by a brief narrative that explains the cause and the implication.

The narrative should:

- explain what drove the key variances;
- distinguish one-time from recurring factors;
- flag risks, opportunities, and watch items;
- state what is being done about unfavorable trends.

A package of numbers with no commentary leaves interpretation to the reader, who may draw the wrong conclusion.

### Ensure Accuracy, Consistency, And Timeliness

A package that is inaccurate, inconsistent, or late loses credibility quickly. The numbers must tie across sections, agree to the source systems, and be delivered in time to be useful.

Confirm before distribution:

- all numbers tie across the package and to the general ledger and source systems;
- KPI definitions and calculations are consistent with prior periods;
- the package is delivered on the agreed schedule;
- any known data limitations or estimates are disclosed.

One section showing a different revenue total than another destroys trust in the whole package.

### Curate And Refresh The Package Periodically

Packages accrete. Slides and schedules added for a specific period often stay forever. Review the package periodically to confirm every section still earns its place.

Retire content that no one uses, consolidate redundant schedules, and add content when the business or strategy shifts. A package that reflects the current business, not the history of every request ever made, stays useful.

### Protect Confidentiality And Distribution

Board and management packages contain sensitive financial, operational, and strategic information. Control distribution to the appropriate audience and protect the content accordingly.

Confirm:

- the distribution list is appropriate and authorized;
- sensitive content is marked and handled per policy;
- retention and deletion follow the entity's governance rules.

## Common Traps

### Accretion Without Curation

Adding slides each period and never removing any produces a bloated package no one reads. Curate and prune regularly.

### All Detail, No Message

A package that dumps every schedule without an executive summary forces the reader to find the story. Lead with what matters.

### All Summary, No Support

A few high-level numbers with no backup leaves questions unanswered and undermines credibility. Provide the supporting detail.

### Numbers That Do Not Tie

When the income statement in one section disagrees with the KPI dashboard in another, trust collapses. Reconcile every number across the package.

### No Variance Explanation

Presenting actual versus budget without explaining the variances leaves the reader guessing. Explain the causes.

### No Forward-Looking Content

A purely historical package tells leadership where they have been, not where they are going. Include forecast and outlook.

### Late Delivery

A package delivered too late to inform the meeting or decision loses its value. Hit the schedule.

### One-Size-Fits-All Distribution

Sending the full detailed package to an audience that needs only the summary, or a thin summary to an audience that needs detail, misses the mark. Tailor content and protect confidentiality.

## Self-Check

- Is the package designed around the specific audience and the decisions it must inform, with appropriate depth and format?
- Does it lead with an executive summary of the key results, variances, issues, and requested decisions?
- Are the core financials presented with actual versus budget, forecast, and prior period, plus variance explanation?
- Is the package layered, with summary first and supporting detail in appendices that trace back to the summary?
- Does it include forward-looking forecast, liquidity, and risk information, with assumptions clearly labeled?
- Is each major result or variance accompanied by a narrative explaining the cause, the recurrence, and the action?
- Do all numbers tie across sections and to the source systems, with consistent KPI definitions and on-time delivery?
- Is the package periodically curated to retire unused content and reflect the current business?
- Are distribution, confidentiality, and retention handled per governance policy?
- Have jurisdictional governance and confidentiality requirements for board materials been confirmed before distribution?
