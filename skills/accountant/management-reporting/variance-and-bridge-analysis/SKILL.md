# Variance And Bridge Analysis

Variance analysis explains why a number changed. Why did revenue rise, why did margin fall, why did actual miss budget. A bridge takes it further, decomposing the total change into its drivers, such as volume, price, mix, cost, and rate effects, so management can see not just that results moved but what moved them. The trap is that variance analysis is often shallow. It states that revenue increased because units and price both rose, without quantifying how much of the increase came from each. Or it lists variances without investigating causes, leaving management with a list of differences rather than an explanation of the business. Agents frequently produce variance tables that balance arithmetically but explain nothing, when the value is in the narrative that turns numbers into business understanding.

Use this skill before explaining variances, building a bridge, decomposing a change into drivers, or presenting variance analysis to management. The goal is to produce analysis that quantifies the drivers, explains the causes, and supports action.

This skill covers variance and bridge analysis methods. The appropriate decomposition method, the level of detail, and the presentation depend on the business model and the audience. Adapt the approach to the specific decision the analysis supports.

## Core Rules

### Frame The Variance Around A Specific Comparison

A variance is always relative to a baseline. State the baseline explicitly, whether it is prior period, prior year, budget, forecast, or plan.

Define for each analysis:

- the actual result and the comparison baseline;
- the period or periods being compared;
- the level of detail, total, segment, product, or line item;
- whether the comparison is like-for-like, adjusting for acquisitions, divestitures, or currency.

A variance without a clear baseline is ambiguous. Comparing to prior year and to budget can tell very different stories, and both may be needed.

### Quantify The Drivers, Do Not Just List Them

The core of bridge analysis is decomposing a total change into quantified drivers. Saying revenue rose due to volume and price is not analysis until each effect is measured.

A revenue bridge typically decomposes into:

- volume effect, the change in units times the base price;
- price effect, the change in price times the base volume;
- mix effect, the shift toward higher- or lower-priced products;
- currency effect, the impact of exchange rate changes;
- acquisition or divestiture effect, the impact of scope changes.

A margin or cost bridge decomposes into:

- volume or activity effect;
- rate or price effect of inputs;
- efficiency or usage effect;
- mix effect;
- fixed cost or overhead effect.

Each driver should carry a quantified amount, and the drivers should sum to the total variance. A bridge that does not foot to the total has a missing or miscalculated driver.

### Investigate Causes, Not Just Effects

Quantifying drivers explains what moved. Management also needs to know why. Each significant driver should be traced to its business cause.

For each material driver, ask:

- what business event or decision caused this movement;
- is it a one-time event or an ongoing trend;
- is it within management control or driven externally;
- does it affect other lines or future periods.

A volume increase driven by a one-time contract win is different from one driven by sustained market share gain. The narrative distinguishes signal from noise.

### Separate Recurring From One-Time Items

Mix recurring drivers with one-time items and the trend becomes unreadable. Separate them so management can see the underlying run rate.

Identify and flag:

- one-time events like a large settlement, a restructuring charge, or an asset sale;
- seasonal effects that recur annually;
- currency or commodity swings that may reverse;
- accounting or classification changes that distort comparability.

Presenting a clean underlying trend alongside the reported total helps management distinguish structural change from noise.

### Reconcile The Bridge To The Reported Numbers

The bridge must reconcile to the actual reported results. The starting point plus or minus each quantified driver must equal the ending point.

Confirm that:

- the bridge starts from the baseline and ends at the actual;
- every driver is quantified and included;
- no driver is double-counted or omitted;
- the total of drivers equals the total variance.

A bridge that does not foot is not credible and will be questioned. Reconcile before presenting.

### Present At The Right Level Of Detail

Too much detail buries the message, too little hides the drivers. Match the detail to the audience and the decision.

For executive audiences, lead with the three to five drivers that explain most of the variance, then support with detail. For operational teams, go deeper into the specific products, regions, or cost lines they control. The same variance can be presented at multiple levels for different audiences.

### Connect Variances To Action

Variance analysis that does not lead to action is academic. Each significant variance should prompt a question about what to do next.

For each material driver, address:

- whether the trend is favorable or unfavorable;
- whether it is expected to continue;
- what action, if any, management should take;
- whether the forecast or budget should be revised.

The analysis should end with implications, not just explanations.

### Apply Consistent Decomposition Methods

The method for decomposing a variance, such as which factor is held constant when measuring another, should be consistent across periods. Changing the method changes the apparent drivers and undermines comparability.

Document the decomposition convention and apply it the same way each period so that drivers are comparable over time.

## Common Traps

### Listing Variances Without Quantifying Drivers

Saying margin fell due to cost increases and mix shift, without quantifying each, is description, not analysis. Measure each driver.

### Stopping At Effects Without Causes

Quantifying that volume drove the revenue increase is the first step. Without explaining why volume rose, management cannot act. Trace to the cause.

### A Bridge That Does Not Foot

If the quantified drivers do not sum to the total variance, a driver is missing, double-counted, or miscalculated. Reconcile before presenting.

### Mixing Recurring And One-Time Items

Blending a one-time gain with ongoing trends makes the run rate unreadable. Separate them clearly.

### Inconsistent Decomposition Methods

Changing which factor is held constant period to period shifts the apparent drivers. Apply a consistent, documented method.

### Too Much Or Too Little Detail

Burying the message in line-item detail or presenting only a total variance both fail. Match the detail to the audience and decision.

### Analysis Without Implications

A variance report that explains what happened but not what to do about it stops short. Connect each material driver to action or forecast revision.

### Ignoring Comparability Issues

Comparing periods with different scope, currency, or accounting without adjustment distorts the drivers. Make the comparison like-for-like and disclose adjustments.

## Self-Check

- Is each variance framed around a specific, explicitly stated baseline, such as prior year, budget, or forecast?
- Are the drivers of each material variance quantified so they sum to the total change, with volume, price, mix, rate, and scope effects measured?
- Is each significant driver traced to its business cause, distinguishing one-time events from ongoing trends and controllable from external factors?
- Are recurring drivers separated from one-time items so the underlying trend is visible?
- Does the bridge reconcile exactly from baseline to actual, with every driver quantified and no double-counting or omission?
- Is the detail matched to the audience, leading with the key drivers for executives and going deeper for operational teams?
- Does the analysis connect each material variance to implications, actions, or forecast revisions rather than stopping at explanation?
- Is the decomposition method consistent and documented across periods?
- Have comparability issues like scope, currency, and accounting changes been adjusted and disclosed?
