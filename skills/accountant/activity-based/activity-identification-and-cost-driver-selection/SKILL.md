# Activity Identification And Cost Driver Selection

Activity-based costing (ABC) begins not with numbers but with a model of how the business actually works. Before any cost is assigned, someone must decide what activities exist, what drives the effort inside each activity, and how resources are consumed. When this foundation is wrong, every downstream cost figure inherits the error. Products look profitable when they are not, customers appear cheap to serve when they are expensive, and managers make decisions based on allocation conventions rather than economic reality.

Use this skill before defining activities, selecting cost drivers, building activity dictionaries, interviewing department heads about their work, or structuring an ABC model. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing an ABC model that looks rigorous but rests on arbitrary activities, weak drivers, or assumptions that do not match how work is actually performed.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Work, Not The General Ledger

An ABC model describes how the organization performs work and consumes resources. Do not begin by spreading ledger balances across products. Begin by understanding what people and equipment actually do.

Gather context first:

- interview the people who perform the work;
- observe processes or review process documentation;
- identify the major activities that consume time and resources;
- understand what triggers each activity and how often;
- note activities that are manual, automated, batch, or exception-driven.

A ledger account tells you how much was spent. It does not tell you why. The activity model answers the why.

### Define Activities At A Useful Level Of Aggregation

Activities that are too granular produce an unmanageable model with hundreds of cost pools. Activities that are too broad collapse distinct work into meaningless buckets and destroy the insight ABC is meant to provide.

Aim for activities that:

- represent a meaningful piece of work with a clear purpose;
- are performed by identifiable resources;
- have a measurable driver that differs from other activities;
- are stable enough to be costed repeatedly;
- are few enough that management can act on the results.

A practical activity dictionary usually contains somewhere between twenty and one hundred activities for a mid-sized operation, but the right number depends on diversity and complexity. Document each activity with a name, a description, the resources involved, and the output it produces.

### Select Drivers That Reflect Cause And Effect

A cost driver should measure the factor that actually causes the activity to be performed. The strength of ABC comes from replacing arbitrary bases such as direct labor hours with drivers that mirror real consumption.

Test each candidate driver:

- Does the driver correlate with the effort and resources consumed?
- Can the driver be measured reliably and at reasonable cost?
- Is the driver available for every cost object that consumes the activity?
- Does the driver distinguish between high-use and low-use cost objects?
- Is the driver stable enough to use period after period?

Common driver types include transaction counts, time, units processed, inspections, setups, engineering changes, purchase orders, customer contacts, and shipments. Avoid drivers chosen only because the data is easy to obtain. A convenient but weak driver reproduces the distortion ABC was meant to fix.

### Separate Unit, Batch, Product, And Facility-Level Activities

Costs behave differently depending on the level at which they are incurred. Mixing levels in one pool obscures how volume, complexity, and variety each drive cost.

Classify each activity:

- unit-level, performed for each unit produced or serviced;
- batch-level, performed for each batch or group;
- product or customer-level, performed to sustain a product or customer;
- facility-level, performed to sustain the operation as a whole.

Facility-level costs often have no clear driver tied to individual products. Allocating them with an arbitrary base can distort product costs, so consider whether they should be allocated at all, kept as a separate unallocated pool, or assigned using a deliberately broad driver with disclosed limitations.

### Validate The Model Against Operational Reality

An ABC model must be reconciled to both the general ledger and operational observation. A model that ties to the ledger but contradicts what managers observe on the floor is not reliable.

Validate by:

- reconciling total resource costs to the ledger;
- comparing activity costs to time studies or staffing data;
- checking that driver quantities sum to plausible totals;
- reviewing results with operating managers for reasonableness;
- testing whether the model explains known profitability patterns.

When the model says a long-accepted product is unprofitable, that may be a genuine insight or a modeling error. Distinguish the two before acting.

### Document Assumptions And Refresh Periodically

ABC models degrade. Processes change, drivers shift, and activities appear or disappear. A model built three years ago may no longer reflect the operation.

Document:

- the activity dictionary and its definitions;
- the resources assigned to each activity;
- the selected driver and the rationale for choosing it;
- the data sources for driver quantities;
- the period the model represents;
- known limitations and simplifications.

Schedule periodic refreshes. Re-interview, re-observe, and re-test drivers, especially after significant operational change, restructuring, automation, or shifts in product or customer mix.

### Respect Jurisdictional And Framework Limits

ABC is primarily a management accounting tool. Its outputs usually do not dictate external financial reporting, where inventory costing and overhead absorption follow the applicable reporting framework. Do not assume ABC results are acceptable for GAAP, IFRS, tax, or regulatory inventory valuation without confirming against the relevant standard. Where ABC informs pricing, government contracting, or regulated rate-setting, specific cost accounting standards may constrain the methods and drivers permitted. Confirm the governing rules with a qualified professional before relying on ABC outputs for external or regulated purposes.

## Common Traps

### Choosing Drivers Based On Data Availability

The most measurable driver is not always the most causal. Selecting a driver because the system already tracks it, rather than because it reflects consumption, reintroduces the distortion ABC was meant to eliminate.

### Over-Granular Activity Definitions

Defining hundreds of micro-activities creates a model that is expensive to maintain, hard to explain, and no more accurate than a simpler one. Activities should be aggregated enough to be managed.

### Treating Interview Estimates As Precise

Time and effort data gathered from interviews is approximate. Presenting ABC results to the dollar when the underlying driver data is estimated to the nearest ten percent conveys false precision. Acknowledge the uncertainty.

### Ignoring Sustaining And Facility Costs

Focusing only on unit and batch activities leaves product and facility-level costs unassigned or dumped into an arbitrary pool. These costs are real and must be handled deliberately.

### Failing To Reconcile To The General Ledger

An ABC model that does not tie back to total spending cannot be trusted for decisions. Reconciliation is not optional. Unreconciled models silently diverge from the official books.

### Keeping A Stale Model

A model that once reflected the operation becomes a liability when processes change. Decisions based on outdated activities and drivers can be worse than no ABC at all.

### Confusing ABC With External Reporting

ABC product costs often differ from GAAP or IFRS inventory costs. Using ABC numbers in external statements or tax filings without adjustment can violate reporting requirements.

## Self-Check

- Does the model begin with an understanding of actual work rather than ledger balances?
- Are activities defined at a level that is both meaningful and manageable, with a documented activity dictionary?
- Does each cost driver reflect cause and effect, and is it measurable and available for all cost objects?
- Are unit, batch, product, customer, and facility-level activities separated rather than blended?
- Does the model reconcile to the general ledger and to operational observation by managers?
- Are assumptions, data sources, the represented period, and known limitations documented?
- Is there a plan to refresh the model after operational change rather than letting it go stale?
- Does the output acknowledge that ABC is a management tool and that external reporting, tax, and regulated uses require confirmation against the applicable framework and qualified professional review?
