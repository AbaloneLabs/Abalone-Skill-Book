# Bank Account Reconciliation

A bank reconciliation is the process of explaining the difference between the cash balance on the bank statement and the cash balance in the general ledger. It is one of the oldest and most important controls in accounting because cash is the most liquid and most theft-prone asset. A clean bank rec proves that every cash movement is recorded, that no deposits are missing, and that no unauthorized disbursements have occurred. The trap is that a bank rec can balance to the penny while still hiding problems, stale outstanding checks, duplicate deposits, missing entries, or items parked in suspense to force the tie. Agents often treat the bank rec as a matching exercise rather than a control, completing it without investigating the items that make it tie.

Use this skill before performing a bank reconciliation, investigating reconciling items, designing bank rec procedures, or reviewing recs for control quality. The goal is to ensure every reconciling item is real, current, and resolved, not merely that the two balances agree.

This skill covers operational bank reconciliation. Specific banking products, positive pay, multi-currency, and controlled disbursement features vary by bank and jurisdiction. Confirm the applicable banking setup before relying on these procedures.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile Every Account Every Period

Every bank account should be reconciled every period, without exception. An unreconciled account is an uncontrolled account. The reconciliation is the control that confirms cash is complete and accurate.

Establish that:

- every operating, payroll, savings, money market, and trust account is reconciled;
- the reconciliation is performed by someone independent of cash handling where possible;
- the reconciliation is reviewed and approved by a second person;
- recs are completed within a defined timeframe after period end.

Late or skipped recs allow errors and irregularities to persist undetected.

### Start From The Bank Balance And Adjust To Book, Or Vice Versa

The reconciliation explains the difference between bank and book by listing items recorded in one but not the other.

Typical reconciling items include:

- deposits in transit, recorded in books but not yet on the bank;
- outstanding checks, recorded in books but not yet cleared the bank;
- bank fees and interest not yet recorded in books;
- electronic transfers or direct debits on the bank not yet recorded in books;
- errors in either the bank or the books.

Each item should be identifiable, supported, and expected to clear in a future period. Items that never clear indicate an error or a stale item.

### Investigate Every Reconciling Item

A reconciling item is not a place to park a difference. Each item must be real, supported, and tracked to resolution.

For each item, ask:

- what is this item, and when did it arise;
- is it expected to clear next period, and if not, why;
- is there a missing entry, a duplicate, or an error behind it;
- has a similar item appeared before, suggesting a recurring problem.

Deposits in transit should clear within a few days. Outstanding checks should clear within a normal window. Items that linger for months are red flags.

### Clear Stale Outstanding Checks

Outstanding checks that never clear are a common problem. A check written months ago that has not been cashed may be lost, stale, or part of an escheatment obligation.

Manage outstanding checks by:

- reviewing the outstanding check list each period;
- following up with payees on old checks;
- voiding and reissuing checks that are confirmed lost;
- considering escheatment, unclaimed property rules, for checks stale beyond the jurisdictional threshold.

Leaving stale checks on the rec indefinitely inflates the book balance relative to reality and may create an unclaimed property liability.

### Do Not Force The Rec To Tie With Suspense Or Plug Entries

If the bank and book do not reconcile, the answer is to find the difference, not to plug it into a suspense or miscellaneous account. A plug entry hides the problem and defeats the control.

When the rec does not tie:

- recheck the arithmetic and the starting balances;
- verify that all bank transactions were recorded;
- check for transpositions, duplicates, and omitted entries;
- trace specific transactions to support;
- escalate if the difference cannot be found.

A small unexplained difference should be investigated, not written off casually, because the same error could mask a larger problem.

### Record Bank Fees, Interest, And Direct Items Promptly

Items that appear on the bank statement but not yet in the books, such as fees, interest, direct debits, and returns, should be recorded promptly so the book balance stays current.

Recording these only at reconciliation time, rather than as they are identified, means the book cash balance is inaccurate between recs and decisions based on it may be wrong.

### Reconcile To The General Ledger, Not Just To The Subledger

The bank rec should tie to the general ledger cash account. If cash is also tracked in a subledger or treasury system, that subledger must reconcile to the general ledger, and the general ledger must reconcile to the bank. Two layers of reconciliation confirm completeness.

### Review And Approve The Completed Reconciliation

A bank rec that is prepared but never reviewed is only half a control. A second person, independent of the preparer, should review the rec, confirm that reconciling items are valid and aging appropriately, and approve it. The review is what catches manipulation, overlooked items, and forced ties.

## Common Traps

### Parking Differences In Suspense To Force A Tie

When the rec does not balance, plugging the difference into a suspense account makes it tie but hides the real error. Suspense balances should be investigated and cleared, not used as a permanent balancing tool.

### Ignoring Old Outstanding Checks

Checks that sit on the rec month after month may be lost, stale, or subject to escheatment. Leaving them inflates the apparent book balance and may create an unreported liability.

### Treating A Balanced Rec As A Clean Rec

A rec that ties to the penny can still hide stale items, duplicates, or missing entries that happen to offset. The quality of the reconciling items matters as much as the tie.

### Skipping Recs For Small Or Inactive Accounts

Small or rarely used accounts are often skipped, which makes them attractive targets for diverting funds. Every account, including inactive ones, should be reconciled.

### Recording Bank Fees Only At Reconciliation

Bank fees, interest, and direct debits that sit unrecorded until reconciliation distort the book balance in the interim. Record them as they are identified.

### Same Person Prepares And Approves

When the person who handles cash also prepares and approves the rec, the independent check is gone. Even in small organizations, a different person should review.

### Failing To Reconcile Subledger To General Ledger To Bank

If the treasury subledger does not tie to the general ledger, or the general ledger does not tie to the bank, a break can hide in the gap. All three layers must reconcile.

## Self-Check

- Is every bank account, including small and inactive ones, reconciled every period within a defined timeframe?
- Does the reconciliation list and explain every reconciling item, with each item real, supported, and expected to clear?
- Are deposits in transit and outstanding checks reviewed for aging, with stale checks voided, reissued, or escheated as required?
- Are differences investigated rather than plugged into suspense or miscellaneous accounts to force a tie?
- Are bank fees, interest, and direct items recorded promptly as identified rather than only at reconciliation?
- Does the cash subledger reconcile to the general ledger, and does the general ledger reconcile to the bank?
- Is the completed reconciliation reviewed and approved by a second person independent of the preparer and of cash handling?
- Are unexplained differences escalated and resolved rather than written off casually?
- Have bank-specific features, multi-currency, and jurisdictional escheatment rules been confirmed before relying on these procedures?
