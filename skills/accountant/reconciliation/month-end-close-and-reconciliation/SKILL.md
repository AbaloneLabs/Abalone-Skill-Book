# Month End Close And Reconciliation

The close process turns transaction records into reports that can be trusted. A month-end close is not just "finish entering transactions." It is a controlled process for completing entries, reconciling balances, reviewing unusual activity, correcting errors, and deciding whether the period is ready to report. Without a close discipline, financial statements can look polished while still containing duplicate transactions, missing liabilities, stale receivables, unsupported assets, misstated revenue, or hidden suspense balances.

Use this skill before closing a period, reconciling bank or balance sheet accounts, preparing management reports, handing books to a tax preparer, assessing whether a period can be locked, or reviewing financial results for decision-making. The goal is to prevent reports from being treated as final before the underlying accounts are complete and explainable.

## Core Rules

### Define The Close Scope And Calendar

Start by defining what period is being closed, which entities or locations are included, who owns each task, and when each task is due. A close without ownership becomes a collection of informal reminders.

Clarify:

- close date;
- reporting deadline;
- included legal entities;
- included bank and credit accounts;
- revenue systems;
- payroll systems;
- inventory systems;
- billing systems;
- required management reports;
- review and approval steps;
- reopening policy.

Separate transaction completion, reconciliation, review, adjustment, approval, and reporting. Do not let one vague "close books" task hide multiple dependencies.

### Reconcile Cash And Bank Activity First

Cash accounts are often the entry point for many errors. Reconcile each bank, credit card, payment processor, petty cash, and clearing account to an external statement or reliable system report.

Review:

- beginning balance agrees to prior close;
- ending balance agrees to statement;
- deposits in transit are valid;
- outstanding checks are still expected;
- duplicate imports are removed;
- transfers match both sides;
- merchant deposits reconcile from gross activity to net deposits;
- bank fees and interest are recorded;
- stale reconciling items are investigated;
- uncleared items are not hiding errors.

A reconciliation is not complete because the difference is zero. It is complete when reconciling items are legitimate, aged items are explained, and the preparer understands what remains open.

### Review Receivables, Revenue, And Customer Balances

Accounts receivable and revenue are high-risk because they depend on timing, customer terms, performance, collectability, credits, and billing accuracy. Review the detail, not only the total.

Check:

- invoices issued for completed work;
- revenue recorded in the correct period;
- unbilled revenue or work in progress;
- deferred revenue for payments received before performance;
- customer deposits;
- credit memos;
- refunds;
- bad debt or collectability issues;
- aged receivables;
- unapplied payments;
- customer disputes.

Cutoff is critical. Billing in a month does not necessarily mean the revenue belongs in that month. Receiving cash in a month does not necessarily mean revenue was earned in that month.

### Review Payables, Expenses, And Vendor Balances

Expenses are frequently understated when bills arrive after month-end or when work has been performed but not invoiced. Ask what obligations exist, not only what has already appeared in the accounting system.

Check:

- unpaid vendor bills;
- received goods or services not yet invoiced;
- recurring expenses missing from the period;
- credit card charges not yet imported;
- payroll earned but unpaid;
- payroll taxes and benefits payable;
- sales tax or VAT payable if applicable;
- contractor accruals;
- utilities, rent, software, insurance, and professional fees;
- vendor credits and unapplied payments.

If the business uses accrual accounting, missing liabilities can make profit look better than reality. If the business uses cash accounting, still identify material unpaid obligations for management understanding.

### Validate Accruals, Deferrals, And Prepaids

Accruals and deferrals are estimates or allocations, so they need documentation and review. Do not copy prior-month entries without asking whether the underlying obligation or benefit still exists.

For each recurring adjustment, document:

- purpose;
- calculation;
- source data;
- period covered;
- reversal date if applicable;
- owner;
- review evidence.

Prepaid expenses should decline as the related benefit is consumed. Deferred revenue should decline as performance obligations are satisfied. Accrued expenses should reverse when the actual bill arrives. Stale balances mean the close process is carrying old assumptions forward.

### Reconcile Every Material Balance Sheet Account

A balance sheet account should represent something real: cash, receivable, inventory, asset, liability, equity, or a documented timing difference. If nobody can explain what a balance represents, the account is not reconciled.

Reconcile:

- cash and credit cards;
- accounts receivable;
- inventory;
- fixed assets;
- accumulated depreciation;
- prepaids;
- deposits;
- accounts payable;
- accrued expenses;
- payroll liabilities;
- debt and interest;
- taxes payable;
- deferred revenue;
- owner equity;
- intercompany balances.

Use external evidence where possible. For internal-only balances, use a detailed schedule that ties to the general ledger and explains additions, reductions, and ending balance.

### Perform Cutoff And Completeness Review

Cutoff review asks whether transactions are in the right period. Completeness review asks whether all required transactions are present.

Review activity around period-end:

- sales before and after close date;
- shipments or delivery dates;
- service completion dates;
- customer deposits;
- vendor invoices received after close;
- payroll period dates;
- inventory receipts;
- credit card charges;
- bank transactions after month-end that relate to the prior period.

Look both ways. Some current-period transactions may belong next period, and some next-period entries may belong in the current period.

### Analyze Variances And Unexpected Results

After reconciliations and adjustments, review results for reasonableness. Variance analysis is a detection control. It identifies errors, missing activity, unusual events, or genuine business changes that need explanation.

Compare:

- current month to prior month;
- current month to budget;
- year-to-date to prior year;
- gross margin trends;
- payroll as a percentage of revenue;
- major expense categories;
- balance sheet account movements;
- cash flow versus profit;
- revenue by product, customer, or channel.

Do not accept "it changed" as an explanation. A useful explanation states the driver, the amount, whether it is one-time or recurring, and whether action is needed.

### Lock Or Reopen Periods Deliberately

Once a period is reviewed and reported, lock it when the system allows. Closed periods should not be changed casually. If a closed period must be adjusted, document the reason, approval, report impact, and whether prior external reporting needs correction.

Set rules for:

- who may post to closed periods;
- materiality threshold for reopening;
- approval required;
- whether changes are posted to current period or prior period;
- communication to tax preparer, lender, investor, board, or management.

The integrity of trend reporting depends on knowing when numbers changed and why.

## Common Traps

### Closing Only The Profit And Loss

Many teams review income and expenses but ignore the balance sheet. This misses stale assets, invalid liabilities, duplicate receivables, unreconciled credit cards, and old clearing accounts. The balance sheet is where many close problems accumulate.

### Treating Reconciliation As A Checkbox

A reconciliation that contains old unexplained items is not reliable. A zero difference can hide incorrect matching, duplicate entries, or offsetting errors. Review the quality of reconciling items, not only the mathematical difference.

### Copying Prior-Month Journal Entries Blindly

Recurring entries are useful, but they can preserve wrong estimates. Accruals, prepaids, depreciation, deferred revenue, and allocations should be refreshed against current evidence.

### Ignoring Subledger And General Ledger Differences

Accounts receivable, accounts payable, inventory, payroll, and fixed assets often have subledgers. The subledger total should tie to the general ledger. Differences should be explained and cleared. Do not report from one system while the official ledger says another number.

### Letting Deadlines Replace Review

A close deadline matters, but speed does not make the numbers right. If a material account is unreconciled or a major estimate is unsupported, disclose that limitation rather than presenting the report as fully final.

### Missing Access And Segregation Issues

The person preparing a reconciliation should not always be the same person approving it, especially for cash, payroll, refunds, vendor changes, and journal entries. Small teams may need compensating controls, but they should still recognize the risk.

### Forgetting Nonrecurring Events

Financing, asset purchases, litigation, insurance claims, owner transactions, restructuring, grants, acquisitions, large refunds, and unusual contracts may need special accounting treatment. Do not let unusual events pass through normal recurring rules without review.

## Self-Check

- Is the close scope clear by period, entity, account, system, owner, and deadline?
- Are cash, bank, credit card, payment processor, and clearing accounts reconciled to external evidence?
- Are receivables, revenue, customer deposits, deferred revenue, credits, and collectability reviewed?
- Are payables, accrued expenses, payroll liabilities, taxes payable, and recurring missing bills reviewed?
- Are accruals, deferrals, prepaids, depreciation, and allocations supported by current calculations?
- Is every material balance sheet account tied to a schedule, subledger, statement, or documented explanation?
- Has cutoff been reviewed around the period-end date for both revenue and expenses?
- Have unusual variances been explained with drivers, amounts, and follow-up actions?
- Are stale reconciling items, suspense balances, and miscellaneous accounts investigated?
- Is the period locked or reopened only under a documented policy with approval and impact assessment?
