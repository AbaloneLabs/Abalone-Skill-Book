# Reversing And Correcting Entries

Reversing and correcting entries are where accounting discipline is most often tested. A reversal is meant to undo a temporary entry cleanly so the next period starts fresh. A correction is meant to fix an error that was already posted. Both look simple on the surface, but each carries risks around timing, period integrity, audit trail, and whether the fix itself needs disclosure. Agents frequently treat corrections as ordinary entries and reverse things at the wrong time, creating duplicate expense, understated balances, or a trail that auditors cannot follow.

Use this skill before reversing accruals, correcting posted errors, reclassifying amounts between accounts, or adjusting entries in a closed period. The goal is to make each reversal and correction clean, traceable, and compliant with the applicable framework rather than a quiet edit that obscures what happened.

This skill addresses general correction mechanics. The distinction between an error correction requiring retrospective restatement and a prospective change depends on the applicable standard such as US GAAP, IFRS, or local GAAP. Confirm the governing framework and consult a qualified accountant for material prior-period errors.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Difference Between Reversal And Correction

A reversing entry automatically or manually undoes a temporary entry, typically an accrual, so that when the actual invoice or payment arrives it is recorded without doubling the expense. A correcting entry fixes an error in an already-posted transaction, such as a wrong account, wrong amount, or wrong period.

Confusing the two leads to problems. Reversing an entry that should have been corrected leaves the error in place. Correcting an entry that should have been reversed creates a duplicate. Before acting, determine whether the original entry was temporary by design or erroneous by mistake.

### Reverse Accruals Consistently And On Time

Accruals created at period end should be reversed in the following period so that the actual transaction replaces the estimate. If reversal is skipped, the expense or revenue is recorded twice, once in the accrual and once when the real invoice arrives.

Confirm for each accrual:

- whether the system is set to auto-reverse on the first day of the next period;
- whether manual reversal is needed and has been scheduled;
- that the reversal exactly mirrors the original entry in accounts and amount;
- that any partial accrual is handled when the actual amount differs.

Document the reversal convention used so that all accruals follow the same rule. Inconsistency between auto-reverse and manual-reverse accruals is a common source of duplicate recording.

### Choose The Right Correction Method Based On Period And Materiality

The method for correcting an error depends on when the error occurred and whether it is material.

Consider:

- if the error is in the current open period, a direct correcting entry or void-and-repost is usually appropriate;
- if the error is in a closed prior period but immaterial, a current-period correction may be acceptable depending on policy;
- if the error is material to a prior period, retrospective restatement may be required, adjusting opening balances and comparative figures;
- if the error affects a previously issued financial statement, tax filing, or regulatory filing, escalation is required before correcting.

Do not assume a current-period entry is always sufficient. Material prior-period errors handled as current-period adjustments can misstate both periods and trigger audit findings.

### Preserve The Audit Trail In Every Correction

A correction should never erase the original error. The books must show what was originally recorded, what was wrong, what was changed, and who approved the change.

For each correction, capture:

- the original entry reference and amount;
- a description of the error and how it was identified;
- the correcting entry with cross-reference to the original;
- the approver and date;
- any impact on prior reports, tax filings, or covenants.

Deleting or overwriting the original entry destroys evidence and makes it impossible to verify what happened. Use void-and-repost or adjusting entries rather than silent edits.

### Handle Reclassifications Separately From Corrections

A reclassification moves a balance between accounts, segments, or entities without changing total assets, liabilities, revenue, or expense. It is not an error correction in the strict sense, but it still needs support and approval.

Reclassifications are common at period end when mapping accounts to financial statement lines or correcting segment allocation. Document the reason for each reclassification so that the movement is explainable and not confused with a real change in activity.

### Escalate Before Touching Closed Periods, Tax, Or External Reports

Corrections that affect a closed period, a filed tax return, a published financial statement, a lender covenant calculation, or a regulatory filing carry consequences beyond the general ledger. They may require amended filings, disclosure, lender notification, or audit committee awareness.

Before posting such a correction, confirm:

- whether the period can be reopened and who authorizes it;
- whether an amended tax filing is needed;
- whether financial statement disclosure or restatement is required;
- whether lenders, auditors, or regulators must be notified.

Never quietly adjust a balance that has already been reported externally without going through the proper escalation.

### Reconcile After Correction To Confirm The Fix Worked

After posting a correction, verify that the affected account balances now agree to expectation and that no new imbalance was introduced. A correction that fixes one account but breaks a reconciliation or a subledger tie-out has created a new problem.

## Common Traps

### Forgetting To Reverse An Accrual

The most common reversal error is simply forgetting. The accrual posts at month end, the actual invoice arrives next month, and both hit the books. The expense is doubled until someone notices. Build reversal into the close checklist.

### Reversing The Wrong Amount Or Wrong Accounts

A reversal must mirror the original exactly. If the original accrued three accounts and the reversal hits only two, a residual balance remains. If the amount differs, the difference sits in the books permanently. Always reverse from the original entry reference.

### Treating A Material Prior-Period Error As A Current-Period Adjustment

Posting a material correction to the current period hides the error in the prior period and distorts the current period. This can trigger restatement requirements and audit qualifications. Confirm materiality and the applicable standard before choosing the period of correction.

### Silent Edits That Destroy The Original Record

Some systems allow editing a posted transaction directly. This erases the error as if it never happened, which removes the audit trail and makes it impossible to assess what went wrong. Prefer void-and-repost over direct edits.

### Correcting Without Checking Downstream Impact

An error in one account may have flowed into a depreciation schedule, a reserve calculation, a commission accrual, a tax provision, or a management report. Correcting the source entry without tracing downstream effects leaves dependent balances wrong.

### Confusing A Change In Estimate With An Error Correction

A change in estimate, such as revising a useful life or a reserve rate, is applied prospectively. An error correction may require retrospective treatment. Applying the wrong treatment misstates the period of change and comparative periods.

### Reversing Entries That Were Not Meant To Be Temporary

Not every adjusting entry should be reversed. Depreciation, amortization, and certain allocations are permanent adjustments. Reversing them creates incorrect balances. Confirm which entries are designated as reversible before reversing.

## Self-Check

- Is the action a reversal of a temporary entry or a correction of an error, and has the distinction been made explicitly?
- Are period-end accruals reversed consistently and on time, with the reversal mirroring the original accounts and amount?
- For each error, has the period of occurrence and materiality been assessed to determine whether current-period correction or retrospective restatement applies?
- Does every correction preserve the original entry, document the error, cross-reference the fix, and record the approver?
- Are reclassifications documented separately and not confused with error corrections or real activity changes?
- Have corrections affecting closed periods, tax filings, published statements, covenants, or regulatory reports been escalated before posting?
- After correction, do the affected account balances and downstream calculations reconcile to expectation?
- Has the applicable accounting framework been confirmed and a qualified accountant consulted for material prior-period errors?
