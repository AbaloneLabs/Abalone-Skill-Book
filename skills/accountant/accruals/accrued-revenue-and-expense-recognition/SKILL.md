# Accrued Revenue And Expense Recognition

Accruals are the entries that bridge the gap between when economic activity happens and when the paperwork or cash catches up. Accrued revenue recognizes revenue that has been earned but not yet billed. Accrued expense recognizes cost that has been incurred but not yet paid or invoiced. Both are essential for accrual-basis statements to reflect the true period result. The danger is that accruals are estimates and judgments wrapped in a journal entry. They are easy to get wrong, easy to forget, and easy to leave in place after the real transaction arrives. Agents frequently accrue the wrong amount, accrue in the wrong direction, or fail to reverse an accrual when the actual invoice posts, creating doubled revenue or expense that distorts the period.

Use this skill before recording accrued revenue or expense, building period-end accrual procedures, or reviewing accrual balances during the close. The goal is to recognize the right amount in the right period and to ensure every accrual is supportable, reversible, and reconciled.

This skill covers general accrual recognition mechanics. Specific standards for revenue accruals, such as percentage-of-completion or performance obligation methods, and specific expense accrual rules depend on the applicable framework. Confirm the governing standard and consult a qualified accountant for complex or material accruals.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Accrued Revenue When Earned, Not When Billed

Accrued revenue arises when the entity has performed its obligation and earned revenue but has not yet issued an invoice. Under accrual accounting, the revenue belongs in the period earned, regardless of billing timing.

Accrued revenue typically applies to:

- services performed but not yet billed, especially when billing is monthly or milestone-based;
- subscription or contract revenue where the billing cycle lags the service period;
- interest earned but not yet received;
- usage-based revenue where the measurement period differs from the billing period.

The accrual should be supported by a calculation of the work performed or the service delivered, with a receivable recorded for the unbilled amount. When the invoice is later issued, the accrual should be reversed or cleared so revenue is not double-counted.

### Recognize Accrued Expense When Incurred, Not When Paid

Accrued expense arises when the entity has received a good or service but has not yet been invoiced or paid. The cost belongs in the period of consumption.

Common accrued expenses include:

- wages and salaries earned by employees but not yet paid at period end;
- bonus and commission accruals tied to the period;
- utilities and services consumed but not yet billed;
- professional fees for work performed but not yet invoiced;
- interest accrued on debt between payment dates;
- warranty obligations tied to products sold in the period.

Each accrual should be based on a defensible estimate or actual calculation. Vague accruals without support become audit findings and restatement risks.

### Base Every Accrual On A Supportable Calculation

An accrual is only as good as its underlying support. A round-number guess is not an accrual, it is an adjustment without basis.

Support for an accrual may include:

- timesheets or payroll records for wage accruals;
- meter readings or usage data for utility accruals;
- contract terms and progress for service accruals;
- prior-period actuals adjusted for known changes;
- vendor confirmations or receiving records for uninvoiced purchases;
- interest rate and days outstanding for interest accruals.

Document the calculation method and retain it. An auditor should be able to reproduce the accrual amount from the support.

### Reverse Accruals When The Actual Transaction Arrives

Accruals are temporary. When the real invoice or payment is recorded, the accrual must be reversed or cleared to avoid double-counting.

Confirm the reversal convention:

- set period-end accruals to auto-reverse on the first day of the next period;
- ensure manual accruals are scheduled for reversal;
- verify that the reversal mirrors the original in accounts and amount;
- handle partial accruals when the actual amount differs from the estimate.

A common error is accruing an expense at month end and then recording the full vendor invoice the next month without reversing, which doubles the expense. Build reversal discipline into the close.

### Review Accrual Balances For Stale And Residual Items

Accrual accounts should be reviewed each period. Balances that linger without explanation often indicate a reversal that did not post, an estimate that was never trued up, or an accrual that should have been cleared.

The review should check:

- whether each accrual balance is supported by a current calculation;
- whether any accrual should have reversed but did not;
- whether residual balances remain after the actual transaction posted;
- whether the accrual methodology is still appropriate.

Stale accrual balances distort the balance sheet and can hide errors or manipulation.

### Distinguish Accruals From Estimates And Reserves

An accrual recognizes a specific incurred amount, even if estimated. A reserve or provision recognizes an estimated obligation whose amount or timing is uncertain, such as a warranty reserve, bad debt allowance, or litigation provision. The two are related but involve different levels of judgment and different disclosure.

Do not blur the line. A specific accrued invoice is not the same as a general reserve, and the support and review requirements differ.

### Apply Consistency In Accrual Methodology

Once an accrual methodology is established for a type of cost, apply it consistently. Changing the method period to period, for example switching from a days-outstanding calculation to a flat percentage, changes the period result without an economic basis and undermines comparability.

If a methodology change is justified, document it and consider whether retrospective adjustment is needed.

## Common Traps

### Forgetting To Reverse An Accrual

The single most common accrual error. The accrual posts at month end, the real invoice arrives next month, and both hit the books. The period is overstated until someone catches it. Auto-reversal and close-checklist discipline prevent this.

### Accruing Based On A Round Number Without Support

An accrual that is a clean round number with no calculation behind it is a red flag. Auditors and reviewers will challenge it. Base every accrual on a defensible method, even if the method is a simple prior-period extrapolation.

### Accruing In The Wrong Direction

Accruing revenue when the obligation is actually a deferred revenue liability, or accruing an expense that is actually a prepaid asset, reverses the correct treatment. Confirm whether the entity has earned the revenue or incurred the cost before posting.

### Leaving Residual Balances After The Actual Posts

When the actual invoice differs from the accrual, the difference should be resolved, not left in the accrual account. A residual accrual balance that grows period over period signals unreversed or untrued accruals.

### Using Accruals To Smooth Earnings

Accruals are estimates, which creates room to manage results. Inflating an expense accrual to dampen a strong period, or under-accruing to support a weak one, is earnings management. Accruals should reflect the best estimate, not a desired result.

### Confusing Accrued Revenue With Deferred Revenue

Accrued revenue is earned but unbilled. Deferred revenue is billed but unearned. They move in opposite directions. Recording a prepayment as accrued revenue recognizes revenue before it is earned, violating the standard.

### Ignoring Accruals For Related Parties Or Intercompany

Services between related entities or intercompany arrangements still need accruals if the performance and billing periods differ. Skipping these accruals leaves intercompany balances and consolidated results inaccurate.

## Self-Check

- Is accrued revenue recognized only when the performance obligation is satisfied and earned, supported by a calculation of work performed?
- Is accrued expense recognized in the period of consumption, supported by a defensible estimate or actual data?
- Does every accrual have documented support, such as timesheets, usage data, contract terms, or prior-period actuals, that allows the amount to be reproduced?
- Are period-end accruals set to reverse, and are manual reversals scheduled so the actual invoice or payment does not double-count?
- Are accrual balances reviewed each period for stale items, missed reversals, and residual balances?
- Are accruals distinguished from reserves and provisions, with the appropriate level of judgment and support applied to each?
- Is the accrual methodology applied consistently, with any justified change documented?
- Are related-party and intercompany accruals included where performance and billing periods differ?
- Has the applicable revenue and expense recognition standard been confirmed and a qualified accountant consulted for complex or material accruals?
