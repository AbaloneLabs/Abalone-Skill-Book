# Period Allocation And Cutoff Accruals

Period allocation and cutoff accruals are the mechanisms that ensure each accounting period contains the revenue and expense that belong to it, no more and no less. A cost that benefits three months must be allocated across those three months. A service consumed at month end but not yet invoiced must be accrued into that month. Cutoff is the testing discipline that confirms transactions near the period boundary landed on the correct side. These tasks are deceptively important because they directly determine period profit, trend lines, and comparability. Agents often treat cutoff as a mechanical date check and allocation as a simple straight-line spread, when both require judgment about which period a transaction truly belongs to and whether the allocation basis reflects real consumption.

Use this skill before allocating costs across periods, building cutoff accrual procedures, or testing period-end completeness. The goal is to ensure every period captures its own activity and that allocations and accruals are supportable and consistent.

This skill covers allocation and cutoff principles. Specific standards for revenue cutoff, long-term contract allocation, and regulated cost allocation depend on the applicable framework and industry. Confirm the governing requirements before applying these rules.

## Core Rules

### Allocate Costs To The Periods That Benefit

When a cost benefits multiple periods, it should be allocated across those periods rather than dropped entirely into the payment period. The allocation basis should reflect how the benefit is consumed.

Allocation decisions require:

- identifying the total cost and the benefit period;
- choosing a basis that reflects consumption, such as straight-line, usage, or time-weighted;
- applying the basis consistently across periods;
- reviewing the allocation when the underlying cost or benefit changes.

An annual insurance premium benefits twelve months and should be allocated monthly. A marketing campaign may benefit a specific window and should be allocated to that window. Match the allocation to the economics.

### Build Cutoff Accruals For Uninvoiced Period Costs

At every period end, costs that have been incurred but not yet invoiced must be accrued so the period is complete. Cutoff accruals cover the gap between consumption and invoicing.

Typical cutoff accruals include:

- goods or services received but not yet billed by the supplier;
- utility and telecom usage through period end;
- professional services performed through period end;
- freight and logistics costs for shipments in transit;
- subscription and software costs for the period.

The accrual should be based on receiving records, usage data, contract terms, or a reasoned estimate from prior periods. A period that omits these costs understates expense and overstates profit.

### Test Cutoff By Examining Transactions Near The Boundary

Cutoff testing confirms that transactions near the period end are recorded in the correct period. This applies to both revenue and expense, and to both shipping and receiving.

For sales cutoff, examine:

- shipments made just before and just after period end;
- whether revenue was recorded in the period the control transferred;
- whether goods shipped after period end were excluded from current-period revenue.

For purchase cutoff, examine:

- goods received just before and just after period end;
- whether the cost was recorded in the period the goods were received;
- whether goods in transit were correctly included or excluded based on shipping terms.

Cutoff errors move revenue or expense between adjacent periods, distorting both. The risk is highest in the days around the period boundary.

### Match Revenue Cutoff To The Performance Obligation

Revenue cutoff is not just about shipping date. It depends on when control of the good or service transfers to the customer under the applicable revenue standard.

Consider:

- for goods, when control transfers, often at delivery or shipment depending on terms;
- for services, when the service is performed;
- for subscriptions, over the period the service is provided;
- for milestones, when the milestone is achieved.

Recording revenue before control transfers, or deferring it after, violates the standard and misstates the period.

### Apply Shipping Terms To Goods In Transit

For inventory and cost of goods sold cutoff, the shipping terms determine who owns goods in transit at period end.

Under FOB shipping point terms, the buyer owns the goods once they leave the seller, so goods in transit belong to the buyer. Under FOB destination terms, the seller owns the goods until they arrive, so goods in transit belong to the seller. Applying the wrong terms puts inventory and the related payable or sale in the wrong period and on the wrong balance sheet.

### Reconcile Accrual Estimates To Actuals

Cutoff accruals are often estimates. When the actual invoice arrives, compare it to the accrual and true up any material difference.

The true-up process should:

- compare the actual invoice to the reversed accrual;
- investigate material variances to improve future estimates;
- record any difference in the appropriate period;
- avoid leaving residual accrual balances unexplained.

A pattern of large variances signals that the estimation method needs improvement.

### Maintain Consistency In Allocation And Cutoff Methods

Once an allocation basis or cutoff accrual method is established, apply it consistently. Changing methods period to period without an economic reason undermines comparability and can look like earnings management.

Document the methods used so that the same approach is followed each close, and so a new person can execute the process correctly.

## Common Traps

### Dropping Multi-Period Costs Into The Payment Period

A twelve-month insurance premium expensed entirely in the payment month distorts that month and understates the following months. Allocate to the benefit period.

### Skipping Cutoff Accruals Because The Invoice Has Not Arrived

The absence of an invoice does not mean the cost does not belong in the period. If the service was consumed, accrue it. Waiting for the invoice shifts the expense to the next period.

### Testing Cutoff Only On One Side

Examining sales cutoff but not purchase cutoff, or shipping but not receiving, leaves half the boundary untested. Both directions matter for period accuracy.

### Using The Wrong Shipping Terms For Goods In Transit

Assuming all in-transit goods belong to the seller, or all to the buyer, ignores the actual terms. Apply the specific FOB terms to each shipment near period end.

### Recording Revenue On Invoice Date Rather Than Performance Date

The invoice date is an administrative event. Revenue belongs in the period control transfers or performance occurs. Recording on invoice date can shift revenue across periods.

### Leaving Large Estimate Variances Unexplained

When the actual invoice differs significantly from the accrual and no one investigates, the estimation method never improves and the period result remains unreliable. True up and analyze variances.

### Changing Allocation Or Cutoff Methods To Smooth Results

Switching the allocation basis or cutoff approach to move expense between periods is a form of earnings management. Methods should reflect economics and be applied consistently.

## Self-Check

- Is each multi-period cost allocated across the periods that benefit it, on a basis that reflects consumption?
- Are cutoff accruals recorded at every period end for costs incurred but not yet invoiced, based on receiving records, usage, or reasoned estimates?
- Is cutoff tested on both sales and purchases, and on both shipping and receiving, for transactions near the period boundary?
- Is revenue cutoff based on when control transfers or performance occurs, not on the invoice date?
- Are shipping terms, such as FOB shipping point or destination, applied correctly to goods in transit at period end?
- Are accrual estimates reconciled to actual invoices, with material variances investigated and the method improved?
- Are allocation and cutoff methods documented and applied consistently across periods?
- Have the applicable revenue, inventory, and industry-specific cutoff standards been confirmed before relying on these procedures?
