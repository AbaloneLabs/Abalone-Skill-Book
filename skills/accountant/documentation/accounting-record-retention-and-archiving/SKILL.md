# Accounting Record Retention And Archiving

Retention is not storage. It is the deliberate decision about how long each category of accounting record must be kept, in what form, under what conditions, and when it may be destroyed. Getting this wrong cuts both ways. Destroying records too early can destroy evidence needed for audit, tax defense, litigation, or property basis, and in some cases it is itself an offense. Keeping everything forever creates cost, search burden, and risk, because retained records remain discoverable. The discipline is to apply the correct retention period for each record type under the governing rules, and to execute archiving and disposal in a controlled, documented way.

Use this skill before setting retention policies, archiving records, disposing of expired records, or advising on how long to keep a category of accounting evidence. The goal is to prevent the agent from inventing a universal retention period, destroying records that must be kept, or retaining records indefinitely without considering discovery and cost.

## Core Rules

### Base Retention On The Applicable Rules, Not A Guess

Retention periods are set by law, regulation, tax authority, and policy. They differ by jurisdiction, record type, and the matter the record supports. There is no single correct retention period.

Determine retention by identifying:

- the tax authority rules for the jurisdiction, which often set a base period for tax records;
- company law or corporate filing rules for accounting records and minutes;
- employment and payroll rules for wage and benefit records;
- industry-specific rules, such as for financial services, healthcare, or government contractors;
- contractual or lender requirements that extend retention;
- litigation hold or investigation requirements that suspend any destruction.

Do not assume that a common rule of thumb, such as seven years, applies to every record in every jurisdiction. Confirm the governing rule for each category.

### Classify Records And Assign Retention By Type

Retention should be assigned by record category, not applied as a single blanket. Build a retention schedule that maps categories to periods.

Typical categories include:

- general ledger and financial statements;
- invoices, sales and purchase;
- bank statements and reconciliations;
- payroll records and timesheets;
- tax returns and supporting work papers;
- contracts, leases, and debt agreements;
- fixed asset and depreciation records;
- board minutes and corporate records;
- audit work papers and management letters;
- employee benefit and equity records.

For each category, record the retention period, the governing basis, the form required, and the disposal method.

### Extend Retention When Circumstances Require

The standard retention period is a floor, not a ceiling. Some circumstances require keeping records longer.

Extend retention when:

- a tax return is under examination or a refund claim is open;
- litigation, investigation, or regulatory inquiry is pending or reasonably anticipated;
- a litigation hold has been issued;
- records support property basis that will be needed on disposal;
- an unresolved accounting or estimate matter remains open;
- a contractual or lender requirement exceeds the standard period.

When in doubt, retain. The cost of keeping a record longer is almost always less than the cost of having destroyed evidence that is later required.

### Preserve Records In A Usable And Authentic Form

Retention is meaningless if the records cannot be read, understood, or trusted when retrieved. Form matters.

Ensure retained records are:

- legible and complete;
- stored in stable, accessible formats, with attention to software and format obsolescence;
- protected from unauthorized alteration, with audit trails for electronic records;
- indexed and retrievable within a reasonable time;
- backed up and protected against loss.

Electronic records should meet the same practical reliability expectations as paper, including the ability to demonstrate authenticity and integrity over the retention period.

### Control Disposal Of Expired Records

Disposal is not casual deletion. It should be controlled, documented, and suspended when holds exist.

For disposal:

- confirm the retention period has expired for the category;
- confirm no litigation hold, investigation, or open matter applies;
- use a disposal method appropriate to the sensitivity, secure destruction for confidential data;
- document what was disposed of, when, by whom, and under what authority;
- obtain approval where policy requires it.

Uncontrolled disposal, or disposal without checking for holds, creates legal and regulatory exposure. Documented disposal demonstrates compliance.

### Suspend Destruction Under Hold

When litigation, investigation, or regulatory action is pending or reasonably anticipated, the duty to preserve overrides the retention schedule. Destruction must stop for relevant records.

Implement:

- a process to issue and communicate litigation holds;
- identification of the records and custodians in scope;
- suspension of routine disposal for affected categories;
- periodic reaffirmation of holds while the matter is open;
- controlled release when the matter concludes.

Destroying records subject to a hold can constitute spoliation, with serious legal consequences.

### Coordinate Across Tax, Legal, And Operational Owners

Retention sits at the intersection of accounting, tax, legal, and operations. No single function owns all the rules.

Coordinate:

- accounting and finance for operational records;
- tax for tax filings and support;
- legal for contracts, litigation, and corporate records;
- HR for employment and benefit records;
- IT for system, backup, and electronic record management.

A retention policy built by one function in isolation will miss requirements owned by others.

### Acknowledge Jurisdictional And Professional Limits

Retention rules are jurisdiction-specific and change over time. This skill describes the discipline of retention, not the specific periods for any jurisdiction. Confirm the applicable tax, corporate, employment, industry, and litigation rules for the relevant jurisdictions, and update the retention schedule when rules change. Where records support tax positions, legal matters, or regulated activity, qualified tax, legal, or compliance professionals should review the retention approach. Do not treat any retention period stated here as authoritative for a specific jurisdiction or matter.

## Common Traps

### Applying A Universal Retention Period

Using a single period for all records ignores the different rules that apply to tax, corporate, employment, and industry records.

### Destroying Records Too Early

Disposing of records before the governing period expires, or during a hold, creates legal and audit exposure.

### Retaining Everything Forever

Keeping all records indefinitely increases cost, search burden, and discovery risk without benefit.

### Ignoring Litigation Holds

Routine disposal that continues during a pending matter can constitute spoliation.

### Unreadable Or Obsolete Formats

Records retained in formats that can no longer be opened provide no evidence when needed.

### Uncontrolled Disposal

Deleting or discarding records without confirmation, approval, or documentation cannot demonstrate compliance.

### Siloed Retention Policy

A policy built by one function misses requirements owned by tax, legal, HR, or IT.

### Treating Stated Periods As Authoritative

Retention periods vary by jurisdiction and change over time; any specific period must be confirmed against current applicable rules.

## Self-Check

- Is the retention period for each record category based on the applicable tax, corporate, employment, industry, and contractual rules rather than a universal guess?
- Is there a retention schedule mapping each category to period, governing basis, required form, and disposal method?
- Is retention extended when tax examination, litigation, investigation, property basis, or open matters require it?
- Are retained records preserved in legible, stable, accessible, and authentic form, protected from alteration and loss?
- Is disposal controlled, documented, approved, and suspended when holds apply?
- Is there a process to issue and communicate litigation holds and suspend destruction for relevant records?
- Does retention policy coordinate across accounting, tax, legal, HR, and IT?
- Does the approach acknowledge jurisdictional variation and confirm specific periods with qualified tax, legal, or compliance professionals rather than treating any stated period as authoritative?
