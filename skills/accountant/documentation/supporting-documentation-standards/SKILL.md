# Supporting Documentation Standards

Every accounting entry, estimate, and disclosure should rest on documentation that proves it. The question is not whether a document exists, but whether the documentation is sufficient to satisfy a reviewer, an auditor, or a regulator who was not involved in the transaction. Weak documentation standards produce records that look complete but cannot be verified: an entry with no memo, an estimate with no method, a payment with no approval, a journal entry with no support. When documentation is insufficient, even correct accounting becomes indefensible, because no one can reconstruct why a number is what it is.

Use this skill before setting documentation standards, deciding what evidence an entry or estimate requires, reviewing documentation sufficiency, or embedding documentation requirements into a process. The goal is to prevent the agent from accepting documentation that exists but does not substantiate the transaction, or from setting standards so burdensome that people work around them.

## Core Rules

### Define What Sufficient Documentation Means

Sufficiency is not volume. It is whether the documentation allows an independent reviewer to understand and verify the transaction or balance.

Sufficient documentation should let a reviewer determine:

- what the transaction or event was;
- when it occurred and in what period it belongs;
- who was involved, including authorization and approval;
- the amount and how it was determined;
- the accounting treatment and why it is appropriate;
- the source of any estimate or assumption;
- the link to the recorded entry and the supporting detail.

If a reviewer cannot answer these from the documentation, it is insufficient regardless of how many documents are attached.

### Set Documentation Requirements By Transaction Type

Different transactions need different evidence. A single standard for all entries is either too weak or too burdensome.

Define requirements for:

- routine expenses, invoice, approval, account coding, payment evidence;
- payroll, timesheet or salary record, authorization, remittance;
- journal entries, memo explaining purpose, support for amount, approval;
- estimates, method, inputs, assumptions, preparer and approver;
- accruals and provisions, basis, calculation, period, reversal plan;
- fixed asset additions, invoice, capitalization basis, asset record;
- revenue recognition, contract, performance obligation, evidence of delivery or performance;
- intercompany and related party, nature, terms, pricing basis.

Document the standard so preparers know what is expected and reviewers know what to check.

### Require A Clear Narrative For Non-Routine Entries

Routine entries from integrated systems need less narrative. Non-routine, manual, or adjusting entries need a clear explanation that a reviewer can follow.

For non-routine entries require:

- a description of the purpose and the event behind it;
- the calculation or basis for the amount;
- the accounts affected and why;
- the approval and the authority for the entry;
- the supporting documents referenced;
- whether the entry is expected to recur or reverse.

An entry posted with only a reference number or a generic memo cannot be reviewed or audited. The narrative is part of the evidence.

### Document Estimates With Method, Not Just Result

Estimates are where documentation most often fails, because the support is reasoning rather than a document. Require the reasoning to be captured.

For each significant estimate document:

- the method or model used;
- the inputs and their source;
- the key assumptions and why they were chosen;
- the sensitivity to changes in assumptions;
- who prepared and who approved the estimate;
- the period the estimate relates to and when it will be revisited.

An estimate supported only by a final number cannot be audited or refreshed consistently. The method is the evidence.

### Link Documentation To The Entry

Documentation that exists but is not linked to the recorded entry is hard to retrieve and easy to lose. Build the link.

Link by:

- referencing the document in the entry memo;
- attaching the document in the accounting system where supported;
- storing documents in a predictable, indexed location;
- using consistent naming that includes date, entity, and transaction reference;
- retaining the link for the full retention period.

A reviewer should be able to move from the entry to the support without guesswork.

### Require Evidence Of Authorization And Review

Documentation should show not only what happened but that it was authorized and reviewed. This is control evidence.

Capture:

- who initiated the transaction;
- who approved it and under what authority or limit;
- who reviewed and posted the entry;
- segregation between these roles where required;
- the date and method of approval, system workflow or signed authorization.

Approval stored only in informal channels is weak. Prefer system workflows or retained signed approvals.

### Balance Sufficiency Against Burden

Documentation standards that are too light produce unverifiable records. Standards that are too heavy encourage workarounds, where people avoid the system or attach meaningless documents to satisfy the rule.

Balance by:

- focusing narrative and approval requirements on material or non-routine items;
- allowing integrated system entries to rely on the system audit trail for routine facts;
- avoiding duplicate documentation where the system already captures the evidence;
- periodically reviewing whether standards are being followed or circumvented.

A standard that everyone works around provides no assurance. Design standards that people can actually follow.

### Review And Refresh Documentation Periodically

Documentation standards drift as processes and systems change. Review them periodically.

Review:

- whether current standards match how transactions actually flow;
- whether reviewers are applying the standards consistently;
- whether recurring documentation gaps indicate a standard that is unclear or unworkable;
- whether new transaction types or systems need new standards.

### Acknowledge Jurisdictional And Professional Limits

Documentation standards support auditability and control, but specific legal, tax, and regulatory requirements for documentation vary by jurisdiction and record type. Some jurisdictions and frameworks impose specific requirements for invoices, e-invoicing, payroll records, or evidence of deduction. Confirm the applicable requirements with qualified tax, legal, or compliance professionals, and do not treat general documentation good practice as a substitute for mandatory local rules.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Volume With Sufficiency

Attaching many documents that do not substantiate the transaction does not make documentation sufficient.

### Generic Memos On Non-Routine Entries

An entry with a memo like monthly accrual or per CFO, without calculation or support, cannot be reviewed.

### Estimates Documented Only By Result

A final estimate number without method, inputs, and assumptions cannot be audited or refreshed.

### Unlinked Documentation

Support that exists but is not linked to the entry is hard to find and easy to lose.

### Missing Authorization Evidence

A correct amount without evidence of approval is a control deficiency.

### Standards So Burdensome They Are Circumvented

Over-heavy documentation requirements encourage workarounds that defeat the purpose.

### Stale Standards

Documentation requirements that have not been updated no longer match how transactions flow.

### Ignoring Local Legal Requirements

General good practice does not override specific jurisdictional rules for invoices, payroll, or deductible expense evidence.

## Self-Check

- Does the documentation for each transaction allow an independent reviewer to determine what, when, who, how much, why, and the link to the recorded entry?
- Are documentation requirements set by transaction type, covering routine expenses, payroll, journal entries, estimates, accruals, assets, revenue, and related party activity?
- Do non-routine and manual entries carry a clear narrative of purpose, calculation, accounts, approval, and support?
- Are significant estimates documented with method, inputs, assumptions, sensitivity, preparer, approver, and period?
- Is documentation linked to the entry through references, attachments, indexed storage, and consistent naming?
- Is evidence of authorization and review captured, with segregation and retained approval?
- Are standards balanced to be sufficient without being so burdensome that people circumvent them?
- Are standards reviewed periodically and confirmed against applicable jurisdictional legal, tax, and regulatory documentation requirements with qualified professionals?
