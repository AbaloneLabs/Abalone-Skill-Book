# Close Checklist And Standardization

The financial close is the most repeated and most consequential routine in finance. It happens every period, and its reliability determines whether the financial statements can be trusted. A close that depends on individual memory, informal coordination, and ad hoc heroics will vary in quality, slip in timing, and produce errors that surface late. A standardized close, built on a complete checklist with defined tasks, owners, dependencies, and evidence, is repeatable, auditable, and improvable. Standardization is not bureaucracy; it is the foundation of a close that is both fast and reliable.

Use this skill before building or standardizing a close checklist, defining close tasks and owners, standardizing close across entities or periods, or reducing close duration and variability. The goal is to prevent the agent from building a checklist that is incomplete, that ignores dependencies, or that standardizes the form of the close without standardizing its reliability.

## Core Rules

### Build A Complete Checklist Covering Every Required Task

A close checklist is the single source of truth for what must happen. It must be complete; a missing task is a missed task.

Cover in the checklist:

- subledger close for AP, AR, inventory, fixed assets, payroll;
- bank and credit card reconciliation;
- accruals, provisions, and estimates;
- intercompany matching and elimination;
- consolidation if multiple entities;
- account reconciliations for material balances;
- pre-close and post-close trial balance review;
- adjusting and reclassification entries;
- financial statement preparation;
- review and approval gates;
- period lock and access control.

Build the checklist from the actual close, not from a template, and update it whenever the close changes. A stale checklist is as dangerous as none.

### Define Owner, Dependency, Timing, And Evidence For Each Task

A task without an owner, a dependency, a deadline, and evidence is an aspiration. Define each.

For each task specify:

- the owner, by role with a named backup;
- the dependency on prior tasks that must complete first;
- the target day in the close calendar, such as BD+1 or BD+3;
- the expected output or evidence of completion;
- the reviewer who confirms the task is done.

Dependencies matter as much as owners. A reconciliation that starts before the subledger closes is wasted effort. Sequence tasks that depend on each other.

### Standardize Across Entities And Periods

A close that is different in every entity and every period cannot be managed or improved. Standardize the structure while allowing necessary variation.

Standardize:

- the close calendar and target close day;
- the checklist structure and task definitions;
- the reconciliation templates and formats;
- the accrual and estimate methods;
- the review and approval gates;
- the financial statement templates.

Allow variation only where genuine differences, such as local tax rules or reporting requirements, require it. Document the variation and the reason. Standardization enables comparison, delegation, and improvement.

### Sequence The Close To Respect Dependencies

The close is a sequence, not a set of parallel tasks. Enforce the sequence to avoid rework.

Sequence so that:

- transactional cutoff completes before reconciliation;
- subledgers close before GL reconciliation;
- bank and intercompany reconcile before consolidation;
- accruals and estimates post before pre-close review;
- review and adjustments precede statement preparation;
- approval and lock follow final review.

Allow parallelism only where tasks are genuinely independent. A close calendar that ignores dependencies produces reconciliations on incomplete data and late adjustments.

### Embed Review And Approval Gates

The close is not complete when tasks are done; it is complete when tasks are reviewed and approved. Build gates into the sequence.

Build gates for:

- review of significant account reconciliations by someone other than the preparer;
- review of non-routine and adjusting journal entries;
- review of estimates and their support;
- review of the pre-close trial balance for unusual movements;
- final review of the financial statements before release;
- approval to lock the period.

Gates are where errors are caught before they reach the statements. A close without review gates relies on the hope that every preparer was right.

### Track Status, Exceptions, And Slippage

A close that is not visible cannot be managed. Track status against the calendar.

Track:

- task completion against target day;
- dependencies that are blocking downstream tasks;
- exceptions and errors requiring resolution;
- sign-offs and approvals at each gate;
- slippage and the reason for it.

Visibility lets the close manager reallocate effort, unblock dependencies, and predict the actual close day. Lack of visibility is why closes slip silently until the deadline.

### Reduce Close Duration Through Root Cause, Not Shortcuts

A faster close is valuable, but only if it remains reliable. Reduce duration by addressing root causes, not by cutting tasks.

Reduce duration by:

- front-loading tasks that can be done before period end, such as pre-close accruals;
- automating routine accruals, allocations, and reconciliations;
- resolving recurring reconciling items at their source;
- standardizing and simplifying the chart of accounts and reporting;
- improving subledger close timing to unblock downstream tasks.

Do not reduce duration by skipping reconciliation, review, or investigation of exceptions. A fast close that produces restatements is slower in the long run.

### Treat The Checklist As A Living Document

The close changes. New accounts, new entities, new standards, and process improvements all require checklist updates. Treat the checklist as living.

Update the checklist when:

- new accounts or entities are added;
- new accounting standards take effect;
- recurring issues reveal a missing task;
- a task is automated or eliminated;
- a close review identifies gaps or redundancy.

A checklist that is never updated drifts from reality and loses its value as the source of truth.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

The close checklist implements accounting policy and control, but the policy must comply with the applicable reporting framework, tax, and regulatory requirements. Close tasks involving revenue, leases, hedging, intercompany, tax, and estimates often involve framework-specific requirements. Confirm significant close design decisions with qualified accounting professionals, and validate that the standardized close produces framework-compliant results. Do not treat close standardization as purely operational; it is an accounting integrity process.

## Common Traps

### Incomplete Checklist

A checklist that omits required tasks guarantees missed work and late adjustments.

### Tasks Without Owners Or Dependencies

Tasks without owners, sequence, or evidence are aspirations, not a managed process.

### No Standardization Across Entities

A close that differs everywhere cannot be compared, delegated, or improved.

### Ignoring Dependencies

Running dependent tasks in parallel produces reconciliations on incomplete data.

### No Review Gates

A close without independent review relies on hope that every preparer was right.

### Invisible Status

A close without status tracking slips silently until the deadline.

### Shortcuts Instead Of Root Cause

Cutting reconciliation or review to close faster produces restatements and is slower overall.

### Stale Checklist and standardization Without Framework Review

A checklist that is never updated drifts from reality and loses its value.

The close must implement framework-compliant policy confirmed by qualified professionals.

## Self-Check

- Does the checklist cover every required close task, built from the actual close and updated when it changes?
- Does each task have an owner, backup, dependency, target day, evidence, and reviewer?
- Is the close standardized across entities and periods, with variation only where required and documented?
- Does the sequence respect dependencies, with parallelism only where tasks are genuinely independent?
- Are review and approval gates embedded for reconciliations, entries, estimates, trial balance, statements, and period lock?
- Is close status tracked against the calendar, with visibility into completion, blockers, exceptions, and slippage?
- Is close duration reduced through front-loading, automation, and root-cause resolution rather than cutting controls?
- Is the checklist treated as a living document, updated for new accounts, entities, standards, and improvements?
- Does the standardized close reflect framework-compliant accounting policy confirmed with qualified professionals?
