# Restructuring And Exiting Costs

Restructuring is a program designed and controlled by management to materially change the scope or manner of an entity's operations: closing facilities, laying off staff, disposing of business lines, or relocating operations. The accounting problem is timing and scope. A restructuring provision is only recognized when a constructive obligation exists, which usually requires a detailed formal plan and communication to affected parties before the balance sheet date. Agents frequently accrue restructuring costs when management merely decides internally, or include costs that are not part of the restructuring (ongoing operations, future operating losses, retraining), or miss the impairments that the restructuring triggers on assets. The result is provisions recognized too early, costs misclassified to make the restructuring look cheaper, and balance sheet assets left at carrying amounts that the restructuring has already rendered unrecoverable.

Use this skill before recording a restructuring provision, communicating a restructuring plan, classifying exit or disposal costs, accounting for severance, terminating leases, or testing assets for impairment triggered by a decision to exit. The goal is to prevent the agent from booking restructuring costs on the basis of an internal decision and missing the obligation-timing, scope, and impairment tests.

Restructuring and exit-activity accounting differs between US GAAP (ASC 420 for exit or disposal activities, ASC 712 for ongoing termination benefits) and IFRS (IAS 37 for restructuring provisions), particularly on what costs are included and the timing of the constructive obligation. Local GAAP and labor-law environments vary further. This skill gives the general framework; the applicable standard governs. For material restructurings involving severance law, lease exits, and impairment, involve a qualified accountant, HR or legal counsel, and where relevant a valuation specialist. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific program.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Constructive Obligation Before Recognizing

A restructuring provision is recognized only when the recognition criteria for a provision are met, which for restructuring hinges on a constructive obligation. Under IFRS, a constructive obligation arises when the entity has a detailed formal plan for the restructuring and has raised a valid expectation in those affected that it will carry out the restructuring by starting to implement it or announcing its main features to them.

Identify the obligating event. A board decision alone, kept internal, generally does not create a constructive obligation. Communication to affected employees, customers, or suppliers, or the start of implementation, creates the expectation. Document the date of the detailed plan, the date of communication, and the main features communicated. Timing drives whether the provision belongs in this period or the next.

### Define The Scope Of Costs Included

A restructuring provision includes only costs that are directly arising from the restructuring and are not associated with ongoing activities. Include: termination benefits for employees whose employment is terminated; costs of terminating leases that will not be offset by sublease income; and costs of dismantling and removing assets. Exclude: retraining or relocating continuing staff; marketing; investment in new systems; and future operating losses of the discontinued activity, which are not restructuring costs.

The principle is that only incremental costs of the exit belong in the provision. Costs that benefit the ongoing business, even if incurred during the restructuring, are expensed as incurred in the period they relate to. Do not load ordinary operating costs into the restructuring provision to make ongoing operations look better.

### Account For Severance And Termination Benefits By Type

Termination benefits are accounted for based on whether they are one-time involuntary termination benefits or ongoing benefits under an existing plan. One-time involuntary termination benefits (a defined window, employees leave, receive a lump sum) are recognized when the entity communicates the offer and the employees accept, or when the offer is irrevocable and a constructive obligation exists.

Ongoing termination benefits under an existing arrangement (severance under a plan that pays over time, enhanced early retirement) are generally recognized over the future service period if the employee must continue to provide service to receive the benefit. Distinguish the two because the recognition timing differs: one-time benefits at communication or acceptance; ongoing benefits over service. Match the severance type to the recognition model.

### Handle Lease Termination And Exit Costs

When a restructuring exits a leased facility before the end of the lease term, recognize a liability for the remaining lease payments less any estimated sublease income, net of the right-of-use asset. Under current lease standards, the right-of-use asset and lease liability are generally derecognized or adjusted when the entity ceases to use the space and the lease is terminated or a termination penalty is incurred.

Estimate sublease income conservatively and reassess it. Do not assume a sublease will fully offset the remaining payments. Costs of returning a leased property to its required condition (make-good, dilapidations) are part of the exit cost. Document the lease-exit calculation and the sublease assumption.

### Recognize Asset Impairments Triggered By The Restructuring

A decision to close a facility or exit a business line is an indicator of impairment for the related assets. Test property, plant, and equipment, right-of-use assets, goodwill, intangibles, and inventory for impairment when the restructuring decision is made. Assets no longer in use, or whose use will change, may need to be written down to fair value less costs to sell or recoverable amount.

Impairment is recognized separately from the restructuring provision, through impairment accounting, not as part of the restructuring cost. However, the restructuring decision is the trigger. Missing the impairment leaves assets overstated and misstates the total cost of the restructuring. Coordinate the impairment test with the provision recognition.

### Distinguish Restructuring Provision From Ongoing Expense

Costs that are part of the restructuring provision (incremental exit costs, one-time severance, lease termination) are recognized as restructuring expense, often presented separately so users can see the one-time nature of the cost. Costs that relate to ongoing operations, even during the restructuring (continuing staff, marketing, new systems), are recognized in their normal expense categories.

Do not reclassify ongoing operating costs into restructuring to create a cleaner ongoing margin, and do not leave genuine exit costs in operating expense where they distort the run-rate. The classification should reflect the economic nature, not the desired presentation.

### Present And Disclose Restructuring Activity

Present restructuring costs in a manner that allows users to distinguish them from ongoing operations, often as a separate line item. Disclose the nature of the restructuring, the costs recognized, the provisions made and used, the expected timing of completion, and any significant assumptions. For exit or disposal activities, disclose the assets held for sale and the results of discontinued operations where applicable.

Users rely on restructuring disclosure to assess the one-time nature of the costs and to normalize ongoing performance. Vague or missing disclosure prevents that assessment and is a frequent deficiency.

### Reassess The Provision Each Period

A restructuring provision is an estimate. Reassess it each period as the program progresses: actual severance costs, lease exit outcomes, asset proceeds, and changes in plan scope. Adjust the provision and recognize the change in restructuring expense. A provision set at announcement and never revisited becomes stale.

When the restructuring is complete, any remaining provision should be reversed. Carrying a residual restructuring provision after completion misstates liabilities and expense.

## Common Traps

### Recognizing The Provision On An Internal Decision

A board decision or management plan that has not been communicated does not create a constructive obligation. Booking the provision before communication recognizes the cost too early and overstates the liability.

### Including Future Operating Losses Or Ongoing Costs

Future operating losses of the exited activity, retraining, marketing, and new-system costs are not restructuring costs. Including them inflates the provision and misclassifies ongoing expense as one-time.

### Missing The Asset Impairment Trigger

The decision to exit is an impairment indicator. Leaving assets at carrying amount when they are no longer recoverable overstates assets and understates the total cost of the restructuring.

### Treating All Severance As One-Time

Ongoing termination benefits that require continued service are recognized over the service period, not at communication. Treating them as one-time overstates current-period expense.

### Assuming Full Sublease Recovery On Lease Exits

Sublease income is uncertain. Assuming a full offset understates the lease termination liability and overstates the recovery. Estimate conservatively and reassess.

### Reclassifying Ongoing Costs As Restructuring

Moving continuing operating costs into the restructuring line to flatter ongoing margin misleads users about the run-rate and is a form of earnings management.

### Leaving A Stale Provision After Completion

A restructuring provision that is never reversed after the program ends overstates liabilities and distorts future expense when it is eventually cleared.

## Self-Check

- Has a constructive obligation been established through a detailed formal plan and communication to affected parties before the balance sheet date?
- Does the restructuring provision include only directly arising exit costs, excluding future operating losses, retraining, marketing, and new-system costs?
- Are one-time involuntary termination benefits recognized at communication or acceptance, and ongoing benefits over the service period?
- Are lease termination costs measured as remaining payments less conservatively estimated sublease income, with the right-of-use asset and liability adjusted?
- Have assets affected by the restructuring decision been tested for impairment, with impairments recognized through impairment accounting rather than the provision?
- Are restructuring costs presented separately from ongoing operations so users can distinguish one-time from run-rate expense?
- Is the provision reassessed each period as the program progresses, with adjustments recognized in restructuring expense?
- Has any residual provision been reversed once the restructuring is complete?
- Are the nature, costs, provisions, expected timing, and significant assumptions of the restructuring disclosed?
- Has a qualified accountant, legal counsel, or valuation specialist reviewed material severance, lease exits, and impairment positions?
