# Contingent Liabilities And Provisions

Provisions and contingencies are where accounting judgment meets uncertainty. A provision is a liability of uncertain timing or amount that is recognized on the balance sheet; a contingent liability is a possible obligation that is only disclosed. The line between them turns on probability thresholds and whether a reliable estimate can be made, and the same set of facts can produce a recognized provision, a disclosed contingency, or nothing at all depending on how the evidence is weighed. Agents tend to either over-recognize (booking a provision for every possible bad outcome) or under-recognize (ignoring likely obligations because the amount is uncertain), and they frequently mishandle contingent assets by recognizing them too early. Getting this wrong misstates liabilities, distorts profit, and creates audit and disclosure failures.

Use this skill before assessing litigation, claims, warranties, environmental obligations, restructuring costs, onerous contracts, or any uncertain obligation, and before deciding whether to recognize, disclose, or do nothing. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying a single default (always accrue or never accrue) and missing the probability, measurement, and disclosure framework.

Provision and contingency accounting differs between US GAAP and IFRS, particularly on the probability thresholds (probable versus more likely than not), the treatment of contingent assets, and discounting. Local GAAP and legal environments vary further. This skill gives the general framework; the applicable standard governs. For litigation, environmental, and regulatory matters, involve legal counsel and a qualified accountant. This is structural guidance, not a legal or actuarial conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Recognition Test For A Provision

A provision is recognized when three conditions are met: there is a present obligation (legal or constructive) arising from a past event; it is probable (IFRS: more likely than not; US GAAP: likely) that an outflow of resources will be required; and a reliable estimate of the amount can be made. All three must be present. If any fails, no provision is recognized.

Identify the past event that creates the obligation. For litigation, it is the event giving rise to the claim, not the filing of the lawsuit. For warranties, it is the sale of the product. For restructuring, it is the constructive or legal obligation event. Without an obligating past event, there is no provision regardless of likely outflow. Distinguish a present obligation from a future intention.

### Distinguish Legal From Constructive Obligations

A legal obligation arises from a contract, legislation, or other operation of law. A constructive obligation arises from an entity's actions that create a valid expectation in others that it will accept certain responsibilities, typically through an established pattern of past practice, a public statement, or a specific commitment that has been communicated and cannot realistically be withdrawn without penalty.

For a constructive obligation, the entity must have created the expectation before the balance sheet date. A board decision to restructure that has not been communicated to affected parties before period-end generally does not create a constructive obligation. Document the date and nature of the obligating event carefully, because timing drives whether the provision belongs in this period or next.

### Measure The Provision At The Best Estimate

A provision is measured at the best estimate of the expenditure required to settle the obligation at the balance sheet date. For a single obligation, the best estimate may be the most likely outcome. For a large population of items (warranty claims), the best estimate is the expected value weighted by probabilities.

Include only those future events that are closely related to the obligation and that can be reliably estimated. Consider risks and uncertainties, but do not build in excessive conservatism or double-count. Where the effect of the time value of money is material, discount the provision using a rate that reflects current market assessments of the time value of money and the risks specific to the liability, not the entity's own credit risk. Reassess the provision at each reporting date.

### Disclose Contingent Liabilities That Are Not Recognized

Where a present obligation exists but outflow is not probable, or where a reliable estimate cannot be made, or where the obligation is only possible (a possible obligation), no provision is recognized. Instead, disclose the contingent liability: its nature; an estimate of the financial effect; the uncertainties; and the possibility of any reimbursement.

Do not recognize a contingent liability on the balance sheet. Disclosure is the mechanism for possible or not-probable obligations. The distinction between a provision and a disclosed contingent liability is one of the most tested areas, because it moves a number from the balance sheet to the notes.

### Apply Conservatism To Contingent Assets

Contingent assets are possible assets arising from past events whose existence will be confirmed by future events not wholly within the entity's control. A contingent asset is generally not recognized in the financial statements because that could recognize income that may never be realized. It is disclosed only when an inflow of economic benefits is probable.

Even when realization becomes virtually certain, recognition occurs only at that point, and the asset recognized is limited to the amount that will not result in a highly significant adjustment. The conservatism on contingent assets is asymmetric with provisions: entities recognize provisions when outflow is probable but recognize contingent assets only when inflow is virtually certain. Do not book a contingent asset at the same threshold as a provision.

### Account For Reimbursements Separately

Where some or all of the expenditure required to settle a provision is expected to be reimbursed by another party (insurance, indemnity), the reimbursement is recognized as a separate asset only when it is virtually certain that reimbursement will be received. The expense related to the provision may be presented net of the reimbursement.

Do not net the reimbursement against the provision unless recognition of the reimbursement asset is virtually certain. The provision is recognized at gross; the reimbursement asset is a separate item. Mixing them understates both the obligation and the related asset.

### Consider Onerous Contracts And Restructuring Specifically

An onerous contract is one in which the unavoidable costs of meeting the obligations exceed the economic benefits expected. A provision is recognized for the lower of the cost of fulfilling or exiting the contract. Restructuring provisions require a detailed plan, communication to affected parties, and a constructive obligation; see the restructuring skill for the specific timing tests.

These categories have their own recognition triggers and should not be lumped into a general accrual. Identify the category of obligation before applying the test, because the triggers differ.

### Review And Update Each Reporting Period

Provisions are estimates and must be reassessed at each reporting date. New information, changes in legal advice, settlement negotiations, revised cost estimates, and the passage of time all change the best estimate. Adjust the provision, and unwind discounting through finance costs where discounting was applied.

Stale provisions that are never revisited are a common audit finding. Document the basis for each material provision at each period-end, including the legal or expert advice relied upon.

## Common Traps

### Recognizing A Provision Without A Present Obligation

Likely outflow is not enough; there must be a present obligation from a past event. A future intention to spend, or a general risk of litigation, does not create a provision. Booking a provision for every possible risk overstates liabilities and understates profit.

### Using The Wrong Probability Threshold

IFRS uses "more likely than not" (greater than 50 percent) for provisions; US GAAP uses "probable" which is generally a higher threshold. Applying the wrong framework's threshold misclassifies obligations between recognized provisions and disclosed contingencies.

### Disclosing Instead Of Recognizing A Probable Obligation

When outflow is probable and a reliable estimate can be made, the obligation must be recognized as a provision, not merely disclosed. Leaving a probable, measurable obligation in the notes understates liabilities.

### Recognizing A Contingent Asset Too Early

Contingent assets are recognized only when inflow is virtually certain, not at the probable threshold. Booking a contingent gain when it is merely likely overstates assets and income, and reverses if the gain does not materialize.

### Double-Counting Or Overstating The Estimate

Including future events that are not closely related, building in excessive prudence, or failing to weight outcomes by probability inflates the provision. The best estimate is the expected outflow, not a worst-case scenario.

### Forgetting To Reassess And Unwind Discounting

Provisions change as facts develop and as time passes. A discounted provision must be unwound through finance costs each period. A provision set once and never revisited becomes stale and misstated.

### Netting Reimbursements Without Virtual Certainty

Insurance or indemnity recoveries are recognized as a separate asset only when virtually certain. Netting them against the provision before that point understates both the obligation and the asset.

## Self-Check

- For each uncertain obligation, has it been determined whether a present obligation (legal or constructive) exists from a past event?
- Are provisions recognized only when outflow is probable (or more likely than not under IFRS) and a reliable estimate can be made, using the correct threshold for the applicable framework?
- Are contingent liabilities that do not meet recognition criteria disclosed with nature, estimated financial effect, and uncertainties rather than recognized?
- Are contingent assets recognized only when inflow is virtually certain, with disclosure used at the probable threshold?
- Is each provision measured at the best estimate (expected value for populations, most likely for single obligations), discounted where material?
- Are constructive obligations supported by evidence of a valid expectation created before the balance sheet date?
- Are reimbursements recognized as separate assets only when virtually certain, with the provision shown gross?
- Are provisions reassessed at each reporting date with discounting unwound through finance costs?
- Are onerous contracts and restructuring provisions tested against their specific recognition triggers rather than a general accrual?
- Are material litigation, environmental, and regulatory provisions supported by current legal or expert advice, and has a qualified accountant or legal counsel reviewed the position?
