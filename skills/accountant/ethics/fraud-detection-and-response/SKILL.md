# Fraud Detection And Response

Fraud is a persistent risk in every organization that handles money, and accounting is the discipline most positioned to detect it. Financial statement fraud, asset misappropriation, corruption, and expense manipulation rarely appear as obvious crimes. They hide inside ordinary-looking transactions, estimates pushed in a favorable direction, vendor relationships that are too convenient, approvals that are too routine, and adjustments made at period-end when no one is watching. The danger for accountants is twofold: failing to recognize fraud signals because they are embedded in normal activity, and mishandling a suspicion once it arises through premature confrontation, contaminated evidence, or breach of confidentiality. Fraud response is as important as fraud detection, because a mishandled investigation can destroy evidence, alert the perpetrator, expose the organization to legal liability, and prevent recovery.

Use this skill before assessing fraud risk, designing detection procedures, investigating anomalies, responding to a suspicion, preserving evidence, or deciding on reporting obligations. The goal is to prevent the agent from either ignoring fraud risk out of discomfort or acting on a suspicion in a way that compromises the investigation, the evidence, or the organization's legal position.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Fraud Triangle And Where Pressure Hides

Fraud is most likely where three conditions converge: pressure or incentive to commit fraud, opportunity to do so without detection, and rationalization that justifies the act. Recognizing these conditions helps identify where to focus attention.

Consider:

- incentives, such as bonus targets, covenant compliance, financing conditions, performance expectations, or personal financial distress;
- opportunities, such as weak segregation of duties, override-capable positions, system access without monitoring, or trusted long-tenured roles;
- rationalizations, such as perceived unfairness, entitlement, belief that the amount is temporary or will be repaid, or pressure framed as loyalty.

Fraud risk is not a reflection of distrust in individuals. It is a recognition that the right conditions can lead otherwise reliable people to act wrongly. Design controls and detection around conditions, not around personalities.

### Identify The Common Fraud Schemes In Each Area

Different parts of the accounting function face different fraud schemes. Detection is more effective when the agent knows what to look for in each area.

Common schemes include:

- revenue fraud, such as premature recognition, fictitious sales, channel stuffing, or side agreements;
- expense fraud, such as personal expenses charged to the business, fictitious vendors, or inflated invoices;
- asset misappropriation, such as skimming receipts, lapping accounts receivable, unauthorized disbursements, or theft of inventory;
- payroll fraud, such as ghost employees, falsified hours, or unauthorized rate changes;
- procurement fraud, such as kickbacks, bid manipulation, or vendor bank detail changes;
- financial statement manipulation, such as smoothing results, hiding liabilities, or understating expenses through capitalization;
- related-party concealment, such as undisclosed transactions with entities controlled by management.

Map detection procedures to the schemes most likely in the organization's specific operations.

### Design Detection Analytics And Exception Reports

Detection depends on analytics and exception reporting that surface anomalies for review. The reports must actually be reviewed by someone with authority to investigate.

Use detection procedures such as:

- duplicate payment and duplicate invoice detection;
- payments just below approval thresholds;
- new vendors with immediate large payments;
- vendor bank detail changes followed by payments;
- journal entries to revenue, cash, or suspense accounts near period-end;
- entries posted by unexpected users or at unusual times;
- manual overrides of system controls;
- negative inventory or negative cash balances;
- customer credits and write-offs concentrated with specific employees;
- expense reimbursements with patterns of rounding or recurrence;
- bank reconciling items that age without resolution;
- related-party transactions lacking disclosure.

Each report should have an owner, a review frequency, and a documented resolution for exceptions found.

### Apply Professional Skepticism Without Assuming Guilt

Professional skepticism means maintaining a questioning mind and recognizing that conditions may exist that indicate misstatement due to fraud. It does not mean assuming that everyone is guilty. The balance is critical: too little skepticism misses fraud, while too much creates a toxic environment and wrongful accusations.

Apply skepticism by:

- corroborating explanations with independent evidence;
- testing the completeness of populations, not only samples;
- looking for corroborating or contradicting patterns;
- asking why a transaction occurred, not only whether it was approved;
- following up on inconsistent, evasive, or changing explanations;
- not accepting convenience explanations for unusual activity.

Treat anomalies as questions to investigate, not as conclusions. Investigation resolves the question; presumption does not.

### Respond To Suspicion Deliberately And Quietly

When a suspicion arises, the immediate response shapes whether the matter can be resolved. Premature confrontation, broad discussion, or unilateral action can destroy the investigation.

On suspicion, take these initial steps:

- do not confront or accuse the suspected individual;
- limit knowledge of the suspicion to those with a need to know;
- preserve relevant records and restrict alteration or deletion;
- document what was observed, when, and by whom;
- consult legal counsel and, where applicable, fraud investigation specialists;
- follow the organization's established fraud response plan;
- involve governance, internal audit, or external auditors as appropriate;
- assess whether to suspend access of the suspected individual to systems and assets.

The goal of the initial response is to protect evidence and options, not to reach a conclusion. Conclusions come from a properly conducted investigation.

### Preserve Evidence And Maintain Chain Of Custody

Fraud investigation depends on evidence that is intact, authentic, and traceable. Mishandled evidence can be challenged, excluded, or rendered useless for recovery or prosecution.

Preserve evidence by:

- securing original documents, electronic records, and system logs promptly;
- preventing deletion, alteration, or overwrite of relevant data;
- maintaining a chain of custody documenting who collected, handled, and stored each item;
- using forensic methods for electronic evidence where feasible;
- retaining backups and system images;
- avoiding working on original copies, use copies for analysis;
- documenting the scope and method of the investigation.

Engage forensic specialists when the matter is significant, complex, or likely to involve legal proceedings.

### Understand Reporting And Disclosure Obligations

Fraud discovery triggers reporting obligations that vary by role, jurisdiction, and the nature of the entity. Failing to meet these obligations can create liability; exceeding them can breach confidentiality or prejudice rights.

Consider obligations to:

- management and governance, through the appropriate level and channel;
- external auditors, who must be informed of fraud that affects the financial statements;
- regulators, where laws require reporting, such as financial services or public companies;
- law enforcement, where criminal activity is involved;
- insurers, where a claim may be made;
- lenders or investors, where covenants or representations are affected;
- external stakeholders, where disclosure in financial statements is required.

Determine the applicable obligations before acting, and engage legal counsel to confirm. Reporting the right information to the right party at the right time is both a legal and an ethical duty.

### Assess And Remediate After The Event

After fraud is confirmed, the organization must recover financially and prevent recurrence. Remediation addresses both the specific weakness exploited and the broader control environment.

Remediate by:

- quantifying the loss and pursuing recovery where feasible;
- assessing whether prior-period financial statements require restatement;
- reviewing insurance coverage and claims;
- strengthening the specific controls that failed;
- reassessing segregation of duties and access across the organization;
- improving detection analytics based on how the fraud was concealed;
- training staff on red flags and reporting channels;
- reviewing culture, pressure, and incentive structures that contributed.

A fraud event is a diagnostic opportunity. The controls that failed reveal where the environment is weak, and the response determines whether the weakness is corrected or merely survived.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Anomalies As Errors

Many frauds are initially flagged as mistakes and then dropped. Persistent anomalies, especially patterns, deserve investigation until a satisfactory explanation is confirmed.

### Confronting Suspects Prematurely

Early confrontation alerts the perpetrator, enables evidence destruction, and can lead to claims of wrongful accusation. Investigate quietly and preserve options first.

### Assuming Trust Eliminates Risk

Long-tenured, trusted employees are often the perpetrators of significant fraud precisely because trust reduces oversight. Familiarity is a recognized fraud risk factor.

### Focusing Only On Large Transactions

Much fraud occurs through many small transactions that fall below approval thresholds. Detection analytics must look for patterns across volume, not only for large individual items.

### Breaching Confidentiality During Investigation

Discussing the suspicion broadly contaminates the investigation, exposes the organization to defamation claims, and can tip off the perpetrator. Limit knowledge to a need-to-know basis.

### Ignoring Override Of Controls

Management override of controls is one of the hardest fraud risks to detect and prevent. Period-end journal entries, manual adjustments, and policy exceptions warrant specific scrutiny.

### Neglecting Recovery And Remediation

Confirming fraud without pursuing recovery or fixing controls leaves the loss and the vulnerability in place. Remediation is part of the response, not an afterthought.

### Overlooking Reporting Obligations

Each fraud discovery may carry obligations to auditors, regulators, governance, and others. Assuming no reporting is required can itself become a liability.

## Self-Check

- Has the fraud risk assessment considered incentives, opportunities, and rationalizations specific to the organization's operations?
- Are detection analytics and exception reports designed for the fraud schemes most likely in each accounting area, and are they actually reviewed and resolved?
- Is professional skepticism applied through corroboration and pattern testing without presuming guilt?
- When suspicion arises, is the response deliberate and quiet, preserving evidence and options rather than confronting prematurely?
- Is evidence preserved with documented chain of custody, using forensic methods and specialists where the matter is significant?
- Are reporting and disclosure obligations to management, governance, auditors, regulators, and others assessed with legal counsel before acting?
- After confirmation, are recovery, restatement assessment, control remediation, and cultural review undertaken?
- Are management override, small-transaction patterns, and trusted-role risks given specific attention rather than dismissed?
- Is confidentiality maintained throughout the investigation to protect evidence, rights, and the organization's legal position?
- Does the fraud response acknowledge jurisdiction-specific reporting, disclosure, and investigation requirements and recommend qualified legal and forensic professional engagement?
