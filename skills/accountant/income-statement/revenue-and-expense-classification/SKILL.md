# Revenue And Expense Classification

Beyond the overall structure of the income statement, individual items often present ambiguous classification questions: Should revenue be presented gross or net (agent versus principal)? Is a government grant operating income or a reduction of expense? Where does a foreign exchange gain belong? Is a restructuring charge part of operating expenses or a separate line? These classifications affect revenue totals, gross margin, operating margin, and the comparability of results. Agents often classify based on the trial-balance account name rather than applying the substantive test, or they apply gross/net presentation inconsistently.

Use this skill before classifying a specific revenue or expense item, resolving gross versus net revenue presentation, classifying government grants or gains and losses, or deciding where unusual or infrequent items belong. The goal is to prevent the agent from classifying by convention and missing the substantive tests that determine correct presentation.

Classification rules draw on multiple standards (ASC 606 / IFRS 15 for revenue, ASC 845 / IAS 20 for grants, ASC 225 / IAS 1 for presentation). This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For material or unusual items, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific item.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Principal-Versus-Agent Test To Determine Gross Or Net Revenue

When an entity is involved in providing goods or services that are provided by another party, determine whether the entity is acting as a principal (present revenue gross, at the gross amount billed to the customer) or as an agent (present revenue net, at the commission or fee retained). Under ASC 606 and IFRS 15, an entity is a principal if it controls the good or service before transfer to the customer. Indicators of control include: primary obligor for the product or service; inventory risk before or after the customer order; latitude to set the price; and responsibility for fulfillment including acceptability.

An entity is an agent if it does not control the good or service — its performance obligation is to arrange for the provision by the other party. Present revenue at the net amount (the fee or commission). The gross/net choice materially affects reported revenue (though it often does not affect net income), and users and analysts care about it for revenue-based metrics.

### Classify Government Grants As Operating Income Or A Reduction Of Expense Consistently

Under IFRS (IAS 20), government grants related to income may be presented either as income (often in other income) or as a deduction from the related expense, and the approach should be disclosed and applied consistently. Grants related to assets may be presented as deferred income (amortized over the asset's life) or as a reduction of the asset's carrying amount. Under US GAAP, government grants are generally presented as income when conditions are met, though industry-specific guidance may apply.

The classification affects operating margin and the expense base. Apply it consistently and disclose the policy. Do not move a grant between income and expense reduction across periods without a change in policy.

### Classify Gains And Losses Based On Whether They Are Part Of Core Operations

Gains and losses on disposal of non-current assets, foreign exchange gains and losses on monetary items, and gains on settlement of liabilities are generally classified as non-operating (other income or other expense) unless they are part of the entity's core operations. For a financial institution, foreign exchange gains and losses are operating; for a manufacturer, they are typically non-operating (though IFRS presents finance costs separately).

The test is whether the item arises from the entity's ordinary, core activities. Apply the test consistently and disclose the boundary. Do not classify a one-off gain as operating to flatter operating income.

### Classify Finance Costs Separately From Operating Expenses

Finance costs (interest expense on borrowings, unwinding of discounts on provisions, interest on lease liabilities) are presented separately from operating expenses, generally below operating income. This separation helps users assess operating performance before financing decisions. Under IFRS, finance costs are a required separate line item. Under US GAAP, interest expense is typically presented below operating income.

Interest income may be presented within finance income or within other income, depending on the entity's business model (for a financial institution, interest income is operating). Apply the classification consistently.

### Present Restructuring Charges Within Operating Expenses, Not As An Extraordinary Item

Restructuring charges (severance, lease termination, asset impairment from restructuring) are part of operating expenses, presented within the appropriate function (often within general and administrative or as a separate operating line if material). Under both IFRS and US GAAP, extraordinary items are no longer permitted — all items are presented within profit or loss, with material unusual items disclosed or presented as separate lines for transparency.

Do not present restructuring charges as "extraordinary" or below the line. Present them within operating expenses and disclose the nature and amount.

### Classify Income Tax Expense Separately As A Single Line

Income tax expense (current plus deferred) is presented as a single line on the income statement, generally after income before tax. Do not split current and deferred tax on the face of the income statement (they are disclosed in the notes). The tax line produces net income from continuing operations.

### Present Other Comprehensive Income Items Below Net Income

Items of other comprehensive income (OCI) — foreign currency translation adjustments, unrealized gains and losses on certain financial instruments, remeasurements of defined benefit plans, changes in revaluation surplus — are presented below net income in a separate statement or section, producing total comprehensive income. OCI items are not part of profit or loss (unless recycled), but they affect total equity.

Do not present OCI items within profit or loss. Present them separately and disclose whether each is potentially recyclable to profit or loss.

## Common Traps

### Presenting Revenue Net When The Entity Is A Principal

If the entity controls the good or service (inventory risk, sets price, primary obligor), present revenue gross. Presenting net understates revenue and may mislead revenue-based metrics.

### Inconsistently Classifying Government Grants

Moving grants between income and expense reduction across periods, or presenting asset-related grants inconsistently, impairs comparability. Apply a consistent policy and disclose it.

### Classifying A One-Off Gain As Operating Income

A gain on disposal or a settlement gain is generally non-operating. Including it in operating income overstates core performance and distorts the operating margin trend.

### Presenting Restructuring As Extraordinary

Extraordinary items are no longer permitted. Present restructuring within operating expenses and disclose it, rather than below the line.

### Including OCI Items In Profit Or Loss

OCI items belong below net income, not within profit or loss. Including them inflates reported profit and misstates the distinction between realized and unrealized items.

### Splitting Current And Deferred Tax On The Face

Current and deferred tax are presented as a single tax line on the face. Splitting them on the face is non-standard; disclose the split in the notes.

### Misclassifying Finance Costs As Operating Expenses

Interest expense is a finance cost, presented below operating income. Including it in operating expenses understates operating income and distorts the operating margin.

## Self-Check

- Is revenue presented gross or net based on the principal-versus-agent control test (inventory risk, price setting, primary obligor, fulfillment responsibility)?
- Are government grants classified consistently as income or expense reduction, with the policy disclosed and applied across periods?
- Are gains and losses classified based on whether they arise from core operations, with non-operating items presented below operating income?
- Are finance costs (interest, unwinding of discounts) presented separately from operating expenses, below operating income?
- Are restructuring charges presented within operating expenses (not as extraordinary items), with the nature and amount disclosed?
- Is income tax expense presented as a single line (current plus deferred), with the split disclosed in the notes?
- Are OCI items presented below net income, separate from profit or loss, with recyclability disclosed?
- Have material or unusual classification decisions been reviewed by a qualified accountant or auditor, and is the applicable standard confirmed?
