# Income Statement Preparation

The income statement (statement of profit or loss under IFRS) reports an entity's financial performance over a period. Its preparation involves classifying expenses (by nature or by function), choosing a presentation format (single-step or multi-step), sequencing line items to show the path from revenue to net income, and ensuring the statement ties to the balance sheet via retained earnings. Agents often mix nature and function classification, omit subtotals that users expect (gross profit, operating income), or present an income statement that does not reconcile to the trial balance or the equity rollforward.

Use this skill before drafting an income statement from a trial balance, reviewing a prepared statement for classification and format errors, choosing between by-nature and by-function presentation, or deciding which subtotals to present. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a statement that is arithmetically correct but poorly structured, inconsistently classified, or missing the subtotals and disclosures users need.

Income statement presentation rules differ between US GAAP (ASC 225, no mandated nature/function choice but industry conventions) and IFRS (IAS 1, with an explicit nature/function choice and minimum line items). This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For audited or filed statements, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Choose Between By-Nature And By-Function Expense Classification

Expenses are classified either by nature (what the expense is: raw materials consumed, employee benefits, depreciation) or by function (what the expense is for: cost of sales, selling expenses, administrative expenses). Under IFRS (IAS 1), the entity must choose one approach and apply it consistently, with disclosure of the choice. Under US GAAP, a function-based presentation (multi-step with cost of sales, selling, general and administrative) is common, but nature-based presentation is also acceptable.

By-nature is simpler and requires less allocation, but it does not show gross profit or the cost of sales function. By-function shows gross profit and operating margin but requires allocating expenses (especially payroll and depreciation) to functions, which involves judgment. The choice affects comparability with peers — choose the approach used in the entity's industry where possible.

### Apply The Minimum Line Items Required By The Framework

Under IFRS (IAS 1), the income statement must include, at a minimum, line items for: revenue; finance costs; share of profit or loss of associates and joint ventures accounted for using the equity method; a single amount for the total of the post-tax profit or loss of discontinued operations; and profit or loss. Additional line items (cost of sales, gross profit, operating expenses, other income) are added when relevant.

Under US GAAP (ASC 225), the income statement presents revenues, expenses, and net income, with costs and expenses disaggregated sufficiently for users to understand performance. Specific industries (banks, insurance, utilities) have specialized presentation requirements.

### Present Meaningful Subtotals That Reflect The Business Model

Subtotals help users understand the components of performance. Common subtotals include: gross profit (revenue minus cost of sales), operating income (gross profit minus operating expenses), income before tax (operating income plus other income minus other expenses), and net income. Present the subtotals that are relevant to the entity's business model and that industry peers present.

Under IFRS, an entity may present an operating result subtotal if it is not misleading and the items included are consistently classified. Do not present a subtotal that obscures material items or that is not defined consistently across periods.

### Classify Items Below Operating Income Appropriately

Items below operating income (other income, other expenses, finance costs, share of profit of associates) are separated from operating results because they are not part of core operations. Other income includes gains and income not part of the core business (rental income for a manufacturer, gains on disposal of non-current assets). Finance costs include interest expense and similar financing costs. The share of profit of associates is presented separately because it is the entity's share of another entity's results.

The boundary between operating and non-operating is a judgment. Apply it consistently and disclose the basis. Do not move items below the line to flatter operating income without a consistent rationale.

### Present Discontinued Operations As A Single Amount

The post-tax profit or loss of discontinued operations is presented as a single line on the income statement, separately from continuing operations. The components are disclosed in the notes. This separation helps users assess ongoing performance. Under both IFRS (IFRS 5) and US GAAP (ASC 205), the criteria for discontinued operations classification are specific — do not classify a segment as discontinued unless it meets the criteria.

### Present Earnings Per Share Where Required

Public entities must present basic and diluted earnings per share on the face of the income statement (under IFRS IAS 33 and US GAAP ASC 260). EPS is presented for profit or loss from continuing operations and for total profit or loss, and for each class of share capital. Non-public entities are generally not required to present EPS.

### Ensure The Statement Reconciles To Retained Earnings

Net income on the income statement flows to retained earnings on the balance sheet: ending retained earnings equals opening retained earnings plus net income minus dividends. Comprehensive income (net income plus other comprehensive income) flows to total equity. An income statement that does not reconcile to the equity rollforward has an error.

### Disclose The Expense Classification Choice And Material Judgments

Disclose whether expenses are classified by nature or by function (IFRS requires this), the basis for allocating expenses to functions, and material judgments about classification (what is included in cost of sales, the boundary between operating and non-operating). Users rely on this to compare entities and to understand the statement's structure.

## Common Traps

### Mixing Nature And Function Classification

Classifying some expenses by nature (depreciation) and others by function (cost of sales) in the same statement is inconsistent. Choose one approach and disclose it. (IFRS permits disclosing depreciation and employee costs by nature in the notes even when function is used on the face.)

### Omitting Expected Subtotals

Omitting gross profit or operating income when the industry presents them impairs comparability. Present the subtotals users expect for the business model.

### Inconsistently Defining The Operating Boundary

Moving items below the operating line in one period and above it in another, without a consistent rationale, distorts the operating result. Apply the boundary consistently.

### Classifying A Disposal Gain As Operating Income

Gains on disposal of non-current assets are generally non-operating (other income). Including them in operating income overstates core performance.

### Presenting Discontinued Operations Without Meeting Criteria

The criteria for discontinued operations are specific. Presenting a segment as discontinued without meeting them misleads users about ongoing performance.

### Failing To Reconcile To Retained Earnings

An income statement whose net income does not flow correctly to retained earnings has an error. Reconcile before finalizing.

### Presenting EPS Inconsistently Or Not At All

Public entities must present basic and diluted EPS on the face. Omitting it or presenting it inconsistently with the standard is non-compliant.

## Self-Check

- Are expenses classified consistently by nature or by function, with the choice disclosed (IFRS) and applied across all periods?
- Are the minimum line items required by the applicable framework included on the face of the statement?
- Are meaningful subtotals (gross profit, operating income, income before tax, net income) presented consistently with the business model and industry peers?
- Are items below operating income (other income, finance costs, share of associates) classified consistently, with the operating boundary disclosed?
- Are discontinued operations presented as a single post-tax amount, only when the specific criteria are met?
- Are basic and diluted EPS presented on the face where required (public entities)?
- Does net income reconcile to the retained earnings rollforward, and does comprehensive income reconcile to total equity?
- Is the expense classification choice, allocation basis, and operating boundary disclosed, and has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed the statement?
