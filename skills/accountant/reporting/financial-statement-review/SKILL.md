# Financial Statement Review

Financial statements are not independent tables. The balance sheet, income statement, cash flow statement, schedules, disclosures, and management commentary should tell one coherent story. A report can be mathematically formatted and still be misleading if accounts are inconsistent, revenue is timed incorrectly, liabilities are missing, cash flow contradicts profit, or important assumptions are not disclosed.

Use this skill before presenting financial statements, analyzing reported performance, reviewing management accounts, preparing board or lender materials, comparing periods, or deciding whether the numbers are ready for business decisions. The goal is to make the agent slow down and test whether the statements are complete, consistent, explainable, and appropriately qualified.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Reporting Purpose And Basis

The first question is not "what do the numbers say?" It is "what are these statements for, and what rules or basis do they follow?" A tax-basis report, cash-basis management report, accrual-basis internal report, GAAP report, IFRS report, lender package, and investor presentation may require different treatment.

Clarify:

- reporting period;
- reporting entity or consolidated group;
- accounting basis;
- currency;
- level of rounding;
- audience;
- materiality threshold;
- whether statements are internal, compiled, reviewed, audited, or unaudited;
- whether estimates are preliminary or final;
- known limitations.

Do not imply assurance that does not exist. If statements are unaudited, preliminary, cash-basis, management-only, or missing disclosures, state that clearly.

### Review The Balance Sheet For Existence And Obligations

The balance sheet should show what the entity owns, what it owes, and the residual equity at a point in time. Review whether balances represent real assets and obligations, not merely old accounting entries.

For assets, ask:

- Does cash agree to reconciled bank balances?
- Are receivables collectible and aged?
- Does inventory exist and is it valued appropriately?
- Are prepaid expenses still future benefits?
- Are fixed assets still in use?
- Are deposits recoverable?
- Are related-party balances documented?

For liabilities, ask:

- Are unpaid bills complete?
- Are payroll and tax liabilities recorded?
- Are loans tied to lender statements?
- Is interest accrued when needed?
- Are customer deposits and deferred revenue separated from earned revenue?
- Are contingencies or disputed obligations identified for review?

Equity should reflect ownership activity, retained earnings, distributions, contributions, and prior-period results. If equity movements cannot be explained, stop and reconcile before relying on the statements.

### Review The Income Statement For Performance And Cutoff

The income statement should explain operating performance over a period. It is vulnerable to timing errors, classification errors, one-time events, and missing expenses.

Review:

- revenue by product, service, customer, channel, or geography when relevant;
- gross margin and cost of goods sold;
- payroll and contractor costs;
- sales and marketing spend;
- software, rent, insurance, professional fees, and utilities;
- depreciation and amortization;
- interest and financing costs;
- one-time gains or losses;
- owner compensation or related-party costs;
- tax expense where applicable.

Ask whether revenue and related costs are matched in the correct period. A high-margin month may be real, or it may mean cost was recorded later. A low-expense month may be efficiency, or it may mean invoices were missing.

### Connect Profit To Cash Flow

Profit and cash are different. A profitable entity can run out of cash because customers pay slowly, inventory grows, debt is repaid, equipment is purchased, or owners take distributions. A cash-positive period can still be unprofitable if collections relate to past sales or expenses are unpaid.

Review cash movement through:

- operating cash flow;
- receivable changes;
- inventory changes;
- payable and accrual changes;
- deferred revenue changes;
- capital expenditures;
- debt proceeds and repayments;
- owner contributions and distributions;
- tax payments;
- financing costs.

If the statements do not include a formal cash flow statement, create a bridge from beginning cash to ending cash using known activity. Do not let a profit report substitute for liquidity analysis.

### Test Cross-Statement Relationships

Financial statements should agree with each other. Review relationships that expose errors.

Examples:

- ending retained earnings equals beginning retained earnings plus profit minus distributions, subject to entity-specific adjustments;
- depreciation expense connects to accumulated depreciation and fixed asset schedules;
- accounts receivable movement connects to revenue and cash collections;
- accounts payable movement connects to expenses and cash payments;
- inventory movement connects to purchases, cost of goods sold, and inventory counts;
- debt balances connect to proceeds, principal payments, and interest expense;
- deferred revenue movement connects to billings, cash, and recognized revenue.

When a relationship does not make sense, investigate before explaining the result as a business trend.

### Use Variance Analysis To Detect Errors And Understand Drivers

Variance analysis should distinguish accounting error from business change. Compare periods and ask what changed, why it changed, and whether the change is expected to continue.

Analyze:

- month-over-month variance;
- year-over-year variance;
- actual versus budget;
- actual versus forecast;
- gross margin trend;
- operating margin trend;
- cash conversion;
- working capital movement;
- headcount-related costs;
- revenue concentration;
- unusual or nonrecurring items.

A useful variance explanation includes the driver, amount, period, account, whether it is recurring, and any follow-up action. Avoid vague explanations such as "higher sales" or "timing" when the report needs decision support.

### Check Classification And Presentation

Presentation affects interpretation. Misclassified items can distort margins, operating profit, debt metrics, cash flow, and covenant calculations.

Check whether:

- revenue categories are meaningful;
- cost of goods sold includes direct delivery costs;
- operating expenses are separated from financing or nonoperating items;
- owner transactions are not mixed with ordinary expenses;
- capital expenditures are not expensed without review;
- loan proceeds are not revenue;
- principal repayments are not expense;
- customer deposits are not earned revenue before performance;
- related-party transactions are visible when needed.

Use consistent categories across periods unless there is a documented reason to change. If categories change, disclose the change so comparisons remain fair.

### Respect Materiality Without Ignoring Patterns

Materiality is not just size. Small errors can matter when they affect sensitive accounts, trends, covenants, compensation, taxes, regulatory reporting, fraud risk, or repeated control failures.

Consider:

- absolute amount;
- percentage of revenue, profit, assets, or equity;
- impact on a key metric;
- impact on a debt covenant;
- impact on tax or regulatory filing;
- whether the error changes a trend;
- whether the error is intentional or repeated;
- whether the account is sensitive.

Do not dismiss a small error if it reveals a process weakness or possible manipulation.

### Add Qualifications And Open Items

A good financial review separates conclusions from unresolved issues. Decision-makers need to know what is reliable, what is estimated, what is missing, and what requires professional review.

State:

- completed reconciliations;
- open reconciliations;
- major estimates;
- missing supporting documents;
- unusual transactions under review;
- accounting policy questions;
- tax or legal questions;
- limitations of the report;
- actions required before external use.

This protects the reader from treating preliminary work as final assurance.

## Common Traps

### Looking Only At The Income Statement

Revenue and profit are attractive, but many serious errors live on the balance sheet. Receivables, payables, inventory, debt, deferred revenue, deposits, and equity must be reviewed before drawing conclusions from profit.

### Assuming A Formatted Report Is Reliable

Accounting software can produce professional-looking statements from messy records. Formatting, subtotals, and charts do not prove that accounts were reconciled or that entries are correct.

### Ignoring Cash Flow Contradictions

If profit is rising while cash is falling, explain the bridge. If cash is rising while profit is weak, identify collections, financing, delayed payments, owner contributions, or one-time events. Cash and profit should be reconciled conceptually.

### Treating Estimates As Facts

Bad debt, inventory reserves, depreciation, accrued expenses, revenue recognition, contingent liabilities, useful lives, and allocations may involve judgment. Label estimates and review their basis.

### Comparing Periods After Changing Categories

If accounts were reclassified, chart of accounts changed, entity scope changed, or accounting basis changed, period comparisons may be misleading. Normalize or disclose the change.

### Hiding Related-Party Or Owner Activity

Owner loans, distributions, personal expenses, below-market services, related-party rent, and family payroll can distort performance. These items may be legitimate, but they should not be invisible.

### Overstating Assurance

Do not call a statement audited, reviewed, GAAP-compliant, tax-ready, or lender-ready unless the appropriate process has actually occurred. An internal reasonableness review is not independent assurance.

## Self-Check

- Is the reporting purpose, period, entity scope, accounting basis, audience, and assurance level explicit?
- Do cash balances tie to reconciled accounts, and do receivables, inventory, fixed assets, deposits, and prepaids represent real assets?
- Are payables, payroll liabilities, tax liabilities, debt, accrued expenses, customer deposits, and deferred revenue complete and supported?
- Does revenue belong in the reported period, and are related costs matched or explained?
- Can profit be reconciled conceptually to cash movement and working capital changes?
- Do cross-statement relationships make sense for retained earnings, receivables, payables, inventory, fixed assets, debt, and deferred revenue?
- Are variances explained with specific drivers, amounts, recurrence, and follow-up actions?
- Are classifications consistent and meaningful for margin, operating performance, financing, owner activity, and capital spending?
- Are small but sensitive errors reviewed instead of dismissed automatically?
- Are open items, estimates, missing support, limitations, and required professional reviews clearly stated?
