# Comprehensive Income And OCI

Comprehensive income is the total change in equity from non-owner transactions during a period. It has two parts, net income, which flows through the income statement, and other comprehensive income, or OCI, which bypasses the income statement and goes directly to equity. OCI exists because certain gains and losses are recognized in equity even though they are not considered realized for income statement purposes. Examples include foreign currency translation adjustments, unrealized gains and losses on certain investments, pension remeasurements, and certain hedging instruments. The trap is that OCI is easy to overlook because it does not hit profit, but it still changes equity and accumulated OCI, and mishandling it misstates the balance sheet and the statement of changes in equity. Agents frequently omit OCI items, classify income statement items as OCI or vice versa, or forget the reclassification adjustment when an OCI item is later realized.

Use this skill before classifying an item as OCI, preparing the statement of comprehensive income, handling reclassification adjustments, or reviewing accumulated OCI. The goal is to ensure OCI items are correctly identified, measured, presented, and recycled when realized.

This skill covers OCI classification and presentation principles. The specific items that qualify for OCI, the measurement methods, and the reclassification rules depend on the applicable standard, such as US GAAP or IFRS. Confirm the governing framework before classifying any item.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Net Income From Other Comprehensive Income

The first step is determining whether a gain or loss belongs in net income or in OCI. This is not a choice based on preference, it is determined by the applicable standard.

Items that typically go through OCI include:

- foreign currency translation adjustments on foreign operations;
- unrealized gains and losses on available-for-sale or certain equity investments;
- pension and postretirement remeasurements, including actuarial gains and losses;
- effective portion of certain cash flow hedges;
- changes in the revaluation surplus for certain assets under permitted standards.

Items that belong in net income include all realized gains and losses, operating results, and most routine income and expense. Misrouting an item between the two misstates both profit and total comprehensive income.

### Present Comprehensive Income Correctly

Comprehensive income can be presented either in a single combined statement of comprehensive income or in two statements, an income statement followed by a statement of comprehensive income starting from net income. The choice depends on the applicable standard and presentation preference.

Regardless of format:

- net income and OCI must each be clearly presented;
- total comprehensive income must be shown;
- OCI should be presented net of applicable tax with the tax effect disclosed;
- the components of OCI should be itemized.

A muddled presentation that blends net income and OCI obscures the distinction the standard requires.

### Apply Reclassification Adjustments When OCI Items Are Realized

Many OCI items are temporary. When the underlying item is realized, the amount previously recognized in OCI is reclassified, recycled, to net income. This prevents double-counting, recognizing the gain or loss in both OCI and net income.

Common reclassification situations:

- when an available-for-sale investment is sold, the cumulative unrealized gain or loss in OCI is reclassified to profit;
- when a hedged forecasted transaction affects profit, the related OCI amount is reclassified;
- when a foreign operation is disposed of, the cumulative translation adjustment is reclassified to profit;
- when pension obligations are settled, certain OCI amounts are reclassified.

Forgetting the reclassification adjustment leaves the gain or loss in accumulated OCI forever and double-counts or omits it in profit. Showing the reclassification gross, without offsetting the original OCI, inflates both the OCI component and the reclassification line.

### Track Accumulated OCI As An Equity Component

Each OCI item accumulates in a separate equity component, accumulated other comprehensive income or loss. This balance rolls forward each period.

The accumulated OCI rollforward includes:

- opening accumulated OCI by category;
- current-period OCI by category;
- reclassification adjustments by category;
- closing accumulated OCI by category.

Accumulated OCI is part of total equity and appears on the balance sheet. It can be positive or negative, and it must reconcile to the statement of changes in equity.

### Classify OCI Items By Category

OCI should be presented by category so readers can see the source of each component. Mixing categories into one line hides the different nature and risk of each.

Typical categories:

- foreign currency translation;
- unrealized gains and losses on investments;
- pension and postretirement remeasurements;
- cash flow hedges;
- revaluation surplus, where permitted.

Each category has its own measurement basis, its own tax treatment, and its own reclassification behavior. Presenting them separately is required for transparency.

### Present OCI Net Of Tax With Disclosure

OCI items are generally presented net of their tax effect, with the tax amount disclosed separately or in the notes. The tax treatment of OCI can differ from that of income statement items.

Confirm that:

- the tax effect on each OCI category is calculated;
- OCI is presented net of tax;
- the tax effect is disclosed.

Presenting OCI gross of tax misstates the after-tax change in equity and omits required disclosure.

### Confirm Reclassification Is Permitted For The Specific Item

Not all OCI items can be reclassified. Some, like certain pension remeasurements under recent standards and revaluation surplus in some cases, may never be recycled to profit. Others must be recycled when realized.

Confirm for each OCI item whether reclassification is permitted and when it applies. Forcing a reclassification where none is allowed, or omitting one that is required, misstates both OCI and profit.

## Common Traps

### Omitting OCI Because It Does Not Hit Profit

OCI bypasses the income statement, so it is easy to forget. But it still changes equity. Omitting an OCI item understates total comprehensive income and misstates accumulated OCI and total equity.

### Misclassifying Between Net Income And OCI

Routing a realized gain through OCI, or an unrealized item through profit, violates the standard. The classification is determined by the rules, not by which result looks better.

### Forgetting The Reclassification Adjustment

When an OCI item is realized, the prior OCI amount must be reclassified to profit. Forgetting this double-counts or omits the gain or loss and leaves accumulated OCI distorted permanently.

### Showing Reclassification Gross Instead Of Net

The reclassification should offset the original OCI so the net OCI movement is shown. Presenting both the original and the reclassification gross inflates the lines and misleads readers about the real movement.

### Not Tracking Accumulated OCI By Category

Accumulated OCI is not a single pool. Each category rolls forward separately. Mixing them loses the ability to reconcile and to apply the correct reclassification rules.

### Presenting OCI Gross Of Tax

OCI should be net of tax with the tax disclosed. Gross presentation overstates the after-tax equity movement and omits required disclosure.

### Assuming All OCI Items Recycle

Some OCI items, like certain pension remeasurements, never reclassify. Applying a reclassification where none is permitted misstates profit. Confirm the recycling rule for each item.

## Self-Check

- Is each gain or loss classified as net income or OCI based on the applicable standard rather than preference?
- Is comprehensive income presented with net income, OCI by category, and total comprehensive income clearly shown, with OCI net of tax and the tax disclosed?
- Are reclassification adjustments applied when OCI items are realized, offsetting the original OCI to avoid double-counting?
- Does accumulated OCI roll forward by category, reconciling opening balance, current OCI, reclassifications, and closing balance?
- Are OCI items presented by category, separating translation, investments, pensions, hedges, and revaluation?
- Is the tax effect on each OCI category calculated, with OCI presented net of tax and the tax disclosed?
- Has it been confirmed for each OCI item whether reclassification is permitted and when, with non-recycling items treated correctly?
- Does accumulated OCI reconcile to the balance sheet equity component and the statement of changes in equity?
- Has the applicable standard been confirmed and a qualified accountant consulted for complex OCI classification, measurement, and reclassification decisions?
