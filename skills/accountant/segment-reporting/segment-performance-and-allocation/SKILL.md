# Segment Performance And Allocation

Once segments are identified, the next challenge is measuring their performance. Segment profit or loss is not the same as a hypothetical standalone profit. It is the measure, or measures, that the CODM actually uses to assess the segment. This means the allocation of shared costs, corporate overhead, interest, taxes, and shared assets must follow the internal reporting the CODM receives, not a theoretical full-cost allocation. The trap is twofold. Agents either impose a full absorption allocation that the CODM never sees, producing segment results that do not match any internal view, or they leave so much unallocated that the segments look more profitable than the internal reporting suggests. Both misstate segment performance and mislead users about where the business makes its money.

Use this skill before measuring segment profit or loss, allocating shared costs or assets, defining the segment performance measure, or handling unallocated items. The goal is to ensure segment performance reflects the CODM's actual measure and that allocations are consistent, disclosed, and not manipulated.

This skill covers segment performance measurement and allocation. The specific measures, allocation bases, and disclosure depend on the applicable standard and the entity's internal reporting. Confirm the governing standard and the actual CODM reporting before finalizing segment measures.

## Core Rules

### Report The Measure The CODM Uses

Segment profit or loss is the measure, or measures, that the CODM reviews. If the CODM reviews contribution margin, report contribution margin. If the CODM reviews earnings before interest and taxes, report that. Do not substitute a different measure because it seems more standard.

Confirm the segment measure by examining:

- what line the CODM's reporting package presents as the segment result;
- whether the CODM reviews one measure or multiple measures;
- whether the measure is before or after allocated corporate costs, interest, and taxes;
- whether the measure includes or excludes specific items like stock compensation or impairments.

Reporting a measure the CODM does not use defeats the purpose of the management approach and misleads users about how performance is evaluated.

### Allocate Shared Costs On A Consistent, Disclosed Basis

Shared costs that the CODM allocates to segments in the internal reporting should be allocated the same way in segment reporting. The allocation basis should be consistent and disclosed.

Common allocation bases include:

- revenue for broad shared services;
- headcount for people-related overhead;
- usage metrics for IT or shared infrastructure;
- direct costs for certain support functions.

The key is that the external allocation matches the internal allocation the CODM sees. If the CODM does not allocate a cost, it generally stays in unallocated corporate, even if a theoretical allocation seems possible.

### Handle Corporate And Unallocated Items Clearly

Costs and items the CODM does not allocate to segments remain in a corporate or unallocated category. This category is legitimate, but it must be presented clearly and not used to bury costs that should be in segments.

Typical unallocated items include:

- corporate headquarters costs not pushed down to segments;
- interest expense, if the CODM manages debt centrally;
- income taxes, if managed at the consolidated level;
- certain shared services the CODM does not allocate;
- goodwill impairments or restructuring managed centrally.

The unallocated category reconciles the segment results to consolidated totals. A large or growing unallocated balance should be explained, because it can hide costs that distort segment profitability.

### Allocate Shared Assets On The Same Principle

Segment assets are the assets the CODM uses to assess the segment, typically including assets directly attributable to the segment and shared assets allocated on the internal basis. Assets the CODM does not allocate, like consolidated goodwill or corporate cash, may remain unallocated.

Apply the same management approach to assets:

- include assets the CODM attributes to the segment;
- allocate shared assets on the internal basis where the CODM does so;
- keep unallocated assets, like corporate-level assets, in a separate category.

Forcing a full asset allocation the CODM never sees produces segment asset numbers that do not match any internal view.

### Reconcile Segment Totals To Consolidated Amounts

The sum of segment revenue, profit or loss, and assets must reconcile to the consolidated totals, with the reconciling items identified. This reconciliation is a required disclosure and a key control.

The reconciliation typically shows:

- total segment revenue to consolidated revenue, with intersegment eliminations;
- total segment profit or loss to consolidated profit before tax, with unallocated corporate items;
- total segment assets to consolidated assets, with unallocated assets and eliminations.

A reconciliation that does not tie indicates a missing segment, a misallocated item, or an error in the consolidated numbers.

### Apply Allocation Consistently Across Periods

The allocation bases and the segment measure should be consistent across periods. Changing the allocation or the measure period to period, without a genuine change in CODM reporting, undermines comparability and can be used to flatter segment results.

If the CODM genuinely changes the internal measure or allocation, recast prior periods to conform and disclose the change. Do not change the external segment reporting unilaterally.

### Avoid Manipulating Allocations To Improve Segment Results

Because allocations affect reported segment profitability, there is temptation to shift costs to the unallocated category or to change the allocation base to favor a segment. The external reporting must follow the internal reporting faithfully, not an optimized version of it.

Review allocations for changes that conveniently improve a struggling segment or burden a strong one, and ensure any change reflects a real change in CODM reporting.

## Common Traps

### Substituting A Standard Measure For The CODM Measure

Reporting EBITDA or operating income because it is conventional, when the CODM reviews a different measure, violates the management approach. Report what the CODM sees.

### Imposing Full Absorption Allocation The CODM Never Uses

Allocating all corporate costs to segments to show fully loaded segment profit, when the CODM does not allocate them, produces numbers that match no internal view and mislead users.

### Leaving Too Much Unallocated

The opposite trap is leaving so much in corporate that segments look more profitable than the CODM's view. The unallocated category should reflect what the CODM does not allocate, not a dumping ground.

### Inconsistent Allocation Across Periods

Changing the allocation base or measure period to period without a real change in CODM reporting breaks comparability and can manipulate segment results.

### Failing To Reconcile Segments To Consolidated Totals

If the segment totals do not reconcile to the consolidated statements, with identified reconciling items, the segment disclosure is incomplete and likely wrong.

### Allocating Assets The CODM Does Not Attribute

Forcing goodwill, corporate cash, or other central assets into segments when the CODM does not attribute them distorts segment asset measures and return metrics.

### Shifting Costs To Flatter A Segment

Moving costs into unallocated or changing the allocation to improve a segment's reported result is manipulation. The external reporting must follow the internal reporting.

## Self-Check

- Is the reported segment profit or loss the measure the CODM actually reviews, not a substituted conventional measure?
- Are shared costs allocated on the same basis the CODM uses internally, with the basis disclosed?
- Are corporate and unallocated items presented clearly and not used to bury costs that belong in segments?
- Are segment assets based on what the CODM attributes to the segment, with unallocated assets shown separately?
- Do segment totals for revenue, profit or loss, and assets reconcile to the consolidated amounts with identified reconciling items?
- Are the allocation bases and segment measure consistent across periods, with any genuine change recast and disclosed?
- Have allocations been reviewed for changes that conveniently improve or burden specific segments?
- Has the applicable segment standard and the actual CODM reporting package been confirmed before finalizing segment measures?
