# Segment Disclosure And Reconciliation

Segment disclosure is where the internal view of the business meets the external reporting requirement. The footnote must describe the segments, the products and services they offer, the CODM, the measure of segment profit or loss, and a reconciliation of segment amounts to the consolidated financial statements. The trap is that segment disclosures are often incomplete or inconsistent. Required information about segment assets, liabilities, capital expenditures, depreciation, and significant non-cash items is omitted. The reconciliation does not tie. The general information about how segments were identified is vague. Agents frequently treat the segment footnote as a copy of last year with updated numbers, when the standard requires specific, current, and reconciled information.

Use this skill before preparing segment disclosures, building the reconciliation, or reviewing the footnote for completeness. The goal is to ensure the disclosure contains all required information, ties to the consolidated statements, and faithfully reflects the current segment structure.

This skill covers segment disclosure and reconciliation principles. The specific required disclosures, the level of detail, and the presentation depend on the applicable standard, such as US GAAP or IFRS, which have been updated in recent years. Confirm the current requirements of the governing standard before preparing the footnote.

## Core Rules

### Provide The Required General Information

The segment footnote must begin with general information that explains how segments were identified and how they operate.

Required general information typically includes:

- the basis for identifying segments, the management approach;
- the identity of the CODM and their function;
- the products or services offered by each reportable segment;
- the measure of segment profit or loss used by the CODM;
- significant accounting policies specific to segment reporting.

Vague general information, such as omitting the CODM identity or not describing the segment measure, fails the disclosure requirement even if the numbers are right.

### Disclose Segment Profit Or Loss And Its Components

For each reportable segment, disclose the measure of segment profit or loss and the significant components that make it up.

Typical components to disclose include:

- external revenue;
- intersegment revenue;
- cost categories the CODM reviews, such as materials, labor, or overhead;
- significant expense categories, where required by the standard;
- depreciation, amortization, and other significant non-cash items;
- interest expense, if included in the segment measure;
- income tax expense, if included in the segment measure.

The components disclosed should match what the CODM reviews. Omitting a significant expense category that the standard requires, or that the CODM sees, makes the disclosure incomplete.

### Disclose Segment Assets, Liabilities, And Capital Additions

Beyond profit or loss, the standard requires disclosure of other segment information where it is regularly provided to the CODM.

This may include:

- segment assets;
- segment liabilities, where regularly reviewed;
- capital expenditures and additions to segment assets;
- depreciation and amortization by segment;
- significant non-cash items other than depreciation.

Disclose what the CODM reviews. If the CODM does not review segment liabilities, that may not be required, but confirm the specific requirement of the standard, which has expanded in recent updates.

### Build A Reconciliation For Each Disclosed Amount

Every material segment amount must reconcile to the corresponding consolidated total. The reconciliation is both a required disclosure and a control.

Reconciliations are needed for:

- segment external and total revenue to consolidated revenue;
- segment profit or loss to consolidated profit before tax;
- segment assets to consolidated assets;
- any other disclosed segment amount to the corresponding consolidated line.

Each reconciliation should show the segment total, the reconciling items such as unallocated corporate amounts and eliminations, and the consolidated amount. A reconciliation that does not tie, or that omits material reconciling items, is a disclosure failure.

### Explain Significant Reconciling Items

The reconciling items should be identifiable and explained. Large unexplained differences between segment totals and consolidated amounts undermine the usefulness of the disclosure.

Common reconciling items include:

- intersegment revenue eliminations;
- unallocated corporate costs and assets;
- consolidation adjustments from acquisitions or foreign currency;
- items the CODM excludes from segment measures, like goodwill impairment or restructuring.

Identify each material reconciling item so a reader can understand the bridge from segments to consolidated.

### Disclose Changes In Segment Structure

When the segment structure changes, the footnote must disclose the change and recast prior-period segment information to conform, unless impracticable.

Disclose:

- the nature and reason for the change;
- the impact on reportable segments;
- whether prior periods were recast;
- if recasting is impracticable, the fact and the reason.

Silently changing the segment structure without disclosure or recasting breaks comparability and confuses users comparing periods.

### Present Geographic And Major Customer Information

Beyond operating segments, the standard requires disclosure of revenue by geography and information about major customers, regardless of the segment structure.

Typically disclose:

- revenue from external customers attributed to the country of domicile and to all foreign countries, or by geographic region;
- long-lived assets by country or region, where required;
- the extent of reliance on major customers, generally any customer representing 10 percent or more of revenue.

This information is required even for single-segment entities and supplements the segment disclosure.

### Tie Segment Disclosure To The Current Structure And Period

The footnote must reflect the current period's segment structure and the current CODM reporting. Copying last year's footnote and updating only the numbers, without confirming the structure, the CODM, and the measure are still current, produces stale or inaccurate disclosure.

## Common Traps

### Copying Last Year's Footnote Without Review

Segment structures, CODM measures, and required disclosures change. A copied footnote that does not reflect the current structure or the current standard's requirements is incomplete and potentially wrong.

### Omitting Required General Information

Failing to identify the CODM, describe the segment measure, or explain the basis for segmentation makes the disclosure noncompliant even if the numbers tie.

### Missing The Reconciliation

Every material segment amount must reconcile to the consolidated total. Omitting the reconciliation, or presenting one that does not tie, is a disclosure failure.

### Omitting Segment Assets, Liabilities, Or Capital Additions

The standard requires disclosure of segment assets and other information where regularly provided to the CODM. Leaving these out, especially given recent standard expansions, is incomplete.

### Unexplained Large Reconciling Items

A big gap between segment totals and consolidated amounts, with no explanation of the reconciling items, undermines trust in the disclosure and hides what drives the difference.

### Not Disclosing Geographic And Major Customer Information

These disclosures are required regardless of segment structure and are often forgotten. Single-segment entities still need them.

### Failing To Disclose And Recast After A Structure Change

Changing segments without disclosure or recasting breaks comparability. The change must be explained and prior periods conformed.

## Self-Check

- Does the footnote provide general information identifying the CODM, the segment measure, the basis for segmentation, and the products or services of each segment?
- For each reportable segment, are profit or loss and its significant components, including required expense categories, depreciation, and non-cash items, disclosed?
- Are segment assets, liabilities where applicable, capital additions, and depreciation disclosed where regularly provided to the CODM?
- Does every material segment amount reconcile to the corresponding consolidated total, with reconciling items identified?
- Are significant reconciling items, like eliminations, unallocated corporate amounts, and consolidation adjustments, explained?
- Are changes in segment structure disclosed, with prior periods recast or the impracticability exception explained?
- Is geographic revenue and long-lived asset information, and major customer reliance, disclosed regardless of segment structure?
- Does the footnote reflect the current period's segment structure and CODM reporting, not a copied prior version?
- Has the current applicable segment standard been confirmed before preparing the footnote?
