# Operating Segment Identification

Segment identification is the foundation of segment reporting, and it is driven by how the business is actually managed, not by how it is organized on paper. Under the management approach used by major standards, an operating segment is a component of the business whose operating results are regularly reviewed by the chief operating decision maker, the CODM, to allocate resources and assess performance. The trap is that agents often define segments by legal entity, geography, or product line as it appears on an org chart, when the standard requires the segments to match the internal reporting that actually reaches the CODM. Getting identification wrong cascades into every segment disclosure, because the segments, their performance, and their reconciliation all depend on this first step.

Use this skill before identifying operating segments, determining reportable segments, aggregating components, or reviewing the segment structure for reporting. The goal is to ensure the segment structure reflects the management approach and the quantitative and aggregation criteria are applied correctly.

This skill covers segment identification principles. The specific thresholds, the definition of the CODM, and the disclosure requirements depend on the applicable standard, such as US GAAP or IFRS. Confirm the governing standard before finalizing the segment structure.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Management Approach

Segment reporting follows the management approach. Segments are identified based on the internal financial information that is regularly provided to the CODM for decision-making, not based on an external view of the business.

The management approach requires identifying:

- who the CODM is, which may be an individual or a group;
- what internal reporting the CODM actually receives;
- how the CODM uses that information to allocate resources and assess performance;
- which components of the business are the subjects of that review.

Segments that the CODM does not review are not operating segments, even if they look like logical business units. Conversely, components the CODM reviews closely are operating segments even if they are not separate legal entities.

### Identify The Chief Operating Decision Maker

The CODM is the function that allocates resources to and assesses the performance of segments. It may be a CEO, a board, an executive committee, or another decision-making group.

Determine the CODM by examining:

- who makes the resource allocation decisions;
- who reviews segment performance and holds segment leaders accountable;
- what level of financial detail is presented to that person or group;
- whether the CODM function is an individual or a committee.

The identity of the CODM shapes what internal reporting counts for segment purposes. A different CODM or a different reporting package can change the segment structure.

### Confirm The Component Is Regularly Reviewed

An operating segment must be a component whose operating results are regularly reviewed by the CODM. Occasional or ad hoc review is not enough.

Confirm that:

- the component's results are provided to the CODM on a recurring basis, typically monthly or quarterly;
- the CODM uses the information to make resource and performance decisions;
- discrete financial information is available for the component.

A business unit that exists organizationally but whose results are not separately reviewed by the CODM is not an operating segment.

### Apply Quantitative Thresholds For Reportable Segments

Not every operating segment must be separately reported. A segment is reportable if it meets one of the quantitative thresholds.

A segment is generally reportable if it meets any of:

- revenue, including external and intersegment, is 10 percent or more of total revenue;
- profit or loss is 10 percent or more of the greater of combined profit or combined loss of all segments;
- assets are 10 percent or more of total segment assets.

Apply the thresholds to each operating segment. Segments below all thresholds may still need to be reported if required for the total to reach a sufficient coverage of the business, typically 75 percent of external revenue.

### Aggregate Operating Segments When Criteria Are Met

Two or more operating segments may be aggregated into a single reportable segment if they share similar economic characteristics and similar characteristics in products, processes, customers, and distribution.

Aggregation criteria typically require:

- similar long-term financial performance;
- similar products or services;
- similar production or delivery processes;
- similar customer types;
- similar distribution methods;
- similar regulatory environment.

Aggregation that does not genuinely meet these criteria combines segments that should be reported separately, masking their different performance and risk. Apply the criteria strictly, not as a convenience to reduce disclosure.

### Ensure Sufficient Coverage Of External Revenue

The reportable segments must collectively account for at least 75 percent of external revenue. If they fall short, additional segments must be identified as reportable until the threshold is met, even if those segments did not individually meet the 10 percent tests.

If coverage is below the threshold after applying the quantitative tests, add the next-largest segments until 75 percent is reached. Remaining segments are combined into an all other category.

### Review The Segment Structure When The Business Changes

The segment structure is not permanent. If the business reorganizes, if the CODM changes, or if the internal reporting package changes, the segments may need to be re-identified.

Trigger a segment review when:

- there is a reorganization that changes how the CODM reviews the business;
- the CODM function changes;
- a significant acquisition or divestiture changes the business composition;
- the internal reporting package is redesigned.

Recasting prior-period segment information to conform to the new structure is generally required so comparatives are meaningful.

## Common Traps

### Defining Segments By Org Chart Instead Of CODM Reporting

Segments defined by legal entity, geography, or product line that do not match what the CODM reviews do not satisfy the management approach. The segments must reflect internal decision-making reporting.

### Treating The CODM As A Formality

The CODM is the anchor of segment identification. Assuming the CEO is the CODM without examining actual reporting, or ignoring a committee structure, misidentifies the segments.

### Aggregating Segments For Convenience

Combining segments to reduce disclosure, without genuinely similar economics and characteristics, violates the aggregation criteria and hides real differences in performance and risk.

### Missing The 75 Percent Coverage Test

Applying the 10 percent tests and stopping, without checking whether reportable segments cover 75 percent of external revenue, leaves the segment disclosure incomplete.

### Keeping A Stale Segment Structure After A Reorganization

If the business reorganizes but the segment reporting does not change, the segments no longer reflect how the business is managed. Recast to the new structure.

### Including Components The CODM Does Not Review

A business unit that is not regularly reviewed by the CODM is not an operating segment, regardless of how distinct it appears. Including it misstates the segment structure.

### Applying Quantitative Tests To The Wrong Base

The 10 percent profit test uses the greater of combined profit or combined loss, not absolute profit. Applying the test to the wrong base misclassifies reportable segments.

## Self-Check

- Are operating segments identified based on the components the CODM regularly reviews for resource allocation and performance, not on the org chart?
- Has the CODM been identified by examining who allocates resources and assesses performance, whether an individual or a group?
- Is each operating segment one whose discrete results are provided to the CODM on a recurring basis and used for decisions?
- Are the quantitative thresholds, 10 percent of revenue, profit or loss, or assets, applied to identify reportable segments?
- Are segments aggregated only when they genuinely share similar economics, products, processes, customers, and distribution?
- Do the reportable segments collectively cover at least 75 percent of external revenue, with additional segments added if needed?
- Is the segment structure reviewed and recast when the business reorganizes, the CODM changes, or the internal reporting changes?
- Have prior periods been recast to conform to the current segment structure for comparability?
- Has the applicable segment reporting standard been confirmed before finalizing the segment structure?
