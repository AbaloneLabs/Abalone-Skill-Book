# Excise And Other Indirect Taxes

Excise taxes, customs duties, and other indirect taxes sit alongside sales tax and VAT but follow different rules. Some are embedded in the cost of goods (excise on fuel, alcohol, tobacco) and become part of inventory or expense; others are pass-through liabilities collected and remitted. The accounting error pattern is distinctive: agents treat an embedded excise as a pass-through, or a pass-through as a recoverable input, or accrue a duty at the wrong point in the supply chain, or ignore the interaction between customs valuation and transfer pricing. Each misclassification misstates inventory cost, expense, or liability, and customs and excise errors can carry significant penalties.

Use this skill before accounting for excise taxes, customs duties, or other indirect taxes, determining whether a tax is embedded or pass-through, computing dutiable value, timing an accrual, assessing recoverability, or reviewing customs and transfer pricing interaction. The goal is to classify each indirect tax correctly, accrue it at the right point, and capture recoverability or cost capitalization accurately.

This is an indirect tax domain. Excise, customs, and other indirect tax rules vary by jurisdiction and product: excise rates and per-unit charges, customs classification (HS codes), valuation methods, duty deferment and drawback schemes, and recoverability all differ across countries and authorities. There is no single universal rule. Do not apply one jurisdiction's classification or valuation rules to another. Final determinations on classification, valuation, and duty exposure should be routed to a qualified customs or indirect tax professional. This skill is operational guidance for the accounting accrual and classification, not professional tax advice, and it carries an explicit limitation that jurisdiction-specific excise and customs rules are not exhaustively covered.

## Core Rules

### Classify Each Indirect Tax As Embedded Or Pass-Through

The first decision is whether the tax is embedded in the cost of the goods (becoming part of inventory or expense) or a pass-through liability (collected and remitted like sales tax). Excise taxes are frequently embedded: the producer or importer pays the excise, and it becomes part of the cost of the product, recovered through the selling price rather than refunded. Customs duties paid on imported goods are typically embedded into the cost of the inventory. Other indirect taxes, such as certain environmental levies, may be pass-through.

Classify each tax based on its legal incidence and recoverability. An embedded tax is capitalized into inventory or expensed; a pass-through tax is a liability collected and remitted. Misclassifying reverses the accounting.

### Determine Dutiable Value And Classification

Customs duties are computed on dutiable value, which generally includes the transaction value plus certain additions (freight, insurance, assists, royalties) and excludes others, depending on the jurisdiction's valuation rules. Classification under the harmonized system (HS) determines the duty rate. Both value and classification drive the duty, and errors here compound across shipments.

Confirm the dutiable value components required by the jurisdiction, classify the goods under the correct HS code, and apply the resulting rate. Maintain documentation supporting the declared value and classification, because customs authorities audit both.

### Time The Accrual At The Correct Point In The Supply Chain

Indirect taxes must be accrued at the point the taxable event occurs. For excise, this may be at production, removal from a bonded warehouse, or import, depending on the regime. For customs duty, it is generally at importation. Accruing at the wrong point (for example, at sale rather than at import) misstates the period of the liability and can create late-payment exposure.

Map the supply chain and identify where each tax's taxable event occurs, then accrue at that point. For deferred duty or excise schemes, confirm the deferral conditions and accrue accordingly.

### Assess Recoverability And Capitalization

Determine whether each indirect tax is recoverable or becomes part of cost. Non-recoverable excise and customs duty are capitalized into inventory cost and flow through cost of goods sold when the inventory is sold. Recoverable amounts are presented as an asset or netted against the related liability. Duty drawback or refund schemes may allow recovery of duty paid on re-exported goods; account for these as they are claimed, not prospectively.

Do not expense embedded excise or duty as incurred if it relates to inventory; capitalize it and release it through cost of goods sold on sale.

### Coordinate Customs Valuation With Transfer Pricing

Customs valuation and transfer pricing interact. The transaction value used for customs must reflect the price actually paid, and transfer pricing adjustments (year-end true-ups between related parties) can affect customs value. A transfer pricing adjustment that increases the price may increase dutiable value and duty owed; a decrease may support a refund or reduction. Conversely, customs-compliant values may inform transfer pricing.

Coordinate the two regimes: ensure transfer pricing adjustments are considered for customs impact, and that customs declarations are consistent with the transfer pricing policy. Ignoring the interaction creates both customs exposure and transfer pricing risk.

### Account For Penalty And Interest Exposure

Excise and customs errors carry penalty and interest exposure distinct from the tax itself. When a misclassification or underpayment is identified, assess whether penalties and interest are probable and estimable, and accrue them per the contingency framework and the entity's policy. Coordinate with the broader interest and penalties policy for consistency.

### Disclose Material Indirect Tax Exposures

Where excise, customs, or other indirect tax positions are material or uncertain, disclose the exposure and any contingencies consistent with the applicable framework. For businesses where excise is a significant cost component, consider whether separate disclosure of the cost is informative to users.

## Common Traps

### Treating An Embedded Excise As A Pass-Through

Recording excise that is legally embedded in product cost as a collected-and-remitted liability misstates both inventory cost and the liability. Confirm the legal incidence.

### Expensing Duty On Inventory At Import

Capitalizable customs duty expensed at import understates inventory and overstates cost of goods sold in the wrong period. Capitalize duty into inventory cost.

### Wrong Dutiable Value Components

Omitting required additions (freight, insurance, assists) or including excluded items misstates dutiable value and duty across all shipments. Apply the jurisdiction's valuation rules.

### Misclassification Under The Harmonized System

An incorrect HS code applies the wrong duty rate. Classification errors compound and attract customs scrutiny. Confirm the HS code with documentation.

### Accruing At Sale Instead Of The Taxable Event

Excise or duty accrued when the goods are sold rather than at production, removal, or import misstates the period and creates late-payment exposure.

### Ignoring Transfer Pricing Adjustments On Customs Value

Year-end transfer pricing true-ups that change the price paid can change dutiable value. Failing to consider the customs effect leaves duty under- or over-declared.

### Assuming All Indirect Taxes Are Recoverable

Excise and customs duty are often non-recoverable and become cost. Assuming recoverability overstates assets and understates cost.

## Self-Check

- Is each indirect tax classified as embedded (capitalized to inventory or expense) or pass-through (liability collected and remitted) based on legal incidence?
- Is customs duty capitalized into inventory cost and released through cost of goods sold on sale rather than expensed at import?
- Is dutiable value computed with the jurisdiction's required additions (freight, insurance, assists, royalties) and exclusions?
- Are goods classified under the correct harmonized system code with supporting documentation determining the duty rate?
- Is each tax accrued at the point its taxable event occurs (production, removal, import) rather than at sale?
- Is recoverability assessed, with non-recoverable amounts capitalized to cost and recoverable amounts presented as asset or net, including drawback claims?
- Are transfer pricing adjustments reviewed for customs valuation impact and customs declarations reconciled to the transfer pricing policy?
- Is penalty and interest exposure on identified excise or customs errors assessed for probability and estimability and accrued per policy?
- Are material or uncertain indirect tax positions and contingencies disclosed per the applicable framework?
- Is professional referral noted for classification, valuation, and duty determinations, and is the explicit limitation on jurisdiction-specific rules acknowledged?
