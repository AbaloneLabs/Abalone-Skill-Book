# VAT Reconciliation And Filing

The VAT return is the point where the accumulated output and input tax postings become a legal declaration to the tax authority. By the time the return is filed, the underlying ledger activity is usually months old, and errors that were never reconciled become commitments that are hard to unwind. A return that is filed on time but does not reconcile to the books, or that omits a rate change or a batch of transactions, creates exposure that often surfaces only at audit. Reconciliation is not bureaucracy. It is the control that makes the return defensible.

Use this skill before preparing a VAT return, reconciling VAT control accounts, resolving differences between ledgers and returns, or reviewing a filed or draft return. The goal is to prevent the agent from filing a return built on unreconciled or incomplete data. VAT and GST filing rules, return periods, deadlines, and correction mechanics vary by jurisdiction and registration type. This skill provides structural judgment, not final tax determinations. A qualified tax or VAT professional should review any return before filing.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile Control Accounts To The General Ledger Before Filing

The VAT payable or receivable balance on the return must reconcile to the VAT control accounts in the general ledger. Start by tying output tax and input tax control accounts to the sales and purchase ledgers, then to the general ledger, and finally to the return. Every step must foot. A return prepared from ledger totals that have not been reconciled to the control accounts is built on unverified data.

The reconciliation chain is typically:

- sales and purchase ledger detail to VAT subledgers or tracking categories;
- VAT subledgers to output and input tax control accounts;
- control accounts to the general ledger VAT balance;
- general ledger balance to the draft return.

Breaks at any link must be investigated before filing.

### Confirm The Return Period And Coverage

VAT returns cover a defined period, and the transactions included must match that period based on the applicable tax point rules, not merely the posting date. Confirm the period dates, the transactions captured, and that no period is double counted or omitted. A return that includes last period's late postings and excludes this period's unbilled supplies misstates both periods.

Check:

- does the period match the registration and filing frequency?
- are transactions captured by tax point rather than posting date where required?
- are prior period corrections included in the right place?
- are any estimates or accruals handled consistently with prior returns?

### Validate Rate And Box Mapping

Each jurisdictional return has specific boxes or lines for output tax, input tax, net output, adjustments, and other categories. Mapping ledger activity to the correct box is where many errors occur. A standard-rated sale posted to a reduced-rate box, or input tax posted to an adjustment box, distorts the return even when totals tie.

Confirm:

- each rate category maps to the correct return box;
- reverse charge entries map to the correct boxes on both sides;
- adjustments, corrections, and credit notes map to the correct lines;
- zero-rated and exempt turnover is reported where the return requires it.

### Resolve Unreconciled Differences Before Filing

Unreconciled differences are not acceptable noise. They indicate posting errors, missing transactions, wrong rates, duplicate entries, or timing issues. Investigate every material variance. Small immaterial differences should be documented and monitored, not silently absorbed. Filing with a known unreconciled difference converts a controllable error into a committed liability.

### Handle Corrections And Prior Period Errors Correctly

Errors from prior returns are corrected through specific mechanisms that vary by jurisdiction, such as adjustment boxes on the current return, voluntary disclosure, or separate correction procedures. The threshold and method often depend on the error size and direction. Do not silently net a prior error into the current period output or input totals. Apply the jurisdictional correction mechanism and document the original error and the correction.

### Reconcile The Cash Movement After Filing

After the return is filed and paid or refunded, the payment or refund must clear the VAT control account to zero, or to the expected carryforward. A lingering balance after payment indicates that the cash does not match the return, which often reveals a reconciliation error that was masked. Confirm the control account reflects the filed return and the settled cash.

### Retain The Reconciliation And Supporting Detail

The reconciliation, the return, and the supporting detail should be retained for the period required by the jurisdiction, which is often several years. Retention is not optional. Without the reconciliation, defending the return at audit is far harder. Organize the evidence by period so it can be retrieved.

### Confirm Deadlines And Filing Mechanics

Filing deadlines, payment deadlines, and the mechanics of submission vary by jurisdiction and can carry penalties for lateness even when the return is correct. Confirm the deadline, the submission method, and whether payment is due with the return or separately. Treat the deadline as a hard constraint that gates the reconciliation effort.

## Common Traps

### Filing From Unreconciled Ledger Totals

Preparing the return directly from ledger reports without reconciling to control accounts hides posting errors. The return may tie to the ledger but both may be wrong. Reconcile before filing, not after.

### Using Posting Date Instead Of Tax Point

Transactions captured by posting date rather than tax point fall into the wrong period. Late-posted invoices and prepayments distort the return. Apply the tax point rules for the jurisdiction.

### Misusing Adjustment And Correction Boxes

Prior period errors dumped into current output or input totals, rather than the correction mechanism, distort both periods and may breach rules on how corrections must be made. Use the correct adjustment line.

### Ignoring Reverse Charge Reporting

Reverse charge transactions often require reporting in specific boxes even when the net tax is zero. Omitting the reporting, or posting it to the wrong box, triggers mismatches and queries from the authority.

### Carrying Forward Unreconciled Balances

A control account balance that never clears, or that grows over periods, signals unresolved errors. Carrying it forward compounds the problem and makes eventual resolution much harder.

### Filing On Time But Wrong

Meeting the deadline with an unreconciled or incorrect return is not a success. The penalty and interest exposure from a wrong return often exceeds late filing penalties. Prioritize accuracy within the deadline.

### Forgetting To Clear The Control Account After Settlement

After payment or refund, the VAT control account should reflect the settled position. A balance left behind indicates the cash did not match the return, hiding a reconciliation gap.

### Assuming The Software Is Always Right

Accounting software maps transactions to return boxes based on settings that can be wrong or stale. Validate the mapping rather than trusting the generated return blindly.

## Self-Check

- Do output and input tax control accounts reconcile to the sales and purchase ledgers, the general ledger, and the draft return before filing?
- Does the return period and transaction coverage match the registration and filing frequency, with transactions captured by tax point where required?
- Does each rate category, reverse charge entry, and adjustment map to the correct return box for the relevant jurisdiction?
- Are all material unreconciled differences investigated and resolved before the return is filed rather than carried forward?
- Are prior period errors corrected through the jurisdictional correction mechanism and documented, rather than silently netted into current totals?
- After filing and settlement, does the VAT control account clear to the expected position with no unexplained lingering balance?
- Are the reconciliation, the return, and supporting detail retained for the period required by the jurisdiction and organized for retrieval?
- Are filing and payment deadlines and submission mechanics confirmed and treated as hard constraints on the reconciliation effort?
- Has a qualified tax or VAT professional reviewed the return for the relevant jurisdiction before it is filed?
