# VAT Input And Output Accounting

Value-added tax (VAT) and goods and services tax (GST) are accounted for as a net system: output tax is charged on supplies the business makes, input tax is recovered on purchases the business makes, and the net amount is either payable to or recoverable from the authority. The accounting is deceptively simple in concept and error-prone in practice. Agents often assume all input tax is recoverable, miss reverse charge mechanics on imports or cross-border services, misstate output tax on exempt versus taxable supplies, or treat intercompany charges without considering deferred or disregarded tax rules. Each error misstates the net VAT position and can create material exposure or lost recovery.

Use this skill before accounting for VAT or GST input and output tax, assessing input tax credit eligibility, applying reverse charge, computing the net payable or recoverable, or reviewing intercompany VAT. The goal is to account for output and input tax correctly, recover all eligible input tax, and report the net position accurately.

This is an indirect tax domain. VAT and GST rules vary by country and region: standard rates, reduced rates, zero-rated and exempt supplies, input tax credit eligibility, partial exemption methods, reverse charge triggers, registration thresholds, and intercompany rules all differ across jurisdictions. There is no single VAT system. Do not apply one country's rules to another. Final determinations on credit eligibility, partial exemption, and reverse charge should be routed to a qualified indirect tax professional. This skill is operational guidance for the accounting of input and output tax, not professional tax advice, and it carries an explicit limitation that jurisdiction-specific VAT and GST rules are not exhaustively covered.

## Core Rules

### Account For Output Tax On All Taxable Supplies

Output tax is charged on taxable supplies the business makes at the rate applicable in the jurisdiction of supply. Confirm the place of supply rules, because cross-border supplies may be taxed where the customer is located, where the service is performed, or where goods are delivered, depending on the rule. Distinguish standard-rated, reduced-rated, zero-rated, and exempt supplies, because exempt supplies generate no output tax and may block related input tax recovery.

Record output tax as a liability at the time of supply (or per the jurisdiction's tax point rules), not when cash is received, unless the cash accounting scheme applies. Track output tax by rate and by supply type to support the return.

### Assess Input Tax Credit Eligibility On Each Purchase

Not all input tax is recoverable. Input tax credit eligibility depends on whether the purchase relates to a taxable supply, whether the business is partially exempt, whether the input is blocked (such as entertainment or certain motor expenses), and whether a valid tax invoice or equivalent supports the claim. Recover input tax only where eligible and supported.

Maintain a process at invoice entry to capture input tax, flag blocked or potentially restricted inputs, and retain the supporting tax invoice. Recoverable input tax is presented as a current asset or netted against output tax per the jurisdictional presentation rules.

### Apply Partial Exemption Methods Where Relevant

A business making both taxable and exempt supplies is partially exempt and cannot recover all input tax. Partial exemption requires an apportionment method to split input tax between taxable (recoverable) and exempt (non-recoverable) uses. Confirm the approved or standard method for the jurisdiction, apply it consistently, and review it periodically for fairness.

Direct attribution (input tax directly attributable to taxable supplies is recoverable; directly attributable to exempt is not) comes first, with residual input tax apportioned by the method. Non-recovered input tax becomes a cost to the business.

### Apply Reverse Charge On Imports And Cross-Border Services

Reverse charge shifts the liability to account for tax from the supplier to the customer, common on imports of services or goods and on certain domestic supplies. Under reverse charge, the customer accounts for both output tax (as if making the supply) and input tax (as if receiving it), often netting to zero if full recovery applies, but the gross amounts must still be reported.

Missing reverse charge is a frequent error that understates both output and input tax on the return and can create exposure even when the net is zero. Identify cross-border purchases of services and imports that trigger reverse charge and account for both legs.

### Compute Net VAT Payable Or Recoverable

The net VAT position is output tax minus recoverable input tax for the period. A positive net amount is payable to the authority; a negative amount is recoverable, either as a refund or carried forward per jurisdictional rules. Present the net payable as a current liability and any recoverable amount as a current asset, or net them per the jurisdictional presentation rules and the framework.

Confirm whether the jurisdiction allows carryforward of excess input tax or requires refund claims, and account accordingly.

### Account For Deferred Or Disregarded Tax On Intercompany

Intercompany transactions within a group may be subject to deferred, disregarded, or group registration rules depending on the jurisdiction. Some regimes disregard VAT on certain intercompany supplies within a group registration; others require it to be accounted for and may allow deferral. Confirm the applicable rule before accounting for intercompany VAT, because assuming standard treatment can create spurious liabilities or miss required accounting.

### Monitor Registration Thresholds

VAT registration is required once taxable supplies exceed the jurisdictional threshold, and voluntary registration may be available below it. Monitor turnover against thresholds, because failing to register on time creates back-tax exposure, and registering unnecessarily creates compliance cost. Distinguish sales threshold from distance selling and cross-border thresholds where relevant.

## Common Traps

### Assuming All Input Tax Is Recoverable

Recovery depends on taxable use, partial exemption, blocked inputs, and valid invoices. Assuming full recovery overstates the recoverable asset and understates cost.

### Missing Reverse Charge On Cross-Border Purchases

Reverse charge on imported services or goods requires reporting both output and input tax. Missing it understates both legs and can create exposure even when net neutral.

### Treating Exempt Supplies As Zero-Rated

Exempt supplies generate no output tax and block related input tax recovery; zero-rated supplies generate output tax at zero percent but allow full input recovery. Confusing them misstates both output and recovery.

### No Partial Exemption Apportionment

A partially exempt business that recovers all input tax overstates recovery. Apply an approved method and review it periodically.

### Wrong Place Of Supply For Cross-Border Services

Charging output tax based on the supplier's location when the place of supply rule points to the customer's location misstates output tax and may trigger reverse charge incorrectly.

### Spurious Intercompany VAT

Accounting for standard VAT on intercompany supplies that are disregarded or deferred under a group regime creates unnecessary liabilities and reconciling differences.

### Ignoring Registration Thresholds

Failing to register when thresholds are exceeded creates back-tax exposure; unnecessary registration creates compliance cost. Monitor turnover against thresholds.

## Self-Check

- Is output tax accounted for on all taxable supplies at the correct rate, with place of supply rules applied for cross-border transactions?
- Are exempt supplies distinguished from zero-rated, with the input tax recovery consequences of each applied?
- Is input tax credit eligibility assessed on each purchase for taxable use, blocked inputs, partial exemption, and valid tax invoice support?
- Is a partial exemption method applied consistently for businesses making mixed supplies, with residual input tax apportioned and reviewed periodically?
- Is reverse charge applied on imports of goods and cross-border services, with both output and input legs reported?
- Is the net VAT position computed as output minus recoverable input, classified as payable liability or recoverable asset per the rules?
- Are intercompany supplies reviewed for deferred, disregarded, or group registration treatment rather than assumed standard?
- Are registration thresholds monitored, with required and voluntary registration decisions documented?
- Does the VAT account reconcile to the returns filed and amounts paid or refunded each period?
- Is professional referral noted for credit eligibility, partial exemption, and reverse charge determinations, and is the explicit limitation on jurisdiction-specific rules acknowledged?
