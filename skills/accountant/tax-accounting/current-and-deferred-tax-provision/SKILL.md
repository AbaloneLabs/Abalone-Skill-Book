# Current And Deferred Tax Provision

The income tax provision is not a single number pulled from a tax return. It splits into a current component, which approximates taxes payable for the period, and a deferred component, which reflects future tax consequences of differences between book and tax treatment of assets and liabilities. Getting this split wrong understates or overstates both the current liability on the balance sheet and the deferred balances that drive future effective tax rate movement. Because deferred taxes are estimates about the future, they are easy to get approximately right in form and materially wrong in substance.

Use this skill before computing a tax provision, recording income tax expense, evaluating deferred tax assets, assessing valuation allowances, or reviewing whether a tax provision entry is defensible. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a provision that balances numerically but misstates the underlying current and deferred economics. Tax accounting under standards such as ASC 740 and IAS 12 is highly jurisdiction-specific and entity-specific. This skill provides structural judgment, not final tax determinations. A qualified tax professional must review and approve any actual provision.

## Core Rules

### Separate Current Tax From Deferred Tax Deliberately

Current tax expense or benefit is the estimated tax payable or refundable for the current period based on taxable income or loss and applicable statutory rates. Deferred tax expense or benefit is the net change in deferred tax assets and liabilities during the period. These two components must be computed independently and then summed for total tax expense.

Do not derive the deferred piece as a plug from a desired total. If current tax is computed first and the deferred is whatever makes the total look reasonable, the deferred balances lose meaning. Compute each on its own basis, then reconcile the total to the effective tax rate.

### Identify All Temporary Differences

Deferred taxes arise from temporary differences between the book carrying amount and the tax basis of assets and liabilities. The agent must systematically enumerate these rather than rely on memory. Common sources include:

- depreciation and amortization timing differences;
- bad debt and warranty reserves accrued for book but not yet deductible for tax;
- deferred revenue recognized differently for book and tax;
- pension and postretirement accruals;
- net operating loss and tax credit carryforwards;
- lease accounting differences under newer lease standards;
- inventory write-downs and lower of cost or market adjustments;
- stock-based compensation with timing differences;
- unrealized gains and losses on investments and derivatives;
- intercompany profit in ending inventory;
- capitalized research and experimental expenditures.

Missing a recurring temporary difference corrupts the deferred balances and the effective tax rate for many periods. Maintain a schedule that rolls forward each difference by category with both book and tax bases.

### Apply The Correct Balance Sheet Liability Method

Most modern frameworks use a balance sheet approach. Deferred tax liabilities are recognized for taxable temporary differences expected to increase future taxes. Deferred tax assets are recognized for deductible temporary differences and carryforwards expected to reduce future taxes, net of any valuation allowance. The agent measures these at the enacted or substantively enacted tax rates expected to apply when the differences reverse.

Check the enacted rate carefully. Use the rate that will apply in the period of reversal, not the current period rate, and update for rate changes prospectively. Substantively enacted is a concept under IAS 12 that is narrower than merely proposed, so do not treat an announced rate change as enacted unless the jurisdictional rule supports it.

### Assess Valuation Allowances On Deferred Tax Assets

A deferred tax asset is only realizable if there will be sufficient future taxable income to absorb the deduction or loss. When it is more likely than not that some or all of the asset will not be realized, a valuation allowance reduces the asset to its expected realizable amount.

Evidence falls into positive and negative categories. Positive evidence includes:

- a history of profitability;
- existing contracts generating future taxable income;
- prudent and feasible tax planning strategies;
- strong forecasted earnings supported by objective data.

Negative evidence includes:

- cumulative losses in recent years;
- a history of operating losses or expired carryforwards;
- unsettled circumstances that undermine forecasts;
- excess capacity or contractual decline.

Cumulative losses are heavy negative evidence. Do not offset them with optimistic forecasts without strong, specific, and objective support. The more likely than not threshold is jurisdictional in its exact wording, so confirm the applicable standard before concluding.

### Distinguish Temporary Differences From Permanent Differences

Permanent differences never reverse and never create deferred taxes. They affect the current provision and the effective tax rate but not the deferred balances. Examples include nondeductible fines, certain meals and entertainment limits, tax-exempt interest income, and certain dividends received deductions.

Misclassifying a permanent difference as temporary creates a spurious deferred balance that will never reverse and will distort the reconciliation. Classify each difference as temporary, permanent, or a carryforward deliberately.

### Reconcile To The Effective Tax Rate

The total provision divided by pretax book income is the effective tax rate. Reconcile it from the statutory rate by explaining each material driver. If the reconciliation does not explain the rate to a reasonable residual, something is missing. The reconciliation is a diagnostic tool, not cosmetic formatting.

### Respect Jurisdiction, Entity, And Consolidation Boundaries

Deferred taxes are computed at the legal entity level in many jurisdictions, then combined for consolidated reporting. Mixing entities, ignoring foreign subsidiaries, or applying a single rate to a multinational group produces materially wrong balances. Identify the reporting entity, each component unit, its jurisdiction, its applicable rates, and its carryforwards separately before aggregating.

## Common Traps

### Plugging Deferred Tax To Hit A Target Total

When the current provision is firm and management wants a specific effective rate, it is tempting to set the deferred provision as the residual. This hides errors in the temporary difference schedule and makes the valuation allowance a tuning knob. Compute deferred taxes from the actual temporary differences and valuation analysis.

### Using The Current Statutory Rate For All Deferred Balances

Deferred balances must use the rate expected at reversal. Applying the current period rate to all categories ignores scheduled rate changes, progressive rate structures, and jurisdictional differences. This trap is common when a rate change is enacted mid-period.

### Ignoring Carryforwards Until They Expire

Net operating losses, capital losses, tax credits, and charitable carryforwards create deferred tax assets but have expiration clocks. Failing to schedule expiration and realizability leads to sudden write-offs and rate surprises. Track carryforwards by year of origin, jurisdiction, and expiration.

### Treating Forecasted Profit As Sufficient Realization Evidence

Forecasts are weaker evidence than past results, especially after a loss year. Relying on a multi-year projection to support full realization of a deferred tax asset, without credible positive evidence or tax planning strategies, overstates the asset. Cumulative recent losses generally require significant objective evidence to overcome.

### Forgetting To Update For Enacted Rate Changes

When a rate change is enacted, existing deferred tax assets and liabilities must be remeasured in the period of enactment, with the effect recognized in that period. Missing this creates a step error that distorts the next provision.

### Mixing Book And Tax Depreciation Assumptions

Depreciation is one of the largest temporary differences. Using the same useful life or method for book and tax, or failing to track tax basis separately, eliminates or distorts the deferred balance. Maintain tax basis schedules independently.

### Overlooking Intercompany And Foreign Complexity

Undistributed foreign earnings, indefinite reinvestment assertions, withholding taxes on potential repatriation, and intercompany transfer pricing adjustments all create deferred tax judgment. Treating the group as a single domestic entity hides these items entirely.

## Self-Check

- Is the current tax provision computed independently from the deferred tax provision rather than used as a plug?
- Does a temporary difference schedule enumerate each category with both book and tax bases and roll forward by period?
- Are deferred tax assets and liabilities measured at the enacted or substantively enacted rate expected to apply at reversal, not merely the current period rate?
- Has a valuation allowance assessment weighed positive and negative evidence, with cumulative losses treated as significant negative evidence?
- Are permanent differences correctly excluded from deferred balances while still reflected in the effective tax rate reconciliation?
- Does the effective tax rate reconciliation explain the gap between statutory and actual rate with identifiable, material drivers and a small residual?
- Are deferred taxes computed at the correct entity and jurisdiction level before consolidation, with carryforwards tracked by origin and expiration?
- Have enacted rate changes been applied prospectively with remeasurement recognized in the period of enactment?
- Has a qualified tax professional reviewed the provision for the relevant jurisdiction and entity type before it is finalized?
