# Tax Basis Balance Sheet And Maintenance

A tax-basis balance sheet tracks the tax basis of every asset, liability, and equity account, distinct from the book basis shown in financial statements. It is essential for preparing tax returns, computing gain or loss on disposition, supporting pass-through basis for partnerships and S-corporations, and identifying temporary differences for deferred tax. Agents often maintain only a book balance sheet and try to derive tax basis at year-end, which produces incomplete and error-prone results. Basis is built up transaction by transaction, and once lost or mis-tracked it is difficult to reconstruct. Pass-through entities add complexity: outside basis, inside basis, capital accounts, and loan basis each move differently and drive separate limitations.

Use this skill before maintaining a tax-basis balance sheet, computing partner or shareholder basis, rolling forward capital accounts, tracking loan basis, or evaluating whether losses or deductions are limited by basis. The goal is to maintain tax basis continuously and accurately so that returns, gain computations, and pass-through allocations are supportable.

This is a tax domain. Basis rules vary by entity type and jurisdiction: partnership outside and inside basis rules, S-corporation stock and loan basis, capital account maintenance conventions, basis restoration ordering, and loss limitation rules all differ under the applicable tax code, and state and local rules may add further adjustments. Do not assume a single basis framework applies. Final basis computations, especially for pass-through loss limitations and basis restoration, should be routed to a qualified tax professional. This skill is operational guidance for maintaining tax-basis balances, not professional tax advice, and it carries an explicit limitation that entity- and jurisdiction-specific basis rules are not exhaustively covered.

## Core Rules

### Track Tax Basis Continuously, Not Just At Year-End

Tax basis is built from each transaction: contributions, purchases, capitalized costs, recoverable expenditures, depreciation, distributions, allocations, and dispositions. Maintaining basis only by reconstructing it at year-end from the book balance sheet invites errors, because book and tax capitalization, recovery, and classification differ. Record tax basis effects at the transaction level, and reconcile the tax-basis balance sheet to the return each period.

A continuously maintained tax-basis balance sheet supports both the return and the deferred tax schedule, because the temporary differences are simply book basis minus tax basis line by line.

### Track Basis By Individual Asset And Liability

Aggregate basis is not enough. Gain or loss on disposition, depreciation recapture, and temporary difference reversal all require the basis of the specific asset. Maintain basis by asset (or asset class where supported), recording original cost, capitalized additions, tax depreciation or amortization, improvements, and dispositions. For liabilities, track the tax basis separately where it differs from book, such as deferred revenue or certain accrued liabilities.

An asset-level schedule supports both the tax return and the book-tax reconciliation, and it is the only way to compute gain on a partial disposition accurately.

### Compute Partnership Outside And Inside Basis Separately

For partnerships, outside basis (the partner's basis in the partnership interest) and inside basis (the partnership's basis in its assets) are distinct concepts that move differently. Outside basis changes with contributions, distributions, the partner's share of income and loss, and liabilities; inside basis changes with the partnership's transactions in its assets. Outside basis drives the partner's gain on sale and loss deduction limits; inside basis drives the partnership's depreciation and gain computation.

Track both. A partner cannot deduct losses in excess of outside basis, and basis differences between inside and outside can create complex results on contribution, distribution, and sale of an interest.

### Maintain Capital Accounts And Tax-Basis Equity Rollforward

Capital accounts track each owner's equity for tax purposes and roll forward with contributions, distributions, allocations of income and loss, and other specified items. The tax-basis equity rollforward reconciles beginning to ending equity through these movements. For partnerships, capital accounts must often be maintained per the applicable convention (such as the IRS-required capital account presentation), and negative capital accounts can signal basis issues.

Reconcile the capital account rollforward to the tax-basis balance sheet equity and to the partners' outside basis, because inconsistencies among these indicate errors.

### Track Loan Basis For S-Corporations And Partnerships

In pass-through entities, loan basis (basis in debt owed by the entity to the owner) is separate from equity basis and supports additional loss deductions. S-corporation shareholders track stock basis and loan basis separately, with losses first reducing stock basis and then loan basis, and restoration governed by specific ordering rules. Partners may have basis in partnership liabilities that affects outside basis.

Track loan basis by debt instrument, and apply the ordering rules for basis reduction and restoration. Mis-sequencing basis reduction can overstate deductible losses or basis available for distributions.

### Apply Basis Limitations To Losses And Deductions

Losses and deductions from a pass-through are limited by the owner's basis (and, where applicable, at-risk and passive activity rules). A loss cannot reduce basis below zero in that basis category; excess loss is suspended and carried forward until basis is restored. Confirm basis before allowing a full loss deduction, and track suspended losses by category and year for future use.

Basis limitations are entity- and jurisdiction-specific; apply the rules for the entity type and confirm any additional limitations (at-risk, passive) that may further restrict the deduction.

### Reconcile And Document Basis Changes

Each basis change should be documented: the transaction, the amount, the basis category affected, and the resulting basis. Reconcile the tax-basis balance sheet, capital accounts, and outside or loan basis to the return and to each other. A basis schedule that cannot be reconciled cannot support gain computations, loss deductions, or distribution treatment.

## Common Traps

### Reconstructing Basis Only At Year-End From Book

Deriving tax basis from the book balance sheet at year-end misses differences in capitalization, recovery, and classification. Maintain basis at the transaction level.

### Aggregating Basis Instead Of Tracking By Asset

Aggregate basis cannot support gain on partial disposition, recapture, or asset-level temporary differences. Track basis by asset.

### Confusing Outside And Inside Basis In Partnerships

Outside basis belongs to the partner; inside basis belongs to the partnership. They move differently and drive different outcomes. Track both.

### Mis-Sequencing Basis Reduction And Restoration

Reducing loan basis before stock basis, or restoring basis in the wrong order, overstates deductible losses or distribution capacity. Apply the entity's ordering rules.

### Allowing Losses In Excess Of Basis

Deducting a pass-through loss without confirming basis overstates the deduction and creates exposure. Apply basis and related limitations before allowing the loss.

### Unreconciled Capital Accounts And Outside Basis

Capital accounts, tax-basis equity, and outside basis that do not reconcile indicate errors in contributions, distributions, or allocations. Reconcile them.

### Ignoring Negative Capital Accounts

A negative capital account can signal that distributions exceeded basis or that losses were deducted without basis. Investigate rather than carry forward.

## Self-Check

- Is tax basis tracked continuously at the transaction level rather than reconstructed from book at year-end?
- Is basis maintained by individual asset (or supported asset class) to support gain, recapture, and temporary difference reversal?
- For partnerships, are outside basis and inside basis tracked separately, with each reconciled to its driver?
- Are capital accounts rolled forward with contributions, distributions, allocations, and other items, and reconciled to tax-basis equity?
- Is loan basis tracked separately from equity basis for S-corporations and partnerships, with reduction and restoration sequenced per the entity's rules?
- Are pass-through losses and deductions tested against basis (and at-risk and passive rules where applicable) before being allowed, with suspended losses tracked by category and year?
- Do the tax-basis balance sheet, capital accounts, and outside or loan basis reconcile to each other and to the return?
- Is each basis change documented by transaction, amount, basis category, and resulting basis?
- Are negative capital accounts investigated rather than carried forward without review?
- Is professional referral noted for basis, loss limitation, and restoration computations, and is the explicit limitation on entity- and jurisdiction-specific rules acknowledged?
