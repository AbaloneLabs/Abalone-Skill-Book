# Defined Benefit Plan Accounting

Defined benefit (DB) plan accounting is one of the most judgment-heavy areas in financial reporting. The employer promises a future benefit tied to a formula (commonly final pay, career-average pay, or a flat amount per year of service), and the accounting must reflect today the cost of a promise that will settle decades later. Because the obligation depends on future salaries, future discount rates, mortality, turnover, and investment returns, small changes in assumptions move the balance sheet and income statement by large amounts.

The risk is not arithmetic error. The risk is that an agent applies a clean-looking calculation while missing the judgment, governance, and disclosure obligations that surround it. Benefit plan accounting standards (such as US ASC 715 and international IAS 19) differ materially between jurisdictions, and public-sector and insurance frameworks add further variants. This skill provides operational judgment, not a final accounting determination. Final assumptions, measurement, and disclosures must be confirmed with a qualified actuary and a professional accountant licensed in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Separate The Components Of The Obligation And The Expense

A DB plan is not a single number. Track the obligation and the expense by component so each can be reviewed and disclosed correctly.

Obligation components to distinguish:

- service cost (the increase in obligation from employee service in the period);
- interest cost (unwinding of the discount on the opening obligation);
- actuarial gains and losses (changes from assumption updates and experience differences);
- benefits paid;
- past service cost from plan amendments, curtailments, or settlements.

Expense components to distinguish:

- service cost (generally in operating expense);
- interest cost;
- expected or actual return on plan assets;
- net interest on the net defined benefit liability or asset;
- remeasurements (actuarial gains and losses, and differences between expected and actual asset returns).

Do not collapse these into one line. Where they are presented matters: under some frameworks service cost is an operating item while interest and return are non-operating or financing in nature.

### Confirm An Actuarial Valuation Exists And Is Current

DB obligations require actuarial science. The agent should not invent a discount rate, mortality table, or salary escalation rate. Confirm that a qualified actuary has produced a valuation appropriate for the reporting date, and that the valuation basis matches the accounting framework the entity reports under.

Check:

- valuation date relative to the reporting date (and whether a roll-forward or update is required);
- the actuary's scope and qualifications;
- whether the plan text, trust document, and amendments are reflected;
- whether the census data (headcount, compensation, ages, service) was reconciled to payroll and HR records;
- whether the assumption set was reviewed and approved by those charged with governance.

If no current actuarial valuation exists, the obligation cannot be reliably stated. Flag this rather than estimating.

### Scrutinize Key Actuarial Assumptions

Assumptions are where most judgment and most manipulation live. Each material assumption should be reasonable, internally consistent, and supportable.

Financial assumptions typically include:

- discount rate (set with reference to high-quality corporate or government bond yields matching the duration of the obligation);
- expected long-term return on plan assets (reflecting the actual asset allocation and realistic, not aspirational, returns);
- salary or pay escalation;
- inflation.

Demographic assumptions typically include:

- mortality (current generational tables with improvement);
- turnover or withdrawal;
- retirement age and early retirement behavior;
- disability;
- optional forms of benefit election.

Ask whether assumptions are best-estimate, unbiased, and mutually compatible. A high discount rate combined with a high salary escalation rate and an optimistic asset return is a red flag. Document who set each assumption and the basis for it.

### Handle Plan Assets At The Right Basis And Date

Plan assets are usually measured at fair value at the reporting date. Confirm:

- the asset valuation is as of the same date as the obligation;
- fair value reflects an exit price, not cost or manager NAV shortcuts unless justified;
- the asset list is reconciled to the trustee or custodian statement;
- related-party transactions, employer-contributed assets, and insurance policies that exactly match the obligation are handled under the specific rules of the framework;
- unfunded, insured, and self-administered arrangements are distinguished.

The net defined benefit asset or liability is the obligation less the fair value of plan assets, adjusted for any asset ceiling required by the applicable standard.

### Apply The Correct Treatment Of Remeasurements

Remeasurements are the volatile part. Different frameworks route them differently. Under IAS 19, remeasurements go through other comprehensive income and are not recycled through profit or loss. Under US GAAP, actuarial gains and losses may sit in accumulated other comprehensive income and be amortized into income under corridor or recognition rules.

Before recording, confirm:

- which framework governs;
- whether there is a net asset or net liability position and any resulting asset ceiling or minimum liability adjustment;
- whether past service cost is recognized immediately or deferred depending on vesting and framework;
- whether a settlement or curtailment has occurred and the gain or loss recognition triggers.

Do not assume symmetry across frameworks. The same plan can produce very different income statement effects under IAS 19 versus US GAAP.

### Respect Multiemployer, State, And Hybrid Plan Differences

Not every plan with "pension" in the name is a single-employer DB plan. Multiemployer plans, agent multiemployer plans, government and public-sector plans, and hybrid plans (cash balance, pension equity) each carry distinct accounting.

Ask:

- is this a single-employer plan, a multiemployer plan, or a government plan?
- for multiemployer plans, is the contribution rate the required contribution, and is the plan in endangered or critical status requiring disclosure?
- for cash balance or hybrid plans, is the interest credit and the resulting obligation correctly reflected?
- does the plan provide postretirement benefits other than pensions that belong in a separate OPEB accounting?

Misclassifying the plan type produces structurally wrong accounting, not a rounding error.

### Maintain Disclosure And Governance Discipline

DB accounting carries heavy disclosure obligations and governance expectations. Confirm the entity has:

- a governance process for assumption setting and plan changes;
- sensitivity disclosures for the discount rate and other key assumptions;
- reconciliation of opening to closing obligation and plan assets;
- the composition of the expense;
- the maturity profile and concentration of the plan;
- related-party and employer contribution disclosures;
- any regulatory minimum funding or solvency requirements and their effect.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Actuarial Report As A Black Box

The actuary produces numbers, but accounting responsibility stays with the entity. Accepting a valuation without understanding the assumptions, census data, and plan terms means the entity cannot defend the balance or explain the volatility. Review the actuarial report critically, not reverently.

### Using An Inconsistent Discount Rate Or Stale Yield Curve

A discount rate pulled from last year, from the wrong currency, or from bonds that do not match the obligation duration materially distorts the liability. The rate must be re-derived each measurement date for the plan's currency and duration, and changes must be explained.

### Mixing Plan Asset Returns With Net Interest

Confusing expected return, actual return, and net interest on the net asset or liability double counts or omits amounts. Each framework defines these precisely; mixing them creates an expense that looks reasonable but is internally inconsistent.

### Ignoring Past Service Cost And Amendments

Plan amendments, curtailments, and settlements are infrequent but large. Treating an amendment as an ordinary remeasurement, or failing to separate the past service cost from ongoing service cost, misstates both the period of expense and the obligation.

### Overlooking Asset Ceilings And Minimum Liabilities

When a plan is overfunded, an asset ceiling may cap the recognized asset. When underfunded under certain frameworks, a minimum liability or additional contribution may apply. Ignoring these produces a balance sheet that looks tidy but violates the standard.

### Assuming One Jurisdiction's Rules Apply Everywhere

ASC 715, IAS 19, public-sector standards, and local insurance or pension regulation differ in recognition, presentation, and disclosure. Applying one framework's shortcut to an entity reporting under another produces non-compliant accounts. Confirm the governing framework before recording anything.

## Self-Check

- Are service cost, interest cost, return on assets, net interest, and remeasurements each identified and routed to the correct presentation line for the governing framework?
- Is there a current actuarial valuation from a qualified actuary as of the appropriate date, with census data reconciled to HR and payroll?
- Are the discount rate, salary escalation, mortality, and expected return assumptions unbiased, mutually consistent, and documented with their basis?
- Are plan assets measured at fair value at the reporting date and reconciled to the trustee or custodian statement?
- Is the plan correctly classified as single-employer, multiemployer, public-sector, or hybrid, and is the accounting matched to that type?
- Have past service costs, curtailments, settlements, asset ceilings, and minimum funding requirements been considered and treated correctly?
- Do the disclosures include sensitivity analysis, reconciliations, expense composition, and maturity information as required by the applicable standard?
- Has the final measurement, assumption set, and disclosure been confirmed with a qualified actuary and professional accountant for the relevant jurisdiction?
