# Other Postretirement Benefits

Other postretirement benefits (OPEB) cover promises an employer makes to provide benefits other than pensions after employees retire, most commonly retiree medical, prescription drug, dental, vision, and life insurance coverage. OPEB accounting shares the actuarial structure of defined benefit pension accounting, but the underlying economics are different and often more volatile. Medical trend assumptions, per-capita cost, and the interaction with government programs can swing the obligation dramatically.

Because OPEB obligations are frequently unfunded and very sensitive to healthcare trend, they can become one of the largest unrecognized risks on a balance sheet. The trap is treating OPEB like a smaller version of a pension. This skill provides operational judgment for measuring, recognizing, and disclosing OPEB. Benefit accounting standards (such as US ASC 715 subtopic on OPEB and international IAS 19) differ by jurisdiction, and healthcare regulation and tax treatment add further variation. Final assumptions, measurement, and disclosures must be confirmed with a qualified actuary and professional accountant licensed in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Substantive Plan Before Calculating

OPEB obligations are driven by the substantive plan, meaning the understanding between the employer and employees as evidenced by past practice and communications, not only the written document. This is especially important because retiree medical promises are often informal, historical, or documented only in employee handbooks.

Confirm:

- the actual benefit promise (what coverage, for whom, for how long, at what subsidy level);
- who is eligible (retirees only, spouses, dependents, surviving spouses);
- the cost-sharing arrangement (employer-paid, shared premium, fixed-dollar subsidy, or percentage of premium);
- minimum age and service requirements;
- whether the promise is capped, inflation-protected, or explicitly disclaimed;
- whether the substantive plan differs from any written plan document.

If the substantive plan is unclear, the obligation cannot be reliably measured. Resolve the ambiguity with HR, legal, and the actuary before recording.

### Build A Per-Capita Cost And Trend Assumption Set

OPEB is usually measured by projecting per-capita healthcare costs forward and applying a trend rate that decays toward a long-term level. These assumptions dominate the result.

Typical assumption inputs:

- current per-capita cost by age and coverage type;
- healthcare trend rate for near-term years, declining to an ultimate trend rate;
- the age at which the trend reaches its ultimate level;
- mortality, turnover, retirement age, and disability;
- the percentage of employees expected to elect coverage at retirement (the take-up or eligibility rate);
- dependent coverage assumptions.

Ask whether the trend assumption is unbiased and supportable, and whether it reflects the specific plan's cost-sharing. A trend rate that is too flat or too optimistic understates the obligation. Document the basis and the governance approval for each assumption.

### Handle The Subsidy And Government Program Interaction

Retiree medical obligations interact with government healthcare programs in ways that materially change the measurement. Under some regimes, an employer subsidy or a government program offset reduces the net cost the employer bears.

Consider:

- whether the employer benefit coordinates with government retiree healthcare, and how the offset is reflected;
- whether a prescription drug subsidy or similar program reduces the obligation, and whether that treatment is still permitted under current rules;
- whether the plan is a fixed-dollar subsidy (where the employer caps its cost) versus a percentage-of-premium or pay-related design;
- whether cost caps shift future inflation risk to retirees, which can dramatically reduce the obligation.

A plan with a hard employer cost cap behaves very differently from an open-ended percentage subsidy. Modeling them identically is a serious error.

### Recognize The Components Consistently With The Framework

Like other DB-type accounting, OPEB splits into service cost, interest cost, return on plan assets, and remeasurements. Confirm the governing framework's routing.

Check:

- service cost recognized in operating expense as employees render service;
- interest cost on the opening obligation;
- expected and actual return on any plan assets;
- net interest on the net OPEB liability or asset;
- remeasurements routed to other comprehensive income or amortized according to the framework;
- any transition obligation or asset from initial adoption and its required recognition pattern;
- past service cost or plan amendments, including whether prior-period credits are recognized immediately or deferred.

Do not assume OPEB uses identical mechanics to pensions in every respect; the framework and the plan design create differences.

### Treat Plan Assets And Funding Carefully

Most OPEB plans are unfunded or partially funded through a trust or a Voluntary Employees' Beneficiary Association-type arrangement. Funding status affects recognition and disclosure.

Confirm:

- whether any assets are held in a qualifying trust that must be included in plan assets;
- whether assets are measured at fair value at the reporting date;
- whether insurance policies or employer assets purport to fund the obligation and whether they qualify for offset under the framework;
- whether contributions intended to prefund benefits are reflected correctly;
- any asset ceiling on a net asset position.

Assets that are not held by a qualifying funding vehicle generally cannot be netted against the obligation.

### Manage Disclosure And Sensitivity

OPEB disclosure obligations are substantial because the obligation is assumption-sensitive and often unfunded. Confirm disclosure of:

- the nature of the plan and the benefit promise;
- the composition of the expense;
- reconciliation of opening to closing obligation and plan assets;
- the principal actuarial assumptions, including healthcare trend;
- sensitivity of the obligation to a change in the trend rate and the discount rate;
- the expected future contributions and benefit payments;
- any regulatory or contractual funding requirements.

## Common Traps

### Treating OPEB As A Discounted Pension

OPEB is not a small pension. Healthcare trend, per-capita cost growth, take-up rates, and government program interaction make it behave very differently. Applying pension defaults without rebuilding the assumption set produces a misleading obligation.

### Ignoring The Substantive Plan And Informal Promises

Retiree medical promises are often made informally over decades. If the agent relies only on a written plan that does not match actual practice, the obligation is understated or overstated. The substantive plan governs.

### Underestimating The Effect Of Healthcare Trend

A one percentage point change in the near-term trend rate can move the obligation by a large percentage. Using a flat or stale trend assumption, or failing to disclose sensitivity, hides the dominant risk in OPEB accounting.

### Missing Cost Caps And Fixed Subsidy Designs

A plan where the employer caps its share of cost shifts future inflation to retirees and can be far less costly than an open-ended percentage subsidy. Modeling all plans as percentage subsidies overstates the obligation and distorts expense.

### Double Counting Government Subsidies Or Excluding Required Offsets

Some frameworks require recognition of a government program subsidy or prescription drug offset; others limit or eliminate it. Applying the wrong treatment double counts a benefit or omits a required reduction.

### Netting Non-Qualifying Assets Against The Obligation

Reserves, general corporate assets, or insurance policies that do not meet the funding vehicle requirements generally cannot offset the OPEB liability. Netting them produces a balance sheet that violates the standard.

## Self-Check

- Has the substantive plan, including informal promises and cost-sharing, been established and documented with HR and legal before measurement?
- Are per-capita cost, healthcare trend (declining to an ultimate rate), take-up, and demographic assumptions unbiased, supportable, and governance-approved?
- Is the plan design correctly modeled, distinguishing fixed-dollar subsidies, percentage-of-premium, and hard cost caps?
- Are government program offsets and any prescription drug subsidy reflected in accordance with the current applicable framework?
- Are service cost, interest, return on plan assets, net interest, and remeasurements routed to the correct presentation lines for the governing standard?
- Are only qualifying trust or funding vehicle assets netted against the obligation, measured at fair value at the reporting date?
- Do disclosures include the expense composition, reconciliations, principal assumptions, and sensitivity to trend and discount rate changes?
- Has the final measurement, assumption set, and disclosure been confirmed with a qualified actuary and professional accountant for the relevant jurisdiction?
