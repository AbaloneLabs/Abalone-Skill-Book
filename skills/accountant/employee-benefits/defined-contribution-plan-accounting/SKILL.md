# Defined Contribution Plan Accounting

A defined contribution (DC) plan shifts investment and longevity risk from the employer to the employee. The employer's obligation is to make the promised contributions; the employee bears the outcome of the investments. Because the accounting seems simple, it is often done carelessly. The danger is not the arithmetic of a percentage of pay; the danger is misstating the period in which the contribution is earned and owed, mishandling matching and vesting mechanics, and confusing a genuine DC plan with arrangements that secretly carry more obligation.

This skill covers operational judgment for recording and disclosing DC plan costs. Contribution rules, vesting law, tax qualification, and reporting standards (such as US ASC 715 and international IAS 19) vary by jurisdiction and entity type. Confirm final determinations, plan qualification, and tax treatment with a qualified benefits professional and accountant licensed in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Expense When The Service Is Rendered, Not When Paid

The defining rule of DC accounting is that the employer recognizes a liability and an expense as the related employee service is rendered. This is not optional and it is not the same as the cash contribution date.

Establish cutoff discipline:

- contributions owed for service performed in the period are accrued even if remittance happens later;
- the accrual should reflect the plan formula (for example a percentage of eligible compensation) applied to eligible employees;
- contributions remitted early or late are timing differences, not a change in the period of expense;
- true-ups for year-end compensation adjustments (bonuses, retro pay, corrections) must be accrued in the period the related pay is earned.

Ask whether the recorded DC expense equals the plan formula applied to eligible compensation for the period, adjusted for true-ups, regardless of the cash funding date.

### Apply The Plan Formula Precisely And Identify Eligibility

A DC plan is only as clean as the formula application. Before recording, confirm the inputs.

Confirm:

- the contribution rate or amount per the governing plan document, not a memo or a manager's recollection;
- which compensation base is eligible (base pay only, base plus bonus, total cash, or a capped amount);
- which employees are eligible (service thresholds, age, classification, union status);
- whether there are safe harbor, nonelective, matching, or profit-sharing components;
- annual contribution limits, catch-up provisions, and compensation caps under the applicable tax rules.

Eligibility errors are the most common source of misstatement. A change in headcount, a reclassification of workers, or a new bonus structure can silently change who and what is eligible.

### Handle Matching Contributions And Vesting Correctly

Matching and vesting mechanics create traps that a flat-percentage calculation misses.

For matching contributions:

- distinguish true matching (tied to employee deferrals) from nonelective employer contributions;
- apply the match formula including caps, tiers, and any true-up at year end;
- account for the timing of when a match is earned versus when it is deposited;
- handle employees who change deferral elections mid-year.

For vesting:

- vesting affects employee accounts and forfeitures, not the period in which the employer recognizes expense under most accounting frameworks;
- track unvested employer contributions and the forfeiture schedule;
- recognize forfeitures to reduce future employer contributions or expense according to the plan and framework;
- do not reverse previously recognized expense merely because an employee later forfeits.

### Reconcile Employee Deferrals And Remittances

Employee elective deferrals are withheld from pay and held in trust until remitted. The employer is a fiduciary for those amounts. This creates accounting and compliance duties beyond expense recognition.

Reconcile:

- deferrals withheld per payroll versus deferrals remitted to the plan;
- timing of remittance against the regulatory deadline (late remittance can be a prohibited transaction under some regimes);
- loan repayments withheld from pay and forwarded to the plan;
- Roth versus pretax deferrals tracked separately;
- employer matching and nonelective contributions separately from employee money.

A reconciliation break between withheld and remitted amounts is a fiduciary and legal problem, not just a reconciliation nuisance.

### Distinguish Multiemployer Plan Contributions

Contributions to a multiemployer plan are accounted for as a DC cost when the contribution obligation is incurred, but the disclosure obligations differ. Confirm:

- the contractual contribution rate and whether it is a fixed amount per hour, a percentage, or a flat dollar amount;
- whether the plan is in endangered, critical, or critical and declining status triggering additional disclosure;
- any withdrawal liability exposure, which may create a separate contingent liability consideration distinct from routine contribution accounting;
- that the entity is not accidentally treating a multiemployer plan as if it were a single-employer DB plan.

### Maintain Documentation And Disclosure

Even simple DC accounting needs evidence and disclosure. Keep:

- the plan document and any amendments;
- the contribution calculation supporting each period;
- reconciliation of accrued contributions to remittances;
- evidence of timely deposit;
- disclosure of the nature of the plan, the basis of contribution, and the amount recognized as expense;
- for multiemployer plans, the required statutory disclosures.

## Common Traps

### Recording Expense On The Cash Funding Date

Many employers record DC expense when the contribution is wired to the provider. This misstates periods whenever the funding date crosses a month-end or year-end. The obligation accrues as service is rendered; funding is a settlement of the accrual.

### Applying The Match Formula Too Simply

Matching formulas frequently include caps, tiered rates, year-end true-ups, and interaction with employee deferral changes. Applying a single flat match to total compensation understates or overstates the true obligation and can create plan qualification problems.

### Ignoring Late Remittance As A Fiduciary Breach

Treating a delay in forwarding employee deferrals as a minor timing item misses the legal exposure. Under several regimes, late remittance of employee money is a prohibited transaction requiring correction, excise taxes, and disclosure. Escalate breaks between withholding and remittance immediately.

### Confusing Vesting With Expense Recognition

Some assume unvested employer contributions should not be expensed until vested. Under most accounting frameworks the expense is recognized when the contribution is earned; vesting governs forfeitures and account balances, not the expense period.

### Using A Stale Plan Document

Plan terms change through amendments, and compensation structures change through HR decisions. A contribution rate memorized from an old plan document or an old compensation definition produces a quietly wrong accrual. Reconfirm the formula and eligible compensation each cycle.

### Treating All Pension-Like Arrangements As DC

Some arrangements labeled as DC, including certain cash balance plans, target benefit plans, or unfunded promise plans, carry investment or longevity risk that makes them DB or hybrid. Mislabeling them as DC removes the obligation and disclosure that actually apply.

## Self-Check

- Is DC expense recognized in the period employees render service, with contributions funded after period-end still accrued?
- Does the recorded contribution equal the plan formula applied to the correct eligible compensation and eligible employee population for the period?
- Are matching, nonelective, and profit-sharing components separated and calculated with their caps, tiers, and true-ups?
- Are employee deferrals, loan repayments, and employer contributions reconciled between payroll withholding and plan remittance, with any late remittance flagged?
- Is vesting tracked for forfeitures without improperly deferring or reversing recognized expense?
- Are multiemployer contributions accounted for as periodic cost with the required status and contribution disclosures?
- Is the plan genuinely a defined contribution plan and not a disguised DB, hybrid, or unfunded promise arrangement?
- Has the plan document, contribution calculation, qualification, and tax treatment been confirmed with a qualified benefits professional and accountant for the relevant jurisdiction?
