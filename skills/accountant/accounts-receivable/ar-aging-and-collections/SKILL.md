# AR Aging And Collections

A receivable is a promise of future cash, and its value depends on whether that promise will be kept. Aging and collections are the disciplines that turn recorded receivables into actual cash and that identify which balances have stopped being assets and become losses. Weak collections activity looks busy because reminders are sent, but it fails when aging is reviewed without action, when credit limits are set once and never revisited, when disputes linger without resolution, or when obviously uncollectible balances remain on the books inflating assets. The allowance for doubtful accounts is where optimism most often corrupts the balance sheet.

Use this skill before reviewing an aging report, designing a collections workflow, setting or revising customer credit limits, calculating the bad-debt allowance, writing off a customer balance, or reviewing whether the receivables balance is fairly stated. The goal is to prevent the agent from certifying receivables that are overstated, unsupported, or unlikely to be collected.

Credit regulation, interest on overdue balances, and write-off deductibility differ by jurisdiction. Where a write-off, interest charge, or derecognition is involved, confirm the treatment with a qualified professional.

## Core Rules

### Read The Aging As A Risk Map

An aging report is not just a list; it is a map of where cash is stuck and where loss is forming. Review it by bucket and by customer, not just as a total.

For each bucket, assess:

- current: are invoices issuing correctly and terms being met?
- 0 to 30 days past due: normal timing or early warning?
- 31 to 60 days: is follow-up underway and disputes identified?
- 61 to 90 days: is escalation and credit hold appropriate?
- over 90 days: are these genuine disputes, slow payers, or emerging losses?

Concentration matters as much as age. A single large customer in the over-90 bucket can outweigh dozens of small current balances. Review the aging by customer and by amount, and flag concentrations for credit review.

### Follow A Documented Collections Cadence

Collections works when it is consistent and escalating, not when it is reactive. Define a cadence that intensifies as a balance ages.

A typical cadence:

- before due date: confirm invoice received and correct;
- shortly past due: friendly reminder and statement;
- 30 to 60 days: direct contact, identify disputes, agree a payment plan if needed;
- 60 to 90 days: escalation to a senior contact, credit hold on new orders;
- over 90 days: final demand, consider interest or fees per terms, refer to collections or legal.

Document every contact, every promise to pay, and every dispute raised. A promise to pay that is not recorded cannot be followed up, and a dispute that is not logged cannot be resolved.

### Resolve Disputes Before They Age

Many old receivables are not credit problems; they are unresolved disputes. An invoice contested for a pricing error, a short shipment, or a service issue will never be paid until the dispute is settled, and it will distort the aging and the allowance the entire time.

For each disputed balance:

- identify the root cause and the responsible party internally;
- decide whether the dispute is valid, partially valid, or rejected;
- issue a credit note, a corrected invoice, or a formal rejection as appropriate;
- track the dispute separately so it does not inflate routine aging.

Resolving disputes promptly also protects the customer relationship and prevents valid balances from being lumped with disputed ones in collection effort.

### Set And Revise Credit Limits Based On Evidence

Credit limits control exposure before a problem begins. A limit set once at onboarding and never revisited does not reflect changes in a customer's payment behavior, order size, or financial health.

Set and revise limits based on:

- payment history and current aging;
- credit references and reports where available;
- order volume and trend;
- concentration risk for large customers;
- the customer's own disclosed financials where appropriate.

Place customers on credit hold proactively when aging deteriorates, not only after a loss. A credit hold is a control, not a punishment; it prevents new exposure while an existing problem is resolved.

### Estimate The Allowance With A Defensible Method

The allowance for doubtful accounts is a judgmental estimate, and it is where optimism most often overstates assets. The estimate should be based on a consistent, documented method, not a round-number guess.

Common methods:

- aging approach: apply historical loss rates to each aging bucket, adjusted for current conditions;
- specific identification: individually assess large, old, or troubled balances;
- historical loss rate: apply an overall expected loss rate to the receivables base.

Whichever method applies under the relevant framework, document the inputs, the assumptions, and the adjustments for current conditions. Review the allowance every period, not only when it looks too low. A reserve that never moves while aging worsens is not a sign of good collections; it is a sign of a stale estimate.

### Write Off Uncollectible Balances With Authority

A balance that is clearly uncollectible should be written off, not left on the books indefinitely. Carrying a known loss inflates assets and distorts the allowance. But write-offs carry authority and tax consequences and should not be done casually.

Before writing off:

- confirm collection effort is exhausted or the balance is demonstrably uncollectible;
- obtain approval per the write-off authority threshold;
- record the write-off against the allowance or directly to bad-debt expense per policy;
- retain evidence supporting the determination;
- consider whether to continue pursuing the balance through collections or legal action even after write-off.

A write-off does not always mean forgiveness; in many cases the business retains the right to collect and can reinstate the receivable if recovery becomes likely.

### Monitor Concentration And Customer Health

A receivables portfolio concentrated in a few customers carries hidden risk. If one large customer fails, the loss can dwarf many small ones. Review concentration periodically and watch for warning signs in key customers: stretching payments, partial payments, returned payments, or changes in ordering pattern.

For material customers, consider obtaining updated credit information, monitoring public signals, and tightening terms proactively when risk increases.

## Common Traps

### Reviewing Aging Without Acting

An aging report that is read but does not trigger contact, holds, or write-offs is decorative. The value of aging is in the action it drives. Tie every review to a set of collection tasks.

### Leaving Disputed Balances In Routine Aging

A disputed invoice ages alongside genuine receivables, inflating the apparent risk and the allowance, and consuming collection effort that cannot resolve it. Separate disputes and resolve them through billing, not through reminders.

### Setting Credit Limits Once And Forgetting Them

A credit limit reflects a point in time. Customers change, orders grow, and payment behavior shifts. Revisit limits periodically and on adverse signals, and use credit holds proactively.

### Understating The Allowance To Protect Income

A low allowance makes earnings look better and assets look stronger, but it misstates the financial position. The allowance should follow the evidence, not the desired result. Document the method and review it every period.

### Carrying Known Losses To Avoid Recognizing Them

Leaving an obviously uncollectible balance on the books delays the loss but does not eliminate it. It also distorts ratios and invites audit findings. Write off when the evidence supports it.

### Treating A Promise To Pay As Collection

A customer promise to pay by a date is not cash. Track promises, follow up on the promised date, and escalate when a promise is broken. Unrecorded promises are forgotten, and broken promises are a strong loss signal.

### Ignoring Concentration Risk

A portfolio that looks healthy in aggregate can be dangerously concentrated. A single large customer's deterioration can dominate the loss experience. Review concentration, not just average aging.

## Self-Check

- Is the aging reviewed by bucket, by customer, and by concentration, with action tied to each review?
- Is there a documented, escalating collections cadence with every contact, promise, and dispute recorded?
- Are disputed balances separated from routine aging and resolved through billing rather than reminders?
- Are credit limits set on evidence and revised periodically and on adverse signals, with proactive credit holds?
- Is the allowance for doubtful accounts estimated with a documented, consistent method and reviewed every period?
- Are clearly uncollectible balances written off with proper authority, evidence, and accounting treatment?
- Is concentration risk in large customers monitored, with warning signs triggering tighter terms?
- Are broken promises to pay treated as strong loss signals and escalated?
- Are write-offs and allowance changes routed to a qualified professional where tax or disclosure is involved?
- Does the allowance move with aging and conditions rather than staying flat while risk worsens?
