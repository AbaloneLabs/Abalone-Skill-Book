# Customer Billing And Invoicing

Customer billing is where earned revenue becomes a documented claim for payment. Each invoice is simultaneously a revenue record, a tax event, a contractual demand, and the seed of a receivable that must later be collected. Weak billing looks productive because invoices go out, but it fails when the billed amount does not match what was sold, when tax is computed on the wrong base or at the wrong rate, when invoices are issued in the wrong period, when credit notes are issued without control, or when customers receive invoices they cannot understand or verify. Billing errors are expensive because they create both revenue distortion and collection friction.

Use this skill before setting up a billing process, issuing invoices, configuring recurring or milestone billing, handling credit notes and adjustments, applying tax, preparing billing for audit, or reviewing whether the invoicing function produces reliable, collectible, and compliant output. The goal is to prevent the agent from issuing invoices that misstate revenue, mishandle tax, or undermine collection.

Sales tax, VAT, GST, place-of-supply, and digital-services rules vary widely by jurisdiction and by the nature of the customer and transaction. Where tax treatment is uncertain, route it to a qualified tax professional rather than guessing.

## Core Rules

### Bill From Evidence Of What Was Delivered

An invoice should reflect what was actually sold, delivered, or earned, not a convenient round number or a contract value assumed in advance. The billing source depends on the business model: time and materials records, milestone sign-offs, subscription periods, shipment confirmations, usage meters, or contract schedules.

Before issuing, confirm:

- the customer, billing entity, and remittance terms;
- the description, quantity, and unit price of what was delivered;
- the delivery or service period the billing covers;
- the contract or order authorizing the charge;
- any approved change orders, discounts, or rebates;
- whether the amount is final or an estimate subject to true-up.

Billing ahead of delivery, billing an estimate as final, or billing a contract value without confirming performance all create receivables that may not be collectible and revenue that may need to be reversed.

### Determine The Correct Billing Period And Revenue Trigger

The date an invoice is issued is not always the date revenue is earned, and the date revenue is earned is not always the date cash is received. Billing, revenue recognition, and cash collection are three different events that must be kept distinct.

Ask:

- Does the invoice recognize revenue immediately, or does it create a deferred-revenue liability for performance still owed?
- Does the billing cover a future subscription period that requires deferral?
- Is this a milestone or percentage-of-completion bill where revenue recognition follows a different rule than billing?
- Should the invoice be dated in the period of delivery, even if issued slightly later?

Misaligning billing and revenue recognition is a common cause of period distortion and restatement risk. When the two diverge, document both treatments.

### Apply Tax Correctly On Every Invoice

Tax on a sales invoice is not a flat percentage to be applied mechanically. It depends on the product or service, the customer's location and tax status, the place of supply, exemptions, reverse-charge rules, and registration thresholds.

For each invoice, determine:

- which tax type applies (sales tax, VAT, GST, excise, digital services tax);
- the correct rate for the customer's jurisdiction;
- whether the customer is a business or consumer, and whether that changes the treatment;
- whether a reverse charge or customer self-accounting applies;
- whether an exemption, resale, or zero-rate certificate is valid and on file;
- whether the tax is included in the price or added to it.

Record the customer's tax registration number where required, and never issue a tax invoice with a missing or invalid tax identifier if the jurisdiction requires one. Tax errors on issued invoices can require credit-and-rebill cycles or formal corrections, both of which create audit exposure.

### Control Credit Notes And Adjustments

Credit notes reduce revenue and reduce the receivable, so they carry the same integrity risk as invoices but in reverse. Uncontrolled credits can be used to hide collection failures, appease disputes without authority, or manipulate revenue.

For each credit note, capture:

- the original invoice being credited;
- the reason for the credit (error, return, concession, write-off, pricing correction);
- the amount including and excluding tax;
- the approver and the authorization threshold;
- the accounting effect on revenue, tax, and the receivable.

Credits should reference the original invoice and be applied to it. A credit issued without a clear reason or approval is a control failure regardless of the amount.

### Make Invoices Clear And Collectible

An invoice that a customer cannot understand will not be paid promptly. Clarity is a collection tool. Each invoice should let the customer verify what they are being charged and how to pay without contacting the seller.

Include:

- seller identity, tax registration, and contact for billing queries;
- invoice number, issue date, and due date;
- customer name and billing address;
- clear line-item descriptions, quantities, and prices;
- tax breakdown by rate and jurisdiction;
- total amount and currency;
- payment instructions, banking details, and reference to use;
- reference to the contract, order, or project where relevant.

Ambiguous invoicing extends days-sales-outstanding and increases dispute volume. Treat invoice design as part of the credit-control process.

### Manage Recurring And Milestone Billing Deliberately

Recurring subscriptions and milestone contracts introduce automation and judgment that can silently drift. Standing billing schedules keep charging even when services change, and milestone bills require verification that the milestone was reached.

For recurring billing, periodically review:

- whether the customer is still active and the terms are current;
- whether usage-based components are metered correctly;
- whether price escalations and renewals are applied as contracted;
- whether dormant accounts are still being billed.

For milestone billing, confirm each milestone against documented acceptance or completion evidence before invoicing. Do not bill a milestone based on a calendar date alone.

### Approve And Segregate Billing Functions

The person who issues invoices should not be the sole person who can issue credit notes, adjust customer terms, or receive cash. Segregation prevents an employee from creating a fake invoice, collecting it, and crediting it off without detection.

At minimum, separate:

- customer setup and credit-limit approval;
- invoice issuance;
- credit-note issuance and approval;
- cash receipting and application;
- write-off authority for uncollectible balances.

Where segregation is limited by staffing, implement supervisory review of credits, write-offs, and customer master changes.

## Common Traps

### Billing The Contract Value Instead Of Performance

Charging the full contract amount up front, or billing a scheduled amount without confirming delivery, creates receivables and revenue that may need reversal if performance fails. Bill what was earned, and defer or true-up the rest.

### Applying A Single Tax Rate To All Customers

Tax depends on customer location, status, and product type. Applying one rate mechanically leads to over- or under-charged tax, customer disputes, and filing errors. Determine tax per invoice based on the rules.

### Issuing Credit Notes To Avoid Hard Conversations

When a customer disputes a valid charge, issuing a credit to end the argument forfeits revenue and signals that disputes are rewarded. Investigate disputes, approve concessions against policy, and document the reason for every credit.

### Letting Recurring Billing Run Unattended

Automated subscriptions and renewals keep billing until stopped. Without review, the business invoices cancelled customers, applies outdated prices, and misses usage changes. Review recurring schedules on a cadence.

### Confusing Billing With Revenue Recognition

Issuing an invoice does not always mean revenue is earned, and earning revenue does not always mean an invoice has been issued. Conflating the two distorts periods and creates audit findings. Keep billing and recognition distinct and documented.

### Omitting Required Invoice Content

Some jurisdictions prescribe mandatory invoice fields, including tax registration numbers, sequential numbering, and tax-rate breakdowns. Missing content can make an invoice invalid for the customer's tax recovery and trigger penalties for the seller.

## Self-Check

- Does each invoice reflect delivered and earned performance supported by evidence, rather than assumed contract value?
- Is the billing period aligned with the revenue recognition treatment, with deferrals or true-ups documented where they diverge?
- Is tax determined per invoice based on product, customer, jurisdiction, and place of supply, with valid registrations and certificates on file?
- Are credit notes controlled with reasons, approvers, original-invoice references, and documented accounting effects?
- Are invoices clear enough that customers can verify charges and pay without follow-up?
- Are recurring and milestone billing schedules reviewed periodically against active customers and completed milestones?
- Are billing, credit-note, cash receipting, and write-off functions segregated or covered by compensating controls?
- Are jurisdiction-specific mandatory invoice fields included on every tax invoice?
- Are disputes investigated and concessions approved against policy rather than credited reflexively?
- Is uncertain tax treatment routed to a qualified professional rather than guessed?
