# Bond Issuance And Discount Premium Amortization

Bonds issued at a price different from face value create a discount or premium that must be amortized over the life of the bond. The effective interest method produces a constant periodic rate of interest on the carrying amount, while the straight-line method is a simplification that is not permitted under IFRS and only acceptable under US GAAP when the result is not materially different. Errors in initial recording, cost allocation, or amortization compound over the bond's life and distort both interest expense and the liability balance.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during bond issuance and amortization accounting. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist treasury advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 470, IFRS IAS 39 or IFRS 9) and consult a qualified professional for bonds with embedded derivatives or convertible features.

## Core Rules

### Record The Bond At Issue Proceeds Net Of Issuance Costs

The initial measurement of a bond payable determines the discount or premium and the basis for amortization.

- Record the bond payable at the face amount, with a discount or premium for the difference between face value and the issue price.
- Under IFRS 9, transaction costs are deducted from the initial measurement of the financial liability, adjusting the discount or premium. The bond is initially measured at fair value minus transaction costs.
- Under US GAAP, debt issuance costs are presented as a deduction from the carrying amount of the debt, similar to a discount, and amortized over the life of the debt. They are no longer presented as a deferred asset.
- Confirm the issue price reflects the market rate of interest at issuance. The market rate, not the stated coupon rate, drives the effective interest expense.

### Determine The Effective Interest Rate

The effective interest rate is the rate that exactly discounts the future cash payments (interest and principal) to the initial carrying amount of the bond.

- The effective interest rate incorporates the stated coupon rate, the discount or premium, and the transaction costs. It is the market rate at issuance adjusted for costs.
- Calculate the effective interest rate using the present value of all future cash flows, including periodic interest payments and the principal repayment at maturity.
- Document the calculation and use the effective rate consistently throughout the life of the bond, unless the bond is modified or there is a change in estimate for fees that are part of the original terms.

### Apply The Effective Interest Method

The effective interest method amortizes the discount or premium so that interest expense is a constant percentage of the carrying amount each period.

- Interest expense for each period equals the carrying amount at the beginning of the period multiplied by the effective interest rate.
- The cash interest paid equals the face amount multiplied by the stated coupon rate.
- The difference between interest expense and cash interest is the amortization of the discount or premium. A discount amortization increases the carrying amount; a premium amortization decreases it.
- For a discount bond, interest expense exceeds cash interest, and the carrying amount accretes toward face value. For a premium bond, cash interest exceeds interest expense, and the carrying amount declines toward face value.
- At maturity, the carrying amount equals the face amount, and the discount or premium is fully amortized.

### Handle Issuance Costs Correctly

Debt issuance costs include legal fees, underwriting fees, registration fees, printing costs, and other incremental costs of issuing the debt.

- Under IFRS, issuance costs reduce the initial measurement of the liability and are amortized through the effective interest rate.
- Under US GAAP, issuance costs are presented as a deduction from the debt and amortized over the term of the debt using the effective interest method, or another method that produces results not materially different.
- Do not expense issuance costs immediately. They are a cost of obtaining the financing and are amortized over the life of the debt.
- Allocate issuance costs across multiple instruments if a single transaction involves multiple debt instruments.

### Account For Bonds With Interest Payment Frequencies Different From Accounting Periods

When bonds pay interest semiannually or quarterly but financial statements are prepared monthly or annually, accrue interest between payment dates.

- Accrue interest expense using the effective interest method for the partial period.
- Amortize the discount or premium for the partial period consistently.
- At the interest payment date, reverse the accrued interest payable and record the cash payment.

### Distinguish Between Discount Or Premium And Issuance Costs

A discount or premium arises from the difference between the face amount and the issue price driven by the market rate versus the coupon rate. Issuance costs are incremental costs of the transaction. Both reduce the initial carrying amount and are amortized, but they have different economic drivers.

- Do not confuse a deep-discount bond, where the issue price is far below face value, with a bond issued at par with significant issuance costs. The accounting is similar but the disclosures and analysis differ.
- For zero-coupon bonds, the entire face value is accreted through the effective interest method, with no periodic cash interest.

### Handle Early Extinguishment And Calls

If bonds are redeemed before maturity, recognize a gain or loss on extinguishment.

- The gain or loss is the difference between the reacquisition price (the amount paid) and the net carrying amount of the bonds, including unamortized discount or premium and unamortized issuance costs.
- Recognize the gain or loss in profit or loss in the period of extinguishment.
- Remove the unamortized discount or premium and issuance costs from the books upon extinguishment.
- Call premiums paid to the bondholder are part of the reacquisition price and affect the gain or loss.

### Prepare And Maintain Amortization Schedules

An amortization schedule is essential for accurate period-end accruals and disclosure.

- The schedule shows the beginning carrying amount, cash interest paid, interest expense, discount or premium amortization, and ending carrying amount for each period.
- Reconcile the schedule to the general ledger at each reporting date.
- Update the schedule for any modifications, partial redemptions, or changes in estimate.

## Common Traps

### Using The Straight-Line Method When It Is Not Permitted Or Materially Different

Under IFRS, the straight-line method is not permitted for financial liabilities. Under US GAAP, it is only acceptable if the result is not materially different from the effective interest method. Using straight-line for long-maturity or deep-discount bonds misstates interest expense.

### Recording The Bond At Face Value Without A Discount Or Premium

If a bond is issued below or above face value, the discount or premium must be recorded. Recording the bond at face value and expensing the difference at issuance misstates the liability and front-loads the loss.

### Expensing Issuance Costs Immediately

Issuance costs must be amortized over the life of the debt under both frameworks. Expensing them immediately understates the liability and overstates expense in the issuance period.

### Miscalculating The Effective Interest Rate

The effective interest rate must exactly discount all future cash flows to the initial carrying amount. Errors in the calculation propagate through the entire amortization schedule. Verify the rate by reconstructing the present value.

### Failing To Accrue Interest Between Payment Dates

If financial statements are prepared between coupon payment dates, interest must be accrued. Recording interest only on payment dates understates expense and the liability in interim periods.

### Ignoring Unamortized Discount Or Premium At Extinguishment

Upon early extinguishment, the net carrying amount includes unamortized discount, premium, and issuance costs. Using only the face amount to calculate the gain or loss misstates the result.

### Confusing Issuance Costs With A Discount

Issuance costs reduce the proceeds and are amortized, but they are distinct from a discount arising from market rate differences. Mixing the two in the amortization schedule creates confusion and errors.

### Not Reconciling The Amortization Schedule To The Ledger

An amortization schedule that is not reconciled to the general ledger drifts from the actual balances. Reconcile at each reporting date to catch posting errors.

## Self-Check

- Is the bond payable recorded at face value with a discount or premium for the difference between face value and issue price?
- Are issuance costs deducted from the initial carrying amount under IFRS, or presented as a deduction from the debt and amortized under US GAAP?
- Is the effective interest rate calculated to exactly discount all future cash flows to the initial carrying amount, and is it documented?
- Is the effective interest method applied consistently, with interest expense equal to the carrying amount times the effective rate, and the difference from cash interest recorded as discount or premium amortization?
- Is the straight-line method avoided under IFRS and only used under US GAAP when not materially different?
- Is interest accrued between coupon payment dates for interim financial reporting?
- At early extinguishment, is the gain or loss calculated using the net carrying amount including unamortized discount, premium, and issuance costs?
- Is an amortization schedule maintained and reconciled to the general ledger at each reporting date?
- Are disclosures complete, including the face amount, carrying amount, effective interest rate, and maturity analysis?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for bonds with embedded derivatives or convertible features?
