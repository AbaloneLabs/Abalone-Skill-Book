# Debt Covenants And Compliance

Debt covenants are the contractual tripwires inside a loan agreement. They are easy to ignore between reporting dates because nothing visible happens when a covenant is met, but a single breach can reclassify all debt to current, accelerate repayment, trigger cross-default, and force emergency refinancing. Covenant compliance is not a once-a-year exercise; it is an ongoing measurement that depends on how the borrower defines the covenant ratios, which adjustments the lender permits, and whether the balance sheet date position has changed since the last test. Agents often treat covenants as a footnote and discover too late that the financial statements and the compliance certificate disagree.

Use this skill before signing a loan with covenants, preparing a compliance certificate, testing ratios at a reporting date, evaluating whether a breach has occurred, accounting for a waiver, or disclosing covenant exposure. The goal is to prevent the agent from assuming compliance and missing a technical default that reshapes the financial statements.

Covenant definitions and remedies depend on the specific loan agreement, the governing law, and the reporting framework. US GAAP, IFRS, and local GAAP differ on how covenant breaches affect debt classification and disclosure. This skill provides general structure; the binding terms are in the credit agreement. For material or disputed covenants, route the analysis to a qualified accountant, treasurer, or legal counsel. This is guidance, not a legal or accounting conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Read The Credit Agreement Before Testing Anything

Covenant compliance is governed by the definitions in the credit agreement, not by standard accounting ratios. Two lenders can call a ratio "debt to EBITDA" and define it differently.

Extract from the agreement: each covenant (financial and nonfinancial); the precise ratio definition; the numerator and denominator components; permitted add-backs and exclusions; the measurement period and frequency; the threshold or maximum/minimum level; the cure rights; the grace period; the consequences of breach; and the reporting deadline. Build a covenant definition sheet that maps each covenant to its calculation, source accounts, and required evidence.

### Distinguish Affirmative, Negative, And Financial Covenants

Affirmative covenants require action: deliver financial statements, maintain insurance, pay taxes, preserve assets. Negative covenants prohibit action: no additional debt, no restricted payments, no asset sales, no liens, no change of control. Financial covenants require a measured outcome: maximum leverage, minimum interest cover, minimum liquidity, maximum capital expenditure.

Each type needs a different monitoring approach. Affirmative covenants are checklist items. Negative covenants require ongoing review of every proposed transaction against the basket and cap structure. Financial covenants require periodic calculation against defined ratios. Missing a negative covenant (for example, drawing extra debt beyond a permitted basket) can be as serious as failing a ratio.

### Calculate Financial Covenants Using The Defined Basis

Use the agreement's definitions, not the statutory accounts. Common adjustments include: EBITDA add-backs for noncash items, one-off charges, pro forma acquisitions, and management adjustments; debt definitions that include or exclude letters of credit, leases, intercompany loans, and subordinated debt; cash definitions that include or exclude restricted cash and short-term investments.

Document each calculation step with the source number and the agreement clause that permits it. A compliance certificate that cannot be traced to the definitions is indefensible if challenged by the lender or auditor.

### Monitor Continuously, Not Only At Reporting Dates

A covenant tested quarterly can be breached at any point during the quarter if the agreement requires continuous compliance or in-period testing. Track leading indicators: EBITDA trend against the interest cover floor; debt balance against the leverage cap; liquidity against the minimum cash covenant; planned acquisitions or distributions against the permitted baskets.

Run a forecast covenant test before committing to any major transaction (additional borrowing, dividend, acquisition, asset sale, related-party payment). A transaction that fits the business strategy may breach a negative covenant and create technical default.

### Account For A Breach At The Balance Sheet Date

A covenant breach at the balance sheet date, even without lender demand, generally requires reclassification of the debt to current unless the lender has agreed to a waiver that cures the breach for classification purposes before the financial statements are issued. The classification question is separate from whether the lender has actually demanded repayment.

Evaluate: the date of breach; whether a grace period applies; whether a waiver was obtained before the reporting date or before issuance; whether the waiver is temporary or permanent; and whether the lender has the right to demand repayment. Document the conclusion and the evidence. Disclose the breach, the waiver, and the classification impact in the notes.

### Handle Waivers And Amendments Correctly

A waiver can be a short-term forbearance or a permanent amendment. A temporary waiver that does not change the terms may not cure the classification issue if the breach could recur. A permanent waiver that resets the covenant or extends maturity may require evaluation as a debt modification or extinguishment under the applicable standard.

Distinguish: waiver of a breach (does not necessarily change terms); amendment of terms (may trigger modification or extinguishment accounting); and forbearance agreement (lender agrees not to enforce for a period). Each has different accounting and disclosure consequences.

### Prepare Lender Reporting That Matches The Books

The compliance certificate delivered to the lender must reconcile to the financial statements and the general ledger. A certificate that uses different numbers from the audited accounts creates disputes and can itself be an event of default if the agreement requires consistency.

Reconcile: EBITDA in the certificate to the income statement with agreed add-backs; debt in the certificate to the debt schedule; cash to the reconciled bank balances; and any pro forma adjustments to documented acquisitions. Have the certificate reviewed and approved before signature.

### Disclose Covenant Exposure Transparently

Disclose the existence of covenants, the key terms, whether the entity is in compliance, any breaches and waivers, and the headroom on financial covenants where required or material. Investors and lenders rely on this disclosure to assess refinancing risk. Hiding tight headroom or a recent breach misleads users and creates liability risk.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Compliance Because The Lender Has Not Complained

Lenders often do not police covenants until a problem arises. The borrower's obligation to test and report is independent of lender action. A silent lender does not mean compliance; it means the breach has not yet been enforced.

### Using Statutory EBITDA Instead Of Defined EBITDA

Statutory net income and covenant EBITDA differ because of add-backs, exclusions, and pro forma adjustments. Testing a covenant on statutory numbers can show compliance while the defined calculation shows a breach, or vice versa. Always calculate on the defined basis.

### Missing A Negative Covenant On A New Transaction

A planned dividend, acquisition, or asset sale may fit the business plan but exceed a permitted basket in the negative covenants. Testing only financial ratios and ignoring negative covenants before a transaction is a frequent cause of technical default.

### Treating A Temporary Waiver As A Permanent Cure

A waiver that lasts one quarter does not change the lender's rights for the next quarter. If the underlying position has not improved, the debt may remain current-classified and the breach may recur. Distinguish forbearance from amendment.

### Filing A Compliance Certificate That Disagrees With The Accounts

If the certificate shows different debt, EBITDA, or cash from the financial statements without a documented reconciliation, the lender can challenge the certificate and treat the inconsistency as a reporting default. Reconcile before signing.

### Ignoring Cross-Default And Acceleration Clauses

A breach in one facility can trigger default in another through cross-default clauses. A technical default can accelerate repayment of the entire facility, not just the missed amount. Model the full consequence of a breach, not only the immediate covenant.

## Self-Check

- Has the credit agreement been read and a covenant definition sheet built mapping each covenant to its calculation, components, exclusions, threshold, and reporting deadline?
- Are affirmative, negative, and financial covenants each monitored with an appropriate method, and are negative covenants tested before major transactions?
- Is each financial covenant calculated on the agreement's defined basis with documented add-backs, exclusions, and source numbers?
- Is compliance monitored continuously with leading indicators, and are forecast covenant tests run before additional borrowing, distributions, acquisitions, or asset sales?
- For any breach at the balance sheet date, has current/noncurrent classification been reassessed and the waiver, grace period, and lender rights been documented?
- Are waivers distinguished from amendments and forbearance, with modification or extinguishment accounting considered where terms changed?
- Does the lender compliance certificate reconcile to the financial statements and general ledger with documented adjustments?
- Are covenants, key terms, compliance status, breaches, waivers, and material headroom disclosed in the notes?
- Have cross-default and acceleration consequences been considered for any breach?
- Has a qualified accountant, treasurer, or legal counsel reviewed material, disputed, or complex covenant positions?
