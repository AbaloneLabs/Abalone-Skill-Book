# Debt Modification And Extinguishment

When the terms of a debt instrument change, the accounting depends on whether the change is a modification or an extinguishment. The distinction hinges on a quantitative test under US GAAP and on substance under IFRS, and the consequences differ significantly. A modification continues the old debt with adjusted terms, while an extinguishment derecognizes the old debt and recognizes a new one, with a gain or loss in profit or loss. Getting this wrong misstates both the balance sheet and current period earnings.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during debt modification and extinguishment accounting. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist treasury and legal advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 470, IFRS 9) and consult a qualified professional for complex restructurings or troubled debt.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish A Modification From An Extinguishment

The first decision is whether the change in terms is treated as a modification of the existing debt or an extinguishment that replaces it with new debt.

- Under US GAAP, apply the 10 percent test. Compare the present value of the cash flows under the new terms, discounted at the original effective interest rate, to the present value of the remaining cash flows under the original terms. If the difference is 10 percent or more, the transaction is an extinguishment. If less than 10 percent, it is a modification.
- Under IFRS 9, the distinction is based on whether the terms are substantially different. Compare the discounted present value of the new terms with the original terms. If the difference is 10 percent or more, the terms are substantially different and the old debt is extinguished and replaced.
- For the 10 percent test, include all cash flows, including fees paid to the lender, and exclude fees paid to third parties.

### Account For An Extinguishment

When the change is an extinguishment, derecognize the old debt and recognize the new debt at fair value.

- Remove the old debt, including unamortized discount, premium, and issuance costs, from the balance sheet.
- Recognize the new debt at fair value, which is typically the present value of the new cash flows discounted at the current market rate.
- Recognize a gain or loss on extinguishment equal to the difference between the carrying amount of the old debt (net of unamortized discount, premium, and issuance costs) and the reacquisition price, which includes amounts paid to the lender and third-party costs.
- Recognize the gain or loss in profit or loss in the period of extinguishment.
- Treat third-party costs incurred in the extinguishment as part of the reacquisition price and included in the gain or loss calculation.

### Account For A Modification

When the change is a modification, the old debt is not derecognized. Instead, adjust the carrying amount for the new terms.

- Under US GAAP, for a modification that is not an extinguishment, adjust the carrying amount of the debt for any fees paid to the lender, and amortize the adjustment over the remaining term of the modified debt using the effective interest method. Recalculate the effective interest rate to equate the present value of the revised cash flows to the carrying amount plus fees paid.
- Under IFRS 9, for a modification that is not substantially different, adjust the carrying amount and recognize any difference between the modified carrying amount and the original carrying amount in profit or loss as a modification gain or loss, and recalculate the effective interest rate.
- Do not recognize a gain or loss in profit or loss under US GAAP for a modification, except to the extent fees are paid. Under IFRS, a modification gain or loss may be recognized if the adjusted carrying amount differs from the original.

### Handle Fees Paid To The Lender And Third Parties

The treatment of fees depends on who receives them and whether the transaction is a modification or extinguishment.

- Fees paid to the lender in a modification are added to the carrying amount and amortized over the remaining term under US GAAP, or recognized as an adjustment under IFRS.
- Fees paid to the lender in an extinguishment are part of the reacquisition price and included in the gain or loss.
- Fees paid to third parties, such as legal or advisory fees, are expensed as incurred under both frameworks. They are not capitalized as part of the debt.
- Debt issuance costs for a new debt instrument recognized in an extinguishment are deducted from the carrying amount of the new debt and amortized.

### Account For Troubled Debt Restructurings

A troubled debt restructuring occurs when a creditor grants a concession to a debtor that it would not otherwise consider, due to the debtor's financial difficulties.

- Under US GAAP, the treatment depends on whether the restructuring is a modification or extinguishment under the 10 percent test, with specific guidance for modifications that are not extinguishments, including the recognition of a gain when the restructuring results in a present value of future cash flows below the carrying amount.
- Under IFRS, troubled debt restructurings are accounted for under the general modification and extinguishment guidance of IFRS 9, without separate guidance.
- Assess whether a concession has been granted and whether the debtor is experiencing financial difficulties. Document the assessment.
- Forgive principal or interest, extended maturity at a below-market rate, or reduced interest rates are common forms of concession.

### Apply The 10 Percent Test Correctly

The 10 percent test is quantitative and must be applied carefully.

- Discount the cash flows under the new terms at the original effective interest rate, not the new market rate.
- Include all cash flows under the new terms, including any fees paid to the lender.
- Compare the present value of the new cash flows to the present value of the remaining cash flows under the original terms.
- If the difference is less than 10 percent, the transaction is a modification. If 10 percent or more, it is an extinguishment.
- For term extensions with no other changes, apply the 10 percent test. A short extension with similar terms is often a modification, while a long extension may be an extinguishment.

### Recognize And Disclose Gains And Losses

Gains or losses on extinguishment are recognized in profit or loss and must be disclosed.

- Disclose the nature of the extinguishment or modification, the amounts involved, and the gain or loss recognized.
- For material extinguishments, disclose the line item in the income statement where the gain or loss is presented.
- Disclose the carrying amount and fair value of debt, and the terms of significant modifications.

## Common Traps

### Misapplying The 10 Percent Test

Using the new market rate instead of the original effective interest rate, or excluding fees paid to the lender, distorts the test. Apply the test using the original rate and including all relevant cash flows.

### Treating A Modification As An Extinguishment Or Vice Versa

The accounting consequences differ fundamentally. A modification does not recognize a gain or loss in profit or loss under US GAAP, while an extinguishment does. Misclassifying the transaction misstates current earnings.

### Failing To Remove Unamortized Discount Premium And Issuance Costs At Extinguishment

The carrying amount of the old debt includes unamortized discount, premium, and issuance costs. Using only the face amount in the gain or loss calculation misstates the result.

### Capitalizing Third-Party Fees

Third-party costs, such as legal and advisory fees, are expensed as incurred. Capitalizing them as part of the debt overstates the liability and defers expense.

### Ignoring Troubled Debt Indicators

Failing to identify a concession granted due to financial difficulty means the specific troubled debt guidance under US GAAP is not applied. Document the assessment of whether a concession exists.

### Not Recalculating The Effective Interest Rate After A Modification

After a modification, the effective interest rate must be recalculated to reflect the adjusted carrying amount and revised cash flows. Continuing to use the old rate misstates interest expense.

### Recognizing A Gain Or Loss On A US GAAP Modification

Under US GAAP, a modification that is not an extinguishment does not produce a gain or loss in profit or loss, except for the amortization of fees. Recognizing a gain or loss misstates earnings.

### Failing To Disclose Material Modifications And Extinguishments

Material debt changes must be disclosed with enough detail for users to understand the effect. Omitting disclosures hides the impact on leverage and interest expense.

## Self-Check

- Has the change in debt terms been assessed using the 10 percent test (US GAAP) or the substantially different test (IFRS) to determine whether it is a modification or extinguishment?
- Is the 10 percent test applied using the original effective interest rate and including all relevant cash flows and lender fees?
- For an extinguishment, is the old debt derecognized including unamortized discount, premium, and issuance costs, and is the new debt recognized at fair value?
- Is the gain or loss on extinguishment recognized in profit or loss, calculated as the difference between the net carrying amount and the reacquisition price?
- For a modification, is the carrying amount adjusted for lender fees and amortized over the remaining term, with the effective interest rate recalculated?
- Are third-party fees expensed as incurred and not capitalized as part of the debt?
- For troubled debt restructurings, is the concession and financial difficulty assessment documented, and is the applicable guidance applied?
- Is the effective interest rate recalculated after a modification to reflect the adjusted carrying amount and revised cash flows?
- Are gains or losses on extinguishment recognized in profit or loss and disclosed with sufficient detail?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for complex restructurings or troubled debt?
