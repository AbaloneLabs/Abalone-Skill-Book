# Interest Accrual And Payment

Interest accrual is one of the most routine accounting tasks, but it is also one where small, repeated errors accumulate into material misstatements. The effective interest method must be applied to financial liabilities measured at amortized cost, interest must be accrued for the correct period regardless of payment timing, and the loan subledger must reconcile to the general ledger. Errors in rate, days, period boundaries, or compounding produce expense that drifts from the true cost of borrowing.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during interest accrual and payment accounting. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist treasury advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 835, IFRS 9) and consult a qualified professional for complex instruments with embedded derivatives or variable rate features.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Effective Interest Method To Amortized Cost Debt

For financial liabilities measured at amortized cost, interest expense is recognized using the effective interest method.

- The effective interest rate is the rate that exactly discounts the estimated future cash payments through the expected life of the instrument to the initial carrying amount, including transaction costs and fees that are part of the original terms.
- Interest expense for each period equals the carrying amount at the beginning of the period multiplied by the effective interest rate.
- The carrying amount accretes or amortizes based on the difference between the effective interest expense and the cash paid.
- Apply the effective interest method to bonds, term loans, notes payable, and other amortized cost debt. Do not use the stated coupon rate as the expense rate when there is a discount, premium, or issuance costs.

### Calculate Interest For The Correct Period

Interest must be recognized in the period to which it relates, regardless of when cash is paid.

- For monthly financial statements, accrue one month of interest expense at each month-end, even if the loan pays quarterly, semiannually, or at maturity.
- For a loan with monthly payments, recognize one month of interest based on the beginning carrying amount for that month.
- For a loan with a single payment at maturity, accrue interest each period using the effective interest method, recognizing the full accretion as interest expense over the life.

### Handle Day Count Conventions Consistently

The day count convention determines how interest accrues within a period and affects the exact interest amount.

- Common conventions include actual over 365, actual over 360, 30 over 360, and actual over actual. Confirm the convention specified in the loan agreement.
- Apply the convention consistently for each instrument. Mixing conventions for the same loan produces inconsistent accruals.
- For actual day counts, count the exact days in the period. For 30 over 360, treat each month as 30 days and the year as 360 days.
- Document the convention for each loan and ensure the subledger and accrual calculations use the same convention.

### Accrue Interest Between Payment Dates

When the financial statement date does not coincide with a payment date, accrue interest for the partial period.

- Calculate the interest from the last payment date to the financial statement date using the effective interest rate and the applicable day count convention.
- Record the accrued interest as interest expense and accrued interest payable.
- At the next payment date, reverse the accrued interest payable and record the full cash payment, splitting it between interest and principal according to the amortization schedule.

### Distinguish Interest From Fees And Penalties

Not all amounts paid on a loan are interest expense. Classify amounts correctly.

- Periodic interest based on the outstanding principal and the effective rate is interest expense.
- Origination or commitment fees that are part of the original terms are included in the initial measurement and amortized through the effective interest rate, not expensed separately.
- Fees for unwinding a commitment are generally part of the debt measurement.
- Late payment penalties or default interest are generally expense when incurred, but assess whether they represent a modification of terms requiring separate accounting.
- Break costs or prepayment penalties on early repayment are part of the reacquisition price and affect the gain or loss on extinguishment.

### Account For Variable Rate Debt

For variable rate debt where the rate resets to a benchmark, the interest expense reflects the current rate.

- Under IFRS 9, if the rate resets to a benchmark and the benchmark change is reflected in the cash flows, the effective interest rate is recalculated prospectively when the rate changes, without adjusting the carrying amount for the change.
- Under US GAAP, variable rate interest is recognized based on the rate in effect during the period.
- Accrue interest using the current rate and update the accrual when the rate resets.
- Distinguish between a true variable rate that resets to a market benchmark and a rate that changes for other reasons, which may require modification accounting.

### Reconcile The Loan Subledger To The General Ledger

The loan subledger, which tracks individual loans, balances, and interest, must reconcile to the general ledger at each reporting date.

- Reconcile the principal balance, accrued interest, and any unamortized discount, premium, or issuance costs between the subledger and the general ledger.
- Investigate and resolve differences before closing the period.
- Confirm that payments are split correctly between interest and principal in both the subledger and the general ledger.
- Reconcile the total interest expense recognized in the period to the subledger totals.

### Present Interest Accrual In The Financial Statements

Present accrued interest payable consistently with the debt classification.

- Accrued interest on short-term debt is classified as current. Accrued interest on long-term debt is generally classified consistent with the debt, current for the next payment and non-current for the remainder, or presented as part of the debt balance.
- Under US GAAP, accrued interest may be presented within the debt line or separately as accrued liabilities. Apply the policy consistently.
- Disclose the total interest expense recognized in the period, including amounts capitalized, if applicable.

### Capitalize Interest When Applicable

Interest incurred on debt used to finance the construction of qualifying assets may be capitalized under both frameworks.

- Under US GAAP (ASC 835), capitalize interest on funds acquired to finance the construction of qualifying assets, using the weighted average rate of outstanding borrowings.
- Under IFRS (IAS 23), capitalize borrowing costs directly attributable to the acquisition, construction, or production of a qualifying asset.
- Capitalization begins when expenditures are being incurred, activities are in progress, and borrowing costs are being incurred. It ceases when substantially all activities are complete.
- Disclose the amount of interest capitalized during the period.

## Common Traps

### Using The Stated Coupon Rate Instead Of The Effective Interest Rate

For bonds or loans issued at a discount, premium, or with issuance costs, the effective interest rate differs from the coupon rate. Using the coupon rate misstates interest expense and fails to amortize the discount or premium.

### Accruing Interest Only On Payment Dates

If financial statements are prepared between payment dates, interest must be accrued for the partial period. Recognizing interest only when cash is paid understates expense and the liability in interim periods.

### Inconsistent Day Count Conventions

Using actual over 365 for one period and 30 over 360 for another, or mixing conventions across the loan portfolio, produces inconsistent and incorrect accruals. Confirm and apply the convention in the loan agreement.

### Failing To Reconcile The Subledger To The General Ledger

Without reconciliation, posting errors in principal, interest, or amortization accumulate. The subledger and general ledger must agree at each reporting date.

### Expensing Origination Or Commitment Fees Immediately

Fees that are part of the original terms are included in the initial measurement and amortized through the effective interest rate. Expensing them immediately understates the liability and front-loads expense.

### Not Updating Variable Rate Accruals On Rate Resets

When a variable rate resets, the accrual must be updated to the new rate. Continuing to accrue at the old rate misstates interest expense until the next reset.

### Misclassifying Penalties Or Break Costs

Late payment penalties and prepayment break costs have specific treatments. Treating all amounts paid as interest misstates expense and the gain or loss on extinguishment.

### Failing To Capitalize Qualifying Interest

Interest on construction financing for qualifying assets should be capitalized. Expensing it understates the asset and overstates current period expense.

## Self-Check

- Is interest expense recognized using the effective interest method for all amortized cost debt, with the effective rate incorporating discounts, premiums, and issuance costs?
- Is interest accrued for the correct period, including partial periods between payment dates, using the applicable day count convention?
- Is the day count convention confirmed from the loan agreement and applied consistently for each instrument?
- Are accrued interest payable balances classified consistently with the related debt?
- Are origination and commitment fees that are part of the original terms amortized through the effective interest rate, not expensed immediately?
- For variable rate debt, are accruals updated when the rate resets, with the effective interest rate recalculated prospectively under IFRS?
- Is the loan subledger reconciled to the general ledger for principal, accrued interest, and unamortized discount, premium, and issuance costs at each reporting date?
- Are late payment penalties and prepayment break costs classified correctly, with break costs included in the gain or loss on extinguishment?
- Is qualifying interest capitalized under the applicable framework, with the amount disclosed?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for instruments with embedded derivatives or complex variable features?
