# Loan And Borrowing Accounting

Borrowings look like simple cash-in, cash-out events, but the accounting is full of judgment. A loan is not recorded at the cash received; it is measured at amortized cost using the effective interest method, which means the interest expense recognized each period differs from the cash coupon paid. Origination fees, commitment fees, debt issuance costs, and lender charges change the carrying amount and the effective rate rather than reducing expense immediately. Classification between current and noncurrent can flip based on covenant breaches, lender waivers, or refinancing timing. Getting these wrong misstates interest expense, distorts leverage ratios, breaks covenant tests, and produces financial statements that disagree with lender confirmations.

Use this skill before recording a new borrowing, reclassifying debt, reconciling a loan schedule to the general ledger, preparing debt disclosures, testing covenant compliance, or responding to auditor debt confirmations. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating a loan as a cash transaction and ignoring measurement, classification, and disclosure consequences.

Accounting standards vary by jurisdiction. US GAAP, IFRS, and local GAAP differ on debt issuance cost presentation, the definition of a debt modification versus extinguishment, and current/noncurrent classification. This skill gives general guidance; specific transactions require the applicable standard and, for complex or high-value borrowings, a qualified accountant or auditor. Do not treat this as a substitute for professional judgment on a specific entity's debt arrangements.

## Core Rules

### Measure At Amortized Cost Using The Effective Interest Method

A borrowing is initially measured at the fair value of consideration received, adjusted for transaction costs that are directly attributable to the arrangement. The cash received is rarely the carrying amount when fees and costs are present.

The effective interest rate is the rate that exactly discounts the estimated future cash payments (principal and coupon) through the expected life of the debt back to the net carrying amount. Each period, interest expense equals the carrying amount multiplied by the effective rate; the cash coupon is paid separately; the difference adjusts the carrying amount toward face value at maturity.

Build and maintain a loan amortization schedule for every borrowing that shows: opening balance; effective interest accrual; cash coupon paid; amortization of premium, discount, or fees; principal repayments; ending carrying amount. Reconcile the schedule to the general ledger every period.

### Handle Origination Fees And Debt Issuance Costs Correctly

Origination fees, front-end fees, commitment fees on drawn facilities, and other lender charges are not expensed when paid. They are netted against the carrying amount of the debt and amortized through interest expense over the life of the borrowing using the effective interest method.

Direct debt issuance costs (legal, accounting, printing, registration, trustee fees directly attributable to the borrowing) receive the same treatment under IFRS. Under US GAAP, presentation differs historically (these costs may be presented as a direct deduction from the debt or as a deferred asset), though the amortization effect on interest expense is similar. Confirm the applicable presentation rule for the reporting framework.

Commitment fees on undrawn revolving facilities are generally expensed as incurred unless they are incremental and directly attributable to setting up the facility, in which case they may be deferred. Document the treatment for each facility.

### Classify Current Versus Noncurrent Deliberately

The current portion of debt is the amount due within the longer of one year or the operating cycle from the balance sheet date. But classification also depends on lender rights and waivers.

Key triggers that can reclassify long-term debt to current: a covenant breach at the balance sheet date without a lender waiver obtained before the financial statements are issued; a maturity that falls due within twelve months with no refinancing completed; a lender right to demand repayment on demand or at short notice.

If a waiver or refinancing is obtained after the balance sheet date but before issuance, evaluate whether it cures the breach for classification purposes under the applicable standard. Do not assume a post-balance-sheet event automatically cures current classification. Document the date of the waiver, its terms, and whether it is temporary or permanent.

### Separate Principal, Interest, And Fees In Presentation

Present borrowings gross or net of unamortized fees consistently with the applicable framework. Show the current portion separately from noncurrent. Disclose significant terms: lender; principal amount; interest rate; maturity; security or collateral; covenants; conversions; and any guarantees.

Interest expense belongs in finance costs or nonoperating expense, not in operating expenses. Principal repayments are financing cash outflows, not operating expenses. Loan proceeds are financing cash inflows, not revenue. These sound obvious but are common errors when borrowings are processed through operating accounts.

### Reconcile To Lender Statements And Confirmations

Every debt balance should reconcile to an external source: lender statements, loan confirmation responses from auditors, or amortization schedules agreed with the lender. Reconcile principal balance, accrued interest, fees, and any escrow or reserve balances held by the lender.

Investigate differences immediately. A lender balance that disagrees with the books may indicate missed payments, misapplied fees, rate resets, unrecorded accruals, or currency translation differences on foreign-currency borrowings.

### Address Foreign Currency And Rate Resets

Borrowings denominated in a foreign currency create a monetary liability that is retranslated at the closing exchange rate each period, with the gain or loss recognized in profit or loss. This retranslation is separate from effective interest.

Variable-rate borrowings (SOFR, base rate, Euribor) recompute the coupon each period based on the reset rate. The effective interest rate is recalculated prospectively when the variable rate changes, not retroactively. Document the reset mechanism and confirm the rate source.

### Document Every Borrowing Arrangement

Maintain a debt register with: facility name; lender; type (term, revolving, note, bond); original and current principal; drawdowns and repayments; interest rate type and reset dates; maturity; security; covenants; fees; and accounting treatment. This register is the basis for disclosure, covenant testing, and audit support.

## Common Traps

### Recording The Loan At Cash Received And Ignoring Fees

When a borrower receives 980,000 on a 1,000,000 note because of 20,000 in fees, recording the liability at 1,000,000 and expensing the 20,000 immediately understates the true interest cost and overstates the liability. The correct approach recognizes the 20,000 as a reduction of carrying amount and amortizes it through a higher effective rate.

### Using The Coupon Rate Instead Of The Effective Rate

The coupon rate is the contractual cash rate. The effective rate reflects fees, discounts, premiums, and costs. Using the coupon rate as interest expense understates the true cost of borrowing and produces a schedule that never reconciles to the carrying amount.

### Missing The Covenant Breach Reclassification

A breached covenant at the balance sheet date, even if the lender has not demanded repayment, can require reclassification of the entire debt to current. Agents often leave the debt noncurrent because the lender has been friendly, but classification follows the contractual rights unless a qualifying waiver exists.

### Treating Revolving Facility Drawdowns And Repayments Casually

Revolving facilities have a single commitment, multiple drawdowns, and repayments that restore availability. Each drawdown creates a liability measured at amortized cost; repayments reduce it. Undrawn commitment fees are expensed. Mixing the commitment amount with the drawn balance, or treating repayments as expense, distorts the balance sheet and cash flow statement.

### Forgetting Accrued Interest At Period-End

The coupon payment date rarely aligns with the period-end. Accrued interest from the last payment date to the balance sheet date must be recorded as an accrued liability and interest expense. Missing this understates liabilities and expense and breaks the reconciliation to lender statements.

### Ignoring Debt Issuance Cost Amortization On Early Repayment

When debt is repaid early, the unamortized portion of fees and issuance costs is generally written off as part of the gain or loss on extinguishment. Carrying the unamortized balance forward, or writing it off to operating expense, misstates the extinguishment result.

## Self-Check

- Is each borrowing measured at amortized cost with an effective interest schedule that reconciles to the general ledger?
- Are origination fees, commitment fees on drawn amounts, and direct debt issuance costs netted against the carrying amount and amortized through interest expense?
- Is the current versus noncurrent classification reassessed at each balance sheet date for covenant breaches, maturities, and lender rights?
- Is accrued interest recorded from the last coupon date to the balance sheet date and reconciled to lender statements?
- Are variable-rate and foreign-currency borrowings re-measured each period, with retranslation gains or losses recognized separately from effective interest?
- Are principal repayments classified as financing cash flows and loan proceeds as financing inflows, with neither shown as expense or revenue?
- Does the debt register capture lender, principal, rate, maturity, security, covenants, and fees for every facility?
- Is every debt balance reconciled to a lender statement, amortization schedule, or auditor confirmation, with differences investigated?
- Are significant terms, security, covenants, and maturity disclosed in the notes consistent with the applicable reporting framework?
- Has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed complex borrowings, covenant waivers, or early extinguishments where judgment is material?
