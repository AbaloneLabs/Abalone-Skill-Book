# New Standard Implementation

Once the decision to adopt a new standard is made and the transition approach is chosen, the work of implementation begins. Implementation is the operational phase: building the calculations, modifying systems, updating processes, training staff, and embedding the new requirements into the daily and period-end routines so that compliance is sustained, not just achieved for the first period. A standard that is implemented for the first reporting period but not embedded in the ongoing process will drift, because the old habits, reports, and controls remain in place. Implementation is not complete when the first compliant report is produced; it is complete when the organization reliably produces compliant reports period after period without extraordinary effort.

Use this skill when implementing a new standard in systems and processes, designing new calculations, building disclosure workflows, or managing the operational change. The goal is to prevent the agent from implementing the standard for one period without embedding it, from building calculations that cannot be sustained, or from leaving staff and controls unprepared for the new requirements.

## Core Rules

### Translate The Standard Into Specific Operational Requirements

A standard is written in accounting language. Implementation requires translating it into specific operational requirements that systems and staff can execute.

Translate by:

- identifying each recognition, measurement, and disclosure requirement in the standard;
- mapping each requirement to a specific calculation, data input, process step, or report;
- defining the data elements needed, their source, and their required granularity;
- specifying the output, such as a journal entry, a balance, or a disclosure table;
- documenting the translation so that staff and auditors can trace from the standard to the operation.

A standard that is understood in principle but not translated into operations will be implemented inconsistently. The translation is the bridge from principle to practice.

### Build Sustainable Calculations, Not One-Time Spreadsheets

The temptation in implementation is to build a spreadsheet that produces the first period's numbers. This is not sustainable and creates key-person risk and audit difficulty.

Build sustainable calculations by:

- embedding the calculation in the ERP or a controlled application where possible, not in a personal spreadsheet;
- defining the inputs, the formula, and the outputs with documentation;
- ensuring the calculation can run every period without manual reconstruction;
- building controls around the calculation, including review and reconciliation;
- planning for the data that each future period will require.

A spreadsheet built by one person for the first period is a calculation that will break when that person leaves or when the data changes. Build for sustainability from the start.

### Update The Close Process And Checklist

A new standard adds tasks to the close. The close process and checklist must be updated to include them.

Update the close by:

- adding new calculation, entry, and disclosure tasks to the close checklist;
- assigning owners, dependencies, and target days for the new tasks;
- integrating the new tasks into the close sequence so they do not conflict with existing tasks;
- updating reconciliations to include new balances and calculations;
- updating review gates to cover the new entries and disclosures.

A close checklist that does not include the new standard's tasks will miss them in future periods. The checklist is the mechanism that sustains compliance.

### Design And Update Internal Controls

New calculations and processes require new or updated controls. Controls designed for the old process will not cover the new one.

Design controls by:

- identifying the risks the new standard introduces, such as estimation error or data completeness;
- building preventive controls, such as validation rules on new data inputs;
- building detective controls, such as reconciliation of new balances and review of new estimates;
- updating the control matrix and documentation to reflect the new controls;
- testing the new controls before relying on them.

A new standard without corresponding controls is an uncontrolled process. Controls are part of implementation, not an add-on.

### Train Staff On The New Requirements And Process

Staff who execute the process must understand the new requirements and their role. Training is essential.

Train staff by:

- explaining what the standard requires and why, in operational terms;
- demonstrating the new calculations, entries, and disclosures;
- defining each person's role and the controls they perform;
- providing reference documentation for ongoing use;
- confirming understanding before the process goes live.

Staff who do not understand the new process will revert to the old one or execute it incorrectly. Training is what makes the implementation stick.

### Validate The First Period End-To-End Before Reliance

Before relying on the new process for reporting, validate it end-to-end. The first live period is a test, not a routine.

Validate by:

- running the full process from data input through calculation, entry, reconciliation, and disclosure;
- comparing the output to an independent calculation or expectation;
- identifying and resolving errors before the close is finalized;
- having the calculation and disclosures reviewed by someone who understands the standard;
- confirming with auditors that the approach and output are acceptable.

A first period that is not validated may contain errors that flow into reported numbers and disclosures. Treat the first period as a dress rehearsal with full review.

### Embed Ongoing Monitoring And Periodic Review

Implementation is not done after the first period. The process must be monitored and reviewed to ensure it remains correct as conditions change.

Embed monitoring by:

- reviewing the new calculations and balances each period for reasonableness;
- tracking the key estimates and assumptions for significant change;
- assessing whether new transaction types are captured by the process;
- periodically re-validating the process, especially after system or business changes;
- staying alert to standard amendments that affect the implementation.

A process that is implemented and never monitored will drift as the business changes, as systems are upgraded, and as staff turn over. Monitoring sustains compliance.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

New standard implementation involves complex, framework-specific, and jurisdiction-specific requirements that are the professional domain of qualified accountants and auditors. Standard interpretation, calculation design, control design, and disclosure preparation require professional expertise and judgment. Confirm all significant implementation decisions with qualified accounting professionals, and validate the implementation with auditors and, where relevant, regulators. Do not treat any operational translation as authoritative; the standard text, implementation guidance, and professional judgment govern.

## Common Traps

### Standard Understood In Principle But Not Translated To Operations

A standard not translated into specific calculations, data, and process steps will be implemented inconsistently.

### One-Time Spreadsheet Instead Of Sustainable Calculation

A personal spreadsheet built for the first period breaks when the builder leaves or the data changes.

### Close Checklist Not Updated

A close that does not include the new standard's tasks will miss them in future periods.

### New Process Without New Controls

A new calculation or process without corresponding controls is an uncontrolled process.

### Staff Not Trained

Staff who do not understand the new process will revert to the old one or execute it incorrectly.

### First Period Not Validated

A first period that is not validated end-to-end may contain errors that flow into reported numbers.

### Implementation Done But Never Monitored

A process that is never monitored drifts as the business, systems, and staff change.

### Operational Translation Treated As Authoritative

Implementation requires professional expertise; confirm all significant decisions with qualified professionals.

## Self-Check

- Is the standard translated into specific operational requirements, with documentation tracing from the standard to each calculation, data element, and output?
- Are calculations built as sustainable, controlled processes rather than one-time personal spreadsheets?
- Is the close process and checklist updated with the new tasks, owners, dependencies, and review gates?
- Are internal controls designed or updated for the new calculations, processes, and risks, and tested before reliance?
- Is staff trained on the new requirements, their roles, and the controls, with reference documentation provided?
- Is the first period validated end-to-end, with output compared to independent expectation and reviewed by someone who understands the standard?
- Is ongoing monitoring embedded, with periodic re-validation and attention to standard amendments?
- Are all significant implementation decisions confirmed with qualified accounting professionals and validated with auditors?
