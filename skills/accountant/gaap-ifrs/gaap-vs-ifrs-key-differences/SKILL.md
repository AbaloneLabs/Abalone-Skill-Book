# GAAP Vs IFRS Key Differences

Many companies report under more than one accounting framework. US GAAP governs reporting for US-registered companies, while IFRS is required or permitted in most other jurisdictions. Although both frameworks aim to produce faithful financial statements, they differ in specific recognition, measurement, and disclosure requirements that can produce materially different reported results for the same underlying economics. Understanding these differences is essential for dual-reporting, for cross-border investment analysis, for consolidation of subsidiaries under different frameworks, and for any communication with stakeholders who rely on a specific framework. Treating the two as interchangeable, or assuming a treatment correct under one is correct under the other, produces reporting errors that can affect compliance, covenants, and investor decisions.

Use this skill when comparing GAAP and IFRS, preparing dual reports, reconciling between frameworks, or evaluating framework-specific treatment of transactions. The goal is to prevent the agent from assuming equivalence where none exists, from applying one framework's rule under the other, or from presenting differences without identifying the jurisdiction-specific authority that governs each.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Governing Framework And Jurisdiction First

Before any analysis, establish which framework governs and in which jurisdiction. The answer determines the applicable standards and the relevant standard-setter.

Establish:

- whether the entity reports under US GAAP, IFRS as issued by the IASB, or a jurisdiction-specific variant;
- whether the entity is subject to dual reporting, such as a foreign private issuer in the US;
- which specific standards apply to the transaction, including their effective dates;
- whether any jurisdiction-specific modifications or interpretations apply.

A comparison that does not first establish the governing framework is built on sand. The framework and jurisdiction are the starting point, not an afterthought.

### Identify Differences By Topic, Not In The Abstract

Framework differences are meaningful only in the context of a specific transaction or balance. Analyze differences by topic.

Topics with significant differences include:

- inventory costing, where LIFO is permitted under US GAAP but prohibited under IFRS;
- development costs, which are capitalized under IFRS when criteria are met but generally expensed under US GAAP;
- long-lived asset revaluation, permitted under IFRS but not under US GAAP;
- lease classification and measurement, which differ in detail despite convergence;
- financial instrument classification and impairment, which use different models;
- provisions and contingencies, with different recognition and measurement thresholds;
- statement of cash flows classification, such as interest and dividends.

Analyze the specific topic, identify the treatment under each framework, and quantify the difference. General statements that "GAAP and IFRS differ" are not actionable.

### Quantify The Impact On The Specific Entity

A known difference is only relevant if it affects the entity. Quantify the impact on the entity's specific facts.

Quantify by:

- identifying which transactions and balances the entity has that are affected by the difference;
- calculating the measurement or disclosure difference in the entity's amounts;
- assessing whether the difference is material to the financial statements;
- considering the cumulative effect on retained earnings and equity;
- documenting the reconciliation for dual-reporting entities.

A difference that does not affect the entity, or that is immaterial, may not require action. A difference that materially affects reported earnings or equity requires careful analysis and disclosure.

### Prepare Reconciliations For Dual Reporting

Entities that report under both frameworks must prepare a reconciliation that bridges from one to the other. This reconciliation is itself a financial statement item.

Prepare reconciliations by:

- starting from equity and net income under one framework;
- identifying each difference and its equity and earnings impact;
- presenting the reconciliation in the format required by the relevant regulator;
- ensuring the reconciliation ties to the underlying records and is auditable;
- disclosing the significant reconciling items and their nature.

A reconciliation that does not tie, or that omits a significant difference, is a reporting failure. The reconciliation is often subject to audit and regulatory scrutiny.

### Recognize That Convergence Is Partial And Ongoing

The frameworks have converged in some areas but remain distinct in others, and both evolve. Do not assume past convergence is permanent or that current differences are stable.

Stay current by:

- tracking standard-setting activity at both the FASB and the IASB;
- identifying areas of active convergence or divergence projects;
- assessing the impact of new or amended standards on existing differences;
- updating analyses as standards change, rather than relying on outdated comparisons.

A comparison based on standards from five years ago may be wrong today. Both frameworks are living, and differences can narrow, widen, or emerge anew.

### Distinguish IFRS As Issued From Jurisdiction-Specific Variants

IFRS as issued by the IASB is not always identical to the IFRS adopted in a specific jurisdiction. Some jurisdictions modify or interpret standards.

Distinguish by:

- confirming whether the jurisdiction adopts IFRS as issued or a modified version;
- identifying any jurisdiction-specific interpretations or carve-outs;
- assessing whether EU, UK, Australian, Canadian, or other variants differ on the relevant topic;
- documenting the specific version of IFRS that governs the entity's reporting.

Assuming all IFRS reporters apply identical standards can produce errors for entities in jurisdictions with modifications. Specify the version.

### Address Disclosure Differences, Not Only Recognition And Measurement

Disclosure requirements differ between frameworks, sometimes significantly. A compliant presentation requires attention to disclosure, not only to the numbers.

Address disclosure by:

- identifying the disclosure requirements under each framework for the relevant topic;
- assessing whether the entity's disclosures satisfy both frameworks;
- noting differences in required risk disclosures, sensitivity analyses, and narrative;
- ensuring dual-reporting disclosures do not contradict each other.

Recognition and measurement differences are visible in the numbers; disclosure differences are visible only to someone who reads the notes. Both must be correct.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

GAAP and IFRS comparison and dual reporting involve complex, framework-specific, and jurisdiction-specific requirements that are the professional domain of qualified accountants and auditors. Standard interpretation, materiality judgment, and reconciliation preparation require professional expertise and often involve consultation with auditors and regulators. This skill provides awareness of where differences exist and what to consider, not definitive accounting conclusions. Confirm all significant framework comparisons, reconciliations, and reporting decisions with qualified accounting professionals in the relevant jurisdiction. Do not treat any summary of differences as authoritative; the standards themselves and professional judgment govern.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Framework Equivalence

Treating GAAP and IFRS as interchangeable produces reporting errors where the frameworks genuinely differ.

### Analyzing Differences In The Abstract

General statements that the frameworks differ are not actionable; differences must be analyzed by specific topic and entity impact.

### Ignoring Materiality

A difference that does not affect the entity or is immaterial may not require action; one that is material requires careful analysis and disclosure.

### Reconciliation That Does Not Tie

A dual-reporting reconciliation that does not tie to the records or omits a significant item is a reporting and audit failure.

### Relying On Outdated Comparisons

Both frameworks evolve; a comparison based on old standards may be wrong today.

### Treating All IFRS As Identical

Jurisdiction-specific IFRS variants can differ from IFRS as issued; specify the governing version.

### Ignoring Disclosure Differences

Recognition and measurement differences are visible in the numbers, but disclosure differences require attention to the notes.

### Treating Summaries As Authoritative

Framework comparisons require professional expertise; confirm all significant decisions with qualified professionals in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Self-Check

- Is the governing framework and jurisdiction established before analysis, including any dual-reporting obligation?
- Are differences analyzed by specific topic, with the treatment under each framework identified and documented?
- Is the impact quantified for the entity's specific transactions and balances, with materiality assessed?
- For dual-reporting entities, is a reconciliation prepared that ties to the records and discloses significant items?
- Is the comparison current, reflecting the latest standard-setting activity at FASB and IASB?
- Is the specific version of IFRS confirmed, distinguishing IFRS as issued from jurisdiction-specific variants?
- Are disclosure differences addressed in addition to recognition and measurement differences?
- Are all significant framework comparisons and reporting decisions confirmed with qualified accounting professionals in the relevant jurisdiction?
