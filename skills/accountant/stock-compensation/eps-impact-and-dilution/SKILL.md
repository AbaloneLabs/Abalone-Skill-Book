# EPS Impact And Dilution

Earnings per share is one of the most widely used metrics, and small errors in the calculation can significantly affect the reported number. Basic EPS uses the weighted average shares outstanding, while diluted EPS incorporates the effect of potentially dilutive securities such as options, warrants, RSUs, and convertibles. The treasury stock method, the if-converted method, and antidilution sequencing all require careful application. Errors here mislead investors and attract regulatory scrutiny.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during EPS calculation and dilution analysis. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 260, IFRS IAS 33) and consult a qualified professional for complex capital structures or contingent share provisions.

## Core Rules

### Calculate Basic EPS Correctly

Basic EPS measures the earnings attributable to each common share outstanding.

- Basic EPS equals income available to common shareholders divided by the weighted average number of common shares outstanding during the period.
- Income available to common shareholders is net income attributable to the parent entity, less preferred dividends declared, whether or not paid, and less amounts attributable to non-controlling interests.
- For cumulative preferred stock, deduct the periodic dividend whether or not declared. For non-cumulative, deduct only if declared.
- The weighted average shares outstanding considers the number of shares outstanding each day, weighted by the number of days outstanding. Use a monthly or daily average depending on the level of precision required.

### Determine The Weighted Average Shares Outstanding

The weighted average considers changes in shares outstanding during the period.

- Include shares issued during the period from the issuance date. Include treasury shares repurchased from the repurchase date as a reduction.
- For shares issued due to a stock dividend or split, adjust retroactively as if the shares were outstanding for the entire period presented, including comparative periods.
- For shares issued in a business combination, include them from the acquisition date.
- Calculate the weighted average using the treasury stock method for repurchases, or a simple time-weighting for issuances.

### Identify All Potentially Dilutive Securities

Diluted EPS requires identifying all potentially dilutive securities that could increase the number of shares outstanding.

- Stock options, warrants, and their equivalents give the holder the right to acquire common shares.
- Restricted stock units and other contingent share arrangements that will be settled in shares.
- Convertible debt and convertible preferred stock that can be converted into common shares.
- Contingent shares issuable upon the satisfaction of certain conditions.

### Apply The Treasury Stock Method For Options And Warrants

The treasury stock method assumes that proceeds from the exercise of options and warrants are used to repurchase shares at the average market price.

- Add the dilutive effect of options and warrants as the difference between the shares assumed issued on exercise and the shares assumed repurchased.
- The shares assumed repurchased equal the exercise proceeds divided by the average market price of the shares during the period.
- If the average market price exceeds the exercise price, the options are dilutive. If the exercise price exceeds the average market price, the options are antidilutive and excluded.
- Include the exercise proceeds, which comprise the exercise price and any amounts the employee is required to pay, such as tax withholding amounts under US GAAP if included in the proceeds.

### Apply The If-Converted Method For Convertibles

The if-converted method assumes that convertible securities are converted into common shares at the beginning of the period or the issuance date.

- Add the shares assumed issued on conversion to the weighted average shares.
- Adjust the numerator by adding back the after-tax interest expense on convertible debt, or the preferred dividends on convertible preferred, that would not have been incurred if the securities had been converted.
- For convertible debt, add back the interest expense net of tax. For convertible preferred, add back the preferred dividends.
- Apply the if-converted method only if the effect is dilutive. If antidilutive, exclude the security.

### Test For Antidilution And Sequence The Dilutive Securities

Diluted EPS must reflect the maximum dilution, which requires testing and sequencing.

- Calculate the per-share effect of each potentially dilutive security. Rank securities from most dilutive to least dilutive based on their incremental per-share effect.
- Include securities in the diluted EPS calculation in order, from the most dilutive to the least, adding each only if it reduces diluted EPS further.
- Exclude any security that would increase diluted EPS (antidilutive) from the calculation.
- Report the diluted EPS after including all dilutive securities in the correct sequence.

### Handle Contingent Shares And Performance Conditions

Contingent shares that are issuable upon meeting conditions require judgment.

- If the contingency is based on a market condition and the condition is met at the end of the period, include the shares in diluted EPS.
- If the contingency is based on a performance condition and the condition is met at the end of the period, include the shares.
- If the contingency is based on a condition that has not been met, include the shares only if both the condition would have been met based on current period performance and the shares would be dilutive.
- Contingent shares that require future service are included if the necessary service period has been completed.

### Apply The Two-Class Method For Participating Securities

If the entity has participating securities, such as participating preferred stock, apply the two-class method.

- Allocate earnings between common shareholders and participating securities based on the contractual participation rights.
- Calculate basic and diluted EPS using the earnings allocated to common shareholders and the weighted average common shares.
- The two-class method applies even if the participating securities are not convertible.

### Disclose EPS Calculations Adequately

Disclose the basic and diluted EPS, the reconciliation of the numerators and denominators, and the potentially dilutive securities excluded as antidilutive.

- Disclose the basic and diluted EPS on the face of the income statement.
- Provide a reconciliation of the numerator (net income attributable to common shareholders) and the denominator (weighted average shares) from basic to diluted.
- Disclose the potentially dilutive securities excluded from the diluted calculation because they were antidilutive.

## Common Traps

### Using Period-End Shares Instead Of Weighted Average Shares

Basic and diluted EPS use the weighted average shares, not the period-end shares. Using period-end shares misstates EPS when shares change during the period.

### Not Adjusting Retroactively For Stock Splits And Dividends

Stock splits and stock dividends require retroactive adjustment of all periods presented. Failing to adjust comparative periods makes the EPS not comparable.

### Including Antidilutive Securities In Diluted EPS

Diluted EPS must reflect the maximum dilution, which means excluding securities that would increase EPS. Including antidilutive securities understates the dilution incorrectly.

### Not Sequencing Dilutive Securities

Securities must be ranked from most to least dilutive and included in order. Including them out of sequence can overstate or understate dilution.

### Forgetting To Add Back Interest Or Dividends For Convertibles

The if-converted method requires adding back the after-tax interest on convertible debt or the dividends on convertible preferred. Omitting the add-back understates the numerator and overstates dilution.

### Misapplying The Treasury Stock Method

The treasury stock method uses the average market price during the period, not the period-end price. Using the wrong price misstates the dilutive effect.

### Not Applying The Two-Class Method For Participating Securities

Participating securities require the two-class method, which allocates earnings. Ignoring participating securities and using a simple EPS calculation misstates both basic and diluted EPS.

### Failing To Disclose Antidilutive Securities

Securities excluded as antidilutive must be disclosed. Omitting the disclosure hides the potential dilution from users.

## Self-Check

- Is basic EPS calculated using income attributable to common shareholders, after deducting preferred dividends and non-controlling interests, divided by the weighted average shares outstanding?
- Is the weighted average shares outstanding calculated considering all issuances, repurchases, and retroactive adjustments for stock splits and dividends?
- Are all potentially dilutive securities identified, including options, warrants, RSUs, convertibles, and contingent shares?
- Is the treasury stock method applied to options and warrants using the average market price and exercise proceeds?
- Is the if-converted method applied to convertibles, with the numerator adjusted for after-tax interest or preferred dividends?
- Are potentially dilutive securities tested for antidilution and ranked from most to least dilutive, with only dilutive securities included?
- Are contingent shares included based on whether the condition is met or would have been met based on current period performance?
- For participating securities, is the two-class method applied with earnings allocated between common and participating shareholders?
- Are basic and diluted EPS presented on the face of the income statement with a reconciliation of the numerators and denominators?
- Are antidilutive securities excluded and disclosed, and has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework (US GAAP ASC 260 or IFRS IAS 33) been confirmed?
