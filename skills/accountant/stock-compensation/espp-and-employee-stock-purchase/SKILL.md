# ESPP And Employee Stock Purchase

An employee stock purchase plan lets employees buy company shares at a discount, often through after-tax payroll deductions over an offering period, with the purchase price typically set using a lookback to the lower of the starting or ending price. For accounting, the central judgment is how much of the discount is compensatory and therefore gives rise to expense, versus how much is treated as a capital transaction. The tax side adds another layer: the disposition of the shares, qualifying or disqualifying, drives the employee's tax treatment and the employer's payroll tax and reporting obligations. Agents often treat an ESPP as a simple share issuance, but the discount, the lookback, and the disposition each carry accounting and payroll tax consequences.

The harm this skill prevents is understated compensation cost from failing to expense the portion of the discount beyond a non-compensatory threshold, misclassified equity from treating compensatory discounts as capital, and missed payroll tax and reporting obligations on ESPP discounts. The judgment problem is pricing the plan's terms against the non-compensatory safe harbor, recognizing the right expense over the offering period, and tracking the payroll tax triggers.

This is accounting guidance, not legal, tax, or valuation advice. ESPP qualification rules, non-compensatory safe harbors, discount and lookback limits, qualifying versus disqualifying disposition treatment, and payroll tax triggers vary by jurisdiction and plan document. Route definitive plan design, tax, and compliance questions to a qualified equity compensation specialist or tax advisor. The agent's freedom is to structure the expense, equity, and payroll tax accounting correctly and to flag where specialist input is required; it is not to assert plan-specific rules as universal facts.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether The Plan Is Non-Compensatory Or Compensatory

The accounting starts with whether the ESPP qualifies as non-compensatory under the applicable safe harbor. A plan that meets the safe harbor, typically a discount no greater than a defined percentage, no lookback beyond the offering terms permitted, and broad employee participation, generally records no compensation expense. A plan that exceeds the safe harbor, through a larger discount, a favorable lookback, or other market-beating terms, gives rise to compensation expense for the excess.

Safe harbor assessment:

- confirm the discount does not exceed the non-compensatory threshold, commonly five percent but jurisdiction and framework specific;
- confirm the lookback and purchase price terms comply with the safe harbor;
- confirm participation is broad and not limited to a select group;
- if all terms are within the safe harbor, treat the purchase as a capital transaction with no expense;
- if any term exceeds the safe harbor, measure and recognize compensation expense for the excess.

### Measure Compensation Expense For The Discount Beyond Market Terms

When a plan is compensatory, the compensation cost is the value of the benefit beyond what an outside investor would receive. This is typically measured at grant or offering date using an option pricing model that captures the discount and the lookback.

Measurement criteria:

- measure the excess benefit at the start of the offering period, generally the offering or enrollment date;
- for the lookback feature, value the option-like benefit of paying the lower of starting or ending price;
- spread the expense over the offering or service period during which the employee participates;
- recognize the offsetting credit to equity, similar to other stock-based compensation;
- do not remeasure for later share price movement; the offering-date measurement is fixed.

### Spread Expense Over The Offering Period

ESPP compensation expense is recognized over the offering period, the time during which the employee renders service, typically through payroll deductions, to earn the purchase right. The expense does not wait until the purchase date.

Recognition principles:

- recognize the offering-date fair value of the compensatory portion straight-line over the offering period;
- handle overlapping offering periods where a new offering begins before the prior one ends;
- adjust for withdrawals or terminations during the offering period;
- on the purchase date, reclassify the accumulated credit within equity as appropriate;
- reverse unvested expense for participants who withdraw before purchase to the extent permitted by the framework.

### Account For The Purchase As A Capital Transaction

The share purchase itself, the cash received from the employee and the shares issued, is a capital transaction regardless of whether the plan is compensatory. The compensation expense, if any, is separate from the issuance.

Purchase accounting:

- record the cash received from payroll deductions on the purchase date;
- record the issuance of shares at the purchase price;
- for a compensatory plan, the compensation expense and APIC credit are already recognized over the offering period;
- for a non-compensatory plan, no expense is recorded, only the share issuance;
- reconcile the shares issued to the cap table and the plan's available pool.

### Track Qualifying Versus Disqualifying Disposition For Tax

The employee's tax treatment, and the employer's payroll tax and reporting obligations, depend on whether the disposition of the shares is qualifying or disqualifying. A qualifying disposition holds the shares beyond the required holding period and generally favors the employee on capital gains. A disqualifying disposition occurs before the holding period and can trigger ordinary income and employer payroll tax.

Disposition tracking:

- track the purchase date and the holding period for each participant's shares;
- when shares are sold, determine whether the disposition is qualifying or disqualifying;
- for a disqualitory disposition, the ordinary income element may create an employer payroll tax obligation and reporting requirement;
- obtain broker or participant confirmation of the disposition to support the calculation;
- record any payroll tax liability and reporting when triggered.

### Handle Payroll Tax On ESPP Discounts

In many jurisdictions, the compensatory element of an ESPP creates payroll tax obligations, often triggered at purchase or at disqualifying disposition depending on the framework. The employer must withhold where required and remit the employer-side payroll tax.

Payroll tax handling:

- determine whether the discount or disqualifying disposition creates a payroll tax event;
- withhold the employee portion through payroll where required;
- accrue the employer portion of payroll tax as expense;
- remit both portions on the regular payroll tax schedule;
- reconcile the payroll tax liability to the deposits each cycle.

### Reconcile Payroll Deductions, Issuances, And The Plan Pool

An ESPP moves money from payroll to share issuance, and every leg must reconcile. Payroll deductions accumulate as a liability until the purchase date, the purchase converts them to equity, and the plan pool tracks shares available for future offerings.

Reconciliations:

- tie accumulated payroll deductions to the cash remitted on the purchase date;
- tie shares issued to the cap table and the plan pool reduction;
- tie compensation expense, if any, to the offering-date fair value spread over the offering period;
- tie payroll tax withholdings and employer expense to the deposits and reports;
- investigate any drift between payroll, equity, and the plan tracker each offering cycle.

## Common Traps

### Treating Every ESPP As Non-Compensatory

Assuming the plan meets the safe harbor without testing the discount, lookback, and participation terms can miss compensatory expense. Confirm the safe harbor terms against the plan document.

### Measuring Expense At Purchase Instead Of Offering Date

Compensatory ESPP expense is measured at the offering or enrollment date, not when the shares are purchased. Measuring at the purchase date captures later share price movement that should not affect the fixed cost.

### Recognizing Expense Only At Purchase

Even when measured correctly, ESPP expense must be spread over the offering period, not recognized in a single entry at purchase. Concentrating the cost at purchase distorts the periods of service.

### Running The Share Issuance Through Compensation Expense

The cash and shares of the purchase are a capital transaction. Recording the issuance as expense double counts the cost already recognized over the offering period.

### Ignoring Qualifying Versus Disqualifying Disposition

The disposition type drives the employee's tax and the employer's payroll tax and reporting. Treating all sales the same misses the payroll tax trigger on disqualifying dispositions.

### Missing The Payroll Tax On The Discount

The compensatory discount or disqualifying disposition often creates employer payroll tax. Failing to withhold and remit leaves a liability and a reporting gap.

### Letting Payroll Deductions Drift From Issuances

When accumulated payroll deductions do not tie to the cash used at purchase, the gap reflects missed deductions, withdrawals, or remittance errors. Skipping the reconciliation lets the misstatement grow.

## Self-Check

- Is the ESPP tested against the non-compensatory safe harbor, including discount, lookback, and participation terms, before concluding no expense is required?
- For a compensatory plan, is the excess benefit measured at the offering or enrollment date using an appropriate model that captures the lookback?
- Is compensatory ESPP expense spread over the offering period rather than recognized at purchase?
- Is the share purchase recorded as a capital transaction, separate from any compensation expense?
- Is the disposition of each participant's shares tracked to determine qualifying versus disqualifying treatment?
- For disqualifying dispositions, is the ordinary income element assessed for employer payroll tax and reporting obligations?
- Is the employee payroll tax withheld and the employer portion accrued and remitted on the regular schedule?
- Do accumulated payroll deductions tie to the cash remitted on the purchase date?
- Do shares issued tie to the cap table and the plan pool reduction each offering cycle?
- Are plan-specific safe harbor limits, disposition rules, and payroll tax triggers confirmed with a qualified equity compensation specialist or tax advisor rather than asserted as universal facts?
