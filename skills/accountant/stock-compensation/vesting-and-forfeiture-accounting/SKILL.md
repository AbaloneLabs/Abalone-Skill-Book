# Vesting And Forfeiture Accounting

Vesting and forfeiture accounting determines when and how much stock compensation expense is recognized. Vesting conditions define the service or performance required, and forfeitures reduce the expense for awards that never vest. The interaction between vesting terms, forfeiture estimates, and expense recognition creates significant judgment. Errors here produce compensation expense that is either too high, too low, or recognized in the wrong period.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during vesting and forfeiture accounting. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist valuation advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 718, IFRS 2) and consult a qualified professional for complex performance awards or modifications.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Vesting Conditions For Each Award

Vesting conditions determine when an award vests and how expense is recognized. Identify the type of condition for each award.

- Service conditions require the employee to provide service for a specified period. Expense is recognized over the service period.
- Performance conditions require the achievement of a specified target, such as revenue or earnings per share. Expense is recognized based on the probability of achieving the target, assessed at each reporting date.
- Market conditions, such as a stock price target, are factored into the grant date fair value. Expense is recognized over the service period regardless of whether the market condition is ultimately met, as long as service is rendered.
- Non-market performance conditions affect the number of awards expected to vest and the amount of expense, but not the per-award fair value.

### Determine The Service Period For Expense Recognition

The service period is the period over which the employee renders service in exchange for the award.

- The service period typically begins on the grant date or the service commencement date if service begins before grant, and ends on the vesting date.
- For awards with a performance condition, the service period is the period over which performance is measured, which may differ from the vesting date.
- If the vesting conditions are not specified, presume the service period is the period over which the related services are rendered.
- Recognize expense over the service period, adjusted for the probability of vesting.

### Recognize Expense Based On Vesting Conditions

The expense recognition pattern depends on the vesting conditions.

- For service-condition awards, recognize expense straight-line over the service period.
- For graded vesting awards under US GAAP, treat each tranche as a separate award and recognize expense over its individual vesting period. Under IFRS, recognize expense straight-line over the total vesting period or tranche by tranche.
- For performance-condition awards, recognize expense based on the probability of achieving the performance target, with the estimate updated at each reporting date.
- For market-condition awards, recognize the full grant date fair value over the service period, regardless of whether the market condition is met.

### Estimate Forfeitures And Update At Each Reporting Date

Forfeitures reduce the number of awards that vest and the total expense.

- Under IFRS, the entity must estimate forfeitures at grant and update the estimate at each reporting date based on the number of awards expected to vest.
- Under US GAAP, the entity can elect to estimate forfeitures and adjust expense accordingly, or account for forfeitures when they occur. If estimating, update the estimate at each reporting date.
- Base forfeiture estimates on historical forfeiture rates, adjusted for current factors such as employee turnover trends, award size, and market conditions.
- The cumulative effect of a change in estimate is recognized in the period of change, as a catch-up adjustment.

### Account For Performance Conditions And Probability Assessment

Performance conditions require ongoing probability assessment.

- At each reporting date, estimate the probability that the performance target will be achieved. Recognize expense based on the number of awards expected to vest.
- If it becomes probable that the target will not be achieved, reverse cumulative expense recognized to date for the awards that will not vest.
- If the target is achieved and the awards vest, the expense recognized to date should equal the grant date fair value of the vested awards.
- Distinguish performance conditions that can be achieved at any point during the measurement period from those that must be achieved at a specific date.

### Handle Accelerated Vesting

Accelerated vesting occurs when vesting conditions are waived or modified to accelerate the vesting date.

- If vesting is accelerated by a modification, recognize the remaining unvested expense immediately if the service condition is waived.
- If acceleration is due to a change in control or termination event specified in the award terms, recognize the expense according to the award terms, typically accelerating the remaining expense.
- Accelerated vesting does not create additional expense beyond the grant date fair value unless the modification also increases the fair value.

### Account For Post-Vesting Conditions And Restrictions

Some awards have post-vesting restrictions or conditions that affect the accounting.

- Post-vesting restrictions that do not affect the employee's right to the shares, such as transfer restrictions, do not change the expense recognition.
- Post-vesting conditions that could cause the shares to be forfeited after vesting, such as continued employment requirements, may require the award to be treated as subject to additional service conditions.
- Assess whether post-vesting conditions are substantive and affect the probability of the employee retaining the award.

### Reconcile The Expense To The Award Activity

Maintain a stock compensation schedule that reconciles the expense to the award activity.

- Track the number of awards granted, vested, forfeited, outstanding, and expired.
- Reconcile the expense recognized to the grant date fair value multiplied by the number of awards vested and expected to vest.
- Update the schedule at each reporting date for grants, forfeitures, vesting, and modifications.

## Common Traps

### Not Estimating Forfeitures Or Failing To Update Estimates

Ignoring forfeitures overstates expense for awards that will forfeit. Under IFRS, estimating is required. Under US GAAP, the election must be disclosed and estimates updated if elected.

### Treating Graded Vesting As A Single Award Under US GAAP

Under US GAAP, each tranche of a graded vesting award is a separate award. Recognizing the entire award on a straight-line basis over the total period understates early period expense.

### Confusing Market Conditions With Performance Conditions

Market conditions are built into the grant date fair value, and expense is recognized regardless of whether the condition is met. Performance conditions affect the probability of vesting. Confusing them misstates expense.

### Failing To Reverse Expense When Performance Targets Are Not Met

If a performance target will not be achieved, cumulative expense must be reversed for the awards that will not vest. Continuing to recognize expense for awards that will not vest overstates compensation expense.

### Not Accelerating Expense On Vesting Modifications

When vesting is accelerated by a modification waiving the service condition, the remaining expense must be recognized immediately. Continuing to recognize it over the original period understates current period expense.

### Using A Static Forfeiture Rate Without Updating

Forfeiture rates change with employee turnover and market conditions. Using a static rate without updating leaves the estimate stale and misstates expense.

### Ignoring Post-Vesting Conditions

Post-vesting conditions that could cause forfeiture may require additional service period accounting. Treating the award as fully vested when post-vesting conditions exist overstates vested awards.

### Not Reconciling The Expense Schedule To The General Ledger

The stock compensation schedule must reconcile to the general ledger expense and the equity accounts. Without reconciliation, errors in grants, forfeitures, or vesting accumulate.

## Self-Check

- Have vesting conditions been identified for each award as service, performance, or market conditions?
- Is the service period determined based on the vesting terms, with expense recognized over that period?
- For graded vesting under US GAAP, is each tranche accounted for as a separate award with its own vesting period?
- Are forfeitures estimated at each reporting date under IFRS, or elected and updated under US GAAP, based on historical rates adjusted for current factors?
- For performance conditions, is the probability of achievement assessed at each reporting date, with expense adjusted and reversed when targets will not be met?
- For market conditions, is the full grant date fair value recognized over the service period regardless of whether the condition is met?
- Is expense accelerated when vesting is modified to waive service conditions or on change of control events?
- Are post-vesting conditions assessed for whether they require additional service period accounting?
- Is the stock compensation schedule reconciled to the general ledger expense and equity accounts at each reporting date?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework (US GAAP ASC 718 or IFRS 2) been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for complex performance awards or modifications?
