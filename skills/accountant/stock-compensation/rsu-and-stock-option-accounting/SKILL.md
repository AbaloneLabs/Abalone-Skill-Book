# RSU And Stock Option Accounting

Stock-based compensation requires measuring the fair value of equity awards and recognizing the cost over the period that the related service is received. The fair value of options depends on valuation models and assumptions that are difficult to verify, and the expense recognition pattern depends on vesting terms. Errors in valuation, service period determination, or classification between liability and equity awards distort compensation expense and equity.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during RSU and stock option accounting. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist valuation advice. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 718, IFRS IAS 2... IFRS 2) and engage valuation specialists for option pricing.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether The Award Is Equity-Settled Or Cash-Settled

The classification drives the measurement and remeasurement approach.

- Equity-settled awards, such as RSUs settled in shares or stock options, are measured at fair value at the grant date and are not remeasured after grant, except for certain modifications.
- Cash-settled awards, such as stock appreciation rights settled in cash, are measured at fair value at each reporting date until settlement, with changes recognized in profit or loss and a liability.
- Awards with a choice of settlement, where the entity or the employee has the choice, are classified based on who has the choice and whether the entity has a present obligation to settle in cash.
- Confirm the settlement terms at grant and reassess if the terms change.

### Measure The Fair Value At Grant Date

The grant date fair value is the basis for expense recognition for equity-settled awards.

- For RSUs, the fair value is typically the market price of the underlying share at grant date, adjusted for any post-vesting restrictions or dividend rights.
- For stock options, use an option pricing model such as Black-Scholes-Merton, binomial, or Monte Carlo simulation. The model must incorporate the exercise price, expected term, current share price, expected volatility, risk-free interest rate, and expected dividends.
- The expected term considers the vesting period, exercise patterns, and post-vesting restrictions. For employees, use simplified or expected term approaches based on historical exercise data.
- Expected volatility is based on historical volatility of the entity's shares or comparable entities, adjusted for future expectations.
- Document the valuation methodology, assumptions, and the involvement of valuation specialists.

### Determine The Service Period And Recognize Expense

Compensation expense is recognized over the period during which the related service is received, typically the vesting period.

- The service period begins on the grant date or the beginning of the service period if services begin before grant, and ends on the date the vesting conditions are met.
- For awards with performance conditions, the service period is the period over which the performance is measured.
- Recognize compensation expense on a straight-line basis over the service period for cliff vesting awards.
- For graded vesting awards, under US GAAP, expense is recognized as if each tranche is a separate award, with each tranche expensed over its own vesting period. Under IFRS, graded vesting can be expensed on a straight-line basis over the total vesting period or tranche by tranche.

### Account For Forfeitures Appropriately

Forfeitures reduce the total expense because forfeited awards do not vest.

- Under US GAAP, an entity can elect to either estimate forfeitures and adjust expense accordingly, or account for forfeitures when they occur. If estimating, update the estimate at each reporting date.
- Under IFRS, the entity must estimate forfeitures at grant and update the estimate at each reporting date based on the number of awards expected to vest.
- Do not reverse recognized expense for awards that vest but are forfeited after vesting. Once vested, the expense is final.

### Handle Performance And Market Conditions

Performance and market conditions affect the probability of vesting and the measurement of fair value.

- For performance conditions, estimate the probability of meeting the condition at each reporting date, and recognize expense based on the number of awards expected to vest. If the condition is not met, reverse cumulative expense.
- For market conditions, such as a stock price target, the condition is factored into the grant date fair value. Expense is recognized in full even if the market condition is not met, as long as the service is rendered.
- Distinguish service conditions, performance conditions, and market conditions, as each affects expense recognition differently.

### Account For Modifications

When the terms of an award are modified, assess the accounting impact.

- Compare the fair value of the modified award to the original award as of the modification date. If the modification increases the fair value, recognize the incremental fair value as additional expense over the remaining service period.
- If the modification decreases the fair value, the original expense continues to be recognized. The decrease is not recognized.
- If the modification changes the classification from equity to liability or vice versa, account for the change prospectively.
- If a modification is a cancellation and replacement, account for the cancellation and new grant separately, accelerating the original expense and starting new expense for the replacement.

### Account For Cash-Settled Awards With Remeasurement

For cash-settled awards, remeasure the fair value at each reporting date.

- Measure the liability at the fair value of the award at each reporting date, based on the current share price and updated assumptions.
- Recognize the change in fair value in profit or loss, with a corresponding adjustment to the liability.
- On settlement, derecognize the liability and record the cash payment.

### Disclose Stock-Based Compensation Adequately

Disclose the fair value measurement methodology, the assumptions, the expense recognized, and the effect on the financial statements.

- Disclose the valuation model used, the weighted average assumptions for options, including expected term, volatility, risk-free rate, and dividends.
- Disclose the total compensation expense recognized, by income statement line if material.
- Disclose the number and weighted average exercise prices of options outstanding and exercisable, by exercise price range.
- Disclose the assumptions and methods used to estimate forfeitures, if applicable.

## Common Traps

### Using Unsupported Or Stale Valuation Assumptions

Option valuation is sensitive to assumptions. Using outdated volatility, an unsupported expected term, or ignoring dividends produces an incorrect fair value. Assumptions must be current and supportable.

### Remeasuring Equity-Settled Awards

Equity-settled awards are measured at grant date and not remeasured, except for modifications. Remeasuring them at each reporting date, like cash-settled awards, overstates or understates expense.

### Not Recognizing Expense For Graded Vesting Tranches Separately Under US GAAP

Under US GAAP, each tranche of a graded vesting award is a separate award with its own vesting period. Recognizing the entire award on a straight-line basis over the total period understates early period expense.

### Ignoring Forfeiture Estimates

Failing to estimate forfeitures (IFRS) or electing not to estimate without disclosure (US GAAP) overstates expense for awards that will forfeit. Update estimates at each reporting date.

### Treating Market Conditions Like Performance Conditions

Market conditions are factored into the grant date fair value, and expense is recognized in full if service is rendered. Performance conditions affect the probability of vesting and the amount of expense. Confusing the two misstates expense.

### Failing To Account For Modifications

Modifications that increase fair value require additional expense. Treating a modification as if nothing changed understates expense.

### Not Disclosing The Valuation Methodology And Assumptions

Option valuation assumptions are critical to the expense. Failing to disclose them hides the basis for the expense and the uncertainty.

### Misclassifying Cash-Settled Awards As Equity-Settled

Cash-settled awards require remeasurement at each reporting date. Treating them as equity-settled fails to remeasure and misstates the liability.

## Self-Check

- Has the award been classified as equity-settled or cash-settled based on the settlement terms, with liability awards remeasured at each reporting date?
- Is the grant date fair value measured using an appropriate model and current, supportable assumptions, with valuation specialists engaged for options?
- Is compensation expense recognized over the service period, with graded vesting tranches accounted for separately under US GAAP?
- Are forfeitures estimated at each reporting date under IFRS, or elected and disclosed under US GAAP?
- Are performance conditions assessed for probability of vesting at each reporting date, with market conditions factored into the grant date fair value?
- Are modifications accounted for by measuring the incremental fair value and recognizing additional expense over the remaining service period?
- Are cash-settled awards remeasured at each reporting date with changes recognized in profit or loss?
- Are disclosures complete, including the valuation model, assumptions, expense recognized, and outstanding options by exercise price range?
- Is the effect on earnings per share assessed, including the dilutive effect of options and RSUs?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework (US GAAP ASC 718 or IFRS 2) been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for complex awards or modifications?
