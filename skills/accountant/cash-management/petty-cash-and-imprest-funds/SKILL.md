# Petty Cash And Imprest Funds

Petty cash is small in amount but disproportionately risky. Because the balances are tiny, controls are often relaxed, custodians act alone, receipts are missing, and replenishment becomes automatic rather than reviewed. The result is leakage that is hard to detect: undocumented spending, personal use, duplicate reimbursement, fictitious vouchers, and balances that never reconcile. A poorly run petty cash fund also undermines the larger control environment, because it teaches the organization that cash controls are optional when the amounts are small.

Use this skill before establishing a petty cash or imprest fund, reviewing a custodian's records, replenishing a float, investigating a shortage, writing off a loss, or designing segregation of duties for small cash holdings. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating petty cash as a trivial bookkeeping chore when it is in fact a live cash control that needs documentation, independent review, and honest reconciliation.

Banking rules, tax treatment of small cash expenses, and acceptable reimbursement practices vary by jurisdiction and currency, and by the entity's tax and expense-reporting framework. Do not state a single universal rule. Complex judgment about fraud, theft, or tax-deductibility of disallowed items should be routed to a qualified accountant or, where fraud is suspected, to the appropriate authority. This skill provides operational control guidance, not a final determination in any specific case.

## Core Rules

### Choose Between Imprest And Non-Imprest Deliberately

The imprest system keeps the fund at a fixed authorized amount. At any time, cash on hand plus vouchers equals the authorized float. Replenishment restores the exact amount spent. This structure is easier to control and audit because the total is predictable.

A non-imprest system allows the balance to fluctuate and is harder to reconcile because there is no fixed anchor. Prefer imprest for any fund where control matters. If a non-imprest system is unavoidable, build stronger compensating controls such as more frequent counts and independent review.

Decide:

- the authorized float amount and the business reason for that level;
- whether the imprest structure can be maintained at all times;
- who approves changes to the authorized amount;
- how top-up requests are triggered and approved.

### Assign A Single Custodian With Clear Accountability

Every fund needs one named custodian who is responsible for the cash, the vouchers, and the reconciliation. Shared custody destroys accountability because no one person can be held responsible when cash is missing.

Establish:

- the custodian by name and role;
- the custodian's duties and prohibited actions;
- a backup custodian with documented handover rules;
- the person who independently counts and reviews, who must not be the custodian.

The custodian should not also approve their own replenishment, sign their own checks, or have access to the bank account used to replenish the fund.

### Require Vouchers And Receipts For Every Disbursement

No cash leaves the fund without a supporting voucher. A voucher records the date, amount, payee, business purpose, account code, and approval. A receipt supports the voucher wherever the vendor provides one.

Require:

- a pre-numbered or sequentially tracked voucher for each disbursement;
- the original receipt attached where available;
- a written business purpose, not just a category;
- approval by someone other than the custodian for items above a threshold;
- rejection and follow-up for missing or incomplete support.

Receipts that are handwritten, undated, or from the custodian's own relationship should be scrutinized. Pattern matters more than any single item.

### Replenish On Evidence, Not On Habit

Replenishment is not automatic. It is a controlled event that should restore the fund to the authorized amount based on documented vouchers, with the expenses recorded in the correct accounts and periods.

At each replenishment:

- count cash and vouchers to confirm the fund equals the authorized amount;
- review vouchers for completeness, proper approval, and correct coding;
- record the expenses in the general ledger by account and period;
- request replenishment for the exact amount spent;
- reset the voucher file and keep spent vouchers for audit.

Replenishment is also the natural moment to challenge whether the fund is the right size, whether some expenses should move to a corporate card, and whether any voucher pattern looks unusual.

### Perform Surprise And Scheduled Cash Counts

The fund must be counted independently and compared to the expected balance, which is authorized float minus unvouchered disbursements. Counts should happen both on a schedule and without warning.

During a count:

- the custodian is present but does not control the count;
- cash is counted physically, not assumed from the ledger;
- vouchers are reviewed for legitimacy and not double counted;
- the reconciled total is compared to the authorized float;
- any difference is documented, explained, and escalated.

A fund that is never independently counted is effectively uncontrolled, regardless of how trustworthy the custodian appears.

### Investigate And Write Off Differences Properly

Shortages and overages happen. What matters is that they are detected, explained, documented, and resolved rather than quietly corrected. A pattern of small shortages can indicate theft; a pattern of overages can indicate unrecorded receipts or manipulation.

For each difference:

- document the amount, date, and circumstances;
- determine the cause before deciding treatment;
- obtain approval for any write-off or replenishment adjustment;
- record the accounting entry in the correct account, such as an expense or a loss account;
- consider whether the difference indicates a control failure requiring process change.

Write-offs of employee-related shortages may have tax, employment, and legal implications and should not be handled as a routine bookkeeping entry.

### Enforce Segregation Of Duties Even At Small Scale

Petty cash fails most often when one person can request, approve, hold, disburse, replenish, and reconcile. Small teams may lack enough people for perfect separation, but the risk must be acknowledged and compensated.

Separate at minimum:

- custody from replenishment approval;
- custody from independent counting;
- voucher approval from disbursement;
- replenishment from bank account control where possible.

Where separation is impossible, add owner review, surprise counts, and periodic outside review as compensating controls.

## Common Traps

### Replenishing Without Reviewing Vouchers

When replenishment becomes a routine top-up, no one checks what the money was spent for. Unsupported, personal, or duplicate expenses pass through indefinitely.

### Letting The Custodian Approve Their Own Spending

If the custodian can approve, disburse, and replenish without independent review, the fund has no effective control. Trust is not a substitute for segregation.

### Treating Missing Receipts As Trivial

Missing receipts are the most common sign of leakage. A policy that accepts "receipt lost" without follow-up invites abuse and weakens the entire expense-control culture.

### Counting Only On A Fixed Schedule

Predictable counts let a custodian temporarily restore the fund or hide problems before inspection. Surprise counts are what make the control real.

### Using Petty Cash To Avoid Approval Thresholds

Employees sometimes route expenses through petty cash to dodge the corporate-card or purchase-order approval that larger items require. This defeats the entity's broader spending controls.

### Quietly Absorbing Shortages

Recording a shortage as a rounding difference or an unspecified expense hides the issue. Undocumented write-offs make theft invisible and prevent the control improvement the loss should trigger.

### Letting IOUs And Personal Advances Sit In The Fund

Loans to employees, personal advances, and unvouchered IOUs turn a controlled fund into an uncontrolled lending operation. These items should be prohibited or strictly documented and cleared quickly.

## Self-Check

- Is the fund operated on an imprest basis with a fixed authorized amount, and does cash plus vouchers always equal that amount?
- Is a single named custodian accountable, with an independent person performing counts and review?
- Does every disbursement have a voucher with date, amount, payee, business purpose, account code, and approval, plus a receipt where available?
- Is replenishment based on reviewed vouchers and recorded in the correct accounts and periods, not performed as an automatic top-up?
- Are both scheduled and surprise independent cash counts performed and documented?
- Are shortages and overages investigated, explained, approved, and recorded in the correct account rather than quietly absorbed?
- Is custody separated from replenishment approval, independent counting, and bank control, with compensating controls where separation is impossible?
- Are IOUs, personal advances, and unvouchered items prohibited or strictly documented and cleared promptly?
- Have jurisdiction, tax, and expense-reporting rules been confirmed before applying a specific treatment?
- Would an independent reviewer be able to reconstruct the fund's activity from the vouchers, counts, and replenishment records alone?
