# Restricted Cash And Escrow

Restricted cash is cash that belongs to the entity but cannot be used freely. The restriction changes everything: where it sits on the balance sheet, whether it counts toward liquidity, how it is disclosed, and whether it appears in the cash flow reconciliation. The most common error is to leave restricted balances inside the unrestricted cash line because the money is still technically the entity's. That single mistake overstates liquidity, misleads lenders who rely on available cash, triggers covenant breaches when corrected, and creates audit findings that are awkward to explain.

Use this skill before classifying a restricted deposit, setting up or reviewing an escrow or sinking fund, responding to a lender about compensating balances, deciding current versus noncurrent presentation, or drafting the notes that explain restrictions. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating "it is still our money" as sufficient reason to leave restricted cash in the general cash balance.

Legal restriction, escrow regulation, sinking-fund requirements, and disclosure rules vary by jurisdiction and currency, and by the reporting framework in use. Do not state a single universal rule. Complex legal or covenant-driven restriction questions should be routed to a qualified treasury professional, accountant, or legal advisor. This skill provides decision criteria and operational judgment, not a definitive legal conclusion for any specific arrangement.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Source And Nature Of Each Restriction

Restrictions arise from different sources and behave differently. Before classifying, identify what created the restriction and what it requires.

Common sources:

- legal or regulatory requirements that block withdrawal;
- contractual covenants with lenders, lessors, or acquirers;
- escrow arrangements held by a third party for a defined purpose;
- sinking funds for scheduled debt repayment;
- security deposits or collateral supporting obligations;
- court orders, litigation holds, or regulatory seizures;
- exchange controls in foreign jurisdictions that prevent remittance.

The nature of the restriction determines whether the cash is truly restricted for accounting purposes and how it must be presented and disclosed.

### Distinguish Legally Restricted From Informally Set Aside

Not every "set aside" balance is restricted cash. A legally or contractually restricted balance cannot be used for general purposes and is excluded from unrestricted cash. An informal reserve that management has chosen to keep, with no legal barrier, is generally still unrestricted cash, even if management intends to use it for a specific purpose.

Ask:

- is there a legal, regulatory, or contractual barrier to withdrawal;
- does a third party control the release of the funds;
- could management access the cash tomorrow for general use without breaching an agreement;
- is the restriction enforceable against the entity.

Intent to reserve is not the same as a restriction. Conflating the two misleads readers about true liquidity.

### Classify As Current Or Noncurrent By Release Timing

Restricted cash is not automatically noncurrent. Classification depends on when the restriction releases and the purpose the cash serves.

Apply:

- restricted cash whose restriction releases within one year, or that supports current operations, is generally current;
- restricted cash supporting long-term debt, such as a sinking fund for a bond maturing in five years, is generally noncurrent;
- escrow held for a near-term acquisition closing may be current until the condition resolves;
- collateral held for the life of a multi-year facility is generally noncurrent.

Match the classification to the underlying obligation and the expected release, not to a blanket rule.

### Treat Escrow And Trust Accounts By Their Substance

Escrow and trust accounts hold funds for a specific purpose under a third party's control. Their accounting depends on whether the entity still controls the cash.

Decide:

- does the entity still have the right to the cash, or has control passed to the escrow agent or beneficiary;
- is the escrow conditional, releasing on a defined event;
- is the purpose current or long-term;
- should the balance be presented as restricted cash, a receivable, or excluded entirely depending on control.

Escrow is not a magic label. The economic substance of who controls the funds governs the accounting.

### Handle Sinking Funds Against The Related Debt

Sinking funds are cash set aside to retire debt on schedule. They are typically restricted and classified based on the debt they support.

Confirm:

- the sinking fund balance is excluded from unrestricted cash;
- the classification matches the related debt's maturity profile;
- the fund is disclosed together with the debt it supports;
- any investment of the sinking fund is accounted for consistently with the entity's investment policy.

A sinking fund that quietly sits inside cash makes the entity look more liquid than it is and obscures the debt-retention obligation.

### Disclose Compensating Balance Arrangements

Compensating balances required by a bank may or may not be legally restricted. The treatment differs, but disclosure is usually required either way.

Apply:

- legally restricted compensating balances are excluded from cash and disclosed separately;
- informal, short-term compensating arrangements that do not legally restrict withdrawal remain in cash but are disclosed so readers understand part of the balance supports borrowing;
- disclose the minimum amount required, the duration, and the arrangement it supports.

Readers who rely on the cash balance to assess liquidity must be told how much of it is committed to the bank.

### Disclose Restrictions Clearly And Consistently

A correct classification without disclosure is still incomplete. Material restrictions, the amounts involved, the reasons, and the expected release should be explained so a reader can assess true available liquidity.

Disclose:

- the amount of restricted cash and its classification;
- the nature and source of each restriction;
- the expected timing of release where relevant;
- compensating balance arrangements and whether legally restricted;
- significant balances subject to foreign exchange controls or transfer restrictions;
- any reclassification of restricted cash between periods with explanation.

Disclosure is what turns a correctly classified number into a transparent one.

## Common Traps

### Leaving Restricted Cash In The General Cash Balance

Because the money is still legally the entity's, it is often left in unrestricted cash. This overstates liquidity and misleads anyone using the cash figure for covenant or solvency analysis.

### Treating All Restricted Cash As Noncurrent

Some restrictions release within a year and the related cash may be current. Blanket noncurrent classification misrepresents near-term liquidity.

### Confusing Management Reserve With Legal Restriction

Management deciding to keep cash aside for a project is not a restriction. Treating an informal reserve as restricted cash understates available liquidity and misleads in the opposite direction.

### Ignoring Foreign Exchange Control Restrictions

Cash held in a jurisdiction with transfer restrictions may be legally owned but practically unavailable. Leaving it in unrestricted cash overstates usable liquidity.

### Presenting Escrow Without Analyzing Control

Slapping an "escrow" label on a balance does not determine the accounting. If control has passed to the beneficiary or agent, the substance may differ from the label.

### Disclosing Only The Amount, Not The Arrangement

Stating that restricted cash exists without explaining why, how long, and what it supports leaves readers unable to assess liquidity. The disclosure must explain the arrangement, not just the number.

### Inconsistent Treatment Between Balance Sheet And Cash Flow

Restricted cash excluded from the balance sheet cash line must also be excluded from the cash flow reconciliation. Mixing the two creates an unreconciled difference that signals weak classification discipline.

## Self-Check

- Has the source and nature of each restriction been identified as legal, contractual, escrow, sinking fund, collateral, or exchange control?
- Is legally or contractually restricted cash excluded from unrestricted cash, while informal management reserves are not mislabeled as restricted?
- Is restricted cash classified as current or noncurrent based on the expected release timing and the obligation it supports?
- Have escrow and trust accounts been analyzed for control and substance, not just labeled?
- Are sinking funds excluded from unrestricted cash and classified consistently with the related debt?
- Are compensating balances either excluded from cash or disclosed, with the legal restriction status documented?
- Do the notes explain the amount, nature, reason, and expected release of each material restriction?
- Is restricted cash treated consistently between the balance sheet and the cash flow reconciliation?
- Have jurisdiction, currency, and reporting framework been confirmed before applying a specific classification rule?
- Has complex legal or covenant-driven restriction judgment been escalated to a qualified treasury professional, accountant, or legal advisor?
