# Investment Impairment And Fair Value

Investment impairment and fair value measurement are two related judgments that agents routinely fudge, and the consequences compound. An investment that has dropped in value is often left at cost because "it might recover," turning a real economic loss into a hidden balance-sheet distortion. Fair value, meanwhile, is treated as a number when it is often a range built on assumptions, and the level of the fair value hierarchy is treated as a footnote when it is actually a reliability signal. The result is portfolios that look stable while carrying unrecognized losses, and Level 3 valuations presented with the false precision of a market quote. Agents who do not understand the difference between a credit loss and a fair value movement, or who cannot tell when a valuation requires a specialist, produce investment balances that mislead readers and fail audit scrutiny.

Use this skill before assessing investment impairment, applying expected credit loss or other-than-temporary-decline concepts, identifying impairment indicators for equity instruments, measuring fair value, selecting a valuation technique, or deciding whether to involve a specialist. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating impairment as optional and fair value as objective, when both are evidence-driven judgments with real disclosure and reliability consequences.

Impairment models, the expected credit loss approach, and fair value measurement rules vary significantly by jurisdiction and reporting framework, and the IFRS 9 expected-loss model differs materially from US GAAP other-than-temporary-decline concepts. Do not state a single universal rule. Complex impairment, Level 3 valuation, and unobservable-input modeling should be routed to a qualified accountant or valuation specialist. This skill provides operational impairment and fair value judgment, not a definitive valuation for any specific instrument.

## Core Rules

### Separate Credit Loss From Fair Value Movement

For debt instruments, a decline in value can come from credit risk, interest rate movement, liquidity, or general market conditions. Impairment models focus on credit loss, while fair value captures everything. Confusing the two leads to either recognizing losses that are not credit-driven or failing to recognize losses that are.

Distinguish:

- credit losses, the risk the issuer will not pay contractual cash flows, addressed by expected credit loss or impairment models;
- fair value movements from interest rates, liquidity, or market spreads, which affect fair value but may not be credit impairment;
- whether a decline is credit-related, market-related, or both.

An expected credit loss model generally recognizes credit deterioration through an allowance or loss, while non-credit fair value movements are recognized according to the instrument's classification. Do not collapse the two.

### Apply The Correct Impairment Model For Debt

Debt instruments are subject to an impairment model that depends on the classification and the framework. The model determines whether a loss allowance is maintained and how it is measured.

Apply:

- an expected credit loss approach that considers forward-looking information and probability-weighted outcomes where the framework requires;
- a stage approach that escalates loss recognition as credit risk increases significantly;
- consideration of whether credit risk has increased significantly since initial recognition;
- measurement of lifetime versus twelve-month expected credit losses according to the stage.

Defaulting to "no impairment because interest is still being paid" ignores the forward-looking, probability-weighted nature of expected credit loss models. The model demands judgment about future cash flows, not just current payment status.

### Identify Impairment Indicators For Equity Instruments

Equity instruments do not have contractual cash flows, so they are not subject to expected credit loss models in the same way. Instead, impairment is assessed by looking for indicators that a decline in fair value is other than temporary or that the investment is impaired.

Watch for indicators such as:

- a significant or prolonged decline in fair value below cost;
- deterioration in the investee's financial condition, results, or prospects;
- adverse changes in the regulatory, economic, or technological environment;
- a change in management intent or ability to hold for a recovery;
- evidence that the decline is not expected to recover in the foreseeable future.

A decline that is significant and prolonged, or that reflects a fundamental deterioration, generally requires impairment recognition. Holding the investment at cost while waiting indefinitely for recovery hides a real economic loss.

### Measure Fair Value At The Appropriate Hierarchy Level

Fair value is the price that would be received to sell an asset in an orderly transaction between market participants. The hierarchy classifies the inputs used to measure it, and the level signals how much the value relies on observable market data versus judgment.

Classify:

- Level 1, quoted prices in active markets for identical assets, the most reliable;
- Level 2, inputs other than quoted prices that are observable, such as interest rates and yield curves;
- Level 3, unobservable inputs that require significant judgment, the least reliable.

A Level 3 valuation is not equivalent to a market quote. Presenting it with the same precision misleads readers. The level must drive both the confidence in the number and the disclosure around it.

### Select Valuation Techniques By Instrument And Inputs

The valuation technique must fit the instrument and the available inputs. Using the wrong technique, or forcing a market technique onto an instrument with no active market, produces a number that looks precise but is not reliable.

Select from:

- market approach, using prices and multiples from comparable transactions or quoted instruments;
- income approach, discounting expected cash flows using appropriate rates;
- cost approach, reflecting the amount required to replace the asset, where relevant;
- techniques that maximize the use of relevant observable inputs.

When a technique requires unobservable inputs, the sensitivity of the value to those inputs must be understood and disclosed. A small change in an unobservable assumption can move a Level 3 value dramatically.

### Disclose Fair Value Measurement Transparently

Fair value disclosure is not optional decoration. It tells readers how the value was determined, what inputs were used, and how much judgment is involved. Weak disclosure turns a defensible valuation into an unsupported assertion.

Disclose:

- the level within the fair value hierarchy for each instrument;
- the valuation techniques and inputs used;
- significant unobservable inputs for Level 3 measurements and their sensitivity;
- transfers between levels and the reasons for them;
- the amount of changes in fair value recognized and where they are reported.

Readers cannot assess reliability without this disclosure. A Level 3 balance without input and sensitivity disclosure is effectively unverifiable.

### Recognize When A Specialist Is Required

Some valuations are beyond general accounting judgment. Illiquid debt, private equity, complex derivatives, and instruments with bespoke features require specialist valuation skills, and improvising produces values that do not withstand audit or regulatory review.

Involve a specialist when:

- the instrument has no active market and few comparable transactions;
- Level 3 inputs require significant modeling or forecast assumptions;
- the value is material to the financial statements;
- the instrument has embedded options, conversion features, or contingent terms;
- the valuation requires industry-specific expertise the team lacks.

The cost of a specialist is trivial compared to the cost of a materially misstated fair value that fails audit and requires restatement.

## Common Traps

### Treating Impairment As Optional Because It Might Recover

A real economic loss does not disappear because the holder hopes for recovery. Significant and prolonged declines, or fundamental deterioration, require recognition.

### Confusing Credit Loss With Fair Value Movement

Not every fair value decline is a credit impairment, and not every credit deterioration shows up cleanly in fair value. The two must be analyzed separately for debt.

### Defaulting To No Impairment Because Interest Is Paid

Expected credit loss models are forward-looking and probability-weighted. Current payment status alone is not sufficient to conclude there is no impairment.

### Presenting Level 3 Values With Market-Quote Precision

Level 3 valuations rely on significant unobservable inputs and carry real uncertainty. Treating them like Level 1 quotes misleads readers about reliability.

### Using The Wrong Valuation Technique

Forcing a market approach onto an instrument with no comparable transactions, or a simplistic income approach onto a complex instrument, produces an unreliable number.

### Skipping Fair Value Disclosure

Without hierarchy level, techniques, inputs, and sensitivity disclosure, a fair value cannot be assessed for reliability. The disclosure is part of the measurement, not an afterthought.

### Improvising Complex Valuations Without A Specialist

Material, illiquid, or complex instruments require specialist skills. Guessing the value to avoid the cost of a specialist risks restatement and audit failure.

## Self-Check

- For debt instruments, has the impairment been assessed under the correct expected credit loss or other-than-temporary-decline model for the framework in use?
- Has credit loss been separated from non-credit fair value movement so each is recognized appropriately?
- For equity instruments, have impairment indicators such as significant or prolonged decline and fundamental deterioration been assessed?
- Has fair value been classified to the correct Level 1, 2, or 3 based on the inputs used?
- Is the valuation technique appropriate for the instrument and does it maximize the use of observable inputs?
- Are significant unobservable Level 3 inputs and their sensitivity identified and disclosed?
- Are transfers between hierarchy levels explained, with reasons and timing?
- Is the amount of fair value change recognized, and is it reported in the correct statement?
- Has a valuation specialist been involved for material, illiquid, or complex instruments?
- Has the applicable reporting framework, including IFRS versus US GAAP impairment model differences, been confirmed before applying any rule?
