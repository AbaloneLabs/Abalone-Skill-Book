# Fair Value And Available-For-Sale Classification

Investment classification determines how changes in value flow through the financial statements, whether gains and losses hit net income or other comprehensive income, and how credit losses are measured. The classification decision is made at acquisition and is difficult or impossible to reverse without consequences. Because the rules differ significantly between US GAAP and IFRS, and because classification drives earnings volatility, this is an area where casual decisions create lasting distortion.

This skill covers the classification, measurement, and presentation decisions for debt and equity investments measured at fair value. It is not a substitute for professional judgment or jurisdiction-specific guidance. Confirm the applicable framework and consult a qualified professional for instruments with optionality, embedded derivatives, or complex features.

## Core Rules

### Classify At Initial Recognition Based On The Business Model And Contractual Cash Flow Test

Classification is determined at initial acquisition and generally cannot be changed prospectively without a rare recategorization event. The classification drives subsequent measurement.

For debt instruments under IFRS 9, apply two tests:

- The business model test examines how a portfolio of financial assets is managed to generate cash flows. Models are hold-to-collect, hold-to-collect-and-sell, or other. The test is applied at the portfolio level, not the individual instrument level.
- The contractual cash flow characteristics test (SPPI, solely payments of principal and interest) examines whether the contractual terms give rise to cash flows that are solely payments of principal and interest on the principal outstanding. Instruments with equity exposure, foreign currency exposure beyond the issuer's functional currency, leverage, or contingent features typically fail this test.
- Debt that passes both tests and is held in a hold-to-collect model is measured at amortized cost. Debt that passes both tests and is held in a hold-to-collect-and-sell model is measured at fair value through OCI (FVOCI). Debt that fails either test is measured at fair value through profit or loss (FVTPL).
- An entity can make an irrevocable election at initial recognition to present fair value changes of an otherwise amortized-cost or FVOCI equity investment in OCI (the FVOCI election for equities). This election is on an instrument-by-instrument basis.

For equity instruments under IFRS 9:

- Equity investments are measured at fair value through profit or loss unless the irrevocable FVOCI election is made at acquisition. If FVOCI is elected, dividends are recognized in profit or loss but fair value changes go to OCI and are never recycled to profit or loss on disposal.

Under US GAAP (ASC 321), debt securities are classified into three categories at acquisition:

- Held-to-maturity (amortized cost) requires both the positive intent and ability to hold to maturity. Sales before maturity can taint the portfolio and call the classification into question.
- Available-for-sale (fair value with unrealized changes in OCI) is the default for debt securities not classified as held-to-maturity or trading.
- Trading (fair value with changes in profit or loss) applies to securities held primarily for sale in the near term.

Equity securities under US GAAP are measured at fair value with changes in profit or loss, with no available-for-sale category for equities (except those accounted for under the equity method or the practical expedient for investments that do not have a readily determinable fair value, which can use the measurement alternative).

### Understand What Drives The Business Model Assessment

The business model assessment is forward-looking but grounded in how the entity actually manages the portfolio, not how it intends to manage it in theory. Consider documented investment policies, compensation structures for portfolio managers, past sales activity, and the reasons for sales.

- Occasional sales do not necessarily contradict a hold-to-collect model, but frequent or significant sales suggest a hold-to-collect-and-sell or other model.
- Sales triggered by credit deterioration may be consistent with a hold-to-collect model under IFRS, because credit risk management is part of holding to collect.
- Document the business model assessment and revisit it when the actual management approach changes.

### Measure Fair Value Consistently And Document The Hierarchy

Fair value is the price that would be received to sell an asset in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date. Use the fair value hierarchy consistently.

- Level 1 inputs are quoted prices in active markets for identical assets. Use these directly when available.
- Level 2 inputs are observable inputs other than Level 1, such as quoted prices for similar assets, interest rates, yield curves, and credit spreads.
- Level 3 inputs are unobservable inputs requiring significant judgment, such as discounted cash flow models using entity-specific assumptions.
- Document the valuation technique, inputs, and level for each investment. Disclose transfers between levels and the policy for determining them.

### Recognize Unrealized Changes Correctly By Category

The destination of unrealized gains and losses depends on classification.

- Trading securities and FVTPL instruments: unrealized changes go to profit or loss each period.
- Available-for-sale debt securities (US GAAP) and FVOCI debt instruments (IFRS): unrealized changes go to OCI, with realized gains and losses reclassified to profit or loss on disposal.
- FVOCI equity instruments (IFRS): unrealized changes go to OCI and are never recycled to profit or loss on disposal.
- Held-to-maturity and amortized cost: no fair value remeasurement; measured at amortized cost using the effective interest method.

### Apply Credit Loss Requirements To Debt Instruments

Credit loss accounting differs by framework and classification.

- Under US GAAP, the current expected credit loss (CECL) model applies to available-for-sale debt securities and held-to-maturity securities. For available-for-sale securities, credit losses are measured as the difference between the amortized cost basis and the present value of expected cash flows, limited so that the allowance does not reduce the carrying amount below fair value. For held-to-maturity, the allowance reduces the amortized cost basis.
- Under IFRS 9, the expected credit loss (ECL) model applies to amortized-cost and FVOCI debt instruments. ECL is measured on a 12-month or lifetime basis depending on whether there has been a significant increase in credit risk. For FVOCI debt, the credit loss is recognized in profit or loss with a corresponding adjustment to OCI so the carrying amount equals fair value.
- Equity instruments measured at FVTPL do not have a separate credit loss recognition because all changes in fair value are already in profit or loss. FVOCI equity instruments are not subject to impairment; all value changes go to OCI.

### Handle Reclassification And Derecognition Carefully

Reclassification between categories is restricted. Under IFRS 9, reclassification is permitted only when the business model changes, which should be infrequent and significant, and determined by senior management. Under US GAAP, transfers between categories for debt securities are permitted when the entity's intent or ability changes, and are accounted for at fair value on the transfer date.

- Document the trigger event and effective date of any reclassification.
- Apply the appropriate transfer accounting for the source and destination categories.
- On disposal or derecognition, reclassify accumulated OCI for instruments where recycling is required (available-for-sale debt, FVOCI debt) and recognize realized gain or loss in profit or loss. For FVOCI equity, do not recycle; transfer the accumulated OCI directly within equity.

## Common Traps

### Choosing The Classification Based On Earnings Impact Rather Than Substance

Selecting a classification to manage earnings volatility rather than reflecting the actual business model violates the standard and distorts results. The business model must reflect how the portfolio is actually managed.

### Applying The Cash Flow Test At The Individual Instrument Level

Under IFRS 9, the business model test is at the portfolio level, while the SPPI test is at the instrument level. Confusing the two scopes leads to misclassification.

### Failing To Document The Business Model And Valuation Inputs

Without documentation of the business model assessment, valuation techniques, and fair value hierarchy levels, the classification and measurement cannot be defended on audit or review. Undocumented Level 3 valuations are a frequent audit finding.

### Recycling FVOCI Equity Changes To Profit Or Loss

Under IFRS, once the FVOCI election is made for an equity instrument, fair value changes in OCI are never recycled to profit or loss, even on disposal. Recycling them is a clear error.

### Ignoring The CECL Or ECL Requirement For Debt Instruments

Treating an available-for-sale or amortized-cost debt instrument as if it has no credit risk, or recognizing credit losses only when a default occurs, understates losses. Both US GAAP and IFRS require forward-looking expected credit loss measurement from initial recognition.

### Tainting The Held-To-Maturity Category

Under US GAAP, selling or reclassifying more than an insignificant amount of held-to-maturity securities before maturity (other than for specific permitted reasons) can taint the entire category, forcing reclassification and raising questions about the entity's intent. Document any sales and their permitted reasons carefully.

### Using Inconsistent Fair Value Measurements Across Periods

Changing valuation techniques or inputs period over period without justification undermines comparability. Changes in technique are permitted only if they result in a more representative fair value, and must be disclosed as a change in valuation technique.

### Forgetting That The FVOCI Election For Equities Is Irrevocable

The election to measure an equity instrument at FVOCI is made at initial recognition and cannot be changed. Once elected, the instrument stays in OCI forever. This has permanent consequences for where gains and losses appear.

## Self-Check

- Has each debt instrument been classified using both the business model test and the contractual cash flow characteristics test, with the business model assessed at the portfolio level?
- Has each equity instrument been classified as FVTPL or, if elected, FVOCI, with the election made and documented at initial recognition?
- Are held-to-maturity securities supported by positive intent and ability to hold, with any sales documented against permitted reasons?
- Is fair value measured using a consistent technique with documented inputs and hierarchy levels, and are transfers between levels disclosed?
- Are unrealized gains and losses routed to the correct destination (profit or loss, OCI, or amortized cost) based on classification?
- Are expected credit losses measured and recognized for amortized-cost and FVOCI debt instruments under the applicable model (CECL or ECL)?
- For FVOCI equity instruments, are fair value changes recognized in OCI and never recycled to profit or loss on disposal?
- Are reclassifications triggered only by permitted events, documented, and accounted for at fair value on the transfer date?
- Are disclosures complete, including classification policies, carrying amounts, fair values, hierarchy levels, and credit risk?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for instruments with optionality or embedded features?
