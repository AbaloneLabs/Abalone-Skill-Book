# Short Term Investments And Marketable Securities

Investment classification is not a labeling exercise; it determines where gains and losses appear, how the balance sheet responds to market movement, and whether the entity can hold a debt instrument to maturity at all. The errors agents make are expensive and persistent. A debt security classified as held-to-maturity when the business model does not support it triggers premature-sale restrictions and reclassification of the entire portfolio. An equity instrument put through profit or loss when an irrevocable election for other comprehensive income was available changes the earnings trend permanently. And applying outdated available-for-sale thinking under a framework that has moved to business-model classification produces balances that cannot be reconciled to the actual accounting model in force.

Use this skill before classifying an investment, measuring a marketable security, deciding where unrealized gains and losses belong, testing a business model or cash flow characteristic, or responding to a question about why an investment's value moved. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying a single familiar classification framework when the correct treatment depends on the instrument type, the entity's business model, the contractual cash flows, and the applicable reporting standard.

Investment classification, measurement, and disclosure rules vary significantly by jurisdiction and reporting framework, and the IFRS 9 business-model approach differs materially from older US GAAP categories. Do not state a single universal rule. Complex classification, embedded derivatives, or fair value measurement of illiquid instruments should be routed to a qualified accountant or valuation specialist. This skill provides operational classification judgment, not a definitive treatment for every portfolio.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Instrument Type

Debt and equity instruments follow different classification paths. The first question is always what kind of instrument the entity holds, because that determines which tests and elections apply.

Identify:

- debt instruments with contractual cash flows, such as bonds and notes;
- equity instruments representing residual interests, such as shares;
- instruments with embedded derivatives or conversion features;
- instruments whose legal form does not match their economic substance.

The instrument type anchors every subsequent decision. Misidentifying a compound instrument as simple debt or equity derails the entire classification.

### Apply The Business Model And Cash Flow Tests For Debt

Under the business-model approach, debt instruments are classified by combining the entity's business model for managing the asset with the contractual cash flow characteristics of the asset itself. Both tests must be considered, and the result determines the measurement category.

Assess:

- the business model: hold to collect, hold to collect and sell, or other;
- the contractual cash flow characteristics: payments of principal and interest on the principal amount outstanding, or something else;
- the resulting category: amortized cost, fair value through other comprehensive income, or fair value through profit or loss.

A debt instrument measured at amortized cost requires both a hold-to-collect business model and payments that are solely principal and interest. Failing either test moves the asset to a fair value category.

### Treat Equity Instruments Through Fair Value

Equity instruments are generally measured at fair value. Where the framework permits, an entity may make an irrevocable election at initial recognition to present changes in fair value in other comprehensive income rather than profit or loss. This election has lasting consequences and is not available for equity held for trading.

Decide:

- whether the instrument is held for trading, which requires profit or loss;
- whether an irrevocable election for other comprehensive income is available and appropriate;
- that the election, once made, cannot be reversed and dividends may be the only item ever recycled.

The election is a strategic decision, not a convenience switch. Choosing it without understanding the permanence locks the entity into a presentation it cannot later change.

### Place Unrealized Gains And Losses Correctly

Where unrealized gains and losses appear is the most visible consequence of classification, and it is where readers and lenders focus. Each category has a prescribed destination.

Place:

- trading instruments and those at fair value through profit or loss: changes in profit or loss;
- debt at fair value through other comprehensive income: changes in other comprehensive income, with recycling on derecognition;
- equity instruments elected for other comprehensive income: changes in other comprehensive income, generally without recycling;
- held-to-maturity or amortized-cost debt: no fair value changes recognized, measured at amortized cost.

Routing gains and losses to the wrong statement distorts earnings, equity, and every ratio derived from them. The destination is dictated by the category, not by management preference.

### Respect The Held-To-Maturity Constraint

Classifying debt as held-to-maturity, or at amortized cost under a hold-to-collect model, carries real constraints. Premature sales can call the entire classification into question and, under some frameworks, force reclassification of the remaining portfolio to fair value.

Respect:

- the entity's demonstrated intent and ability to hold to maturity;
- the consequences of selling before maturity, including tainting of the portfolio;
- exceptions for rare, non-recurring, and unforeseeable events;
- the documentation and evidence supporting the hold-to-collect business model.

Selling a held-to-maturity asset for convenience, such as to realize a gain or manage liquidity, can destroy the classification for the rest of the portfolio. The constraint must be understood before the category is chosen.

### Measure Fair Value Consistently And Disclose The Hierarchy

Where fair value is required, it must be measured consistently and the level of the fair value hierarchy disclosed. The hierarchy signals how much judgment and how little observable market data went into the value.

Apply and disclose:

- Level 1, quoted prices in active markets for identical assets;
- Level 2, observable inputs other than quoted prices;
- Level 3, unobservable inputs requiring significant judgment;
- the valuation techniques used and any transfers between levels.

A portfolio with significant Level 3 balances carries very different reliability and disclosure obligations than one dominated by Level 1 quoted securities. The hierarchy is not a footnote; it is a reliability signal.

### Reassess Classification When Facts Change

Classification is not permanent. Changes in business model, when they occur and are significant, can trigger reclassification of an entire category. Reassessment must be done deliberately and documented.

Reassess:

- whether a change in business model has occurred that affects the category;
- the accounting for any reclassification, including the date and measurement;
- whether the change was significant and not merely incidental;
- the disclosure required for reclassifications between categories.

Failing to reassess after a genuine business-model change leaves investments in a category that no longer reflects how they are managed, while reassessing too frequently signals instability that auditors will challenge.

## Common Traps

### Applying An Outdated Classification Framework

Using legacy available-for-sale or held-to-maturity labels under a framework that has moved to business-model classification produces balances that cannot be reconciled to the actual accounting model. Always confirm which framework is in force.

### Classifying By Liquidity Instead Of Business Model

Calling an investment held-to-maturity because it is liquid, or trading because it is short-term, ignores the tests. Classification follows the business model and cash flow characteristics, not the holding period.

### Choosing The OCI Election For Equity Without Understanding Its Permanence

The election to present equity fair value changes in other comprehensive income is irrevocable and usually prevents recycling. Choosing it for short-term smoothing locks the entity into a presentation it cannot change.

### Ignoring The Held-To-Maturity Tainting Risk

Selling a held-to-maturity asset for convenience can force reclassification of the whole portfolio. The constraint must be weighed before the category is selected.

### Routing All Unrealized Gains And Losses To Profit Or Loss

Some categories require other comprehensive income. Blanket profit-or-loss treatment overstates earnings volatility and misleads readers about the nature of the portfolio.

### Overlooking Embedded Derivatives

A bond with a conversion feature or an embedded option may not have cash flows that are solely principal and interest, which changes the classification entirely. Treating a compound instrument as plain debt is a frequent error.

### Treating Level 3 Valuations As If They Were Market Quotes

Level 3 fair values rely on significant unobservable inputs and judgment. Presenting them with the same confidence as Level 1 quoted prices misleads readers about reliability.

## Self-Check

- Has the instrument type, debt versus equity versus compound, been identified before any classification test is applied?
- For debt, have both the business model test and the contractual cash flow characteristics test been assessed?
- Are equity instruments measured at fair value, with any irrevocable OCI election made deliberately and with awareness of its permanence?
- Are unrealized gains and losses routed to profit or loss or other comprehensive income according to the classification category?
- Has the held-to-maturity or hold-to-collect constraint, including tainting risk from premature sales, been understood before classification?
- Is fair value measured consistently, with the Level 1, 2, or 3 hierarchy and valuation techniques disclosed?
- Have embedded derivatives and conversion features been evaluated for their effect on classification?
- Has classification been reassessed when a significant business-model change occurs, with reclassification accounted for and disclosed?
- Has the applicable reporting framework, including IFRS versus US GAAP differences, been confirmed before applying any category?
- Has complex classification, embedded-derivative, or illiquid-instrument fair value judgment been escalated to a qualified accountant or valuation specialist?
