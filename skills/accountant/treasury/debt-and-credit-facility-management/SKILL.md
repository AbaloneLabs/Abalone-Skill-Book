# Debt And Credit Facility Management

Debt and credit facilities are not just balances on the balance sheet; they are live obligations with covenants, fees, availability limits, and maturity profiles that determine whether the entity can keep operating. Treasury management of debt is fundamentally different from booking a loan payment. It requires understanding how much can still be drawn, when facilities mature, what covenants restrict action, how interest and fees accrue, and what happens if the facility cannot be refinanced. Agents who treat debt as a static liability, rather than as a dynamic facility with utilization, availability, and refinancing risk, leave the entity exposed to surprises that can trigger default, acceleration, or a liquidity crisis at exactly the wrong moment.

Use this skill before reporting on debt, assessing revolver availability, accruing facility interest and fees, testing covenant headroom, evaluating refinancing risk, or classifying revolving credit balances. The goal is to prevent the agent from presenting debt as a number when it is a structure with constraints, costs, and risks that must be actively monitored.

Banking, credit facility terms, covenant definitions, and refinancing conventions vary by jurisdiction, currency, and lender. Do not state a single universal rule. Complex treasury, covenant, or refinancing judgment should be routed to a qualified treasury professional. This skill provides operational facility-management judgment, not a definitive conclusion for any specific facility agreement.

## Core Rules

### Track Utilization And Availability Continuously

A credit facility has a committed limit, a drawn balance, and available headroom. Treasury must know all three at all times, because availability is what determines whether the entity can respond to a cash need.

Monitor:

- the committed facility limit and any borrowing base that reduces it;
- the drawn balance at each reporting date;
- available headroom after draws, letters of credit, and other contingencies;
- any blocks on availability from covenants, conditions precedent, or default events;
- the time and process required to draw additional funds.

A facility that appears available on paper but is blocked by a covenant breach or a borrowing-base shortfall is not actually available. Availability is only real if it can be accessed.

### Record Drawdowns And Repayments Accurately

Each draw and repayment must be recorded on the date it settles, with the correct principal, interest, and fee components, and in the correct liability account. Facility activity that is recorded late or netted distorts both the balance and the interest accrual.

Record:

- the draw amount, date, and settling account;
- the repayment amount, date, and whether it reduces principal or pays interest;
- the applicable interest rate, including any basis, margin, or floor;
- fees such as utilization, commitment, and letter-of-credit fees;
- the impact on available headroom.

Netting draws and repayments into a single movement hides the gross activity and makes it impossible to reconcile to the lender statement or to verify interest calculations.

### Accrue Interest And Facility Fees To The Correct Period

Interest accrues daily on drawn balances, and commitment fees accrue on undrawn amounts. If the charge is billed after period-end, the accrued portion must be estimated and recorded, or the period understates its financing cost and the liability is incomplete.

Accrue:

- interest accrued on drawn balances through the reporting date;
- commitment fees on undrawn availability;
- utilization and letter-of-credit fees for the period;
- any margin or basis adjustments applicable during the period;
- the reversal or true-up when the actual charge arrives.

Facility fees are financing costs and should be classified and presented consistently with interest expense, subject to framework-specific treatment for arrangement and commitment fees.

### Test Covenant Headroom Against Forecasts

Covenants define the boundaries of facility availability and can trigger default, acceleration, or margin increases when breached. Headroom is not a static snapshot; it must be tested against the forecast, because the forecast low point is where breaches happen.

Test:

- financial covenants such as leverage, interest cover, and minimum liquidity against the forecast;
- non-financial covenants such as reporting requirements and negative pledges;
- the impact of forecasted draws on leverage and interest cover;
- the impact of working capital and seasonal swings on covenant ratios;
- the consequences of breach, including margins, blocks, and acceleration.

Covenant headroom is part of liquidity management. A facility that is available today but breaches a covenant next month is not a reliable source of funding.

### Assess Refinancing Risk On The Maturity Profile

Every facility has a maturity date. When a material portion of debt matures within the near term, the entity faces refinancing risk: the chance that the facility cannot be renewed on comparable terms, or at all. This risk must be identified and managed before it becomes a crisis.

Assess:

- the maturity profile of all facilities, highlighting near-term maturities;
- the likelihood of renewal based on lender relationships and market conditions;
- alternative sources of refinancing if the current lender withdraws;
- the impact of covenant or ratio deterioration on refinancing options;
- the lead time required to arrange replacement financing.

Near-term maturities that depend on refinancing should be classified and disclosed to reflect the refinancing risk, subject to framework-specific rules.

### Classify Revolving Credit Balances By Substance

A revolving credit facility can be drawn and repaid repeatedly, which complicates classification. The drawn balance may be current or noncurrent depending on the facility's terms and the entity's intent, and the classification rules differ by framework.

Classify:

- the drawn balance based on the facility's contractual maturity and renewal terms;
- whether the facility has an unconditional right to defer settlement for at least twelve months;
- whether revolving balances that are continuously used should be presented as noncurrent;
- the treatment of any portion due within twelve months as current.

Do not classify a revolver based on the fact that it can be repaid quickly. Classification follows the contractual right to defer, not the practical pattern of use.

### Reconcile To Lender Statements And Document Terms

Debt balances must reconcile to the lender's statements, and the facility terms must be documented so any question about interest, fees, covenants, or availability can be answered. Undocumented terms become disputes, and unreconciled balances become audit findings.

Maintain:

- the facility agreement, including all amendments;
- the current interest rate basis, margin, and floor;
- the covenant package and the latest compliance calculation;
- the borrowing base or availability calculation where applicable;
- lender statements reconciled to the general ledger each period.

A debt balance that cannot be tied to a lender statement or explained from the facility agreement is not reliably reported.

## Common Traps

### Treating Availability As Unlimited

Assuming the full committed facility can be drawn ignores borrowing bases, covenant blocks, and conditions precedent. Real availability is often lower than the committed limit.

### Recording Draws And Repayments Net

Netting facility activity hides gross flows, makes interest verification impossible, and breaks the reconciliation to the lender statement.

### Missing Period-End Interest And Fee Accruals

When interest and commitment fees are billed after period-end, the accrued portion is often omitted, understating financing costs and the related liability.

### Ignoring Covenant Forecasts

Testing covenants only at the current date misses the forecast low point where breaches actually occur. Covenant management must be forward-looking.

### Underestimating Refinancing Risk

A facility maturing in the near term that is assumed to roll over automatically can become a crisis if market conditions or the entity's ratios deteriorate. Refinancing risk must be planned for, not assumed away.

### Classifying Revolvers By Pattern Of Use

Classifying a revolving balance as current because it is repaid quickly ignores the contractual right to defer. Classification follows the agreement, not the usage pattern.

### Losing Track Of Facility Terms

Without the agreement, rate basis, margin, and covenant package documented, questions about interest, fees, and availability cannot be answered, and disputes with the lender become difficult to resolve.

## Self-Check

- Are committed limit, drawn balance, available headroom, and any borrowing-base or covenant blocks tracked continuously?
- Are drawdowns and repayments recorded gross on the settlement date with correct principal, interest, and fee components?
- Are interest, commitment, utilization, and letter-of-credit fees accrued through the reporting date and trued up when billed?
- Are facility fees classified and presented consistently with interest expense as financing costs?
- Are financial and non-financial covenants tested against the forecast low point, not just the current date?
- Has refinancing risk been assessed for near-term maturities, with alternative sources and lead times identified?
- Are revolving credit balances classified based on the contractual right to defer, not the pattern of use?
- Do debt balances reconcile to lender statements each period?
- Are the facility agreement, rate basis, margin, covenant package, and borrowing base documented and current?
- Has complex treasury, covenant, or refinancing judgment been escalated to a qualified treasury professional?
