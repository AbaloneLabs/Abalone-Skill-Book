# Working Capital Management

Working capital is the cash tied up in the gap between paying suppliers and collecting from customers. It is invisible on the income statement but it is where many otherwise profitable businesses fail. A company can grow sales, increase profit, and still run out of cash because growth consumes working capital faster than operations generate it. This phenomenon, called overtrading, is one of the most dangerous and least understood risks in finance. Agents who analyze working capital only as a ratio, without understanding the cash conversion cycle, the financing choices, and the tradeoffs between discount capture and liquidity, produce analysis that looks complete but misses the dynamic that actually drives survival.

Use this skill before analyzing working capital, recommending early-payment discount strategy, evaluating factoring or invoice discounting, diagnosing a cash squeeze in a profitable business, or selecting trade financing instruments. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating working capital as a static set of ratios when it is a living system of timing, financing, and risk tradeoffs.

Banking, trade finance, factoring regulation, and discount conventions vary by jurisdiction, currency, and provider. Do not state a single universal rule. Complex trade finance, factoring, or overtrading diagnosis should be routed to a qualified treasury professional or accountant. This skill provides operational working capital judgment, not a definitive financing recommendation for any specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Measure The Full Cash Conversion Cycle

The cash conversion cycle measures how long cash is locked up from the moment suppliers are paid to the moment customers pay. It combines three timing components into one liquidity signal.

Calculate and interpret:

- days sales outstanding, how long receivables take to convert to cash;
- days inventory outstanding, how long inventory sits before sale;
- days payable outstanding, how long the entity takes to pay suppliers;
- the cycle as inventory days plus receivable days minus payable days.

A shorter cycle frees cash; a longer cycle ties it up. Trend matters more than any single number, because a lengthening cycle in a growing business can consume cash faster than profit generates it.

### Analyze Each Component Against Reality, Not Just Averages

Ratios built from annual averages hide the months and segments where working capital is actually trapped. Effective management requires looking at the detail behind each days metric.

Examine:

- receivable aging by customer and by segment, not just the average;
- inventory turnover by product line, including slow-moving and obsolete stock;
- payable terms by supplier and actual payment behavior versus contracted terms;
- seasonality that compresses or extends each component;
- concentration risk where one large customer or supplier distorts the average.

A single average days figure can look healthy while a major customer's slow payment or a pile of obsolete inventory quietly drains cash.

### Evaluate Early-Payment Discounts As A Financing Decision

Suppliers often offer a discount for early payment, such as a reduction for paying in ten days rather than thirty. Whether to take the discount is a financing decision, not a habit. The implied annual cost of forgoing the discount is often far higher than the entity's borrowing cost.

Assess:

- the implied annualized cost of forgoing each discount;
- the entity's cost of borrowing or opportunity cost of cash;
- whether taking the discount strains liquidity in a seasonal trough;
- whether the supplier relationship or volume rebates offset the discount math.

Taking discounts when cash is abundant and cheap is usually sound; forgoing them when borrowing is expensive is usually costly. The decision should be explicit, not automatic.

### Assess Factoring And Invoice Discounting By Cost And Risk

Financing receivables through factoring or invoice discounting accelerates cash but transfers risk and adds cost. The choice depends on whether the financing is recourse or non-recourse, the discount or fee, and the impact on customer relationships.

Evaluate:

- whether the facility is with recourse, leaving the entity liable for non-payment, or non-recourse;
- the effective annual cost including fees, interest, and any minimum-volume commitments;
- whether the factor contacts customers directly, affecting the relationship;
- the advance rate and how much cash is actually unlocked;
- whether the entity still recognizes the receivable or derecognizes it.

Factoring is not free money. It is borrowing secured by receivables, and its true cost and risk must be compared against the entity's other liquidity options.

### Diagnose Overtrading Before It Becomes A Crisis

Overtrading happens when a business grows faster than its working capital can support. Sales rise, profit looks strong, but cash disappears into receivables and inventory, and the entity cannot fund its own growth. It is especially dangerous because the income statement signals success while the balance sheet signals failure.

Watch for:

- revenue growth outpacing working capital growth;
- a lengthening cash conversion cycle during expansion;
- rising reliance on short-term debt or supplier stretching to fund growth;
- declining cash despite rising profit;
- increasing concentration in slow-paying customers.

Overtrading is not solved by more sales; it is solved by slower growth, equity injection, longer supplier terms, receivable financing, or tighter collection. Diagnosing it early prevents a profitable business from failing for lack of cash.

### Select Trade Financing Instruments By Fit

Trade financing instruments, such as letters of credit, bank guarantees, supply chain finance, and invoice finance, serve different purposes. Selecting the wrong instrument adds cost without solving the underlying timing problem.

Match instruments to needs:

- letters of credit to reduce risk in cross-border supplier relationships;
- bank guarantees to support performance or bid obligations;
- supply chain finance to let suppliers get paid early while the entity pays later;
- invoice finance to accelerate customer receipts;
- inventory finance to fund stock before sale.

Each instrument has a cost, a risk transfer, and a documentation burden. The choice should follow the working capital problem, not the availability of a particular product.

### Connect Working Capital To Cash Forecasting And Covenants

Working capital is not an isolated metric. Movements in receivables, inventory, and payables flow directly into the cash forecast and into covenants that reference working capital or liquidity ratios.

Connect:

- forecasted changes in days metrics to the cash flow forecast;
- covenant ratios that depend on working capital, such as current ratio or leverage;
- the impact of factoring or discounting on covenant calculations;
- the seasonality of working capital against minimum cash thresholds.

Working capital management that ignores the forecast and covenants optimizes a ratio while risking a breach or a liquidity gap.

## Common Traps

### Treating Working Capital Ratios As Static

Annual average ratios hide the seasonal and segment-level dynamics that actually drive cash. A healthy average can conceal a crisis month or a trapped product line.

### Ignoring The Cash Conversion Cycle

Looking at receivable, inventory, or payable days in isolation misses how they combine. The cycle is the metric that predicts cash consumption, especially in growth.

### Taking Or Forgoing Discounts By Habit

Automatic discount behavior ignores the financing math. The implied cost of forgoing a discount is often higher than borrowing, and taking a discount can strain liquidity when cash is tight.

### Assuming Factoring Is Free Cash

Factoring and invoice discounting have real costs, recourse risk, and customer-relationship effects. Treating them as free liquidity understates the true cost of financing growth.

### Missing Overtrading In A Profitable Business

Strong sales and profit can mask a working capital drain that ends in a cash crisis. Overtrading is the failure mode most often missed because the income statement looks good.

### Stretching Suppliers As A Strategy

Delaying supplier payment improves payable days but damages relationships, loses discounts, and can trigger supply disruption. It is a short-term lever, not a sustainable strategy.

### Selecting Trade Finance By Product, Not By Problem

Choosing a letter of credit or supply chain facility because it is familiar, rather than because it fits the working capital problem, adds cost and complexity without solving the timing issue.

## Self-Check

- Has the full cash conversion cycle been calculated from receivable, inventory, and payable days, and is the trend analyzed?
- Are each component's drivers examined by customer, product, supplier, and season, not just by annual average?
- Are early-payment discounts evaluated as a financing decision against the entity's borrowing cost and liquidity?
- Are factoring and invoice discounting assessed for recourse, effective cost, advance rate, and customer impact?
- Has overtrading been diagnosed by comparing revenue growth, cycle length, cash, and short-term debt reliance?
- Are trade financing instruments selected to fit the specific working capital problem rather than by familiarity?
- Are working capital movements connected to the cash flow forecast and minimum cash thresholds?
- Do covenant ratios that depend on working capital or liquidity get tested under the working capital plan?
- Has the impact of receivable financing on covenant calculations and balance sheet presentation been considered?
- Has complex trade finance, factoring, or overtrading judgment been escalated to a qualified treasury professional?
