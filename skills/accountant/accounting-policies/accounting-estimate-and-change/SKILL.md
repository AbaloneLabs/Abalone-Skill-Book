# Accounting Estimate And Change

Many items in the financial statements are not known with certainty and must be estimated. Allowance for doubtful accounts, warranty provisions, useful lives, fair values, impairment, restructuring, and tax provisions all depend on estimates that involve judgment about uncertain future events. Estimates are not guesses; they are informed, documented judgments based on the best available information. But because they depend on assumptions about the future, they are inherently uncertain and subject to revision. A change in estimate, when new information becomes available, is normal and expected. What is not acceptable is an estimate made without a defensible basis, an estimate never revisited as conditions change, or a change characterized incorrectly as a change in policy to avoid the consequences of a prospective adjustment. The integrity of estimates depends on the rigor of their basis and the honesty of their revision.

Use this skill when developing or revising an estimate, assessing estimation uncertainty, distinguishing a change in estimate from a change in policy, or preparing disclosures. The goal is to prevent the agent from making estimates without a defensible basis, from failing to revisit estimates as conditions change, or from misclassifying a change to achieve a desired accounting result.

## Core Rules

### Build Estimates On A Documented, Defensible Basis

Every estimate must rest on a documented basis that a reviewer or auditor can understand and challenge. An estimate without a basis is an unsupported number.

Build the basis by:

- identifying the item being estimated and the uncertainty involved;
- defining the estimation method, such as historical loss rates, discounted cash flows, or expert valuation;
- documenting the key assumptions and the evidence supporting them;
- identifying the data sources and their reliability;
- recording who prepared the estimate, who reviewed it, and when.

An estimate whose basis is "management judgment" with no supporting analysis is indefensible. The basis is what makes an estimate an informed judgment rather than an assertion.

### Use The Best Available Information And Update It Each Period

Estimates should reflect the best information available at the reporting date. Stale assumptions produce inaccurate estimates.

Use current information by:

- updating assumptions with the latest actual experience and forward-looking information;
- incorporating changes in the business, market, and economic environment;
- revisiting the estimation method if the prior method no longer fits the current environment;
- comparing prior estimates to actual outcomes to identify and correct systematic bias;
- documenting the information used and why it represents the best available.

An estimate based on last year's assumptions, applied mechanically, ignores information that has since become available. The reporting date is the reference point for relevance.

### Identify And Disclose Significant Estimation Uncertainty

Some estimates involve significant uncertainty, where reasonable alternatives could produce materially different results. These must be identified and disclosed.

Identify uncertainty by:

- assessing the range of reasonably possible outcomes for each significant estimate;
- identifying assumptions that are highly sensitive or subject to significant judgment;
- disclosing the nature of the uncertainty and the assumptions, as required by the framework;
- providing sensitivity information where required or useful for user understanding;
- explaining why the estimate is uncertain and what would change it.

Users need to know which numbers are solid and which depend on uncertain assumptions. Hiding significant uncertainty behind a single point estimate misleads users about the reliability of the statements.

### Distinguish A Change In Estimate From A Change In Policy

Changes in estimate and changes in policy have different accounting treatments. Misclassification produces incorrect financial statements.

Distinguish by:

- recognizing that a change in estimate is applied prospectively, affecting current and future periods;
- recognizing that a change in policy is generally applied retrospectively, restating prior periods;
- identifying whether the change arises from new information or events, which indicates a change in estimate;
- identifying whether the change arises from a different policy choice or standard application, which indicates a change in policy;
- documenting the nature of the change and the basis for the classification.

A change in the useful life of an asset, because experience shows it was wrong, is a change in estimate applied prospectively. A change from one permitted inventory method to another is a change in policy applied retrospectively. Confusing the two produces wrong numbers in both current and prior periods.

### Apply Changes In Estimate Prospectively And Disclose Them

A change in estimate is not an error correction and is not applied retrospectively. It is applied prospectively and disclosed.

Apply prospectively by:

- adjusting the carrying amount from the date the change is applied, with the effect in current and future periods;
- not restating prior periods or adjusting opening retained earnings;
- disclosing the nature and amount of the change, and its effect on the current period;
- disclosing the effect on each affected line item and on earnings per share, if material.

Treating a change in estimate as retrospective, or failing to disclose it, misrepresents the nature of the change and its effect on comparability.

### Watch For Bias And Earnings Management Through Estimates

Estimates are a common vehicle for earnings management, because they involve judgment and affect reported results. Be alert to bias.

Watch for:

- estimates that consistently produce favorable results or smooth earnings;
- estimates that change conveniently around thresholds, such as bonus targets or analyst expectations;
- estimates that are always revised in the same direction, suggesting systematic bias;
- estimates made by the same person who benefits from the result, without independent review;
- estimates that are aggressive or conservative without a defensible basis.

Estimates should be unbiased best estimates, not tools for achieving a desired result. Independent review and documentation of the basis are the controls against biased estimation.

### Revisit Estimates When Triggering Events Occur

Estimates should be revisited not only at period end but when events suggest the estimate may no longer be appropriate.

Revisit estimates when:

- actual experience diverges significantly from the estimate;
- market, economic, or regulatory conditions change materially;
- specific events occur, such as litigation, restructuring, or asset impairment indicators;
- new information becomes available that affects the key assumptions;
- the business or its strategy changes in a way that affects the estimate.

An estimate that is set at acquisition and never revisited, despite changing conditions, is an abandoned estimate. Triggering events require revisiting.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

Accounting estimates and changes in estimate involve complex, framework-specific, and jurisdiction-specific judgment that is the professional domain of qualified accountants and auditors. Estimation method selection, uncertainty assessment, change classification, and disclosure require professional expertise and often involve consultation with auditors and valuation specialists. This skill provides awareness of what to consider, not definitive accounting conclusions. Confirm all significant estimates, changes, and disclosures with qualified accounting professionals in the relevant jurisdiction. Do not treat any estimation guidance as authoritative; the standards, professional judgment, and specialist input govern.

## Common Traps

### Estimate Without A Defensible Basis

An estimate whose only basis is management judgment, with no supporting analysis, is indefensible.

### Stale Assumptions Applied Mechanically

An estimate based on outdated assumptions ignores information that has since become available.

### Hidden Estimation Uncertainty

Failing to identify and disclose significant uncertainty misleads users about the reliability of the statements.

### Misclassifying Change In Estimate As Change In Policy

Confusing a prospective change in estimate with a retrospective change in policy produces wrong numbers in both periods.

### Change In Estimate Not Disclosed

Failing to disclose the nature and effect of a change in estimate misrepresents its effect on comparability.

### Biased Estimation For Earnings Management

Estimates that consistently smooth earnings or conveniently meet thresholds suggest manipulation.

### Estimates Never Revisited After Triggering Events

An estimate set once and never revisited, despite changing conditions, is an abandoned estimate.

### Estimation Guidance Treated As Authoritative

Estimates require professional expertise and often specialist input; confirm significant decisions with qualified professionals.

## Self-Check

- Is each estimate built on a documented, defensible basis with method, assumptions, evidence, data sources, and approval?
- Are estimates updated each period with the best available information, incorporating actual experience and forward-looking data?
- Is significant estimation uncertainty identified, with the range of outcomes and sensitive assumptions disclosed?
- Are changes in estimate correctly distinguished from changes in policy, with the classification documented?
- Are changes in estimate applied prospectively and disclosed with their nature, amount, and effect on affected line items?
- Are estimates reviewed for bias and earnings management, with independent review and documentation of the basis?
- Are estimates revisited when triggering events occur, such as divergent actuals, market changes, or specific events?
- Are all significant estimates, changes, and disclosures confirmed with qualified accounting professionals in the relevant jurisdiction?
