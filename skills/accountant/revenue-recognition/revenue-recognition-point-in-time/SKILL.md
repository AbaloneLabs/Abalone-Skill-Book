# Revenue Recognition Point In Time

When a performance obligation is satisfied at a point in time, revenue is recognized in a single moment when control of the asset transfers to the customer. The question is not when the invoice is issued, when cash is collected, or when the product ships. It is when the customer obtains control, meaning the ability to direct the use of and obtain substantially all the remaining benefits of the asset. Because this single moment can fall in one period or the next, and because it shifts revenue, margin, and profit, the determination is heavily scrutinized. An agent that recognizes revenue on shipment when control passes on delivery accelerates revenue improperly; one that waits for cash when control passed long ago defers it.

Use this skill before setting a point-in-time recognition policy, before evaluating a specific transaction, while reviewing bill-and-hold or consignment arrangements, while determining principal versus agent timing, or while auditing cutoff. The goal is to make the agent determine the transfer of control from evidence rather than from billing convenience, and to prevent period-end manipulation through timing choices.

Revenue recognition standards vary by jurisdiction and reporting framework. The dominant converged frameworks are US GAAP ASC 606 and IFRS 15, which share the control-transfer approach and the same set of control indicators, but entities may also face industry-specific guidance, local GAAP, tax-basis rules, or sector regulation. Do not state a single universal rule. Where the timing is judgmental or material, especially near a period boundary, route the question to a qualified accountant or auditor. This skill offers operational judgment, not a definitive accounting conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Determine Control Transfer From Evidence, Not From Billing Events

Control is the ability to direct the use of, and obtain substantially all the remaining benefits from, the asset. It is a substantive economic concept, and the moment it transfers must be determined from facts. Billing, shipping, and cash collection are administrative events that may or may not coincide with control transfer.

Anchor the determination to:

- the substantive terms of the contract;
- the entity's customary business practice;
- physical and legal realities of the transaction;
- the relative weight of the control indicators.

When billing occurs before control transfers, the amount is a contract liability or deposit, not revenue. When control transfers before billing, the amount is a contract asset or unbilled receivable, not deferred revenue. Never let the invoice drive the accounting.

### Apply The Control Indicators Collectively And In Context

No single indicator is determinative. The frameworks provide a set of indicators to assess which party possesses control, and the entity must weigh them in the context of the specific arrangement. Treat the indicators as evidence to be weighed, not as a checklist where one tick decides the outcome.

The indicators include:

- the entity has a present right to payment for the asset;
- the customer has legal title to the asset;
- the customer has physical possession of the asset;
- the customer has the significant risks and rewards of ownership;
- the customer has accepted the asset.

In some arrangements legal title transfers early but risks and rewards remain with the entity until acceptance; in others, possession passes but the entity retains the right to redirect the goods. Weigh all relevant indicators together and document the reasoning.

### Evaluate Present Right To Payment And Risks And Rewards Together

Two indicators deserve particular attention because they often decide close cases. The present right to payment asks whether the entity is entitled to consideration now, conditional only on the passage of time, regardless of future events. Risks and rewards of ownership asks which party bears the upside and downside of the asset after transfer.

Assess:

- does the entity have a right to payment even if the asset is damaged or destroyed?
- does the customer bear loss if the asset declines in value, and gain if it appreciates?
- is there a substantive acceptance provision that delays the right to payment?
- are there return rights so broad that the customer has not effectively assumed the risks?

If the entity retains significant risks and rewards, or lacks a present right to payment, control has likely not transferred even if other indicators point the other way.

### Scrutinize Customer Acceptance Provisions

Acceptance can be the moment control transfers, but only if the acceptance provision is substantive. A boilerplate acceptance clause that the entity historically bypasses does not delay control. A genuine inspection or testing requirement that the customer can fail does.

Distinguish:

- substantive acceptance, where the customer evaluates the asset against defined criteria and can reject it, which delays control until acceptance;
- non-substantive acceptance, where acceptance is administrative or perfunctory, which does not delay control;
- acceptance based on subjective aesthetic judgments, which may or may not be substantive depending on the contract.

When acceptance is substantive, revenue is recognized only after acceptance occurs, not on delivery.

### Handle Bill-And-Hold Arrangements Under Strict Conditions

In a bill-and-hold arrangement, the entity bills the customer but retains physical possession until a later date. Revenue may be recognized at billing only if control has transferred despite the entity holding the goods. This is a high-risk area because it lets the entity recognize revenue while still holding inventory.

Before recognizing bill-and-hold revenue, confirm:

- the reason for the bill-and-hold is substantive and business-driven, not accounting-driven;
- the product is identified separately and ready for transfer;
- the product is committed to the customer and cannot be redirected;
- the customer has control, including the ability to direct use and the risks of ownership;
- delivery is scheduled for a definite date.

If any condition is absent, the goods remain the entity's inventory and no revenue is recognized. Bill-and-hold used to pull revenue into a period is a classic cutoff manipulation.

### Treat Consignment As Not A Sale Until Control Transfers

In a consignment arrangement, the entity transfers goods to a dealer or agent who will sell them to end customers, but control has not necessarily passed. Revenue is recognized only when the consignee sells to the end customer or when control otherwise transfers, not when goods move to the consignee.

Confirm:

- the entity retains control and the significant risks and rewards until sale to the end customer;
- the consignee has no unconditional obligation to pay;
- the entity can require return or redirect the goods;
- inventory on consignment remains on the entity's books.

Recognizing revenue on shipment to a consignee overstates revenue and removes inventory improperly.

### Align Principal Versus Agent Timing With Control

For principal versus agent determinations, the timing of recognition also depends on control. A principal recognizes revenue gross when it controls the specified good or service before transfer to the customer. An agent recognizes revenue net, its commission, when the other party provides the good or service. The timing follows the substance of who controls the asset at the relevant moment.

Where the entity is an agent, revenue is the net amount retained, recognized as the underlying performance occurs, not the gross amount billed to the customer.

## Common Traps

### Recognizing Revenue On Shipment When Control Passes On Delivery

Shipping is a logistics event. If the customer bears no risk during transit, cannot direct use, and has a right of return, control has not transferred at shipment. Recognizing on shipment accelerates revenue improperly.

### Treating Billing Or Cash Collection As The Trigger

Billing and cash are financing and administrative events. They create receivables, contract assets, or contract liabilities, but they do not by themselves transfer control. Revenue follows control, not the invoice or the bank deposit.

### Relying On A Single Indicator In Isolation

Legal title can transfer while risks and rewards remain with the entity. Possession can pass while acceptance is still pending. Deciding control from one indicator while ignoring contradicting evidence produces the wrong timing.

### Using Bill-And-Hold To Pull Revenue Into A Period

Bill-and-hold is legitimate only when control has genuinely transferred. Using it to recognize revenue while still holding inventory, without a substantive business reason, is a cutoff manipulation that auditors target.

### Recognizing Consignment Shipments As Sales

Goods sent on consignment are usually still the entity's inventory. Recognizing revenue when goods move to the consignee overstates revenue and understates inventory until the end-customer sale.

### Treating Boilerplate Acceptance As Substantive

A perfunctory acceptance clause the entity never enforces does not delay control. Conversely, ignoring a genuine inspection requirement recognizes revenue before the customer has control. Match the accounting to the substance of the acceptance provision.

### Ignoring Broad Return Rights

If the customer can return the goods for any reason and the entity retains the risks, control may not have transferred. Broad return rights can mean the customer has not assumed the risks and rewards of ownership.

## Self-Check

- Is revenue recognized at the moment control transfers, determined from substantive evidence rather than billing or shipping events?
- Have all relevant control indicators been weighed together in context, with no single indicator treated as determinative?
- Are present right to payment and risks and rewards of ownership assessed to confirm which party holds control?
- Where acceptance provisions exist, is the accounting matched to whether acceptance is substantive or non-substantive?
- For bill-and-hold arrangements, are all conditions met, including a substantive business reason and the customer's control over the goods?
- Are consignment shipments kept on the entity's books until control transfers to the end customer?
- For principal versus agent, is the timing and gross or net presentation aligned with who controls the asset at the relevant moment?
- Are period-end transactions near the cutoff tested for the correct timing rather than recognized for convenience?
- Does the analysis identify the applicable reporting framework and recommend qualified professional review where timing is judgmental or material?
