# Standalone Selling Price Allocation

Once performance obligations are identified and the transaction price is determined, the price must be allocated to each obligation based on its standalone selling price. This allocation determines how much revenue attaches to each stream and, consequently, when and how that revenue is recognized. Allocation is not a pro-rata or equal split; it reflects the relative standalone value of each obligation. Errors here misstate the revenue attached to each obligation, which in turn distorts timing, margin, and the pattern of recognition. An obligation that should carry little price may be over-allocated and recognized too slowly, or under-allocated and recognized too quickly.

Use this skill before allocating a transaction price, while estimating standalone selling prices, while allocating discounts or variable consideration, while reviewing allocation changes, or while auditing the allocation. The goal is to make the agent allocate based on a defensible standalone selling price rather than an arbitrary or convenient split, and to prevent estimation methods from being chosen to produce a desired revenue pattern.

Revenue recognition standards vary by jurisdiction and reporting framework. The dominant converged frameworks are US GAAP ASC 606 and IFRS 15, which share the allocation approach and the standalone selling price estimation methods, but entities may also be subject to industry-specific guidance, local GAAP, tax-basis rules, or sector regulation. Do not state a single universal rule. Where the allocation involves significant estimation or affects material revenue, route the question to a qualified accountant or auditor. This skill offers operational judgment, not a definitive accounting opinion.

## Core Rules

### Allocate Based On Standalone Selling Price, Not Pro-Rata Or Equal Split

The transaction price is allocated to each performance obligation in proportion to its standalone selling price relative to the sum of standalone selling prices of all obligations. This relative allocation reflects the economic value of each obligation, not an equal or arbitrary division.

Confirm:

- each performance obligation has a standalone selling price, even if not sold separately;
- the allocation uses the relative standalone selling price method;
- the sum of allocated amounts equals the total transaction price.

Equal splits, pro-rata splits by cost, or allocations based on invoice line items are not substitutes for standalone selling price allocation unless they happen to reflect the same result.

### Establish Standalone Selling Price From The Best Available Evidence

The standalone selling price is the price at which the entity would sell a promised good or service separately to a customer. The best evidence is an observable price, the price the entity charges for the same good or service when sold separately in similar circumstances and to similar customers.

When an observable price exists, use it. Observable prices are preferred because they reflect actual market transactions rather than estimates. Document the source and the basis for concluding the price is comparable.

### Use An Estimation Method When Observable Price Is Unavailable

When the entity does not sell the good or service separately, or no observable price exists, estimate the standalone selling price using an appropriate method. The framework allows several approaches, and the entity should choose the method that best reflects the price the entity would charge.

Acceptable estimation methods include:

- adjusted market assessment, evaluating the market in which the entity sells the good or service and estimating the price a customer would pay;
- expected cost plus margin approach, forecasting expected costs and adding an appropriate margin;
- residual approach, using the total transaction price less the sum of observable or reliably estimated prices of other obligations, permitted only in limited circumstances.

The residual approach is restricted. It may be used only where one or more, but not all, of the standalone selling prices are highly variable or uncertain, and only for those uncertain obligations. Do not use residual as a default convenience. Apply estimation methods consistently to similar obligations and document the data, assumptions, and calculations.

### Allocate Discounts To The Obligations They Relate To

When the sum of standalone selling prices exceeds the transaction price, a discount exists. The discount must be allocated to the performance obligations in proportion to their standalone selling prices, unless there is observable evidence that the discount relates to only some of the obligations.

Allocate the discount:

- proportionally across all obligations as a default;
- only to specific obligations when observable evidence shows the discount relates solely to them, such as a bundle price that applies only to a defined subset.

Evidence that a discount relates to specific obligations must be observable, not inferred. Allocating a discount to the obligations that produce the most favorable revenue pattern, without evidence, misstates the allocation.

### Allocate Variable Consideration According To Its Association

Variable consideration and subsequent changes in the transaction price are allocated to the performance obligations to which they relate. The allocation depends on whether the variable amount relates to specific obligations or to the contract as a whole.

Allocate variable consideration:

- entirely to a specific performance obligation when the variable terms relate specifically to that obligation;
- across all obligations proportionally when the variable terms relate to the entire contract.

When a discount or variable consideration relates entirely to one or more specific obligations, allocate it only to those obligations. Misallocating variable consideration spreads the effect across obligations that did not cause it, distorting the revenue attached to each.

### Handle Changes In Standalone Selling Price Consistently

Standalone selling prices can change over time as markets, costs, or the entity's pricing evolve. The allocation is generally based on standalone selling prices at contract inception and is not remeasured simply because prices change later. However, changes in the transaction price after inception, such as revised variable consideration, are allocated based on the same relative standalone selling prices unless the change relates to specific obligations.

Maintain:

- standalone selling prices at contract inception for allocation;
- a documented basis for any reestimation of variable consideration allocated after inception;
- consistency of estimation method across periods.

Avoid reallocating past amounts simply because current standalone prices have moved, unless a modification or the framework specifically requires it.

### Document The Allocation With Traceable Support

Allocation is a calculation that auditors will reconstruct. Each step should be traceable from the transaction price to the standalone selling prices to the allocated amounts.

Document:

- the standalone selling price of each obligation and its source or estimation method;
- the discount, if any, and how it was allocated;
- the variable consideration, if any, and how it was allocated;
- the calculation showing the sum of allocated amounts equals the transaction price;
- any observable evidence supporting allocation to specific obligations.

An allocation that cannot be traced cannot be defended.

## Common Traps

### Allocating Equally Or Pro-Rata Instead Of By Standalone Selling Price

Equal or cost-based splits do not reflect the relative value of obligations. Only relative standalone selling price allocation is correct unless it happens to produce the same result.

### Defaulting To The Residual Approach For Convenience

Residual is permitted only for highly variable or uncertain standalone prices, and only for those obligations. Using it as a default for all obligations misstates the allocation and is not allowed.

### Allocating Discounts To Favored Obligations Without Evidence

Directing a discount to obligations that produce a desired revenue pattern, without observable evidence that the discount relates to them, misstates allocation. Evidence must be observable, not inferred.

### Spreading Variable Consideration Across All Obligations When It Relates To One

When variable terms relate specifically to one obligation, spreading the effect across all obligations misallocates revenue. Allocate variable consideration according to what it actually relates to.

### Remeasuring Allocation Because Current Prices Changed

Standalone prices at inception govern the allocation. Reallocating past amounts because current prices moved, unless a modification requires it, distorts comparative revenue.

### Using Invoice Line Items As A Proxy For Standalone Selling Price

Invoice line items reflect billing choices, not necessarily standalone value. They are acceptable only if they genuinely reflect the price the entity would charge separately.

### Leaving The Allocation Unsupported And Untraceable

An allocation without documented standalone prices, estimation methods, and calculations cannot be reconstructed or defended. Auditors test allocation, and unsupported allocations fail.

## Self-Check

- Is the transaction price allocated in proportion to each obligation's relative standalone selling price, not equally or pro-rata?
- Where an observable standalone price exists, is it used in preference to an estimate, with its source documented?
- Where estimation is needed, is the method, adjusted market assessment, expected cost plus margin, or residual, appropriate and consistently applied?
- Is the residual approach used only for highly variable or uncertain standalone prices, and only for those obligations?
- Are discounts allocated proportionally unless observable evidence shows they relate to specific obligations?
- Is variable consideration allocated to the obligations it relates to, rather than spread across all obligations by default?
- Is the allocation based on standalone selling prices at inception, without improper remeasurement when current prices change?
- Does the sum of allocated amounts reconcile to the total transaction price, and is the full calculation traceable?
- Does the analysis identify the applicable reporting framework and recommend qualified professional review where allocation is judgmental or material?
