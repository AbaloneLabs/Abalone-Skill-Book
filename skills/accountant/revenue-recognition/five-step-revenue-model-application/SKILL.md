# Five-Step Revenue Model Application

Revenue recognition under the converged framework (ASC 606 for US GAAP and IFRS 15 for international standards) is organized around a five-step model. The steps appear mechanical, but the real difficulty is judgment: whether a contract qualifies, whether goods or services are distinct, when control transfers, and how an uncertain or variable amount should be reflected. Agents often produce answers that look defensible because each step has a label, yet the model breaks down when a step is applied out of order or when a decision in one step silently contradicts a decision in another.

Use this skill before concluding how a customer arrangement should be recognized. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating the five steps as a checklist to fill in, and instead force it to gather the right contract evidence, surface the judgment calls, and flag where professional determination is required.

This guidance is general and educational. Revenue recognition standards vary by jurisdiction, by the reporting framework the entity has adopted, and by industry-specific interpretations and regulator guidance. Final determinations on any specific contract should be made by a qualified accounting professional with access to the full contract and the entity's accounting policies. This skill does not provide legal, tax, or audit advice and is not a substitute for the governing standard text.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Steps In Sequence And Keep Them Consistent

The five steps are identifying the contract, identifying performance obligations, determining the transaction price, allocating the transaction price, and recognizing revenue as each obligation is satisfied. Although the model is presented as a sequence, the steps are interdependent. A decision in one step can invalidate an assumption made in another.

Before finalizing any conclusion, walk through all five steps and confirm they are internally consistent. For example, if you decide an item is a separate performance obligation, that decision affects how the transaction price is allocated and when revenue is recognized for that item. If you later treat the item as part of a combined obligation, the allocation and timing conclusions must change to match.

### Confirm A Contract Actually Exists

Revenue cannot be recognized without a contract that meets the criteria. A signed document is not automatically a contract for this purpose, and a verbal or customary arrangement may qualify if the evidence supports it.

Check whether:

- the parties have been approved and are committed to perform;
- each party's rights regarding the goods or services are identifiable;
- payment terms are identifiable;
- the contract has commercial substance;
- it is probable the entity will collect the consideration.

The collection probability test is a frequent source of error. It is not the same as a credit-loss estimate. It asks whether collection of the amount the entity is entitled to is probable. If collection is not probable at inception, the arrangement may not be a recognized contract, and the treatment differs from a normal revenue transaction. When the collection assessment is borderline, escalate rather than guess.

### Identify Performance Obligations Based On Distinctness

A performance obligation is a promise to transfer a distinct good or service, or a series of distinct goods or services that are substantially the same and transferred in the same pattern. The key judgment is whether the good or service is distinct both capable of being distinct and distinct within the context of the contract.

Ask:

- Can the customer benefit from the good or service on its own or with readily available resources?
- Is the promise separately identifiable from other promises, or is it significantly integrated, customized, highly interdependent, or highly interrelated with other promises?

Bundling is common. Installation, software licenses, ongoing updates, training, and support may each be separate obligations or may be one combined obligation depending on how they function together. Do not assume a line item on an invoice equals one performance obligation, and do not assume a single contract equals one obligation.

### Determine The Transaction Price Including Variable And Noncash Consideration

The transaction price is the amount the entity expects to be entitled to in exchange for transferring the goods or services. This is not always the stated contract price. It must reflect variable consideration, significant financing components, noncash consideration, and consideration payable to the customer.

For variable consideration, estimate the amount using either the expected value or the most likely amount method, depending on which better predicts the outcome. Then apply the constraint: include variable consideration only to the extent it is highly probable a significant reversal will not occur. The constraint is a real judgment and is often applied too loosely.

Flag these common price components:

- discounts, rebates, refunds, and credits;
- performance bonuses and penalties;
- contingent payments and milestone payments;
- consideration payable to the customer that is not in exchange for a distinct good or service;
- noncash consideration measured at fair value or contract value depending on timing;
- the effect of a significant financing component when applicable.

### Allocate The Price On A Relative Standalone Selling Price Basis

Unless there is a direct, observable allocation method, allocate the transaction price to each performance obligation based on its relative standalone selling price. Standalone selling price is the price at which the entity would sell the good or service separately to a customer. When it is not directly observable, it must be estimated, and the method used should be documented.

Discounts and variable amounts generally allocate across all obligations unless there is observable evidence that they relate to one or more specific obligations. Do not assign a discount to a single item simply because it is convenient; the allocation must be supported by evidence.

### Recognize Revenue When Or As Control Transfers

Revenue is recognized when control of the promised good or service transfers to the customer. Control can transfer at a point in time or over time. Over-time recognition requires meeting at least one specific criterion, such as the customer simultaneously receiving and consuming benefits, the entity creating or enhancing an asset the customer controls, or the entity having no alternative use plus an enforceable right to payment for performance to date.

If none of the over-time criteria are met, revenue is recognized at a point in time. Indicators of control transfer include legal title, physical possession, customer acceptance, present right to payment, and risks and rewards of ownership. Do not default to over-time recognition just because performance spans multiple periods.

### Document The Significant Judgments

Many revenue conclusions depend on estimates and judgments that are not obvious from the contract face. Document the inputs, the method, and the rationale. This includes standalone selling price estimation methods, variable consideration estimates and constraints, the measure of progress for over-time recognition, and the reasons a contract does or does not qualify.

Documentation protects the entity during audit, supports consistent application across similar contracts, and makes the conclusion reviewable. An undocumented judgment is fragile even when it is correct.

### Reassess When Facts Change

Contracts are modified, prices are adjusted, and obligations change. A contract modification may be treated as a separate contract, as a termination of the old contract and creation of a new one, or as a cumulative catch-up adjustment, depending on the nature of the additional goods or services and their pricing. Reassess the steps when the arrangement changes rather than carrying forward stale assumptions.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Signed Document As Automatically Qualifying

A signed agreement does not by itself satisfy the contract criteria. If payment terms are unclear, commercial substance is absent, or collection is not probable, the arrangement may not qualify as a contract. Treating every signed document as a contract can lead to premature revenue recognition and understated credit risk.

### Applying The Collection Test As A Bad-Debt Estimate

The probable collection threshold is not the same as an expected credit loss allowance. An entity can recognize revenue and also record an expected credit loss, but only if collection of the consideration the entity is entitled to is probable at contract inception. Conflating the two tests understates the risk of recognizing revenue that should be deferred or treated differently.

### Overlooking Variable Consideration Or Applying The Constraint Too Loosely

Variable consideration is easy to miss when it is buried in discount tiers, rebate programs, or contingent milestones. Even when identified, agents often include the full expected value without honestly testing whether a significant revenue reversal is highly probable. The constraint exists precisely because optimistic estimates reverse.

### Defaulting To Over-Time Recognition For Long Contracts

Length of performance does not determine the pattern of recognition. A long engagement may still be point-in-time if the customer does not receive benefit continuously, the entity has an alternative use for the asset, or there is no enforceable right to payment for work completed to date. Defaulting to over-time because the work takes months distorts revenue timing.

### Assuming Invoice Lines Map To Performance Obligations

Invoices are billing instruments, not accounting conclusions. One invoice line can contain several obligations, and several lines can collapse into one obligation. Mapping accounting directly to invoice structure without analyzing distinctness produces allocation and timing errors.

### Allocating Discounts By Convenience

Assigning an entire discount to the largest line item, or to the item with the most observable price, is only correct when supported by evidence. Without observable evidence that a discount relates to specific obligations, it should generally be allocated proportionally. Convenience allocations are a common audit finding.

### Ignoring Contract Modifications

When scope or price changes, the original conclusions may no longer hold. Continuing to apply the initial transaction price, performance obligations, and measure of progress after a modification creates cumulative errors that grow over the life of the contract.

### Forgetting The Significant Financing Component

When payment timing differs substantially from delivery timing, a significant financing component may need to be separated from the transaction price. This is often missed in multi-year prepaid arrangements, long-term construction contracts, and extended payment terms. Ignoring it misstates both revenue and interest.

## Self-Check

- Have all five steps been applied, and are the conclusions in each step internally consistent with the others?
- Is there documented evidence that the arrangement meets the contract criteria, including the probable collection test applied correctly rather than as a bad-debt estimate?
- Have all performance obligations been identified based on distinctness, rather than assumed from invoice lines?
- Does the transaction price reflect variable consideration, noncash consideration, consideration payable to the customer, and any significant financing component?
- Has the variable consideration constraint been applied honestly, testing whether a significant reversal is highly probable?
- Is the transaction price allocated using relative standalone selling prices, with any discount or variable amount allocation supported by evidence rather than convenience?
- Is the point-in-time versus over-time determination based on the control transfer criteria, not on the length of the contract?
- Have contract modifications been reassessed rather than carried forward under stale assumptions?
- Are significant judgments, estimation methods, and measures of progress documented so a reviewer can follow the reasoning?
- Has the conclusion been flagged for review by a qualified accounting professional where the judgment is material, borderline, or outside the entity's established policy?
