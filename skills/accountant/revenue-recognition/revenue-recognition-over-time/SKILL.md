# Revenue Recognition Over Time

Some performance obligations are satisfied over time, meaning revenue is recognized progressively as the entity performs. Others are satisfied at a point in time, meaning revenue is recognized all at once when control transfers. The choice is not a matter of preference or smoothing. It is a rules-based determination driven by specific criteria about how the customer receives and controls the benefit. Getting it wrong moves revenue between periods, distorts margins, and can mislead users about when performance actually occurs. An obligation recognized over time when it should be point-in-time front-loads revenue that has not been earned; the reverse defers revenue that has already been earned.

Use this skill before deciding the recognition pattern for a performance obligation, before selecting a measure-of-progress method, while building or reviewing percentage-of-completion schedules, while evaluating whether a customer controls an asset as it is created, or while auditing over-time revenue for accuracy. The goal is to make the agent test the criteria rigorously rather than defaulting to whichever method produces the most convenient number, and to prevent unreliable progress measures from driving material revenue.

Revenue recognition standards vary by jurisdiction and reporting framework. The dominant converged frameworks are US GAAP ASC 606 and IFRS 15, which share the same three over-time criteria, but entities may also be subject to industry-specific guidance, local GAAP, tax-basis rules, or sector regulation that changes the outcome. Do not state a single universal rule. Where the determination is judgmental or material, route the question to a qualified accountant or auditor. This skill provides operational judgment, not a definitive accounting opinion.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Over-Time Criteria Strictly And In Order

Revenue is recognized over time only if at least one of three criteria is met. The burden is on meeting a criterion; if none is met, recognition is at a point in time by default. Test each criterion against the substance of the arrangement.

The three criteria are:

- the customer simultaneously receives and consumes the benefits provided as the entity performs;
- the entity's performance creates or enhances an asset that the customer controls as it is created or enhanced;
- the entity's performance does not create an asset with an alternative use to the entity, and the entity has an enforceable right to payment for performance completed to date.

The first criterion typically applies to routine or recurring services. The second applies when the customer controls the work-in-process. The third is the construction-style test requiring both no alternative use and a right to payment. Document which criterion is met and why, because auditors test this determination heavily.

### Judge Simultaneous Receipt And Consumption Carefully

The first criterion, simultaneous receipt and consumption, is often misapplied. It is not enough that the service is performed over a period; the customer must benefit from each increment of work as it is done, such that another entity would not need to re-perform the work already done.

Indicators that this criterion is met include:

- routine or repetitive services such as cleaning, security, or standard servicing;
- services where another provider could continue seamlessly from the point of handover;
- the customer getting value continuously rather than only at completion.

Indicators that it is not met include:

- design or consulting work whose value emerges only at completion;
- deliverables that require rework if a new provider took over;
- performance whose benefit is realized only upon delivery of a final output.

When uncertain, consider whether another entity would have to substantially redo the work. If yes, the criterion likely fails.

### Assess Whether The Customer Controls The Asset As Created

The second criterion asks whether the customer controls the asset while the entity is creating or enhancing it. Control is more than legal title; it is the ability to direct the use of and obtain substantially all the remaining benefits.

Evidence the customer controls the asset as created includes:

- the customer owns or holds legal title to the work-in-process;
- the customer has the practical ability to direct how the asset is used;
- the customer can sell or pledge the asset;
- the customer bears the significant risks and rewards of the asset during creation.

If the entity controls the work-in-process until delivery, this criterion fails and the obligation is likely point-in-time unless the third criterion applies.

### Apply The No-Alternative-Use And Right-To-Payment Test Together

The third criterion requires both conditions simultaneously. Many agents check only one. Both must hold for the criterion to be met.

No alternative use means the entity is not practically able to redirect the asset to another customer, for example because of design specificity or contractual restrictions that are enforceable. Right to payment means the entity has an enforceable right to compensation for performance completed to date, not merely a right to a deposit or a penalty, and the amount must cover at least the entity's costs plus a reasonable profit margin.

Check:

- could the entity practically sell the asset to someone else without major rework?
- does the contract give a right to payment for work done if the customer terminates for convenience?
- does the payment right cover costs plus margin, or only reimbursement of costs?

If the asset has an alternative use, or the right to payment is weak or limited to cost reimbursement, the criterion fails.

### Choose A Measure Of Progress That Faithfully Depicts Performance

Once over-time recognition applies, the entity must measure its progress toward completion using a method that depicts the transfer of control. The method should be applied consistently and should reflect the actual pattern of performance, not merely the most convenient or flattering number.

Input methods measure the entity's efforts, such as:

- cost-to-cost, based on costs incurred relative to total estimated costs;
- labor hours expended relative to total estimated hours;
- machine hours or production inputs consumed;
- time elapsed, when time is a faithful proxy for performance.

Output methods measure results delivered to the customer, such as:

- units delivered or milestones completed;
- surveys of performance performed or appraisals of results achieved;
- direct measurement of the value transferred.

Choose the method that best reflects how control transfers. If costs are incurred early but value transfers late, an input method will misstate progress. If outputs are discrete and measurable, an output method is usually more faithful.

### Estimate Total Cost And Total Revenue Realistically And Update Each Period

Over-time recognition depends on estimates of total cost and total revenue for the full obligation. These estimates must be realistic, supported, and updated every reporting period as conditions change. Stale estimates produce cumulative adjustments that can distort a single period dramatically.

Maintain:

- a current estimate of total costs to complete, with supporting detail;
- a current estimate of total transaction price, including variable consideration;
- identification of costs that should be excluded from the measure of progress because they do not depict performance, such as uninstalled materials or upfront costs;
- a documented basis for each significant assumption;
- a comparison of prior estimates to actual outcomes to test reliability.

When estimates change, the cumulative catch-up is recognized in the period of change. This is required, not optional, but it signals that estimation discipline matters.

### Recognize And Disclose Contract Losses And Onerous Obligations Promptly

If the estimated total cost of an over-time obligation exceeds the transaction price, a loss is expected. The loss must generally be recognized in full when it becomes evident, not spread over the remaining performance period. Ignoring a looming loss until completion overstates revenue and profit in the interim.

Also consider whether the obligation is onerous under the applicable framework, which may require a separate provision. Flag expected losses and onerous positions to a qualified professional for proper treatment and disclosure.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Over-Time Because The Service Lasts A Long Time

Duration alone does not justify over-time recognition. A year-long consulting engagement that delivers value only at completion may be point-in-time. Always test the criteria rather than assuming length equals over-time.

### Using Cost Incurred As A Proxy Without Testing Whether It Depicts Performance

Cost-to-cost is popular but unreliable when costs are front-loaded, when materials sit uninstalled, or when inefficiency drives cost without driving progress. A method that rewards the entity for spending rather than delivering misstates revenue.

### Checking Only One Of The Two Third-Criterion Conditions

The no-alternative-use and right-to-payment conditions are conjunctive. Confirming only that the asset is custom ignores whether the entity can bill for work done. Confirming only a termination clause ignores whether the asset has an alternative use.

### Treating A Weak Right To Payment As Sufficient

A right to a deposit, a penalty, or mere cost reimbursement is generally not an enforceable right to payment for performance to date. The right must cover costs plus a reasonable margin to satisfy the criterion.

### Failing To Update Estimates, Then Absorbing Large Catch-Ups Silently

Stale estimates create large cumulative adjustments that look like sudden performance swings. Updating estimates each period and documenting the changes is what makes over-time revenue defensible.

### Ignoring Expected Losses To Avoid A Hit

Recognizing an expected contract loss only at completion overstates interim profit. Losses must be recognized when evident, and the onerous position assessed for additional provisions.

### Switching Measure-Of-Progress Methods To Smooth Results

Changing from cost-to-cost to units-delivered, or vice versa, to avoid an unfavorable number is a change in accounting policy that requires disclosure and justification, not a quiet adjustment. Consistency of method is expected unless the change better depicts performance.

## Self-Check

- Have you tested each of the three over-time criteria against the substance of the arrangement rather than assuming over-time based on duration?
- Where simultaneous receipt and consumption is claimed, would another entity need to re-perform the work, or could it continue seamlessly?
- Where customer control of the asset as created is claimed, does the customer direct use, bear risks, and obtain benefits during creation?
- For the third criterion, are both no alternative use and an enforceable right to payment for costs plus margin satisfied?
- Does the chosen measure of progress faithfully depict the transfer of control, and is it applied consistently?
- Are total cost and total revenue estimates realistic, supported, documented, and updated each reporting period?
- Are costs that do not depict performance, such as uninstalled materials, excluded from the input measure?
- Are expected contract losses recognized when evident, and is the onerous position assessed for provisions and disclosure?
- Does the analysis identify the applicable reporting framework and recommend qualified professional review where the determination is judgmental or material?
