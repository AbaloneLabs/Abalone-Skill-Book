# Contract Identification And Combining

Revenue recognition begins with a contract, but not every agreement, purchase order, or handshake qualifies as a contract for accounting purposes. Before any performance obligation is identified or price is allocated, the entity must confirm that a contract exists under the relevant criteria, decide whether separate agreements should be treated as one, and handle modifications correctly. These determinations sit at the very front of the revenue model, and errors here cascade through every later step. A contract that should have been combined with another produces fragmented obligations and misallocated price; a modification treated as a new contract when it should be a cumulative catch-up shifts revenue between periods and distorts margins.

Use this skill before identifying performance obligations, while evaluating whether a signed agreement qualifies, while deciding whether related agreements are one arrangement, while accounting for a change to an existing contract, or while auditing contract existence and combination. The goal is to make the agent test the existence and combination criteria deliberately rather than assuming a signed document is automatically in scope, and to prevent modifications from being misclassified in ways that distort revenue.

Revenue recognition standards vary by jurisdiction and reporting framework. The dominant converged frameworks are US GAAP ASC 606 and IFRS 15, which share the same contract existence criteria and combination guidance, but entities may also be subject to industry-specific guidance, local GAAP, tax-basis rules, or sector regulation. In addition, some arrangements fall under other standards entirely, such as leases, insurance, or financial instruments, and must be scoped out of the revenue standard. Do not state a single universal rule. Where the determination is judgmental or material, route the question to a qualified accountant or auditor. This skill provides operational judgment, not a definitive accounting opinion.

## Core Rules

### Confirm All Contract Existence Criteria Before Recognizing Revenue

A contract exists for revenue recognition only when all of the following criteria are met. The entity must confirm each, not assume them from the presence of a signature.

The criteria are:

- the parties have approved the contract and are committed to perform their obligations;
- each party's rights regarding the goods or services to be transferred are identifiable;
- the payment terms are identifiable;
- the contract has commercial substance, meaning the entity's future cash flows change as a result;
- it is probable that the entity will collect the consideration to which it will be entitled in exchange for the goods or services.

If any criterion fails, the contract does not fall within the revenue standard in its usual form, and the entity must follow alternative guidance, typically recognizing consideration received as a deposit or as revenue only when certain conditions are met. Document the assessment of each criterion, because the existence determination is the gate to everything that follows.

### Assess Collectibility As Probable Collection Of The Entitled Amount

Collectibility is one of the most misapplied criteria. It asks whether it is probable that the entity will collect the consideration to which it is entitled, not the full invoiced amount if part of that amount represents variable consideration that may be conceded. Assess collectibility on the amount the entity expects to be entitled to after estimating variable consideration.

Consider:

- the customer's creditworthiness and payment history;
- the economic environment and industry conditions;
- whether the price is fixed or includes significant variable elements;
- whether the entity has a history of granting price concessions to this customer;
- any collateral, guarantees, or third-party assurance.

A customer that routinely negotiates price down may still meet the collectibility test if the entity expects to collect the reduced amount. Conversely, a full-price invoice to a customer who cannot pay fails the test. Assess the entitled amount, not the sticker price.

### Evaluate Commercial Substance On Cash Flow Change

Commercial substance requires that the entity's future cash flows change as a result of the contract. This is usually obvious in ordinary business, but it filters out arrangements that are merely exchanges of equivalent value or that have no real economic effect. Confirm that the contract alters the entity's risk, timing, or amount of future cash flows before treating it as in scope.

### Combine Contracts When The Indicators Of A Single Arrangement Are Present

Separately signed agreements may need to be accounted for as a single contract when they are, in substance, one arrangement. Combining affects how obligations are identified and how price is allocated, so it must be assessed deliberately.

Combine contracts when one or more of the following are present:

- the contracts are negotiated as a package with a single commercial objective;
- the amount of consideration to be paid in one contract depends on the price or performance of the other;
- the goods or services promised in the contracts, together, represent a single performance obligation.

Do not combine merely because two agreements share a customer or were signed on the same day. The indicators must show a single commercial objective or interdependence. Over-combining distorts allocation; under-combining fragments obligations that should be evaluated together.

### Scope Out Arrangements Covered By Other Standards

Not every agreement that looks like a sale is within the revenue standard. Some arrangements are governed by other standards and must be removed from revenue analysis entirely.

Watch for arrangements that are actually:

- leases, governed by lease standards;
- insurance contracts;
- certain financial instruments and guarantees;
- certain nonmonetary exchanges between commercial customers;
- contingent consideration in a business combination.

When in doubt about scope, identify the nature of the arrangement before applying revenue criteria. Applying revenue rules to a lease or an insurance contract produces fundamentally wrong accounting.

### Classify Contract Modifications Correctly

A modification to an existing contract can take one of three forms, each with very different accounting. Misclassification is a frequent source of revenue distortion.

The three treatments are:

- a separate contract, when the modification adds distinct goods or services at their standalone selling prices;
- a cumulative catch-up adjustment, when the remaining goods or services are not distinct and form a single obligation, requiring a retrospective adjustment to date;
- a prospective adjustment, when the remaining goods or services are distinct from those already transferred, treating the modification as a new obligation going forward and leaving past revenue unchanged.

A modification is a separate contract only if it adds distinct goods or services at prices that reflect their standalone selling prices. If the price does not reflect standalone prices, or the additional goods are not distinct, it is a catch-up or prospective modification instead. Determine the form from the substance of the change, not from how the amendment is labeled.

### Reassess When Facts Change During The Contract

Contract existence is assessed at inception, but circumstances can change. A customer's credit may deteriorate, the parties may dispute the terms, or the contract may be terminated. Reassess whether the contract remains in scope and whether collectibility still holds when significant changes occur, and follow the appropriate guidance for contracts that cease to qualify.

## Common Traps

### Assuming A Signed Document Automatically Qualifies As A Contract

A signature does not confirm collectibility, identifiable payment terms, or commercial substance. Each criterion must be tested. Treating any signed agreement as automatically in scope skips the gate that the standard requires.

### Assessing Collectibility On The Full Invoiced Price

If the customer routinely negotiates the price down, the full invoice overstates the collectible amount. Collectibility is assessed on the amount the entity expects to be entitled to, after variable consideration.

### Combining Contracts Based On A Shared Customer Alone

Two agreements with the same customer are not automatically one contract. Combining requires a single commercial objective, interdependent pricing, or a combined single obligation. Over-combining distorts the analysis.

### Missing Interdependence That Requires Combination

Conversely, two agreements whose pricing or performance depends on each other may be one arrangement in substance. Treating them as separate when they should be combined fragments a single obligation and misallocates price.

### Treating Every Modification As A Separate Contract

A modification is a separate contract only if it adds distinct goods or services at standalone selling prices. Most modifications are catch-up or prospective, and misclassifying them shifts revenue between periods.

### Applying Revenue Rules To Arrangements In Other Standards

Leases, insurance, and certain financial instruments are not revenue. Forcing them through the revenue model produces wrong accounting and wrong disclosure.

### Ignoring Deteriorating Collectibility After Inception

A contract that qualified at inception may cease to qualify if the customer's ability to pay collapses. Failing to reassess keeps recognizing revenue on a contract that no longer meets the criteria.

## Self-Check

- Have all five contract existence criteria been confirmed, including approval, identifiable rights, identifiable payment terms, commercial substance, and probable collection?
- Is collectibility assessed on the amount the entity expects to be entitled to, after variable consideration, rather than the full invoiced price?
- Does the contract have commercial substance that changes the entity's future cash flows?
- Where multiple agreements exist, are they combined only when a single commercial objective, interdependent pricing, or a combined single obligation is present?
- Have arrangements that are actually leases, insurance, financial instruments, or other scoped-out categories been removed from revenue analysis?
- For each modification, is the classification as separate contract, cumulative catch-up, or prospective based on the substance of distinct goods and standalone prices?
- Is the contract reassessed when collectibility deteriorates, terms are disputed, or the arrangement is terminated?
- Are existence, combination, and modification determinations documented with contract evidence and rationale?
- Does the analysis identify the applicable reporting framework and recommend qualified professional review where the determination is judgmental or material?
