# Foreign Currency Transaction Accounting

Foreign currency accounting is where small rate mistakes become large profit distortions. When an entity buys or sells in a currency other than its functional currency, every transaction carries an exchange rate, and that rate changes between the transaction date, the reporting date, and the settlement date. The errors agents make are predictable: using the wrong functional currency, applying a single average rate to everything, failing to remeasure monetary items at the closing rate, treating nonmonetary items the same as monetary ones, and misclassifying exchange differences. Each of these silently distorts revenue, expenses, and profit, and they are hard to unwind at year-end because the rate movements are already history.

Use this skill before recording a foreign-currency transaction, determining or challenging a functional currency, remeasuring balances at period-end, recognizing exchange gains and losses, or settling a foreign-currency receivable or payable. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying a simplistic "convert everything at one rate" approach when the correct treatment depends on whether the item is monetary or nonmonetary, when it was recognized, and what currency the entity truly operates in.

Foreign exchange rules, functional currency indicators, and the presentation of exchange differences vary by jurisdiction, currency, and reporting framework, and the treatment under IFRS differs in important ways from US GAAP and other local frameworks. Do not state a single universal rule. Complex functional currency determination, hyperinflation, or hedge-related judgment should be routed to a qualified accountant or FX specialist. This skill provides operational transaction-accounting judgment, not a definitive treatment for every fact pattern.

## Core Rules

### Determine The Functional Currency Before Recording Anything

The functional currency is the currency of the primary economic environment in which the entity operates. It drives every other decision in foreign currency accounting, so it must be established first and supported by evidence, not chosen for convenience.

Consider indicators such as:

- the currency that mainly influences sales prices for goods and services;
- the currency of the country whose competitive forces and regulations determine prices;
- the currency that mainly influences labor, material, and other costs;
- the currency in which financing is generated;
- the currency in which receipts from operating activities are retained.

When indicators are mixed, management judgment with documentation is required. The functional currency is rarely a free choice; it reflects economic substance. Changing it requires strong justification and prospective application.

### Recognize Transactions At The Spot Rate On The Transaction Date

A foreign-currency transaction is initially recorded in the functional currency using the spot exchange rate on the transaction date. For practical purposes, an average rate for a period may be used if rates do not fluctuate significantly, but the principle is the spot rate at recognition.

Apply:

- the spot rate on the transaction date for initial recognition;
- an average rate only where it approximates the spot rate without material distortion;
- consistent rate selection for similar transactions;
- documentation of the rate source and date.

Using a month-end rate or an arbitrary rate for initial recognition misstates the transaction from the start and creates avoidable exchange differences later.

### Distinguish Monetary From Nonmonetary Items At Subsequent Measurement

After initial recognition, the treatment diverges based on whether the item is monetary or nonmonetary. This distinction is the heart of foreign currency transaction accounting and the most common source of error.

Apply:

- monetary items, such as cash, receivables, and payables, are remeasured at the closing rate at each reporting date;
- nonmonetary items carried at historical cost, such as property or inventory, remain at the historical rate;
- nonmonetary items carried at fair value are remeasured using the rate at the date the fair value was determined;
- dividends and equity transactions use the rate at the date of the transaction.

Treating a nonmonetary historical-cost item as if it must be retranslated at the closing rate creates phantom exchange differences. Treating a monetary item as fixed creates unrecognized gains and losses that distort the balance sheet.

### Recognize Exchange Differences In The Correct Period And Statement

Exchange differences arise on monetary items between the transaction date, prior reporting dates, and the settlement date. They are generally recognized in profit or loss in the period they arise, with specific exceptions.

Recognize:

- exchange differences on monetary items in profit or loss when they arise;
- differences on settlement as realized gains or losses in the period of settlement;
- differences on items that are part of a qualifying hedge according to the hedge accounting rules;
- certain differences on foreign operations in other comprehensive income under translation rules, not transaction rules.

The default home for transaction-level exchange differences is profit or loss. Routing them to equity or deferring them without a specific rule understates or smooths reported performance inappropriately.

### Handle Settlement And Realized Gains And Losses

When a foreign-currency payable or receivable is settled, the difference between the carrying amount, remeasured at the closing rate, and the actual cash paid or received in the functional currency is a realized exchange gain or loss.

At settlement:

- restate the monetary item to the closing rate immediately before settlement if a reporting date has passed;
- record the cash movement at the settlement-date rate;
- recognize the resulting difference as a realized gain or loss in profit or loss;
- clear the receivable or payable balance.

Settlement is the moment unrealized differences become realized. The accounting must capture this transition rather than simply removing the balance at its last carrying amount.

### Apply Consistent Policies And Document Rate Sources

Consistency and documentation are what make foreign currency accounting auditable. Rate sources, spot-versus-average conventions, and the monetary or nonmonetary classification of each account must be defined and applied consistently.

Document:

- the source of exchange rates, such as a central bank or a specified service;
- the time of day used for the spot rate;
- the policy for average rates where used;
- the classification of each balance sheet account as monetary or nonmonetary;
- the treatment of any accounts with mixed characteristics.

Without this documentation, a later question about an exchange difference cannot be answered, and year-end remeasurement becomes guesswork.

### Watch For Functional Currency Red Flags

Certain signals indicate the functional currency may be wrong or that a foreign operation's economics have changed. These should trigger a review rather than be ignored.

Watch for:

- an entity whose costs and revenues are predominantly in a currency different from its recorded functional currency;
- persistent, large exchange differences that do not reflect real economic exposure;
- financing and retention of cash in a currency different from the functional currency;
- indicators of hyperinflation in an economy where the entity operates;
- a change in the primary economic environment that has not been reassessed.

A wrong functional currency contaminates every downstream calculation, so the cost of getting it wrong is far higher than the cost of reviewing it.

## Common Traps

### Using One Rate For Everything

Applying a single average or month-end rate to all transactions and balances ignores the distinction between initial recognition, monetary remeasurement, and nonmonetary historical cost. It produces balances that look consistent but are wrong.

### Misclassifying Nonmonetary Items As Monetary

Property, inventory, and equity instruments carried at historical cost are nonmonetary and stay at the historical rate. Remeasuring them at the closing rate creates fictitious exchange differences and misstates the balance sheet.

### Forgetting Period-End Remeasurement Of Monetary Items

Monetary payables and receivables must be restated to the closing rate at each reporting date. Leaving them at the transaction rate leaves unrealized gains and losses unrecognized.

### Choosing The Functional Currency For Convenience

Selecting a functional currency to avoid exchange differences or to smooth results ignores the economic indicators. The functional currency reflects substance and requires documentation.

### Deferring Exchange Differences Without A Rule

Routing exchange differences to equity or a deferred account to avoid volatility in profit or loss is only acceptable under specific rules, such as qualifying hedge accounting or translation of foreign operations. Default deferral misstates performance.

### Ignoring Settlement Realization

Removing a settled receivable or payable at its carrying amount without capturing the realized difference understates or overstates the settlement-period result.

### Overlooking Functional Currency Changes

When the economic environment changes, the functional currency may need to change. Failing to reassess leaves the entity accounting in a currency that no longer reflects its operations.

## Self-Check

- Has the functional currency been determined from documented economic indicators before any transaction is recorded?
- Are foreign-currency transactions initially recognized at the spot rate on the transaction date, with average rates used only where they approximate spot?
- Are monetary items, such as cash, receivables, and payables, remeasured at the closing rate at each reporting date?
- Are nonmonetary historical-cost items kept at the historical rate and nonmonetary fair-value items remeasured at the fair-value date rate?
- Are exchange differences on monetary items recognized in profit or loss in the period they arise, unless a specific rule applies?
- Are settlements recorded with the realized gain or loss captured at the settlement-date rate?
- Are rate sources, spot-versus-average conventions, and account classifications documented and applied consistently?
- Have functional currency red flags, including mixed indicators and hyperinflation, been reviewed?
- Are persistent or unusually large exchange differences investigated rather than accepted?
- Has complex functional currency, hyperinflation, or hedge-related judgment been escalated to a qualified accountant or FX specialist?
