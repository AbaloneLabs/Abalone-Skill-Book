# Auditor Inquiry Response

Auditors ask questions throughout the engagement. Some are routine, about a process or a balance. Others are pointed, about an unusual entry, a missing document, a change in estimate, or a variance that does not make sense. How the company responds shapes the auditor's risk assessment and the scope of testing. A clear, honest, well-supported response can close an issue. An evasive, inconsistent, or unsupported response expands testing, erodes trust, and can turn a minor question into a material finding. Responding to auditor inquiries is a discipline of accuracy, completeness, and documented support.

Use this skill before responding to auditor inquiries, preparing written explanations, drafting answers to walkthrough questions, or explaining unusual transactions or variances to an external auditor. The goal is to prevent the agent from giving responses that are technically responsive but unsupported, inconsistent across team members, or that inadvertently expand audit risk.

## Core Rules

### Answer The Question Asked, Accurately And Completely

The most common failure in inquiry response is answering a different question than the one asked, or answering partially. Read the inquiry carefully and address it directly.

For each inquiry:

- identify the specific assertion or risk the auditor is testing;
- answer the question asked, not the question you wish had been asked;
- provide the complete answer, including facts that may be uncomfortable;
- distinguish fact from interpretation;
- avoid speculation where you do not know, say so.

An answer that omits a known fact, hoping the auditor will not find it, almost always ends badly. The omitted fact becomes the story.

### Base Every Answer On Evidence

An inquiry response is only as strong as the evidence behind it. Do not assert facts you cannot support.

For each response:

- identify the source documents, systems, or records that support the answer;
- reference the evidence specifically, account, document, date;
- attach or link the supporting documents;
- confirm the evidence actually says what the response claims;
- avoid characterizing documents you have not reviewed.

An unsupported assertion invites follow-up and erodes credibility. A supported answer closes the issue.

### Coordinate Responses Across The Team

Auditors often ask different people the same question. Inconsistent answers are interpreted as risk. Coordinate before responding.

Coordinate:

- identify who is best placed to answer each inquiry;
- align on the facts before responding;
- use a single point of contact to deliver responses;
- keep a log of inquiries and responses;
- review significant written responses before sending.

When team members genuinely disagree, surface the disagreement and resolve it rather than letting the auditor discover the inconsistency.

### Explain Unusual Items With Root Cause

Auditors focus on what is unusual: a large journal entry, a change in estimate, a variance from trend, a transaction outside normal process. A surface explanation is not enough.

For unusual items explain:

- what the transaction or event was;
- why it occurred, the business reason;
- how it was recorded and why that treatment is appropriate;
- who authorized it and under what authority;
- whether it is expected to recur;
- what evidence supports the explanation.

An explanation that describes what happened but not why leaves the auditor to infer the reason, and the inference may be worse than the truth.

### Distinguish Process From Exception

When asked about a process, describe how it normally works and how exceptions are handled. Auditors test both.

Describe:

- the standard process from initiation to recording;
- the controls embedded in the process;
- how exceptions, errors, and overrides are handled;
- how exceptions are documented and reviewed;
- the frequency and nature of exceptions in the period.

A process description that ignores exceptions is incomplete. An auditor who finds an unexplained exception after a clean process description loses confidence in the response.

### Document The Response And The Support

Inquiry responses should be documented so they can be referenced later and so the support is preserved.

Retain:

- the auditor's inquiry;
- the written response;
- the supporting documents referenced;
- who prepared and reviewed the response;
- the date and the context of the inquiry.

Oral responses should be confirmed in writing where material. Memory of what was said diverges over time, and the written record protects both sides.

### Be Honest About Uncertainty And Limitations

Not every question has a clean answer. Some involve judgment, estimate, or unresolved matters. Represent these honestly.

When uncertain:

- state what is known and what is not;
- describe the basis for any judgment;
- identify what additional review or professional input is needed;
- avoid presenting a tentative view as settled.

An honest acknowledgment of uncertainty, with a plan to resolve it, is far better than a confident answer that later proves wrong.

### Do Not Negotiate Accounting Treatment Through Inquiry Responses

Inquiry responses explain facts and support. They are not the vehicle for arguing accounting treatment or pressing the auditor toward a preferred conclusion. Where treatment is genuinely in dispute, that is a matter for documented technical analysis and, where needed, qualified professional involvement, not for slanting inquiry answers. Let the facts speak, and engage technical specialists for the accounting debate.

### Acknowledge Professional And Independence Limits

Responding to auditor inquiries supports the audit, but the company's responses are management's representations. They do not replace the auditor's independent procedures, and they do not constitute an audit opinion. Complex accounting matters may require the involvement of qualified accounting, tax, or valuation professionals, and their input should be reflected in the response. Do not present management's view as a substitute for independent professional judgment where that is required.

## Common Traps

### Answering A Different Question

Redirecting to a comfortable topic leaves the real question unanswered and signals evasion.

### Omitting Uncomfortable Facts

Hoping the auditor will not find a known issue almost always backfires and damages credibility.

### Unsupported Assertions

An answer without referenced evidence invites follow-up and suggests the support does not exist.

### Inconsistent Answers Across Team Members

Different answers to the same question are read as risk and expand testing.

### Surface Explanations For Unusual Items

Describing what happened without explaining why lets the auditor infer a worse reason.

### Ignoring Exceptions In Process Descriptions

A clean process story that omits exceptions collapses when an exception is found.

### Slanting Answers Toward A Preferred Treatment

Using inquiry responses to argue accounting conclusions undermines the integrity of the process.

### Overstating Certainty

Presenting a tentative or uncertain matter as settled creates exposure when the truth emerges.

## Self-Check

- Does each response answer the specific question asked, accurately and completely, distinguishing fact from interpretation?
- Is every material assertion in the response supported by referenced and attached evidence?
- Are responses coordinated across the team, with a single point of contact and a log of inquiries?
- Do explanations for unusual items address root cause, authorization, recurrence, and supporting evidence?
- Do process descriptions cover both the standard flow and how exceptions are handled?
- Are responses and their supporting documents retained in writing?
- Is uncertainty acknowledged honestly, with a plan to resolve it, rather than presented as settled?
- Does the response avoid negotiating accounting treatment and acknowledge that complex matters may require qualified professional input and that management responses do not replace the independent audit?
