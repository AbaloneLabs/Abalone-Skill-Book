# Representation And Confirmation Support

Near the end of an audit, management provides written representations and the auditor completes third-party confirmations. These are not formalities. A management representation letter is a signed assertion by those responsible for the financial statements, and it carries legal and professional weight. Confirmations are independent external evidence that corroborates recorded balances and disclosures. Weak support for either creates real risk: a representation that overstates management's knowledge or understates known issues can constitute misrepresentation, and a confirmation process that is compromised undermines the audit's most reliable external evidence.

Use this skill before drafting management representation letters, supporting confirmation processes, responding to representation requests, or assembling the evidence behind representations and confirmations. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing representations that are broader than management's actual knowledge, confirmations that are compromised by management interference, or support that does not actually substantiate what is being asserted.

## Core Rules

### Limit Representations To What Management Actually Knows

A representation letter asserts what management knows and has considered. It should not assert facts management cannot support or has not actually assessed.

For each representation confirm:

- management has actually considered the matter;
- there is evidence supporting the assertion;
- the representation is within management's knowledge and responsibility;
- the wording is accurate and not overbroad;
- known exceptions or uncertainties are disclosed, not hidden.

A representation that asserts completeness of liabilities when management has not performed a review, or that asserts no fraud when concerns exist, is dangerous. Represent what is known, and disclose what is uncertain.

### Align Representations With The Applicable Framework And Audit Standard

Representation letters are drafted to meet the requirements of the applicable auditing standards and the specific framework, GAAP, IFRS, or another basis. The auditor usually provides the draft, but management must understand and agree with every assertion.

Review the letter for:

- consistency with the applicable financial reporting framework;
- coverage of the specific matters the auditing standard requires;
- consistency with the company's actual positions and disclosures;
- accuracy of dates, periods, and entity references;
- identification of the correct signatories, usually those with responsibility and knowledge.

Do not sign a letter containing assertions management does not understand or cannot support. Request clarification or revision before signing.

### Support Each Material Representation With Evidence

A representation is stronger when it is backed by evidence that management actually performed the relevant assessment.

Assemble support for representations about:

- completeness of recording liabilities and subsequent events;
- management's responsibility for the financial statements and internal control;
- significant accounting estimates and the basis for them;
- related party transactions and completeness of disclosure;
- litigation and claims and whether the disclosures are complete;
- fraud or non-compliance known or suspected;
- going concern assessment and plans to address material uncertainty;
- uncorrected misstatements and why they are immaterial.

The evidence need not equal audit-level testing, but management should be able to show it considered the matter and has a basis for the assertion.

### Maintain The Integrity Of The Confirmation Process

Confirmations derive their value from being independent. Management prepares or facilitates them, but must not compromise that independence.

Protect integrity by:

- using recipient addresses that are independent and verified;
- letting the auditor control the sending and receiving where the standard requires;
- not intercepting, altering, or withholding responses;
- reconciling confirmation amounts to the records before sending;
- investigating exceptions and differences promptly and honestly;
- documenting unmatched or disputed confirmations.

Confirmation interference is a classic fraud red flag and, in many jurisdictions, a serious matter. Preserve the chain of control.

### Reconcile Confirmation Results To The Records

Confirmations either corroborate the recorded balance or reveal differences. Both outcomes must be documented.

For each confirmation:

- compare the confirmed amount to the recorded balance;
- investigate and explain any difference;
- determine whether the difference indicates an error, a timing item, or a dispute;
- quantify the effect of confirmed exceptions;
- assess whether exceptions indicate a broader population issue.

A confirmation that agrees supports the assertion. A confirmation that differs must be resolved, not explained away.

### Coordinate Signatories And Timing

Representation letters must be signed by the appropriate people and dated correctly, usually as of the auditor's report date or the date the audit evidence is complete.

Coordinate:

- the correct signatories, typically the chief executive and financial officer or equivalent;
- that signatories have actually reviewed and understand the representations;
- the date, which should align with the completion of audit procedures;
- any updated or subsequent representation letters if the report date moves.

A letter signed by the wrong person, or dated before audit completion, can create procedural defects.

### Document The Full Representation And Confirmation File

The representation and confirmation process should be fully documented for retention and future reference.

Retain:

- the final signed representation letter;
- the evidence supporting each material representation;
- the confirmation requests, responses, and reconciliations;
- the resolution of exceptions and differences;
- communications with the auditor about representations and confirmations.

This file is reviewed in future audits and may be examined in legal or regulatory matters. Completeness and organization matter.

### Acknowledge Limits And Escalate

Representations and confirmations support the audit, but they are management's assertions and third-party evidence, not the audit opinion itself. Where a matter is complex, uncertain, or potentially material, such as litigation, going concern, fraud, or a significant estimate, the representation should reflect the involvement of qualified professionals, including legal counsel where appropriate. Do not use a representation to paper over a matter that has not been genuinely assessed, and do not treat management's assertion as a substitute for independent audit or professional review where those are required.

## Common Traps

### Overbroad Representations

Asserting matters management has not assessed, such as completeness of liabilities without a review, creates real exposure.

### Signing Without Understanding

Signing a letter with assertions management cannot explain or support undermines the representation and the audit.

### Interference With Confirmations

Altering, intercepting, or withholding confirmation responses is a serious integrity breach and a fraud red flag.

### Unresolved Confirmation Exceptions

Explaining away differences without investigation hides potential errors and population issues.

### Unsupported Representations

A representation with no evidence that management considered the matter is weak and vulnerable to challenge.

### Wrong Signatory Or Date

Procedural defects in signatory or dating can undermine the letter's validity.

### Incomplete Documentation

Failing to retain the representation and confirmation file creates gaps in the audit trail.

### Overstating The Representation's Authority

A representation is management's assertion, not an audit opinion or a substitute for professional or legal assessment of complex matters.

## Self-Check

- Is each representation limited to what management has actually considered and can support, with uncertainties disclosed?
- Does the representation letter align with the applicable framework and auditing standard, with accurate dates, periods, and signatories?
- Is each material representation supported by evidence that management assessed the matter?
- Is the confirmation process protected from interference, with independent addresses and auditor control where required?
- Are confirmation results reconciled to the records, with differences investigated and resolved?
- Are signatories appropriate, informed, and dated to align with audit completion?
- Is the full representation and confirmation file retained, including support, exceptions, and communications?
- Does the process acknowledge that complex matters require qualified professional involvement and that representations do not replace independent audit or professional review?
