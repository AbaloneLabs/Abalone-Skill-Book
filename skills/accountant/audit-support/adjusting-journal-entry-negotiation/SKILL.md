# Adjusting Journal Entry Negotiation

Near the end of an audit, the auditor proposes adjusting journal entries. Some are clearly correct and should be recorded. Others involve judgment, classification, estimate, or materiality, and the company must decide whether to accept, dispute, or negotiate them. This is a high-stakes moment. Accepting a wrong adjustment misstates the financial statements. Disputing a correct adjustment weakens the audit and can lead to a qualified opinion. The discipline is to evaluate each proposed adjustment on its merits, grounded in evidence and the applicable framework, and to negotiate in good faith rather than by reflex.

Use this skill before reviewing proposed audit adjustments, negotiating adjusting journal entries, quantifying their impact, or deciding whether to record or dispute them. The goal is to prevent the agent from accepting adjustments without analysis, rejecting them without basis, or treating the negotiation as adversarial rather than evidence-driven.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Assertion Behind Each Adjustment

Every proposed adjustment tests a specific assertion: existence, completeness, valuation, rights, presentation, or cutoff. Understand what the auditor is asserting before evaluating the entry.

For each proposed adjustment identify:

- the assertion or risk it addresses;
- the account and period affected;
- the amount and direction of the proposed change;
- whether it is factual, involving a clear error, or judgmental, involving estimate or interpretation;
- whether it is isolated or indicative of a broader issue.

A factual error should generally be corrected. A judgmental adjustment deserves careful analysis of the method and assumptions.

### Evaluate Each Adjustment Against Evidence And Framework

Do not accept or reject an adjustment based on its size or convenience. Evaluate it against the evidence and the applicable reporting framework.

Evaluate:

- the source of the auditor's proposed amount;
- the evidence supporting or contradicting the adjustment;
- the applicable GAAP, IFRS, tax, or regulatory treatment;
- whether the company's recorded position was supportable;
- whether the proposed adjustment is supportable;
- the materiality of the adjustment, both individually and aggregated with others.

Where the company's original treatment was unsupported, the adjustment is likely warranted. Where both positions are supportable, the question is which better reflects the framework.

### Quantify Materiality And Aggregated Impact

A single adjustment may be immaterial, but proposed adjustments are evaluated in aggregate. Track the cumulative effect.

Quantify:

- the individual impact of each proposed adjustment on net income, equity, and key balances;
- the aggregated impact of all proposed adjustments;
- the effect on financial statement captions and key ratios;
- the effect on debt covenants, earn-outs, bonuses, or other metrics tied to reported results;
- whether the aggregated adjustments cross materiality thresholds.

Materiality is not just a number; it includes qualitative factors such as trend reversal, consistency, and the effect on user decisions.

### Distinguish Factual, Judgmental, And Projected Adjustments

Auditors classify proposed adjustments, and the classification affects how they should be handled.

Understand:

- factual adjustments arise from identified errors and are usually agreed and recorded;
- judgmental adjustments involve differences in estimate or interpretation and require analysis;
- projected adjustments arise from sampling and are evaluated for whether they represent actual error;
- uncorrected differences are recorded by the auditor in evaluating materiality even if not booked by the company.

Do not treat a judgmental difference as if it were a factual error, or dismiss a factual error as if it were a matter of opinion.

### Negotiate In Good Faith, Grounded In Analysis

Negotiation is legitimate when it is grounded in evidence and framework analysis, not when it is an attempt to wear down the auditor.

Negotiate by:

- presenting the company's analysis and support clearly;
- identifying the specific points of disagreement;
- proposing alternative treatments that are themselves framework-compliant;
- acknowledging where the auditor's position has merit;
- escalating genuinely technical matters to qualified professionals.

Negotiation that amounts to refusing to book clearly correct adjustments damages the relationship and can lead to a modified opinion. Negotiation that resolves genuine technical differences improves the outcome for both sides.

### Document The Resolution Of Each Adjustment

Every proposed adjustment should have a documented resolution, whether recorded, revised, or not recorded.

Document:

- the proposed adjustment and its basis;
- the company's analysis and position;
- the final resolution and the rationale;
- the effect on the financial statements;
- the approval of the resolution.

Undocumented resolutions create confusion in future audits and weaken the audit trail. The record should allow a reviewer to understand why each adjustment was accepted or rejected.

### Consider The Effect On Internal Records And Systems

An adjustment recorded for the audit may need to be reflected in the company's books and systems, not just in the financial statements.

Address:

- whether the adjustment is recorded in the general ledger or only as a reclassification in the statements;
- the effect on subledgers, fixed asset registers, or other subsystems;
- whether the adjustment affects prior period comparatives or requires a restatement;
- whether the adjustment triggers a change in process or control to prevent recurrence.

An adjustment recorded only on paper, without fixing the underlying system, guarantees the same issue next year.

### Acknowledge Independence And Professional Limits

The decision to record or dispute an adjustment is management's, but it is made within the framework the auditor is independently assessing. Complex adjustments, such as those involving fair value, business combinations, revenue recognition, or tax, often require qualified professional analysis. Do not treat negotiation as a substitute for getting the accounting right. Where the adjustment reflects a genuine error or an unsupported position, the right answer is usually to record it, not to fight it.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Adjustments Without Analysis

Recording proposed adjustments without evaluating the evidence and framework misstates the statements and cedes judgment to the auditor.

### Rejecting Adjustments Without Basis

Disputing correct adjustments reflexively damages the relationship and can lead to a modified opinion.

### Ignoring Aggregated Materiality

Evaluating adjustments only individually misses the cumulative effect on the financial statements.

### Confusing Factual And Judgmental Adjustments

Treating a clear error as a matter of opinion, or a judgmental difference as a clear error, leads to wrong resolutions.

### Adversarial Negotiation

Negotiating to wear down the auditor rather than to resolve technical differences undermines the audit's integrity.

### Undocumented Resolutions

Failing to document why each adjustment was accepted or rejected creates confusion and weakens the audit trail.

### Paper-Only Adjustments

Recording an adjustment in the statements without fixing the underlying system guarantees recurrence.

### Overstepping Into Audit Judgment

Negotiation is management's right within the framework, but it is not a substitute for correct accounting or for independent professional analysis of complex matters.

## Self-Check

- Is the assertion, account, period, amount, and classification of each proposed adjustment clearly understood?
- Is each adjustment evaluated against the evidence and the applicable reporting framework, not just its size or convenience?
- Is the individual and aggregated materiality of proposed adjustments quantified, including qualitative effects?
- Are factual, judgmental, projected, and uncorrected differences distinguished and handled appropriately?
- Is negotiation conducted in good faith, grounded in analysis and framework-compliant alternatives?
- Is the resolution of every proposed adjustment documented with basis, rationale, and approval?
- Are recorded adjustments reflected in the books and underlying systems, with process fixes where needed?
- Does the process acknowledge that complex adjustments may require qualified professional analysis and that negotiation is not a substitute for correct accounting?
