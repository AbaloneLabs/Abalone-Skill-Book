# Bank Errors And Discrepancy Resolution

Bank errors are rare relative to the volume of transactions, but when they occur they are confusing precisely because the bank is usually right. That assumption, that the bank statement is the source of truth, is exactly what makes bank-side errors hard to catch. Reconcilers instinctively adjust their own books to match the bank, and in doing so they sometimes bury a genuine bank error under a book adjustment, or worse, they chase the bank for weeks over what is actually their own recording mistake. The discipline of discrepancy resolution is to remain neutral about where the error lies until the evidence identifies it. A discrepancy is a question, not a conclusion. The agent's job is to classify it correctly, pursue the right party, document the trail, and avoid the two opposite failures of adjusting the books to force a match and of blaming the bank to avoid admitting a bookkeeping error.

Use this skill before investigating a reconciliation difference, pursuing a correction with the bank, adjusting the books for a discrepancy, or deciding how to treat an unresolved difference. The goal is to prevent the agent from resolving discrepancies by erasing them rather than by identifying and correcting their true cause.

Bank error correction procedures, dispute timelines, unauthorized-transaction liability rules, and adjustment authority vary by jurisdiction, banking system, account type, and the entity's banking agreement. Do not state a single universal rule. Disputed amounts, suspected fraud, and unauthorized transactions should be reported to the bank promptly and may require escalation to a qualified accountant, the bank's fraud team, or the relevant authority. This skill provides operational discrepancy-resolution judgment, not a final determination in any specific dispute.

## Core Rules

### Isolate The Discrepancy Before Acting

A single large difference in a reconciliation may be the net of several smaller errors in opposite directions. Acting on the net number leads to chasing the wrong cause. The first step is always to isolate the discrepancy into its component parts.

Break the difference down by:

- comparing the bank statement line by line to the book entries for the period;
- identifying items on the bank not in the books and items in the books not on the bank;
- separating timing differences from genuine errors;
- grouping the unmatched items by type, amount, and likely source;
- checking whether the net difference is the sum of several smaller mismatches.

An isolated, itemized discrepancy is solvable. A net difference treated as one thing is a guess.

### Classify Each Difference As Bank-Side Or Book-Side

Every discrepancy is either an error the bank made, an error the entity made in its books, or a timing difference that will clear naturally. The classification determines the action, and misclassification causes the most damage.

For each difference determine:

- bank-side error, the bank recorded the wrong amount, wrong party, wrong date, or a transaction that does not belong to the account;
- book-side error, the entity recorded the wrong amount, duplicated an entry, posted to the wrong account, or missed a transaction;
- timing difference, the item is genuine but has not yet cleared, such as a deposit in transit or outstanding check;
- unauthorized or fraudulent transaction, an item on the bank that the entity did not initiate.

Bank-side errors are pursued with the bank and left as reconciling items. Book-side errors are corrected in the books with an approved journal entry. Timing differences are carried and monitored. Unauthorized transactions are reported immediately.

### Pursue Bank Errors With The Bank, Not In The Books

When the bank is wrong, the correct response is to report the error to the bank and track it until corrected. Adjusting the books to match a wrong bank balance hides the error, misstates the records, and forfeits the right to pursue the correction.

When reporting a bank error:

- notify the bank promptly within any dispute or notification window;
- provide the transaction details, amounts, dates, and supporting evidence;
- obtain a reference number or case identifier;
- retain the item as a reconciling item until the bank corrects it;
- follow up on a defined schedule until resolution.

A bank error that is corrected in the books instead of with the bank becomes invisible. The cash balance may look right, but the underlying error persists and the reconciliation loses its evidentiary value.

### Correct Book Errors With Evidence And Approval

When the books are wrong, the correction is a journal entry, but it is not a silent edit. Cash adjustments are high-risk, and every book correction must explain what was wrong, why, and who approved the change.

For each book correction:

- document the original entry and the error identified;
- state the cause, such as transposition, duplication, wrong account, or missed item;
- record the correcting entry in the correct account and period;
- obtain approval from an independent reviewer;
- assess whether the error affected prior reports, tax filings, or covenants.

If a book error affects a closed period or a filed return, escalate before posting. Routine corrections within the current period are normal; corrections that ripple into filings are not.

### Act Immediately On Unauthorized And Fraudulent Transactions

An item on the bank statement that the entity did not initiate may be fraud, a bank processing error, or a misapplied third-party transaction. These are time-sensitive because liability often depends on how quickly the entity reports the issue.

For unauthorized items:

- report to the bank immediately, within the notification window in the account agreement;
- freeze or restrict the affected account or credentials if compromise is suspected;
- document the transaction, the report, and the bank's response;
- reclassify the item out of cash while it is disputed;
- escalate to the entity's fraud contact, bank fraud team, or relevant authority as required.

Delay in reporting an unauthorized transaction can shift liability to the entity. Speed matters more than certainty in these cases.

### Distinguish A Real Discrepancy From A Reconciliation Method Error

Sometimes the difference is not in the transactions at all. It is in how the reconciliation was prepared. A wrong opening balance, a duplicated import, a statement period mismatch, or a cut-off error can manufacture a discrepancy that has no underlying transaction cause.

Check the method before chasing transactions:

- does the opening balance agree to the prior reconciled closing balance;
- is the statement period correct and non-overlapping with the prior period;
- have transactions been imported once, not duplicated;
- are the bank and book being compared for the same period;
- is the reconciliation using the correct account and currency.

A discrepancy caused by method error will never be solved by investigating transactions. Always validate the reconciliation mechanics first.

### Document And Age Every Unresolved Difference

Not every discrepancy resolves immediately. Some require bank investigation, vendor follow-up, or management decision. The key is that unresolved differences are tracked, aged, and escalated rather than forgotten.

For each unresolved item:

- record the amount, description, date identified, and suspected cause;
- assign an owner responsible for resolution;
- set a follow-up date and track aging;
- escalate items that exceed a defined age or materiality threshold;
- retain the item as a reconciling item with full support until cleared.

An unresolved difference that is neither documented nor aged is a control failure. It will surface at audit or, worse, never, leaving the error embedded in the records.

## Common Traps

### Adjusting The Books To Match The Bank

When the bank appears authoritative, the temptation is to force the books to agree. This hides bank errors, misstates cash, and destroys the reconciliation's ability to detect problems. Only genuine book errors should be corrected in the books.

### Blaming The Bank To Avoid A Bookkeeping Error

It is easier to believe the bank made a mistake than to admit a transposition or duplicate entry. Chasing the bank for weeks over a self-made error wastes time and delays the real correction. Classify based on evidence, not convenience.

### Treating A Net Difference As A Single Error

A large discrepancy is often the net of several smaller errors in opposite directions. Acting on the net number leads to fixing the wrong thing or giving up. Always isolate the difference into its components first.

### Missing The Notification Window For Unauthorized Transactions

Account agreements often limit the entity's ability to recover unauthorized transactions if they are not reported within a set period. Delay in reporting can shift liability permanently to the entity.

### Correcting A Bank Error In The Books

Recording an adjusting entry to offset a bank error makes the cash balance look right while the real error persists at the bank. The item must be pursued with the bank and retained as a reconciling item.

### Ignoring Reconciliation Method Errors

A wrong opening balance, duplicated import, or period mismatch can create a discrepancy that has nothing to do with the transactions. Chasing transaction-level causes for a method error never resolves it. Validate the mechanics first.

### Letting Unresolved Differences Age Silently

A discrepancy that is neither documented nor followed up becomes a permanent feature of the reconciliation. Without aging and escalation, errors embed themselves and surface only at audit, if at all.

### One Person Investigating And Approving Their Own Adjustment

If the person who identifies a discrepancy also approves the book correction, there is no independent check. Cash adjustments need independent review to prevent an investigator from masking their own error or misuse.

## Self-Check

- Has the discrepancy been isolated into its component parts rather than treated as a single net difference?
- Is each difference classified as bank-side error, book-side error, timing difference, or unauthorized transaction, based on evidence rather than assumption?
- Are bank-side errors reported to the bank, tracked with a reference, and retained as reconciling items rather than corrected in the books?
- Are book-side errors corrected with documented cause, correct account and period, and independent approval?
- Are unauthorized or fraudulent transactions reported to the bank immediately within the notification window, reclassified out of cash, and escalated as required?
- Have reconciliation method errors such as opening balance, duplicated import, and period mismatch been ruled out before investigating transaction-level causes?
- Is every unresolved difference documented, owned, aged, and escalated rather than carried silently?
- Are corrections that affect closed periods, tax filings, or covenants escalated before posting?
- Is investigation and approval performed by separate people to preserve independent review of cash adjustments?
- Have jurisdiction-specific dispute timelines, unauthorized-transaction liability rules, and banking-agreement terms been confirmed with a qualified professional or the bank?
