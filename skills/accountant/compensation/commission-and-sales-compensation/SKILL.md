# Commission And Sales Compensation

Sales commission accounting is deceptively hard because the expense is tied to revenue, and revenue is increasingly recognized over time under contract-based standards. A commission earned at the moment of sale may need to be expensed immediately, deferred and amortized, or split across multiple performance periods. Getting this wrong distorts both the cost of acquiring revenue and the reported margin on contracts.

The core trap is treating commissions like ordinary payroll that is expensed when earned. Under many modern revenue standards, commissions that relate to obtaining a contract must follow a specific recognition path. This skill provides operational judgment for commission expense, deferral, and reconciliation. Revenue and contract-cost standards (such as US ASC 340-40 and international IFRS 15) and employment law on commission earn-back and clawback vary by jurisdiction and entity. Confirm final policy elections, recoveries, and tax treatment with a qualified accountant and, where contracts or law are involved, legal counsel in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Commission Against The Right Recognition Model

Not all commissions are expensed when earned. The first step is to classify the payment against the applicable standard's model for costs to obtain a contract.

Determine:

- is the commission incremental and directly related to obtaining a contract (it would not be incurred absent the contract)?
- does it relate to a single performance obligation satisfied at a point in time, over time, or to multiple obligations?
- is the amortization period the expected life of the customer relationship or the contract term, per the elected policy?
- is there a practical expedient available for amortization of one year or less?

The classification drives everything that follows. A commission that qualifies for deferral but is expensed immediately understates assets and overstates current expense; the reverse misstates both.

### Match Amortization To The Revenue Pattern

When a commission is deferred and amortized, the amortization should be consistent with the transfer of the goods or services to which the commission relates.

Apply:

- amortize over the period of benefit, which may be the contract term or the expected customer relationship, depending on the entity's facts and the policy election;
- use a systematic method that reflects the pattern of performance, typically straight-line unless another basis better matches revenue;
- for renewals, assess whether the commission relates only to the initial term or also to the renewal period;
- for bundled arrangements with multiple performance obligations, allocate the commission consistently with the revenue allocation.

Ask whether the commission expense recognized in the period corresponds to the revenue recognized from the related contract in the same period.

### Handle Variable, Tiered, And SPIFF Commissions

Sales compensation plans are full of variable and conditional elements that complicate the accrual and deferral.

Address:

- tiered or accelerator rates that change the per-unit commission as targets are met;
- bonuses and SPIFFs tied to specific products or periods;
- commissions on variable consideration that itself is constrained or estimated;
- splits between multiple salespeople or channels;
- recurring or residual commissions paid over the life of a customer.

For each, determine whether the cost is a cost to obtain the contract (subject to deferral) or general selling cost (expensed when incurred). Variable commissions should be estimated consistently with the related revenue estimate and trued up as the consideration resolves.

### Manage Clawbacks, Recoveries, And Chargebacks

Commissions can be reversed when contracts cancel, customers churn, or revenue is refunded. The accounting must anticipate and reflect recoveries.

Establish:

- whether the plan permits clawback of paid commissions on cancellation or non-payment;
- how recoveries from salespeople are recorded (reduction of expense, receivable, or reduction of the deferred asset);
- the treatment of chargebacks against future commissions;
- the expected forfeiture or reversal rate used in estimating the deferrable amount;
- the effect of credit losses or bad debts on previously recognized commissions.

Ignoring recoveries overstates the net commission cost and the deferred asset when churn is significant.

### Reconcile Commission Calculations To Source Data

Commission expense depends on the integrity of the calculation inputs. Reconcile before recording.

Reconcile:

- the commission calculation back to the compensation plan and the underlying sales or bookings data;
- the population of eligible transactions and salespeople;
- the revenue basis (bookings, recognized revenue, cash collected) against the plan definition;
- splits, overrides, and manager draws;
- the deferral and amortization schedule to the population of active contracts.

Calculation errors are common because plan rules are complex and source systems differ. A reconciliation break is a signal to investigate, not to plug.

### Separate Costs To Obtain From Other Selling Costs

Only incremental costs to obtain a contract are eligible for deferral. Other selling costs are expensed as incurred.

Separate:

- commissions that would not be paid absent winning the contract (deferrable);
- base salary and fixed sales compensation (expensed);
- marketing, travel, and entertainment (expensed);
- referral fees and channel incentives depending on whether they obtain a contract.

Misclassifying fixed costs as deferrable creates an asset that does not meet the standard.

## Common Traps

### Expensing All Commissions When Earned

The instinct to expense commission when the salesperson earns it conflicts with contract-cost rules when the revenue is recognized over time. This front-loads expense and distorts contract margin in the early periods.

### Amortizing Over The Wrong Period

Amortizing over the initial contract term when the customer relationship is expected to last longer, or vice versa, mismatches commission expense to revenue. The amortization period must reflect the entity's facts and a supported policy election.

### Ignoring Renewal Commissions

Renewal commissions may indicate that the benefit period extends beyond the initial term. Treating all commissions as initial-term costs understates the deferred asset and overstates early expense.

### Treating Variable Or Estimated Commissions As Fixed

When commissions depend on variable consideration, expensing or deferring a fixed amount ignores the constraint and true-up mechanics. The commission estimate should move with the revenue estimate.

### Forgetting Clawbacks And Forfeiture Adjustments

If the plan allows recovery on cancellation but the accounting never reverses commission, the net cost and the deferred asset are overstated for businesses with meaningful churn.

### Letting The Calculation Become A Black Box

Complex plans run in spreadsheets or sales tools that few people understand produce silent errors. Without reconciliation to plan documents and source data, the recorded expense can drift far from the true obligation.

## Self-Check

- Has each commission been classified as a cost to obtain a contract (potentially deferrable) versus general selling cost (expensed when incurred)?
- Where deferred, is the amortization period and method consistent with the transfer of the related goods or services and supported by a documented policy election?
- Are tiered, accelerator, SPIFF, split, and residual commissions calculated per the plan and reconciled to source sales or revenue data?
- Are variable commissions estimated consistently with the related variable consideration and trued up as the amount resolves?
- Are clawbacks, recoveries, chargebacks, and expected forfeitures reflected in the net commission cost and the deferred asset?
- Is the deferral limited to incremental costs that would not be incurred absent the contract, with fixed selling costs expensed?
- Is the commission expense recognized in the period consistent with the revenue recognized from the related contract in that period?
- Has the final recognition policy, recoveries, and tax treatment been confirmed with a qualified accountant and legal counsel for the relevant jurisdiction?
