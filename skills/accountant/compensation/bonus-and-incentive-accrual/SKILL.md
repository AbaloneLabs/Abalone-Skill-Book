# Bonus And Incentive Accrual

Bonus and incentive accrual looks straightforward because it is often a percentage of salary or a pool of profit. In practice it is a recurring source of period-end misstatement. The difficulty is that the amount is usually not finalized when the accounting period closes, the performance conditions may be only partially met, the population eligible to receive a bonus can change, and the timing of when the obligation is incurred differs from when it is paid. An accrual that feels reasonable can be wrong on period, probability, or amount.

This skill covers judgment for accruing bonus and incentive compensation. The accounting treatment of contingent compensation is governed by standards such as US ASC 710 and ASC 450 and international IAS 19, but employment contracts, bonus plan documents, tax withholding, and payroll law vary by jurisdiction and entity. Final determinations should be confirmed with a qualified accountant and, where contracts or law are involved, legal and tax professionals in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether A Present Obligation Exists

Accrual requires a present obligation as of the reporting date. The question is whether the entity, through its bonus plan, contract, board action, or past practice, is obligated or effectively committed to pay.

Establish:

- is there a formal written plan, an individual contract, a board-approved pool, or only historical practice?
- does the obligation arise from service rendered in the period, or is payment genuinely discretionary at the reporting date?
- have the conditions that create the obligation been met as of period end?
- is there a communicated commitment that creates an expectation of payment?

If payment is entirely discretionary and uncommitted at the reporting date, accrual may not be appropriate. If the entity has a pattern or a communicated intent that creates a constructive obligation, accrual may be required even without a signed document.

### Recognize Expense Over The Service Period

Bonus cost belongs in the period the related service is performed, not the period of approval or payment. This is the most common error.

Apply period discipline:

- an annual bonus tied to a calendar year of service is earned ratably across the year, even if approved the following year;
- a performance bonus tied to a multi-year cycle should be recognized over the performance period as service is rendered, subject to probability;
- a sign-on or retention bonus should generally be recognized over the required service or retention period, not all at payment;
- a bonus tied to a transaction or milestone is recognized when the related service or condition is met.

Ask whether the expense recorded matches the service performed in the period, independent of when the board approves or the cash is paid.

### Assess Probability And Estimate The Amount

When the final bonus is not yet determined, accrual requires a best estimate of the amount and an assessment that payment is probable, using the threshold defined by the applicable framework.

Estimation inputs:

- the formula or pool basis per the plan document;
- performance-to-date against targets and the likelihood of achieving year-end thresholds;
- the eligible population and expected headcount at payment date;
- historical adjustment patterns between accrual and final payout;
- caps, floors, modifiers, and discretionary adjustment authority;
- the effect of leavers, new hires, and promotions.

When a range of outcomes exists with no best estimate, some frameworks require use of the minimum of the range. Document the estimation method and the evidence supporting it, and update the estimate each reporting date.

### Separate Bonus Cost From Other Compensation

Do not let bonus blur into other compensation categories. Separate accounts and tracking support correct reporting, tax withholding, and analysis.

Distinguish:

- incentive or performance bonuses from base salary accrual;
- sign-on, retention, and referral bonuses with different service periods;
- commission-based incentives, which may follow revenue recognition timing;
- profit-sharing tied to defined results;
- discretionary bonuses with no formula;
- bonuses paid in equity or equity-equivalent instruments, which may fall under share-based payment rules.

Mixing these into one account hides service-period differences and creates reconciliation problems at payout.

### Reconcile Accruals To Final Payout And Adjust

A bonus accrual is an estimate that must be trued up to actual. Reconcile the accrued balance to the final approved payout and recognize the adjustment in the correct period.

Reconcile:

- opening accrual plus current-period expense less payments;
- the final approved bonus versus the accrued estimate, with variance explained;
- the effect of plan changes, forfeitures, and population shifts;
- unpaid balances still owed to current and former employees;
- amounts withheld for taxes and any employer payroll taxes.

Large recurring variances between accrual and payout signal that the estimation method is biased and should be revised.

### Handle Tax, Withholding, And Disclosure

Bonus payments carry payroll tax withholding, reporting, and disclosure obligations distinct from the expense accrual.

Confirm:

- tax withholding at the applicable supplemental or aggregate rate;
- employer payroll taxes accrued on the bonus;
- related-party disclosure for key personnel bonuses;
- segment reporting treatment where bonus cost follows the employee's segment;
- any regulatory or contractual cap on incentive compensation (for example in financial services).

## Common Traps

### Accruing Only At Year-End Or At Approval

Recording the entire bonus in the approval or payment period overstates expense in one period and understates it in the others. The cost is incurred as service is rendered, even when the amount is uncertain.

### Treating A Discretionary Bonus As Accrued Too Early

If the bonus is genuinely discretionary and no obligation exists at the reporting date, accruing it creates a liability that does not meet the recognition threshold. Distinguish constructive obligations from mere intent.

### Ignoring Performance Probability

Accruing the full target bonus when achievement is not probable overstates the liability. Conversely, accruing nothing when partial achievement is probable understates it. The estimate must reflect the probability-weighted or best-estimate outcome.

### Applying The Wrong Service Period For Sign-On And Retention

Recording a sign-on or retention bonus entirely at grant or at payment ignores the service condition. Most such bonuses should be recognized over the period of required service, with clawback and forfeiture considered.

### Letting Estimates Drift Without Reconciliation

If the accrual is never trued up to actual, errors compound and the liability becomes unreliable. Persistent variance indicates a flawed estimation method that should be corrected, not tolerated.

### Forgetting Employer Payroll Taxes And Withholding

Bonus expense is not just the net amount to employees. Employer payroll taxes and employee withholding affect both the liability and cash flows, and omission distorts compensation cost and the balance sheet.

## Self-Check

- Has it been determined whether a present or constructive obligation exists at the reporting date, supported by plan documents, contracts, or past practice?
- Is bonus expense recognized over the period of related service rather than at approval or payment?
- Does the accrued amount reflect a best estimate or probability-weighted outcome, with performance probability and population changes considered?
- Are performance, sign-on, retention, referral, commission, profit-sharing, and discretionary bonuses separated by service period and accounting treatment?
- Is the accrual reconciled to final approved payout, with variances explained and the estimation method revised when bias appears?
- Are employee tax withholding, employer payroll taxes, related-party disclosure, and any regulatory caps reflected?
- Is the bonus genuinely cash compensation, or should equity or share-based components be accounted for under share-based payment rules?
- Has the final treatment been confirmed with a qualified accountant and, where contracts or law are involved, legal and tax professionals for the relevant jurisdiction?
