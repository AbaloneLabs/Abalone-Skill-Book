# Product And Channel Profitability

A company can grow revenue and still lose money, because profitability varies enormously across products and channels. A premium product sold direct may carry a rich margin, while the same product sold through a discount channel with high returns and co-op funding may lose value. Product and channel profitability analysis separates winners from losers, but only when the cost assignment reflects the real economics of each product and route to market. A model that spreads shared costs by revenue hides the very differences it is meant to reveal.

Use this skill before analyzing profitability by product, brand, SKU, channel, or distribution partner, before recommending product mix, pricing, or channel decisions, or before reviewing whether a product or channel should be grown, restructured, or discontinued. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing profitability rankings that look precise but rest on arbitrary allocations that distort the comparison.

## Core Rules

### Define The Analysis Dimension Clearly

Profitability can be measured by product, product line, brand, SKU, channel, partner, region, or any combination. The dimension chosen drives the data and allocation logic.

Define:

- the primary dimension, product, channel, or both;
- the level of aggregation, SKU, line, brand, or category;
- the time period and whether seasonality matters;
- whether the analysis is gross margin, contribution margin, or fully allocated;
- the scope, one business unit, one region, or the whole company.

A combined product-by-channel view is often the most revealing, because the same product can be profitable in one channel and unprofitable in another. But it is also the most demanding on data.

### Capture Channel-Specific Revenue Adjustments

Channels shape net revenue differently. A direct sale, a distributor sale, and a marketplace sale of the same product yield different net proceeds.

Adjust revenue by channel for:

- trade discounts, rebates, and co-op or marketing allowances;
- commissions and referral fees;
- marketplace and payment processing fees;
- returns, allowances, and chargebacks typical of the channel;
- freight terms and who bears logistics cost;
- payment timing and credit risk by channel.

Gross revenue by channel hides these effects. Net revenue by channel is the starting point for meaningful comparison.

### Assign Direct Costs By Actual Driver

Direct costs should follow the product or channel that actually consumes them.

Assign by driver:

- cost of goods sold to the product;
- fulfillment, packaging, and freight to the channel or product;
- sales commissions to the channel;
- channel-specific marketing and merchandising;
- returns processing and reverse logistics;
- dedicated service or support for a product or channel.

Where a cost is shared across products or channels, use the strongest available driver and document it. Avoid the temptation to allocate everything by revenue.

### Handle Shared And Fixed Costs In Layers

Shared costs, such as corporate marketing, shared warehousing, executive oversight, and shared technology, support multiple products and channels. Their allocation is judgmental and can swing the ranking.

Report in layers:

- gross margin, revenue less product cost;
- contribution margin, less directly traceable channel and product costs;
- allocated shared cost layer, with the driver disclosed;
- unallocated corporate or fixed cost held below the line.

Layered reporting lets decision-makers see where value is created before arbitrary allocations obscure it. A single fully allocated number is often less useful and less honest.

### Account For Product And Channel Interdependence

Products and channels do not exist in isolation. A loss-leading product may drive traffic to profitable ones. A channel may look unprofitable but serve as the entry point for customers who later buy direct.

Consider:

- halo effects, where one product supports sales of others;
- customer acquisition value of a channel beyond the immediate transaction;
- cross-sell and upsell dependencies;
- minimum scale required to sustain a channel or product line.

Discontinuing an apparently unprofitable product or channel without considering interdependence can destroy more value than it saves.

### Validate Against Operational And Market Knowledge

A profitability model must reconcile to the ledger and make sense to the people who run the products and channels.

Validate by:

- reconciling total revenue and cost to the ledger;
- reviewing surprising results with product, sales, and operations leaders;
- testing sensitivity to allocation bases;
- confirming the model explains known margin patterns.

When the model contradicts market experience, investigate before acting. The model may be wrong, or the intuition may be wrong, but the gap must be understood.

### Use The Output To Inform Strategy, Not Replace Judgment

Profitability analysis is decision support. It informs pricing, mix, channel investment, and discontinuation, but it does not replace strategic judgment about brand, market position, customer relationships, and future potential.

Present results with assumptions and limitations explicit. Recommend actions, but frame them as options to consider alongside strategic factors the model cannot capture.

### Acknowledge Management And External Reporting Boundaries

Product and channel profitability is management accounting. External segment reporting, transfer pricing, and tax follow their own rules and may require different allocation methods. Where profitability analysis informs regulated pricing, government contracts, or intercompany arrangements, confirm the permitted methods with a qualified professional before relying on the outputs externally.

## Common Traps

### Allocating Shared Cost By Revenue

Spreading shared cost by revenue makes high-revenue products and channels look more profitable than they are and entrenches distortion.

### Comparing Only Gross Margin

Gross margin ignores the cost to serve each channel. Two products with the same gross margin can have very different net profitability.

### Ignoring Channel Revenue Adjustments

Commissions, allowances, fees, and returns vary by channel. Using gross revenue hides the real net economics.

### Discontinuing Based On A Single Number

Cutting an unprofitable product or channel without considering halo effects, customer acquisition, or scale can destroy hidden value.

### Stale Allocation Drivers

Cost drivers change as the business evolves. A model built years ago no longer reflects current economics.

### Over-Allocation Hiding Contribution

Loading all corporate cost onto products and channels can make everything look unprofitable and paralyze decision-making.

### Confusing Management Analysis With Segment Reporting

Internal profitability analysis is not external segment reporting. Methods and disclosures differ and must be confirmed against the applicable framework.

## Self-Check

- Is the analysis dimension, product, channel, or both, defined with a consistent level of aggregation and period?
- Is revenue adjusted for channel-specific discounts, commissions, fees, returns, freight, and credit risk?
- Are direct costs assigned by actual driver rather than by a revenue spread?
- Are shared and fixed costs reported in layers, with allocation drivers disclosed and unallocated cost held below the line?
- Are product and channel interdependencies, halo effects, and customer acquisition value considered before recommending discontinuation?
- Does the model reconcile to the ledger and validate against the operational experience of product and channel leaders?
- Is the output used to inform strategy alongside factors the model cannot capture?
- Does the analysis acknowledge management accounting limits and flag where external segment reporting, transfer pricing, or regulated use requires framework confirmation and professional review?
