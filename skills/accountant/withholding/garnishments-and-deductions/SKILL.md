# Garnishments And Deductions

Garnishments and voluntary deductions reduce an employee's net pay and create a temporary liability the employer holds for a third party: a court, a tax authority, a retirement custodian, or a benefit vendor. The accounting job is to withhold the right amount under the right limit, respect the priority order when multiple orders compete for the same wages, remit on time, and never push the employee's remaining pay below the legally protected floor. Agents often treat these as routine payroll deductions, but they carry fiduciary responsibility, statutory limits, and real consequences for the employee if mishandled.

The harm this skill prevents is twofold. For the employer, mishandling a garnishment can create liability for the underlying debt the order was meant to collect, plus penalties. For the employee, an over-withheld garnishment or a mis-prioritized deduction can leave them with less than the legally protected minimum take-home pay. Both outcomes are avoidable with disciplined accounting.

This is accounting guidance, not legal or tax advice. Garnishment types, withholding limits, priority rules, protected earnings thresholds, and remittance timing vary enormously by jurisdiction and by order type. Route definitive garnishment compliance, withholding limits, and priority questions to a qualified payroll provider, HR professional, or legal advisor. The agent's freedom is to structure the withholding, liability, and remittance accounting correctly and to surface the constraints; it is not to assert legal limits or priorities as universal facts.

## Core Rules

### Classify Each Deduction By Authority And Purpose

Not all deductions are alike. Some are involuntary court or agency orders, some are statutory, and some are voluntary employee elections. Each type carries different rules for limit, priority, and remittance, so classify before recording.

Classification categories:

- involuntary garnishments such as child support, spousal support, tax levies, bankruptcy orders, and creditor garnishments;
- statutory deductions required by law, such as certain disability or paid leave contributions;
- voluntary benefit deductions such as health, dental, and life insurance premiums;
- voluntary retirement deductions such as plan deferrals and loan repayments;
- other voluntary deductions such as union dues, charitable contributions, or loan repayments to the employer.

The classification determines which limits and priorities apply, so document it on the employee's deduction record.

### Apply Statutory Withholding Limits And Protected Earnings

Most garnishment jurisdictions cap the amount that can be withheld from a single paycheck, and many protect a floor of earnings below which no garnishment may reach. The cap is often a percentage of disposable earnings, defined after mandatory deductions, and the protected floor often increases for low earners or for support orders.

Limit considerations:

- compute disposable earnings after legally required deductions, not after voluntary ones;
- apply the percentage cap that corresponds to the order type, which differs for support, tax, and creditor garnishments;
- protect the earnings floor so the employee never receives less than the legally permitted minimum;
- recognize that support orders often permit higher withholding than creditor garnishments;
- handle multiple orders so the combined withholding does not breach the overall cap.

When the limit calculation is unclear, flag it and route to a specialist rather than guessing, because over-withholding harms the employee and under-withholding exposes the employer.

### Order Competing Garnishments By Legal Priority

When two or more orders target the same wages, they are not paid first-come, first-served by default. Jurisdictions impose a priority order, typically placing child support and certain tax levies ahead of creditor garnishments, and limiting the total that can be withheld across all orders.

Priority handling:

- identify each active order and its type;
- apply the jurisdiction's priority ranking, which commonly elevates support and tax over creditors;
- allocate the permitted withholding across orders in priority sequence until the cap is exhausted;
- document the allocation so a later dispute can be reconstructed;
- hold lower-priority orders unpaid when the cap is reached, and resume them when capacity opens.

### Record Withholding As A Liability To A Third Party

The moment a garnishment or voluntary deduction is withheld, it ceases to be the employee's wages and becomes a liability owed to the third party. Record it in its own account, not blended into a generic deductions payable.

Accounting treatment:

- debit wage expense at gross and credit net pay to the employee;
- credit a separate liability account for each garnishment or deduction type;
- keep garnishments distinct from voluntary benefit and retirement deductions;
- retain the case number, order reference, or vendor reference on the liability record;
- reconcile each liability to the underlying orders or vendor invoices.

### Remit On The Order's Or Vendor's Schedule

Garnishments and deductions are not remitted on the employer's convenience. Each order specifies its remittance cadence, often per pay period, and each benefit or retirement vendor has its own deposit rule, often tied to the pay date.

Remittance discipline:

- remit child support and most court orders on the schedule in the order, frequently within days of the pay date;
- remit tax levies on the authority's schedule;
- remit benefit and retirement deductions on the vendor's required timing;
- record the remittance when cash leaves, retaining proof and the reference number;
- reconcile the liability to zero, or to a known in-transit balance, after each remittance cycle.

### Protect Net Pay And Communicate With The Employee

Garnishments affect take-home pay, and employees have a right to understand what was withheld and why. Beyond the legal floor, clear communication reduces disputes and errors.

Practices:

- provide a pay statement that itemizes each garnishment and deduction with its reference;
- respond to employee questions about the order, the amount, and the remittance;
- never disclose garnishment details beyond those entitled to know;
- track remittance so the employee can confirm the third party received the funds;
- escalate any order that appears invalid, expired, or conflicting before withholding.

### Track Expiration, Modification, And Release Of Orders

Garnishment orders are not permanent. They expire on a stated date, are modified by subsequent court action, or are released when the underlying debt is satisfied. Continuing to withhold under a stale order creates employer liability.

Lifecycle tracking:

- record the effective date, expiration, and satisfaction terms of each order;
- stop withholding when the order expires or is released;
- adjust withholding when a modification arrives;
- remit any final balance and obtain a release confirmation;
- close the liability account and retain the documentation.

## Common Traps

### Treating Garnishments As Ordinary Payroll Deductions

Garnishments are fiduciary obligations to courts and agencies, not routine line items. Blending them into a generic deductions payable hides the case references, the priority logic, and the remittance deadlines.

### Breaching The Withholding Cap Or Protected Floor

Over-withholding beyond the statutory cap, or pushing the employee below the protected earnings floor, harms the employee and exposes the employer. Compute disposable earnings and the cap for every check, not just at setup.

### Paying Competing Orders In Arrival Order

When multiple orders compete, arrival order does not determine payment order. Applying first-come-first-served instead of legal priority underpays support and tax orders and overpays creditors.

### Remitting On The Employer's Convenience

Each order and vendor has its own remittance cadence. Holding withheld funds until a convenient monthly batch breaches the order's timing and can be treated as a failure to remit.

### Blending Garnishment And Voluntary Deduction Liabilities

Combining child support, tax levies, creditor garnishments, and voluntary benefit deductions into one account makes reconciliation and proof of remittance impossible. Separate accounts per type are what make discipline visible.

### Continuing To Withhold Under An Expired Or Released Order

Orders expire, are modified, or are satisfied. Withholding under a stale order creates employer liability for amounts that should not have been collected and requires reversal and refund.

### Disclosing Garnishment Details Improperly

Garnishment information is sensitive. Sharing it beyond those with a legitimate need can breach privacy obligations and damage the employee, even when the withholding itself was correct.

## Self-Check

- Is each deduction classified by authority and purpose, distinguishing involuntary garnishments, statutory deductions, and voluntary elections?
- Is the statutory withholding cap computed on disposable earnings and the protected earnings floor respected for every paycheck?
- Are competing garnishments ordered by the jurisdiction's legal priority rather than by arrival sequence?
- Is each withholding recorded in its own liability account with the case, order, or vendor reference retained?
- Is each liability remitted on the order's or vendor's required schedule rather than a convenient monthly batch?
- Is the remittance recorded when cash leaves, with proof and reference retained, and the liability reconciled to zero?
- Does the pay statement itemize each garnishment and deduction so the employee can understand and verify it?
- Are expired, modified, or released orders detected and stopped so no stale withholding continues?
- Are conflicting, invalid, or unclear orders escalated before withholding rather than applied automatically?
- Are jurisdiction-specific caps, priority rules, protected floors, and remittance timing confirmed with a qualified payroll provider, HR professional, or legal advisor rather than asserted as universal facts?
