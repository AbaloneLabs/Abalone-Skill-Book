# Lessor Lease Accounting

Lessor accounting determines whether a lease is treated as a sale, a financing arrangement, or a simple rental. The classification drives whether the lessor derecognizes the underlying asset, recognizes profit at commencement, and how income is recognized over the lease term. Because lessor accounting interacts with revenue recognition and derecognition, casual classification can front-load profit, misstate assets, or fail to derecognize risks that have transferred.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during lessor lease accounting. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist advice on leveraged leases or sale-leaseback arrangements. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 842, IFRS 16) and consult a qualified professional for complex structures.

## Core Rules

### Classify The Lease From The Lessor's Perspective

Under US GAAP ASC 842, a lessor classifies a lease as a sales-type lease, a direct financing lease, or an operating lease. Under IFRS 16, a lessor classifies a lease as a finance lease or an operating lease.

- A lease is a finance lease (IFRS) or sales-type lease (US GAAP) if it transfers substantially all the risks and rewards incidental to ownership. Indicators include transfer of ownership, a purchase option the lessee is reasonably certain to exercise, the lease term covering the major part of the asset's economic life, the present value of lease payments amounting to substantially all of the asset's fair value, the asset being specialized, the lessor's losses borne by the lessee, and residual value fluctuations accruing to the lessee.
- Under US GAAP, apply the same five classification criteria as lessee accounting. If any criterion is met, the lease is a sales-type or direct financing lease. If none are met, it is an operating lease.
- Distinguish sales-type from direct financing leases under US GAAP. A sales-type lease arises when the lease meets a classification criterion and the lessor is a manufacturer or dealer, or when the present value of lease payments plus any third-party residual value guarantee equals or exceeds the carrying amount of the underlying asset. A direct financing lease arises when the criteria are met but the lessor is not a manufacturer or dealer and the present value of lease payments plus residual does not equal or exceed the carrying amount.
- Under IFRS 16, there is no separate sales-type and direct financing distinction; all finance leases are accounted for similarly, with manufacturer or dealer lessors recognizing selling profit or loss at commencement.

### Recognize A Sales-Type Or Finance Lease Correctly

At commencement of a sales-type or finance lease, the lessor derecognizes the underlying asset and recognizes a lease receivable and, where applicable, unearned income and a residual asset.

- The net investment in the lease comprises the lease receivable plus the residual asset (the estimated unguaranteed residual value discounted to present value).
- The lease receivable is measured at the present value of the future lease payments plus any initial direct costs (for direct financing leases under US GAAP) using the rate implicit in the lease.
- For sales-type leases, recognize the selling profit or loss at commencement as the difference between the fair value of the underlying asset (or the present value of lease payments plus residual, if lower) and the carrying amount of the asset.
- For direct financing leases, do not recognize profit at commencement. Instead, recognize an unearned income amount and amortize it into income over the lease term using the effective interest method.
- For manufacturer or dealer lessors under IFRS, recognize selling profit or loss at commencement, with costs reduced by initial direct costs.

### Account For Operating Leases

For operating leases, the lessor retains the underlying asset on its balance sheet and recognizes lease income over the lease term.

- Keep the underlying asset on the balance sheet and continue to depreciate it according to the lessor's depreciation policy.
- Recognize lease income, including any variable lease payments that are not based on an index or rate, on a straight-line basis over the lease term unless another systematic basis is more representative.
- Capitalize initial direct costs incurred in obtaining the operating lease and recognize them as an expense over the lease term on the same basis as lease income.
- Recognize variable lease payments not included in the lease receivable in income in the period they are incurred.

### Measure The Rate Implicit In The Lease

The rate implicit in the lease is central to lessor accounting because it determines the lease receivable, the unearned income, and the income recognition pattern.

- The rate implicit in the lease is the discount rate that causes the aggregate present value of the lease payments and the unguaranteed residual value to equal the sum of the fair value of the underlying asset and any initial direct costs of the lessor (for a direct financing lease) at commencement.
- The lessor generally knows this rate because it sets the lease terms and estimates the residual value. Document the calculation.
- If the residual estimate changes after commencement, do not retroactively adjust the rate. Instead, adjust the residual asset prospectively and recognize the effect in income.

### Handle Initial Direct Costs Appropriately

Initial direct costs are incremental costs of obtaining a lease that would not have been incurred if the lease had not been obtained.

- For operating leases, capitalize initial direct costs and expense them over the lease term.
- For sales-type leases, expense initial direct costs immediately as part of the cost of sales, offset against selling profit.
- For direct financing leases, include initial direct costs in the initial measurement of the net investment in the lease and recognize them as a reduction of income over the lease term.
- Do not capitalize costs that would have been incurred regardless of whether the lease was obtained, such as general advertising or negotiating costs that did not result in a lease.

### Account For Residual Value Guarantees And Unguaranteed Residuals

The treatment of residual values affects the measurement of the net investment and the income pattern.

- A residual value guarantee provided by the lessee or a third party unrelated to the lessor is included in the lease receivable at its present value. An unguaranteed residual is included in the residual asset at present value.
- Reassess the estimated residual value at each reporting date. Under US GAAP, decreases in the estimated residual value are recognized immediately. Increases are prohibited. Under IFRS, revisions to the estimated residual value are accounted for prospectively.
- For sales-type leases, the lessor is exposed only to the unguaranteed residual, so the income pattern reflects that exposure.

### Apply Sale-Leaseback And Initial Direct Cost Rules Carefully

A sale-leaseback transaction involves the owner of an asset selling it and immediately leasing it back. The accounting depends on whether the transfer qualifies as a sale under the revenue recognition standard.

- If the transfer qualifies as a sale, the seller-lessee derecognizes the asset and recognizes a right-of-use asset, and the buyer-lessor accounts for the purchase and the lease from the lessor's perspective.
- If the transfer does not qualify as a sale, the transaction is a financing arrangement. The seller-lessee retains the asset and recognizes a financial liability, and the buyer-lessor recognizes a financial asset.
- Confirm that the sale criteria are met before applying sale accounting, because failing sale criteria means the arrangement is a failed sale with financing.

## Common Traps

### Misclassifying A Finance Lease As Operating

Treating a lease that transfers substantially all risks and rewards as an operating lease keeps the asset on the balance sheet and defers profit recognition, misstating both the balance sheet and income. The classification criteria must be applied rigorously.

### Recognizing Profit At Commencement For A Direct Financing Lease

Only sales-type leases (or manufacturer or dealer lessors under IFRS) recognize profit at commencement. Direct financing leases defer the difference as unearned income and recognize it over the lease term. Front-loading profit in a direct financing lease overstates income.

### Using The Wrong Treatment For Initial Direct Costs

The treatment of initial direct costs depends on classification. Expensing costs that should be capitalized for an operating lease, or capitalizing costs that should reduce selling profit for a sales-type lease, distorts both assets and income.

### Failing To Update Residual Value Estimates

A residual estimate that becomes clearly unrealistic must be updated. Under US GAAP, decreases are recognized immediately and increases are prohibited. Holding an overstated residual overstates the net investment and defers loss recognition.

### Treating Variable Lease Payments Inconsistently

Variable payments based on an index or rate are included in the lease measurement and remeasured when the index or rate changes. Variable payments based on performance or usage are recognized in income when incurred. Confusing the two misstates the receivable.

### Recognizing Operating Lease Income On A Cash Basis

Operating lease income must be recognized on a straight-line or systematic basis over the lease term, even if cash payments are uneven. Recognizing income as cash is received misstates period income, especially with rent holidays or escalating payments.

### Failing To Derecognize Or Retain The Asset Correctly

For finance leases, the underlying asset must be derecognized and replaced with the net investment in the lease. Retaining the asset while also recognizing a receivable double-counts. For operating leases, the asset must be retained. Mixing the treatments is a fundamental error.

### Overlooking Sale-Leaseback Failed Sale Treatment

If the sale-leaseback does not meet sale criteria, treating it as a sale derecognizes an asset that should remain on the books and recognizes a gain that does not exist. The financing alternative must be applied.

## Self-Check

- Has the lease been classified as a finance or sales-type, direct financing, or operating lease using the applicable criteria, and is the classification documented?
- For a sales-type or finance lease, has the underlying asset been derecognized and the net investment in the lease recognized, with selling profit or unearned income treated correctly?
- For an operating lease, is the underlying asset retained on the balance sheet and depreciated, with income recognized on a straight-line or systematic basis?
- Is the rate implicit in the lease calculated and documented, using the lessor's residual assumptions?
- Are initial direct costs treated according to classification, capitalized for operating leases, expensed for sales-type leases, and included in the net investment for direct financing leases?
- Are residual value estimates reassessed at each reporting date, with decreases recognized immediately under US GAAP and revisions accounted for prospectively under IFRS?
- Are variable lease payments based on an index or rate included in the lease measurement and remeasured, while performance-based payments are recognized when incurred?
- For sale-leaseback transactions, has the sale criterion been assessed before applying sale accounting, with failed sales treated as financing?
- Are disclosures complete, including the maturity analysis of lease receivables, income recognition, and significant judgments?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework (US GAAP ASC 842 or IFRS 16) been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for leveraged leases or complex residual guarantees?
