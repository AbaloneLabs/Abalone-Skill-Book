# Lease Modification And Remeasurement

Lease accounting does not end at commencement. Leases are modified, extended, terminated, and reassessed throughout their lives. Each of these events can require remeasurement of the lease liability and the right-of-use asset, and the accounting differs depending on whether the change is a modification, a change in estimate, or a full or partial termination. Applying the wrong treatment or failing to remeasure when required leaves lease balances stale and misstates both the balance sheet and expense.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during lease modification and remeasurement. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist legal advice on lease amendments. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 842, IFRS 16) and consult a qualified professional for complex modifications.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish A Lease Modification From A Change In Terms

A lease modification is a change to the terms and conditions of a contract that creates or changes the scope of the lease, or the consideration for the lease, that was not part of the original terms. Not every change in the lease relationship is a modification.

- A modification includes adding or terminating the right to use one or more underlying assets, or extending or shortening the contractual lease term.
- A change in the lease payment amount alone, driven by a mechanism in the original contract such as a CPI escalation or a market rent reset, is not a modification. It is a change in estimate that triggers remeasurement, not modification accounting.
- If the modification is not accounted for as a separate lease, remeasure the lease liability and right-of-use asset using the modified terms.
- Determine whether the modification grants an additional right of use and is priced commensurately. If so, account for it as a separate lease.

### Determine Whether A Modification Is A Separate Lease

A modification is accounted for as a separate lease if it both adds an additional right of use and the increase in consideration is commensurate with the standalone price of the additional right of use.

- Adding additional floor space or an additional asset at a price reflecting standalone pricing is a separate lease. The original lease continues unchanged, and the new lease is recognized from its commencement.
- If the modification does not add an additional right of use, or the pricing is not commensurate, it is not a separate lease and the existing lease is remeasured.

### Remeasure The Lease Liability And Right-Of-Use Asset For Modifications

When a modification is not a separate lease, remeasure the lease liability and adjust the right-of-use asset prospectively from the modification date.

- Update the lease payments to reflect the modified terms.
- Update the discount rate. For modifications that change the scope of the lease (adding or removing assets, changing the term), use a revised discount rate reflecting the modified terms at the modification date. For changes that do not change the scope (such as a change in payment amount), use the original discount rate.
- Remeasure the lease liability at the present value of the revised lease payments using the appropriate discount rate.
- Adjust the right-of-use asset by the difference between the remeasured lease liability and the previous carrying amount, adjusted for any payment made at the modification date. If the modification reduces the scope, reduce the right-of-use asset and recognize a gain or loss for any difference.

### Handle Partial Terminations And Scope Reductions

A partial termination is a reduction in the scope of the lease, such as giving back part of the leased space. This is a form of modification that requires specific treatment.

- Reduce the lease liability and the right-of-use asset in proportion to the reduction in scope.
- Recognize a gain or loss for the difference between the reduction in the right-of-use asset and the reduction in the lease liability, recognized in profit or loss.
- If the partial termination is combined with other modifications, separate the accounting for the termination from the modification of the remaining lease.

### Reassess The Lease Term And Options

The lease term must be reassessed when there is a significant event or significant change in circumstances that is within the lessee's control and that affects whether the lessee is reasonably certain to exercise an option.

- Events triggering reassessment include the lessee exercising an option not previously included, the lessee not exercising an option previously included, or a significant event or change that affects the reasonably certain assessment.
- When the lease term changes, remeasure the lease liability using a revised discount rate reflecting the revised term at the reassessment date.
- Adjust the right-of-use asset for the change in the lease liability.

### Remeasure For Changes In The Estimate Of Index Or Rate Based Payments

Variable lease payments based on an index or rate, such as CPI, are included in the lease liability using the index or rate at commencement. When the index or rate changes, remeasure.

- Adjust the lease liability for the change in the index or rate using the original discount rate.
- Adjust the right-of-use asset for the change in the lease liability.
- Do not include variable payments based on performance or usage in the lease liability. These are recognized as expense when incurred.

### Remeasure For Changes In Residual Value Guarantees And Purchase Options

If the lessee provides a residual value guarantee or the assessment of a purchase option changes, remeasure the lease liability.

- Changes in the amount expected to be payable under a residual value guarantee trigger remeasurement using the original discount rate.
- Changes in the assessment of whether a purchase option is reasonably certain to be exercised trigger remeasurement using a revised discount rate.

### Account For Lease Modifications Under IFRS And US GAAP Consistently

The core principle of remeasuring the lease liability and adjusting the right-of-use asset applies under both frameworks, with limited differences in presentation and the treatment of certain modifications.

- Under both frameworks, modifications that are separate leases are accounted for as new leases.
- Under both frameworks, modifications that are not separate leases require remeasurement of the lease liability and a corresponding adjustment to the right-of-use asset.
- Confirm the specific disclosure requirements of the applicable framework, including the effect of modifications and remeasurements.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Market Rent Reset As A Modification

A change in lease payments driven by a contractual mechanism such as a market rent review or CPI escalation is a change in estimate, not a modification. Using modification accounting and a revised discount rate when the original rate should be used distorts the liability.

### Failing To Account For A Separate Lease

A modification that adds an additional right of use at commensurate pricing should be a separate lease. Combining it into the existing lease and remeasuring distorts the original lease and misstates the new lease's commencement.

### Using The Wrong Discount Rate On Remeasurement

Scope changes require a revised discount rate, while non-scope changes use the original rate. Applying the revised rate to a non-scope change, or vice versa, misstates the lease liability and right-of-use asset.

### Failing To Recognize A Gain Or Loss On Partial Termination

When part of a lease is terminated, the difference between the reduction in the right-of-use asset and the reduction in the lease liability must be recognized in profit or loss. Simply reducing both balances without recognizing the gain or loss omits the economic effect.

### Not Reassessing The Lease Term When Circumstances Change

A lessee that decides to extend a lease or abandon a renewal option has experienced a significant event within its control. Failing to reassess and remeasure leaves the lease liability understated or overstated.

### Overlooking Index-Based Remeasurement

When CPI or another index changes, the lease liability must be remeasured. Treating the adjustment as a period expense without remeasuring the liability understates the balance sheet obligation.

### Failing To Update The Right-Of-Use Asset Correctly

The right-of-use asset is adjusted for the change in the lease liability, but the adjustment is not always one-for-one. Payments at the modification date, scope reductions, and amortization patterns all affect the right-of-use asset adjustment. Applying a simplistic one-to-one adjustment is often wrong.

### Inconsistent Treatment Of Modifications Across The Portfolio

Modifications should be identified and accounted for systematically. Relying on manual tracking and ad hoc adjustments leads to missed modifications and stale balances. Implement a process to identify and account for modifications at each reporting date.

## Self-Check

- Has each change in lease terms been assessed to determine whether it is a modification, a change in estimate, or a separate lease, and is the conclusion documented?
- For modifications that are separate leases, is the additional right of use and commensurate pricing confirmed and the new lease recognized from its commencement?
- For modifications that are not separate leases, is the lease liability remeasured using the revised payments and the correct discount rate, with the right-of-use asset adjusted accordingly?
- For partial terminations, is the scope reduction accounted for with a proportional reduction in the lease liability and right-of-use asset, and is the gain or loss recognized?
- Is the lease term reassessed when significant events within the lessee's control occur, with the liability remeasured using a revised discount rate?
- Are index or rate based variable payments remeasured when the index or rate changes, using the original discount rate?
- Are residual value guarantee and purchase option reassessments accounted for with remeasurement using the appropriate discount rate?
- Is the right-of-use asset adjustment calculated correctly, reflecting payments, scope changes, and amortization, not a simplistic one-to-one adjustment?
- Are disclosures complete, including the nature and effect of modifications and remeasurements?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework (US GAAP ASC 842 or IFRS 16) been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for complex amendments or real estate modifications?
