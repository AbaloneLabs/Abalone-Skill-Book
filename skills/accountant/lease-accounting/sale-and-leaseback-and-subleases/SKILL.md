# Sale And Leaseback And Subleases

A sale-and-leaseback looks like a sale and a lease stapled together, but the accounting hinges on a single gate: did control of the asset actually transfer to the buyer-lessor under revenue recognition criteria? If yes, the seller-lessee derecognizes the asset, recognizes a right-of-use asset for the leaseback, and may recognize a gain, but only to the extent that it has not retained control through the lease. If no, the whole transaction is a financing and nothing is derecognized. Subleases add a second classification problem: the intermediate lessor (the original lessee now subleasing) must classify the sublease based on its own right-of-use asset, not the underlying asset. Agents routinely book the gain in full and skip the control test, or classify a sublease as if it were the head lease.

Use this skill before recording a sale-and-leaseback, evaluating whether a sale occurred, recognizing or deferring gain, accounting for a failed sale-leaseback, or classifying and measuring a sublease. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating a sale-and-leaseback as an automatic sale and a sublease as a passthrough.

Sale-and-leaseback and sublease accounting differ between US GAAP (ASC 842 and ASC 606) and IFRS (IFRS 16 and IFRS 15), particularly on the measurement of the right-of-use asset retained, gain recognition limits, and sublease classification inputs. Local GAAP may vary. This skill gives the general framework; the applicable standard governs. For material or unusual transactions, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific deal.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Sale Assessment Before Anything Else

A sale-and-leaseback is only a sale if the transfer of the asset to the buyer-lessor meets the revenue recognition criteria for a sale of goods. That requires the seller-lessee to transfer control of the asset to the buyer-lessor. Control means the buyer-lessor obtains the ability to direct the use of, and obtain substantially all the remaining benefits from, the asset.

Indicators that a sale did not occur include: a repurchase option or obligation that effectively keeps control with the seller-lessee; a leaseback term that is for substantially all of the asset's remaining economic life; lease payments that are not at market rate; or other terms that make the transaction economically a financing secured by the asset. Evaluate substance over form. If control did not transfer, the transaction is a failed sale-and-leaseback.

### Account For A Successful Sale-And-Leaseback

When a sale occurred, the seller-lessee derecognizes the asset and recognizes a right-of-use asset and a lease liability for the leaseback under normal lessee accounting. The right-of-use asset is measured by reference to the proportion of the previous carrying amount that relates to the right of use retained, not at the sale proceeds.

Gain or loss on the sale is the difference between the sale proceeds and the carrying amount of the asset. Under IFRS, only the portion of the gain or loss that relates to the rights transferred to the buyer-lessor is recognized; the portion relating to the rights retained is not recognized. Under US GAAP, gain recognition rules may differ based on whether the leaseback is a finance or operating lease and the size of the leaseback. Apply the applicable standard's gain limit precisely; do not book the full gain by default.

### Account For A Failed Sale-And-Leaseback As Financing

When control did not transfer, no sale occurred. The seller-lessee does not derecognize the asset and does not recognize a sale or gain. The proceeds received are accounted for as a financial liability (a loan), and the buyer-lessor accounts for the consideration as a financial asset.

Recognize interest expense on the financial liability over time using the effective interest method, and continue to depreciate the asset as if it had never been sold. The "lease" payments are treated as principal and interest repayments of the financing. This treatment applies regardless of how the documents are titled.

### Measure The Right-Of-Use Asset Retained Correctly

In a successful sale-leaseback, the right-of-use asset retained is not the full asset and not the sale proceeds. It is the previous carrying amount adjusted for the proportion of the asset's fair value that relates to the rights retained. This prevents the seller-lessee from recognizing a gain on the rights it still controls through the leaseback.

Document the calculation: previous carrying amount; fair value of the asset; proportion of rights transferred versus retained; resulting right-of-use asset; and the gain limited to the rights transferred. A miscalculation here overstates the asset and the gain.

### Classify A Sublease From The Intermediate Lessor's Perspective

When a lessee subleases an asset to a third party, the original lessee becomes an intermediate lessor. The sublease is classified (sales-type, direct financing, or operating) based on the right-of-use asset arising from the head lease, not the underlying physical asset. The intermediate lessor applies the lessor classification criteria to the right-of-use asset.

The intermediate lessor continues to account for the head lease as a lessee (right-of-use asset and lease liability) and accounts for the sublease as a lessor (net investment or retained right-of-use asset, depending on classification). Do not classify the sublease by looking at the underlying asset's economic life; look at the right-of-use asset the intermediate lessor controls.

### Recognize Gain Or Loss On A Sublease

When the intermediate lessor subleases the asset and the sublease qualifies as a finance or sales-type lease, the intermediate lessor may recognize a gain or loss representing the difference between the sublease receivable plus residual and the carrying amount of the right-of-use asset. For an operating sublease, the right-of-use asset is retained and income is recognized over the sublease term.

The head-lease liability and any remaining right-of-use asset continue on the books. The intermediate lessor now has both a lessee obligation (to the head lessor) and a lessor position (against the sublessee). Reconcile both sides each period.

### Eliminate Inappropriate Profit On Related-Party Or Synthetic Arrangements

If the buyer-lessor and seller-lessee are related parties, or the transaction is designed to achieve a specific accounting result rather than reflect a genuine sale, the substance must be assessed over the legal form. A sale to a special-purpose entity controlled by the seller, or a transaction with terms engineered to derecognize the asset while retaining all risks, may not qualify as a sale. Apply consolidation and substance tests before recognizing gain.

### Disclose Sale-Leaseback And Sublease Activity

Disclose the nature of sale-and-leaseback transactions, whether a sale was determined to have occurred, the gain or loss recognized and any gain deferred, the carrying amount of right-of-use assets that arose from sale-and-leasebacks, and any commitments under failed sale-leasebacks. For subleases, disclose sublease income and the carrying amount of any right-of-use assets subleased to third parties.

## Common Traps

### Booking The Full Gain Without The Control Test

The sale-and-leaseback only produces a sale and a gain if control transferred under revenue criteria. Assuming a sale because the documents say so derecognizes the asset and recognizes a gain that should not exist.

### Recognizing Gain On The Rights Retained

Even when a sale occurred, the gain is limited to the rights transferred to the buyer-lessor. The portion of the gain relating to the rights the seller-lessee retains through the leaseback is not recognized. Booking the full sale-versus-carrying-amount gain overstates income.

### Treating A Failed Sale-Leaseback As A Sale Anyway

If control did not transfer, the proceeds are a loan. Recording a sale, derecognizing the asset, and recognizing a financing liability without interest leaves the balance sheet wrong and misses the interest expense.

### Classifying A Sublease Using The Underlying Asset

The sublease classification uses the intermediate lessor's right-of-use asset, not the underlying physical asset. Using the underlying asset's life or fair value can flip the classification and misstate whether profit is recognized upfront.

### Forgetting The Head Lease Continues After A Sublease

Subleasing does not release the intermediate lessor from the head lease. The head-lease liability and the obligation to the head lessor remain on the books. Treating the sublease as terminating the head lease understates liabilities.

### Ignoring Related-Party Substance

A sale to a related party or a special-purpose entity may be consolidated, eliminating the sale entirely. Recognizing gain on an intra-group or controlled-entity transaction misstates the group accounts.

### Mispricing The Leaseback And Missing The Financing Signal

If the leaseback payments are not at market rate, the transaction likely contains an embedded financing element. The off-market portion is separated and accounted for as additional consideration or financing, not as part of the sale price.

## Self-Check

- Has a sale assessment been performed under revenue control-transfer criteria before any sale or gain is recognized?
- If a sale occurred, is the asset derecognized, a right-of-use asset measured by reference to the rights retained, and the gain limited to the rights transferred to the buyer-lessor?
- If control did not transfer, are the proceeds accounted for as a financial liability with interest recognized over time and the asset retained and depreciated?
- Is the right-of-use asset retained in a successful sale-leaseback calculated from the previous carrying amount and the proportion of rights retained, not from sale proceeds?
- Is any sublease classified from the intermediate lessor's perspective using the right-of-use asset, not the underlying physical asset?
- For a finance or sales-type sublease, is gain or loss recognized between the sublease receivable plus residual and the right-of-use asset carrying amount?
- Does the head-lease liability and any retained right-of-use asset remain on the intermediate lessor's books after the sublease?
- Have related-party or special-purpose-entity transactions been assessed for substance and consolidation before recognizing gain?
- Are off-market leaseback payments separated into a financing or additional-consideration element rather than included in the sale price?
- Are sale-and-leaseback outcomes, gains recognized or deferred, failed sale financings, and sublease income disclosed in line with the applicable standard?; has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed material sale-and-leaseback or sublease transactions where the sale assessment or classification is judgmental?
