# Lessee Lease Accounting

Lease accounting for lessees is no longer a simple rent-expense decision. Under current standards, a lessee recognizes a right-of-use asset and a lease liability for almost every lease, which puts a large new asset and liability on the balance sheet and changes the expense pattern. The judgment lies in the inputs: the discount rate, the lease term including renewal and termination options, the payments including variable and residual guarantees, and the remeasurement triggers. Small changes in these inputs move the liability, the asset, and the front-loaded expense materially. Agents often default to the contractual term and the headline rent, missing options that are reasonably certain to be exercised and payments that should be included.

Use this skill before recognizing a new lease, reassessing an existing lease, modifying or extending a contract, deciding whether a contract contains a lease, or preparing lease disclosures. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating a lease as a flat rent charge and missing the asset, liability, rate, term, and remeasurement judgments that drive the numbers.

Lease accounting differs significantly between US GAAP (ASC 842) and IFRS (IFRS 16), particularly on lease classification for lessees, the low-value exemption, and presentation. Local GAAP may differ further. This skill gives the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For portfolios with many leases or unusual terms, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific contract.

## Core Rules

### Identify Whether The Contract Contains A Lease

A lease exists when the contract conveys the right to control the use of an identified asset for a period of time in exchange for consideration. Control requires both the right to obtain substantially all the economic benefits from use of the asset and the right to direct how and for what purpose the asset is used.

Assess: is there an identified asset (explicitly or implicitly specified, and physically distinct); does the customer have the right to direct the use; and does the customer obtain substantially all the economic benefits over the term. Service contracts, capacity contracts, and shared-asset arrangements need careful evaluation because they may or may not contain an embedded lease. Separate lease components from nonlease components (services) unless a practical expedient is elected.

### Determine The Lease Term Including Options

The lease term is the noncancellable period plus any periods covered by an option to extend if the lessee is reasonably certain to exercise it, plus any periods covered by an option to terminate if the lessee is reasonably certain not to exercise it. "Reasonably certain" is a high threshold that considers economic incentives.

Factors that affect reasonable certainty: significant leasehold improvements that lose value if the lessee leaves; penalties for termination; the cost and disruption of relocating; the importance of the asset to operations; bargain renewal rates versus market rates; and options controlled by the lessee versus the lessor. Document the assessment for each material option. Do not default to the contractual noncancellable term without considering options.

### Select The Correct Discount Rate

The lease liability is discounted using the rate the lessor charges in the lease (the rate implicit in the lease) if readily determinable; otherwise the lessee's incremental borrowing rate, which is the rate the lessee would have to pay to borrow funds to purchase the asset of similar value and term in a similar economic environment.

For a portfolio of leases, a portfolio incremental borrowing rate may be appropriate, but it must reflect the term, currency, security, and economic environment of the leases. Using a single generic corporate borrowing rate for all leases, or the risk-free rate as a shortcut (only permitted as an election for certain lessees under US GAAP), distorts the liability. Document the basis for the rate and update it on remeasurement using a revised rate when the lease is modified.

### Measure The Lease Payments To Include In The Liability

Include in the lease liability: fixed payments less any lease incentives; in-substance fixed payments; variable lease payments that depend on an index or rate (using the index or rate at commencement); exercise prices of purchase options the lessee is reasonably certain to exercise; payments of penalties for terminating if the term reflects termination; and residual value guarantees that are probable to be owed.

Exclude from the initial liability: variable payments based on performance or usage (recognized in expense as incurred); optional payments for services; and amounts allocated to nonlease components. Reassess variable payments tied to an index or rate only when the liability is remeasured for another reason, not at each payment.

### Classify The Lease And Apply The Right Expense Pattern

Under IFRS 16, lessees apply a single finance-lease-type model to almost all leases, with straight-line expense only for short-term and low-value exemptions. Under US GAAP, lessees classify each lease as finance or operating based on criteria (transfer of ownership, purchase option, lease term major part of asset life, present value of payments substantially all of fair value, specialized nature).

Classification changes the expense pattern. A finance lease produces front-loaded interest expense plus amortization of the right-of-use asset, lowering operating profit over time. An operating lease produces a single straight-line lease expense, with the right-of-use asset amortized on a straight-line basis net of interest. Get the classification right because it affects operating margin, EBITDA, and covenant ratios.

### Apply Exemptions Deliberately

Short-term leases (lease term of twelve months or less, with no purchase option the lessee is reasonably certain to exercise) may be accounted for on a straight-line basis with no asset or liability. Low-value leases (IFRS only) may be similarly expensed. These are optional elections applied consistently by class of underlying asset.

Do not apply the short-term exemption to a lease with a renewal option the lessee is reasonably certain to exercise, because the effective term then exceeds twelve months. Do not apply the low-value exemption to assets that are significant when new, even if the individual lease payment is small.

### Remeasure When Triggered

Remeasure the lease liability and right-of-use asset when: the lease term changes (an option becomes reasonably certain to exercise or not); the assessment of a residual value guarantee changes; the index or rate used for variable payments changes; the lease is modified. Use a revised discount rate for term and payment changes; use the original rate for index or rate changes.

A modification that adds an additional right of use at fair value is treated as a separate lease. Other modifications adjust the existing liability and asset. Document each remeasurement trigger and the inputs used.

### Maintain A Lease Schedule And Disclose

Maintain a schedule for each lease showing: commencement date; lease term; discount rate; lease payments by year; right-of-use asset balance; lease liability balance; depreciation or amortization; interest expense; and cash payments. Reconcile to the general ledger each period.

Disclose the carrying amounts, the maturity analysis of lease liabilities, the total cash outflow for leases, the basis for the discount rate, and the expense recognized. Users rely on this to assess leverage and future cash commitments.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To The Contractual Term And Ignoring Options

Using the noncancellable term without assessing renewal or termination options understates the liability and asset when the lessee is reasonably certain to extend. The reasonable-certainty test is economic, not contractual; significant leasehold improvements or relocation cost often make extension reasonably certain.

### Using A Single Discount Rate For Every Lease

A generic corporate rate does not reflect the asset, term, and security of a specific lease. The liability is highly sensitive to the discount rate; a rate that is too high understates the liability, a rate too low overstates it. Build a rate that reflects the lease characteristics.

### Excluding In-Substance Fixed And Index-Based Payments

Payments that are structurally fixed (minimums, payments that must be made regardless of use) and variable payments tied to an index or rate belong in the liability at commencement. Excluding them understates the liability and overstates later expense.

### Misclassifying Finance Versus Operating Under US GAAP

Because classification drives the expense pattern and operating margin, an incorrect classification distorts EBITDA, operating profit, and covenant ratios. Test each lease against the criteria rather than assuming all real estate is operating and all equipment is finance.

### Applying The Short-Term Exemption To A Lease With Likely Renewal

If the lessee is reasonably certain to renew, the effective term exceeds twelve months and the short-term exemption is not available. Applying it anyway keeps the lease off-balance-sheet incorrectly.

### Forgetting To Remeasure On A Term Change

When a lessee exercises an extension that was not in the original term, or terminates early, the liability and asset must be remeasured. Carrying the original balance forward leaves the balance sheet stale and the expense wrong.

### Treating Variable Performance Payments As Part Of The Liability

Variable payments based on sales, usage, or performance are expensed as incurred, not included in the liability. Including them overstates the liability and front-loads expense incorrectly.

## Self-Check

- Has it been determined whether each contract contains a lease based on identified asset, right to direct use, and substantially all economic benefits?
- Does the lease term include renewal or termination options assessed as reasonably certain, with documented economic incentives?
- Is the discount rate the rate implicit in the lease if determinable, otherwise an incremental borrowing rate reflecting the asset, term, currency, and security?
- Are fixed, in-substance fixed, index-based, probable residual guarantee, and reasonably certain option payments included in the initial lease liability?
- Has the lease been classified correctly under the applicable standard, with the matching expense pattern applied (finance versus operating)?
- Are short-term and low-value exemptions applied only where genuinely available and consistently by class of asset?
- Is the lease remeasured for term changes, residual guarantee changes, index changes, and modifications, with the correct original or revised discount rate?
- Does a lease schedule reconcile each lease's asset, liability, expense, and cash payments to the general ledger?
- Are lease commitments, maturity analysis, discount rate basis, and total expense disclosed in line with the applicable standard?
- Has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed portfolios, unusual terms, or modifications where judgment is material?
