# Self-Assessment And Pre-Audit

A pre-audit self-assessment is the company's chance to find its own problems before the auditor does. Done well, it surfaces control gaps, unreconciled accounts, unsupported estimates, and disclosure weaknesses early enough to fix them. Done poorly, it becomes a box-checking exercise that confirms what management already wanted to believe and misses the issues that will become findings. The value of self-assessment is not in declaring the organization ready; it is in honestly identifying where it is not.

Use this skill before conducting a pre-audit self-assessment, evaluating control or financial statement risk in advance of an audit, identifying likely findings, or building a remediation plan. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a self-assessment that confirms readiness without testing it, that scopes out high-risk areas for convenience, or that produces a remediation plan too vague to execute.

## Core Rules

### Scope The Assessment To Real Audit Risk

A self-assessment that reviews only comfortable areas is useless. Scope it to where the audit risk actually lies.

Include in scope:

- significant and unusual transactions during the period;
- new accounting standards adopted this year;
- significant estimates and the evidence behind them;
- accounts with prior-year findings or adjustments;
- processes that changed since the last audit;
- IT and manual controls over financial reporting;
- revenue recognition, cutoff, and related estimates;
- related party and intercompany activity;
- going concern indicators and liquidity.

Areas that were clean last year may have degraded. Areas that are new this year have no track record. Both deserve attention.

### Test Rather Than Assert

A self-assessment that asks managers whether controls are working will usually be told they are. Testing is what separates a real assessment from a comfort exercise.

For each significant area:

- select a sample of transactions and walk them through the process;
- inspect the actual control evidence, approvals, reconciliations, sign-offs;
- attempt to tie balances to the ledger and source documents;
- test a few estimates by re-performing the calculation;
- confirm reconciliations are actually complete, not just marked done.

Document what was tested, what was found, and what was concluded. Untested assertions are not findings.

### Identify Likely Findings Honestly

The purpose of self-assessment is to surface issues, not to explain them away. Identify what an auditor would reasonably find.

Look for:

- unreconciled or aged reconciling items;
- missing or weak approval evidence;
- estimates without documented support;
- disclosures that are incomplete or unsupported;
- cutoff errors around period end;
- exceptions that were not investigated;
- controls that did not operate as described;
- prior findings that were not fully remediated.

For each potential finding, assess severity, root cause, and whether it can be remediated before the audit.

### Quantify The Financial Statement Impact

A control gap is more urgent when it could materially affect the financial statements. Quantify where possible.

Assess:

- the potential misstatement from each identified issue;
- the aggregated exposure across related issues;
- the effect on key balances, metrics, and disclosures;
- whether the issue could cross materiality;
- whether the issue indicates a broader population problem.

A finding that looks minor in isolation may be material in aggregate. Track cumulative exposure.

### Build A Concrete Remediation Plan

Identifying issues without remediating them wastes the assessment. Build a plan that is specific and executable.

For each issue define:

- the specific action to remediate;
- the owner responsible;
- the target completion date before fieldwork;
- the evidence that will demonstrate remediation;
- whether remediation is feasible before the audit or whether the issue must be disclosed.

A remediation plan that says improve controls without specifics will not be executed. Make it concrete.

### Involve The Right People And Preserve Independence

Self-assessment works best when it is not run solely by the people whose work is being assessed. Bring in independent perspective.

Structure:

- involve internal audit or a reviewer outside the process owner;
- include process owners for knowledge, but not as the sole evaluator;
- escalate significant findings to the appropriate level;
- preserve objectivity, do not let the assessment become self-justification.

Where internal resources lack the expertise or independence, consider involving qualified external advisors for the self-assessment.

### Use The Results To Prepare, Not To Conceal

The results of self-assessment should drive preparation and honest disclosure, not a strategy for hiding issues from the auditor. Issues that cannot be remediated should be disclosed proactively, with the company's analysis, rather than discovered by the auditor. Proactive disclosure of a known issue, with a remediation plan, is far better than a late-emerging finding that looks like concealment.

### Acknowledge Professional And Framework Limits

Self-assessment supports audit readiness, but it is not an audit and does not provide assurance. It is performed under the company's own procedures, not under auditing standards. Complex accounting matters, such as fair value, business combinations, tax positions, and new standards, may require qualified professional analysis as part of the assessment. Escalate uncertain or material treatments, and do not present a self-assessment as a substitute for the external audit or for independent professional review where those are required.

## Common Traps

### Scoping Out High-Risk Areas

Excluding uncomfortable or complex areas from the self-assessment guarantees the auditor will find what was avoided.

### Asserting Instead Of Testing

Asking managers whether controls work produces optimistic answers. Testing produces findings.

### Explaining Away Potential Findings

Rationalizing an issue as a one-off or immaterial prevents remediation and invites an auditor finding.

### Ignoring Aggregated Exposure

Evaluating issues only individually misses the cumulative effect that can cross materiality.

### Vague Remediation Plans

Plans that say improve or strengthen without specifics, owners, and dates are not executed.

### Self-Justifying Assessment

Letting process owners assess their own work without independent review turns the assessment into reassurance.

### Using Results To Conceal

Hiding identified issues from the auditor, rather than disclosing and remediating, creates far worse exposure when discovered.

### Overstating The Assessment's Authority

A self-assessment is preparation, not assurance, and does not replace the external audit or professional review of complex matters.

## Self-Check

- Is the self-assessment scoped to significant transactions, new standards, estimates, prior findings, changed processes, and going concern, rather than only comfortable areas?
- Are significant areas tested through transaction walkthroughs, control evidence inspection, and re-performance, rather than asserted?
- Are likely findings identified honestly, with severity, root cause, and remediation feasibility assessed?
- Is the potential financial statement impact of each issue quantified, individually and in aggregate?
- Is the remediation plan concrete, with specific actions, owners, dates, and evidence of completion?
- Does the assessment involve independent perspective and avoid pure self-justification by process owners?
- Are unremediated issues disclosed proactively with analysis rather than concealed from the auditor?
- Does the assessment acknowledge it is preparation, not assurance, and that complex matters may require qualified professional input and the external audit?
