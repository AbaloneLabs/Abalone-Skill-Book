# Control Testing And Walkthrough

A control walkthrough traces a process from start to finish to confirm it works as described and that controls are present where risks exist. Control testing then evaluates whether those controls actually operated during the period. Together they form the evidence that a process can be relied upon. When the walkthrough is shallow, the control inventory is incomplete or the testing misses the real points of failure. The result is a control structure that looks documented on paper but does not withstand scrutiny when a transaction goes wrong.

Use this skill before performing a control walkthrough, testing design or operating effectiveness, building a process and control matrix, or evaluating whether controls operate as intended. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing walkthroughs that confirm the documented process without testing reality, or control tests that count signatures without assessing whether the control actually mitigated the risk.

## Core Rules

### Walk The Process End To End

A walkthrough is not a desk review of a procedure document. It follows a real transaction through the real process.

Walk the process by:

- selecting a recent, complete transaction;
- tracing it from initiation through recording and reporting;
- observing each step and the system or person performing it;
- identifying where approvals, reconciliations, and checks occur;
- noting where the actual process differs from the documented one;
- confirming the systems and reports actually used.

A walkthrough that confirms the procedure manual without observing reality misses the gaps where errors and fraud occur. The differences between documented and actual process are often the most important findings.

### Identify Risks And Match Controls To Each

A control is only meaningful if it mitigates a specific risk. Build the analysis from risk to control, not the reverse.

For each process identify:

- the financial statement assertions at risk, existence, completeness, valuation, cutoff, presentation;
- the specific risks that could cause a misstatement;
- the control or controls designed to mitigate each risk;
- whether the control is preventive or detective, manual or automated;
- who performs the control and how frequently.

A control that is not linked to a risk is documentation, not assurance. A risk with no control is a gap.

### Test Design Effectiveness First

Before testing whether a control operated, confirm it is capable of mitigating the risk. A well-operated but poorly designed control provides no real protection.

Assess design by asking:

- does the control address the specific risk;
- does it operate at the right point in the process;
- does it produce evidence that it operated;
- is it independent of the person whose work it checks;
- could it be overridden or bypassed without detection.

A control that depends on the same person who initiated the transaction, or that produces no evidence of operation, is weak by design regardless of how diligently it is performed.

### Test Operating Effectiveness With Sufficient Evidence

Operating effectiveness asks whether the control actually functioned during the period. This requires evidence, not assertion.

For each key control:

- determine the frequency of operation;
- select an appropriate number of items based on frequency and risk;
- inspect the evidence of operation for each selected item;
- confirm the control was performed by the right person;
- confirm exceptions were identified and resolved;
- document the test, the population, the sample, and the result.

Testing too few items, or testing only items known to be clean, undermines the conclusion. The sample must reflect how the control operated across the period.

### Distinguish Manual, Automated, And IT-Dependent Controls

Controls behave differently depending on their nature, and testing differs accordingly.

Understand:

- manual controls depend on consistent human performance and are tested by inspecting evidence over the period;
- automated controls depend on configuration and are tested by confirming the configuration and re-performing the logic;
- IT-dependent manual controls depend on both system-generated data and human review, so both must be tested;
- application and IT general controls, access, change management, and operations, underpin automated controls.

An automated control that rests on weak IT general controls cannot be relied upon, because the configuration could change without authorization.

### Document The Process, Risks, Controls, And Tests

The walkthrough and testing should produce a coherent record that another reviewer can follow.

Document:

- the process flow and systems involved;
- the risk and control matrix linking risks to controls;
- the design assessment for each control;
- the test plan, population, sample, and procedures;
- the test results, exceptions, and conclusions;
- any control deficiencies identified and their severity.

A control file that only the tester can interpret is a liability. Organize it for independent review.

### Evaluate And Classify Deficiencies

When testing reveals that a control did not operate, or operated weakly, classify the deficiency appropriately.

Classify:

- a deficiency exists when a control is missing or fails to prevent or detect a misstatement;
- a significant deficiency is less severe than a material weakness but important enough to merit attention by those overseeing financial reporting;
- a material weakness is a reasonable possibility of a material misstatement that would not be prevented or detected on a timely basis.

Classification depends on the risk, the aggregation with other deficiencies, and whether compensating controls exist. Document the rationale.

### Acknowledge Professional And Framework Limits

Control walkthrough and testing performed internally supports management's assessment of controls and audit readiness, but it is not performed under auditing standards and does not constitute an audit opinion on internal control. Where controls relate to complex areas, or where the organization is subject to specific regulatory control reporting requirements, the applicable framework and any external auditor's requirements must be considered. Escalate significant deficiencies or material weaknesses to qualified professionals, and do not present internal testing as a substitute for independent audit or attestation where those are required.

## Common Traps

### Walkthroughs That Confirm The Document, Not Reality

Reviewing procedure manuals without observing actual transactions misses the gaps that matter.

### Controls Without Linked Risks

Documenting controls that are not tied to specific risks produces paperwork, not assurance.

### Assuming A Control Is Well Designed

Testing operation before confirming design wastes effort if the control cannot mitigate the risk regardless of how it is performed.

### Insufficient Or Curated Samples

Testing too few items, or only clean items, produces an unfounded conclusion that the control operated.

### Ignoring IT General Controls

Relying on automated controls without testing the underlying access, change, and operations controls rests on an unverified foundation.

### Undocumented Or Uninterpretable Files

A control file that only the tester understands cannot be reviewed or relied upon.

### Misclassifying Deficiencies

Understating the severity of a deficiency, or ignoring aggregation and compensating controls, misrepresents the control environment.

### Overstating Internal Testing's Authority

Internal walkthrough and testing support readiness, but they are not an audit opinion or an attestation on internal control.

## Self-Check

- Does the walkthrough follow a real transaction end to end, observing the actual process and noting differences from documentation?
- Is each control linked to a specific financial statement risk and assertion it is meant to mitigate?
- Is design effectiveness assessed before operating effectiveness, confirming the control is capable of mitigating the risk?
- Is operating effectiveness tested with an appropriate sample drawn from the full period, with evidence of operation inspected?
- Are manual, automated, and IT-dependent controls distinguished, with IT general controls considered for automated reliance?
- Is the process, risk and control matrix, test plan, results, exceptions, and conclusions documented for independent review?
- Are control deficiencies evaluated and classified as deficiency, significant deficiency, or material weakness, with rationale?
- Does the work acknowledge it supports readiness and is not an audit opinion or attestation, with significant issues escalated to qualified professionals?
