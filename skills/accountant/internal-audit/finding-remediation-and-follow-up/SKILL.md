# Finding Remediation And Follow Up

An audit finding is only valuable if it leads to lasting change. Identifying a control gap or an accounting weakness is the beginning, not the end. Remediation is the work of designing a fix, implementing it, validating that it actually works, and sustaining it over time. Many organizations remediate on paper, declaring a finding closed when a new procedure has been written but not embedded. The result is the same finding reappearing the next year, with eroded credibility and expanded scope. Effective remediation is concrete, tested, and followed up.

Use this skill before building a corrective action plan, implementing a remediation, validating that a fix works, or tracking and reporting remediation status. The goal is to prevent the agent from declaring findings closed based on documentation alone, building remediation plans too vague to execute, or failing to validate that the fix actually mitigated the original risk.

## Core Rules

### Start From Root Cause, Not Symptom

A finding describes a symptom. Remediation must address the root cause, or the symptom will recur in a different form.

For each finding determine:

- the underlying cause, not just the observed error;
- whether the cause is a missing control, a weak design, a failure to operate, a training gap, or a system limitation;
- whether the cause is isolated or systemic;
- whether the cause affects other processes or accounts.

Fixing a symptom, such as adding a manual review after a breakdown, without addressing why the breakdown occurred produces a fragile fix. Root cause analysis makes remediation durable.

### Design The Fix To Mitigate The Specific Risk

The remediation must close the specific gap that created the finding. A general improvement does not count.

Design the fix so that:

- it directly addresses the root cause;
- it mitigates the specific financial statement risk;
- it is proportionate to the severity of the finding;
- it produces evidence that it operates;
- it is sustainable without heroic effort.

A fix that depends on a single overworked person, or that produces no evidence of operation, is not a reliable remediation.

### Make The Corrective Action Plan Concrete

A remediation plan that says improve the process or strengthen controls will not be executed. Make it specific.

For each corrective action specify:

- the exact action to be taken;
- the owner accountable for completion;
- the target completion date;
- the resources required;
- the evidence that will demonstrate completion;
- the validation step that will confirm effectiveness.

Track the plan against these elements. Slippage on owner, date, or evidence is an early warning that the remediation is stalling.

### Validate Before Declaring Closed

A finding is not remediated when the plan is written or the control is designed. It is remediated when the fix is operating effectively. Validate before closing.

Validate by:

- confirming the new or revised control is actually in place;
- testing a sample of transactions through the revised process;
- inspecting evidence that the control operated over a sufficient period;
- confirming the original risk is now mitigated;
- documenting the validation result and the basis for closure.

Closing a finding without validation guarantees recurrence. Require evidence of effective operation, not just implementation.

### Distinguish Implemented, Operating, And Sustained

Remediation progresses through stages, and the status should reflect the real stage, not the most optimistic one.

Track:

- implemented, the fix is in place but not yet tested;
- operating, the fix has been tested and is functioning;
- sustained, the fix has operated effectively over a sufficient period.

Reporting a fix as complete when it is only implemented overstates the remediation and sets up a repeat finding.

### Follow Up On A Recurring Cycle

Remediation is not a one-time event. Controls degrade, people change, and pressures shift. Build a follow-up cycle.

Follow up by:

- re-testing remediated controls in the next assessment or audit cycle;
- monitoring the metrics or exceptions the control is meant to address;
- reviewing whether process or personnel changes have weakened the fix;
- escalating any recurrence promptly.

A control that passed validation but fails the next cycle indicates the fix was not durable. Investigate why.

### Report Status Honestly To Oversight

Those overseeing financial reporting, the audit committee, board, or senior management, need honest status, not reassurance.

Report:

- the finding and its root cause;
- the corrective action plan with owner and date;
- the current status, implemented, operating, or sustained;
- any slippage and the reason;
- any findings that cannot be fully remediated and why;
- the validation evidence supporting closed items.

Overstating remediation progress undermines the credibility of the entire control function. Honest reporting, including of delays, is essential.

### Acknowledge Professional And Framework Limits

Remediation of internal control and accounting findings is management's responsibility, but the classification of deficiencies, the validation of remediation, and any external reporting of control status may be governed by specific frameworks and by the external auditor's requirements. Significant deficiencies and material weaknesses often require disclosure and may require qualified professional involvement in designing and validating the fix. Do not present internal remediation and follow-up as a substitute for independent audit validation or attestation where those are required.

## Common Traps

### Fixing Symptoms, Not Root Cause

Addressing the observed error without the underlying cause guarantees recurrence, often in a new form.

### Vague Corrective Action Plans

Plans without specific actions, owners, dates, and evidence are not executed and cannot be tracked.

### Closing On Implementation Alone

Declaring a finding closed when the control is designed but not yet operating effectively sets up a repeat finding.

### Overstating Status

Reporting a fix as complete when it is only implemented or only briefly operating misleads oversight.

### No Follow-Up Cycle

Failing to re-test remediated controls allows silent degradation and recurrence.

### Cosmetic Fixes

Adding a procedure document or a checklist without embedding the control produces paperwork, not remediation.

### Reassuring Rather Than Reporting

Overstating progress to oversight erodes credibility when the real status emerges.

### Overstating Internal Authority

Internal remediation supports the control environment, but it does not replace independent audit validation or framework-specific attestation where required.

## Self-Check

- Does each remediation start from root cause rather than addressing only the observed symptom?
- Is the fix designed to mitigate the specific financial statement risk and to produce evidence of operation?
- Is the corrective action plan concrete, with specific actions, owners, dates, resources, and evidence?
- Is each finding validated through testing of the revised control before it is declared closed?
- Does status tracking distinguish implemented, operating, and sustained, reflecting the real stage?
- Is there a follow-up cycle that re-tests remediated controls and monitors for recurrence?
- Is status reported honestly to oversight, including slippage and items that cannot be fully remediated?
- Does the process acknowledge that significant deficiencies and material weaknesses may require disclosure and qualified professional involvement, and that internal remediation does not replace independent audit validation where required?
