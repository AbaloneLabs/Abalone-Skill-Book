# Hedge Effectiveness Testing And OCI Reclassification

Effectiveness testing is the ongoing test that determines whether a hedging relationship continues to qualify for hedge accounting, and the results drive where gains and losses go. For a cash flow hedge, the effective portion of the change in the hedging instrument's fair value is deferred in OCI and later reclassified to profit or loss when the hedged item affects earnings, while the ineffective portion is recognized immediately in profit or loss. For a fair value hedge, the change in fair value of both the instrument and the hedged item attributable to the hedged risk is recognized in profit or loss, with a corresponding basis adjustment to the hedged item. Agents often treat effectiveness as a one-time designation check rather than an ongoing measurement, miscalculate the effective versus ineffective split, defer amounts in OCI that should be recognized immediately, or fail to reclassify OCI amounts at the right time. The consequence is that the balance sheet accumulates a deferred gain or loss in OCI that has no valid hedge supporting it, or earnings are distorted because reclassification is mistimed.

Use this skill before performing effectiveness testing, measuring the effective and ineffective portions, determining OCI deferral or reclassification, applying a fair value hedge basis adjustment, or deciding whether to discontinue a hedge. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating effectiveness as a pass-fail label and to ensure that the measurement, deferral, and reclassification mechanics are applied correctly each period.

Effectiveness testing and reclassification rules differ between IFRS 9 and US GAAP (ASC 815), including the acceptable methods, the frequency of testing, and the treatment of excluded components. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For material hedging relationships, involve a qualified accountant and a hedge accounting specialist. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific relationship.

## Core Rules

### Test Effectiveness On An Ongoing Basis

Effectiveness is not established once at designation and then forgotten. The relationship must be assessed for effectiveness at inception and on an ongoing basis, typically each reporting period and whenever there is a significant change in circumstances. Under IFRS 9, the assessment considers whether an economic relationship exists between the hedging instrument and the hedged item, the effect of credit risk, and the hedge ratio. Under US GAAP, retrospective and prospective effectiveness testing rules differ by hedge type and method.

Schedule the effectiveness test into the close process so it is not missed. A relationship that was effective at inception can become ineffective as the instrument and item diverge, and continuing to apply hedge accounting to an ineffective relationship misstates both OCI and profit or loss.

### Apply The Right Effectiveness Method For The Framework

Under IFRS 9, effectiveness is a qualitative and quantitative judgment about whether an economic relationship exists that achieves offsetting, supported by quantitative analysis where relevant. There is no fixed numerical threshold, but a relationship with no economic relationship, or one dominated by credit risk, fails. Under US GAAP, methods include the long-haul method, the shortcut method for certain interest rate swaps, and simplified approaches for certain hedges, each with specific conditions.

Confirm the method chosen at inception and apply it consistently. Do not switch methods mid-relationship to manufacture a passing result. If the chosen method shows the relationship is no longer effective, discontinue hedge accounting prospectively.

### Separate The Effective From The Ineffective Portion For Cash Flow Hedges

For a cash flow hedge, the change in fair value of the hedging instrument is split into the effective portion and the ineffective portion. The effective portion, which represents the amount that achieves offsetting with the hedged item, is deferred in OCI. The ineffective portion, which is the residual, is recognized immediately in profit or loss. The split must be calculated each period based on the actual cumulative change in the instrument and the cumulative change in the hedged item.

Calculate the split precisely. A common error is deferring the entire change in the instrument regardless of effectiveness, which overstates OCI and understates current-period profit or loss. Another is recognizing the entire change immediately, which defeats the purpose of the hedge. The effective portion is bounded by the change in the hedged item; any excess is ineffective.

### Reclassify OCI Amounts When The Hedged Item Affects Earnings

Amounts deferred in OCI for a cash flow hedge are not permanent. They are reclassified to profit or loss in the same period that the hedged item affects profit or loss. For a forecasted purchase hedged for commodity price risk, the deferred amount is reclassified into the cost of the purchased item as it is consumed or sold. For a forecasted sale hedged for foreign currency risk, the deferred amount is reclassified when the sale is recognized in revenue. For a floating-rate interest hedge, the deferred amount is reclassified into interest expense over the hedged periods.

Time the reclassification to the earnings effect of the hedged item, not to a convenient period. Deferring reclassification to smooth earnings is earnings management. Accelerating reclassification to release a gain is also earnings management. The reclassification follows the hedged item mechanically.

### Apply The Fair Value Hedge Basis Adjustment

For a fair value hedge, the change in fair value of the hedging instrument is recognized in profit or loss, and the change in fair value of the hedged item attributable to the hedged risk is also recognized in profit or loss, with a corresponding adjustment to the carrying amount of the hedged item. This basis adjustment means the hedged item is no longer carried at its original amount but at an amount adjusted for the hedged risk's effect.

Apply the basis adjustment to the hedged item each period. A common error is recognizing the instrument's change in profit or loss but failing to adjust the hedged item, which produces double counting or no offsetting effect. Confirm that the hedged item's carrying amount reflects the cumulative basis adjustment.

### Handle Excluded Components Deliberately

Hedge documentation may exclude certain components from the effectiveness assessment, such as forward points, time value of options, or cross-currency basis spreads. Under IFRS 9, excluded components may be deferred in a separate reserve (the cost of hedging) or recognized in profit or loss, depending on the election and the hedge type. Under US GAAP, the treatment of excluded components differs. Excluded components are not part of the effective portion and must be accounted for under the framework's specific rules.

Identify excluded components in the documentation and account for them under the correct treatment. Treating an excluded component as part of the effective portion, or ignoring it, misstates both OCI and profit or loss.

### Discontinue Hedge Accounting Correctly When Effectiveness Fails

When a relationship is no longer effective, or when the qualifying criteria cease to hold, hedge accounting is discontinued prospectively. For a cash flow hedge, amounts already deferred in OCI remain there and are reclassified when the hedged item affects earnings, provided the forecasted transaction is still expected to occur. If the forecasted transaction is no longer expected to occur, the OCI balance is reclassified immediately to profit or loss. For a fair value hedge, the basis adjustment on the hedged item is amortized over the remaining life under the framework's rules.

Discontinue prospectively, not retroactively. Do not reverse OCI amounts that were validly deferred while the relationship qualified. But do reclassify immediately if the forecasted transaction is cancelled, because the deferral no longer has a future earnings effect to match.

## Common Traps

### Treating Effectiveness As A One-Time Designation Check

Effectiveness must be reassessed each period. Assuming a hedge effective at inception remains effective without testing leads to continued application of hedge accounting to a relationship that no longer qualifies.

### Deferring The Entire Instrument Change As Effective

For a cash flow hedge, only the portion that achieves offsetting with the hedged item is effective and deferred. The residual is ineffective and recognized immediately. Deferring everything overstates OCI and understates current volatility.

### Mistiming OCI Reclassification

Reclassifying OCI amounts in a period other than when the hedged item affects earnings, to smooth or release gains, is earnings management. Reclassification follows the hedged item mechanically.

### Failing To Apply The Fair Value Hedge Basis Adjustment

Recognizing the hedging instrument's change in profit or loss without adjusting the hedged item's carrying amount produces an incomplete picture and can double count or omit the offsetting effect.

### Ignoring Excluded Components

Forward points, option time value, and cross-currency basis spreads that are excluded from the effectiveness assessment must still be accounted for under the framework's rules. Ignoring them leaves gains or losses unrecorded.

### Continuing Hedge Accounting After Effectiveness Fails

When a relationship is no longer effective, hedge accounting is discontinued prospectively. Retaining it misstates the period of failure and accumulates an unsupported OCI balance.

### Reversing Validly Deferred OCI On Discontinuance

Amounts validly deferred in OCI while the relationship qualified remain deferred and are reclassified when the hedged item affects earnings, unless the forecasted transaction is cancelled. Reversing them on discontinuance misstates the period.

## Self-Check

- Is effectiveness tested at inception and on an ongoing basis each reporting period and upon significant changes in circumstances?
- Is the effectiveness method chosen at inception, documented, and applied consistently, with an economic relationship demonstrated rather than a manufactured result?
- For a cash flow hedge, is the change in the hedging instrument split into the effective portion (deferred in OCI) and the ineffective portion (recognized in profit or loss) each period?
- Are OCI amounts reclassified to profit or loss in the same period the hedged item affects earnings, following the hedged item mechanically rather than a convenient period?
- For a fair value hedge, is the basis adjustment applied to the hedged item's carrying amount each period, in addition to recognizing the instrument's change in profit or loss?
- Are excluded components such as forward points, option time value, and cross-currency basis spreads identified and accounted for under the framework's specific rules?
- When effectiveness fails or criteria cease to hold, is hedge accounting discontinued prospectively, with OCI balances retained and reclassified on the hedged item unless the forecasted transaction is cancelled?
- Has a qualified accountant and hedge accounting specialist reviewed the effectiveness testing, the effective-ineffective split, OCI deferral and reclassification, and any discontinuance?
