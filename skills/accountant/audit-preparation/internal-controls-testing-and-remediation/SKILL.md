# Internal Controls Testing And Remediation

Internal controls are the mechanisms that keep financial reporting reliable, assets protected, obligations met, and compliance maintained. Testing those controls is how an organization learns whether they actually work, not merely whether they exist on paper. A control that is well-designed but never performed, performed inconsistently, performed by the wrong person, or performed without evidence is not a control. It is a description of a control. The discipline of testing and remediation is what converts intent into assurance. Without it, management and auditors rely on assumptions, and weaknesses remain hidden until they cause a material misstatement, a loss, or a compliance failure.

Use this skill before testing controls, documenting walkthroughs, evaluating deficiencies, designing remediation, supporting an audit's controls-based approach, or reporting control effectiveness to governance. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating controls as a documentation exercise, and to ensure that conclusions about effectiveness are based on evidence of actual operation, not on the existence of a policy.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Design Effectiveness From Operating Effectiveness

A control can be well-designed and still fail in operation. Conversely, a control can operate faithfully but address the wrong risk. Testing must evaluate both dimensions separately.

Assess design effectiveness:

- does the control address a specifically identified risk;
- is the control placed to prevent or detect the risk before it becomes material;
- is the control assigned to a competent person with authority to act;
- does the control leave evidence that it was performed;
- does the control operate at a frequency matching the risk;
- are dependent controls, such as system configurations, correctly set.

Assess operating effectiveness:

- was the control actually performed throughout the period;
- was it performed consistently and at the right frequency;
- was it performed by the intended person or role;
- was it performed correctly, with exceptions identified and resolved;
- does evidence exist for each tested instance.

A walkthrough confirms design. Sample testing confirms operation over time. Both are needed.

### Identify The Population And Define The Sampling Approach

Testing requires a complete population from which to draw samples. An incomplete or filtered population produces unrepresentative results and invalid conclusions.

For each control, establish:

- the population of transactions or events the control applies to;
- the period covered;
- how the population is generated and whether it is complete;
- the sampling method, statistical or judgmental;
- the sample size and its rationale;
- the selection criteria and any stratification;
- how exceptions will be evaluated.

If the population cannot be reliably generated, the control cannot be effectively tested through sampling. This itself is a finding.

### Document Walkthroughs That Trace A Transaction End To End

A walkthrough demonstrates that the control operates as designed across the full process flow. It is not a read-through of a policy document. It is a traced path through actual transactions.

For each significant process, document:

- the initiation of the transaction;
- each system and manual step involved;
- the control points where prevention or detection occurs;
- the person performing each control;
- the evidence left at each control point;
- how exceptions are handled and escalated;
- the interfaces between systems and the reconciliation between them;
- the points where segregation of duties should exist.

Walkthroughs reveal gaps that policies hide: missing approvals, system overrides, manual workarounds, and controls that exist in documentation but not in practice.

### Evaluate Deficiencies By Severity And Materiality

Not every control failure is significant. Deficiencies must be evaluated against the risk they were meant to address and the potential magnitude of misstatement.

Classify deficiencies by considering:

- the type of control that failed, preventive or detective;
- the risk the control was designed to mitigate;
- the population and dollar magnitude exposed;
- whether compensating controls exist and operate;
- whether the failure was isolated or pervasive;
- whether the failure resulted in actual misstatement;
- the likelihood that a misstatement could occur and not be prevented or detected.

A deficiency that could allow a material misstatement to occur and not be detected is significant. A deficiency with a reasonable possibility of material misstatement may be a material weakness. Classification drives reporting obligations, so apply the applicable framework's definitions and escalate uncertain classifications to a qualified professional.

### Test Both Manual And Automated Controls Appropriately

Manual and automated controls fail differently and require different testing.

For manual controls, test by:

- selecting samples across the period;
- inspecting evidence of performance;
- re-performing the control where feasible;
- inquiring about how exceptions were handled;
- confirming the performer had authority and competence.

For automated or IT-dependent controls, test by:

- verifying the system configuration or rule that performs the control;
- testing the completeness and accuracy of system-generated reports;
- confirming change management over the relevant configuration;
- testing access controls over the system and the rule;
- verifying that interfaces and data feeds operate correctly.

Automated controls are powerful but only as reliable as the configuration, the access controls, and the data feeding them. A wrong rule automated is a wrong rule scaled.

### Assess Segregation Of Duties Realistically

Segregation of duties is a foundational control, but many organizations cannot achieve perfect separation due to size. The assessment must be honest about both the ideal and the practical.

Identify conflicting combinations:

- initiating and approving a transaction;
- recording and reconciling a transaction;
- custody of assets and recording of related entries;
- system administration and transaction posting;
- vendor setup and payment release;
- payroll setup and payroll approval.

Where segregation is impossible, document the compensating controls: owner review, exception reports, independent reconciliation, periodic outside review, or monitoring analytics. A compensating control that does not actually operate is not a control.

### Design Remediation That Addresses Root Cause

Remiaration that addresses symptoms will fail again. Effective remediation identifies why the control failed and changes the conditions that allowed the failure.

For each deficiency, remediate by:

- identifying the root cause, not only the observed failure;
- redesigning the control or the process if design was the issue;
- adding resources, training, or technology if execution was the issue;
- adding monitoring or review if oversight was the issue;
- assigning an owner and a target completion date;
- defining how remediation effectiveness will be tested;
- documenting the remediation for the next testing cycle.

Remediation is not complete when the plan is written. It is complete when the redesigned control has been tested and found effective over a sufficient period.

### Preserve Evidence Of Control Operation

A control that operates but leaves no evidence cannot be relied upon by auditors or governance. Evidence is what converts operation into assurance.

Require evidence such as:

- signed approvals or electronic signatures;
- dated review checklists;
- exception logs with resolution;
- reconciliation files with reviewer sign-off;
- system-generated reports with run dates;
- access logs and change logs;
- training records for control performers.

Evidence should be organized, retained according to policy, and retrievable without relying on one person's memory.

### Report Results To The Right Audience At The Right Level

Control testing results inform multiple audiences, each with different needs. The level of detail and the framing should match the audience.

Report to:

- process owners with specific, actionable findings;
- management with aggregated results and remediation status;
- the audit committee or governance body with significant deficiencies, material weaknesses, and remediation progress;
- external auditors with the testing performed, results, and conclusions.

Do not overstate effectiveness. A clean report based on weak testing creates false assurance and potential liability when a weakness later surfaces.

## Common Traps

### Testing Controls That Exist Only On Paper

A policy describing a control is not evidence that the control operates. Testing must confirm actual performance through the period, not the existence of a document.

### Drawing Samples From An Incomplete Population

If the population is filtered, missing periods, or excludes exceptions by construction, sample results are meaningless. Confirm population completeness before selecting samples.

### Treating Walkthroughs As Substitutes For Testing

A walkthrough confirms design for one or a few transactions. It does not demonstrate consistent operation across the period. Both walkthrough and sample testing are required.

### Classifying All Deficiencies As Minor

Underclassifying deficiencies avoids uncomfortable reporting but creates risk. A deficiency that could allow material misstatement must be evaluated against the severity definitions of the applicable framework, not minimized for convenience.

### Ignoring IT General Controls

Automated application controls depend on access management, change management, and operations controls. Testing the application rule without testing the IT environment misses the most common source of automated control failure.

### Remediation Plans Without Effectiveness Testing

A remediation plan that is never tested remains an intention. The redesigned control must operate for a sufficient period and be tested before effectiveness can be claimed.

### Overstating Segregation Of Duties

Claiming segregation exists when one person performs multiple incompatible steps, justified by trust or size, is a recurring control failure. Document honest compensating controls instead.

### Reporting Optimistically To Governance

Softening control results for the audit committee undermines oversight and can create personal liability when weaknesses are later discovered. Report accurately and completely.

## Self-Check

- Are design effectiveness and operating effectiveness evaluated separately through walkthroughs and sample testing?
- Is the control population complete, documented, and the sampling method and size justified?
- Do walkthroughs trace actual transactions end to end through systems, control points, evidence, and exception handling?
- Are deficiencies classified by severity using the applicable framework's definitions, with uncertain classifications escalated to a qualified professional?
- Are manual controls tested by sampling and re-performance, and automated controls tested by configuration, report accuracy, change management, and access control?
- Is segregation of duties assessed honestly, with documented compensating controls where separation is impossible?
- Does remediation address root cause, assign owners and dates, and include a plan to test effectiveness after implementation?
- Is evidence of control operation preserved, organized, retained, and retrievable without relying on one person's memory?
- Are results reported accurately to process owners, management, governance, and auditors at the appropriate level of detail?
- Does the testing program acknowledge jurisdiction-specific control, governance, and reporting frameworks and recommend professional review for significant deficiency classification?
