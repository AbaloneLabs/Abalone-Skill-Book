# Sample And Substantive Testing Support

During fieldwork, the auditor moves from the schedules to the transactions. They select samples, request populations, vouch entries to source documents, trace items through the system, and send confirmations. The company's job is to provide complete and accurate populations, locate the selected items, and produce the underlying evidence quickly and honestly. This sounds mechanical, but it is where trust is won or lost. A population that is incomplete silently undermines the audit. A sample item that cannot be supported becomes a projected error. Slow, evasive, or partial responses signal risk and expand the audit scope.

Use this skill before populating testing populations, responding to sample selections, gathering transaction-level evidence, preparing confirmations, or supporting substantive testing during fieldwork. The goal is to prevent the agent from delivering incomplete populations, unsupported sample items, or responses that look cooperative but actually expand audit risk.

## Core Rules

### Provide Complete And Reconciled Populations

The auditor's sample is only as valid as the population it is drawn from. An incomplete or unreconciled population invalidates the sample and can project errors that do not exist, or hide errors that do.

For each requested population:

- define the scope clearly, period, account, transaction type, and entity;
- extract the complete population from the system of record;
- reconcile the population total to the general ledger;
- identify and explain any exclusions or filtering applied;
- document the source system, extraction method, and report parameters;
- provide the population in an analyzable format with relevant fields.

A population that does not tie to the ledger, or that silently excludes transactions, is a serious red flag. Reconcile before delivering.

### Support The Sample Honestly And Completely

When the auditor selects items, every selected item must be supported. Do not curate, delay, or substitute.

For each sample item:

- locate the transaction in the system and the source documents;
- provide the full evidence trail, authorization, contract, invoice, approval, payment;
- explain any exception or deviation from policy;
- acknowledge missing documents rather than reconstructing them silently;
- respond within the agreed timeline.

A missing document for a sampled item may become a projected exception. Honest disclosure of a gap is better than a reconstructed or incomplete response that collapses under scrutiny.

### Distinguish Vouching From Tracing

Auditors test in two directions, and the company should understand both.

Understand:

- vouching starts from a recorded transaction and seeks source documents proving it occurred and was recorded correctly, testing existence and occurrence;
- tracing starts from a source document and follows it into the records, testing completeness;
- each direction answers a different assertion, and supporting both requires different evidence.

Provide evidence that supports both directions. A population that vouches well but traces poorly suggests completeness risk, and vice versa.

### Prepare Confirmations Accurately And Timely

Confirmations with third parties, banks, customers, vendors, and legal counsel are substantive evidence. The company usually prepares or facilitates them, and accuracy matters.

For confirmations:

- use the auditor's preferred format and control process;
- ensure the recipient addresses are current and independent of management;
- reconcile confirmation amounts to the company's records;
- track sent, received, and unmatched confirmations;
- investigate exceptions and differences promptly;
- do not intercept, alter, or selectively suppress responses.

Confirmation control is an area of historical fraud risk. Maintain the integrity of the process and let the auditor control it where required.

### Document Exceptions And Their Disposition

Not every sample item will be perfect. How exceptions are handled matters as much as the exceptions themselves.

For each exception:

- describe the nature of the deviation;
- quantify the amount, if measurable;
- explain the root cause;
- describe whether it is isolated or systemic;
- document any correction or control improvement made.

An isolated, corrected exception with a clear root cause is manageable. A pattern of unexplained exceptions expands testing and signals control weakness.

### Maintain A Clear And Responsive Workflow

Fieldwork runs on responsiveness. Build a process to track auditor requests.

Maintain:

- a log of all requests, with owner, status, and due date;
- a single point of contact to coordinate responses;
- a review step before items are delivered;
- a repository of provided documents to avoid duplicate requests;
- a status meeting cadence with the auditor.

Slow, fragmented, or contradictory responses are interpreted as risk. Coordinated responsiveness builds confidence and keeps the audit on schedule.

### Do Not Pre-Screen Or Influence The Sample

The auditor selects the sample. The company provides the population and the evidence. Pre-screening items, steering selections, or filtering the population to favor certain transactions is a serious integrity breach and, in many jurisdictions, can constitute interference with an audit. Provide the complete population and the unfiltered evidence, and let the audit process work.

### Acknowledge Limits And Escalate Complex Items

Some sample items involve complex accounting, such as a business combination, a derivative, or a tax position. Do not improvise support for these. Involve qualified professionals, and where an item is genuinely uncertain, say so and seek resolution rather than presenting a shaky position as settled. Substantive testing support assists the audit; it does not replace professional judgment on difficult matters.

## Common Traps

### Incomplete Populations

Delivering a population that excludes transactions or does not reconcile to the ledger invalidates sampling and signals risk.

### Curated Sample Responses

Selectively providing favorable documents or delaying unfavorable ones is detected and severely damages credibility.

### Silent Reconstruction

Reconstructing missing documents without disclosure, rather than acknowledging the gap, collapses under auditor scrutiny.

### Confusing Vouching And Tracing

Supporting only one direction leaves the other assertion untested and invites completeness or existence findings.

### Interference With Confirmations

Altering, intercepting, or suppressing confirmation responses is a serious integrity breach and a fraud red flag.

### Unexplained Exceptions

Exceptions without root cause analysis are treated as systemic and expand testing.

### Fragmented Responsiveness

Multiple uncoordinated responders produce inconsistent answers and slow the audit.

### Overstepping Into Audit Judgment

Providing evidence is the company's role. Concluding on accounting treatment for complex items is a professional matter that may require qualified support.

## Self-Check

- Is every requested population complete, reconciled to the general ledger, and documented as to source and extraction?
- Is every sampled item supported with its full evidence trail, with gaps acknowledged rather than reconstructed silently?
- Does the support cover both vouching for existence and tracing for completeness?
- Are confirmations prepared accurately, controlled to maintain integrity, and tracked through resolution?
- Are exceptions documented with nature, amount, root cause, and disposition?
- Is there a coordinated, responsive workflow with a log, owners, review, and a single point of contact?
- Is the sample selected by the auditor without pre-screening, filtering, or influence from the company?
- Are complex or uncertain items escalated to qualified professionals rather than presented as settled?
