# Audit Schedule And PBC List Preparation

An external audit runs on evidence, and the company is responsible for producing it. The audit schedule package and the prepared-by-client (PBC) list are the map of that evidence. When they are well prepared, the audit proceeds efficiently and the auditor can focus on real risk. When they are disorganized, incomplete, or late, the audit slows, fees rise, the auditor loses confidence in the finance team, and material issues may surface only at the last moment. Preparing audit schedules is not clerical work; it is the act of proving that the financial statements can be supported line by line.

Use this skill before building audit schedules, assembling a PBC list, organizing lead schedules, preparing for the start of fieldwork, or coordinating the response to an auditor's initial request. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing schedules that look complete but lack tie-outs, that omit disclosure support, or that leave the company exposed to late-emerging audit findings.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Trial Balance And The Financial Statements

Every audit schedule must tie to the trial balance and ultimately to the financial statements. A schedule that cannot be traced is useless to the auditor.

Build the package so that:

- each material balance sheet and income statement line has a supporting lead schedule;
- each lead schedule ties to the trial balance;
- the lead schedule reconciles to the financial statement caption;
- subsidiary detail, subledgers, and reconciliations support the lead schedule;
- the sum of lead schedules agrees to the financial statements.

A break in this chain is where audit adjustments are born. Reconcile before the auditor arrives, not during fieldwork.

### Build A PBC List That Maps To Audit Risk

A PBC list is not a generic checklist. It should reflect the auditor's planned approach and the company's risk areas. Organize it so items are complete, owned, and trackable.

Structure the PBC list to include:

- prior year audited financial statements and the prior audit report;
- trial balance and general ledger for the current period;
- lead schedules for each material line item;
- account reconciliations for significant balances;
- significant contracts, debt agreements, and leases;
- board minutes and authorizing resolutions;
- equity and capital transaction support;
- related party transactions and intercompany activity;
- tax returns and tax provision work papers;
- significant estimates and their basis;
- subsequent events information through the reporting date.

Assign an owner and a due date to each item. Track status so nothing is forgotten and the auditor is not surprised.

### Reconcile Every Material Account Before Fieldwork

The single most valuable pre-audit activity is completing account reconciliations for every material balance. Unreconciled accounts are the most common source of audit adjustments and fee overruns.

For each material account confirm:

- the reconciliation is complete and reviewed;
- the balance ties to the general ledger;
- reconciling items are investigated and aged;
- old or stale items are cleared or explained;
- supporting documentation is attached or referenced;
- the reconciliation is signed or approved per policy.

Do not hand the auditor a reconciliation with unexplained, aged reconciling items. That signals weak control and invites expanded testing.

### Support Estimates And Judgments With Evidence

Auditors focus heavily on management estimates because they are where bias and error most often hide. Each significant estimate needs a documented basis.

Prepare support for:

- allowance for doubtful accounts and credit loss;
- inventory reserves and net realizable value;
- warranty and return reserves;
- useful lives and impairment indicators for assets;
- fair value measurements and valuation techniques;
- litigation and contingency accruals;
- deferred tax valuation allowances;
- going concern assumptions.

For each estimate, document the method, the inputs, the source of assumptions, the sensitivity, and who approved it. An estimate with no documented basis is an audit finding waiting to happen.

### Organize Disclosure Support Alongside The Schedules

Auditors do not only test the numbers; they test the disclosures. Disclosure support should be prepared with the same rigor as the schedules.

Assemble support for:

- significant accounting policies;
- segment and concentration disclosures;
- debt and lease schedules;
- commitment and contingency disclosures;
- related party disclosures;
- subsequent events;
- fair value hierarchy disclosures;
- equity and share-based compensation disclosures.

A clean set of numbers with missing or unsupported disclosures will still generate audit comments.

### Plan For Subsequent Events Through The Reporting Date

Auditors will ask about events after the balance sheet date that may require adjustment or disclosure. Have the information ready.

Monitor and document:

- significant transactions after period end;
- litigation developments;
- customer or supplier events affecting collectability or valuation;
- financing or refinancing activity;
- impairments or restructuring announcements;
- the intended reporting and release date.

Subsequent events are a required audit procedure, and a gap here is a common late-stage issue.

### Coordinate Timing And Ownership

Audit preparation fails most often on coordination, not on accounting. Build a timeline with owners.

Plan:

- the fieldwork dates and the auditor's planned delivery;
- internal deadlines for each schedule and reconciliation;
- the people responsible for each area;
- review checkpoints before items go to the auditor;
- a single point of contact for auditor questions.

Late or ownerless items cause cascading delays and erode auditor confidence.

### Acknowledge Limits And Escalate To Professionals

Audit preparation supports an external audit performed under the applicable auditing standards and reporting framework. It does not replace the auditor's judgment, and it does not constitute an audit opinion. Complex areas such as fair value, business combinations, tax positions, and going concern often require the involvement of qualified accounting and, where relevant, valuation or tax professionals. Escalate uncertain or material treatments rather than presenting unsupported positions to the auditor.

## Common Traps

### Schedules That Do Not Tie Out

A schedule that does not reconcile to the trial balance or the financial statements wastes the auditor's time and undermines credibility.

### Generic PBC Lists

A PBC list copied from a template without mapping to the company's risks and the auditor's approach leaves gaps and produces redundant work.

### Aged Reconciling Items

Reconciliations with old, unexplained items signal weak control and trigger expanded testing and fees.

### Estimates Without Documented Basis

Significant estimates presented without method, inputs, and approval are a primary source of audit adjustments and findings.

### Missing Disclosure Support

Clean numbers with unsupported disclosures still generate audit comments and can delay issuance.

### Late Subsequent Events Review

Failing to monitor events after period end surfaces issues too late to address cleanly.

### No Clear Ownership

Without owners and deadlines, preparation stalls and the audit slips.

### Treating Preparation As The Audit

Audit preparation is management's responsibility to produce supportable financial statements. It is not a substitute for the independent audit or for professional review of complex areas.

## Self-Check

- Does each material financial statement line have a lead schedule that ties to the trial balance and the financial statements?
- Does the PBC list map to the company's risks and the auditor's planned approach, with owners and due dates?
- Is every material account reconciled, reviewed, and free of unexplained aged items before fieldwork?
- Are significant estimates supported with method, inputs, assumptions, sensitivity, and approval?
- Is disclosure support prepared with the same rigor as the numeric schedules?
- Are subsequent events monitored and documented through the intended reporting date?
- Is there a timeline with owners, review checkpoints, and a single point of contact?
- Does the package acknowledge that complex areas may require qualified professional involvement and that preparation is not a substitute for the independent audit?
