# Management Override And Collusion

Most internal controls are designed assuming that individuals act independently and within their authority. Two scenarios defeat this assumption: management override, where a senior person uses their authority to bypass or disable controls; and collusion, where two or more people cooperate to defeat controls that rely on segregation of duties. These are the hardest fraud scenarios to prevent, because the controls that stop an individual acting alone are precisely the controls that override and collusion are designed to defeat. An entity can have strong segregation of duties, robust approvals, and thorough reconciliations, and still suffer a major fraud if senior management colludes to override them or if two employees in segregated roles cooperate. Acknowledging this reality, and designing specific controls for override and collusion, is what separates a control environment that looks strong on paper from one that is strong in practice.

Use this skill when evaluating management override risk, designing collusion-resistant controls, or strengthening the control environment against difficult fraud scenarios. The goal is to prevent the agent from assuming that segregation of duties is sufficient, from designing controls that management can disable at will, or from ignoring the collusion scenarios that defeat segregation.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Override And Collusion Defeat Standard Controls

Segregation of duties, approvals, and reconciliations prevent fraud by an individual acting alone. They do not prevent a senior manager who can instruct subordinates to bypass them, nor two employees who cooperate across segregated roles.

Recognize that:

- override uses legitimate authority illegitimately, instructing staff to process entries or payments outside normal controls;
- collusion defeats segregation by having two people in supposedly independent roles cooperate;
- controls that depend on honest performance of a duty provide no protection when the duty is performed dishonestly;
- the more senior the person, the more authority they have to override and to discourage challenge.

Acknowledging this is the first step. A control environment that denies override and collusion risk is built on a false premise.

### Design Controls That Management Cannot Easily Override

Because override defeats controls that management performs, the most effective controls are those that management cannot easily disable or influence.

Design controls such as:

- independent review of non-routine, top-side, and post-closing journal entries by the audit committee or internal audit;
- analytics and exception reporting generated by the system, not by management, and reviewed independently;
- external confirmations of significant balances that bypass internal staff;
- whistleblower channels that report directly to the audit committee, not to management;
- mandatory rotation or vacation that requires another person to perform the role and surface irregularities.

The principle is to create independent checkpoints that do not rely on management's willingness to apply a control to itself. Controls that management can turn off will be turned off when needed.

### Address The Specific Mechanisms Of Override

Override occurs through recognizable mechanisms. Address each.

Address mechanisms by:

- monitoring top-side entries, which are journal entries posted at the consolidation or parent level after the close, often outside the normal process;
- reviewing period-end entries that adjust results to meet targets, for pattern and substance;
- controlling the ability to open and post to closed periods, which is a common override tool;
- reviewing manual overrides of automated controls and system validations;
- monitoring access to the general ledger by senior staff who are not normally transactional users.

Top-side entries and period reopening are among the most common override mechanisms. Controls over these specific functions are essential.

### Design For Collusion Resistance

Collusion cannot be prevented by segregation alone, but it can be made harder to sustain and easier to detect.

Design for collusion resistance by:

- rotating staff periodically so that collusion requires constant renegotiation;
- using independent or external parties for key controls, such as bank reconciliation or count observation;
- designing controls that require more than two people to complete a cycle, raising the cost of collusion;
- applying analytics that detect patterns consistent with collusion, such as consistent clearing of exceptions between two roles;
- monitoring relationships and communications that could indicate collusion, within legal and privacy limits.

The objective is not to make collusion impossible, which is unachievable, but to make it harder to sustain over time and more likely to surface through analytics or rotation.

### Strengthen The Audit Committee And Internal Audit As Independent Checks

Because management override is the central risk, independent oversight is the central control. The audit committee and internal audit are the primary independent checks.

Strengthen by:

- ensuring the audit committee has financial expertise and actively questions management, not only receives reports;
- giving internal audit direct access to the audit committee, with protection from management influence;
- having internal audit perform procedures specifically targeted at override, such as journal entry testing;
- ensuring the external auditor performs required override procedures and reports findings to the audit committee;
- empowering the audit committee to commission investigations independent of management.

An audit committee that relies entirely on management's representations provides no independent check. Its value lies in independent skepticism and the authority to act on it.

### Monitor For Indicators Of Override And Collusion

Certain indicators suggest override or collusion may be occurring. Monitor for them.

Monitor for:

- consistent achievement of targets with narrow margins, suggesting smoothing;
- unusual patterns in journal entries, such as round amounts, weekend postings, or entries by unexpected users;
- recurring exceptions cleared between the same two roles;
- reluctance by staff to take vacation or to allow others to perform their role;
- complaints, rumors, or whistleblower reports about pressure to adjust results;
- declining staff morale or turnover in finance, which can signal pressure to compromise.

No single indicator is conclusive, but patterns warrant investigation. Indicators ignored are opportunities missed.

### Foster A Culture Where Override Is Challenged

Culture is the ultimate defense against override. In a culture where staff feel empowered to question authority, override is harder.

Foster by:

- making clear that following instructions to bypass controls is itself a violation;
- protecting staff who raise concerns from retaliation;
- demonstrating that the audit committee and board value challenge, not only compliance;
- training staff to recognize improper override requests and how to escalate;
- responding to override incidents visibly and consequentially.

A culture where questioning a senior manager's unusual request is career-ending is a culture where override flourishes. Culture determines whether controls operate as designed.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

Management override and collusion risk assessment and mitigation involve complex, framework-specific, regulatory, legal, and behavioral considerations that are the professional domain of qualified accountants, auditors, internal audit, legal counsel, and forensic specialists. Override risk assessment, control design, investigation, and response require professional expertise and often involve legal and regulatory obligations, including reporting to audit committees, boards, and regulators. This skill provides awareness of what to consider, not definitive conclusions. Confirm all significant override and collusion risk assessments, control designs, and investigation outcomes with qualified accounting and legal professionals in the relevant jurisdiction. Do not treat any guidance as a substitute for professional and, where required, legal and regulatory compliance.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Segregation Prevents Override And Collusion

Segregation prevents individual fraud but not senior override or cooperation across roles.

### Controls Management Can Disable

Controls that management can turn off will be turned off when needed to conceal fraud.

### Ignoring Top-Side Entries And Period Reopening

These specific override mechanisms are among the most common and must be controlled directly.

### No Independent Oversight

An audit committee or internal audit that relies on management representations provides no independent check.

### Collusion Made Easy By Static Roles

Without rotation or independent controls, collusion between two static roles can persist indefinitely.

### Override Indicators Ignored

Consistent target achievement, unusual entries, and reluctance to take vacation are patterns that warrant investigation.

### Culture That Punishes Challenge

A culture where questioning authority is career-ending enables override to flourish.

### Guidance Treated As Authoritative

Override and collusion response requires professional and legal expertise; confirm with qualified professionals.

## Self-Check

- Is it recognized that override and collusion defeat standard segregation and approval controls?
- Are there controls that management cannot easily disable, such as independent review of top-side entries, system-generated analytics, external confirmations, and whistleblower channels?
- Are the specific mechanisms of override, including top-side entries, period reopening, and manual overrides, directly controlled and monitored?
- Is the control environment designed for collusion resistance through rotation, independent parties, multi-person cycles, and analytics?
- Are the audit committee and internal audit empowered as independent checks with direct access and authority to investigate?
- Are indicators of override and collusion, such as narrow target achievement, unusual entries, and vacation avoidance, monitored and investigated?
- Is a culture fostered where staff are empowered to challenge improper override requests without retaliation?
- Are all significant override and collusion risk assessments, control designs, and investigation outcomes confirmed with qualified accounting and legal professionals in the relevant jurisdiction?
