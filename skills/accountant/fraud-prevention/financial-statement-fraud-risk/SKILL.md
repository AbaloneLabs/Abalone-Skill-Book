# Financial Statement Fraud Risk

Financial statement fraud is the intentional misstatement or omission of amounts or disclosures in financial statements, typically to deceive users about the entity's performance or financial position. Unlike error, fraud is deliberate, and it is usually committed by someone with the access and authority to override controls, most often senior management. The consequences are severe: restatement, regulatory enforcement, litigation, loss of auditor confidence, and collapse of the entity's credibility. Fraud risk cannot be eliminated, but it can be assessed, mitigated, and monitored. The dangerous assumption is that fraud happens to other organizations or that strong controls alone prevent it, because the fraud that matters most is often committed by the very people who can override those controls. Effective fraud risk management combines preventive controls, an honest assessment of pressure and incentive, and a culture where bad news can travel up.

Use this skill when assessing financial statement fraud risk, designing anti-fraud controls, evaluating fraud risk factors, or supporting anti-fraud programs. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating fraud as a remote risk, from relying on controls that management can override, or from ignoring the pressure, incentive, and rationalization that drive fraudulent reporting.

## Core Rules

### Assess Fraud Risk Using The Fraud Triangle

Fraud risk assessment is most effective when it examines the conditions under which fraud occurs. The fraud triangle identifies three conditions usually present.

Assess for:

- incentive or pressure to commit fraud, such as meeting earnings targets, debt covenants, or bonus thresholds;
- opportunity to commit fraud, such as weak controls, override capability, or unmonitored access;
- attitude or rationalization that justifies fraud, such as entitlement, belief that the ends justify the means, or a culture that tolerates aggressive accounting.

A fraud risk assessment that lists controls without examining pressure, opportunity, and rationalization misses the conditions that actually give rise to fraud. All three elements must be assessed for each significant area.

### Identify Financial Statement Fraud Schemes Specific To The Entity

Fraud takes recognizable forms. Identify the schemes most relevant to the entity's profile.

Common schemes include:

- premature or fictitious revenue recognition, such as bill-and-hold or round-tripping;
- understatement of expenses or liabilities through omitted accruals or deferred charges;
- overstatement of assets through inflated inventory, impaired assets not written down, or fictitious assets;
- improper related-party transactions that shift losses or inflate revenue;
- reserve manipulation, such as cookie-jar reserves smoothed to meet targets;
- improper aggregation or classification that obscures unfavorable information.

Map these schemes to the entity's transactions and balances. A generic fraud list that does not connect to the entity's actual activities is not actionable.

### Recognize That Management Override Is The Central Risk

The fraud that causes the most damage is usually committed by senior management, who have the ability to override controls. Standard preventive controls do not address override.

Address override risk by:

- recognizing that segregation of duties and approvals can be bypassed by those with authority;
- designing detective controls that management cannot easily override, such as independent review of estimates and non-routine entries;
- establishing a whistleblower or confidential reporting channel that bypasses the normal hierarchy;
- ensuring the audit committee has direct access to internal and external auditors;
- reviewing post-closing and post-adjustment journal entries for override patterns.

Controls that assume honest management provide no protection against the management that commits fraud. Override risk requires controls designed specifically to detect it.

### Evaluate Incentives And Pressures Honestly

Incentive and pressure are root causes of financial statement fraud. Assess them honestly, even when they are uncomfortable.

Evaluate:

- compensation structures tied to earnings or stock price that create pressure to meet targets;
- debt covenants or financing conditions that, if breached, threaten the entity's survival;
- market or analyst expectations that create pressure to show growth or stability;
- personal pressures on management, such as margin loans or guarantee obligations;
- the gap between actual performance and the performance needed to meet expectations.

A fraud risk assessment that does not honestly evaluate the pressure to meet targets misses the most common driver of fraudulent reporting. Pressure does not cause fraud by itself, but it is usually present when fraud occurs.

### Design Detective Controls That Catch Override And Manipulation

Because management can override preventive controls, detective controls are essential. They must be designed to catch the specific schemes identified.

Design detective controls such as:

- review of non-routine and top-side journal entries by someone independent of management;
- analytics that flag unusual revenue patterns at period end or unusual expense decreases;
- review of estimates and reserves for bias, comparing estimates to actual outcomes over time;
- review of related-party transactions for substance and appropriateness;
- trend and ratio analysis that highlights inconsistencies inconsistent with the business;
- confirmation of significant balances with external parties.

A detective control is valuable only if it is actually performed by someone with the independence and authority to act. A control that management performs on its own transactions provides no protection.

### Foster A Culture And Tone That Deters Fraud

Controls alone do not prevent fraud; culture does. Tone at the top and the ability of bad news to travel up are critical deterrents.

Foster culture by:

- setting a tone that values accurate reporting over meeting targets at any cost;
- ensuring the code of conduct addresses financial reporting integrity, not only ethical behavior generally;
- protecting whistleblowers and demonstrating that reports are investigated without retaliation;
- having the audit committee actively oversee financial reporting and fraud risk, not just receive updates;
- responding visibly to identified issues, so that the consequences of fraud are real.

A culture where missing a target is a career-ending failure, but inaccurate reporting is tolerated, creates the conditions for fraud. Culture is the control that controls cannot replace.

### Coordinate With Auditors On Fraud Risk Assessment

External auditors are required to assess fraud risk and design procedures in response. Coordinate with them rather than treating fraud as solely an internal matter.

Coordinate by:

- sharing the entity's fraud risk assessment with auditors and discussing significant risks;
- providing auditors full access to investigations and identified issues;
- not waiting for auditors to find fraud, but treating their procedures as a backstop to internal efforts;
- discussing management override procedures and journal entry testing openly;
- addressing audit findings about fraud risk factors promptly.

Fraud risk is a shared responsibility. An entity that relies entirely on the external audit to detect fraud has abdicated its own responsibility for reliable reporting.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

Financial statement fraud risk assessment and mitigation involve complex, framework-specific, regulatory, and legal considerations that are the professional domain of qualified accountants, auditors, legal counsel, and forensic specialists. Fraud risk assessment, control design, investigation, and disclosure of identified fraud require professional expertise and often involve legal and regulatory obligations, including reporting to audit committees, boards, and regulators. This skill provides awareness of what to consider, not definitive conclusions. Confirm all significant fraud risk assessments, control designs, and investigation outcomes with qualified accounting and legal professionals in the relevant jurisdiction. Do not treat any anti-fraud guidance as a substitute for professional and, where required, legal and regulatory compliance.

## Common Traps

### Fraud Treated As A Remote Risk

Assuming fraud happens to other organizations ignores the pressure, opportunity, and rationalization present in many entities.

### Controls Relied Upon To Prevent Management Override

Standard preventive controls provide no protection against the senior management who can override them.

### Incentives And Pressures Not Honestly Assessed

A fraud risk assessment that does not honestly evaluate pressure to meet targets misses the most common driver.

### Generic Fraud List Not Connected To Entity

A list of fraud schemes that does not map to the entity's actual transactions is not actionable.

### Detective Controls Not Independently Performed

A detective control performed by management on its own transactions provides no protection.

### Culture That Rewards Targets Over Accuracy

A culture where missing a target is fatal but inaccurate reporting is tolerated creates the conditions for fraud.

### Fraud Treated As Solely An Audit Responsibility

An entity that relies entirely on external audit to detect fraud has abdicated its own reporting responsibility.

### Anti-Fraud Guidance Treated As Authoritative

Fraud risk and investigation require professional, legal, and regulatory expertise; confirm with qualified professionals.

## Self-Check

- Is fraud risk assessed using the fraud triangle, examining incentive, opportunity, and rationalization for each significant area?
- Are financial statement fraud schemes identified and mapped to the entity's specific transactions and balances?
- Is management override recognized as the central risk, with detective controls designed to catch override and manipulation?
- Are incentives and pressures, including compensation, covenants, and expectations, assessed honestly?
- Are detective controls, such as journal entry review, estimate bias review, and analytics, performed by someone independent of management?
- Is a culture fostered that values accurate reporting, protects whistleblowers, and responds visibly to issues?
- Is fraud risk assessment coordinated with external auditors, with full access and open discussion?
- Are all significant fraud risk assessments, control designs, and investigation outcomes confirmed with qualified accounting and legal professionals in the relevant jurisdiction?
