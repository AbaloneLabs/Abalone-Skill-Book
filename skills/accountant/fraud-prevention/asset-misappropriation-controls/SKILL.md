# Asset Misappropriation Controls

Asset misappropriation is the theft or misuse of an entity's assets by employees, vendors, or others. It is the most common category of occupational fraud, far more frequent than financial statement fraud, though usually smaller in amount per incident. Cash is the most frequently targeted asset, through schemes such as billing fraud, expense reimbursement abuse, payroll fraud, skimming, and fraudulent disbursements. Inventory, equipment, and intellectual property are also targeted. While asset misappropriation typically does not distort the financial statements as dramatically as financial statement fraud, it represents a real loss, it often indicates weak controls that could also enable larger fraud, and it can signal a culture of integrity failure that, left unaddressed, escalates. Controls over assets are the day-to-day expression of an entity's commitment to protecting its resources.

Use this skill when designing asset misappropriation controls, evaluating theft or misuse risk, building disbursement and procurement fraud controls, or supporting an asset-protection anti-fraud program. The goal is to prevent the agent from designing controls that address only one scheme, from ignoring the vendor master and payment processes where much fraud occurs, or from treating asset protection as a security matter rather than a control matter.

## Core Rules

### Map Asset Misappropriation Risks Across The Cash And Asset Cycles

Asset misappropriation takes many forms. Map the risks across the cycles where assets move.

Map risks across:

- the disbursement cycle, including fraudulent invoices, shell vendors, duplicate payments, and payment redirection;
- the procurement cycle, including kickbacks, bid rigging, and conflicts of interest;
- the expense cycle, including fictitious or inflated expense reimbursements;
- the payroll cycle, including ghost employees and falsified hours;
- the receipts cycle, including skimming before recording and lapping of receivables;
- the inventory cycle, including theft, scrap manipulation, and write-off abuse;
- physical and intellectual assets, including equipment theft and data exfiltration.

A control program that addresses billing fraud but not payroll, or cash receipts but not inventory, leaves gaps. Map comprehensively.

### Secure The Vendor Master And Payment Process

A large share of cash fraud occurs through the vendor master and the payment process. These are high-risk areas requiring strong controls.

Secure by:

- validating new vendors before activation, including verification of legitimacy and conflicts of interest;
- requiring segregation between vendor creation, invoice entry, and payment approval;
- reviewing changes to vendor master data, especially bank account details, for payment redirection fraud;
- matching invoices to purchase orders and receipts before payment;
- reviewing duplicate payments and recovering or preventing them;
- periodically reviewing the vendor master for duplicates, inactive vendors, and vendors with employee-like details.

Payment redirection, where a fraudster changes a vendor's bank details to their own account, is a growing and high-loss scheme. Controls over vendor bank detail changes are essential.

### Control Expense Reimbursement And Payroll

Expense and payroll are frequent sources of smaller but persistent fraud. Controls must be practical without being so burdensome they are bypassed.

Control by:

- requiring receipts and policy-based approval for expense reimbursements;
- reviewing expense patterns for abuse, such as round amounts, weekend submissions, or threshold clustering;
- reconciling payroll to the employee master and to HR records to detect ghost employees;
- reviewing overtime, bonus, and commission for unusual patterns;
- periodically confirming that employees on the payroll exist and are active.

Expense fraud is often low-tech and persistent. Analytics on expense patterns detect schemes that individual approvals miss.

### Protect Cash Receipts From Skimming And Lapping

Cash receipts are vulnerable to skimming, where cash is taken before it is recorded, and lapping, where a payment from one customer is stolen and covered by a later payment from another.

Protect by:

- segregating custody of cash from recording and reconciliation;
- reconciling bank deposits to the receipts ledger promptly and independently;
- sending customer statements that allow customers to detect missing payments;
- reviewing write-offs and adjustments to receivables for patterns;
- monitoring aging for accounts that should be current but show unexplained balances.

Lapping can persist for long periods because each theft is covered by the next. Independent reconciliation and customer communication are the detective controls that break the cycle.

### Secure Inventory Through Physical And Record Controls

Inventory is vulnerable to theft, manipulation of counts, and abuse of write-offs. Controls must protect both the physical asset and the records.

Secure by:

- restricting physical access to inventory and conducting periodic counts;
- reconciling perpetual records to physical counts and investigating variances;
- reviewing write-offs and adjustments for patterns and authorization;
- monitoring scrap and shrinkage for unusual trends;
- segregating inventory custody from recording and adjustment authority.

Inventory fraud often hides in write-offs and adjustments that are authorized too casually. Review the pattern of adjustments, not only the individual entries.

### Design Detective Analytics That Surface Patterns

Individual transactions often appear legitimate. Analytics that examine patterns across transactions are the most effective detective tool.

Design analytics that flag:

- payments to vendors with employee-like addresses or bank details;
- duplicate invoices or payments across vendors or periods;
- expense claims clustered just below approval thresholds;
- vendors with no purchase order history or unusually high volume;
- journal entries to suspense or clearing accounts by non-finance staff;
- bank account detail changes followed by rapid payment.

Analytics turn thousands of transactions into a manageable set of exceptions for investigation. A control environment without analytics relies on catching fraud one transaction at a time.

### Respond To Identified Misappropriation Decisively

When misappropriation is identified, the response determines whether the control environment improves or the problem persists.

Respond by:

- investigating promptly and thoroughly, with appropriate confidentiality;
- quantifying the loss and pursuing recovery, including insurance and restitution;
- assessing whether the incident indicates a broader control weakness;
- remediating the root cause, not only the specific incident;
- considering legal, regulatory, and disclosure obligations;
- communicating the consequence visibly, to reinforce deterrence.

A misappropriation that is quietly absorbed, without investigation or consequence, signals that the behavior is tolerated and invites recurrence.

### Acknowledge Framework And Professional Limits

Asset misappropriation controls support the protection of assets reported under the applicable framework, but fraud investigation, loss quantification, recovery, and disclosure involve legal, regulatory, and often forensic considerations that are the professional domain of qualified accountants, auditors, legal counsel, and forensic specialists. Identified misappropriation may trigger insurance, legal, regulatory, and employment obligations. This skill provides awareness of what to consider, not definitive conclusions. Confirm all significant control designs, investigations, and responses with qualified accounting and legal professionals in the relevant jurisdiction. Do not treat any anti-fraud guidance as a substitute for professional and, where required, legal and regulatory compliance.

## Common Traps

### Controls Addressing Only One Scheme

A program that addresses billing fraud but not payroll, or cash receipts but not inventory, leaves exploitable gaps.

### Unsecured Vendor Master And Payment Process

The vendor master and payment process are high-risk; weak controls here enable shell vendors, duplicate payments, and redirection fraud.

### Expense And Payroll Fraud Ignored

Low-tech expense and payroll fraud is persistent and often missed by individual approvals.

### Skimming And Lapping Undetected

Cash receipts fraud persists when reconciliation is not independent and customers are not informed.

### Write-Offs And Adjustments Authorized Casually

Inventory and receivable fraud often hides in adjustments authorized without review of the pattern.

### No Analytics To Surface Patterns

Without analytics, fraud is detected one transaction at a time, if at all.

### Misappropriation Quietly Absorbed

Absorbing a loss without investigation or consequence signals tolerance and invites recurrence.

### Anti-Fraud Guidance Treated As Authoritative

Investigation and response require professional and legal expertise; confirm with qualified professionals.

## Self-Check

- Are asset misappropriation risks mapped across the disbursement, procurement, expense, payroll, receipts, inventory, and physical asset cycles?
- Is the vendor master secured with validation, segregation, change review, and periodic cleanup for duplicates and inactive vendors?
- Are expense reimbursements and payroll controlled with receipts, approval, analytics, and reconciliation to HR?
- Are cash receipts protected from skimming and lapping through independent reconciliation and customer communication?
- Is inventory secured through physical access control, counts, variance investigation, and adjustment review?
- Are detective analytics designed to surface patterns such as employee-like vendors, duplicates, threshold clustering, and bank detail changes?
- Is identified misappropriation investigated, quantified, recovered, root-caused, remediated, and responded to visibly?
- Are all significant control designs, investigations, and responses confirmed with qualified accounting and legal professionals in the relevant jurisdiction?
