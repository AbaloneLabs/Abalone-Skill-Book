# Prepaid And Deferred Expense Amortization

A prepaid expense is a payment made in advance for a good or service that will be consumed over future periods. Instead of recognizing the full cost as expense when paid, the cost is recorded as an asset and amortized, expensed, over the period of benefit. This sounds mechanical, but the judgment is in the details. What is the correct benefit period? When does amortization start? Should the item be prepaid at all or expensed immediately? What happens when the contract changes or the service ends early? Agents frequently set up prepaid schedules and then never revisit them, so amortization continues on stale assumptions, balances linger after the benefit has ended, and the expense pattern no longer reflects reality.

Use this skill before creating a prepaid schedule, amortizing a deferred cost, reviewing prepaid balances, or handling changes to an existing prepayment. The goal is to ensure prepaid balances reflect real future economic benefit and that amortization matches the consumption of that benefit.

This skill covers prepaid and deferred expense mechanics. Capitalization thresholds, tax treatment of prepayments, and industry-specific deferral rules vary by jurisdiction and standard. Confirm the applicable policy before setting up or adjusting schedules.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether The Payment Is Truly A Prepaid Asset

Not every advance payment is a prepaid asset. Before setting up a schedule, confirm the payment meets the criteria for deferral.

A payment is a prepaid asset when:

- it provides a future economic benefit beyond the current period;
- the benefit period is identifiable and reasonable;
- the amount is material enough to defer under the entity's capitalization threshold;
- the cost will be consumed over time rather than all at once.

A payment is not a prepaid asset when:

- the benefit is consumed immediately or within the current period;
- the amount is immaterial and expensing is more practical;
- the payment is a deposit or security that is refundable, which is a receivable, not a prepaid expense;
- the payment is for a capital asset, which should be capitalized and depreciated, not prepaid.

Classifying a deposit as a prepaid expense is a common error. A refundable deposit is a receivable. A non-refundable advance for future service is a prepaid expense.

### Define The Amortization Period Based On The Benefit

The amortization period should reflect the period over which the entity benefits from the prepaid cost, not simply the contract term or the payment frequency.

Consider:

- an annual insurance premium is amortized over the twelve months of coverage;
- an annual software subscription is amortized over the subscription term;
- a multi-year maintenance contract is amortized over the maintenance period;
- a prepaid rent is amortized over the lease term on a straight-line basis if required.

If the benefit period differs from the payment period, use the benefit period. A payment for eighteen months of service should amortize over eighteen months, not twelve.

### Start Amortization When The Benefit Begins

Amortization should begin when the entity starts receiving the benefit, not necessarily when the payment is made or the contract is signed.

For example:

- insurance prepaid in December for coverage starting January should begin amortizing in January;
- software prepaid before go-live should begin amortizing when the service period starts, not when paid;
- rent prepaid before occupancy should begin when the lease term starts.

Starting amortization on the payment date understates the prepaid asset and overstates early-period expense.

### Choose The Right Amortization Pattern

Most prepayments amortize on a straight-line basis, equal expense each period. But some costs have a different consumption pattern.

Consider whether:

- straight-line is appropriate for insurance, subscriptions, and rent;
- usage-based amortization is needed for costs consumed variably;
- front-loaded or back-loaded patterns apply to certain contracts;
- the applicable standard requires a specific method, such as straight-line rent expense under lease accounting.

Default to straight-line unless the consumption pattern clearly differs or a standard requires otherwise.

### Maintain A Detailed Prepaid Schedule

Every prepaid balance should be supported by a schedule that shows the original amount, the start date, the amortization period, the monthly amount, the accumulated amortization, and the remaining balance.

The schedule should include:

- vendor and description;
- original payment amount and date;
- benefit start and end dates;
- monthly amortization amount;
- accumulated amortization to date;
- net book balance;
- the general ledger accounts posted.

Without a schedule, the prepaid balance is unverifiable and amortization errors go undetected.

### Review Prepaid Balances Each Period

Prepaid balances must be reviewed at every close, not just when first set up. The review confirms that amortization is posting correctly and that balances still represent real future benefit.

The review should check:

- whether each prepaid still has a valid underlying contract or service;
- whether the amortization amount and period are still correct;
- whether any prepaid should be fully amortized and removed;
- whether any new prepayments were recorded but not scheduled;
- whether contract changes, renewals, or cancellations require schedule updates.

A prepaid balance that lingers after the benefit has ended is overstated expense deferral.

### Handle Changes, Renewals, And Early Termination

When a prepaid contract changes, the schedule must be updated.

Common changes:

- renewal at a new rate requires a new schedule or an adjustment to the existing one;
- early termination requires writing off the remaining unamortized balance;
- a refund of an unamortized portion reduces the prepaid balance;
- a change in scope or pricing requires recalculating the remaining amortization.

Failing to update the schedule when the underlying contract changes leaves amortization posting on stale terms.

### Reconcile Prepaid Subledger To General Ledger

The total of all prepaid schedules must reconcile to the prepaid asset account in the general ledger. A difference indicates a missing schedule, a posting error, or an amortization that did not post.

## Common Traps

### Deferring Immaterial Amounts

Setting up a prepaid schedule for a small, short-period payment creates administrative burden without meaningful accuracy improvement. Apply a materiality threshold and expense immaterial advance payments.

### Treating Refundable Deposits As Prepaid Expense

A security deposit that is refundable is a receivable, not a prepaid expense. It is not consumed over time. Recording it as prepaid and amortizing it incorrectly writes off an asset that should be recovered.

### Never Reviewing Established Schedules

A prepaid schedule set up once and never reviewed will eventually post amortization on a contract that has ended, renewed, or changed. The balance becomes meaningless. Periodic review is essential.

### Starting Amortization On The Payment Date

If a premium is paid in advance but coverage starts later, amortizing from the payment date front-loads expense and understates the true prepaid asset. Start when the benefit begins.

### Using The Wrong Amortization Period

Amortizing a multi-year contract over one year, or a one-year contract over the payment month, distorts expense. Match the amortization period to the actual benefit period.

### Forgetting To Schedule New Prepayments

When a new advance payment is recorded as a prepaid asset but no amortization schedule is created, the balance sits indefinitely without expense recognition. Every new prepaid must be scheduled.

### Ignoring Early Termination Write-Offs

When a service ends early, the remaining unamortized prepaid should be written off. Leaving it to amortize on the original schedule recognizes expense in periods after the benefit ended.

## Self-Check

- Has each advance payment been evaluated to confirm it is a true prepaid asset rather than a deposit, immediate expense, or capital asset?
- Does each prepaid schedule define the amortization period based on the actual benefit period, with amortization starting when the benefit begins?
- Is the amortization pattern, straight-line or otherwise, appropriate for the consumption of the benefit?
- Is every prepaid balance supported by a detailed schedule showing original amount, dates, monthly amortization, accumulated amortization, and net balance?
- Are prepaid balances reviewed each close for validity, correct amortization, expirations, new unscheduled items, and contract changes?
- Are renewals, rate changes, early terminations, and refunds reflected in updated schedules?
- Does the prepaid subledger reconcile to the general ledger prepaid asset account each period?
- Are materiality thresholds applied so that immaterial advance payments are expensed rather than scheduled?
- Have capitalization thresholds, tax treatment, and jurisdictional deferral rules been confirmed before setting up or adjusting prepaid schedules?
