# Unbilled And Bill In Excess Accounting

Unbilled amounts and billings in excess of revenue are the reconciling items between revenue recognized and amounts billed. They arise whenever the timing of performance differs from the timing of billing. Although these balances are routine in long-term contracts, construction, professional services, and subscription businesses, they are a frequent source of error. Agents confuse unbilled receivables with contract assets, mislabel billings in excess as deferred revenue, net balances inappropriately, and fail to reconcile the contract asset and contract liability balances with the underlying performance and billing activity.

Use this skill before concluding how to present and reconcile unbilled and billed-in-excess balances. The goal is to force the agent to connect each balance to its source, classify it correctly under the contract asset and contract liability model, and reconcile it to revenue and billing activity.

This guidance is general and educational. Presentation and labeling differ between US GAAP and IFRS, between industries, and across entity chart of accounts and policies, all of which vary by jurisdiction. Final determinations for a specific entity should be made by a qualified accounting professional. This skill is not legal, tax, or audit advice and is not a substitute for the governing standard or entity policy.

## Core Rules

### Understand What Each Balance Represents

Unbilled amounts, sometimes called cost in excess or underbillings, arise when the entity has recognized revenue or incurred performance that exceeds the amount billed to the customer. The entity has earned the right to consideration but has not yet invoiced it. Billings in excess, sometimes called overbillings, arise when the entity has billed the customer more than the revenue recognized to date, typically through advance billings, milestone billings ahead of performance, or progress billings that exceed earned revenue.

Map each balance to its origin:

- unbilled arises from performance ahead of billing;
- billings in excess arises from billing ahead of performance;
- both are timing differences, not gains or losses.

Do not treat either as revenue or expense. They are balance sheet items that reconcile timing.

### Connect Unbilled Amounts To Contract Assets

Under the converged revenue framework, an unbilled right to consideration is generally a contract asset, not a receivable, when the right is conditioned on future performance or another condition. The legacy label of unbilled receivable is often retained in practice, but the substance must be analyzed. If the right to payment is unconditional and only time remains before payment is due, it is a receivable. If it is conditional on future performance, it is a contract asset.

Ask:

- has the entity performed and earned the right to consideration?
- is the right to payment unconditional, or conditioned on future performance or completion?
- has the entity billed the customer?

If performed but unbilled and conditional, the balance is a contract asset. If performed and the right is unconditional, it is a receivable even if not yet invoiced.

### Connect Billings In Excess To Contract Liabilities

Billings in excess of revenue are generally contract liabilities. The entity has received or is owed consideration before it has performed. The obligation is to transfer goods or services in the future. The legacy label of deferred revenue or unearned revenue is common, but the substance is a contract liability tied to a remaining performance obligation.

Ask:

- has the entity billed or received cash before performing?
- does the entity have an obligation to transfer future goods or services?
- is the amount tied to a specific performance obligation?

If billed ahead of performance, the balance is a contract liability until the related obligation is satisfied.

### Reconcile Revenue, Billings, And Cash To The Balances

The contract asset and contract liability balances must reconcile to the difference between revenue recognized and amounts billed or received. Build a reconciliation that traces each movement.

Track:

- revenue recognized in the period;
- amounts billed in the period;
- cash received in the period;
- change in contract assets;
- change in contract liabilities.

A reconciliation that does not tie means a balance is misclassified, a billing is missing, or revenue is misstated. Do not force a reconciliation by plugging a difference to an unspecified account.

### Separate By Customer And Contract

Unbilled and billed-in-excess balances should be tracked by customer and by contract, not aggregated into a single pool. Aggregation hides which contracts are ahead and which are behind, complicates revenue recognition, and prevents correct netting analysis. Each contract has its own performance and billing pattern.

### Apply Netting Rules Correctly

Contract assets and contract liabilities for the same customer and contract may be presented net only where a right of setoff exists. Across different customers or contracts, gross presentation is generally required. Netting across customers overstates or understates both assets and liabilities and misleads users about liquidity and obligations.

### Review For Collectibility And Impairment

Unbilled contract assets carry credit risk from the moment the right to consideration arises. Assess expected credit losses on contract assets as well as receivables. Billings in excess are obligations, not assets, and are not subject to credit loss, but they should be reviewed to confirm they relate to genuine performance obligations and not to amounts that should be refunded.

### Watch For Onerous Or Loss Contracts

When costs to complete exceed expected consideration, a contract may be onerous or loss-making. The existence of billings in excess or unbilled amounts does not by itself indicate a loss, but a reconciliation that reveals expected losses should trigger a separate review. Do not hide a loss contract inside timing balances.

### Disclose The Composition Of Balances

Disclosure should explain the nature and composition of contract assets and contract liabilities, opening and closing balances, significant changes, and the amount of revenue recognized from contract liabilities. The labels used internally, such as unbilled or billings in excess, should be explained so users understand the substance.

## Common Traps

### Calling Unbilled Amounts Receivables By Default

The label unbilled receivable persists in practice, but an unbilled right that is still conditional is a contract asset. Presenting it as a receivable overstates unconditional rights and misrepresents the quality of working capital.

### Treating Billings In Excess As Deferred Revenue Without Analysis

Billings in excess are contract liabilities, but recording them in a generic deferred revenue account without linking them to a specific performance obligation hides the obligation and complicates recognition. The balance must be tied to the obligation that will satisfy it.

### Netting Across Customers And Contracts

Combining all unbilled and all billed-in-excess balances into one net figure, across different customers, is only correct where setoff rights exist. Netting without that right misstates both sides of the balance sheet.

### Failing To Reconcile

When contract asset and contract liability balances do not reconcile to revenue, billings, and cash, the difference is often plugged to an unspecified account rather than investigated. An unreconciled difference signals a missing billing, a misclassified amount, or misstated revenue.

### Ignoring Credit Risk On Contract Assets

Unbilled contract assets are not immune to credit risk. Assuming only invoiced receivables need impairment assessment leaves contract assets overstated and understates expected credit losses.

### Confusing Timing Differences With Gains Or Losses

Unbilled and billed-in-excess balances are timing differences, not income or expense. Recognizing them as revenue or expense when they arise distorts the income statement and the period of recognition.

### Missing Loss Contract Indications

A reconciliation that shows costs exceeding expected consideration may indicate an onerous or loss contract. Overlooking this because the focus is on timing balances leaves a required provision unrecognized.

### Aggregating Into A Single Pool

Pooling all unbilled and all overbilled amounts without customer or contract detail prevents correct netting, obscures which contracts are ahead or behind, and makes revenue recognition unreliable.

## Self-Check

- Are unbilled amounts classified as contract assets when the right to payment is conditional, and as receivables only when the right is unconditional?
- Are billings in excess classified as contract liabilities tied to specific remaining performance obligations, rather than generic deferred revenue?
- Do the contract asset and contract liability balances reconcile to the difference between revenue recognized, amounts billed, and cash received, without plugs?
- Are balances tracked by customer and contract rather than aggregated into a single pool?
- Is netting applied only where a right of setoff exists, with gross presentation otherwise?
- Have expected credit losses been assessed on contract assets as well as receivables?
- Have balances been reviewed to confirm they relate to genuine performance obligations and not to refundable or misclassified amounts?
- Has the reconciliation been reviewed for indications of onerous or loss contracts requiring separate provision?
- Does disclosure explain the nature, composition, opening and closing balances, significant changes, and revenue recognized from contract liabilities?
- Have material or borderline classification and reconciliation issues been flagged for review by a qualified accounting professional familiar with the entity's framework and policies?
