# Progress Toward Completion Measurement

When revenue is recognized over time, the entity must measure its progress toward completion of the performance obligation. The measure of progress determines how much revenue is recognized each period, and small errors compound across the life of a contract. Agents frequently default to cost-to-cost percentage of completion without analyzing whether it depicts the transfer of control, apply a single measure to every contract, and fail to constrain or revise the measure when inputs change. The result is a revenue pattern that does not reflect the actual transfer of goods or services to the customer.

Use this skill before concluding how progress should be measured for an over-time obligation. The goal is to force the agent to choose a measure that faithfully depicts the transfer of control, to apply it consistently, to revise it when facts change, and to flag where the judgment requires professional review.

This guidance is general and educational. Measures of progress interact with the governing standard, industry practice, and entity policy, all of which vary by jurisdiction and reporting framework. Final determinations for a specific contract should be made by a qualified accounting professional with access to the full arrangement and cost data. This skill is not legal, tax, or audit advice and is not a substitute for the standard text.

## Core Rules

### Confirm Over-Time Recognition Before Measuring Progress

A measure of progress only applies when the obligation is satisfied over time. Confirm first that at least one over-time criterion is met, such as the customer simultaneously receiving and consuming benefits, the entity creating or enhancing an asset the customer controls, or the entity having no alternative use plus an enforceable right to payment for performance to date. If none are met, revenue is recognized at a point in time and no measure of progress is needed.

Do not assume over-time recognition from the length of the contract or the use of a cost-to-cost method. The criterion is about control transfer, not duration.

### Choose A Measure That Depicts Transfer Of Control

The measure of progress must faithfully depict the entity's performance, meaning it must reflect the transfer of control of goods or services to the customer. There are two broad families of methods, input methods and output methods, and the choice depends on which better depicts the transfer.

Input methods measure progress based on the entity's efforts or inputs, such as labor hours, costs incurred, or machine hours. Output methods measure progress based on results or outputs achieved, such as units delivered, milestones reached, or surveys of work performed.

Ask:

- does the measure reflect the actual transfer of value to the customer?
- are the inputs proportional to the transfer of control, or are some costs front-loaded or back-loaded?
- is there a direct relationship between outputs delivered and control transferred?
- does the customer benefit as the entity incurs cost, or only when a discrete output is achieved?

### Apply Input Methods Carefully

Input methods, especially cost-to-cost, are common but contain traps. The assumption is that cost incurred correlates with progress. This fails when costs are incurred that do not transfer value to the customer.

Adjust or exclude from input measures:

- costs of materials that are not significant to the contract or that do not depict progress;
- costs of inefficiencies, rework, or wasted effort that are not recovered;
- uninstalled materials or equipment that the customer does not yet control;
- front-loaded setup costs that overstate early progress;
- costs affected by significant inflation, scarcity, or pricing changes.

If cost does not depict progress, an output method or an adjusted input method may be more faithful.

### Apply Output Methods With Attention To Milestone Gaming

Output methods such as units delivered or milestones reached can depict transfer well, but they can be distorted by milestone selection. If milestones are bunched at the end, revenue is back-loaded even though control transferred earlier. If milestones are front-loaded, revenue is recognized before value is delivered.

For milestone-based measures, ask:

- is each milestone proportional to the value transferred at that point?
- are there periods with no milestones where control is nonetheless transferring?
- are milestones based on contractual billing events rather than on substance?

If milestones reflect billing rather than performance, the measure does not faithfully depict progress.

### Use A Single Measure Per Obligation

Each performance obligation should have one measure of progress applied consistently. Do not mix input and output methods within the same obligation, and do not switch methods period to period without a documented reason. Consistency supports comparability across periods and contracts.

### Constrain Or Limit The Measure When It Overstates Progress

Some measures produce progress that exceeds the value transferred. Limit the measure so it does not overstate. For example, uninstalled materials should generally not generate revenue equal to their cost if the customer does not control them, and early setup costs should not produce disproportionate revenue. When a measure would overstate progress, cap or adjust it.

### Reassess And Revise The Measure Each Period

The measure of progress is an estimate that must be updated as new information arrives. Revised cost estimates, changed scope, inefficiencies, and modified milestones all change the measure. Revisions are generally recognized as a cumulative catch-up adjustment in the period the estimate changes, not retrospectively.

Ask each period:

- have total cost estimates been updated with current information?
- has scope changed through modification?
- are actual inputs tracking the expected pattern?
- has any constraint or cap been revisited?

### Confirm An Enforceable Right To Payment

For the no-alternative-use plus enforceable-right criterion, the right to payment must be for performance to date, not merely for a deposit or a termination penalty. Verify that the contract provides an enforceable right to payment for work already performed, including a reasonable profit margin where the framework requires it. A termination penalty or a forfeiture of deposit is not the same as a right to payment for performance to date.

### Document The Measure And Its Inputs

Record the chosen method, the inputs, the assumptions, and the reasons the measure depicts transfer of control. Document any adjustments, exclusions, or caps. Documentation is what makes the measure defensible and revisable.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Cost-To-Cost Without Analysis

Cost-to-cost is convenient because cost data is usually available, but it is only faithful when cost incurred correlates with transfer of control. Front-loaded setup, uninstalled materials, inefficiencies, and price changes all break that correlation. Applying cost-to-cost by default overstates or understates revenue.

### Letting Billing Milestones Drive The Measure

Billing milestones are often set for cash flow or risk reasons, not to reflect performance. Using billing events as the measure of progress recognizes revenue when invoices are issued rather than when control transfers, distorting the pattern.

### Including Uninstalled Materials In Progress

Materials purchased but not yet incorporated, or not yet controlled by the customer, should generally not generate revenue equal to their cost. Including them overstates progress and revenue.

### Failing To Revise The Estimate

A measure set at inception and never updated becomes stale. As cost estimates change, scope shifts, or inefficiencies emerge, the measure must be revised. Carrying a stale measure produces cumulative errors that grow over the contract.

### Applying The Same Measure To Every Contract

Different obligations transfer control differently. A subscription may suit an output method based on time, a construction contract may suit cost-to-cost with adjustments, and a service with discrete deliverables may suit milestones. Applying one measure to all contracts ignores the nature of each obligation.

### Overstating Progress With Front-Loaded Costs

Significant setup, design, or mobilization costs incurred early can make cost-to-cost show rapid early progress even though little value has transferred to the customer. Without a cap or adjustment, this front-loads revenue.

### Ignoring The Enforceable Right Requirement

Assuming a long contract automatically qualifies for over-time recognition because the entity has no alternative use overlooks the enforceable right to payment test. A termination penalty or deposit forfeiture is not the same as payment for performance to date.

### Mixing Methods Within An Obligation

Switching between input and output methods, or blending them, without a documented reason undermines the faithful depiction of progress and comparability across periods.

## Self-Check

- Has over-time recognition been confirmed against the control transfer criteria before selecting a measure of progress?
- Does the chosen measure, whether input or output, faithfully depict the transfer of control rather than merely the entity's effort or billing?
- For input methods, have uninstalled materials, inefficiencies, front-loaded setup, and pricing distortions been adjusted or excluded so cost reflects progress?
- For output methods, are milestones proportional to value transferred rather than to contractual billing events?
- Is a single measure applied consistently to each obligation, without mixing methods absent a documented reason?
- Has the measure been constrained or capped where it would otherwise overstate progress?
- Is the measure revised each period with updated cost estimates, scope changes, and new information, with revisions recognized as cumulative catch-up?
- For the no-alternative-use criterion, is there an enforceable right to payment for performance to date, not merely a termination penalty or deposit?
- Are the method, inputs, assumptions, adjustments, and caps documented so a reviewer can follow and revisit the conclusion?
- Have material or borderline measurement judgments been flagged for review by a qualified accounting professional familiar with the entity's framework and policies?
