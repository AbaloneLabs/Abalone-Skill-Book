# Contract Asset And Contract Liability Recognition

Contract assets and contract liabilities are the balance sheet consequences of revenue recognition timing. They appear when the entity's performance and the customer's payment are not aligned. Although the definitions seem simple, agents frequently confuse contract assets with receivables, misclassify contract liabilities as deferred income or unearned revenue without analysis, and fail to present and disclose them correctly. These errors distort liquidity, working capital, and the picture of the entity's obligations and rights.

Use this skill before concluding how a customer arrangement should be presented on the balance sheet. The goal is to force the agent to separate unconditional rights from conditional rights, to recognize obligations to transfer goods or services as contract liabilities, and to flag where presentation and disclosure require professional judgment.

This guidance is general and educational. Balance sheet presentation rules differ between ASC 606 and IFRS 15, between US GAAP and IFRS on classification, and across jurisdictions and entity policies. Final determinations for a specific entity should be made by a qualified accounting professional. This skill is not legal, tax, or audit advice and is not a substitute for the governing standard.

## Core Rules

### Recognize A Contract Asset When Performance Precedes An Unconditional Right

A contract asset is the entity's right to consideration in exchange for goods or services already transferred, when that right is conditioned on something other than the passage of time. The defining feature is conditionality. The entity has performed, but its right to payment depends on future performance or on another condition being met.

A contract asset arises when:

- the entity has transferred goods or services;
- the entity has a right to consideration that is not yet unconditional;
- payment depends on future performance, delivery of another item, or contract completion.

Do not present a conditional right as a receivable. A receivable requires an unconditional right to consideration, where only time must pass before payment is due.

### Recognize A Receivable When The Right Is Unconditional

A receivable represents an unconditional right to consideration. Once the entity has performed and the only remaining condition is the passage of time before payment is due, the right is a receivable. At that point, a contract asset is reclassified to a receivable.

Ask:

- has the entity satisfied the performance obligation that gives rise to the right to payment?
- is the right to payment unconditional, with only time remaining before due?
- are there no further conditions, such as future performance or contract completion?

If the right is still conditional, it remains a contract asset. If it is unconditional, it is a receivable. Conflating the two misstates the quality of the entity's rights and the expected timing of cash.

### Recognize A Contract Liability When Payment Precedes Performance

A contract liability is the entity's obligation to transfer goods or services to a customer for which the entity has already received consideration, or for which an amount is due from the customer. It arises whenever the customer pays or owes before the entity performs.

A contract liability arises from:

- advance payments and deposits;
- prepaid subscriptions or annual fees;
- billings ahead of performance;
- nonrefundable upfront fees that relate to future goods or services;
- customer loyalty points or credits with standalone value.

Recognize revenue from the contract liability as the entity satisfies the related performance obligation. Do not recognize the liability as revenue when cash is received; recognize it as performance occurs.

### Separate Contract Balances From Other Liabilities

Contract liabilities are not generic deferred income or accrued liabilities. They are tied to specific performance obligations. Keep them separated so that revenue recognition, disclosure, and the relationship to remaining performance obligations are clear.

Avoid:

- lumping contract liabilities into a general accrued liabilities account;
- treating all customer deposits as the same without analyzing what they obligate;
- presenting contract assets netted against contract liabilities for different customers without checking whether netting is permitted.

Net presentation across customers is restricted. Contract assets and contract liabilities should generally be presented gross by contract, and netting only where a right of setoff exists.

### Distinguish Contract Liabilities From Refund Obligations And Financing

Not every amount received in advance is a contract liability. If the entity has an obligation to refund the amount regardless of performance, the treatment may differ. If the arrangement is in substance a financing arrangement, the receipt may be a financial liability rather than revenue-related. Ask whether the entity's obligation is to perform or to repay, and whether the arrangement is genuinely a customer contract.

### Reassess Credit Risk On Receivables And Contract Assets

Both receivables and contract assets are subject to expected credit loss assessment, but the timing and inputs differ. Receivables are evaluated when the unconditional right exists. Contract assets are evaluated while the right is still conditional. Do not assume a contract asset carries no credit risk simply because it is not yet billed; the risk of noncollection exists from the moment the right to consideration arises.

### Track Performance Obligations Tied To Each Balance

Each contract asset and contract liability should be traceable to the performance obligation that created it and the obligation that will satisfy it. This traceability supports disclosure of remaining performance obligations, revenue recognized from balances, and the expected timing of recognition.

### Disclose The Required Information

Disclosure expectations include the existence and nature of contract assets and liabilities, opening and closing balances, an explanation of significant changes, the amount of revenue recognized from contract liabilities, and the amount of impairment losses. Agents often understate disclosure because they focus only on recognition. Plan disclosure as part of the conclusion, not as an afterthought.

## Common Traps

### Presenting Conditional Rights As Receivables

The most common error is recording a contract asset as a receivable because the entity has performed. If the right to payment is still conditioned on future performance, it is a contract asset, not a receivable. This overstates the entity's unconditional claims and the quality of its working capital.

### Treating Contract Liabilities As Generic Deferred Income

Contract liabilities are specific obligations tied to performance obligations. Dumping them into a general deferred income account hides the link to revenue recognition, complicates disclosure, and can lead to recognizing revenue on the wrong basis or at the wrong time.

### Recognizing Revenue When Cash Is Received

A contract liability is not revenue. Recognizing revenue at the moment of cash receipt, before performance, is premature. The liability converts to revenue only as the entity satisfies the related obligation.

### Netting Contract Assets And Liabilities Without A Right Of Setoff

Aggregating all contract assets and all contract liabilities into a single net figure is only appropriate where a right of setoff exists, typically within the same contract. Netting across different customers misstates both assets and liabilities and misleads users about liquidity.

### Ignoring Credit Risk On Contract Assets

Contract assets carry credit risk from the moment the right to consideration arises. Assuming only receivables need impairment assessment leaves contract assets overstated and understates expected credit losses.

### Confusing Refund Obligations With Contract Liabilities

If the entity must repay the customer regardless of performance, the obligation may not be a contract liability in the revenue sense. Misclassifying a refund obligation or a financing receipt as a contract liability distorts both revenue and the balance sheet.

### Understating Disclosure

Recognition is only half the work. Failing to disclose opening and closing balances, significant changes, revenue recognized from contract liabilities, and impairment leaves the financial statements incomplete and noncompliant, even when the recognition entries are correct.

### Forgetting Reclassification When Conditions Are Met

When a conditional right becomes unconditional, the contract asset must be reclassified to a receivable. Leaving it as a contract asset understates receivables and misrepresents the timing of expected cash.

## Self-Check

- Are conditional rights to consideration presented as contract assets rather than receivables, with conditionality clearly identified?
- Are unconditional rights presented as receivables, and have contract assets been reclassified when the only remaining condition is the passage of time?
- Are amounts received or due before performance recognized as contract liabilities tied to specific performance obligations, rather than as generic deferred income?
- Is revenue recognized from contract liabilities only as the related performance obligation is satisfied, not when cash is received?
- Have contract assets and contract liabilities been presented on a basis consistent with netting rules, without improper aggregation across customers?
- Has credit risk and expected credit loss been assessed for both receivables and contract assets?
- Have refund obligations and financing-type receipts been distinguished from genuine contract liabilities?
- Is each contract balance traceable to the performance obligation that created it and the obligation that will satisfy it?
- Does the conclusion include the required disclosures of balances, significant changes, revenue recognized, and impairment?
- Have material or borderline classification decisions been flagged for review by a qualified accounting professional familiar with the entity's framework and policies?
