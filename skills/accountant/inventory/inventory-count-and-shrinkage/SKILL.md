# Inventory Count And Shrinkage

A physical inventory count confirms that the inventory on the books actually exists and is correctly stated. The difference between the perpetual records and the physical count is shrinkage, which reflects theft, damage, miscounting, mislabeling, or process errors. The count is the control that keeps the perpetual records honest, and the shrinkage adjustment is how errors are corrected. The trap is that counts are often rushed, poorly supervised, or reconciled superficially. When the count and the perpetual disagree, the difference is plugged without investigation, so the underlying problem, whether theft, a receiving error, or a costing mistake, continues. Agents frequently treat the count as a compliance exercise rather than the primary evidence for inventory accuracy.

Use this skill before conducting a physical or cycle count, reconciling to perpetual records, investigating shrinkage, or adjusting inventory. The goal is to ensure the count is accurate, the reconciliation is genuine, and shrinkage is explained and corrected at its source.

This skill covers count and shrinkage procedures. The count method, frequency, and regulatory requirements vary by industry and jurisdiction, with specific rules for certain regulated industries. Confirm the applicable requirements before designing count procedures.

## Core Rules

### Plan The Count To Be Accurate And Complete

An accurate count requires planning. A disorganized count produces errors that are hard to detect and correct later.

Plan for:

- a complete or cycle count approach, and a defined cutoff so movements are controlled during the count;
- pre-count organization, clean aisles, sorted stock, identified damaged goods;
- trained counters and independent recount or verification for high-value items;
- numbered count sheets or scanners to prevent loss or duplication of counts;
- a cutoff procedure so receipts and shipments during the count are properly handled.

A count without cutoff control mixes periods and produces unreconcilable differences. Freeze movement or carefully tag in-transit items.

### Control The Count Cutoff

Cutoff is critical during a count. Goods received or shipped around the count must be recorded in the correct period and not double-counted or omitted.

Apply cutoff by:

- stopping movement during the count where possible;
- tagging and separately counting goods received but not yet recorded;
- ensuring shipped goods are excluded from the count if already recorded as sold;
- reconciling the count to the perpetual as of a specific moment.

A count that does not control cutoff will not reconcile, because the perpetual and the physical reflect different sets of transactions.

### Reconcile Count To Perpetual And Investigate Differences

The count is only useful if it is reconciled to the perpetual records and the differences are investigated. A count that is recorded without reconciliation confirms nothing.

The reconciliation should:

- compare counted quantities to perpetual quantities by item;
- identify items with differences for recount or investigation;
- investigate the cause of material differences before adjusting;
- adjust the perpetual to the verified physical count.

Investigating before adjusting is essential. A difference may be a counting error, a receiving or shipping error, a costing error, theft, or damage. Adjusting without finding the cause leaves the source problem in place to cause future shrinkage.

### Identify The Cause Of Shrinkage

Shrinkage is a symptom. The cause determines whether it is a one-time error or an ongoing loss that needs a control fix.

Common causes of shrinkage:

- theft by employees or others;
- damage or spoilage not recorded;
- receiving errors, goods received but not recorded or short;
- shipping errors, goods shipped but not recorded;
- miscounting or mislabeling during prior counts;
- unit of measure conversion errors;
- costing or system posting errors.

Categorize shrinkage by cause so management can address the root, whether through security, process changes, or system fixes, rather than absorbing a recurring loss.

### Use Cycle Counts To Maintain Accuracy Between Physical Counts

A full physical count is periodic, often annual. Cycle counting, counting a subset of items each period on a rotating basis, maintains accuracy throughout the year and identifies problems earlier.

For cycle counting:

- count high-value and high-risk items more frequently;
- investigate and correct differences immediately;
- track accuracy by item class to find problem areas;
- use cycle count results to assess whether the perpetual system is reliable.

Cycle counting does not eliminate the need for a full count where required, but it reduces surprises and keeps the perpetual closer to reality.

### Adjust Inventory With Proper Authorization And Documentation

Inventory adjustments from count differences should be authorized and documented, not posted casually.

For each adjustment:

- record the item, quantity difference, and cost effect;
- document the cause identified, or that investigation was performed;
- obtain approval per the entity's threshold policy;
- post the adjustment to inventory and the offset, typically cost of goods sold or a shrinkage account.

Large or unusual adjustments may indicate a control weakness or a deeper problem and should be escalated.

### Reconcile The Inventory Subledger To The General Ledger

After count adjustments, the inventory subledger must reconcile to the general ledger inventory account. A difference indicates a posting gap, a timing difference, or an adjustment that did not flow through both.

Confirm the subledger total, adjusted for the count, equals the general ledger balance each period.

### Assess Whether Shrinkage Indicates A Control Problem

A pattern of recurring or increasing shrinkage is a control problem, not a normal cost. When shrinkage exceeds expectations or concentrates in certain locations or categories, investigate the control environment.

Look for:

- locations or product categories with persistent shrinkage;
- breakdowns in receiving, shipping, or access controls;
- staffing or supervision gaps;
- system or process errors causing systematic miscounts.

Shrinkage that is accepted as normal without investigation becomes a permanent leak.

## Common Traps

### Rushed Or Disorganized Counts

A count done in haste, without organization or trained counters, produces errors that are indistinguishable from real shrinkage. Plan and supervise the count.

### No Cutoff Control

Without cutoff control, the count and the perpetual reflect different transactions and will not reconcile. Freeze movement or tag in-transit items.

### Plugging Differences Without Investigation

Adjusting the perpetual to the count without finding the cause hides theft, errors, or process failures. Investigate before adjusting.

### Accepting Recurring Shrinkage As Normal

Persistent shrinkage is a control problem. Treating it as a cost of doing business leaves the leak open.

### Counting Without Reconciling

A count that is not reconciled to the perpetual confirms nothing. The reconciliation is the point of the count.

### Ignoring The Cause Categories

Shrinkage from theft, damage, and process errors need different fixes. Treating all shrinkage the same prevents root-cause resolution.

### Subledger Not Reconciled To General Ledger

If the adjusted inventory subledger does not tie to the general ledger, the balance sheet is wrong even after the count.

## Self-Check

- Is the count planned with organization, trained counters, cutoff control, and numbered sheets or scanners?
- Is cutoff controlled so receipts and shipments during the count are in the correct period and not double-counted or omitted?
- Is the count reconciled to the perpetual by item, with differences investigated for cause before adjusting?
- Is shrinkage categorized by cause, theft, damage, receiving or shipping error, miscounting, or system error, to enable root-cause fixes?
- Are cycle counts used to maintain accuracy and identify problems between full physical counts?
- Are inventory adjustments authorized, documented with cause, and posted to inventory and the offset account?
- Does the inventory subledger reconcile to the general ledger after count adjustments?
- Is recurring or concentrated shrinkage assessed as a control problem requiring investigation rather than accepted as normal?
- Have industry-specific count and regulatory requirements been confirmed before designing count procedures?
