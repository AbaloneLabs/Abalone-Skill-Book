# Lower Of Cost Or NRV And Write-Downs

Inventory must be carried at the lower of its cost or its net realizable value, or market under some standards. This rule requires writing inventory down when its realizable value falls below cost, whether due to obsolescence, damage, price decline, or slow movement. The write-down recognizes the loss in the period the decline occurs, not when the inventory is eventually sold. The trap is that NRV assessment is a judgment that is easy to defer or to make too generously. Agents often carry inventory at cost long after it has become obsolete or unprofitable, overstating assets and understating the loss. Or they write down aggressively without support, then reverse conveniently when it suits results. Both misstate the period.

Use this skill before testing inventory at lower of cost or NRV, recording a write-down, assessing obsolete or slow-moving inventory, or handling a reversal. The goal is to ensure inventory is carried at no more than its realizable value, with write-downs supported, recognized in the right period, and reversible only where the standard permits.

This skill covers lower of cost or NRV principles. The definition of NRV or market, the level at which the test is applied, and the rules on reversal differ between standards, such as US GAAP lower of cost or market with no reversal versus IFRS lower of cost or NRV with reversal permitted. Confirm the governing framework before applying these rules.

## Core Rules

### Understand What NRV Or Market Means Under The Applicable Standard

Net realizable value is the estimated selling price in the ordinary course of business, less reasonably predictable costs of completion and selling. Under US GAAP, the test uses market, defined as replacement cost with a ceiling and a floor based on NRV. The definitions and the resulting test differ.

Confirm for the applicable standard:

- whether the test uses NRV, market, or another measure;
- how the measure is defined, including the costs to deduct;
- the ceiling and floor constraints where applicable;
- whether the test is applied to the entire inventory or to major categories.

Applying the wrong definition produces a wrong write-down amount.

### Assess NRV For Each Category Of Inventory

NRV assessment is not a single calculation for all inventory. Different categories face different risks of obsolescence, price decline, and damage.

Assess NRV considering:

- current selling prices and recent price trends;
- costs still to be incurred to complete and sell the goods;
- the age and turnover of the inventory;
- obsolescence due to new products, technology, or model changes;
- damage, spoilage, or deterioration;
- demand trends and order backlog.

Slow-moving and obsolete categories require more aggressive assessment than fast-moving, current-product inventory. Segment the assessment so problems are not hidden in an aggregate average.

### Apply The Test At The Appropriate Level

The lower of cost or NRV test is generally applied to each item or to groups of similar or related items, not to total inventory. Applying it to the total allows rising values in one category to offset falling values in another, hiding needed write-downs.

Determine the appropriate grouping:

- item-by-item is the most conservative and most common;
- grouping similar or related items is permitted where appropriate;
- offsetting within a group should reflect items that are intended to be sold together.

Applying the test only at the total inventory level overstates inventory by netting gains and losses that should be assessed separately.

### Record Write-Downs In The Period The Decline Occurs

A write-down is required when NRV falls below cost, regardless of whether the inventory has been sold. The loss belongs in the period of the decline.

The write-down:

- reduces inventory to its NRV;
- recognizes the loss, typically in cost of goods sold or as a separate write-down expense depending on presentation;
- is supported by the NRV calculation that shows the decline.

Deferring the write-down until the inventory is sold overstates inventory and understates the loss in the period of decline, shifting it to the period of sale.

### Base The Write-Down On A Supportable NRV Calculation

A write-down needs support, not just a judgment that inventory looks old. The NRV calculation should show the estimated selling price, the costs to complete and sell, and the resulting NRV compared to cost.

Document for each material write-down:

- the category and quantity of inventory affected;
- the estimated selling price and its basis, such as current price lists or recent sales;
- the estimated costs to complete and sell;
- the resulting NRV and the write-down amount;
- the reason for the decline, obsolescence, damage, or price drop.

An unsupported write-down is an audit finding and may be challenged or reversed.

### Identify Obsolete And Slow-Moving Inventory Systematically

Relying on ad hoc identification of obsolete inventory misses problems. Use systematic indicators to flag inventory for NRV review.

Common indicators:

- inventory age exceeding a defined threshold, such as no movement in six or twelve months;
- discontinued or superseded product lines;
- inventory quantities exceeding forecast demand;
- damaged or returned goods;
- negative margin at current selling prices.

Generate an aging and turnover report each period and review flagged items for potential write-down.

### Handle Reversals According To The Standard

Reversal rules differ critically between standards. Under IFRS, a previous write-down is reversed, up to the original cost, when the circumstances that caused the decline no longer exist. Under US GAAP lower of cost or market, once inventory is written down to market, the new lower cost becomes the basis and no reversal is permitted.

Confirm:

- whether reversal is permitted under the applicable standard;
- the conditions for reversal, a genuine change in circumstances;
- the ceiling on reversal, not above original cost;
- the disclosure required for any reversal.

Reversing a write-down where the standard prohibits it, or reversing without a genuine change in circumstances, overstates inventory and manipulates the period result.

### Review The Inventory Reserve Or Allowance

Some entities record write-downs through an inventory reserve or allowance rather than directly reducing the inventory cost. The reserve must be adequate and supported, and the net inventory, cost less reserve, must equal the lower of cost or NRV.

Review the reserve each period for adequacy, and ensure the reserve methodology is consistent and documented.

## Common Traps

### Carrying Obsolete Inventory At Cost

The most common error. Inventory that has not moved, is superseded, or is damaged continues at cost, overstating assets and deferring the loss. Assess systematically each period.

### Applying The Test Only At The Total Level

Netting rising and falling categories at the total inventory level hides the categories that need write-downs. Apply the test to items or groups.

### Using The Wrong NRV Or Market Definition

NRV under IFRS and market under US GAAP differ, including ceiling and floor. Applying the wrong definition miscalculates the write-down.

### Deferring The Write-Down To The Sale Period

The loss belongs in the period of decline, not the period of sale. Waiting until sale overstates interim inventory and shifts the loss.

### Unsupported Write-Downs

A write-down without an NRV calculation is a judgment without basis. Document the selling price, costs, and resulting NRV.

### Reversing Where The Standard Prohibits

Under US GAAP lower of cost or market, write-downs are not reversed. Reversing violates the standard and overstates inventory.

### Reversing Without A Genuine Change

Even where reversal is permitted, it requires a real change in circumstances, not a desire to improve results. Reversing without cause manipulates the period.

## Self-Check

- Is the correct measure, NRV or market with applicable ceiling and floor, used for the lower of cost or NRV test under the applicable standard?
- Is NRV assessed by category, considering selling price, completion and selling costs, age, obsolescence, damage, and demand?
- Is the test applied at the item or group level rather than netted at the total inventory level?
- Are write-downs recorded in the period the decline occurs, reducing inventory to NRV and recognizing the loss?
- Is each material write-down supported by a documented NRV calculation showing selling price, costs, NRV, and the reason for decline?
- Is obsolete and slow-moving inventory identified systematically through aging, turnover, and demand analysis each period?
- For any reversal, is it permitted under the standard, triggered by a genuine change in circumstances, capped at original cost, and disclosed?
- Does the inventory reserve, if used, adequately and consistently bring net inventory to the lower of cost or NRV?
- Has the applicable standard been confirmed and a qualified accountant consulted for material write-downs or reversals?
