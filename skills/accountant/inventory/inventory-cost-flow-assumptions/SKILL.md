# Inventory Cost Flow Assumptions

When inventory items are fungible and purchased at different costs over time, the entity must adopt a cost flow assumption to determine which costs are assigned to cost of goods sold and which remain in ending inventory. The assumption (FIFO, weighted average, specific identification, or LIFO where permitted) does not need to match the physical flow of goods — it is an accounting convention for allocating costs. The choice affects gross profit, the inventory balance, and tax (where the method is permitted for tax). Agents often default to FIFO without considering whether weighted average or specific identification better fits the inventory type, or apply LIFO without understanding its jurisdictional limits and the conformity requirement.

Use this skill before selecting a cost flow assumption for a new inventory type, reviewing whether the current method remains appropriate, handling a method change, or advising on LIFO availability. The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to one method for all inventory without considering the inventory's nature, the jurisdiction's permitted methods, and the consistency requirement.

Inventory cost flow rules differ between US GAAP (ASC 330, permitting LIFO) and IFRS (IAS 2, prohibiting LIFO). Tax rules may impose conformity requirements. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard and tax law govern the detail. For material inventory portfolios or method changes, involve a qualified accountant or tax advisor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Match The Cost Flow Assumption To The Nature Of The Inventory

The cost flow assumption should be appropriate for the inventory's nature. Specific identification is required for inventory that is not ordinarily interchangeable (unique items, high-value goods, custom products) and for goods produced and segregated for specific projects. FIFO (first-in, first-out) assumes the oldest inventory is sold first; it is appropriate for most fungible goods and tends to match the physical flow of perishable or fast-moving inventory. Weighted average (or moving average under a perpetual system) smooths cost fluctuations and is appropriate for homogeneous goods with frequent purchases.

Do not apply a single method to all inventory types without judgment. A jeweler uses specific identification for unique pieces; a grocer uses FIFO for perishables; a manufacturer of screws may use weighted average for homogeneous parts.

### Apply Specific Identification Where Items Are Not Interchangeable

Specific identification tracks the actual cost of each identifiable item. It is required for inventory that is not ordinarily interchangeable and for goods produced and segregated for specific projects. It is optional (and often impractical) for fungible goods. Specific identification produces the most precise cost allocation but requires tracking each item and is vulnerable to earnings management (selecting which specific items are "sold" to manipulate cost of goods sold).

When using specific identification, apply it consistently and based on the actual flow of the specific items, not on a selection designed to achieve a desired profit result.

### Apply FIFO When Older Costs Flow To Cost Of Goods Sold First

Under FIFO, the costs of the earliest purchases are assigned to cost of goods sold first, leaving the most recent costs in ending inventory. In a period of rising prices, FIFO produces the lowest cost of goods sold, the highest gross profit, and the highest ending inventory value (closest to current replacement cost). In a period of falling prices, the reverse.

FIFO is permitted under both US GAAP and IFRS. It tends to report ending inventory at amounts near current replacement cost, which is a strength for balance sheet relevance. Its weakness is that it matches older costs against current revenue in the income statement.

### Apply Weighted Average To Smooth Cost Fluctuations

Under the weighted average method, the cost of goods available for sale is divided by the units available to produce an average cost per unit, applied to both cost of goods sold and ending inventory. Under a periodic system, the average is computed at period end over all purchases. Under a perpetual system (moving average), the average is recalculated after each purchase.

Weighted average smooths the effect of price fluctuations and is simple to apply to homogeneous inventory. It is permitted under both US GAAP and IFRS. Its weakness is that the average may not reflect either the oldest or the most recent cost, particularly under a periodic system with significant price changes during the period.

### Consider LIFO Only Where Permitted, With The Conformity Rule

LIFO (last-in, first-out) assigns the most recent costs to cost of goods sold and the oldest costs to ending inventory. In a period of rising prices, LIFO produces the highest cost of goods sold, the lowest gross profit, and the lowest ending inventory (which may be far below current replacement cost). LIFO is permitted under US GAAP (ASC 330) but is prohibited under IFRS (IAS 2).

In the US, entities using LIFO for tax must use LIFO for book (the LIFO conformity rule), and vice versa. Switching from LIFO is generally a change in accounting principle applied retrospectively. LIFO creates complexities: LIFO layers (each year's incremental inventory at that year's cost), LIFO liquidation (selling older, lower-cost layers, which can produce a one-time profit boost), and the LIFO reserve (the difference between LIFO and FIFO inventory value, which must be disclosed). Consider LIFO only where the tax benefit outweighs the complexity and the method is permitted.

### Apply The Cost Flow Assumption Consistently

The cost flow assumption must be applied consistently to all inventory having a similar nature and use. An entity may use different methods for different classes of inventory with different natures (FIFO for perishables, specific identification for custom goods), but it cannot switch methods for the same inventory class without justification. A change in method is a change in accounting policy, applied retrospectively with restated comparatives and disclosure.

Consistency enhances comparability across periods. Review the method's appropriateness periodically, but do not change it casually.

### Choose Between Periodic And Perpetual Systems Based On Information Needs

A periodic system updates inventory and cost of goods sold at period end (via physical count); a perpetual system updates them continuously (via each transaction). The perpetual system provides better inventory control and more timely information but requires more infrastructure. The cost flow assumption interacts with the system: under a perpetual system, FIFO and moving average are applied continuously; under a periodic system, FIFO and weighted average are applied at period end. Specific identification requires a perpetual system.

Choose the system based on the value of real-time inventory information, the cost of the system, and the inventory's susceptibility to shrinkage or obsolescence.

### Disclose The Cost Flow Method And Any LIFO Reserve

Disclose the cost flow assumption used for each material inventory class. If LIFO is used, disclose the LIFO reserve (the difference between LIFO and FIFO cost) and the effect of any LIFO liquidation. Users rely on this to compare entities and to understand the inventory valuation.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To FIFO For All Inventory Without Considering The Nature

FIFO is reasonable for many fungible goods but is not appropriate for unique or high-value items, which require specific identification. Apply the method that fits the inventory's nature.

### Using Specific Identification To Manage Earnings

Specific identification allows selecting which items are "sold," which can be used to manipulate cost of goods sold. Apply it based on the actual flow of identifiable items, not to achieve a profit target.

### Applying LIFO Under IFRS

LIFO is prohibited under IFRS (IAS 2). Using it for IFRS-reporting entities is incorrect. LIFO is available only under US GAAP.

### Ignoring The LIFO Conformity Rule

In the US, LIFO for tax requires LIFO for book. Using LIFO for tax and FIFO for book violates the conformity rule and can trigger tax penalties.

### Switching Methods Without Retrospective Application

A change in cost flow method is a change in accounting policy, applied retrospectively with restated comparatives. Applying it prospectively is incorrect.

### Applying Different Methods To Similar Inventory

The method must be consistent for inventory of a similar nature and use. Using FIFO for one warehouse of the same product and weighted average for another is inconsistent.

### Failing To Disclose The LIFO Reserve

LIFO inventory may be far below replacement cost. Without disclosing the LIFO reserve, users cannot assess the inventory's current value or compare the entity to FIFO-reporting peers.

## Self-Check

- Is the cost flow assumption matched to the inventory's nature (specific identification for non-interchangeable items, FIFO or weighted average for fungible goods)?
- Is specific identification used for unique, high-value, or project-segregated inventory, applied based on actual item flow and not for earnings management?
- Is FIFO applied where older costs flow to cost of goods sold first, with ending inventory near current replacement cost?
- Is weighted average applied to smooth cost fluctuations for homogeneous inventory, with the average computed appropriately for periodic or perpetual systems?
- If LIFO is used, is it permitted in the applicable jurisdiction (US GAAP only, not IFRS), with the LIFO conformity rule satisfied and the LIFO reserve disclosed?
- Is the cost flow assumption applied consistently to inventory of similar nature and use, with any method change treated as a change in accounting policy applied retrospectively?
- Is the inventory system (periodic or perpetual) appropriate for the information needs and the inventory's susceptibility to shrinkage?
- Are the cost flow methods and any LIFO reserve disclosed, and has a qualified accountant or tax advisor reviewed method selection and changes?
