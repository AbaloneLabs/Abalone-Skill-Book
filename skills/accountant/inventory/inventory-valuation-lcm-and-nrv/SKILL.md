# Inventory Valuation: Lower Of Cost And Net Realizable Value

Inventory is carried at cost until a reporting date, when it must be tested and written down to the lower of cost and net realizable value (IFRS) or lower of cost or market (US GAAP). This test forces the entity to recognize declines in inventory value before the inventory is sold, ensuring the balance sheet does not overstate inventory. The judgment is substantial: estimating net realizable value (the estimated selling price less estimated costs of completion and selling costs), identifying obsolete or slow-moving inventory, and deciding whether a prior write-down can be reversed. Agents often defer write-downs (to protect profit), apply a blanket percentage reserve instead of testing specific items, or fail to reverse a write-down when conditions improve (under IFRS).

Use this skill before performing the period-end inventory valuation test, assessing obsolescence, recognizing or reversing a write-down, or advising on the difference between US GAAP and IFRS valuation rules. The goal is to prevent the agent from carrying inventory above its realizable value, using a formulaic reserve instead of item-level testing, or mishandling the reversal rules.

Inventory valuation rules differ between US GAAP (ASC 330, lower of cost or market with a market defined as replacement cost subject to a ceiling and floor) and IFRS (IAS 2, lower of cost and net realizable value, with reversal permitted). This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For material inventory write-downs or complex valuation, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific entity.

## Core Rules

### Carry Inventory At The Lower Of Cost And Net Realizable Value At Each Reporting Date

After determining cost (via the cost flow assumption), test inventory at each reporting date and write it down to the lower of cost and net realizable value (NRV). NRV is the estimated selling price in the ordinary course of business, less the estimated costs of completion and the estimated costs necessary to make the sale. If NRV is below cost, recognize a write-down (or a provision for decline in value) and reduce the carrying amount.

The test is applied to each item of inventory (or each group of similar items), not to the aggregate inventory. An item with NRV above cost is not written down to offset an item with NRV below cost. Apply the test item by item unless items are similar and grouped appropriately.

### Estimate Net Realizable Value Based On The Best Evidence At Reporting Date

NRV is based on the most reliable evidence of the amount the inventory will realize. This includes: the firm sales contract price, if one exists; the expected selling price based on current market conditions; and the estimated costs of completion and selling costs based on current conditions. If the inventory is a finished good, NRV is the selling price less selling costs. If it is work in progress or raw materials, NRV includes the costs to complete the item into a finished good, then less selling costs.

For raw materials held for use in production, NRV is assessed based on the NRV of the finished goods they will produce, not on the raw materials' own resale value — unless a decline in raw material prices indicates the finished goods will be produced at a loss, in which case the raw materials are written down to NRV (replacement cost).

### Write Down Obsolete, Slow-Moving, And Damaged Inventory

Inventory that is obsolete, slow-moving, damaged, or otherwise impaired must be written down to NRV (or to scrap value if unsaleable). Identify such inventory through aging analysis, turnover ratios, physical inspection, and management's assessment of salability. Obsolete inventory (no longer used or saleable) is written down to zero or scrap value. Slow-moving inventory (turning over slowly but still saleable) is written down if the estimated selling price less costs is below cost.

Do not carry obsolete or damaged inventory at cost in the hope it will eventually sell. The write-down recognizes the decline in value at the reporting date.

### Apply The US GAAP Lower Of Cost Or Market Rule Differently

Under US GAAP (ASC 330), inventory is written down to the lower of cost or market, where market is defined as current replacement cost subject to a ceiling (NRV) and a floor (NRV minus a normal profit margin). If replacement cost is within the ceiling and floor, market equals replacement cost. If replacement cost is above the ceiling, market equals the ceiling (NRV). If replacement cost is below the floor, market equals the floor (NRV minus normal profit). The write-down is the excess of cost over market.

This is more complex than the IFRS NRV test because it incorporates a normal profit margin into the floor. Confirm which framework applies and apply the correct definition of market.

### Recognize The Write-Down In Cost Of Goods Sold Or As A Separate Line

The inventory write-down is recognized as an expense, generally within cost of goods sold or as a separate line if material. The write-down reduces the inventory carrying amount. If the inventory is later sold, the reduced carrying amount flows through cost of goods sold, so the write-down is not double-counted.

### Reverse An Inventory Write-Down Under IFRS When Conditions Improve

Under IFRS (IAS 2), a write-down recognized in a prior period is reversed when the circumstances that caused the write-down no longer exist. The reversal is limited to the amount of the original write-down, so the new carrying amount is the lower of cost and the revised NRV. The reversal is recognized as a reduction in cost of goods sold (or the separate impairment line) in the period of reversal.

Under US GAAP (ASC 330), inventory write-downs create a new cost basis, and subsequent recoveries are not recognized until the inventory is sold. This is a significant difference: IFRS permits reversal; US GAAP does not. Confirm which framework applies before reversing.

### Assess NRV For Inventory Held Under Firm Sales Contracts Differently

Inventory held under a firm sales contract is valued at the contract price (less costs to complete and sell), not at the prevailing market price. If the contract price is below cost, write down to the contract-based NRV. If the contract price is above cost, no write-down is needed for that inventory (but assess whether the inventory can fulfill the contract).

### Disclose The Write-Down And The Accounting Policy

Disclose the accounting policy for inventory valuation (cost flow method and the LCM or NRV basis), the amount of write-downs recognized (and reversals under IFRS), the circumstances leading to them, and the carrying amount of inventory carried at NRV or market. Users rely on this to assess the risk of further write-downs.

## Common Traps

### Deferring Write-Downs To Protect Profit

Writing inventory down reduces profit. Deferring the write-down to a later period (or hoping the inventory will sell at cost) overstates the asset and profit. Recognize the write-down at the reporting date when NRV is below cost.

### Applying A Blanket Percentage Reserve Instead Of Item-Level Testing

A flat percentage reserve applied to total inventory does not reflect the actual condition of specific items. Obsolete inventory needs a full write-down; fast-moving inventory may need none. Test item by item or by homogeneous group.

### Using Current Selling Price Without Considering Completion And Selling Costs

NRV is the selling price less estimated costs of completion and selling costs. Using the gross selling price without deducting these costs overstates NRV and understates the write-down.

### Failing To Reverse A Write-Down Under IFRS

Under IFRS, write-downs are reversed when conditions improve, limited to the original write-down. Failing to reverse understates inventory and overstates cost of goods sold. Under US GAAP, do not reverse.

### Reversing A Write-Down Under US GAAP

Under US GAAP, inventory write-downs create a new cost basis and are not reversed until sale. Reversing them is incorrect. Confirm the framework before reversing.

### Aggregating Items For The LCM/NRV Test

The test is applied item by item (or by homogeneous group). Offsetting a write-down on one item against an unrealized gain on another is incorrect.

### Applying The IFRS NRV Rule Under US GAAP Or Vice Versa

US GAAP uses lower of cost or market (with a ceiling and floor incorporating normal profit); IFRS uses lower of cost and NRV. Applying the wrong definition misstates the write-down.

## Self-Check

- Is inventory carried at the lower of cost and net realizable value (IFRS) or lower of cost or market (US GAAP) at each reporting date, tested item by item or by homogeneous group?
- Is net realizable value estimated based on the best evidence (contract price, current selling price) less estimated completion and selling costs?
- Is obsolete, slow-moving, and damaged inventory identified through aging and inspection and written down to NRV or scrap value?
- Under US GAAP, is market defined as replacement cost subject to a ceiling (NRV) and floor (NRV minus normal profit), applied correctly?
- Is the write-down recognized as an expense (in cost of goods sold or a separate line), reducing the carrying amount without double-counting on later sale?
- Under IFRS, are prior write-downs reversed when conditions improve, limited to the original write-down amount?
- Under US GAAP, are write-downs treated as a new cost basis with no reversal until sale?
- Is inventory held under firm sales contracts valued at the contract price less completion and selling costs?
- Are the valuation policy, write-down amounts, reversals (IFRS), and circumstances disclosed, and has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed material write-downs?
