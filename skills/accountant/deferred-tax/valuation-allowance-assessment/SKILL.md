# Valuation Allowance Assessment

A deferred tax asset is only worth recognizing to the extent it is more likely than not to be realized. The valuation allowance is the mechanism that reduces a deferred tax asset when realizability is not supported. This is one of the most judgmental areas in tax accounting, because it requires forming a view about future taxable income based on evidence that is often mixed, contradictory, and heavily weighted toward the recent past. Agents tend to default either to full recognition (assuming future profits will absorb everything) or to full allowance (assuming recent losses will continue), without assembling and weighing the required evidence. Both defaults produce balances that cannot be defended.

Use this skill before assessing a valuation allowance, revising one period over period, evaluating whether new deferred tax assets can be realized, or reviewing the evidence supporting an existing allowance. The goal is to produce a valuation allowance conclusion that is evidence-based, consistently applied, and documented well enough to survive audit.

This is a tax accounting domain. The valuation allowance framework under ASC 740 (US GAAP) is a more-likely-than-not test with specific rules on positive and negative evidence, prudent limitation, and the weight given to history. IAS 12 (IFRS) recognizes deferred tax assets only to the extent realization is probable, with a different evidentiary emphasis. Local tax codes affect what counts as a source of future taxable income. Do not assume a single universal test. Final realizability judgments should be routed to a qualified tax professional. This skill is operational guidance for preparing and reviewing valuation allowance assessments, not professional tax advice, and it carries an explicit limitation that framework-specific evidentiary rules are not exhaustively covered.

## Core Rules

### Apply The More-Likely-Than-Not Realization Test

The threshold is more-likely-than-not, meaning just over 50 percent likelihood of realization. This is not a high threshold, but it must be met for each component of the deferred tax asset, considering all sources of realization available under the applicable framework. If realization of some or all of the asset is not more likely than not, a valuation allowance is required for that portion.

The test is applied at the level of each tax-paying component or jurisdiction, not in aggregate across the group. A deferred tax asset in a loss entity cannot be supported by projected profits of an unrelated profitable entity unless specific rules (such as consolidated group or same-employer rules) permit offset.

### Identify All Available Sources Of Future Taxable Income

Realization can be supported by several sources, typically considered in a defined order. Confirm the order required by the framework, which commonly includes:

- future reversals of existing taxable temporary differences, which will generate taxable income in the same jurisdiction;
- future taxable income exclusive of reversing temporary differences and carryforwards;
- taxable income in prior carryback years, where carrybacks are permitted;
- tax-planning strategies that are prudent and feasible, such as accelerating recognition or converting assets.

Each source must be specific to the jurisdiction and legally available. Vague assertions of "future profitability" are not a source; a forecast with support is.

### Assemble And Weigh Positive And Negative Evidence

The assessment requires weighing all positive evidence (supporting realization) against all negative evidence (undercutting realization). The weight given to each type of evidence is not equal and is constrained by the framework.

Positive evidence may include:

- a history of profitability;
- contracts or backlog supporting future income;
- existing taxable temporary differences that will reverse and absorb the deferred tax asset;
- prudent, feasible tax-planning strategies;
- expiration horizons long enough to allow recovery.

Negative evidence may include:

- a cumulative loss in recent years;
- current-period and forecasted losses;
- a short or expiring carryforward horizon;
- a history of unused loss carryforwards;
- unsettled or declining business conditions;
- legal or ownership-change limits on carryforward use.

Under ASC 740, negative evidence is given significant weight, and a cumulative loss in recent years is difficult to overcome with projections alone. Confirm the framework's weighting rules.

### Treat Cumulative Recent Losses As Strong Negative Evidence

A history of losses, especially a cumulative loss over recent years, is among the most powerful negative evidence. Frameworks generally require that such history not be dismissed lightly. Projections of future profit cannot simply override a sustained loss record; the agent must explain specifically why the future differs from the past, with credible support such as signed contracts, completed restructuring, or a change in business model already in effect.

If a cumulative loss exists, a valuation allowance against deferred tax assets that depend on future income (rather than on reversing temporary differences) is often required, even when management is optimistic.

### Apply Prudent Limitation To Tax-Planning Strategies

Tax-planning strategies can support realizability, but only those that are prudent, feasible, and within the entity's control count. A strategy the entity would not actually undertake, or that depends on third parties, is generally not given weight. Examples that may count include electing to accelerate taxable income, selling an asset to trigger a gain, or converting debt to equity, provided each is reasonable and actionable.

Do not invent strategies to justify recognition. If a strategy is not genuinely prudent and feasible, exclude it.

### Reassess Each Period And Document The Change

The valuation allowance is reassessed every reporting period. As evidence changes, the allowance changes, and the effect flows through the deferred tax provision, often as a discrete item or a component of the effective rate. Document what changed, why, and the resulting allowance. Period-over-period swings in the allowance without explanation are a red flag for auditors and indicate the assessment is not being performed rigorously.

### Document The Conclusion And Evidence

Because the assessment is judgmental, documentation is the deliverable. Retain the deferred tax asset schedule by component and jurisdiction, the sources of future taxable income considered, the positive and negative evidence weighed, the basis for any forecast, the prudent tax-planning strategies relied on, and the resulting allowance with rationale. Identify preparer and reviewer. A conclusion without supporting evidence cannot be defended.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Future Profits Will Absorb Everything

Defaulting to full recognition because management projects profitability ignores the weight given to a history of losses and the requirement for specific, credible evidence. Optimism is not evidence.

### Defaulting To A Full Allowance Without Weighing Evidence

Conversely, slapping a full allowance on every loss entity ignores reversing temporary differences, carrybacks, and tax-planning strategies that may support partial realization. The assessment must be granular, not all-or-nothing.

### Testing At The Wrong Level

Assessing realizability in aggregate across a group, rather than by tax-paying component or jurisdiction, overstates offset and may recognize assets that cannot be realized in their own jurisdiction.

### Relying On Vague Forecasts

"Future profitability" without signed contracts, backlog, completed restructuring, or other support is not a source of future taxable income. Forecasts must be specific and credible.

### Counting Non-Prudent Strategies

Treating hypothetical or third-party-dependent actions as tax-planning strategies inflates realizability. Only prudent, feasible, controllable strategies count.

### Ignoring Carryforward Expiration

A long horizon makes recovery plausible; a short horizon with ongoing losses makes a valuation allowance likely. Expiration timing must factor into the weight given to future income sources.

### Inconsistent Period-Over-Period Treatment

Large swings in the allowance without documented changes in evidence suggest the assessment is not being performed consistently. Reassess and document each period.

## Self-Check

- Is the more-likely-than-not realization test applied at the level of each tax-paying component or jurisdiction, not in aggregate?
- Are all available sources of future taxable income identified, including reversing temporary differences, future income, carrybacks, and prudent tax-planning strategies?
- Is positive and negative evidence assembled and weighed according to the framework's weighting rules?
- Is a cumulative recent loss history treated as strong negative evidence that projections alone cannot easily overcome?
- Are tax-planning strategies limited to those that are prudent, feasible, and within the entity's control?
- Does the assessment consider carryforward expiration horizons and any ownership-change or legal limits on utilization?
- Is the valuation allowance reassessed each period, with changes documented as to what changed and why?
- Is the conclusion supported by a retained schedule of deferred tax assets, evidence, forecasts, strategies, and rationale with preparer and reviewer?
- Is the applicable framework (ASC 740 more-likely-than-not, IAS 12 probable, or local code) confirmed and applied consistently?
- Is professional referral noted for uncertain or high-judgment positions, and is the explicit limitation on framework-specific evidentiary rules acknowledged?
