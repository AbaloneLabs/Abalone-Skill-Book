# Contract Assets And Unbilled Receivables

When an entity performs before it bills, or before its right to payment becomes unconditional, the amount it is owed is not yet a standard receivable. It is a contract asset, representing a right to consideration in exchange for goods or services already transferred, where that right is conditioned on something other than the passage of time. This distinction matters because contract assets and receivables carry different risks, different impairment models, and different presentation. An agent that lumps unbilled amounts into trade receivables hides the conditionality of the right and applies the wrong impairment logic; one that fails to recognize a contract asset understates the entity's earned but unbilled performance.

Use this skill before classifying amounts owed by customers, while accounting for unbilled revenue, while reconciling the gap between revenue and billings, while testing impairment, or while auditing contract assets and receivables. The goal is to make the agent separate conditional rights from unconditional rights and to prevent the balance sheet from misstating what the entity has actually earned and what it can collect.

Revenue recognition and financial instruments standards vary by jurisdiction and reporting framework. The dominant converged frameworks are US GAAP ASC 606 and IFRS 15 for contract assets, with impairment governed by the relevant financial instruments standards such as the expected credit loss model, but entities may also be subject to industry-specific guidance, local GAAP, tax-basis rules, or sector regulation. Do not state a single universal rule. Where impairment or classification is judgmental or material, route the question to a qualified accountant or auditor. This skill offers operational judgment, not a definitive accounting opinion.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Contract Assets From Receivables By Conditionality

The defining difference is whether the right to payment is unconditional. A receivable is a right to consideration that is unconditional, requiring only the passage of time before payment is due. A contract asset is a right to consideration that is conditional on the entity's future performance or on something other than the passage of time.

Classify as a contract asset when:

- the entity has performed but must perform additional obligations before the right to payment becomes due;
- the right to bill depends on contractually defined milestones not yet reached;
- the entity bills in arrears based on costs incurred or time elapsed;
- payment is conditional on customer acceptance of a combined deliverable.

Classify as a receivable when:

- the entity has billed and the only condition remaining is the passage of time until the due date;
- the right to payment is unconditional even if collection timing is deferred.

The conditionality test is the gate. A conditional right is a contract asset until the condition is met, at which point it reclassifies to a receivable.

### Account For Unbilled Revenue As Performance Precedes Billing

Unbilled revenue arises when the entity has satisfied a performance obligation and recognized revenue, but has not yet invoiced the customer. The offsetting asset is generally a contract asset until billing occurs, after which it becomes a receivable. The gap between revenue recognized and amounts billed is a normal feature of over-time and milestone-based contracts, not an error, but it must be tracked and reconciled.

Track:

- revenue recognized to date;
- amounts billed to date;
- the unbilled balance, which is the contract asset;
- the expected timing of billing for each significant contract asset.

Unbilled balances that grow without eventual billing signal estimation or control problems and warrant investigation.

### Reconcile Billing Timing To Revenue Timing

The relationship between revenue and billings should be explainable. In over-time contracts, revenue may run ahead of billings, creating a contract asset, or behind billings, creating deferred revenue. In fixed-price milestone contracts, billings may cluster at milestones while revenue accrues smoothly.

Reconcile:

- the contract asset balance to revenue recognized minus amounts billed;
- the deferred revenue balance to amounts billed minus revenue recognized;
- movements in both balances to the underlying contract activity;
- the expected conversion of contract assets to receivables and then to cash.

A contract asset that never converts to a receivable, or a receivable that never converts to cash, indicates a problem with collectibility, performance measurement, or billing discipline.

### Apply The Correct Impairment Model To Each Balance

Contract assets and receivables are subject to impairment, but the model and the recognition differ. Receivables are typically subject to an expected credit loss or loss allowance model. Contract assets are also subject to impairment, recognizing an allowance for expected credit losses, and the impairment is presented and disclosed consistent with the applicable financial instruments standard.

Assess impairment by considering:

- the customer's creditworthiness and payment history;
- the age of the receivable or contract asset;
- whether the right to payment is enforceable and unconditional;
- macroeconomic and industry conditions;
- collateral, guarantees, or other credit enhancements;
- the difference between contract assets, which may be conditional, and receivables, which are unconditional.

Recognize impairment losses in the period the expected credit loss arises. Do not treat a contract asset as automatically collectible simply because the entity has performed; the customer may still be unable to pay.

### Present Contract Assets And Receivables Separately And Clearly

Presentation affects how users interpret liquidity. Contract assets, receivables, and contract liabilities should be presented separately, or clearly described if presented together, so users can distinguish conditional rights, unconditional rights, and obligations.

Present:

- contract assets separately from receivables, or with clear disclosure if combined;
- contract liabilities, deferred revenue, separately from assets;
- current and noncurrent portions based on expected realization timing;
- any significant concentrations or related-party amounts.

Lumping all customer balances into a single line obscures conditionality, impairment risk, and the timing of expected cash.

### Age And Monitor Contract Assets Like Receivables

Contract assets should be aged and monitored for collectibility just as receivables are. A contract asset that sits unbilled and uncollected for an extended period signals a billing problem, a dispute, or collectibility risk.

Monitor:

- the age of contract assets from the date of performance;
- the expected conversion to receivables and the actual conversion;
- stale contract assets that have not billed;
- disputes or customer claims that block billing;
- whether the conditions for billing are being met on schedule.

Stale contract assets should be investigated and, where appropriate, written down or disclosed as a risk.

### Document The Basis For Each Significant Contract Asset

Contract assets arise from specific contract terms and performance. Each significant balance should be traceable to the contract, the performance recognized, and the billing terms that create the conditionality.

Document:

- the contract and the performance obligation generating the asset;
- the revenue recognized to date and the amount billed;
- the condition that must be met for the right to become unconditional;
- the expected timing of billing and collection;
- the impairment assessment and its basis.

A contract asset without traceable support cannot be defended in audit or review.

## Common Traps

### Lumping Unbilled Amounts Into Trade Receivables

Unbilled amounts are generally contract assets, not receivables, because the right to payment is conditional. Combining them hides conditionality and applies the wrong impairment and presentation logic.

### Treating A Conditional Right As Automatically Collectible

A contract asset is conditional, and the customer may still fail to pay. Treating it as fully collectible without an impairment assessment ignores credit risk on earned but unbilled amounts.

### Failing To Reconcile The Revenue To Billing Gap

The gap between revenue and billings should be explainable and should convert to receivables and cash. A contract asset that never converts signals a billing, dispute, or collectibility problem.

### Ignoring Stale Contract Assets

Contract assets that sit unbilled for long periods indicate billing delays, disputes, or performance measurement errors. Leaving them on the books without investigation overstates assets.

### Confusing Contract Assets With Contract Liabilities

A contract asset is a right to consideration for performance already given. A contract liability is an obligation to perform for consideration already received. Confusing the two reverses the balance sheet presentation.

### Applying The Wrong Impairment Model

Receivables and contract assets may be subject to different impairment recognition or presentation. Applying a single model indiscriminately misstates the allowance and the loss.

### Presenting All Customer Balances In One Line

Combining contract assets, receivables, and contract liabilities into one line obscures conditionality, timing, and risk. Users cannot assess liquidity from an aggregated balance.

## Self-Check

- Is each amount owed classified as a contract asset or a receivable based on whether the right to payment is conditional or unconditional?
- Is unbilled revenue recorded as a contract asset where the right to bill depends on future performance or milestones?
- Does the contract asset balance reconcile to revenue recognized minus amounts billed, with movements tied to contract activity?
- Is the correct impairment model applied to both contract assets and receivables, recognizing expected credit losses?
- Are contract assets, receivables, and contract liabilities presented separately or clearly disclosed, with current and noncurrent splits?
- Are contract assets aged and monitored for conversion to receivables and cash, with stale balances investigated?
- Is each significant contract asset traceable to the contract, performance recognized, billing conditions, and expected timing?
- Are disputes, customer claims, or billing delays identified and addressed rather than left as stale balances?
- Does the analysis identify the applicable reporting framework, including the relevant financial instruments impairment standard, and recommend qualified professional review where classification or impairment is judgmental?
