# Contract Liability Recognition

A contract liability arises when an entity receives consideration from a customer before transferring the related goods or services. Recognizing it correctly requires applying the five-step revenue model, identifying performance obligations, and determining when those obligations are satisfied. Errors here distort both revenue and the balance sheet, and because revenue is a high-risk area, contract liability errors attract audit and regulatory scrutiny.

This skill covers the decisions an accountant must make before and during contract liability recognition. It is not a substitute for professional judgment, jurisdiction-specific standards, or specialist legal advice on contract terms. Confirm the applicable framework (US GAAP ASC 606, IFRS 15) and consult a qualified professional for complex or bundled arrangements.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Contract And Its Commercial Substance

Revenue recognition begins with identifying a contract with a customer. A contract exists when the parties have approved the commitment, rights are identifiable, payment terms are identifiable, the contract has commercial substance, and collection is probable.

- Confirm that the contract creates enforceable rights and obligations. Assess whether the customer intends to perform and whether collection is probable.
- If a contract does not meet the criteria, defer recognition of received consideration until the criteria are met or the commitment is extinguished.
- Combine contracts that are entered into at or near the same time with the same customer if they are negotiated as a package, or if the amount of consideration depends on the price of the other contract.

### Identify Distinct Performance Obligations

A contract liability exists because the entity has an obligation to transfer goods or services. Identify each performance obligation.

- A good or service is distinct if the customer can benefit from it on its own or with readily available resources, and the promise to transfer it is separately identifiable from other promises in the contract.
- Do not split a promise into separate obligations if the goods or services are highly interrelated or significantly integrated.
- Identify promised goods or services, including grants of licenses, rights to receive services, options to acquire additional goods or services at a discount that provide a material right, and non-cash consideration.
- A series of distinct goods or services that are substantially the same and transferred in the same pattern is treated as a single performance obligation.

### Determine The Transaction Price

The transaction price is the amount of consideration the entity expects to be entitled to in exchange for transferring the promised goods or services.

- Include fixed amounts, variable consideration estimated using the expected value or most likely amount method, and the effects of a significant financing component if material.
- Constrain variable consideration to the amount that is highly probable of not resulting in a significant reversal when the uncertainty is resolved.
- Exclude amounts collected on behalf of third parties, such as sales taxes.
- Assess whether the transaction price includes non-cash consideration, and measure it at fair value.

### Allocate The Transaction Price To Performance Obligations

If a contract has multiple performance obligations, allocate the transaction price to each based on relative standalone selling prices.

- The standalone selling price is the price at which the entity would sell the good or service separately. If not directly observable, estimate it using an adjusted market assessment, expected cost plus margin, or residual approach (only in limited circumstances).
- Allocate discounts proportionally across all performance obligations unless evidence supports allocating to specific obligations.
- Allocate variable consideration to specific obligations if it relates to them, or across all obligations if not.

### Recognize The Contract Liability When Consideration Is Received Before Performance

A contract liability is recognized when the entity receives consideration, or when the entity has an unconditional right to consideration, before transferring the related goods or services.

- Defer the consideration as a contract liability on the balance sheet.
- Do not recognize revenue until the related performance obligation is satisfied by transferring the promised good or service.
- The contract liability is reduced, and revenue is recognized, as the entity satisfies its performance obligations.
- A contract liability is presented as a current or non-current liability based on when the entity expects to satisfy the obligation.

### Distinguish Contract Liabilities From Contract Assets And Receivables

Contract liabilities, contract assets, and receivables represent different stages of the revenue cycle.

- A receivable is the entity's right to consideration that is unconditional, before any performance is required.
- A contract asset is the entity's right to consideration in exchange for goods or services already transferred, when that right is conditioned on something other than the passage of time.
- A contract liability is the entity's obligation to transfer goods or services for which it has already received consideration or has an unconditional right to consideration.
- Present contract assets and contract liabilities separately on the balance sheet. Do not net them unless the entity has a legally enforceable right to offset.

### Recognize Revenue As Performance Obligations Are Satisfied

Revenue is recognized when or as the entity satisfies a performance obligation by transferring control of the promised good or service.

- For performance obligations satisfied at a point in time, recognize revenue when control transfers, considering indicators such as present right to payment, customer acceptance, physical possession, legal title, and risks and rewards.
- For performance obligations satisfied over time, recognize revenue using an appropriate method to measure progress, either output methods (units delivered, milestones) or input methods (costs incurred, labor hours).
- Reduce the contract liability as revenue is recognized.

### Handle Significant Financing Components

If the timing of payments provides a significant benefit to the customer or the entity, the contract includes a significant financing component.

- Adjust the transaction price to reflect the effects of the financing, recognizing interest expense or interest income separately from revenue.
- A contract liability that will be satisfied more than one year after payment is received may include a significant financing component.
- Practical expedients exist to not separately assess for a significant financing component if the period between performance and payment is less than one year.

## Common Traps

### Failing To Identify Distinct Performance Obligations

Bundling distinct goods or services into a single obligation, or splitting highly integrated services into separate obligations, distorts the timing of revenue recognition and the contract liability balance.

### Recognizing Revenue Before Control Transfers

For point-in-time obligations, revenue must not be recognized before control transfers. Recognizing revenue on billing or production milestones without assessing control overstates revenue and understates the contract liability.

### Not Constraining Variable Consideration

Including the full estimated variable consideration without constraining it to the highly probable amount can lead to significant revenue reversal. Apply the constraint rigorously.

### Allocating Discounts Without Evidence

Allocating discounts proportionally without considering evidence that they apply to specific obligations can misallocate the transaction price. Assess whether discounts relate to specific performance obligations.

### Netting Contract Assets And Liabilities Without A Legal Right

Contract assets and liabilities must be presented separately unless a legally enforceable right to offset exists. Netting them hides the gross positions and the entity's obligations.

### Ignoring The Significant Financing Component

For contracts with long gaps between payment and performance, failing to adjust for the financing component misstates both revenue and interest. Assess whether the gap exceeds one year and whether the financing is significant.

### Treating A Contract Liability As A Financial Liability

A contract liability is a revenue obligation, not a debt obligation. Do not classify it as borrowings or include it in debt covenants unless the terms require it.

### Inconsistent Application Of Progress Measurement

For over-time obligations, the method of measuring progress must be applied consistently and reflect the transfer of control. Switching methods or using a method that does not reflect control distorts period revenue.

## Self-Check

- Has the contract been identified and assessed for commercial substance and probable collection?
- Have distinct performance obligations been identified, with goods or services that the customer can benefit from separately and that are separately identifiable?
- Is the transaction price determined including fixed and constrained variable consideration, and excluding amounts collected on behalf of third parties?
- Is the transaction price allocated to performance obligations based on relative standalone selling prices, with discounts allocated based on evidence?
- Is a contract liability recognized when consideration is received or is receivable before the related performance obligation is satisfied?
- Are contract liabilities distinguished from contract assets and receivables, and presented separately without netting unless a legal right to offset exists?
- Is revenue recognized as performance obligations are satisfied, at a point in time or over time using an appropriate progress method?
- For contracts with a significant gap between payment and performance, is the significant financing component assessed and adjusted?
- Are contract liabilities classified as current or non-current based on when the entity expects to satisfy the obligation?
- Has the applicable jurisdiction and reporting framework (US GAAP ASC 606 or IFRS 15) been confirmed, and has specialist advice been obtained for bundled or complex arrangements?
