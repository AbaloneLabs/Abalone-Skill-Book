# Loss Contract Recognition

A long-term contract that was expected to be profitable can turn into a loss contract when costs overrun, scope changes, prices fall, or conditions shift. When that happens, the entire expected loss must be recognized immediately in the period it is discovered, not spread across the remaining life of the contract. This rule is the mirror image of profit recognition. Profit on a long-term contract is recognized over time as performance advances, but a loss is recognized all at once because the entity has a present obligation whose cost exceeds its expected benefit. Delaying or smoothing a known loss misstates the current period and hides a deterioration that users are entitled to see.

The trap is that recognizing a loss feels like admitting failure, and there is strong pressure to believe the next period will recover it. But conservatism in this area is not optional. An agent that defers a loss, hoping for a turnaround, or that recognizes it only gradually, produces financial statements that overstate assets and earnings. The judgment is not whether to recognize the loss but how to measure it correctly and how to separate the loss recognition from the normal profit pattern.

Use this skill before identifying whether a long-term contract is onerous or loss-making, before measuring the expected unrecoverable cost, before recognizing a loss, or before reassessing a contract that has shown signs of deterioration. The goal is to make the agent detect loss contracts promptly, measure the full expected loss, recognize it in the correct period, and keep it distinct from ordinary estimate revisions.

Long-term contract and onerous contract accounting varies by jurisdiction and reporting framework. The dominant converged frameworks are US GAAP ASC 606 and IFRS 15 for revenue, with IFRS 17 and IAS 37 addressing onerous contracts in some contexts, while older construction contract standards such as ASC 605 or IAS 11 legacies may still apply in some jurisdictions. Entities may also be subject to local GAAP, tax-basis rules, or sector regulation. The trigger for loss recognition and the measurement of the loss differ across frameworks. Do not assume a single universal rule. Where the contract is complex, the loss is material, or the cost estimates are highly uncertain, route the determination to a qualified accountant or auditor. This skill offers operational judgment, not a definitive accounting opinion.

## Core Rules

### Test Every Long-Term Contract For Loss At Each Reporting Date

Loss recognition is not a one-time event. At each reporting date, compare the total expected cost of the contract, including costs already incurred and the estimated costs to complete, against the total expected transaction price. If cost exceeds price, the contract is a loss contract and the full excess must be recognized now.

Run the test by:

- summing costs incurred to date and estimated costs to complete;
- comparing the total expected cost against the total expected transaction price;
- identifying any contract where expected cost exceeds expected price;
- recognizing the entire expected loss in the current period.

A contract can move into and out of loss status as estimates change. Test consistently each period so deterioration is caught promptly.

### Recognize The Entire Expected Loss In The Period Discovered

Unlike profit, which accrues over time, a loss is recognized immediately and in full. The rationale is that the entity has a present obligation whose cost exceeds its benefit, and delaying recognition would overstate assets and earnings.

Recognize:

- the full expected loss for the contract, not just the current period's share;
- the loss even if the contract is early in its life;
- the loss even if the entity expects to recover on other contracts.

The loss is a charge against current period earnings, with a corresponding liability or reduction in the contract asset, depending on the framework and the contract structure.

### Measure The Loss From The Total Contract Perspective

The loss is measured by comparing the total expected cost of the entire contract against the total expected transaction price, not by looking at one period in isolation. A loss contract is one where the whole engagement is expected to lose money.

Include in the measurement:

- all costs incurred to date;
- all estimated costs to complete the remaining scope;
- any direct attributable costs of fulfilling the contract;
- contingency for known risks;
- the full transaction price, including variable consideration to the extent it is not constrained.

Do not measure the loss against only the remaining work, because the cumulative position is what determines whether the contract is onerous.

### Separate The Loss From The Normal Profit Catch-Up

When a loss is recognized, it interacts with the percentage of completion profit that would otherwise have been recognized. The loss recognition effectively adjusts the cumulative position to zero profit and then to a loss. This must be kept distinct from ordinary cumulative catch-ups that arise from routine estimate revisions.

Distinguish by asking:

- is the adjustment driven by a routine revision of cost or price estimates within a still-profitable contract, or by the contract becoming loss-making overall?
- does the adjustment reduce cumulative profit to zero and below, indicating a loss contract?
- is the adjustment a current period charge for the full expected loss, or a spreading of the loss over remaining periods?

Routine catch-ups on profitable contracts are changes in estimate. Loss recognition is a distinct event that charges the entire expected loss to the current period.

### Reassess The Cost And Price Estimates That Trigger The Loss

A loss determination depends entirely on the reliability of the cost and price estimates. Before recognizing a loss, and after recognizing one, scrutinize the estimates that produced it.

Examine:

- whether the cost overrun is confirmed or speculative;
- whether the scope change is agreed or disputed;
- whether price increases, claims, or change orders could offset the cost increase;
- whether productivity assumptions are realistic;
- whether contingency is adequate but not excessive.

If the loss rests on uncertain estimates, document the sensitivity. If offsetting price increases are probable and not constrained, they reduce the loss. But do not let optimism defer recognition of a loss that the current best estimate supports.

### Handle Reversals And Improvements Carefully

If conditions improve after a loss is recognized, the loss may be partially or fully reversed. But reversals are not automatic and should not be recognized before the improvement is sufficiently certain.

Handle reversals by:

- reassessing the total expected cost and price at each reporting date;
- recognizing a reversal only when the improvement is supported by current information;
- limiting the reversal to the amount of the previously recognized loss;
- not recognizing additional profit beyond recovering the loss until performance supports it.

Recognizing a reversal too eagerly recreates the original problem in reverse, overstating earnings in the improvement period.

### Document The Loss Determination And Its Basis

Because loss recognition is a high-impact judgment, the basis must be documented so a reviewer can see why the loss was recognized and how it was measured.

Document:

- the total expected cost and the total expected price that produced the loss;
- the specific factors driving the cost increase or price decrease;
- any offsetting price increases, claims, or change orders considered;
- the amount of the loss recognized and the period;
- the treatment of any subsequent reversal;
- who prepared and who reviewed the determination.

Documentation is what distinguishes a defensible loss recognition from a number that cannot be supported under audit.

## Common Traps

### Deferring The Loss In Hope Of Recovery

The most common trap is believing the next period will turn the contract around, so the loss is delayed. But the rule requires recognizing the full expected loss based on the current best estimate. Hoping for recovery is not a basis for deferral.

### Recognizing The Loss Gradually Instead Of All At Once

Spreading the expected loss over the remaining contract life smooths earnings but violates the immediate recognition principle. The entire expected loss belongs in the period it is discovered.

### Measuring The Loss Against Only The Remaining Work

Looking only at the costs and revenues still to come ignores the cumulative position. A contract can be loss-making overall even if the remaining work appears profitable, because sunk costs already incurred are part of the total contract cost.

### Ignoring Offset Price Increases Or Claims That Are Probable

If price increases, claims, or change orders are probable and not subject to constraint, they reduce the loss. Ignoring them overstates the loss. But the converse trap, counting speculative offsets to avoid recognizing a loss, is equally wrong.

### Treating The Loss As An Ordinary Estimate Revision

A loss recognition is a distinct event from a routine cumulative catch-up. Treating it as just another estimate revision obscures the deterioration and may apply the wrong recognition pattern.

### Reversing The Loss Too Eagerly

When conditions improve, there is pressure to reverse the loss quickly. But a reversal should be recognized only when the improvement is sufficiently certain, and only up to the amount of the prior loss. Premature reversal overstates the improvement period's earnings.

### Letting Optimistic Productivity Assumptions Hide The Loss

If the cost estimate assumes unrealistically high productivity or low materials cost, the contract may appear profitable when it is actually loss-making. The loss test is only as good as the underlying cost estimate.

### Confusing An Onerous Contract Provision With A Revenue Adjustment

In some frameworks, a loss contract creates a separate onerous contract provision or liability rather than a direct reduction of the contract asset. Applying the wrong mechanism misstates the balance sheet. Confirm which treatment the governing framework requires.

## Self-Check

- Has every long-term contract been tested for loss status at the current reporting date by comparing total expected cost against total expected price?
- Is the entire expected loss recognized in the period it is discovered, rather than spread over the remaining contract life?
- Is the loss measured against the total contract, including costs incurred to date and estimated costs to complete, rather than only the remaining work?
- Is the loss recognition kept distinct from ordinary cumulative catch-ups on profitable contracts?
- Are the cost and price estimates driving the loss scrutinized for reliability, with offsetting probable price increases, claims, and change orders considered?
- Are any subsequent improvements recognized as reversals only when sufficiently certain and only up to the prior loss amount?
- Are optimistic productivity or cost assumptions challenged rather than accepted as a basis for avoiding loss recognition?
- Is the correct mechanism applied, whether a direct contract adjustment or a separate onerous contract provision, based on the governing framework?
- Is the loss determination, its basis, the amount, the period, and the preparer and reviewer documented well enough to defend?
- Does the analysis confirm which framework applies and recommend qualified professional review where the contract is complex, the loss is material, or the cost estimates are highly uncertain?
