# Completed Contract Versus Over Time

For a long-term contract, the single most consequential accounting decision is when to recognize revenue: over time as performance advances, or at a point in time, historically called the completed-contract method when applied to a long-duration job. That choice changes the entire revenue and profit pattern. Recognize over time and profit accrues steadily across the contract life; recognize at completion and all profit lands in the final period, with nothing before. The decision is not a preference or a smoothing tool. It follows from whether the customer receives and controls the benefit of the work as it is performed, or only when the job is finished and delivered.

The trap is that completed-contract accounting can look attractive because it defers revenue and profit, lowering current tax and current earnings volatility. But applying it where the economics actually support over-time recognition misrepresents performance, and applying over-time recognition where the customer does not control the work in progress front-loads profit that may never be earned. The agent must test the substance of the arrangement, not choose the method that produces a convenient result.

Use this skill before deciding whether a long-term contract is recognized over time or at completion, before testing the control transfer criteria, before assessing the right to payment, or before reviewing whether an entity has correctly classified a contract. The goal is to make the agent apply the recognition criteria in order, examine the evidence of control transfer, and avoid defaulting to a method based on its accounting effect rather than the economics.

Long-term contract accounting varies by jurisdiction and reporting framework. The dominant converged frameworks are US GAAP ASC 606 and IFRS 15, which generally require over-time recognition only when specific criteria are met and otherwise default to point-in-time recognition. Older standards such as ASC 605 and IAS 11 permitted explicit completed-contract or percentage-of-completion method elections in some cases, and those legacies may persist in some jurisdictions. Entities may also be subject to local GAAP, tax-basis rules, or sector regulation, and tax recognition often differs from book recognition. Do not assume a single universal rule. Where the contract is complex, the control analysis is unclear, or the amounts are material, route the determination to a qualified accountant or auditor. This skill offers operational judgment, not a definitive accounting opinion.

## Core Rules

### Test The Over-Time Criteria In Order Before Defaulting To Point In Time

Over-time recognition is not automatic for long-duration work. The entity must meet at least one of the criteria that indicate the customer receives and controls the benefit as the work is performed. If none are met, recognition is at a point in time.

Apply the criteria in sequence:

1. the customer simultaneously receives and consumes the benefit as the entity performs, common for routine and recurring services;
2. the entity's performance creates or enhances an asset that the customer controls as it is created;
3. the entity's performance does not create an asset with an alternative use to the entity, and the entity has an enforceable right to payment for performance completed to date.

If any one criterion is met, recognize over time. Only if none are met does point-in-time, or completed-contract, recognition apply. Testing in order prevents skipping the analysis and defaulting to the method with the preferred accounting outcome.

### Assess Whether The Customer Controls The Asset As It Is Created

The second criterion turns on whether the customer controls the work in progress. Control means the customer has the ability to direct the use of, and obtain substantially all the remaining benefits from, the asset as it is being created.

Indicators of customer control include:

- the customer owns or has legal title to the work in progress;
- the customer directs the design and specification of the asset;
- the asset is physically located on the customer's site or under the customer's control;
- the customer can sell or direct the sale of the in-process asset;
- the customer bears the risks and rewards of ownership during construction.

If the customer controls the asset throughout, over-time recognition reflects the transfer of control. If the entity retains control until delivery, point-in-time recognition is appropriate.

### Apply The No Alternative Use And Enforceable Right Test Rigorously

The third criterion has two parts that must both hold. First, the entity must not have an alternative use for the asset if the other party defaults, meaning the entity cannot readily redirect the asset to another customer. Second, the entity must have an enforceable right to payment for performance completed to date, including a reasonable profit margin.

Assess alternative use by asking:

- could the entity sell the partially completed asset to another customer without significant rework?
- is the asset customized or specialized to this customer?
- would the entity incur significant loss or cost to redirect the asset?

Assess the right to payment by asking:

- does the contract entitle the entity to payment for work performed to date?
- does that payment include compensation for a reasonable profit, not merely reimbursement of costs?
- is the right enforceable in the relevant jurisdiction, including through termination for convenience clauses?

Both conditions must be satisfied. A customized asset with no enforceable right to payment does not qualify, nor does an enforceable right on an asset the entity could redirect elsewhere.

### Recognize That Point In Time Does Not Always Mean The Very End

Point-in-time recognition means revenue is recognized when control transfers, which for some contracts is at delivery or acceptance, but the relevant point is when control passes, not necessarily the contract end date. For a long-duration contract that fails the over-time criteria, control typically transfers at a single milestone such as final delivery, acceptance, or commissioning.

Identify the point of transfer by:

- when legal title passes;
- when physical possession transfers;
- when the customer accepts the asset;
- when the customer has the ability to direct use and obtain benefit;
- when significant risks and rewards of ownership transfer.

Do not assume point in time equals the last invoice date. The transfer point follows the substance of control.

### Separate Book Recognition From Tax Recognition

Tax rules frequently permit or require methods that differ from book recognition. A contract recognized over time for book purposes may be treated under completed-contract or percentage-of-completion for tax, or vice versa, and tax methods may have their own elections and limitations.

Track separately:

- the book recognition pattern under the applicable financial reporting framework;
- the tax recognition pattern under the relevant tax law;
- the resulting book-tax differences and deferred tax effects;
- any elections or methods specific to tax.

Conflating book and tax recognition produces errors in both the financial statements and the tax return.

### Do Not Let The Accounting Outcome Drive The Classification

The criteria determine the method, not the other way around. Choosing completed-contract to defer profit and tax, or over-time to smooth earnings, reverses the logic. The classification must reflect whether the customer controls the benefit as performance occurs.

Resist pressures such as:

- selecting completed-contract to manage earnings or defer tax;
- selecting over-time to accelerate revenue and meet targets;
- applying a legacy method election without confirming it is still permitted;
- classifying based on industry convention rather than the specific contract terms.

The right classification follows the economics; the accounting effect is a consequence.

### Document The Criteria Analysis For Each Significant Contract

Because the classification decision drives the entire revenue pattern, the basis must be documented so a reviewer can see which criterion was met and why.

Document:

- which over-time criterion was tested and whether it was met;
- the evidence supporting customer control or no alternative use;
- the enforceability and terms of the right to payment;
- the point of transfer if point-in-time recognition applies;
- the book versus tax treatment and any differences;
- who prepared and who reviewed the classification.

Documentation lets an auditor verify that the method reflects the contract substance rather than a desired outcome.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Completed-Contract To Defer Profit And Tax

Completed-contract accounting defers revenue and profit to the end, which can lower current earnings and current tax. But applying it where an over-time criterion is met misrepresents performance and may not comply with the governing framework.

### Assuming All Long-Duration Contracts Are Over Time

Long duration does not by itself trigger over-time recognition. A contract to build a standard product that the entity could sell elsewhere, with payment only on delivery, may be point-in-time despite lasting many months. Duration is not the test; control transfer is.

### Applying A Legacy Method Election Without Confirming Current Guidance

Older standards permitted explicit completed-contract or percentage-of-completion elections in some cases. Those elections may have been superseded. Applying a legacy method without confirming it is still permitted produces noncompliant accounting.

### Treating A Termination For Convenience Clause As Satisfying The Right To Payment

A termination for convenience clause must provide a right to payment for performance to date, including a reasonable profit, to support over-time recognition. A clause that merely reimburses costs, or that is not genuinely enforceable, does not satisfy the criterion.

### Ignoring Whether The Entity Has An Alternative Use For The Asset

If the entity could redirect the partially completed asset to another customer without significant rework, the no-alternative-use condition fails. Assuming customization without examining whether the asset is truly specialized undermines the over-time conclusion.

### Conflating Book And Tax Recognition

Tax methods often differ from book methods. Recording tax-driven recognition in the financial statements, or vice versa, produces errors in both. Keep the two frameworks separate and reconcile the differences.

### Picking The Method Based On Industry Convention

Industry practice is not the standard. Two contracts in the same industry can have different recognition patterns depending on their terms. Classifying by convention rather than by the specific contract ignores the control analysis.

### Recognizing Revenue At The Last Invoice Date By Default

Point-in-time recognition occurs when control transfers, which may not coincide with the final invoice. Defaulting to the last billing date can misstate the period of recognition even when point-in-time treatment is otherwise correct.

## Self-Check

- Has each long-term contract been tested against the over-time criteria in order before defaulting to point-in-time recognition?
- If relying on the second criterion, is there evidence that the customer controls the asset as it is created, through title, direction, site control, or risk and reward?
- If relying on the third criterion, are both the no-alternative-use condition and the enforceable right to payment, including a reasonable profit, satisfied?
- Where point-in-time recognition applies, is the transfer point identified based on when control passes rather than assumed to be the contract end or last invoice date?
- Are book recognition and tax recognition tracked separately, with book-tax differences and deferred tax effects reconciled?
- Is the classification driven by the control analysis rather than by the desired accounting outcome, such as deferring profit or accelerating revenue?
- Has any legacy method election been confirmed as still permitted under the current governing framework?
- Are termination for convenience clauses examined for whether they provide a genuine right to payment for performance to date?
- Is the criteria analysis, the evidence, the transfer point, and the book versus tax treatment documented for each significant contract?
- Does the analysis confirm which framework applies and recommend qualified professional review where the control analysis is unclear, the contract is complex, or amounts are material?
