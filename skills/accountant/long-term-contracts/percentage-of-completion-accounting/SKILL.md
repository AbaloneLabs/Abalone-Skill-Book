# Percentage Of Completion Accounting

Long-term contracts, such as construction, engineering, software development, defense, and large service engagements, often span multiple reporting periods. Recognizing all the revenue and profit only at the end would misrepresent performance, because the entity is earning the consideration as work progresses. Percentage of completion accounting spreads revenue, cost, and profit across the life of the contract according to a measure of progress. The difficulty is that the measure of progress is a judgment, the inputs are estimates of an uncertain future, and small errors in the percentage compound into large swings in recognized profit.

The trap is treating the percentage as a mechanical input. In reality the choice of measure, the reliability of the cost estimates, and the handling of changes all determine whether the recognized profit reflects actual performance or a smooth-looking number that later reverses. An agent that applies a convenient measure without examining whether it depicts the transfer of control produces revenue that does not match the economics.

Use this skill before applying percentage of completion, before selecting a measure of progress, before measuring costs incurred and costs to complete, or before updating the estimate as a contract advances. The goal is to make the agent choose a measure that reflects genuine progress, base estimates on reliable inputs, and handle revisions so that recognized profit always reflects the best current expectation.

Long-term contract accounting varies by jurisdiction and reporting framework. The dominant converged frameworks are US GAAP ASC 606 and IFRS 15, which recognize revenue over time when specific criteria are met, but entities may also be subject to older construction contract standards such as ASC 605 or IAS 11 legacies in some jurisdictions, local GAAP, tax-basis rules, or sector regulation. The criteria for over-time recognition and the acceptable measures of progress differ across frameworks. Do not assume a single universal rule. Where the contract is complex, the estimates are uncertain, or the amounts are material, route the determination to a qualified accountant or auditor. This skill offers operational judgment, not a definitive accounting opinion.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Contract Qualifies For Over-Time Recognition First

Percentage of completion applies only when revenue is recognized over time. Before measuring progress, confirm the contract meets the criteria for over-time recognition, which generally require that the customer controls the asset as it is created, the entity creates or enhances an asset the customer controls, or the entity has no alternative use and an enforceable right to payment for performance to date.

Ask:

- does the customer control the work in progress?
- would the entity have an asset with alternative use if the contract ended?
- does the entity have an enforceable right to payment for work completed to date?
- is performance measured by progress toward completion rather than delivery of discrete items?

If none of these hold, the contract is likely a point-in-time arrangement, and percentage of completion does not apply. Confirming eligibility first prevents applying an over-time measure to a contract that should be recognized at a point in time.

### Choose A Measure Of Progress That Reflects The Transfer Of Control

The measure of progress should depict the entity's performance, meaning the transfer of control to the customer. There are input measures and output measures, and the choice should follow which better represents progress for the specific contract.

Input measures include:

- costs incurred relative to total expected costs, the cost-to-cost method;
- labor hours worked relative to total expected hours;
- machine hours or other resource inputs consumed.

Output measures include:

- units delivered or milestones achieved;
- surveys of performance performed;
- physical completion of a contract asset;
- appraisals of results achieved.

Prefer the measure that most faithfully depicts the transfer of control. A cost-to-cost measure works when costs correlate with progress, but it fails when early costs are front-loaded or when one expensive input does not represent proportional value delivered. An output measure works when milestones reflect genuine completion, but it fails when milestones are administrative rather than substantive.

### Exclude Costs That Do Not Depict Progress From The Measure

Not every cost incurred represents progress. Including distorting costs in an input measure corrupts the percentage.

Exclude or adjust for:

- costs of materials that do not transfer control, such as uninstalled materials staged on site;
- costs of inefficiencies or rework that do not advance the contract;
- costs incurred in advance that relate to future performance;
- uninstalled materials that the customer has not yet controlled.

Uninstalled materials can be a particular trap. Including their cost in the numerator inflates the percentage beyond the work actually performed. Some frameworks permit treating uninstalled materials as if transferred when the customer controls them, but this requires a deliberate judgment, not a default.

### Base The Total Cost Estimate On Reliable, Current Information

The denominator of a cost-to-cost measure is total expected costs, and that estimate drives the entire profit pattern. A weak estimate produces a smooth percentage that hides a wrong profit margin.

Build the total cost estimate from:

- costs incurred to date, properly classified;
- committed costs for work under contract or purchase order;
- estimated costs to complete remaining scope;
- contingency for known risks and uncertainties;
- assumptions about labor rates, productivity, materials, and duration.

Reassess the estimate at each reporting date. As actual costs and conditions emerge, the total expected cost changes, and the percentage and margin update accordingly.

### Recognize Revenue And Profit According To The Measure

Once the percentage is determined, recognize revenue equal to that percentage of the transaction price, and recognize cost equal to that percentage of total expected costs, with the difference as profit. The recognized amounts are cumulative, so each period reflects the total that should have been recognized to date.

Handle the mechanics by:

- computing the cumulative revenue and cost to date using the current percentage;
- subtracting amounts already recognized in prior periods;
- recording the current period difference as the period's revenue, cost, and profit.

Do not recognize revenue in excess of amounts billable where a cap applies, and do not front-load profit based on early billings rather than actual progress.

### Update Estimates Cumulatively As The Contract Advances

Long-term contracts almost always require estimate revisions. Costs change, scope shifts, productivity varies. Each revision is a change in estimate handled prospectively through a cumulative catch-up.

Apply updates by:

- recalculating total expected costs with current information;
- recomputing the percentage complete and the cumulative revenue and profit;
- recognizing the difference between the new cumulative amount and what was already recognized in the current period.

A cumulative catch-up is normal, not an error correction. But a pattern of large swings signals that the original estimates were unreliable and the estimation process needs review.

### Billings And Cash Do Not Drive Revenue Recognition

The amount billed or collected is a financing and contract administration matter, not the measure of performance. Billings may lead or lag progress. Do not let billing schedules, contractually set milestones that do not reflect performance, or cash receipts drive the revenue number.

Track contract assets and contract liabilities separately:

- a contract asset when revenue is recognized ahead of billing;
- a contract liability, or unearned revenue, when billing is ahead of revenue.

These balances reflect timing differences between performance and billing, not profit.

## Common Traps

### Using Cost Incurred As A Proxy For Progress When Costs Are Front-Loaded

If significant costs occur early, such as mobilization, design, or expensive materials, a cost-to-cost measure overstates early progress. The percentage races ahead of the actual transfer of control, front-loading revenue and profit.

### Including Uninstalled Materials In The Cost Numerator

Materials purchased and staged but not yet installed or controlled by the customer inflate the percentage. The work appears further along than it is, and profit is recognized prematurely.

### Failing To Reassess Total Expected Costs

Carrying the original budget as the denominator when actual conditions have changed produces a stale percentage. As costs overrun or underrun, the margin and revenue drift away from reality until a large catch-up corrects them, sometimes dramatically.

### Treating Administrative Milestones As Genuine Progress

Output measures based on contractually defined milestones can mislead if the milestones are billing or administrative events rather than markers of real completion. Hitting a milestone does not always mean proportional performance has occurred.

### Letting Billings Drive Revenue

Recognizing revenue equal to amounts billed, or to cash received, ties revenue to the contract's payment schedule rather than to performance. This distorts the revenue pattern and can defer or accelerate profit without economic basis.

### Recognizing Profit On A Contract With No Enforceable Right To Payment

If the entity lacks an enforceable right to payment for work to date and has an alternative use for the asset, over-time recognition does not apply. Applying percentage of completion to such a contract recognizes profit that may never be recoverable.

### Ignoring Claims And Change Orders Until Fully Resolved

Change orders and claims can affect the transaction price and the total cost estimate. Deferring all recognition until they are fully agreed can misstate progress, though recognizing them before they are sufficiently certain creates reversal risk. Handle each based on how resolved it is.

### Confusing A Cumulative Catch-Up With An Error Correction

Routine estimate revisions are changes in estimate, recognized prospectively. Recording them as prior-period restatements misstates comparatives. Reserve restatement for genuine errors.

## Self-Check

- Has the contract been confirmed as qualifying for over-time recognition, with the customer controlling the work in progress or the entity having no alternative use and an enforceable right to payment?
- Is the measure of progress, input or output, chosen because it best depicts the transfer of control rather than because it is convenient?
- Are distorting costs, such as uninstalled materials, inefficiencies, and front-loaded costs, excluded from or adjusted in the input measure?
- Is the total expected cost estimate built from current, reliable information and reassessed at each reporting date?
- Are revenue, cost, and profit recognized on a cumulative basis, with prior period amounts subtracted to derive the current period figures?
- Are estimate revisions handled as cumulative catch-ups reflecting changes in estimate rather than as error corrections?
- Are billings and cash tracked separately from revenue, with contract assets and contract liabilities recorded for timing differences?
- Are change orders and claims assessed for their effect on price and cost based on how resolved they are, rather than ignored or fully recognized prematurely?
- Are large or frequent estimate swings investigated as a sign of an unreliable estimation process?
- Does the analysis confirm which framework applies and recommend qualified professional review where the contract is complex, estimates are uncertain, or amounts are material?
