# Intangible Asset Recognition And Measurement

Intangible assets lack physical substance but generate future economic benefits — brands, patents, software, customer lists, licenses, trademarks. Their recognition is governed by stricter rules than tangible assets because identifying the asset and measuring its cost is harder, and because the link between expenditure and future benefit is often speculative. The central judgment is whether an item meets the definition of an intangible asset and whether its cost can be reliably measured. Agents often default to expensing everything (conservative but potentially understating assets) or capitalizing too much (overstating assets with speculative benefits). The correct treatment depends on how the intangible was acquired and, for internally generated intangibles, whether the expenditure is research or development.

Use this skill before deciding whether to capitalize or expense an intangible-related expenditure, recognizing an acquired intangible in a business combination, classifying an intangible's useful life, or reviewing whether a capitalized intangible still meets recognition criteria. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying a blanket policy (capitalize all or expense all) without testing the recognition criteria, measurement reliability, and acquisition context.

Intangible asset rules differ between US GAAP (ASC 350, ASC 730, ASC 805) and IFRS (IAS 38, IFRS 3), particularly for development cost capitalization, revaluation, and the treatment of internally generated brands. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For business combinations and material intangible valuations, involve a qualified accountant or valuation specialist. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific asset.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Definition Of An Intangible Asset

An intangible asset is an identifiable, non-monetary asset without physical substance. To qualify, the item must be identifiable (separable from the entity or arising from contractual or legal rights), controlled by the entity (power to obtain benefits and restrict others' access), and expected to generate probable future economic benefits whose cost can be measured reliably. If any criterion fails, the expenditure is an expense.

Identifiability is the distinguishing test. Customer relationships tied to the entity as a whole, or a workforce, are generally not separable and are not recognized separately. A patent, license, or trademark arising from legal rights is identifiable. A brand built through advertising is generally not identifiable when internally generated.

### Treat The Acquisition Method As The Primary Driver Of Recognition

How the intangible was acquired determines the recognition and measurement rules. There are four acquisition contexts: separate acquisition, acquisition in a business combination, acquisition by way of a government grant, and internally generated intangibles. Each has different recognition and cost-measurement rules.

Separately acquired intangibles are generally recognized at cost (purchase price plus directly attributable costs of preparing the asset for use), because the price evidences probable future benefits and reliable cost measurement. Business combination intangibles are recognized at fair value at the acquisition date. Government grant intangibles may be recognized at fair value or at a nominal amount plus direct costs, depending on the framework. Internally generated intangibles are subject to the strictest rules.

### Apply The Research-And-Development Split For Internally Generated Intangibles

For internally generated intangibles, divide the expenditure into a research phase and a development phase. Research expenditure (investigating new knowledge, formulations, designs, alternatives) is recognized as an expense when incurred — it produces no identifiable asset with probable benefits. Development expenditure (applying research findings to produce a plan or design for new or improved materials, products, or processes) can be capitalized only when all of the following are demonstrated: technical feasibility, intention to complete, ability to use or sell, future economic benefits, availability of resources to complete, and ability to measure expenditure reliably.

Under IFRS (IAS 38), development costs meeting all six criteria are capitalized. Under US GAAP (ASC 730), most research and development costs are expensed, with limited exceptions (such as software developed for internal use or for sale after technological feasibility, under ASC 985-20 and ASC 350-40). Confirm which framework applies. The research-development boundary is a judgment; document the point at which development began.

### Do Not Recognize Internally Generated Brands, Mastheads, Publishing Titles, Or Customer Lists

Internally generated brands, mastheads, publishing titles, and customer lists are not recognized as intangible assets, because they cannot be distinguished from the cost of developing the business as a whole. Expenditure on advertising, training, relocation, and reorganization is an expense. This rule prevents entities from capitalizing brand-building and restructuring costs that have speculative future benefits.

These same items may be recognized when acquired in a business combination (at fair value), because the acquisition price provides evidence of their value. The prohibition applies only to internally generated items.

### Measure Internally Generated Software Under The Applicable Software Rules

Software developed for internal use or for sale has specific rules. Under US GAAP, costs incurred in the preliminary project stage (conceptual formulation, evaluation of alternatives) are expensed; costs incurred in the application development stage (coding, installation, testing) are capitalized once management authorizes and commits to the project and technological feasibility is established; post-implementation costs (training, maintenance) are expensed. Under IFRS, internally generated software follows the general intangible rules (research expensed, development capitalized when criteria met).

Software purchased as part of a business combination is recognized at fair value. Cloud computing arrangement costs (SaaS) are generally expensed as service charges unless the arrangement includes a software license, in which case the license is capitalized.

### Classify The Useful Life As Finite Or Indefinite

After recognition, classify the intangible as having a finite useful life (amortized over that life) or an indefinite useful life (not amortized, but tested for impairment annually). An indefinite life is one where, based on analysis of all relevant factors, there is no foreseeable limit to the period over which the asset is expected to generate net cash inflows. Indefinite does not mean infinite; it means no limit is foreseeable.

Brands with long renewal histories, perpetual trademarks, and certain licenses may have indefinite lives. Most patents, software, and customer contracts have finite lives. The classification drives the subsequent accounting: finite-life intangibles are amortized; indefinite-life intangibles are not amortized but are tested for impairment annually (and more often if indicators exist).

### Do Not Revalue Under US GAAP; Apply Revaluation Carefully Under IFRS

Under US GAAP, intangibles are carried at cost less accumulated amortization and impairment. Under IFRS, an entity may use a revaluation model (carrying at fair value less subsequent amortization and impairment) only if fair value can be measured by reference to an active market. Active markets for intangibles are rare, so the revaluation model is seldom available. Do not apply revaluation without an active market.

### Measure Acquired Intangibles At Fair Value In A Business Combination

In a business combination, identify and recognize all intangibles that meet the recognition criteria at their fair value at the acquisition date, separately from goodwill. This includes brands, technology, customer relationships, contracts, and licenses. The fair value is typically determined using income (relief-from-royalty, multi-period excess earnings), market, or cost approaches. Identifying and valuing these intangibles requires valuation expertise and affects the amount of goodwill recognized.

## Common Traps

### Capitalizing Internally Generated Brands Or Customer Lists

Internally generated brands, mastheads, publishing titles, and customer lists are not recognized, because they are not separable from the business. Capitalizing advertising or marketing expenditure overstates assets.

### Expensing All Development Costs Without Testing The Criteria

Defaulting to expensing all development costs understates assets when the six capitalization criteria are met. Under IFRS, development costs meeting the criteria must be capitalized. Test each project against the criteria.

### Failing To Separate Research From Development

The research phase (expensed) and development phase (potentially capitalized) must be separated. Treating all expenditure as research, or all as development, misstates the asset. Document the boundary where development begins.

### Recognizing Workforce Or Going-Concern Value As An Intangible

A workforce, customer relationships tied to the entity as a whole, and going-concern value are not separable and are not recognized as intangibles (they form part of goodwill in a business combination).

### Classifying An Intangible As Indefinite Life To Avoid Amortization

Classifying a finite-life intangible as indefinite to avoid amortization overstates profit. An indefinite life requires evidence of no foreseeable limit. Most intangibles have finite lives.

### Capitalizing Preliminary-Stage Software Costs

Costs before management commitment and technological feasibility (planning, evaluation, design alternatives) are expensed. Capitalizing them overstates the asset. The capitalization starts at the application development stage.

### Treating SaaS Payments As A Capitalized Asset

Most cloud computing arrangements (SaaS) are service contracts; payments are expensed. Only if the arrangement includes a software license is the license capitalized. Treating all SaaS as an asset is incorrect.

## Self-Check

- Does the item meet the definition of an intangible asset (identifiable, controlled, probable future benefits, reliably measurable cost)?
- Is the recognition and measurement approach based on how the intangible was acquired (separate, business combination, government grant, internally generated)?
- For internally generated intangibles, is research expenditure expensed and development expenditure capitalized only when all six criteria are met (IFRS) or per the applicable software rules (US GAAP)?
- Are internally generated brands, mastheads, publishing titles, and customer lists expensed, not capitalized?
- Is internally generated software split between preliminary (expensed), application development (capitalized), and post-implementation (expensed) stages?
- Is the useful life classified as finite (amortized) or indefinite (not amortized, tested annually for impairment), with evidence supporting an indefinite classification?
- Under IFRS, is the revaluation model used only where an active market exists for the intangible?
- In a business combination, are all identifiable intangibles recognized at fair value separately from goodwill, with valuation expertise engaged?
- Has a qualified accountant or valuation specialist reviewed material recognition decisions and the applicable standard confirmed?
