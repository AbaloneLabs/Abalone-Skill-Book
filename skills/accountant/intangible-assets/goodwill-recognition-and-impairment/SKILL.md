# Goodwill Recognition And Impairment

Goodwill is the residual asset in a business combination — the amount paid for a business that exceeds the fair value of the identifiable net assets acquired. It represents the future economic benefits from assets that cannot be individually identified and separately recognized: synergy, workforce, going-concern value, expected growth. Because goodwill is not amortized under either US GAAP or IFRS, its accounting is dominated by impairment testing. The judgments are substantial: the recoverable amount of the unit to which goodwill is allocated, the allocation of goodwill to cash-generating units or reporting units, and the timing of the annual test. Agents often struggle with the unit of account, apply optimistic projections to avoid a write-down, or fail to test when indicators exist between annual test dates.

Use this skill before recognizing goodwill in an acquisition, allocating it to units, performing the annual impairment test, or recognizing a goodwill impairment loss. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating goodwill as a permanent asset that never needs testing, using unsupported assumptions to avoid a write-down, or testing at the wrong unit of account.

Goodwill impairment rules differ significantly between US GAAP (ASC 350, with a one-step qualitative and quantitative model for public entities and a simplified model for private entities) and IFRS (IAS 36, with a one-step model based on recoverable amount and mandatory cash-generating unit allocation). This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. Goodwill impairment requires significant judgment and valuation expertise — involve a qualified accountant, valuation specialist, or auditor for material balances. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific acquisition.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Goodwill As The Excess Of Consideration Over Identifiable Net Assets

Goodwill arises in a business combination as the excess of the consideration transferred plus any non-controlling interest plus any previously held equity interest over the fair value of the identifiable net assets acquired at the acquisition date. Measure the consideration at fair value, including contingent consideration. Measure the identifiable net assets (including identifiable intangibles) at fair value. The residual is goodwill.

Goodwill is an asset, capitalized and not amortized. It is tested for impairment annually. Bargain purchases (negative goodwill) are recognized immediately in profit or loss, not as negative goodwill on the balance sheet.

### Allocate Goodwill To The Correct Units At The Acquisition Date

Goodwill must be allocated to each cash-generating unit (IFRS) or reporting unit (US GAAP) that is expected to benefit from the synergies of the combination. The allocation should be made at the lowest level at which the goodwill is monitored for internal management purposes, which cannot be lower than an operating segment under US GAAP or a cash-generating unit under IFRS. The allocation level affects the impairment test because each unit is tested separately.

The unit of account must be consistent across periods. Reallocating goodwill to avoid an impairment (for example, combining a weak unit with a strong one) is inappropriate unless the entity's reporting structure genuinely changed. Document the allocation basis at acquisition and review it when the reporting structure changes.

### Test Goodwill For Impairment At Least Annually

Goodwill is tested for impairment at least annually, regardless of whether indicators exist, and more often if indicators arise between annual tests. The annual test may be performed at any time during the year, but the same timing must be used each year for each unit. Under IFRS, the most detailed recent budget or forecast is the starting point; under US GAAP, the test follows the ASC 350 framework.

The annual test is mandatory because goodwill is not amortized — without testing, a decline in value would never be captured. Do not skip the test because no indicators exist; the annual test is independent of indicators.

### Apply The Qualitative Assessment Under US GAAP Where Appropriate

Under US GAAP (public entities), an entity may first perform a qualitative assessment of whether it is more likely than not that a reporting unit's fair value is less than its carrying amount. If the qualitative assessment concludes it is more likely than not that fair value exceeds carrying amount, no further testing is needed. If not, proceed to the quantitative test (comparing the reporting unit's fair value to its carrying amount, including goodwill).

The qualitative assessment considers macroeconomic, industry, cost, overall financial, and entity-specific factors. It is optional; an entity may proceed directly to the quantitative test. The qualitative assessment should not be used as a rubber stamp to avoid testing — if indicators are present, perform the quantitative test.

### Measure The Impairment Under The Applicable Framework

Under IFRS (IAS 36), the goodwill impairment test compares the carrying amount of the cash-generating unit (including allocated goodwill) with its recoverable amount (the higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use). If carrying amount exceeds recoverable amount, the impairment loss is recognized, allocated first to goodwill and then to other assets pro rata.

Under US GAAP (ASC 350, public entities), the quantitative test compares the reporting unit's fair value with its carrying amount. If fair value is less than carrying amount, a second step measures the impairment as the excess of the implied goodwill (fair value of the unit less fair value of identifiable net assets) over the carrying goodwill. Under the private-entity alternative, a simplified one-step model applies.

Confirm which framework and which model apply before measuring.

### Build Cash Flow Projections On Reasonable And Supportable Assumptions

The recoverable amount or fair value of the unit depends on cash flow projections and discount rates. Projections should be based on reasonable and supportable assumptions reflecting management's best estimate, typically for a maximum of five years unless a longer period is justified. Terminal growth rates should not exceed the long-term average growth rate for the markets in which the unit operates. The discount rate should reflect current market assessments of risk specific to the unit.

Optimistic projections, unjustified terminal growth, or an inappropriately low discount rate inflate the unit's value and avoid a write-down. Document every key assumption and challenge projections that consistently exceed industry growth. Auditors will focus on the assumptions, particularly for units where fair value is close to carrying amount.

### Recognize The Impairment Loss And Do Not Reverse It

A goodwill impairment loss is recognized in profit or loss and reduces the carrying amount of goodwill. Under both IFRS and US GAAP, a goodwill impairment loss is not reversed in subsequent periods, even if the unit's value recovers. This is a critical difference from other asset impairments (which may be reversible under IFRS). Once goodwill is written down, the write-down is permanent.

This irreversibility makes the impairment decision high-stakes. An over-aggressive write-down permanently reduces the asset; a deferred write-down leaves an overstated balance. Measure carefully.

### Disclose The Goodwill Balance, Allocation, And Impairment Testing

Disclose the carrying amount of goodwill by cash-generating unit or reporting unit, the basis of allocation, the key assumptions used in the impairment test, the discount rate, the effect of reasonably possible changes in assumptions, and any impairment losses recognized. Users rely on this to assess the risk that goodwill may be impaired in future periods.

## Common Traps

### Treating Goodwill As Permanent And Never Testing It

Goodwill is not amortized but must be tested annually. Failing to test leaves an overstated balance. The annual test is mandatory regardless of indicators.

### Allocating Goodwill To Avoid An Impairment

Reallocating goodwill from a weak unit to a strong unit, or combining units, to avoid a write-down is inappropriate unless the reporting structure genuinely changed. Keep the unit of account consistent.

### Using Optimistic Projections To Avoid A Write-Down

Inflated cash flows, excessive terminal growth, or a low discount rate keep the unit's value above carrying amount and avoid a write-down. Build projections on supportable assumptions and challenge consistent over-performance.

### Using The Qualitative Assessment As A Rubber Stamp

Under US GAAP, the qualitative assessment is optional and should reflect genuine analysis. Concluding "more likely than not" without addressing present indicators is a rubber stamp. If indicators exist, perform the quantitative test.

### Reversing A Goodwill Impairment

Goodwill impairment losses are not reversible under either IFRS or US GAAP. Reversing a prior write-down is incorrect, even if the unit's value recovers.

### Failing To Test Between Annual Dates When Indicators Arise

The annual test is a minimum. If indicators arise between annual tests (a significant market decline, a loss of a major customer, a restructuring), perform an interim test.

### Testing At The Wrong Unit Of Account

Testing goodwill at the entity level rather than the cash-generating unit or reporting level understates impairment because strong units mask weak ones. Test at the lowest level at which goodwill is monitored.

### Amortizing Goodwill

Under both US GAAP and IFRS (for non-private entities), goodwill is not amortized. Amortizing it is incorrect. (A private-entity alternative under US GAAP permits amortization on a straight-line basis over no more than 10 years, but this is an election, not the default.)

## Self-Check

- Is goodwill recognized as the excess of consideration (plus non-controlling interest and previously held interest) over the fair value of identifiable net assets at acquisition?
- Is goodwill allocated to the correct cash-generating units (IFRS) or reporting units (US GAAP) at the lowest monitored level, with the allocation documented and consistent across periods?
- Is goodwill tested for impairment at least annually (regardless of indicators) and more often if indicators arise between annual tests?
- Under US GAAP, if the qualitative assessment is used, does it reflect genuine analysis rather than a rubber stamp, with the quantitative test performed when indicators exist?
- Is the impairment measured under the correct framework (IFRS recoverable amount, US GAAP fair-value-versus-carrying or the private-entity alternative)?
- Are cash flow projections based on reasonable, supportable assumptions, with terminal growth not exceeding market averages and a risk-appropriate discount rate?
- Is any recognized goodwill impairment loss not reversed in subsequent periods, given the irreversibility of goodwill write-downs?
- Is the goodwill balance, allocation basis, key assumptions, discount rate, and sensitivity disclosed, and has a qualified accountant, valuation specialist, or auditor reviewed the test for material balances?
