# Intangible Asset Amortization

Amortization is the systematic allocation of a finite-life intangible asset's depreciable amount over its useful life. It is conceptually identical to depreciation but applies to intangibles. The key judgments are the useful life (finite versus indefinite), the amortization method (which should reflect the consumption pattern of benefits), and the residual value (almost always zero for intangibles). Agents often apply straight-line amortization by default, set residual value at zero without considering whether a market exists, or fail to reassess whether a finite-life intangible should become indefinite (or vice versa). Because intangible benefits can be consumed unevenly — a patent whose economic protection erodes, a customer list whose attrition accelerates — the amortization method and period must be reviewed regularly.

Use this skill before setting up amortization for a new intangible, reviewing the useful life or method at year-end, reassessing a life reclassification, or handling the interaction between amortization and impairment. The goal is to prevent the agent from amortizing on autopilot without testing whether the period and method still reflect how the intangible's benefits are consumed.

Amortization rules are broadly consistent between US GAAP (ASC 350) and IFRS (IAS 38), with differences in revaluation and component treatment. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs the detail. For material intangible portfolios or unusual lives, involve a qualified accountant or auditor. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific asset.

## Core Rules

### Amortize Only Finite-Life Intangibles; Do Not Amortize Indefinite-Life Intangibles

A finite-life intangible is amortized over its useful life. An indefinite-life intangible is not amortized; instead it is tested for impairment annually (and more often if indicators exist). The life classification is the first decision. If the intangible has a foreseeable limit to its useful life (a patent expiring in 15 years, a license for 10 years, software expected to be replaced in 4 years), it is finite and amortized. If no foreseeable limit exists (a perpetual brand with long renewal history), it is indefinite and not amortized.

Reassess the classification at least annually. A finite-life intangible may become indefinite if circumstances change (a license renewed perpetually), and an indefinite-life intangible may become finite (a brand facing obsolescence). Reclassification changes the accounting prospectively.

### Set The Amortization Period Based On The Shortest Of The Relevant Limits

The useful life of a finite-life intangible is the shortest of: the period over which the asset is expected to generate economic benefits; the legal life (patent term, copyright duration); and the contractual life (license term, franchise agreement). Even if a patent has a 20-year legal life, if the technology is expected to be obsolete in 8 years, the useful life is 8 years. If a license runs for 10 years but the entity expects to use it for 6, the life is 6 years.

Consider renewal options only if there is reasonable certainty they will be exercised. Consider the entity's own expected use, not just the asset's theoretical maximum life.

### Select An Amortization Method That Reflects The Consumption Pattern

The amortization method should reflect the pattern in which the intangible's economic benefits are consumed. Straight-line is appropriate when benefits are consumed evenly (a license used steadily over its term). A different method is appropriate when benefits are consumed unevenly (a customer list whose attrition accelerates, a patent whose economic protection erodes as competitors develop alternatives). Units-of-production is appropriate if consumption tracks usage.

Straight-line is the most common method and is reasonable for most intangibles, but it should be chosen because the consumption pattern is even, not by default. Document the rationale. If the consumption pattern changes, change the method prospectively as a change in estimate.

### Treat Residual Value As Zero Unless An Active Market Exists

For intangibles, residual value is generally zero, because an intangible at the end of its life usually has no disposal value and may have disposal costs. The exception is when there is a commitment by a third party to purchase the asset at the end of its useful life, or when there is an active market for the asset and residual value can be determined by reference to that market. Active markets for intangibles are rare.

Do not set a residual value based on speculative future disposal proceeds. If residual value is zero, the depreciable amount equals cost (or revalued amount). Review residual value at each reporting date; if it changes, adjust amortization prospectively.

### Apply The Reassessment Of Useful Life And Method Prospectively

Under IFRS, the useful life and amortization method must be reviewed at least annually. Under US GAAP, they are reviewed when events or circumstances indicate a change is needed. If the useful life or method changes, the change is a change in accounting estimate applied prospectively: the carrying amount at the date of change is amortized over the revised remaining useful life using the revised method. Do not restate prior periods.

A shortened life (because obsolescence arrived sooner) accelerates amortization. An extended life (because the intangible is being used longer) reduces amortization. Both require evidence and disclosure of the effect.

### Handle Reclassification Between Finite And Indefinite Life

If a finite-life intangible is reclassified to indefinite life, stop amortizing it and begin annual impairment testing. The carrying amount at the reclassification date becomes the basis for impairment testing. If an indefinite-life intangible is reclassified to finite life, begin amortizing the carrying amount over the remaining useful life. Reclassification is a change in accounting estimate applied prospectively.

Reclassification must be supported by changed circumstances (a renewed perpetual license, a brand now facing obsolescence), not by a desire to manage amortization expense. Document the rationale.

### Separate The Intangible From Goodwill

In a business combination, acquired intangibles are recognized separately from goodwill and amortized over their finite useful lives. Goodwill is not amortized under either US GAAP or IFRS (for non-private-entity accounting) but is tested for impairment annually. Do not amortize goodwill. The separation matters because intangible amortization reduces profit, while goodwill impairment is tested separately.

### Coordinate Amortization With Impairment Testing

If a finite-life intangible is impaired, the reduced carrying amount becomes the new basis for amortization, and the useful life and method should be reassessed. An impairment loss may indicate the useful life should be shortened. For indefinite-life intangibles, impairment testing replaces amortization — if impaired, the loss is recognized and the asset is tested again annually.

## Common Traps

### Amortizing An Indefinite-Life Intangible

Indefinite-life intangibles are not amortized; they are tested for impairment annually. Amortizing them understates profit and the asset. Confirm the life classification before amortizing.

### Using The Legal Or Contractual Life Instead Of The Economic Life

The useful life is the shortest of the economic, legal, and contractual lives. Using the full legal life of a patent when the technology is obsolete sooner overstates the asset and understates amortization.

### Defaulting To Straight-Line Without Considering The Consumption Pattern

Straight-line is reasonable when benefits are consumed evenly. For a customer list with accelerating attrition, straight-line understates early amortization. Test the consumption pattern.

### Setting A Residual Value Based On Speculative Disposal Proceeds

Residual value is zero unless there is a purchase commitment or an active market. Setting a residual value to reduce amortization overstates the asset.

### Failing To Reassess The Useful Life Annually

Intangible lives change as technology, markets, and contracts evolve. Failing to reassess leaves amortization based on stale assumptions. Under IFRS, annual review is required.

### Reclassifying To Indefinite Life To Stop Amortization

Reclassifying a finite-life intangible as indefinite to stop amortization overstates profit. Reclassification requires genuine evidence of no foreseeable limit to useful life.

### Amortizing Goodwill

Goodwill is not amortized under either US GAAP or IFRS (for non-private entities). It is tested for impairment annually. Amortizing goodwill is incorrect.

## Self-Check

- Is the intangible classified as finite life (amortized) or indefinite life (not amortized, tested annually for impairment)?
- Is the amortization period the shortest of the economic, legal, and contractual lives, considering renewal options only where reasonably certain?
- Does the amortization method reflect the pattern in which the intangible's economic benefits are consumed, with straight-line used only where consumption is even?
- Is residual value set at zero unless there is a third-party purchase commitment or an active market, and reviewed at each reporting date?
- Are useful life and method reviewed at least annually (IFRS) or when circumstances change (US GAAP), with changes applied prospectively as changes in estimate?
- Are reclassifications between finite and indefinite life supported by changed circumstances and applied prospectively, not used to manage amortization?
- Is acquired goodwill separated from identifiable intangibles, not amortized, and tested for impairment annually?
- After an impairment loss, are amortization, useful life, and method reassessed on the reduced carrying amount?
- Has a qualified accountant or auditor reviewed material amortization decisions and the applicable standard confirmed?
