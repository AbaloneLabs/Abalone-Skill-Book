# Discontinued Operations Classification And Criteria

Discontinued operations classification changes how results are presented, and the criteria are narrower than many assume. A discontinued operation is a component of an entity that has been disposed of, or is classified as held for sale, and that represents a separate major line of business or geographical area, or is part of a single coordinated plan to dispose of such a component, or is a subsidiary acquired exclusively with a view to resale. The results and cash flows of that component are presented separately from continuing operations, which lets users see the ongoing business cleanly. Agents frequently classify any disposal as a discontinued operation, apply the label to a minor asset sale, or conflate the held-for-sale criteria with the discontinued-operation criteria. The harm is that discontinued presentation flatters continuing operations by stripping out losses, so over-classification misleads users about the performance of the ongoing business, and under-classification buries a major disposal within continuing results.

Use this skill before classifying a component as a discontinued operation, applying the strategic-shift criteria, distinguishing discontinued operations from held-for-sale assets, or deciding on continuing versus discontinued presentation. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying the label loosely or missing it where a genuine strategic shift has occurred.

Discontinued operations rules are in IFRS 5 and ASC 205-20 (US GAAP), with differences in the criteria, the measurement of the disposal group, and the disclosure. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs. For material disposals or complex plans, involve a qualified accountant. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific disposal.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Strategic-Shift Criterion Rigorously

The defining criterion for a discontinued operation is that the component represents a separate major line of business or geographical area of the entity, or is part of a single coordinated plan to dispose of such a component. This is a strategic-shift test: the disposal must represent a shift in the entity's business that has a major effect on operations and financial results. A minor asset sale, the disposal of a single store within a retail chain, or the exit of a small product line do not meet the criterion.

Apply the criterion by asking whether the component, on its own, represents a major line of business or geographical area. Consider revenue, profit, assets, and the strategic significance. If the component is integrated into a larger operation and does not stand as a major line, it is not a discontinued operation even if it is being disposed of. Document the strategic-shift analysis, because the conclusion drives presentation.

### Distinguish Discontinued Operations From Held For Sale

Held-for-sale classification and discontinued-operations classification are related but distinct. A disposal group must first meet the held-for-sale criteria to be classified as held for sale, and then, separately, must meet the discontinued-operation criteria to be presented as a discontinued operation. A disposal group can be held for sale without being a discontinued operation, if it does not represent a major line of business. Conversely, a component disposed of in the period may qualify as a discontinued operation even if it was not previously held for sale, if it met the criteria at the disposal date.

Apply the two tests separately. Confirm held-for-sale status first (available for immediate sale in its present condition, sale highly probable, active program to find a buyer, expected within one year, and the other criteria). Then confirm the discontinued-operation criterion. A held-for-sale asset that is not a major line of business is presented within continuing operations, measured at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell, but its results are not separately presented as discontinued.

### Identify The Component And Its Operations Clearly

The discontinued operation is a component, which is operations and cash flows that can be clearly distinguished, operationally and for financial reporting purposes, from the rest of the entity. The component must be identifiable as a unit. If the disposal group mixes continuing and discontinued operations, the component boundaries must be drawn so that only the discontinued portion is presented separately.

Define the component before classifying. A disposal that includes assets and operations from multiple lines of business may need to be split, with only the qualifying portion presented as discontinued. Drawing the boundary too broadly strips continuing results into discontinued presentation; too narrowly leaves discontinued results in continuing operations.

### Recognize The Coordinated Plan Exception

A discontinued operation can also be a component that is part of a single coordinated plan to dispose of a separate major line of business or geographical area. This covers a phased disposal where individual components, none of which is a major line on its own, together form a major line being exited through a coordinated plan. The plan must be single and coordinated, not a series of independent disposals.

Identify whether a coordinated plan exists and whether it collectively represents a major line of business. Document the plan, its components, and the strategic shift. Treating independent disposals as a coordinated plan to access discontinued presentation is an over-classification.

### Apply The Subsidiary Acquired For Resale Exception

A subsidiary acquired exclusively with a view to resale qualifies as a discontinued operation if, on acquisition, it meets the criteria to be classified as held for sale. This applies to acquisitions such as a portfolio purchase where one entity is intended for immediate resale. The subsidiary's results are presented as discontinued from the acquisition date.

Confirm that the subsidiary was acquired exclusively for resale and met held-for-sale criteria at acquisition. A subsidiary acquired to hold and integrate, later put up for sale, does not qualify under this exception from the acquisition date, though it may qualify when held-for-sale criteria are later met.

### Reassess Classification When Facts Change

Classification is reassessed at each reporting date. If a disposal group previously classified as held for sale no longer meets the criteria, it is reclassified out of held for sale, and if it was presented as discontinued, that presentation is reversed with retrospective application. If new information shows a disposal qualifies as discontinued that was not previously classified, the prior periods are restated to present it as discontinued.

Reassess at each close. Do not lock in a classification from an earlier period if facts have changed. A planned sale that is cancelled, or a disposal that is restructured, may require reclassification and restatement.

## Common Traps

### Classifying Any Disposal As A Discontinued Operation

The criterion is a strategic shift representing a major line of business or geographical area. Applying the label to a minor asset sale or a single-store disposal over-classes and flatters continuing operations.

### Conflating Held-For-Sale With Discontinued Operations

A disposal group must meet held-for-sale criteria and separately meet the discontinued-operation criteria. A held-for-sale asset that is not a major line of business is not a discontinued operation.

### Drawing The Component Boundary Too Broadly

Including continuing operations within the discontinued component strips ongoing results into discontinued presentation, flattering continuing operations and misleading users.

### Treating Independent Disposals As A Coordinated Plan

The coordinated plan exception requires a single, coordinated plan to dispose of a major line. Treating independent disposals as a plan to access discontinued presentation is an over-classification.

### Failing To Reassess Classification When The Sale Is Cancelled

If a held-for-sale disposal group no longer meets the criteria because the sale is cancelled or restructured, the classification is reversed with retrospective application. Leaving it as discontinued misstates presentation.

### Missing The Discontinued Classification For A Genuine Strategic Shift

A major divestiture that does meet the criterion but is left within continuing operations buries a significant disposal and prevents users from seeing the ongoing business cleanly.

### Not Restating Prior Periods On Reclassification

When classification changes, prior periods are restated to present the component consistently. Presenting the prior period as continuing and the current as discontinued is inconsistent and misleading.

## Self-Check

- Does the component represent a separate major line of business or geographical area, representing a strategic shift, or is it part of a single coordinated plan to dispose of such a component?
- Is the disposal group classified as held for sale (or disposed of in the period) as a separate test from the discontinued-operation criterion?
- Is the component clearly identifiable, with operations and cash flows distinguishable from the rest of the entity, and are the boundaries drawn to include only the discontinued portion?
- If relying on the coordinated plan exception, is the plan single and coordinated, and does it collectively represent a major line of business?
- If relying on the subsidiary-acquired-for-resale exception, was the subsidiary acquired exclusively for resale and did it meet held-for-sale criteria at acquisition?
- Is classification reassessed at each reporting date, with reclassification and retrospective restatement applied when facts change?
- Are prior periods restated to present the component consistently as discontinued or continuing?
- Has a qualified accountant reviewed the strategic-shift analysis, the component boundary, and the classification conclusion for material disposals?
