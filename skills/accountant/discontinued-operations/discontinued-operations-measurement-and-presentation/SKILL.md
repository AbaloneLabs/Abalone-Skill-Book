# Discontinued Operations Measurement And Presentation

Once a component qualifies as a discontinued operation, the measurement and presentation rules determine what numbers appear and where. The disposal group is measured at the lower of its carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell at the point it meets the held-for-sale criteria, with any excess of carrying amount over fair value less costs to sell recognized as an impairment loss. The results of the discontinued operation are then presented as a single amount, post-tax, in the income statement, separate from continuing operations. Agents frequently fail to recognize the impairment on initial classification, present the discontinued results pre-tax or split across multiple lines, allocate continuing-entity restructuring costs into the discontinued operation, or fail to restate prior periods so that the discontinued operation appears consistently. The consequence is that the discontinued line is not comparable across periods and the continuing operations are distorted by items that belong elsewhere.

Use this skill before measuring a disposal group, recognizing held-for-sale impairment, presenting discontinued results, allocating shared costs, or restating prior periods for a discontinued operation. The goal is to prevent the agent from mis-measuring the disposal group and mis-presenting the discontinued results, both of which distort the picture users rely on.

Measurement and presentation rules are in IFRS 5 and ASC 205-20 (US GAAP), with differences in the level of single-line presentation, the treatment of restructuring, and the disclosure. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs. For material disposals, involve a qualified accountant. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific disposal.

## Core Rules

### Measure The Disposal Group At Lower Of Carrying Amount And Fair Value Less Costs To Sell

When a disposal group meets the held-for-sale criteria, it is measured at the lower of its carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell. If the carrying amount exceeds fair value less costs to sell, an impairment loss is recognized immediately. This write-down reflects that the asset group will not be recovered through use but through sale at a lower amount. Subsequent increases in fair value less costs to sell are recognized as a gain, but only up to the cumulative impairment previously recognized, not beyond the original carrying amount.

Perform the comparison at the classification date and at each subsequent reporting date. A common error is leaving the disposal group at its pre-classification carrying amount, which overstates assets when the sale value is lower. Recognize the impairment in continuing operations, because the write-down occurs before the operation is discontinued for presentation purposes.

### Recognize Subsequent Changes In Fair Value Less Costs To Sell

After initial classification, the disposal group is remeasured at each reporting date at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell. A further decline triggers an additional impairment loss. A recovery triggers a gain, limited to the cumulative impairment previously recognized, so the carrying amount never exceeds what it would have been absent the write-downs.

Remeasure each period until disposal. Ignoring subsequent changes leaves the disposal group misstated relative to the expected sale proceeds. Note that the recovery is capped, so a disposal group cannot be written up above its original carrying amount.

### Present The Discontinued Operation As A Single Post-Tax Amount

The results of a discontinued operation are presented as a single amount in the income statement, comprising the post-tax profit or loss of the discontinued operation and the post-tax gain or loss recognized on the disposal. Under IFRS, this single amount includes the results for the period and the disposal gain or loss. Under US GAAP, the presentation is also a single line but with specific disclosure of the components in the notes.

Present as a single line, post-tax. A common error is presenting the discontinued results pre-tax, or splitting them across revenue, cost of sales, and other lines within the income statement, which defeats the purpose of separate presentation. Calculate the tax attributable to the discontinued operation separately from continuing operations.

### Allocate Shared Expenses And Restructuring Carefully

The discontinued operation's results should include only the expenses directly attributable to it, plus a reasonable allocation of expenses that the continuing entity will continue to incur after the disposal. General administrative expenses, corporate overhead, and interest may be allocated if the allocation is reasonable and consistent. Restructuring costs that are a direct consequence of the disposal, and that will not continue after the disposal, are included in the discontinued operation; restructuring costs that the continuing entity will incur are not.

Apply allocation consistently and document the basis. Allocating continuing-entity restructuring or overhead into the discontinued operation flatters continuing operations by dumping costs that will persist. Allocating too little leaves the discontinued operation understated.

### Do Not Depreciate Or Amortize A Held-For-Sale Disposal Group

Once classified as held for sale, the disposal group is not depreciated or amortized. The rationale is that the assets will be sold, not consumed, so depreciation would understate the disposal group relative to its sale value. Interest and other costs capitalized to the disposal group continue to be assessed under the applicable borrowing-cost rules.

Stop depreciation on classification. A common error is continuing to depreciate a held-for-sale disposal group, which understates its carrying amount and overstates the disposal gain or understates the loss.

### Restate Prior Periods For Consistent Presentation

When a component is classified as a discontinued operation in the current period, the prior-period comparative income statement is restated to present the component's results as a single discontinued line, separate from continuing operations. This makes the periods comparable, so users can see the ongoing business consistently. The balance sheet is generally not restated for held-for-sale classification of a current-period disposal, though disclosure separates the assets and liabilities.

Restate the comparative income statement. Presenting the current period as discontinued and the prior period as continuing is inconsistent and prevents comparison.

### Recognize The Disposal Gain Or Loss On Completion

On disposal, the gain or loss is the difference between the net disposal proceeds and the carrying amount of the disposal group, plus any remaining results up to the disposal date. This is included in the single discontinued-operation line. If the disposal occurs over multiple stages, the gain or loss is recognized as control passes or as the components are sold, depending on the structure.

Recognize on completion, not on signing. A signed agreement that has not closed does not crystallize the gain or loss, though it may trigger held-for-sale classification and impairment.

## Common Traps

### Failing To Recognize Held-For-Sale Impairment

Leaving the disposal group at its pre-classification carrying amount when fair value less costs to sell is lower overstates assets and overstates the eventual disposal loss or understates the gain.

### Continuing To Depreciate A Held-For-Sale Disposal Group

Once classified as held for sale, depreciation stops. Continuing to depreciate understates the carrying amount and distorts the disposal result.

### Presenting Discontinued Results Pre-Tax Or Split Across Lines

The discontinued operation is a single post-tax amount. Presenting pre-tax or splitting across revenue and cost lines defeats separate presentation.

### Allocating Continuing-Entity Restructuring Into The Discontinued Operation

Restructuring that the continuing entity will incur after the disposal belongs in continuing operations. Allocating it into discontinued presentation flatters continuing results.

### Not Restating Prior Periods

When classification changes, the comparative income statement is restated. Presenting prior periods as continuing while the current is discontinued prevents comparison.

### Recognizing The Disposal Gain On Signing Rather Than Completion

The gain or loss is recognized on disposal, when control passes or the components are sold. Recognizing on signing overstates the result before the transaction is complete.

### Writing The Disposal Group Up Above Original Carrying Amount

Recoveries in fair value less costs to sell are capped at cumulative impairment. Writing the group above its original carrying amount overstates assets.

## Self-Check

- Is the disposal group measured at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell at classification, with any excess recognized as an impairment loss in continuing operations?
- Is the disposal group remeasured at each reporting date, with further declines recognized as impairment and recoveries capped at cumulative impairment?
- Has depreciation and amortization on the held-for-sale disposal group been stopped?
- Are the results of the discontinued operation presented as a single post-tax amount, separate from continuing operations, with the tax attributable calculated separately?
- Are shared expenses and restructuring allocated so that only directly attributable costs and a reasonable allocation of continuing overhead are in the discontinued operation, with continuing-entity restructuring excluded?
- Is the prior-period comparative income statement restated to present the component consistently as discontinued?
- Is the disposal gain or loss recognized on completion, as control passes or components are sold, included in the single discontinued-operation line?
- Has a qualified accountant reviewed the impairment measurement, the allocation basis, the restatement, and the disposal gain or loss for material disposals?
