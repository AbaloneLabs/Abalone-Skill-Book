# Discontinued Operations Disclosure

Disclosure is where the single-line presentation of a discontinued operation is explained, because the income statement shows only one post-tax number while users need the detail to understand what is being disposed of, how it performed, and what the disposal means for the entity. The disclosure must provide the major classes of assets and liabilities held for sale, the revenue and profit or loss of the discontinued operation, the cash flows it generated, the disposal gain or loss, and sufficient segment information for users to assess the disposal's significance. Agents frequently provide only the single income-statement line with no supporting note, omit the balance-sheet separation of held-for-sale assets and liabilities, or fail to disclose the cash flows, leaving users unable to assess the disposal's effect on the entity's liquidity and results. The result is a discontinued operation that is technically presented but operationally opaque.

Use this skill before preparing disclosures for a discontinued operation, disclosing the components of the single-line amount, the held-for-sale assets and liabilities, the cash flows, or the segment information. The goal is to ensure the disclosure gives users a real picture of the disposal, not just a single number.

Disclosure requirements are in IFRS 5 and ASC 205-20 (US GAAP), with differences in the level of detail and the segment-reporting interaction. This skill provides the general framework; the applicable standard governs. For material disposals, involve a qualified accountant. This is structural guidance, not a determination for a specific disposal.

## Core Rules

### Disclose The Components Of The Single Post-Tax Amount

The income statement presents the discontinued operation as a single post-tax amount, but the notes must disclose its components so users can understand what drove the result. Disclose the revenue, expenses, and pre-tax profit or loss of the discontinued operation, the income tax expense attributable, and the gain or loss recognized on disposal. Under US GAAP, these components are specifically required in the notes; under IFRS, the disclosure of the components is required to the extent necessary.

Break down the single line. A note that restates only the single number adds nothing. Users need to see whether the discontinued result reflects operating performance, a disposal gain or loss, or tax effects, because each tells a different story.

### Disclose The Major Classes Of Assets And Liabilities Held For Sale

The balance sheet presents held-for-sale assets and liabilities as single line items, but the notes must disclose the major classes of assets and liabilities. This typically includes property, plant and equipment, intangible assets, goodwill, inventory, receivables, and the corresponding payables and other liabilities that make up the disposal group. The disclosure lets users see what is being sold and the carrying amounts.

Disclose the major classes. A balance sheet that shows a single held-for-sale line with no breakdown leaves users unable to assess what assets are leaving the entity and what liabilities are being transferred.

### Disclose The Revenue, Profit Or Loss, And Cash Flows

For the discontinued operation, disclose the revenue, the profit or loss before tax, the income tax expense, and the net cash flows attributable to operating, investing, and financing activities. The cash flow disclosure is particularly important because users need to understand how the disposal affects the entity's liquidity, both while the operation is held and on disposal.

Disclose the cash flows, not just the profit. A discontinued operation can be profitable but cash-absorbing, or loss-making but cash-generative, and users rely on the cash flow disclosure to assess the disposal's effect on liquidity.

### Disclose The Disposal Gain Or Loss Separately

The gain or loss on disposal is part of the single discontinued-operation line, but it should be disclosed separately within the components so users can distinguish the operating result from the disposal result. The disposal gain or loss reflects the difference between net proceeds and the carrying amount of the disposal group, and it is a one-time item that users often exclude from their assessment of ongoing performance.

Separate the disposal gain or loss from the operating result. Combining them into a single disclosed number obscures both the ongoing performance of the discontinued operation and the economics of the disposal.

### Provide Segment Information For Discontinued Operations

Where the entity reports segment information, the discontinued operation is disclosed within the segment reporting, showing which segment or segments the discontinued operation relates to. This lets users understand the disposal in the context of the entity's segment structure and assess the effect on the remaining segments.

Provide the segment information for the discontinued operation, consistent with the segment-reporting framework. Omitting the segment context leaves users unable to see which part of the business is being exited.

### Disclose When A Sale Is No Longer Highly Probable

If a disposal group previously classified as held for sale no longer meets the criteria, the entity discloses the change in classification and the reasons. The assets and liabilities are reclassified back into their previous balance-sheet categories, and any previously recognized impairment is reversed subject to the cap. The disclosure must explain why the sale did not proceed and the effect of reclassification.

Disclose the change transparently. A held-for-sale classification that silently reverts to held-and-used without explanation leaves users confused about the entity's disposal plans and the reliability of its classifications.

### Disclose The Strategic Context Of The Disposal

Beyond the quantitative disclosure, provide qualitative context: the nature of the discontinued operation, the reasons for the disposal, the expected timing, and the expected use of the proceeds. This narrative helps users understand the strategic shift and its implications for the continuing entity.

Provide the context. Numbers alone do not explain why the entity is disposing of a major line of business, what it plans to do with the proceeds, or how the disposal fits the strategy. The narrative disclosure fills that gap.

## Common Traps

### Providing Only The Single Income-Statement Line With No Supporting Note

The single post-tax amount must be supported by a note disclosing the components. Restating only the single number adds nothing for users.

### Omitting The Balance-Sheet Breakdown Of Held-For-Sale Assets And Liabilities

The balance sheet shows single held-for-sale lines, but the notes must disclose the major classes. Omitting the breakdown leaves users unable to assess what is being sold.

### Failing To Disclose The Cash Flows Of The Discontinued Operation

Cash flow disclosure is required and is critical for assessing liquidity effects. Disclosing only profit or loss misses the cash dimension.

### Combining The Operating Result And The Disposal Gain Into One Number

The disposal gain or loss should be disclosed separately within the components so users can distinguish the one-time disposal result from the ongoing operating performance.

### Omitting The Segment Information For Discontinued Operations

The discontinued operation must be disclosed within the segment reporting. Omitting the segment context leaves users unable to see which part of the business is being exited.

### Silently Reclassifying A Cancelled Sale Without Disclosure

When a sale is no longer highly probable, the change in classification and the reasons must be disclosed. A silent reversion leaves users confused about the disposal plans.

### Providing Numbers Without Strategic Context

Quantitative disclosure without the qualitative narrative of why the disposal is happening, the timing, and the use of proceeds leaves users without the strategic picture.

## Self-Check

- Does the note disclose the components of the single post-tax amount, including revenue, expenses, pre-tax profit or loss, income tax, and the disposal gain or loss?
- Are the major classes of held-for-sale assets and liabilities disclosed, showing what is being sold and the carrying amounts?
- Are the net cash flows attributable to operating, investing, and financing activities of the discontinued operation disclosed?
- Is the disposal gain or loss disclosed separately from the operating result, so users can distinguish the one-time disposal result?
- Is segment information provided for the discontinued operation, showing which segment or segments the disposal relates to?
- When a sale is no longer highly probable, is the change in classification, the reasons, and the reclassification effect disclosed?
- Does the disclosure provide the qualitative strategic context, including the nature of the operation, the reasons for disposal, the expected timing, and the use of proceeds?
- Has a qualified accountant reviewed the disclosure for completeness, the component breakdown, the cash flow presentation, and the segment information for material disposals?
