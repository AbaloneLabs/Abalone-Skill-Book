# Pediatric Emergency and Resuscitation

Pediatric resuscitation is rare, high-stakes, and unforgiving of error. The judgment problem is that most nurses will encounter only a few pediatric arrests in their career, the equipment and doses must be correct for the specific size of the child, and the emotional intensity of a dying child can freeze even experienced clinicians. Preparation, standardization, and role clarity are the difference between a resuscitation that saves a life and one that compounds the tragedy. The nurse must know the algorithms cold, use length-based tools to eliminate calculation errors, and run the response with structured communication.

## Core Rules

### Use the Length-Based Tape to Eliminate Calculation Errors

In a pediatric arrest, calculating doses by weight under pressure is error-prone, and the wrong weight estimate leads to wrong doses for every medication and every shock. The Broselow or similar length-based tape converts the child's length into a color-coded zone that specifies the weight, the medication doses, the equipment sizes, and the defibrillation energy. Apply the tape at the start of every pediatric resuscitation, use the color zone to guide all interventions, and post the information visibly so the whole team works from the same data. This single tool eliminates the most common source of pediatric resuscitation error.

### Follow the Systematic PALS Assessment to Identify the Cause

Pediatric arrest is most often respiratory, not cardiac, and the systematic PALS assessment (Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure) is designed to find the reversible cause. In children, the sequence matters: airway and breathing come first because hypoxia is the usual pathway to arrest. Perform the primary assessment rapidly, intervene on life threats immediately, and then move to the secondary assessment to identify the cause (sepsis, arrhythmia, trauma, toxidrome, metabolic). The reversible causes in PALS are the Hs and Ts (Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, Hydrogen ion, Hypo/Hyperkalemia, Hypothermia, Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade, Toxins, Thrombosis, Trauma) — keep these in mind as you work through the assessment.

### Master Pediatric Airway Management and the Differences from Adults

The pediatric airway is anatomically different: a larger occiput (causes flexion when supine), a more anterior and cephalad larynx, the narrowest point at the cricoid (not the vocal cords, as in adults), a larger tongue relative to the oral cavity, and shorter tracheal length (increasing the risk of mainstem intubation or accidental extubation). Use the correct sized equipment (suction catheter, oral airway, bag-mask, laryngoscope blade, endotracheal tube) per the length-based tape. Preoxygenate aggressively. Use the sniffing position, not simple head tilt. Confirm tube placement with both clinical signs and a quantitative CO2 detector, and secure the tube carefully to prevent displacement. Recognize that pediatric oxygen desaturation occurs faster than in adults due to a higher metabolic rate and lower functional residual capacity.

### Coordinate the Team with Closed-Loop Communication and Role Clarity

In a pediatric arrest, confusion and noise are the enemies of effective resuscitation. Assign roles explicitly before or at the start: team leader, airway, compressions, medications, defibrillator, timekeeper, documenter, family liaison. Use closed-loop communication: the leader says "give 1 mg epinephrine IV," the medication nurse repeats "giving 1 mg epinephrine IV," administers, and reports back "1 mg epinephrine IV given." This eliminates ambiguity and prevents both missed and duplicate doses. Keep the room calm: limit conversation to the resuscitation, speak loudly enough to be heard, and use the team leader as the single point of command.

### Provide High-Quality CPR with the Correct Technique for Size

Pediatric CPR differs from adult CPR: use two thumbs with hands encircling the chest for infants (two-person), two fingers for infants (one-person), and one or two hands for children depending on size. Compress at least one-third the anteroposterior chest diameter (about 1.5 inches in infants, 2 inches in children), at a rate of 100-120 per minute, allowing full chest recoil and minimizing interruptions. Rotate compressors every 2 minutes to prevent fatigue, which degrades compression quality within minutes. Quality matters more than speed — shallow or fast compressions without recoil are ineffective. Use a feedback device if available, and listen to the team leader's feedback on compression quality.

### Involve the Family During Resuscitation When Appropriate

Family presence during resuscitation (FPDR) is supported by evidence and by the family's right to be with their child in the most critical moment. Offer the option to be present, assign a dedicated family liaison to explain what is happening and support them, and allow them to leave and return as needed. Not all families will choose to be present, and that is acceptable — but the option should be offered, not assumed away. If the resuscitation is unsuccessful, the family's presence allows them to see that everything was done, which aids grieving. Document the family's choice and the support provided.

### Debrief After Every Pediatric Resuscitation

Pediatric resuscitation is emotionally traumatic for the team as well as the family. A structured debrief after every event — successful or unsuccessful — improves team performance, identifies system issues, and supports the emotional health of the clinicians. Use a standardized format (Plus/Delta, or a formal debrief tool): what went well, what could improve, what do we need to follow up on, and how are people feeling. Normalize the emotional impact — it is appropriate to be affected by a child's death — and provide access to support resources. The team that does not debrief carries the weight silently and is more prone to burnout and errors.

## Common Traps

### Treating Pediatric Arrest Like Adult Arrest (Cardiac-First)

The trap is that adult arrest is usually cardiac, so the reflexive response is chest compressions and defibrillation, with airway as a secondary concern. But pediatric arrest is usually respiratory, and the first intervention should be airway and ventilation — providing oxygen reverses the cause in many cases. The harm is that the team focuses on compressions and shocks while the underlying hypoxia goes uncorrected, and the arrest persists. In children, open the airway, ventilate with bag-mask, and oxygenate before or simultaneously with compressions. Defibrillation is needed for ventricular fibrillation or pulseless VT, which are less common in children but must be recognized on the monitor.

### Estimating the Weight Instead of Using the Length-Based Tape

The trap is that the nurse, asked for the weight of an unresponsive child, guesses ("he looks about 20 kilos"), and that guess becomes the basis for every dose and every shock. The harm is that weight estimates are often wrong by 20-50%, and the resulting doses are wrong proportionally. Use the length-based tape — it takes 10 seconds and eliminates the guess. If no tape is available, ask the parent for a recent weight (they often know it accurately), or use an age-based estimate formula as a last resort, but recognize the imprecision and verify as soon as possible.

### Continuing Resuscitation Without Reversible Cause Identification

The trap is that the team falls into a rhythm of compressions, epinephrine, shock, repeat, without stepping back to ask "why did this child arrest, and what can I reverse?" The harm is that a treatable cause (tension pneumothorax, hypovolemia, hypoglycemia, hypothermia, toxin) goes unidentified, and the resuscitation fails despite technically correct CPR. The team leader should explicitly run through the Hs and Ts every few cycles, and the team should be actively looking for reversible causes (checking glucose, assessing for tension, considering toxidromes). Resuscitation without cause identification is mechanics without medicine.

### Using Adult-Sized Equipment on a Child

The trap is that adult equipment is what is immediately available, and the nurse, under pressure, uses the adult bag-mask, the adult suction catheter, or the adult defibrillator pads because it is at hand. The harm is that adult equipment does not fit a child — the bag delivers too much volume, the suction is too large, the pads do not make proper contact and deliver inappropriate energy. Use the length-based tape to select the correct equipment size, and have a fully stocked pediatric resuscitation cart immediately accessible in any area that cares for children. If you do not have the right equipment, get it before proceeding — improvising with adult equipment causes harm.

### Forgetting the Family in the Chaos

The trap is that the resuscitation is all-consuming, and the family — waiting outside, frightened, knowing nothing — becomes an afterthought. The harm is that the family experiences the worst moment of their lives in isolation, without information or support, and if the outcome is poor, they have no understanding of what happened. Assign a family liaison at the start of every pediatric resuscitation. Offer presence. Provide updates. And after the event, whether the child survived or not, sit with the family, explain what happened in terms they can understand, and offer support. This is not optional — it is part of the care.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Have I applied the length-based tape and is the color-coded information visible to the whole team?
- Am I following the PALS systematic assessment (ABC first for pediatric arrest), or have I defaulted to adult cardiac-first thinking?
- Is the airway equipment the correct size for this child, and have I confirmed tube placement with quantitative CO2?
- Are roles assigned and is the team using closed-loop communication?
- Is the CPR high-quality — correct depth, full recoil, 100-120 rate, rotating compressors every 2 minutes?
- Has the team leader explicitly reviewed the Hs and Ts in the last few cycles, or are we in a mechanical loop?
- Has the family been offered presence and assigned a liaison?
- For the defibrillation energy and the medication doses, do they match the length-based tape zone?
- Have I used the correct-sized equipment, not adult equipment out of convenience?
- Will this team debrief after the event, and do we have access to emotional support?
