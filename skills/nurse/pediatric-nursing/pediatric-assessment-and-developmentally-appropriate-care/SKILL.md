# Pediatric Assessment and Developmentally Appropriate Care

Children are not small adults. Their anatomy, physiology, pharmacology, and psychology differ fundamentally at every developmental stage, and the judgment problem is that the nurse must constantly recalibrate expectations, parameters, and communication style to the specific age in front of them. A medication dose calculated for an adult can be ten-fold toxic in an infant. A vital sign that is normal for a newborn is alarming in a toddler. A child who appears to be compensating well can decompensate rapidly because their physiologic reserve is smaller. The nurse must know the developmental stages, use age-appropriate tools, and engage the family as co-caregivers while maintaining clinical independence.

## Core Rules

### Use Age-Specific Vital Sign Parameters and Respiratory Rates

Pediatric vital signs change dramatically with age, and applying adult norms is dangerous. A heart rate of 150 is normal in an infant and concerning in a school-age child. A respiratory rate of 40 is normal in a newborn and a sign of distress in a teenager. Blood pressure is the last vital sign to change in pediatric shock — a child can maintain a normal blood pressure through vasoconstriction long after tissue perfusion is compromised, then crash suddenly. Use age-specific reference ranges (PEWS, PALS), trend the values, and recognize tachycardia and tachypnea as the earliest, most sensitive signs of deterioration. Never reassure yourself with a normal blood pressure alone in a sick child.

### Assess with Developmentally Appropriate Tools and Communication

A two-year-old cannot describe their pain with a number; use the FLACC scale (Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability) or the r-FLACC for cognitively impaired children. A school-age child can use a 0-10 numeric scale but may need a faces scale. Adolescents need privacy, confidentiality, and an opportunity to speak without a parent present. Adapt your approach to the developmental stage: infants need warm hands, soothing voice, and minimal separation from caregivers; toddlers need play, choices, and honest but simple explanations; preschoolers need magical thinking addressed; school-age children need to understand the "why"; adolescents need to be treated as emerging adults with autonomy. A nurse who approaches every child the same way will miss pain, frighten the patient, and lose cooperation.

### Recognize Pediatric-Specific Deterioration Patterns

Children deteriorate differently than adults, and the trajectory is often respiratory first, then cardiovascular. The key early signs are increased work of breathing (retractions, nasal flaring, grunting, head bobbing), altered mental status (irritability, lethargy, poor interaction), and poor skin perfusion (pallor, mottling, prolonged capillary refill). The trap is that a child who is "compensating" — maintaining oxygenation through increased effort — looks stable until they tire and crash. Use the Pediatric Early Warning Score (PEWS) systematically, and escalate any sustained upward trend, not just threshold crossings. A child who needs 40% more work to breathe than an hour ago is deteriorating, even if the SpO2 is still 95%.

### Calculate Every Medication Dose by Weight and Use Independent Double Checks

Pediatric medication errors are among the most common and most dangerous sentinel events, and the root cause is almost always weight-based dosing gone wrong. Weigh every child in kilograms (not pounds), document the weight in a single location, and calculate every dose using mg/kg (or mg/kg/day divided appropriately). Use independent double checks for high-alert medications (insulin, heparin, opioids, chemotherapy, IV fluids with additives). Know the maximum adult dose — a 100 kg adolescent does not need a pediatric dose. When a dose looks unusual, stop and verify before administering. The nurse who catches a decimal point error (e.g., 10 mg instead of 1.0 mg) saves a life.

### Engage Parents as Essential Partners While Maintaining Clinical Judgment

Parents are not visitors in pediatric nursing — they are co-caregivers, historians, and advocates who know their child's baseline better than anyone. Involve them in assessment (what is the child's normal behavior, eating, activity?), in comfort measures, and in decision-making. At the same time, maintain clinical independence: a parent's anxiety or wish does not override your assessment. If a parent says "something is wrong" and your assessment is reassuring, take that seriously — re-examine, trend the vitals, and consider observation. If a parent insists on a treatment that is not indicated, explain your reasoning and involve the provider. Document parent interactions factually, especially when there is disagreement.

### Adapt Procedures to the Child and Use Comfort Measures Procedurally

Pediatric procedures — IV starts, blood draws, catheterization, wound care — are harder and more traumatic than in adults, and the way they are done shapes the child's relationship with healthcare for life. Use non-pharmacologic comfort measures (positioning for comfort, sucrose for infants, distraction, child life specialists) and pharmacologic measures (topical anesthetics, nitrous, minimal sedation) appropriately. Prepare the child honestly at a developmentally appropriate level, offer choices when possible, and never lie ("this won't hurt"). Allow a parent to stay if it helps the child. After the procedure, comfort and debrief. The goal is not just to complete the procedure but to do it in a way that does not create lasting trauma.

### Maintain Safeguards Against Pediatric Abuse and Neglect

The nurse is often the first to notice signs of child abuse or neglect, and the responsibility to recognize and report is both clinical and legal. Know the sentinel signs: injuries inconsistent with the history or developmental stage, patterned bruises (hand, belt), bruises in non-mobile infants, delayed presentation, inconsistent histories between caregivers, and signs of neglect (failure to thrive, untreated medical conditions). Document factually and objectively. Report suspected abuse per mandatory reporting laws — you do not need proof, only reasonable suspicion, and the report is protective, not accusatory. The nurse who hesitates because they are "not sure" may miss the window to protect a child.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Child as a Miniature Adult

The trap is that adult nursing is the default mental model, and the nurse applies adult vital sign ranges, adult medication doses (or weight-based doses without the weight), and adult communication expectations to a child. The harm is missed deterioration (a "normal" blood pressure that is actually shock in a child), medication overdose, and a frightened, uncooperative patient. Every pediatric encounter starts with recalibrating to the age: what are the normal vitals, what is the appropriate dose, how do I communicate, what is the developmental task? When in doubt, look it up — do not extrapolate from adult practice.

### Reassuring Yourself with a Normal Blood Pressure

The trap is that blood pressure is the most familiar vital sign, and a normal number feels reassuring, so the nurse underestimates the severity of illness in a child whose blood pressure is maintained through compensation. The harm is delayed recognition of shock. In children, blood pressure is a late sign — by the time it drops, the child has exhausted their compensatory mechanisms and is close to arrest. The early signs are tachycardia, tachypnea, altered mental status, and poor perfusion. Treat these as urgent even when the blood pressure is normal.

### Letting Parental Anxiety Override Clinical Assessment

The trap works in both directions. A calm, low-anxiety parent may underreport a child's distress, leading the nurse to underestimate severity. A highly anxious parent may demand interventions that are not indicated, and the nurse, wanting to be responsive, complies. The harm is either missed illness or unnecessary, potentially harmful intervention. Anchor your decisions in the clinical assessment: examine the child, trend the vitals, use the scoring tools. Use the parent's report as one important data point, not the only one. Explain your reasoning, document it, and involve the provider when there is genuine uncertainty.

### Lying to Gain Cooperation for a Procedure

The trap is that honesty ("this will hurt") feels like it will reduce cooperation, so the nurse says "this won't hurt" to get through the procedure. The harm is twofold: the child learns that nurses lie, destroying trust for all future care, and the betrayal compounds the trauma of the procedure itself. Children are remarkably resilient when prepared honestly. Tell them what they will feel, use comfort measures, offer choices (which arm, who stays), and follow through. A child who is lied to once will fight every subsequent procedure.

### Missing the Signs of Abuse Because the Explanation "Makes Sense"

The trap is that an injury with a plausible explanation (a fall down stairs, a sibling fight) is accepted at face value, especially when the caregiver is articulate and the nurse wants to believe the best. The harm is that abused children are often brought in repeatedly with escalating injuries, and each missed opportunity allows the abuse to continue. The pattern matters more than any single injury: bruising in a non-mobile infant, injuries at different healing stages, injuries inconsistent with the mechanism described, and a history that changes over time. Document objectively, share your concern with the provider, and report per law. You are not accusing anyone — you are activating a system designed to investigate and protect.

## Self-Check

- Am I using age-specific vital sign ranges, or am I applying adult norms to this child?
- Is this child's deterioration pattern being recognized early — work of breathing, perfusion, mental status — or am I waiting for the blood pressure to drop?
- For every medication I am giving, was the dose calculated on the current weight in kilograms, and has a high-alert dose been independently double-checked?
- Am I using a developmentally appropriate pain scale and communication style for this child's age?
- Have I involved the parents in assessment and care while maintaining my own clinical judgment?
- For procedures, have I prepared the child honestly and used appropriate comfort measures?
- Have I considered whether the injuries or presentation are consistent with the history, and is there a pattern that warrants reporting?
- If a parent says "something is wrong," have I taken that seriously and re-examined, even if my initial assessment was reassuring?
- Have I documented objectively and factually, especially regarding any concerns about the history or the family interaction?
- Am I recalibrating my mental model to this child's age, or am I defaulting to adult practice?
