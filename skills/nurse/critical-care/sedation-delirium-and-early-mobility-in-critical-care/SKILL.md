# Sedation, Delirium, And Early Mobility In Critical Care

For decades the ICU was run on the assumption that deeply sedated, immobile patients were safer and more comfortable, and the assumption produced a generation of survivors with profound deconditioning, post-intensive-care syndrome, and delirium-related cognitive impairment that persisted long after discharge. The judgment problem is that the three domains — sedation, delirium, and mobility — are not separate problems but a single interlocking system: deeper sedation causes delirium, delirium prevents mobilization, immobility causes deconditioning and more delirium, and the cycle accelerates unless it is actively broken every day. The skilled ICU nurse treats light sedation as the default unless a specific indication requires depth, screens for delirium every shift with a validated tool, treats delirium by removing its causes before reaching for medication, and mobilizes patients — including intubated patients — far earlier than intuition suggests, because the harm of bed rest begins within days and the benefit of early mobility is among the most evidence-supported interventions in critical care. The recurring failures are over-sedating for staff convenience, attributing delirium to the ICU environment rather than treating its causes, chemically sedating delirium that is better treated by removal of precipitants, and deferring mobility because the patient is "too sick" when the patient is sick in part because they are immobile.

This is a high-stakes domain. The nurse's role is to titrate sedation to the lightest effective level, to screen and address delirium, to coordinate and perform early mobility safely, and to advocate for the bundle — SAT, SBT, delirium screening, mobility — as a daily standard rather than an aspiration.

## Core Rules

### Default To Light Sedation And Define The Indication For Depth

Deep sedation may be required for specific indications — intracranial pressure control, severe ARDS with ventilator dyssynchrony, status epilepticus, profound hypoxemia, neuromuscular blockade — but in the absence of such an indication, light sedation (RASS target 0 to -1, calm and cooperative or lightly sedated) is the default. The default matters because depth is where harm accumulates: each additional day of deep sedation increases delirium risk, ventilator days, and mortality.

- Define the sedation target explicitly and daily, tied to a clinical indication, not to habit.
- Use validated scales (RASS for arousal, Riker SAS or other for agitation) and titrate to target, treating an above-target or below-target score as a signal requiring adjustment.
- Prefer analgesia first (analgesia-first or analgosedation): treat pain before sedation, because much ICU "agitation" is untreated pain, and opioids sedate while relieving pain.
- Avoid benzodiazepines except for specific indications (alcohol withdrawal, status epilepticus), because they are independently associated with delirium.

### Perform Daily Sedation Interruption And Spontaneous Awakening Trials

A daily sedation interruption (SAT) or spontaneous awakening trial, paired with a spontaneous breathing trial (SBT), is among the most evidence-supported practices in critical care: it reduces ventilator days, ICU length of stay, and mortality. The trial is performed daily, on every patient who meets safety criteria, unless there is a specific contraindication.

- Screen for SAT safety first: no active seizure, no agitation requiring escalation, no neuromuscular blockade, no elevated ICP concern, acceptable oxygenation and ventilator settings, no high-dose vasopressor or escalating dose.
- Reduce or stop sedation per protocol and observe; if the patient tolerates the awakening (calm or manageable agitation), proceed to SBT; if they become agitated or physiologically unstable, restart sedation at a lower dose.
- Coordinate SAT and SBT as a paired protocol (the "wake up and breathe" bundle); the awakening is what makes the breathing trial possible.
- Document the trial, the result, and the reason if it was not performed; a trial not done should be the exception with a documented contraindication, not the default.

### Screen For Delirium Every Shift And Treat Causes Before Medication

ICU delirium is common, harmful (associated with longer stay, higher mortality, and long-term cognitive impairment), and under-recognized without routine screening. Screen every shift with a validated tool (CAM-ICU or ICDSC), and when delirium is present, treat the causes before reaching for pharmacology.

- Screen every patient every shift, and treat a positive screen as a clinical finding requiring action, not merely documentation.
- Investigate and remove precipitants: pain, sedation depth (over-sedation and under-sedation both cause delirium), sleep disruption, immobility, infection, hypoxia, metabolic derangement, constipation, urinary retention, medications (especially anticholinergics and benzodiazepines), withdrawal, sensory deprivation (no glasses, no hearing aids), and the ICU environment itself (lights, noise, alarms).
- Implement non-pharmacological prevention and treatment: orientation and reorientation, clocks and calendars, sleep promotion (cluster care, reduce nighttime interruptions, earplugs, eye masks), early mobility, sensory aids returned, family presence, cognitive engagement.
- Reserve antipsychotics for delirium with dangerous agitation that has not responded to cause-directed treatment; antipsychotics do not treat the delirium, they manage the agitation, and routine use does not improve outcomes.

### Mobilize Early, Including Intubated Patients, With A Structured Protocol

Early mobility is among the most evidence-supported interventions in critical care, reducing delirium, ventilator days, and deconditioning, and the threshold for mobilization is far lower than intuition suggests — intubated patients, patients on vasopressors at stable low doses, and patients with lines and devices can and should be mobilized. The harm of bed rest begins within days: muscle loss, skin breakdown, thromboembolism, atelectasis, and delirium.

- Use a structured mobility protocol with levels (in-bed range of motion, sitting on edge of bed, chair transfer, standing, walking) and daily progression to the highest level the patient can safely achieve.
- Screen for mobility safety first: stable oxygenation and ventilator settings, stable or low-dose vasopressors, no unstable fractures, no active bleeding, adequate sedation (RASS -1 or better for participation), secured lines and tubes.
- Coordinate with respiratory therapy and physical therapy; secure the endotracheal tube, lines, and monitors before moving; have a plan for accidental extubation or line dislodgement.
- Document the level achieved and the barriers; treat "not mobilized today" as an outcome requiring explanation, not a default.

### Treat The Bundle As A Daily Standard, Not Three Separate Problems

The ABCDEF bundle — Assess and manage pain, Both SAT and SBT daily, Choice of sedation, Delirium monitoring, Early mobility, Family engagement — works because the elements are synergistic: pain control reduces agitation, daily awakening enables breathing trials, light sedation reduces delirium, delirium reduction enables mobility, mobility reduces delirium and deconditioning. Treating any element in isolation is less effective than treating the bundle, and omitting one element undermines the others.

- Perform the bundle elements daily, as a standard, on every patient who is eligible.
- When an element is not performed, document the specific contraindication; chronic non-performance of the bundle for "this patient is too sick" is often the signal that the bundle is most needed.

### Engage Family And Preserve Orientation And Sleep

The ICU environment is deliriogenic by design: lights, noise, alarms, disrupted sleep, absence of day-night cycle, absence of familiar faces. Family engagement, sleep protection, and orientation are not comfort measures; they are delirium prevention.

- Encourage family presence and participation in care and mobility, within the unit's framework.
- Protect sleep: cluster nighttime care, reduce unnecessary awakenings, use earplugs and eye masks, maintain a day-night lighting pattern.
- Provide orientation cues: clocks, calendars, windows, reorientation at each interaction, sensory aids returned.

## Common Traps

### Over-Sedating For Staff Convenience Or Apparent Comfort

A patient is sedated more deeply than their indication requires because they are calmer, easier to manage, and less likely to pull at lines, and the deeper sedation is rationalized as comfort when its actual function is staffing convenience, producing more delirium, more ventilator days, and a harder, longer recovery. The mechanism is that deep sedation reduces the immediate nursing workload, so it is maintained beyond its indication and titrated upward when the patient stirs, and the light-sedation target is documented without being achieved. The harm is prolonged ventilation, ICU-acquired delirium and weakness, longer stay, and the long-term cognitive impairment that follows ICU delirium, all inflicted to reduce the acute staffing burden of a lighter, more interactive patient. The defense is to define the sedation indication and target daily, to titrate to the lightest effective level, to perform daily SATs unless contraindicated, and to recognize that a patient sedated for convenience is a patient being harmed to solve a staffing problem.

### Treating Delirium With Medication Before Removing Precipitants

A delirious patient receives an antipsychotic to control agitation, while the precipitants — untreated pain, over-sedation, urinary retention, constipation, infection, sleep disruption, benzodiazepine use, sensory deprivation — remain in place, and the delirium persists or worsens under the medication, because the antipsychotic managed the agitation without addressing the cause. The mechanism is that the agitation is the visible problem and the medication is the visible solution, so the cause-directed investigation is skipped, and the delirium-driving factors continue to operate beneath the calmer surface. The harm is prolonged delirium, the side effects of antipsychotics (QT prolongation, oversedation, extrapyramidal effects), and the attribution of the persistent delirium to the patient's condition rather than to the unaddressed precipitants. The defense is to screen for delirium every shift, to investigate and remove precipitants before or alongside any pharmacological management, to reserve antipsychotics for dangerous agitation unresponsive to cause-directed treatment, and to recognize that antipsychotics manage agitation, not delirium, while the causes of the delirium continue to harm the patient until they are removed.

### Deferring Mobility Because The Patient Is "Too Sick"

An intubated patient on a low-dose stable vasopressor is kept in bed because they are "too sick" to mobilize, when they meet the safety criteria for at least in-bed or sitting-edge-of-bed mobility, and the immobility contributes to the very deconditioning, atelectasis, and delirium that are prolonging their illness. The mechanism is that the sight of an intubated patient with lines generates an intuitive sense that movement is dangerous, so the patient is deferred day after day, and the threshold for mobilization drifts upward until only the nearly-discharged are mobilized. The harm is the rapid muscle loss, skin breakdown, thromboembolism, and delirium of prolonged bed rest, inflicted on the patients who can least tolerate it, and a recovery trajectory made longer and less complete by the deconditioning that immobility caused. The defense is to use a structured mobility protocol with explicit safety screening, to mobilize to the highest safe level daily including intubated and vasopressor-supported patients who meet criteria, to coordinate with respiratory and physical therapy, and to recognize that "too sick to mobilize" is often the signal that mobilization is most needed, because the patient is sick in part because they are immobile.

### Skipping The Daily SAT-SBT Because Of A Transient Concern

A patient who meets SAT-SBT safety criteria does not receive the daily trial because the nurse is busy, the respiratory therapist is occupied, or a transient vital-sign concern led to deferral "for today," and the deferral becomes the pattern, extending ventilation by days and increasing delirium and mortality risk. The mechanism is that the daily trial requires coordination and carries a small perceived risk, so it is deferred under time pressure or transient concern, and the deferral, repeated daily, accumulates into prolonged ventilation that the trial was designed to shorten. The harm is avoidable ventilator days, ICU days, and mortality, because the SAT-SBT is among the few interventions with demonstrated mortality benefit in critical care. The defense is to treat the paired SAT-SBT as a daily standard performed unless a specific documented contraindication exists, to build it into the unit workflow so it is the default rather than an add-on, and to recognize that deferring the trial "for today" is a decision with measurable harm that compounds when it becomes routine.

### Accepting The ICU Environment As Inevitable Rather Than Deliriogenic

The lights, alarms, frequent nighttime awakenings, absence of day-night cycle, and absence of familiar faces are accepted as the unavoidable ICU environment, when each is a modifiable delirium precipitant that non-pharmacological interventions can reduce, and the untreated environment contributes to the delirium that is then managed with medication. The mechanism is that the environment is experienced as fixed, so the delirium it produces is attributed to the patient's illness, and the modifiable factors — sleep disruption, sensory deprivation, disorientation — are not addressed. The harm is delirium that was preventable through environmental management, prolonged by the assumption that the environment cannot be changed. The defense is to treat the ICU environment as a set of modifiable delirium precipitants: cluster nighttime care, protect sleep with earplugs and eye masks, maintain day-night lighting, return sensory aids, provide orientation cues, and encourage family presence, because these are delirium prevention interventions, not comfort measures.
