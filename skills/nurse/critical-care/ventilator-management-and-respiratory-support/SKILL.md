# Ventilator Management and Respiratory Support

Mechanical ventilation is one of the highest-risk interventions in nursing. The ventilator sustains life, but every setting, alarm, and adjustment involves tradeoffs between oxygenation, ventilation, lung-protective strategy, and patient comfort. The judgment problem is that ventilated patients cannot communicate their distress verbally, the machine generates alarms that range from trivial to life-threatening, and a disconnection or misconfiguration can cause harm within seconds. The nurse must understand the ventilator as deeply as the respiratory therapist, advocate for lung-protective strategies, and recognize the moment a "stable" ventilated patient is actually deteriorating.

## Core Rules

### Know the Ventilator Mode, Settings, and Rationale Before Touching the Machine

Never accept a ventilated patient at handoff without understanding the mode (volume control, pressure control, pressure support, APRV, etc.), the key settings (tidal volume, respiratory rate, PEEP, FiO2, pressure support), and the clinical rationale for each. Know why the patient is intubated, what the oxygenation and ventilation goals are, and what the plan for liberation is. If you do not understand a setting, ask the respiratory therapist and the provider before the next round — not during a crisis. A nurse who cannot explain why PEEP is set at 10 cannot recognize when it is inappropriate.

### Apply Lung-Protective Ventilation Principles and Advocate for Them

In patients with ARDS or at risk for it, lung-protective ventilation (low tidal volume 6 mL/kg predicted body weight, plateau pressure < 30 cmH2O, appropriate PEEP) reduces mortality. This is one of the few interventions in critical care with Level 1 evidence. The trap is that these settings can produce hypercapnia and an uncomfortable patient, tempting the team to increase tidal volume for comfort. Advocate for the protective strategy, tolerate permissive hypercapnia (within reason), and address comfort with sedation and analgesia rather than larger breaths. Know the patient's predicted body weight — actual weight is wrong for this calculation.

### Classify and Respond to Ventilator Alarms by Severity

Ventilator alarms fall into categories: high-priority (apnea, disconnect, loss of circuit integrity, extreme pressure), medium-priority (high or low tidal volume, high or low respiratory rate, low minute ventilation), and low-priority (inspired temperature, FiO2 deviation). The first action on any high-priority alarm is to look at the patient, not the machine — a disconnect can kill in minutes, and a mucus plug can cause a tension pneumothorax if unrecognized. Have a systematic approach: assess the patient first, then check the circuit for disconnection or kinking, then check the settings, then assess for changes in lung compliance (auto-PEEP, bronchospasm, pneumothorax). Never silence an alarm without identifying and addressing the cause.

### Prevent Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia with Bundle Compliance

Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) significantly increases mortality, length of stay, and cost. The VAP prevention bundle — head of bed elevation 30-45 degrees, daily sedation interruption and spontaneous breathing trials, peptic ulcer disease prophylaxis, deep vein thrombosis prophylaxis, oral care with chlorhexidine, and subglottic suctioning for appropriate patients — is evidence-based and effective when applied consistently. The trap is that each element is simple individually, so individual lapses feel minor, but bundle compliance is a system property that degrades quickly without daily attention. Audit your own compliance every shift: is the head of bed actually elevated, not just charted as elevated?

### Manage Sedation and Analgesia to Enable Liberation

Over-sedation is one of the biggest barriers to ventilator liberation and is associated with delirium, prolonged ventilation, and worse outcomes. Practice analgesia-first sedation (pain before sedation), use validated monitoring (CPOT, RASS), and conduct daily sedation interruptions and spontaneous breathing trials per protocol. The goal is a calm, comfortable patient who can trigger breaths and participate in care, not a deeply sedated patient who is "easy to manage." Coordinate with the team to define the sedation target each day and reassess it every few hours. A patient who has been deeply sedated for days will need a structured wean, not an abrupt stop.

### Recognize and Respond to Auto-PEEP (Breath Stacking)

In patients with obstructive lung disease (COPD, asthma) or high respiratory drive, incomplete exhalation leads to air trapping and intrinsic PEEP (auto-PEEP), which can cause hypotension, barotrauma, and cardiac arrest. The signs are a patient who cannot exhale fully, a ventilator that shows the expiratory flow has not returned to baseline before the next breath, and hypotension that worsens with each delivered breath. The response is to disconnect the circuit briefly to allow full exhalation, then adjust the ventilator (lower rate, shorter inspiratory time, lower tidal volume) and treat bronchospasm. If you see hypotension in a ventilated COPD patient, think auto-PEEP before you think of other causes.

### Prepare for and Support Extubation

Extubation is a planned procedure, not an afterthought. Confirm the criteria (awake, following commands, adequate gas exchange, manageable secretions, strong cough, low FiO2 and PEEP, successful spontaneous breathing trial), prepare the patient (suction, explain the procedure, position upright), have reintubation equipment at the bedside, and time the extubation when the team is available — not at the end of a shift or overnight unless urgent. After extubation, monitor closely for stridor, respiratory distress, and the need for non-invasive ventilation. The first 30 minutes after extubation are the highest-risk period for reintubation.

## Common Traps

### Increasing FiO2 Instead of Investigating the Cause of Desaturation

The reflexive response to a desaturation alarm is to increase FiO2, and this often works — but it masks the underlying cause. The trap is that the number improves, the alarm stops, and the investigation never happens, so a developing pneumothorax, a mucus plug, a worsening effusion, or a cardiac event goes unrecognized until the patient crashes at a higher FiO2 that can no longer compensate. The harm is delayed diagnosis of a treatable, life-threatening condition. When a patient desaturates, increase FiO2 as a bridge, but immediately investigate the cause: listen to the lungs, check the circuit, review the chest x-ray, assess hemodynamics.

### Confusing Patient-Ventilator Asynchrony with Agitation

A ventilated patient who appears "fighting the ventilator" is often labeled agitated and given more sedation. But the cause is frequently ventilator asynchrony — the machine is delivering breaths that do not match the patient's neural drive, creating a sensation of suffocation or air hunger. The trap is that sedation suppresses the visible behavior without fixing the underlying mismatch, so the patient remains in distress, now too sedated to communicate it, and the team mistakes quietness for improvement. The harm is prolonged ventilation, delirium, and missed diagnosis of a correctable ventilator setting problem. Before sedating an "agitated" ventilated patient, assess for asynchrony: double-triggering, breath-stacking, ineffective efforts, and mismatched flow.

### Treating All Secretions the Same

Not all secretions require the same response. Thin, copious secretions may indicate pulmonary edema and need diuresis, not suctioning. Thick, tenacious secretions may need hydration, mucolytics, or heated humidification. Purulent secretions may indicate VAP and need cultures and antibiotics. Blood-tinged secretions after intubation may be benign, but new hemoptysis may indicate a serious complication. The trap is that "suction prn" becomes a reflexive, undifferentiated response that addresses the symptom without diagnosing the cause. Characterize secretions every shift: color, consistency, volume, and any change from baseline.

### Discontinuing Spontaneous Breathing Trials Too Quickly for "Failure"

A spontaneous breathing trial (SBT) is the best predictor of readiness for extubation, but it can "fail" for reasons unrelated to the patient's true readiness — anxiety, inadequate analgesia, a wet circuit, too-rapid reduction in support, or a single borderline blood gas. The trap is that one failed SBT gets charted as "not ready," and the patient stays intubated another day, accumulating sedation, delirium, and VAP risk. The harm is prolonged ventilation for patients who could have been extubated. When an SBT fails, analyze why: was it true respiratory failure, or was it a correctable factor? Retry the next day with the lesson learned, and advocate for the patient's liberation.

### Forgetting the Patient Is Awake Under the Sedation

The trap is that a sedated, intubated patient is treated as unconscious, so conversations happen over them, procedures are done without explanation, and the experience of being trapped in a body that cannot move or speak is ignored. ICU survivors describe terrifying experiences of awareness during paralysis, pain during procedures, and hallucinations fueled by delirium. The harm is psychological trauma (PTSD rates in ICU survivors are high) and a failure of basic human dignity. Talk to your ventilated patient. Explain what you are doing. Assess pain even if they cannot report it. Treat them as a person who is listening, because they often are.

## Self-Check

- Can I explain the ventilator mode, key settings, and the clinical rationale for each to a colleague right now?
- Is the tidal volume calculated on predicted body weight, and is the plateau pressure within lung-protective range?
- When did I last lay eyes on the patient (not just the monitor) and assess for asynchrony, distress, or comfort?
- For the last ventilator alarm I addressed, did I identify the cause before silencing it?
- Is the VAP bundle fully compliant — head of bed actually elevated, oral care done, sedation interrupted today, SBT attempted?
- When the patient desaturated, did I investigate the cause, or did I just increase FiO2?
- Have I assessed for auto-PEEP in any patient with obstructive disease or unexplained hypotension?
- What is today's sedation target (RASS), and am I trending toward it or maintaining deep sedation out of habit?
- Did I explain procedures and talk to the patient as if they could hear me, even while sedated?
- If this patient were extubated today, is the equipment ready and is the team available for the first 30 minutes?
