# Forensic Evidence Preservation And Chain Of Custody

The judgment problem in forensic nursing is that the clinical instinct to prioritize immediate treatment can silently destroy the very evidence that a future legal case depends on, while the legal instinct to preserve evidence can make a traumatized patient feel like evidence rather than a person. The nurse is often the first — and sometimes only — person to document injuries, collect bodily fluids, photograph wounds, and package clothing before showers, bathroom use, wound cleaning, or surgical prep irreversibly alter or destroy what cannot be recovered. A single break in chain of custody, an unlabeled swab, a photograph without a scale, or a kit left unattended for a moment can render evidence inadmissible. Yet the patient is not a crime scene; they are a person in crisis who must consent, who may decline, and whose autonomy and dignity must be protected even when the legal stakes are high.

This is a high-stakes domain. Mishandled evidence can prevent prosecution of a violent crime or, equally serious, can contribute to a wrongful conviction if evidence is contaminated or misattributed. Chain-of-custody errors are rarely caught at the time; they surface months or years later in court, when the nurse may not remember and the record is the only witness.

## Core Rules

### Consent comes before collection, always

Forensic evidence collection requires informed consent from a patient who has capacity — and the patient may decline any or all of it. Obtain consent specific to each specimen and each procedure, and document the consent and any refusals explicitly. A patient who has experienced assault may feel re-traumatized by the examination; explain each step, offer a support advocate, allow stops, and never pressure collection because "the evidence will be lost." Declined collection is documented as declined, not worked around. For patients who lack capacity or are minors, follow jurisdictional rules for surrogate consent, mandated reporting, and court orders — these rules vary and must be verified, not assumed.

### Preserve evidence before it is destroyed by routine care

The irreversible loss of forensic evidence usually happens during ordinary clinical care, not through deliberate mishandling. Before the patient showers, bathes, urinates, defecates, changes clothes, brushes teeth, eats, drinks, smokes, or has wounds cleaned or dressed, recognize that each of these acts can destroy trace evidence, bodily fluids, fibers, and debris. When clinically safe to do so, collect or offer to collect before these activities. Do not remove clothing by cutting through existing stains, tears, or defects — cut around them. Place a clean sheet or barrier down before the patient undresses so that trace evidence falling from clothing is caught rather than lost to the floor. Bag the patient's hands or have them preserve fingernail scrapings before hand hygiene.

### Maintain chain of custody without interruption

Chain of custody is the unbroken, documented record of every person who touched the evidence, from collection through final disposition. Each transfer must be documented with the name of the person releasing and receiving, the date and time, and ideally a signature. Evidence must never be left unattended; if you must step away, hand it to another responsible person and document the handoff, or lock it in a designated secure storage. A gap — evidence on a counter with no one watching, a kit transferred without a signature, a swab handed to someone whose name was not recorded — is a break that opposing counsel will exploit to argue the evidence may have been tampered with or misattributed. Treat every specimen as if it will be challenged in court, because it may be.

### Label at the point of collection, not later

Every container, swab, envelope, and bag must be labeled with the patient identifiers, the date and time of collection, the anatomical site or source, the collector's name, and the case number — at the moment of collection, before the item leaves your hands. Labeling "later" is the most common cause of mislabeling, because two identical swabs become indistinguishable once separated from context. Use the forensic kit's own labels and seals where provided. Seal each container with evidence tape or the kit's seal and initial or sign across the seal so that tampering is visible.

### Document injuries with the precision a court will demand

Wound and injury documentation for forensic purposes requires more detail than routine clinical notes. Describe each injury by type (abrasion, contusion, laceration, incision, bite, burn, pattern injury), exact anatomical location, size measured with a ruler, shape, color, borders, and any pattern (bite marks, ligature marks, defensive wounds). Note the stage of healing, which can help establish timing relative to a reported event. Photographs should include an identifying image of the patient, a wide shot showing anatomical context, and close-ups with and without a scale (ruler or coin) and, where relevant, with and without alternate light sources. Document in objective, descriptive language — what you observe — never interpret causation ("this wound is consistent with a belt buckle") unless you have specific forensic training and the jurisdiction permits expert interpretation. Use the patient's own words for history, in quotation marks, attributed to the patient, not paraphrased into your own conclusion.

### Separate clinical care from evidence collection, but coordinate

Clinical stabilization always takes priority over evidence collection — never delay life-saving treatment to preserve evidence. But the two are not in conflict if planned: alert the team that forensic evidence is at stake so that clothing is bagged rather than discarded during resuscitation, so that lines and tubes are placed with awareness of injury patterns, and so that surgical prep does not destroy evidence when clinically safe to document first. Communicate with law enforcement through proper channels, but do not let investigative priorities override clinical judgment or patient consent.

### Understand mandated reporting and its interaction with consent

In many jurisdictions, certain injuries and presentations (gunshot wounds, stab wounds, suspected abuse of a child or vulnerable adult, suspected domestic violence) trigger mandatory reporting regardless of patient consent. Know which presentations are reportable in your setting, and understand the distinction between reporting (a notification obligation) and evidence collection (which still generally requires consent unless a court order or specific authority applies). Do not assume that a report authorizes collection; do not assume that consent to treatment implies consent to forensic collection. When in doubt, contact the forensic nurse examiner (SANE), supervisor, or legal/risk on-call before acting.

## Common Traps

### Treating the patient as a crime scene rather than a person

When the focus narrows to evidence collection, the patient can experience the examination as a second violation. Rushing through consent, performing steps without explanation, or prioritizing the kit over the patient's immediate emotional and physical needs re-traumatizes and can cause the patient to discontinue the examination entirely — losing both the evidence and the therapeutic relationship. Always center the patient; the evidence follows.

### Assuming consent to treatment equals consent to collection

A patient who consents to a physical examination or to treatment has not thereby consented to forensic specimen collection, photography, or release of records to law enforcement. These are separate consents. Collecting forensic evidence under the umbrella of treatment consent is both an ethical violation and a route to inadmissible evidence.

### Letting routine clinical care destroy evidence before collection

The most common forensic loss is not sabotage but normal care: the patient is sent to shower, clothing is placed in a regular laundry bag, wounds are cleaned before swabbing, IV access is established through a site that destroys an injection-related injury pattern, or teeth are brushed before oral swabs. Build the habit of asking "is there evidence to preserve here?" before any routine step when forensic concerns exist.

### Breaks in chain of custody from "I'll document it in a minute"

The most common chain-of-custody break is the deferral of documentation — the kit is set down, the nurse is pulled to another task, a colleague moves it, and the precise sequence of handling becomes unreconstructable. Document each transfer at the moment it happens. An unattended kit, even briefly, is a break.

### Over-interpreting injuries beyond training

A nurse without forensic examiner training who writes "this pattern is consistent with [specific weapon or mechanism]" is offering an expert opinion that may exceed their qualification and that can be challenged and discredited, potentially undermining the entire record. Describe what you see in objective terms; leave interpretation to those with the training and authority to give it.

### Photographing without identifiers, scale, or context

A close-up of a wound with no scale, no anatomical context, and no patient identifier is nearly useless forensically because it cannot be tied to the patient or to the injury's size and location. Always include a scale, a contextual shot, and follow your institution's protocol for linking images to the patient and the record.

### Disclosing information to law enforcement beyond what is authorized

Eager cooperation with investigators can lead a nurse to share information, records, or specimens beyond what consent, statute, or a court order permits. Confirm the legal basis for each disclosure. A well-intentioned but unauthorized release can taint the evidence and expose the nurse and institution to liability.

## Self-Check

- Did I obtain specific, documented consent for each forensic specimen, photograph, and release of information — separate from consent to treatment?
- Before any routine care step (showering, wound cleaning, clothing change, bathroom use), did I consider whether evidence would be destroyed and collect or offer to collect first?
- Is every container labeled at the point of collection with patient identifiers, date, time, source or anatomical site, collector name, and case number?
- Can I reconstruct an unbroken chain of custody — every person who touched each item, with date, time, and signature — from collection to secure storage?
- Are injuries documented objectively (type, exact location, measured size, shape, color, pattern, healing stage) without causation interpretation beyond my training?
- Do photographs include patient identification, anatomical context, and close-ups with a scale?
- Did I separate my clinical obligations from investigative ones, prioritizing clinical stabilization while still preserving evidence when safe?
- Did I verify which presentations trigger mandatory reporting in my jurisdiction, and did I distinguish reporting obligations from collection consent?
- Did I center the patient's dignity, autonomy, and trauma throughout — offering an advocate, explaining steps, and respecting refusals?
- Did I avoid releasing information, records, or specimens to law enforcement beyond what consent, statute, or a court order authorizes?
