# After-Incident Response and Reporting

The aftermath of a workplace violence incident is when most of the harm is either addressed or compounded, and it is overwhelmingly a nursing-systems responsibility. The judgment problem is that the culture of nursing normalizes violence ("part of the job"), so nurses underreport, minimize their injuries, decline medical evaluation, and return to work without psychological support — and the institution, lacking data and engagement, fails to prevent recurrence. The skilled response after an incident is not optional cleanup; it is a clinical, legal, and psychological intervention that protects the affected nurse, preserves the evidence, generates the data for prevention, and breaks the cycle of tolerance. A nurse who knows how to respond after an incident protects themselves and their colleagues.

## Core Rules

### Ensure Immediate Safety and Medical Evaluation of the Affected Nurse

The first priority after any violent incident is the safety and medical care of the affected nurse and any other victims. Move to a safe location, ensure the assailant is contained or removed, and assess for injuries — including injuries that may not be immediately apparent (concussion from a head strike, musculoskeletal injury from a struggle, occult internal injury). Seek evaluation in the emergency department or employee health for any physical injury, no matter how minor it seems, because delayed symptoms are common and documentation of the injury at the time of the incident is critical for workers' compensation and legal purposes. Do not "tough it out" or return to work without evaluation. The nurse's health is the first concern, not the schedule or the unit's staffing.

### Preserve the Evidence and the Scene

For incidents that may involve legal action, criminal charges, or detailed investigation, evidence preservation matters. If there was a physical assault, preserve any physical evidence (torn clothing, the weapon if safe to recover, photographs of injuries as soon as possible and again as bruises develop), do not clean up the scene until it is documented if there was property damage or a struggle, and write down a contemporaneous factual account of what happened while memory is fresh. Identify witnesses and ask them to document their observations. The contemporaneous record and the physical evidence are far more valuable than reconstruction days later, and they support both the criminal investigation and the institutional response.

### File the Incident Report and the Workers' Compensation Claim Promptly

File the institutional incident report as soon as possible after the incident, with objective, factual detail (what happened, when, where, who was involved, what interventions were taken, the outcome). The incident report is the primary data source for institutional safety improvement and is often required for workers' compensation. File the workers' compensation claim promptly per your institution's process, even for injuries that seem minor, because delayed symptoms and delayed claims are harder to attribute to the incident and may be denied. Notify the charge nurse, the manager, and (for serious incidents) the nursing supervisor and administration. The reporting is not optional or alarmist; it is the mechanism by which the institution recognizes patterns and the nurse accesses benefits.

### Access Psychological Support and Recognize the Psychological Impact

Workplace violence has a significant psychological impact — acute stress reaction, post-traumatic stress symptoms, anxiety, depression, loss of confidence, and intent to leave the profession — and these effects are often more disabling than the physical injuries. Access the employee assistance program, peer support, or counseling as soon as possible after the incident, and recognize that psychological symptoms may emerge days to weeks later. Do not minimize the impact ("it wasn't that bad," "others have it worse"), and do not return to full duties before you are ready. The institution should offer support proactively, but the nurse should also seek it actively. Psychological care after violence is part of the medical response, not a luxury.

### Participate in the Investigation and Root Cause Analysis

Serious incidents trigger an institutional investigation and often a root cause analysis (RCA), and the affected nurse's participation is essential for an accurate understanding and meaningful prevention. Provide a factual, detailed account of the incident, the antecedents (the warning signs, the environmental factors, the staffing, the prior behavior), and the contributing factors. Identify systemic issues (inadequate staffing, poor environmental design, lack of security, gaps in training) rather than blaming individuals. Advocate for concrete, implemented changes (not just policy revisions), and follow up on the recommendations. The RCA is the institution's primary tool for prevention, and the nurse's engagement determines whether it produces real change.

### Address the Patient's Clinical Needs and the Care Plan

If the assailant is a patient, the clinical care plan must address the behavior to protect the staff and the patient. Convene the team to review the behavior, identify the triggers and the underlying cause (delirium, pain, intoxication, psychiatric decompensation), implement a behavior plan (sitter, environmental changes, medication, reassignment to a safer room, security presence), and communicate the plan at handoff so every nurse is aware. Consider whether the patient can be safely managed on the current unit or requires transfer (to a psychiatric unit, a more secure setting). The behavior is a clinical issue requiring a care plan, not just a security issue, and the nurse drives the clinical response.

### Support Colleagues and Contribute to a Culture of Safety

Workplace violence affects the whole team, not just the direct victim, and the response should include peer support for colleagues who witnessed or feel vulnerable after the incident. Debrief as a team (what happened, what worked, what could improve, how is everyone coping), share the lessons learned, and reinforce that violence is not acceptable. Contribute to a culture where reporting is encouraged, support is offered, and prevention is prioritized — and challenge the normalization of violence when you encounter it. A safe workplace is built nurse by nurse, incident by incident, through engagement with the response system.

## Common Traps

### Minimizing the Incident and Not Reporting Because It Was "Minor" or "Part of the Job"

The trap is that the nurse experiences verbal abuse, a grab, or a low-grade assault, and dismisses it as not worth reporting because no serious injury occurred or because "this is what nursing is like." The mechanism of harm is that underreporting hides the true prevalence of violence, prevents the institution from identifying patterns and high-risk situations, denies the nurse access to support and benefits, and reinforces a culture that tolerates violence. The harm is recurring incidents that go unaddressed, missed opportunities for prevention, and unaddressed psychological impact on the nurse. Report every incident, regardless of perceived severity, through the event system.

### Declining Medical Evaluation Because the Injury Seems Minor

The trap is that the nurse was struck or strained but feels "fine," so they decline evaluation and return to work, only to develop significant symptoms hours or days later. The mechanism of harm is that injuries from assault (concussion, musculoskeletal, occult internal) often present late, and without contemporaneous medical documentation linking them to the incident, the workers' compensation claim and the legal case become difficult. The harm is untreated injury and denied benefits. Seek medical evaluation for any physical contact, no matter how minor, document the injuries, and follow up if symptoms develop.

### Failing to Document a Contemporaneous Account While Memory Is Fresh

The trap is that the nurse intends to write up the incident later, after the shift, after things settle, and by then the details have blurred and the account is less accurate. The mechanism of harm is that contemporaneous documentation is far more credible and useful than reconstruction, and delayed accounts miss details (exact words, sequence, witnesses) that matter for the investigation and any legal action. The harm is a weaker case for prevention, for workers' compensation, and for any criminal or civil action. Write a factual account as soon as possible after the incident, while memory is fresh, and identify witnesses to do the same.

### Not Accessing Psychological Support Due to Stigma or Minimization

The trap is that the nurse feels they should be able to "handle it," views psychological support as a sign of weakness, or minimizes the impact, and so does not access the employee assistance program or counseling. The mechanism of harm is that workplace violence commonly causes acute stress and post-traumatic symptoms that, untreated, persist and worsen, affecting the nurse's health, work, and life. The harm is prolonged psychological suffering, burnout, and attrition from the profession. Access psychological support proactively after any violent incident, recognize that the impact is real and not a weakness, and encourage colleagues to do the same.

### Accepting "Policy Revision" as the Outcome of the Investigation Without Concrete Change

The trap is that the institutional investigation concludes with a revised policy or a reminder memo, and the nurse accepts this as the outcome, even though the underlying systemic issues (staffing, environment, security, training) remain unchanged. The mechanism of harm is that policy revision without implementation does not prevent recurrence, and the same factors that contributed to the incident persist, putting the next nurse at risk. The harm is a recurring incident that the investigation was supposed to prevent. Advocate for concrete, implemented changes (staffing adjustments, environmental redesign, security presence, training), follow up on the recommendations, and escalate if the changes are not made.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- After the incident, did I move to safety, ensure the assailant was contained, and seek medical evaluation for any injury, however minor?
- Did I preserve the evidence (photographs of injuries, contemporaneous written account, witness identification, physical evidence)?
- Did I file the institutional incident report and the workers' compensation claim promptly, with objective factual detail?
- Did I access psychological support (employee assistance, peer support, counseling), and am I monitoring for delayed symptoms?
- Did I participate fully in the investigation and root cause analysis, identifying systemic contributors and advocating for concrete change?
- If the assailant was a patient, did the team implement a behavior care plan and communicate it at handoff?
- Did I seek support for colleagues who witnessed or feel vulnerable, and debrief the team on the event?
- Did I document the incident objectively, both for the clinical record (if relevant to patient care) and for the institutional report?
- Am I following up on the institutional response to ensure concrete changes are implemented, not just policy revisions?
- Did I treat the incident as unacceptable and contribute to a culture of reporting and prevention, rather than normalization?
