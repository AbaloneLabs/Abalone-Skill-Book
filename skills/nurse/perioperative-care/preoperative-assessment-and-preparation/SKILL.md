# Preoperative Assessment and Preparation

The preoperative period is where most surgical complications are either prevented or set in motion. The nurse's assessment at this stage — of consent, NPO status, medications, allergies, airway, comorbidities, and the patient's understanding — determines whether the patient arrives in the OR safe, optimized, and correctly prepared, or whether critical issues are discovered only after anesthesia has begun. The judgment is in the details that are easy to check off without truly verifying: the consent that is signed but for the wrong side, the NPO status that is assumed but not confirmed, the chronic medication held when it should have been given, the abnormal lab that was noticed but not escalated. Each gap is a latent error waiting for the OR.

## Core Rules

### Verify Identity, Procedure, Site, and Consent — Independently and Specifically

Confirm the patient's identity with two identifiers. Verify the consent form is signed, that it matches the scheduled procedure exactly, that the surgical site is marked correctly (laterality verified with the patient awake), and that the patient understands the procedure, its alternatives, risks, and what to expect. A consent for "right inguinal hernia repair" when the OR schedule says "left" is a stop-the-line finding. Do not assume the surgeon has verified; the nurse's independent check is a required layer.

### Confirm NPO Status With Precision, Not Assumption

Ask specifically what the patient last ate or drank, and when. "Nothing since midnight" is not the same as "clear liquids until two hours ago, light meal six hours ago." Confirm compliance with the facility's NPO guidelines (commonly 2 hours for clear liquids, 6 hours for light meals, 8 hours for fatty meals). Investigate any deviation — a snack, gum, chewing tobacco, or a missed instruction — and escalate, because aspiration risk under anesthesia is significant and NPO violation may require delay or modified anesthesia technique.

### Reconcile Medications: What to Give, What to Hold, What to Adjust

Review the full medication list and the preoperative instructions. Some medications must be given on the day of surgery (cardiac medications, beta-blockers, anti-seizure medications, immunosuppressants, some psychiatric medications, pulmonary medications) with sips of water. Some must be held (oral hypoglycemics, some anticoagulants per protocol, diuretics). Some require bridging or dose adjustment (insulin, anticoagulants). Confirm anticoagulant and antiplatelet management explicitly with the surgeon and anesthesiologist, as errors cause catastrophic bleeding or thrombosis.

### Assess and Escalate Anesthesia and Comorbidity Risk Factors

Assess and document: airway (Mallampati, neck mobility, dentition, prior intubation difficulty), cardiovascular status (recent MI, heart failure, valvular disease, pacemaker/ICD that may need reprogramming), respiratory status (COPD, OSA, recent URI, smoking), metabolic status (diabetes control, renal/hepatic function, electrolytes), and pregnancy status where applicable. Abnormalities — new murmur, uncontrolled hypertension, recent chest infection, abnormal labs — must be escalated to anesthesia and surgery, not noted and passed along.

### Review Labs, Imaging, ECG, and Studies Against the Patient, Not Just the Chart

Confirm that ordered preoperative studies are completed and that the results are reviewed and acted upon. An abnormal potassium, an unrecognized ECG change, a critical hemoglobin, or an incidental finding on imaging must be surfaced to the team before the patient goes to the OR. Do not assume "the team saw it" — confirm explicitly.

### Assess and Manage the Patient's Understanding and Anxiety

Verify the patient understands the procedure (teach-back), what to expect before and after, and their role in recovery (deep breathing, ambulation). Assess anxiety and provide information, presence, and premedication if ordered. An anxious, unprepared patient has worse perioperative outcomes and satisfaction.

### Complete the Surgical Safety Checklist Components in Your Domain

The preoperative phase of the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist (or facility equivalent) depends on nursing verification: site marking, consent, equipment, allergies, airway/aspiration risk, and blood availability. Complete these thoroughly; they are not paperwork, they are the safety net.

## Common Traps

**Checking the consent box without reading it against the schedule.** The consent is present, signed, and in the chart, so the nurse verifies "consent obtained" and moves on. The mechanism of harm is that consents are occasionally for the wrong procedure, wrong side, or wrong patient due to upstream errors, and a checkbox verification that does not compare the consent text to the OR schedule and to the marked site propagates the error into the OR. The harm is wrong-site or wrong-procedure surgery — a catastrophic, never-event harm. Read the consent against the schedule and the marked site, with the patient confirming, every time.

**Assuming NPO status rather than interrogating it.** The chart says "NPO since midnight," and the nurse records it without asking the patient directly what they consumed. The mechanism of harm is that patients misunderstand instructions, sneak a snack, chew gum, or have coffee, and an unverified NPO status leads to aspiration under anesthesia — a leading cause of anesthesia morbidity and mortality. The harm is pulmonary aspiration, pneumonitis, and potentially fatal airway emergency. Ask specifically, believe the patient over the chart, and escalate any discrepancy.

**Holding all medications reflexively because the patient is NPO.** The "NPO" instruction is sometimes overgeneralized to all oral intake including essential medications. The mechanism of harm is that critical cardiac, antiseizure, antihypertensive, or psychiatric medications are withheld, and the patient arrives in the OR with uncontrolled hypertension, arrhythmia, seizure risk, or withdrawal. The harm is intraoperative instability, stroke, seizure, or delirium tremens. Clarify which medications to give with sips of water and which to hold; never apply a blanket hold.

**Noticing an abnormal lab or ECG and assuming the team will catch it.** A preoperative potassium of 3.0 or a new ECG with T-wave inversion is documented, but the nurse assumes the surgeon or anesthesiologist will review and act. The mechanism of harm is that in a busy preoperative flow, abnormal results are missed or seen late, and the patient is anesthetized with an uncorrected risk that causes arrhythmia, cardiac ischemia, or other crisis under anesthesia. The harm is preventable intraoperative catastrophe. Escalate abnormal results explicitly and obtain a response; do not rely on passive chart review by others.

**Failing to verify and communicate pacemaker/ICD status.** A patient with a pacemaker or ICD is scheduled for surgery, and the device is noted but not specifically evaluated for perioperative management. The mechanism of harm is that electrocautery can inhibit pacemakers, reprogram devices, or inappropriately trigger ICD shocks during surgery, causing intraoperative arrhythmia or asystole. The harm is a device-related intraoperative cardiac emergency. Identify the device, confirm its function and settings with cardiology/device clinic, and ensure the anesthesia and surgical teams know the plan (reprogramming, magnet use, or post-op re-interrogation).

**Overlooking the edge case or exception.** The mechanism of harm is that the agent analyzes the typical scenario thoroughly but skips the unusual case. The false signal is that the common path is well-handled; in reality the exception silently produces the wrong outcome because the boundary was never tested. The harm is a failure that only appears in the case nobody checked. The fix is to always ask what the edge case is and whether the conclusion still holds there.

**Treating a confident conclusion as a substitute for evidence.** The mechanism of harm is that a well-written, confident-sounding conclusion is accepted as proof. The false signal is the tone of certainty; in reality it masks unstated assumptions, missing verification, or unresolved uncertainty. The harm is that the confident framing prevents anyone from asking the question that would expose the gap. The fix is to treat every conclusion as a claim that must be backed by stated evidence, not by its phrasing.

## Self-Check

- Did I verify patient identity with two identifiers and confirm the consent matches the scheduled procedure and marked site, with the patient confirming?
- Did I interrogate NPO status specifically (what, when) rather than assuming, and escalate any deviation?
- Did I reconcile medications — what to give, hold, bridge, or adjust — with explicit attention to anticoagulants, insulin, and cardiac drugs?
- Did I assess and document airway, cardiovascular, respiratory, metabolic, and pregnancy risk factors?
- Did I review and escalate abnormal labs, ECG, and imaging findings explicitly to obtain a response?
- Did I verify pacemaker/ICD status and communicate the perioperative plan to anesthesia and surgery?
- Did I confirm the patient's understanding of the procedure and recovery expectations (teach-back)?
- Did I complete my domain of the surgical safety checklist (site marking, consent, equipment, allergies, airway/aspiration risk, blood availability)?
- Did I assess and address patient anxiety with information, presence, and premedication if ordered?
- Did I escalate any concern that the patient is not optimized or prepared, rather than passing it along passively?
