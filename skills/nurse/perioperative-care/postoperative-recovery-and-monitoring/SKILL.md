# Postoperative Recovery and Monitoring

The immediate postoperative period (PACU or post-anesthesia phase) is a high-acuity window in which the residual effects of anesthesia, surgical bleeding, airway compromise, and hemodynamic instability all converge. The patient is emerging from a controlled pharmacologic state, cannot fully protect their own airway, and may deteriorate rapidly from causes that are time-sensitive: airway obstruction, bleeding, hypothermia, pain, nausea, and oliguria. The nurse's job is structured, frequent assessment against recovery criteria, early recognition of deviation, and decisive escalation. The danger is not in missing the obvious — it is in being reassured by "normal-looking" vitals while a subtle sign (rising heart rate, mild anxiety, slightly increased drain output) signals the start of a serious complication.

## Core Rules

### Structured, Frequent Assessment on a Defined Schedule

Assess at the frequency dictated by the phase of recovery (commonly q15 minutes in early PACU, extending as the patient stabilizes). At each assessment, capture the full set: airway patency, respiratory rate and depth, SpO2, heart rate, blood pressure, temperature, level of consciousness, pain score, nausea, surgical site and dressings, drain output, IV access, and urine output. Trend the data; a single value is less informative than the trajectory.

### Airway and Breathing: The First Priority

Residual anesthesia, opioids, and residual muscle relaxation cause airway obstruction and hypoventilation. Position the patient to maintain airway patency (recovery position if unprotected), monitor respiratory rate and depth (not just SpO2), and have airway equipment and reversal agents available. Snoring, stridor, retractions, paradoxical breathing, or decreasing respiratory rate are urgent. Treat hypoventilation from opioids with naloxone titrated to respiratory rate; treat residual neuromuscular blockade per anesthesia direction.

### Hemodynamic Monitoring: Recognize Bleeding and Instability

Tachycardia, hypotension, narrowed pulse pressure, and cool extremities suggest hypovolemia — commonly from bleeding or third-spacing. Compare to baseline and trend. Inspect dressings, drains, and the surgical site; weigh or estimate drain output; monitor urine output (a marker of perfusion). Bleeding that exceeds expected output, causes hemodynamic instability, or enlarges a dressing requires immediate surgeon notification. Hypertension, too, must be managed — it risks bleeding, stroke, and cardiac events, especially in patients with baseline hypertension.

### Pain Management: Aggressive but Safe

Postoperative pain impairs breathing, mobilization, and recovery. Assess frequently and treat aggressively with multimodal analgesia (scheduled acetaminophen/NSAIDs plus opioids for breakthrough). Use regional anesthesia where in place and working. Balance analgesia against sedation and respiratory depression — monitor sedation scale, not just pain score. PCA setup requires patient education and verification of settings.

### Postoperative Nausea and Vomiting (PONV): Prevent and Treat Aggressively

PONV is common, distressing, and dangerous (aspiration, wound stress, dehydration, increased intracranial or intraocular pressure). Identify high-risk patients (female, non-smoker, history of PONV/motion sickness, opioid use, certain surgery types) and administer prophylaxis. Treat established PONV with multimodal antiemetics (different mechanisms). Uncontrolled PONV delays discharge and recovery.

### Temperature Management: Treat Hypothermia Actively

Hypothermia from anesthesia and exposure causes shivering (increasing oxygen demand and pain), coagulopathy, wound infection, and prolonged recovery. Use forced-air warming, warmed fluids, and blankets. Monitor temperature and treat hypothermia until normothermic.

### Monitor the Surgical Site, Drains, and Circulation

Inspect dressings for drainage amount and type (serosanguineous vs. frank blood vs. purulent). Assess drains for output volume, color, and patency. For extremity surgery, assess distal pulses, color, temperature, sensation, and movement (CMS) to detect vascular or nerve compromise. Splinting, severe pain beyond expectation, or tense compartments suggest hematoma or compartment syndrome — urgent findings.

### Apply and Document Recovery Criteria Before Transfer or Discharge

Use a validated recovery score (e.g., Aldrete for PACU, PADSS for day surgery) and meet criteria before transfer or discharge. Criteria include stable vitals, controlled pain and nausea, return of protective reflexes, adequate urine output, minimal drainage/bleeding, and (for discharge) a responsible adult and safe discharge instructions. Do not transfer or discharge a patient who has not met criteria — pressure to move beds does not override safety.

## Common Traps

**Reassurance from normal SpO2 while ignoring respiratory rate and sedation.** A postoperative patient on opioids has an SpO2 of 97% on room air, and the nurse is reassured, while the respiratory rate has drifted to 8 and the patient is increasingly sedated. The mechanism of harm is that supplemental oxygen or residual anesthetic effects can mask hypoventilation, and SpO2 lags behind rising CO2. The harm is undetected opioid-induced respiratory depression progressing to hypercapnic coma or arrest. Monitor respiratory rate and sedation level as primary indicators; SpO2 is supportive, not sufficient.

**Attributing tachycardia to pain alone.** A postoperative patient is tachycardic, and the nurse treats it as pain, giving more opioid. The mechanism of harm is that tachycardia in the postoperative period has a differential — pain, yes, but also hypovolemia from bleeding, anxiety, fever, withdrawal, and early shock — and treating only the pain delays recognition of bleeding or hypovolemia, sometimes while opioid sedation masks the patient's distress. The harm is progression of untreated bleeding or shock to the point of cardiovascular collapse. Investigate tachycardia: assess for bleeding, hemodynamic trend, urine output, and perfusion before attributing it solely to pain.

**Underestimating covert bleeding.** The surgical dressing looks dry, so the nurse is reassured, while bleeding is accumulating internally (retroperitoneal, intraperitoneal, within a joint) or draining into a closed suction device that is not being measured. The mechanism of harm is that postoperative bleeding is often occult — the visible dressing may remain dry while the patient bleeds internally — and the only signs are hemodynamic (rising heart rate, falling blood pressure, narrowing pulse pressure), rising abdominal girth or pain, or falling hematocrit. The harm is delayed recognition of hemorrhage with progression to hemorrhagic shock. Measure drain output, trend vitals and hemodynamics, watch urine output, and have a low threshold to notify the surgeon for hemodynamic instability even with a dry dressing.

**Discharging or transferring before recovery criteria are met.** Under bed-pressure or scheduling pressure, a patient is transferred from PACU or discharged home before meeting recovery criteria — pain not controlled, nausea active, vitals not stable, or no responsible adult at home. The mechanism of harm is that the recovery criteria exist precisely to catch patients who are not yet safe, and bypassing them sends an unstable patient to a lower-monitoring environment where deterioration goes unrecognized. The harm is respiratory depression, uncontrolled bleeding, readmission, or unsafe discharge home. Apply criteria rigorously; escalate pressure to the charge nurse or physician, not to the patient's safety.

**Missing compartment syndrome after extremity surgery.** After orthopedic or vascular extremity surgery, the nurse attributes increasing pain to the surgery itself and gives more opioid, while the real problem is compartment syndrome — rising pressure in a fascial compartment compromising perfusion. The mechanism of harm is that compartment syndrome presents as pain out of proportion to the surgery, pain with passive stretch, and progressive CMS deficits, and attributing it to routine postoperative pain delays the diagnosis. The harm is irreversible muscle and nerve ischemia leading to permanent disability or amputation. Assess CMS frequently, recognize pain disproportionate to the procedure as a red flag, and escalate immediately.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess at the required frequency and trend vitals, pain, sedation, and output rather than treating single values?
- Did I monitor respiratory rate and sedation level as primary indicators, not relying on SpO2 alone?
- Did I investigate tachycardia for bleeding and hypovolemia rather than attributing it to pain alone?
- Did I measure drain output, assess the dressing, and monitor hemodynamics to detect covert bleeding?
- Did I manage pain aggressively with multimodal analgesia while monitoring for sedation and respiratory depression?
- Did I identify and treat PONV with prophylaxis for high-risk patients and multimodal antiemetics?
- Did I actively rewarm the hypothermic patient to normothermia?
- Did I assess CMS (circulation, movement, sensation) after extremity surgery and escalate pain out of proportion?
- Did I apply and document recovery criteria (Aldrete/PADSS) before transfer or discharge?
- Did I resist pressure to transfer or discharge a patient who had not met criteria, escalating to the charge nurse or physician?
