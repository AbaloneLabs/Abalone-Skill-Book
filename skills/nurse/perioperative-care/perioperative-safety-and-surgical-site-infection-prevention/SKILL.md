# Perioperative Safety and Surgical Site Infection Prevention

Surgical safety and infection prevention are bundle-driven: a cluster of evidence-based practices that must all be done correctly for any one to deliver its benefit. The nurse's role spans the entire perioperative continuum — correct-site verification, antibiotic timing, normothermia, glycemic control, skin preparation, sterile technique, surgical counts, and postoperative wound care. The danger is that each element feels like a discrete checkbox and is easy to do "mostly right" while missing the specific detail that determines efficacy: the antibiotic given five minutes too late, the patient allowed to drift hypothermic, the glucose left at 250, the count that was rushed. Surgical site infections and retained surgical items are preventable, and their prevention is nursing work.

## Core Rules

### Correct-Site, Correct-Procedure, Correct-Patient Verification Is Layered and Non-Negotiable

Follow the multi-step verification process: preoperative verification (consent, imaging, site marking), the sign-in before anesthesia, the time-out immediately before incision with the full team stopped and actively confirming, and the sign-out at the end. Each layer catches errors the others miss. The time-out is not a formality — it requires active, audible confirmation of patient, procedure, site, position, implants, equipment, and antibiotics. Anyone may stop the procedure if something is wrong.

### Surgical Antibiotic Prophylaxis: Right Drug, Right Dose, Right Time

Antibiotic prophylaxis must be given within the specific window (commonly within 60 minutes before incision; 120 minutes for fluoroquinolones/vancomycin) so that tissue levels are therapeutic at the time of incision. Confirm the right drug per the surgical protocol, the right dose (weight-based, redosed for prolonged procedures or major blood loss), and the timing. Antibiotics given too early (hours before, in pre-op holding) or too late (after incision) do not prevent SSI. Redose for long cases per the drug's half-life.

### Normothermia: Maintain Core Temperature Throughout

Perioperative hypothermia increases SSI risk (impairs immune function and tissue oxygenation), bleeding (coagulopathy), cardiac events, and patient discomfort. Maintain normothermia (≥36°C) with forced-air warming preoperatively, intraoperatively, and postoperatively, warmed IV fluids, and ambient temperature control. Monitor core temperature continuously where possible.

### Glycemic Control: Perioperative Hyperglycemia Increases SSI

Hyperglycemia impairs wound healing and immune function and increases SSI, particularly in cardiac, colorectal, and diabetic patients. Monitor glucose per protocol (commonly q1–2h intraoperatively and postoperatively for high-risk patients) and treat with insulin per a validated protocol. Avoid both severe hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia. Continue this into the postoperative period, not just intraoperatively.

### Skin Preparation and Hair Removal

Use the approved antiseptic skin preparation (chlorhexidine-alcohol generally preferred over povidone-iodine for most clean surgery) applied correctly — clean to dirty, adequate contact time, allowed to dry (critical for alcohol-based prep to prevent surgical fire). Do not remove hair unless it interferes with the procedure, and if removed, use clippers (not razors, which cause micro-abrasions that increase SSI).

### Sterile Technique and Environmental Control

Maintain strict sterile technique throughout: sterile field, proper gowning and gloving, sterile draping, controlled traffic in the OR, and appropriate ventilation. Breaches are corrected immediately. The scrub nurse and circulating nurse share responsibility for the integrity of the sterile field.

### Surgical Counts: Rigorous and Documented

Perform counts of instruments, sponges, sharps, and miscellaneous items at the specified times (commonly before the procedure begins, before closing a cavity, at skin closure, and at the end). Counts must be deliberate, audible between two people, and documented. A discrepancy is a stop-the-line event — the wound is not closed and the patient is not moved until the item is found or definitively ruled out by imaging. Retained surgical items are never-events and are prevented by rigorous counting.

### Postoperative Wound Care and SSI Surveillance

Apply a sterile dressing per protocol and manage it postoperatively with aseptic technique. Educate the patient and family on wound care and signs of infection (redness, drainage, fever, opening). Monitor for SSI in the postoperative period — fever, increasing pain, wound erythema, purulent drainage — and escalate. SSI may present after discharge, so discharge education is part of prevention.

## Common Traps

**Antibiotic given in holding "before surgery" without confirming the incision time.** The antibiotic is hung in pre-op holding and documented as given, satisfying the checkbox, but the case is delayed and the incision occurs 90 minutes later, outside the therapeutic window. The mechanism of harm is that prophylactic antibiotics work only when tissue levels exceed the minimum inhibitory concentration at the time of incision and for the duration of the procedure; a delayed incision leaves subtherapeutic levels and no SSI protection. The harm is a surgical site infection that the "given antibiotic" falsely appeared to prevent. Confirm timing relative to incision, and redose if the case is delayed or prolonged.

**Rushed or "verbal" surgical counts under time pressure.** At the end of a long case, the team is tired and eager to finish, and the count is done quickly or one person "eyeballs" it while the other records. The mechanism of harm is that rushed counts miss discrepancies, and a missed sponge or instrument becomes a retained surgical item — a never-event causing infection, pain, bowel obstruction, reoperation, and severe harm. The harm is a retained item discovered days to months later. Counts must be deliberate, two-person, audible, and documented; a discrepancy stops the line until resolved.

**Allowing hypothermia because the patient "feels fine" under anesthesia.** Under anesthesia, the patient cannot report cold, and the OR is often kept cool for staff comfort, so core temperature drifts down without anyone treating it. The mechanism of harm is that hypothermia triples SSI risk by impairing tissue oxygenation and immune function, increases bleeding, and prolongs recovery. The harm is a preventable infection and prolonged recovery. Monitor core temperature and actively maintain normothermia with forced-air warming and warmed fluids; do not rely on subjective sense.

**Neglecting perioperative glucose control in non-diabetic or "borderline" patients.** Glucose management is applied rigorously to known diabetics but overlooked in non-diabetic patients who become hyperglycemic under surgical stress. The mechanism of harm is that surgical stress induces hyperglycemia even in non-diabetic patients, and untreated hyperglycemia impairs wound healing and increases SSI. The harm is a preventable infection in a patient whose glucose was never checked. Monitor glucose per protocol for all high-risk surgical patients, not only known diabetics.

**Time-out performed as a recitation rather than active confirmation.** The time-out is announced, the items are read off, and the team nods without actively verifying — a ritual rather than a safety check. The mechanism of harm is that the time-out is the last opportunity to catch a wrong-site, wrong-procedure, or wrong-patient error before irreparable harm, and a passive time-out misses the very errors it exists to catch. The harm is wrong-site surgery — a catastrophic never-event. Require active, audible confirmation from each team member, and empower anyone to stop the line.

## Self-Check

- Did I participate in layered verification (preoperative check, sign-in, time-out, sign-out) with active confirmation of patient, procedure, site, and equipment?
- Did I confirm prophylactic antibiotic: right drug, right weight-based dose, within the therapeutic window of incision, and redosed for prolonged cases?
- Did I monitor and maintain core normothermia (≥36°C) with active warming throughout the perioperative period?
- Did I monitor and control perioperative glucose per protocol for all high-risk patients, not only known diabetics?
- Did I ensure correct skin preparation (right agent, contact time, dry, no razors)?
- Did I maintain sterile technique and correct breaches immediately?
- Did I perform deliberate, two-person, audible, documented surgical counts at all specified times?
- Did I stop the line for any count discrepancy until resolved or imaging ruled out a retained item?
- Did I apply and manage the postoperative dressing with aseptic technique?
- Did I educate the patient and family on wound care and SSI signs, including post-discharge?
- Did I empower any team member, including myself, to stop the line for a safety concern?
