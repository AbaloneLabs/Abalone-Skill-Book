# Shift Handoff And SBARR

The judgment problem in handoff is that the volume of data about each patient is large, the time is short, and the failure mode is the data dump — reciting labs, medications, and history in the order they appear in the chart — which fills the time without conveying the intelligence the next nurse actually needs: what is happening with this patient today, what is the plan, what could go wrong, and what must be watched. The receiving nurse's failure mode is the mirror image: passive reception of report without questioning the gaps, without verifying the high-risk elements (drips, lines, allergies, code status), and without establishing their own situational awareness before the handing-off nurse leaves. The skilled nurse gives report that is structured, prioritized, and forward-looking, and receives report actively, closing the loop on every element that could harm a patient if misunderstood.

This is a high-stakes domain. Communication failures at handoff are a leading contributor to adverse events, because the handing-off nurse holds information the receiving nurse cannot recover from the chart alone.

## Core Rules

### Structure Report To Convey Actionable Intelligence, Not A Data Dump

A structured framework (SBAR: Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation; or I-PASS: Illness severity, Patient summary, Action list, Situation awareness, Synthesis by receiver) ensures the receiver gets the information they need to act, in an order they can process.

- Situation: who is the patient, why are they here, what is happening now.
- Background: relevant history, admission course, key events — not the entire chart, only what bears on today.
- Assessment: the current clinical picture — vital sign trend, active issues, pending results, what you are worried about.
- Recommendation: the plan, the tasks, the time-sensitive items, and what to watch for — the forward-looking intelligence that the receiver cannot infer from the chart.

### Lead With The High-Risk And The Time-Sensitive

Within the structure, front-load the information whose misunderstanding would cause harm: code status, allergies, high-alert drips and their rates, lines and their locations, isolation status, fall and aspiration risk, pending critical results, and tasks due in the next shift.

- A receiver who knows the routine background but misses that the heparin drip rate was changed an hour ago is set up for error.
- State the high-risk elements explicitly and confirm the receiver heard them.

### Communicate The Trend And The Worry, Not Just The Snapshot

The receiver needs to know not only the current vital signs but the direction of travel and your clinical concern, because these frame what to watch for.

- "Blood pressure has been stable around 110" is less useful than "blood pressure has drifted from 130 to 110 over the shift despite fluids; I am worried about ongoing bleeding."
- The worry — your sense that the patient is or is not doing well — is intelligence that the chart does not contain and that the receiver cannot recover once you are gone.

### Specify The Action List And The Time-Sensitive Tasks

The receiver inherits your unfinished work. Make it explicit: what is due, when, and what the expected outcome is.

- Medications due, scheduled procedures, pending consults, orders to be implemented, results to be followed up, family to be called.
- For each, the timeframe and the expected action: "antibiotic due at 2000, after the renal dose adjustment is verified" is actionable; "antibiotic due" is not.

### Receive Report Actively And Close The Loop

The receiver's job is not to listen but to verify, question, and synthesize. Passive reception is how gaps propagate.

- Verify the high-risk elements at the bedside when possible: the lines, the drips and their rates, the pumps, the allergies, the code status, the isolation status.
- Question gaps: if the handing-off nurse cannot account for a line, a drip, or a result, do not accept "I think it's fine" — clarify before they leave.
- Use read-back or synthesis: "So my priorities are the heparin drip, the pending CT, and the family meeting at 1400 — correct?" to confirm shared understanding.

### Use A Bedside Handoff When Possible

Bedside handoff allows verification of lines, drips, pumps, and patient status, and includes the patient, who can correct or supplement the information.

- The patient is a source of information and a participant in their care; bedside handoff improves accuracy and patient satisfaction.
- Verify the high-risk elements physically: the line labels, the pump settings, the wristband, the allergy band.

## Common Traps

### The Data Dump That Omits The Actionable Intelligence

The handing-off nurse recites the admission diagnosis, the full past medical history, the lab values, and the medication list, in the order they appear in the chart, and runs out of time before reaching the plan, the pending results, and the worries. The mechanism is that the chart's structure primes the nurse to report in its order, and the volume of data fills the available time, so the forward-looking intelligence — what the receiver needs to act on — is truncated or omitted. The harm is that the receiver inherits a patient without knowing the plan, the time-sensitive tasks, or the clinical concern, and acts on incomplete understanding, missing a pending critical result or a due task. The defense is to structure report around actionable intelligence, to front-load the high-risk and time-sensitive, and to treat the background as context that supports the assessment and recommendation rather than as the report itself.

### Passive Reception Without Verifying High-Risk Elements

The receiver listens to report, accepts the information as given, and does not verify the lines, the drips, the pump settings, the allergies, or the code status, inheriting errors that the handing-off nurse stated incorrectly. The mechanism is that the receiver trusts the report and does not want to appear to second-guess the colleague, so the high-risk elements are accepted without verification, and an error (a wrong drip rate, a missing allergy, an incorrect code status) propagates into the next shift. The harm is that the receiver acts on incorrect information and the patient is harmed by a discrepancy that a bedside verification would have caught. The defense is to verify the high-risk elements — drips, lines, pumps, allergies, code status, isolation — at the bedside during or immediately after report, to question gaps rather than accept them, and to treat verification as professional diligence rather than distrust.

### Omitting The Clinical Worry Because It Is Subjective

The handing-off nurse has a sense that the patient is not doing well — the vitals are borderline, the patient "looks off" — but does not communicate it because it is subjective and cannot be backed by a number, so the receiver inherits the patient without the concern that would prompt closer monitoring. The mechanism is that subjective clinical worry feels less legitimate than objective data, so it is omitted in favor of the numbers, and the receiver is left with a snapshot that looks acceptable and no signal to watch more closely. The harm is that the patient deteriorates during the next shift and the receiver, lacking the warning, does not detect it as early as they could have. The defense is to communicate the worry explicitly — "I am concerned about this patient even though the numbers are borderline; please watch them closely" — because the subjective sense of deterioration is a validated clinical signal and is intelligence the receiver cannot recover from the chart.

### Failing To Hand Off Unfinished Tasks And Pending Results

The handing-off nurse forgets or omits the pending CT result, the antibiotic due at midnight, the family meeting scheduled, or the order that has not yet been implemented, and the receiver does not know to act on them. The mechanism is that the unfinished work is distributed across the chart and the shift, and under time pressure it is not consolidated into the handoff, so the receiver inherits a set of obligations without knowing they exist. The harm is a missed time-sensitive medication, an unacted-on critical result, a delayed procedure, or a missed family meeting, each of which can cause clinical or relational harm. The defense is to build the action list explicitly during report, to specify each task with its timeframe and expected outcome, and to use a structured handoff tool (I-PASS action list, SBAR recommendation) that forces the unfinished work to the surface.

### Rushing Handoff At End Of Shift And Missing The Complex Patient

The handing-off nurse, eager to leave, rushes through the complex patient's report, compressing the nuance and omitting the intelligence, because the simple patients' reports have consumed the time. The mechanism is that the simple patients are easy to report and fill the time, and the complex patient — who most needs a careful handoff — is reported last and most hurriedly, inverting the priority. The harm is that the patient at highest risk receives the least informative handoff, and the receiver is unprepared for the complexity. The defense is to allocate handoff time by complexity and risk, not by order, to ensure the complex and deteriorating patients receive the most thorough report, and to resist the end-of-shift pressure to compress the report that matters most.

### Handing Off Without Including The Patient

The handoff occurs in the hallway or at the nurses' station, and the patient is not included, so the patient's own understanding and corrections are absent, and the receiver inherits a report the patient might have corrected ("that's not my pain medication" or "I'm supposed to be NPO after midnight"). The mechanism is that the hallway handoff is faster and does not require entering the room, so it is preferred, but it excludes the patient who is both a source of information and the subject of the plan. The harm is that errors and misunderstandings that the patient would have corrected propagate, and the patient feels excluded from their own care. The defense is to conduct handoff at the bedside when possible, to include the patient in the synthesis, and to recognize that the patient is a verification source that the chart and the handing-off nurse cannot replace.
