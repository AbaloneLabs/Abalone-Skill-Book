# Diabetes and Glycemic Management

Inpatient glycemic management is one of the highest-volume, highest-risk nursing responsibilities. Insulin is among the most dangerous medications in common use — a tenfold dosing error can be fatal, and the window between therapeutic and harmful is narrow and shifts hour to hour with intake, stress, steroids, renal function, and activity. Both hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia cause harm: hyperglycemia impairs wound healing, immune function, and outcomes in critical illness; hypoglycemia causes immediate neurologic danger and is associated with increased mortality. The nurse's judgment is to anticipate how each factor (NPO status, steroid dose, missed meal, pump failure, renal decline) will move the glucose, to use insulin safely and flexibly, and to treat hypoglycemia immediately and definitively.

## Core Rules

### Know the Insulin Types, Onsets, and Durations Cold

Errors in insulin timing and type are a leading cause of hypoglycemia. Know the onset, peak, and duration of each insulin you give: rapid-acting analogs (lispro, aspart, glulisine: onset ~15 min, peak ~1–2h), short-acting regular (onset ~30 min, peak ~2–4h), intermediate NPH (onset ~1–2h, peak ~4–10h), and long-acting basal (glargine, detemir, degludec: onset ~1–2h, minimal peak, duration 24h+). Match mealtime insulin to meals — give rapid-acting insulin at the start or end of the meal (after confirming the patient eats), not an hour before, to avoid hypoglycemia if the meal is delayed or refused.

### Use Sliding Scale and Correction Insulin Appropriately

Sliding-scale (reactive) insulin alone is generally inadequate for inpatients with sustained hyperglycemia and should be paired with scheduled basal insulin. Correction doses supplement scheduled insulin for above-target glucose. Use the protocol, but apply judgment: a glucose of 300 mg/dL in a patient who received full scheduled insulin and ate requires provider notification, not just another correction. Recognize patterns — repeated corrections signal the need to adjust the scheduled regimen.

### Anticipate and Adjust for Changing Status

Glucose is dynamic. Anticipate changes with: NPO status (hold or reduce mealtime insulin, continue basal, monitor frequently — NPO does not mean no insulin needs), steroid administration (significantly raises glucose, especially post-prandially; anticipate and adjust), renal function decline (reduces insulin clearance, raising risk of hypoglycemia), increased activity (lowers glucose), infection and stress (raise glucose), and procedure-related interruption of meals or pumps. Communicate with the provider when patterns change.

### Hypoglycemia: Recognize, Treat Immediately, Investigate

Hypoglycemia (commonly glucose <70 mg/dL, severe <54 mg/dL) is an emergency. Symptoms: sweating, tremor, tachycardia, hunger, confusion, irritability, slurred speech, weakness, seizure, coma. Treat immediately per protocol: if alert and able, give 15 g fast-acting carbohydrate (juice, glucose tablets); recheck in 15 minutes; repeat if needed; follow with a complex carbohydrate/protein snack to sustain. If unable to take oral: IV dextrose or IM/SC glucagon. Investigate the cause (insulin timing vs. meal, missed meal, renal change, pump failure) and adjust to prevent recurrence. Severe hypoglycemia is associated with increased mortality and must be prevented, not just treated.

### Insulin Pump Management

Patients on continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion (CSII/pump) have basal and bolus delivery automated. On admission, clarify the plan: will the patient continue to self-manage the pump (if competent), or will the team transition to hospital insulin? Do not disconnect a pump without a plan for alternative insulin (basal insulin must be started before pump discontinuation to prevent diabetic ketoacidosis within hours in type 1 diabetes). Verify pump settings, infusion site, and insulin type. Pump failure (occlusion, dead battery, dislodged catheter) causes rapid hyperglycemia and ketones in type 1 patients — assess and escalate.

### Sick Day Rules and Ketone Monitoring

Illness raises glucose and the risk of DKA and HHS. Patients need more insulin when sick, not less, even with reduced intake. Monitor ketones (urine or blood) in type 1 patients with glucose >240–300 mg/dL or any illness. Educate patients on sick day management: never stop basal insulin, monitor glucose and ketones frequently, hydrate, and seek help for vomiting, persistent hyperglycemia, or ketones.

### Patient Education: Self-Management Is the Long-Term Goal

Most diabetes care happens at home. Teach (and verify with teach-back) glucose monitoring, insulin administration and timing, recognition and treatment of hypoglycemia, sick day rules, foot care, and when to call. Tailor to the patient's literacy, vision, dexterity, and support. Involve diabetes educators for complex regimens.

## Common Traps

**Giving mealtime insulin before confirming the patient will eat.** Rapid-acting insulin is given, then the tray is delayed, the patient is taken for a test, or the patient refuses the meal. The mechanism of harm is that the insulin acts regardless of intake, and without carbohydrate intake, the patient develops hypoglycemia — sometimes severe. The harm is a hypoglycemic event that was entirely preventable. Give rapid-acting mealtime insulin at the meal, after confirming the tray is present and the patient is eating, or immediately after the meal for patients with unpredictable intake.

**Confusing insulin concentrations or units.** U-100 insulin (100 units/mL) is standard, but U-500 and other concentrations exist; using a U-100 syringe with U-500 insulin delivers one-fifth the intended volume but five times the dose. The mechanism of harm is a catastrophic overdose causing profound, prolonged hypoglycemia. The harm is severe neurologic injury or death. Verify the concentration every time, use the correct syringe or pen, and independent-double-check high-concentration insulin. Similarly, never use a tuberculin syringe for insulin (unit confusion causes dosing errors).

**Treating hypoglycemia with a snack and not rechecking.** The nurse gives juice for a low glucose, documents the treatment, and moves on without rechecking in 15 minutes or giving a sustained carbohydrate. The mechanism of harm is that the fast-acting carbohydrate raises glucose briefly, but if the insulin peak is still active, the glucose drops again — sometimes lower — and the patient has a recurrent or severe event. The harm is a preventable recurrent hypoglycemic event. Recheck in 15 minutes, repeat if needed, follow with sustained carbohydrate, and investigate the cause.

**Continuing the home insulin regimen unchanged when the patient is NPO or status changes.** The patient is admitted and the home insulin order is continued verbatim, but the patient is now NPO for a procedure or has reduced intake. The mechanism of harm is that the mealtime (bolus) insulin continues to be given without meals, causing hypoglycemia, while the underlying condition may also be raising insulin needs at other times. The harm is iatrogenic hypoglycemia or, conversely, undertreatment if basal is held. Reconcile the insulin regimen against the current clinical status (NPO, steroid use, renal function, intake) and adjust with the provider.

**Missing pump failure or dislodgement in a type 1 patient.** A type 1 patient on an insulin pump has the infusion site dislodge or the pump occlude, and within hours develops hyperglycemia and ketones, progressing to DKA. The mechanism of harm is that a pump delivers only rapid-acting insulin with no subcutaneous basal reservoir, so interruption of pump delivery causes insulin deficiency within hours (unlike long-acting injected basal insulin, which has a 24-hour tail). The harm is DKA that develops faster than the team expects. Assess pump site and function at each shift, monitor glucose and ketones, and escalate hyperglycemia with ketones immediately.

**Overlooking the edge case or exception.** The mechanism of harm is that the agent analyzes the typical scenario thoroughly but skips the unusual case. The false signal is that the common path is well-handled; in reality the exception silently produces the wrong outcome because the boundary was never tested. The harm is a failure that only appears in the case nobody checked. The fix is to always ask what the edge case is and whether the conclusion still holds there.

## Self-Check

- Did I verify the insulin type, concentration, and dose against the order and give it at the right time relative to meals?
- Did I confirm the patient is eating before giving mealtime (rapid-acting) insulin?
- Did I use sliding scale/correction insulin in conjunction with scheduled basal, recognizing patterns that warrant regimen adjustment?
- Did I anticipate and adjust for NPO status, steroids, renal change, activity, and stress?
- Did I recognize and treat hypoglycemia immediately, recheck in 15 minutes, give sustained carbohydrate, and investigate the cause?
- Did I avoid using the wrong syringe or confusing insulin concentrations?
- Did I assess pump site and function each shift for patients on CSII, and escalate hyperglycemia with ketones?
- Did I monitor ketones in type 1 patients with hyperglycemia or illness?
- Did I reconcile the insulin regimen against current clinical status on admission and with changes?
- Did I educate the patient (with teach-back) on monitoring, insulin timing, hypoglycemia recognition, sick day rules, and foot care?
