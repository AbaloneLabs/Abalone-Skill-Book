# Goals of Care and Advance Directives

The most consequential decisions in healthcare are not about which drug to give but about what the patient wants the overall trajectory of care to be — cure, life prolongation, comfort, or some balance. These decisions are captured in advance directives, code status orders, and goals-of-care conversations, and they are frequently ambiguous, outdated, unknown, or in conflict with what the care team is actually doing. The nurse is often the person who notices the mismatch — the full-code order on a patient with end-stage dementia, the undocumented conversation about comfort, the family demanding "everything" without understanding what "everything" entails. The nurse's role is to surface these mismatches, facilitate clarification, advocate for the patient's expressed wishes, and ensure the care delivered matches the care chosen.

## Core Rules

### Know the Patient's Code Status and Goals at All Times

On every shift, know whether the patient is full code, DNR, DNI, or a combination, and what the basis for that decision is. Know whether an advance directive or POLST/MOLST exists, where it is, and what it says. A code status buried in the chart that the bedside nurse does not know is functionally absent in an emergency. Confirm the order matches the directive and the patient's current expressed wishes.

### Advance Directives Are Instructions, Not Suggestions to Override

A living will, durable power of attorney for healthcare, or POLST/MOLST represents the patient's autonomous voice when they cannot speak. Honor it. If a surrogate decision-maker's choice conflicts with the patient's documented wishes, the patient's documented wishes prevail. If family demand treatment the patient refused, the refusal stands. The nurse's role is to ensure the directive is known, understood, and followed — and to escalate conflict to the provider, ethics committee, or legal counsel.

### Facilitate Goals-of-Care Conversations Early, Not in Crisis

The worst time to discuss code status and goals is during an arrest or acute deterioration, when families are overwhelmed and decisions are made in panic. Identify patients for whom goals-of-care discussion is overdue — serious illness, progressive decline, repeated admissions, advanced age with frailty — and prompt the team to have the conversation while the patient can participate. Use structured frameworks (e.g., ask-tell-ask, the Serious Illness Conversation Guide) and document the discussion.

### Clarify What "Doing Everything" Means

Families often say "do everything," meaning "don't abandon my loved one," without understanding that "everything" includes intubation, CPR with rib fractures, ICU stay, and possible survival with significant disability. Explore what the patient would want: "If your father could see himself now, what would he tell us to do?" Translate medical interventions into functional outcomes the family can grasp. Reframing "everything" as "everything that helps achieve the patient's goals" often clarifies dramatically.

### Surrogate Decision-Makers Apply Substituted Judgment, Not Their Own Wishes

When the patient lacks capacity, the surrogate (healthcare proxy, next of kin) must decide based on what the *patient* would want, not what the surrogate wants. Coach surrogates to this standard: "Think about what she would say if she could sit up and talk to us." If a surrogate is clearly acting against the patient's known wishes or cannot decide, escalate to ethics or the provider.

### Recognize and Surface Mismatches Between Care and Goals

The nurse is uniquely positioned to notice when the intensity of care does not match the patient's goals: feeding tubes placed in advanced dementia against evidence and likely wishes, repeated hospitalizations for a comfort-oriented patient, full-code status on a patient whose prognosis is days. Surface these respectfully to the team: "I want to make sure we're aligned with Mrs. X's goals — can we revisit her code status given her decline?" This is patient advocacy, not insubordination.

### Document Conversations and Decisions Thoroughly

Document who participated in goals-of-care discussions, what was discussed, what the patient/surrogate decided, and the plan. Update the code status order to match. Ambiguity in the chart leads to inappropriate resuscitation or failure to resuscitate — both catastrophic.

## Common Traps

**Deferring code status clarification until crisis.** Code status and goals are discussed in the hallway during an acute decline, or not at all, leaving a default full-code order on a patient who would not want resuscitation. The mechanism of harm is that the default in most systems is full code, so silence becomes an implicit decision to perform CPR, intubate, and transfer to ICU on a patient for whom this is futile or unwanted. The harm is a traumatic, undignified death, family guilt, and moral distress for the staff who perform resuscitation they know is wrong. Have the conversation early, document it, and convert the order before the crisis.

**Treating a DNR as a "do-not-treat" order.** Once a patient is DNR, there is a subtle drift toward withholding all treatment — fluids, antibiotics, admissions, monitoring — as if DNR meant comfort-only or hospice. The mechanism of harm is that DNR specifically refers to not initiating CPR in the event of arrest; it says nothing about treating reversible conditions, providing fluids, or continuing disease-modifying therapy aligned with the patient's goals. The harm is under-treatment of a patient who wanted life-prolonging care short of resuscitation. Clarify what the DNR includes and excludes with the patient and team, and continue appropriate treatment.

**Honoring a family member's demand over the patient's documented wishes.** A daughter demands "everything" for her mother, whose advance directive clearly refuses feeding tubes and ventilators, and the team complies to avoid conflict. The mechanism of harm is that the patient's autonomous voice is overridden by a surrogate acting outside their authority, and the patient undergoes interventions they explicitly refused. The harm is a violation of the patient's autonomy and unwanted suffering. The patient's documented wishes prevail over family preference; escalate conflict to the provider and ethics committee rather than capitulating.

**Assuming the chart's code status reflects current wishes.** A code status was set at admission or during a prior admission and has never been revisited, but the patient's condition and wishes may have changed. The mechanism of harm is that an outdated order drives resuscitation decisions that no longer match what the patient wants now. The harm is either unwanted resuscitation or failure to resuscitate a patient who has changed their mind. Revisit code status with serious illness, with clinical change, and at each admission; do not assume stability.

**Treating goals-of-care conversations as the physician's exclusive job.** Nurses may defer these conversations entirely to the provider, missing the opportunity to surface mismatches and support the patient. The mechanism of harm is that the nurse has the most continuous contact and the earliest sense of the patient's values and decline, and deferring means the conversation happens late, poorly, or not at all. The harm is decisions made in crisis without the patient's input. While ordering code status is a provider act, the nurse can initiate, facilitate, and document the conversation and advocate for it to happen.

## Self-Check

- Do I know my patient's code status, the basis for it, and whether an advance directive or POLST/MOLST exists?
- Did I confirm the order matches the directive and the patient's current expressed wishes?
- Did I identify patients overdue for goals-of-care discussion and prompt the team to have it before crisis?
- Did I clarify what "doing everything" means with the family, translating interventions into functional outcomes?
- Did I coach surrogate decision-makers to use substituted judgment (what the patient would want)?
- Did I surface any mismatch between care intensity and patient goals to the team?
- Did I treat a DNR as applying only to resuscitation, not as do-not-treat?
- Did I ensure the patient's documented wishes prevailed over conflicting family demands?
- Did I document the goals-of-care conversation: participants, content, decision, and plan?
- Did I escalate unresolved conflict to the provider, ethics committee, or legal counsel?
