# Transfusion Reaction Recognition and Response

Transfusion reactions span a spectrum from benign and self-limited to fatal within minutes, and they often present with overlapping early symptoms that the nurse must triage under time pressure. The judgment problem is that the safe reaction (mild allergic or febrile) and the lethal reaction (acute hemolytic, anaphylactic, TRALI, septic) can both begin with chills, fever, or dyspnea, so the default response to any new symptom must be to stop and assess, not to continue and observe. The nurse who can rapidly distinguish the reaction types, initiate the correct emergency response, and document the event is the difference between a recoverable event and a death. This is a domain where hesitation and continuation both kill.

## Core Rules

### Treat Any New Symptom During Transfusion as a Reaction Until Proven Otherwise

The cardinal rule is that any new symptom during a transfusion — fever, chills, rigors, urticaria, pruritus, dyspnea, wheezing, chest or back pain, hypotension or hypertension, hemoglobinuria, anxiety, sense of impending doom — requires immediate action. Stop the transfusion, maintain IV patency with normal saline (new tubing, do not use the blood tubing), assess the patient's airway, breathing, circulation, and vitals, and notify the provider and the blood bank. Do not restart the transfusion to confirm the reaction. The cost of stopping a benign reaction is small; the cost of continuing a dangerous one is catastrophic. The default is stop and assess.

### Recognize the Acute Hemolytic Reaction Immediately

Acute hemolytic reaction, usually from ABO incompatibility due to a verification error, is the most feared transfusion reaction and presents within minutes of starting. The classic presentation is fever, chills, flank or back pain (from renal vasoconstriction), hypotension, tachycardia, hemoglobinuria (red urine), bleeding (from disseminated intravascular coagulation), and a sense of impending doom. Stop the transfusion immediately, maintain IV access with normal saline, support blood pressure (intravenous fluids, vasopressors as ordered), ensure adequate urine output to protect the kidneys (mannitol or diuretics per protocol), draw post-transfusion samples (clotted and anticoagulated) and the unit for the blood bank investigation, and prepare for possible DIC and renal failure management. This is a true emergency requiring rapid response team activation.

### Differentiate the Common Reactions by Pattern

Learn to recognize the common reaction patterns so you can communicate clearly and support the correct management. Febrile non-hemolytic reaction: fever, chills, no hypotension, no hemolysis; managed with antipyretics, slowing or stopping, and exclusion of hemolysis. Allergic (mild) reaction: urticaria, pruritus, no fever, no hypotension; managed with antihistamines, often can resume after symptoms resolve. Anaphylactic reaction: rapid hypotension, bronchospasm, urticaria, gastrointestinal symptoms, often in IgA-deficient patients; managed with epinephrine, airway support, stopping the transfusion definitively. TRALI (transfusion-related acute lung injury): new acute respiratory distress, hypoxia, bilateral infiltrates, within six hours, no evidence of overload; managed with oxygen, respiratory support, stopping the transfusion. TACO (transfusion-associated circulatory overload): respiratory distress, hypertension, edema, jugular venous distension, from volume; managed with stopping the transfusion, diuresis, oxygen. Septic reaction: high fever, rigors, hypotension, vomiting, from bacterial contamination; managed with stopping, blood cultures, broad-spectrum antibiotics, and supportive care. Each requires a different response, and the nurse's accurate recognition drives the management.

### Stop the Transfusion Correctly and Maintain Access

When stopping a transfusion, do not simply clamp the line. Disconnect the blood tubing and replace it with new normal saline and new tubing to maintain IV patency (the old tubing contains the suspect product and must be returned to the blood bank with the unit). Keep the blood bag and attached tubing sealed and labeled, and return them with the completed reaction form and the post-transfusion blood samples per protocol. The blood bank investigation depends on receiving the unit and samples intact, so handling them correctly is part of the clinical response.

### Draw the Correct Post-Reaction Samples and Document the Timeline

Obtain the post-transfusion samples required by your institution (commonly a clotted sample for direct antiglobulin test and compatibility testing, an anticoagulated sample for plasma free hemoglobin and other studies, and the first post-reaction urine for hemoglobinuria). Label them carefully and send them with the unit. Document the timeline objectively: when the transfusion started, the volume infused, the onset of symptoms, the vital signs at each point, the interventions and times, the provider notifications and times, and the patient's response. Transfusion reactions are high-scrutiny events, and contemporaneous, objective documentation protects the patient, the team, and the nurse.

### Report the Reaction and Update the Transfusion History

Complete the transfusion reaction report per institutional protocol and submit it to the blood bank and the hemovigilance system. Report serious reactions through the event reporting system. Ensure the reaction is documented in the patient's transfusion history, because it affects future product selection (washed or leukoreduced products, IgA-deficient products for IgA-deficient patients, premedication for recurrent febrile reactions). Communicate the reaction at handoff, because the next nurse must be aware of the increased risk for subsequent transfusions.

### Provide Emotional Support and Education

Transfusion reactions are frightening for the patient and family, who may fear that the reaction means the transfusion failed or that they are in danger. Explain what happened in plain language, reassure where appropriate, and explain the plan for future transfusions (premedication, different products, closer monitoring). A patient who has had a reaction may be anxious about future transfusions, and clear education reduces that anxiety and improves adherence.

## Common Traps

### Continuing the Transfusion to "See if It Gets Worse"

The trap is that the patient reports a mild symptom — a chill, an itch — and the nurse, hoping to avoid wasting the unit or disrupting the schedule, slows the rate and continues to observe rather than stopping. The mechanism of harm is that the early symptoms of the dangerous reactions overlap with the mild ones, and every additional minute of infusion adds more of the offending product, deepening the reaction. The harm is a preventable severe reaction that progresses while the nurse waits for clarity. Stop the transfusion at any new symptom, assess, and clarify before deciding whether to resume; never continue to see if it worsens.

### Confusing TACO with TRALI Because Both Cause Respiratory Distress

The trap is that the patient develops respiratory distress during or shortly after a transfusion, and the nurse and team treat it as fluid overload (TACO) when it is actually TRALI, or vice versa. The mechanism of harm is that TACO is treated with diuresis and fluid restriction, which is correct for overload but worsens TRALI (which is an inflammatory lung injury, not a volume problem), and TRALI is treated with respiratory support and stopping transfusion, which does not address the overload in TACO. The harm is the wrong therapy that worsens the condition. Differentiate by pattern: TACO shows hypertension, edema, elevated brain natriuretic peptide, and response to diuresis; TRALI shows hypoxia with bilateral infiltrates, often transient leukopenia, no overload signs, and no response to diuresis. When unclear, treat the airway and oxygen first, stop the transfusion, and involve the team for the diagnosis.

### Dismissing Back Pain as a Positioning Complaint

The trap is that the patient reports back or flank pain early in the transfusion, and the nurse attributes it to the bed, positioning, or chronic back pain, rather than recognizing it as a hallmark of acute hemolytic reaction. The mechanism of harm is that back and flank pain from renal vasoconstriction is one of the classic early signs of hemolysis, and dismissing it delays stopping the transfusion and initiating the emergency response. The harm is progression to severe hemolysis, renal failure, and DIC. Treat any new back or flank pain during a transfusion as a possible hemolytic reaction: stop, assess, notify, and investigate.

### Forgetting to Send the Unit and Tubing to the Blood Bank

The trap is that the transfusion is stopped, the patient is stabilized, and the nurse discards or leaves the unit and tubing in the room, missing that the blood bank investigation requires them. The mechanism of harm is that without the unit and the post-reaction samples, the blood bank cannot confirm the reaction type, identify the cause, or prevent recurrence, and the patient's transfusion history is incomplete. The harm is a recurrent reaction in the future that better investigation could have prevented. When stopping a transfusion for a reaction, seal and label the unit and tubing, complete the reaction form, draw the required samples, and return them to the blood bank per protocol.

### Assuming a Patient with Prior Transfusions Has No Reaction Risk

The trap is that the patient has had many transfusions without reaction, so the nurse is less vigilant, treating the transfusion as routine. The mechanism of harm is that transfusion reactions can occur at any time, including after many uneventful transfusions, because antibody formation, product factors, and patient status change over time. The harm is a missed early reaction in a patient assumed to be "safe." Apply the same vigilance and the same first-fifteen-minute monitoring to every transfusion, regardless of the patient's history.

## Self-Check

- For any new symptom during a transfusion, did I stop the transfusion, maintain IV access with new normal saline tubing, assess the patient, and notify the provider and blood bank?
- For suspected acute hemolytic reaction, did I recognize the pattern (fever, back pain, hypotension, hemoglobinuria), stop immediately, support blood pressure and urine output, and draw the post-reaction samples?
- Can I differentiate febrile, allergic, anaphylactic, TRALI, TACO, and septic reactions by pattern, and did I communicate the working diagnosis clearly to the team?
- When stopping the transfusion, did I replace the blood tubing with new saline tubing, seal and label the unit and tubing, and return them with the reaction form and samples to the blood bank?
- Did I draw the correct post-reaction samples (clotted, anticoagulated, urine) and label them carefully?
- Did I document the timeline objectively, including start time, volume infused, symptom onset, vital signs, interventions, notifications, and patient response?
- Did I report the reaction through the institutional hemovigilance and event reporting systems?
- Is the reaction documented in the patient's transfusion history, with implications for future product selection and premedication?
- Did I differentiate TACO from TRALI before committing to diuresis, and treat the airway and oxygen first when unclear?
- Did I explain the reaction to the patient and family in plain language and describe the plan for future transfusions?
