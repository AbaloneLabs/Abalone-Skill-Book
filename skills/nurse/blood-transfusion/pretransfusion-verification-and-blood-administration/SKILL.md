# Pretransfusion Verification and Blood Administration

Blood transfusion is one of the highest-risk procedures a nurse performs, because a verification error can produce an acute hemolytic reaction that kills the patient within minutes, and there is no way to retrieve the product once it is in the circulation. The judgment problem is that the safety of transfusion depends almost entirely on the nurse's pre-administration checks, which are tedious, repetitive, and easy to abbreviate under time pressure — and exactly the steps that, when skipped, produce fatal ABO-incompatible transfusions. Beyond verification, the decision to transfuse, the rate, the access, and the monitoring during the first minutes all carry weight. The skilled transfusion nurse treats every unit as if it were the one that could kill, because some of them are.

## Core Rules

### Confirm the Indication, Consent, and Risk-Benefit Before Starting

Before any transfusion, confirm that the indication is appropriate and that the consent is documented. Blood is not a benign fluid; it carries infection risk, volume and iron overload risk, transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI), transfusion-associated circulatory overload (TACO), and immunologic reactions, and conservative transfusion thresholds (typically hemoglobin below 7 g/dL for most stable inpatients) improve outcomes. Verify that the patient (or surrogate) has given informed consent, understands the risks and alternatives, and has had the opportunity to ask questions. If the indication is unclear or the consent is missing, stop and clarify with the prescriber before proceeding. A transfusion given without indication or consent is a patient safety failure even if no reaction occurs.

### Perform the Two-Person Pretransfusion Verification Without Exception

The pretransfusion verification is the single most important safety step, and it must be performed by two qualified clinicians, at the bedside, against the patient and the unit, immediately before starting. Verify the patient identity (two identifiers, plus the wristband), the unit number, the blood type and Rh of the unit and the patient, the expiration date and time of the unit, the compatibility label, and the order. Confirm the visual inspection of the unit (no hemolysis, clots, discoloration, or leaks). Both nurses must independently verify; one cannot simply watch the other sign. Do not begin the transfusion until every element matches, and never proceed if anything is discrepant — return the unit to the blood bank and clarify. The verification is not bureaucracy; it is the barrier between the patient and a fatal hemolytic reaction.

### Establish Appropriate Vascular Access and Use Dedicated Tubing

Use a fresh, patent IV access appropriate to the product — large enough to flow (commonly 18 gauge or larger for red cells in adults, though smaller may be used in pediatrics or with specific products). Use a dedicated, primed blood administration set with a filter (typically 170 to 260 micron) to trap clots and aggregates; do not piggyback other medications or fluids through the same line during the transfusion (normal saline is the only compatible solution; never use dextrose or lactated Ringer's, which cause hemolysis or clumping). Change the set per protocol (commonly every two units or every four hours) to prevent bacterial growth. Confirm the access is patent before hanging the unit, because a infiltrated line discovered after the transfusion starts wastes product and delays care.

### Begin Slowly and Monitor Intensely During the First Fifteen Minutes

Most acute reactions, including acute hemolytic reactions and anaphylaxis, manifest in the first fifteen minutes of the transfusion. Stay with the patient for the first fifteen minutes, set the initial rate slow (commonly 1 to 2 mL per minute in adults), and obtain baseline and early vital signs (temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation). Teach the patient to report immediately any chills, rigors, back pain, chest tightness, itching, rash, shortness of breath, or sense of impending doom. After the initial observation period, if the patient is tolerating the transfusion, the rate can be increased to complete the unit within the allowed time (commonly two to four hours for red cells; never exceed four hours due to bacterial growth risk). Document the vital signs and the patient's status throughout.

### Monitor Throughout and Time the Transfusion Correctly

Continue to monitor vital signs per protocol (commonly at the start, after fifteen minutes, and at completion, more often if unstable), and reassess the patient throughout. A unit of red cells should generally be completed within two to four hours of hanging; a unit hanging longer than four hours is at risk of bacterial growth and should be discontinued. Platelets and plasma have their own timing. If the transfusion must be paused (for a reaction, a procedure, a transport), follow the time limits — do not resume a unit that has been out of controlled temperature for too long. Document the start time, the end time, the volume, and any reactions or interventions.

### Recognize and Respond to Any New Symptom as a Possible Reaction

Any new symptom during a transfusion — fever, chills, rigors, urticaria, pruritus, dyspnea, wheezing, hypotension, hypertension, back or flank pain, hemoglobinuria, anxiety — is a possible transfusion reaction and requires immediate action: stop the transfusion, maintain IV access with normal saline, assess the patient, notify the provider and the blood bank, and return the unit and tubing (with the attached saline and forms) to the blood bank for investigation. Do not restart the transfusion to "see if it gets worse." Distinguish the common mild febrile or allergic reactions (often manageable with slowing, antipyretics, antihistamines, and continued monitoring) from the dangerous ones (acute hemolytic, anaphylactic, TRALI, TACO, septic), which require stopping the transfusion definitively and escalating. Document the reaction, the interventions, and the patient's response.

### Complete Documentation and Report Reactions Appropriately

Document the transfusion completely: the verification, the start and end times, the vital signs, the volume, the patient response, and any reactions. If a reaction occurs, complete the transfusion reaction report, send the unit and samples to the blood bank per protocol, and ensure the reaction is recorded in the patient's transfusion history (which affects future premedication and product selection). Report serious reactions through the institutional event reporting system. The documentation is both a clinical record and a hemovigilance tool that improves safety for all patients.

## Common Traps

### Abbreviating the Two-Person Verification Under Time Pressure

The trap is that the nurse is busy, the verification feels routine, and the second nurse is "right there," so the verification is reduced to a glance and a signature rather than an independent check at the bedside. The mechanism of harm is that ABO-incompatible transfusion is almost always the result of a verification failure — a wrong patient, a wrong unit, a misread label — and abbreviating the check removes the barrier that prevents it. The harm is an acute hemolytic reaction that can kill within minutes. Perform the full two-person verification at the bedside, independently, every time, regardless of time pressure; the few minutes are not worth a fatal reaction.

### Using the Wrong IV Solution or Piggybacking Medications Through the Blood Line

The trap is that the nurse uses dextrose, lactated Ringer's, or another solution in the same line as the blood, or piggybacks a medication for convenience, assuming the line is just a fluid path. The mechanism of harm is that dextrose causes red cell clumping and hemolysis, lactated Ringer's calcium causes clotting in the line, and medications may be incompatible or may obscure a reaction. The harm is hemolysis, line occlusion, or a missed reaction. Use only normal saline with blood, use a dedicated line and set with a filter, and never piggyback other medications during the transfusion.

### Hanging the Unit and Walking Away, Missing the Early Reaction Window

The trap is that the transfusion is started, the vital signs are taken, and the nurse leaves to attend to other patients, planning to check back in an hour. The mechanism of harm is that the most dangerous reactions (acute hemolytic, anaphylactic) typically manifest in the first fifteen minutes, when the patient has received only a small volume, and early recognition allows stopping before a fatal dose is infused. The harm is a severe reaction that progresses while the nurse is away. Stay with the patient for the first fifteen minutes, run the rate slow, take early vital signs, and teach the patient to report symptoms immediately.

### Dismissing Early Symptoms as "Just a Mild Reaction" and Continuing

The trap is that the patient reports chills or mild itching, the nurse interprets it as a common minor febrile or allergic reaction, gives an antipyretic or antihistamine, and continues the transfusion. The mechanism of harm is that early symptoms of the dangerous reactions (hemolytic, anaphylactic, TRALI, septic) overlap with the mild ones, and continuing the transfusion while the cause is unclear infuses more of the offending product and worsens the reaction. The harm is a preventable severe reaction or death. Stop the transfusion at any new symptom, assess, notify, and clarify before deciding whether to restart — never continue "to see if it gets worse."

### Pushing to Complete a Unit That Has Been Hanging Too Long

The trap is that the transfusion was started, then interrupted by a procedure, transport, or slow flow, and the unit has been hanging near the four-hour limit, so the nurse increases the rate to finish it in time. The mechanism of harm is that bacteria can proliferate in a unit of red cells that has been at room temperature for more than four hours, and infusing a contaminated unit causes septic transfusion reaction, septic shock, and death. The harm is a fatal bacteremia from a unit that should have been discarded. If a unit cannot be completed within the time limit, discontinue it and discard it per protocol; never increase the rate to beat the clock.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm the indication is appropriate, the conservative threshold is respected, and informed consent is documented before starting?
- Did two qualified clinicians perform an independent verification at the bedside, checking patient identity, unit number, blood type and Rh, expiration, compatibility, and visual inspection?
- Is the IV access patent and of adequate gauge, with a dedicated blood administration set and filter, primed with normal saline only?
- Did I stay with the patient for the first fifteen minutes, run the rate slow, take baseline and early vital signs, and teach the patient to report symptoms?
- Am I monitoring vital signs per protocol throughout, and will the unit be completed within the allowed time (commonly four hours for red cells)?
- For any new symptom, did I stop the transfusion, maintain IV access with normal saline, assess, notify the provider and blood bank, and return the unit for investigation?
- Did I avoid using dextrose, lactated Ringer's, or piggybacked medications through the blood line?
- If the unit cannot be completed within the time limit, will I discontinue and discard it rather than increasing the rate?
- Did I document the verification, start and end times, vital signs, volume, patient response, and any reactions completely?
- If a reaction occurred, did I complete the reaction report, send the unit and samples per protocol, and report it through the event system?
