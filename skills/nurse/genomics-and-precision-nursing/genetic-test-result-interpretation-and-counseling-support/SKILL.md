# Genetic Test Result Interpretation And Counseling Support

A genetic test result is not a simple positive or negative; it is a complex piece of information that carries clinical, psychological, familial, and reproductive implications, and the nurse who supports the patient through it must convey the result accurately, support the emotional response, coordinate the cascade testing for the family, and recognize the limits of the nursing scope in the genetic counseling. The judgment problem is that a result that is conveyed without the nuance (the variant of uncertain significance that is not a diagnosis, the negative result that does not exclude the risk, the positive result that is a risk factor and not a certainty) produces the misunderstanding that leads to the wrong decisions, and a result that is conveyed without the support leaves the patient to navigate the implications alone. The recurring failures are overstepping the nursing scope into the genetic counseling, conveying the result without the nuance, not coordinating the cascade testing for the family, and not recognizing the psychological impact of the result. The skilled supporter conveys the result accurately within the scope, supports the emotional response, coordinates the cascade testing, and refers to the genetics professional for the counseling.

This is a domain where the nurse's role is to support the patient through the genetic test result — conveying the result accurately within the nursing scope, supporting the emotional response, coordinating the cascade testing, and referring to the genetics professional for the counseling and the complex interpretation.

## Core Rules

### Recognize And Respect The Limits Of The Nursing Scope In Genetic Counseling

The interpretation of a genetic test result and the counseling that accompanies it — the risk assessment, the implications for the management and the family, the reproductive counseling, the psychological support — are the scope of the certified genetics professional (the genetic counselor, the medical geneticist), and the nurse who steps into this scope without the training risks the misinterpretation and the harm. Recognize the limits of the nursing scope: the nurse conveys the result accurately (as communicated by the genetics professional), supports the emotional response, coordinates the testing and the referrals, and reinforces the education, but the nurse does not interpret the result independently, does not provide the genetic counseling, and does not make the management decisions based on the result. Refer to the genetics professional for the interpretation and the counseling, and support the patient through the referral.

### Convey The Result Accurately, With The Nuance The Result Requires

A genetic test result requires the nuance in its conveyance: a positive result for a pathogenic variant in a cancer susceptibility gene is a significant finding that increases the risk, but it is not a diagnosis of cancer, and the management (the enhanced surveillance, the chemoprevention, the risk-reducing surgery) is decided with the genetics professional and the oncology team. A negative result in a family with a known variant is reassuring, but a negative result in a family without an identified variant may not exclude the risk (the familial risk that is not captured by the test). A variant of uncertain significance (VUS) is a finding of unknown clinical significance that is not acted on, and it is not a pathogenic variant. Convey the result with the nuance, in the language the genetics professional provided, and do not simplify it into a positive or a negative that the result is not.

### Support The Emotional Response To The Result

A genetic test result carries a significant emotional weight — the relief of the negative, the anxiety of the positive, the confusion of the VUS, the guilt of passing a variant to a child, the grief of the risk realized — and the nurse's role is to support the response, not to manage it away. Acknowledge the emotion, provide the space for it, connect the patient to the support resources (the genetic counselor, the support group, the mental health professional), and follow up to ensure the support was received. A result that is conveyed without the emotional support leaves the patient to navigate the implications alone, and the decisions that follow may be driven by the unaddressed emotion rather than the considered judgment.

### Coordinate The Cascade Testing For The Family

A positive genetic test result has implications for the family: the blood relatives may carry the same variant, and the cascade testing (the testing of the relatives, one branch at a time, starting with the first-degree relatives) is the mechanism by which the variant is identified in the family and the relatives who carry it receive the management that prevents the harm. Coordinate the cascade testing: communicate the result to the patient (who communicates it to the family), provide the resources for the family testing (the genetic counseling, the testing facilities), and follow up to ensure the testing was pursued. A result that is not cascaded to the family is a result that prevents the harm in one patient and misses the harm in the relatives who carry the variant.

### Reinforce The Education Provided By The Genetics Professional

The genetics professional provides the education on the result — the implications, the management, the family testing, the reproductive considerations — and the nurse reinforces this education, ensuring the patient understood it and can act on it. Use the teach-back to confirm the understanding, provide the written materials to support the retention, and address the questions that arise after the counseling session, within the nursing scope. A result that is counseled and not reinforced may be misunderstood or forgotten, and the patient who did not retain the education does not act on it.

### Refer To The Genetics Professional For The Complex Interpretation And The Counseling

When the result is complex — the VUS, the variant in a gene of uncertain clinical actionability, the result that conflicts with the family history, the result with the reproductive implications — refer to the genetics professional for the interpretation and the counseling, and do not attempt the interpretation in the nursing scope. The complex interpretation requires the expertise that the genetics professional has and the nurse does not, and the attempt to interpret it without the expertise risks the misinterpretation and the harm. Refer the complex result, and support the patient through the referral.

### Document The Result, The Conveyance, And The Plan

Document the genetic test result, the conveyance to the patient (with the nuance and the language), the emotional support provided, the cascade testing plan, the referrals, and the education reinforced, so the result is communicated to the team, the plan is recorded, and the follow-up is tracked. A result that is not documented is a result that is lost to the team, and the management and the cascade testing that depend on it are not coordinated.

## Common Traps

### Overstepping The Nursing Scope Into The Genetic Counseling

The nurse, in the desire to help, steps into the genetic counseling — interpreting the result independently, providing the risk assessment, making the management recommendations — without the genetics training, and the patient receives the interpretation that may be wrong. The mechanism of harm is that the genetic counseling requires the specialized training, and the interpretation without it may be incorrect, so the patient makes the decisions based on the misinterpretation, and the management that follows may be wrong (the unnecessary surgery, the inadequate surveillance, the false reassurance). The harm is the misinterpretation and the wrong decisions. The defense is to recognize the scope limits and to refer to the genetics professional.

### Conveying The Result Without The Nuance

The result is conveyed as a simple positive or negative, without the nuance that the VUS is not a pathogenic variant, that the negative does not exclude the risk, that the positive is a risk factor and not a certainty. The mechanism of harm is that the simplified conveyance produces the misunderstanding, so the patient who is told the VUS is positive pursues the management that is not indicated, or the patient who is told the negative excludes the risk forgoes the surveillance that is, and the decisions that follow the misunderstanding are wrong. The harm is the wrong decisions from the misunderstanding. The defense is to convey the result with the nuance, in the language the genetics professional provided.

### Not Coordinating The Cascade Testing For The Family

The positive result is conveyed to the patient, but the cascade testing for the family is not coordinated, and the relatives who may carry the variant are not tested. The mechanism of harm is that the un-cascaded result prevents the harm in the patient but misses the harm in the relatives, because the variant that was identified in the patient is not identified in the family, and the relatives who carry it do not receive the management that would prevent the cancer or the cardiac event. The harm is the preventable events in the relatives. The defense is to coordinate the cascade testing, with the resources and the follow-up.

### Not Recognizing Or Supporting The Psychological Impact

The result is conveyed without the recognition or the support of the psychological impact, and the patient navigates the anxiety, the guilt, the grief alone. The mechanism of harm is that the unsupported emotional response may drive the decisions (the preventive surgery chosen from anxiety rather than judgment, the surveillance forgone from the denial), and the patient who is unsupported in the immediate response may develop the longer-term psychological consequences that the support would have prevented. The harm is the decisions driven by the unaddressed emotion and the psychological consequences. The defense is to acknowledge and support the emotional response, and to connect to the resources.

### Treating The VUS As A Pathogenic Variant

The variant of uncertain significance is treated as a pathogenic variant, and the management (the enhanced surveillance, the risk-reducing surgery) is pursued as if the VUS were a confirmed pathogenic finding. The mechanism of harm is that the VUS is a finding of unknown clinical significance, and the management based on it exposes the patient to the risks and the burdens of the intervention without the evidence that it is warranted, and the anxiety of the patient is amplified by the misclassification. The harm is the unnecessary intervention and the amplified anxiety. The defense is to convey the VUS as a finding of unknown significance that is not acted on, and to refer to the genetics professional for the interpretation.

### Not Documenting The Result, The Conveyance, Or The Plan

The result, the conveyance, and the plan are not documented, and the team does not have the information to coordinate the management and the cascade testing. The mechanism of harm is that the undocumented result is lost to the team, so the management that depends on it is not coordinated, and the cascade testing that depends on it is not pursued, and the result that was obtained does not produce the actions it should have. The harm is the uncoordinated management and the missed cascade testing. The defense is to document the result, the conveyance, the support, the plan, and the referrals.
