# Pharmacogenomics In Nursing Practice

Pharmacogenomics — the study of how genetic variation affects the drug response — is moving from the research literature into the medication administration at the bedside, and the nurse who administers the medications is increasingly the one who holds the result that says this patient metabolizes this drug too fast or too slow, and who must act on it. The judgment problem is that a pharmacogenomic result is not a curiosity; it is a safety-critical instruction that changes the dose, the drug selection, or the monitoring, and a result that is filed without action is a harm waiting to occur — the opioid metabolized too fast into respiratory depression, the clopidogrel not activated into the thrombosis, the warfarin dose that produces the bleed. The recurring failures are filing the result without acting on it, assuming the normal dose applies to the variant metabolizer, failing to recognize the drug-gene interactions in the polypharmacy patient, and not consulting pharmacy when the result is ambiguous. The skilled nurse treats the pharmacogenomic result as a safety instruction, recognizes the drug-gene interactions, and consults pharmacy when the result is ambiguous.

This is a domain where the nurse's role is to administer medications safely in the context of the pharmacogenomic variation, to recognize and act on the drug-gene interactions, and to consult pharmacy and the provider when the result changes the plan.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Pharmacogenomic Result As A Safety Instruction, Not A Curiosity

A pharmacogenomic result that identifies the patient as a poor metabolizer, an intermediate metabolizer, an extensive metabolizer, or an ultrarapid metabolizer of a drug is a safety instruction that changes the administration: the poor metabolizer may need a lower dose or an alternative drug to avoid the toxicity, the ultrarapid metabolizer may need a higher dose or an alternative to achieve the effect, and the normal dose may be wrong for both. Treat the result as the instruction it is, and act on it — adjust the dose, select the alternative, increase the monitoring — rather than filing it and administering the normal dose. A result that is filed without action is a harm waiting for the next administration.

### Recognize The Common Drug-Gene Interactions That Affect Nursing

The pharmacogenomic variations that most affect the nursing practice are the ones that change the drugs the nurse administers commonly: the CYP2D6 variants and the opioids (codeine, tramadol) that the ultrarapid metabolizer converts to the active morphine too fast, with the respiratory depression; the CYP2C19 variants and the clopidogrel that the poor metabolizer does not activate, with the thrombosis; the CYP2C9 and VKORC1 variants and the warfarin that the sensitive patient requires at a lower dose, with the bleed; the HLA-B*15:02 and the carbamazepine that the carrier is at risk for the severe skin reaction; the TPMT and the thiopurines that the deficient patient cannot tolerate at the standard dose. Recognize the common interactions, and act on them when the result is present or the drug is administered.

### Adjust The Administration For The Variant Metabolizer

When the pharmacogenomic result identifies the patient as a variant metabolizer of a drug, adjust the administration accordingly: the poor or intermediate metabolizer may need a lower dose, a longer interval, or an alternative drug, with the enhanced monitoring for the effect and the toxicity; the ultrarapid metabolizer may need a higher dose, a shorter interval, or an alternative, with the monitoring for the inadequate effect. Do not administer the standard dose to the variant metabolizer, because the standard dose assumes the normal metabolism that the patient does not have, and the result is the toxicity or the therapeutic failure that the variant predicts.

### Recognize The Drug-Gene Interactions In The Polypharmacy Patient

The patient on multiple medications may have multiple drug-gene interactions, because the enzymes that metabolize the drugs (the CYP family) are shared across many drugs, and the variant that affects one drug may affect several. Recognize the interactions in the polypharmacy patient: the CYP2D6 variant that affects the opioid may also affect the beta-blocker, the antidepressant, and the antiemetic, and the patient who is a poor metabolizer of one may be a poor metabolizer of several. Review the medication list against the pharmacogenomic result, and identify the interactions that require the adjustment, rather than checking each drug in isolation.

### Consult Pharmacy And The Provider When The Result Changes The Plan

When the pharmacogenomic result changes the dose, the drug selection, or the monitoring, consult the pharmacy and the provider to adjust the plan, because the adjustment requires the pharmacogenomic expertise and the prescriptive authority that the nurse does not have. The pharmacist interprets the result, recommends the dose or the alternative, and advises on the monitoring; the provider adjusts the order. Consult them when the result is present and the drug is administered, and do not administer the standard dose while awaiting the consultation if the result indicates a risk — hold the drug and communicate the urgency.

### Educate The Patient On The Pharmacogenomic Testing And The Result

Educate the patient on the pharmacogenomic testing — what it is, why it is done, what the result means for their medications — and on the specific result: which drugs are affected, what the patient should do (carry the result, inform the providers, watch for the effects). The patient who understands their pharmacogenomic result is the patient who can advocate for the safe administration across the providers and the settings, and the result that travels with the patient prevents the harm in the next encounter. Educate the patient, and provide the documentation they can carry.

### Document The Result And The Actions Taken

Document the pharmacogenomic result, the actions taken (the dose adjustment, the alternative drug, the monitoring, the consultation), and the patient education, so the result is communicated to the team, the actions are recorded, and the result is available for the future encounters. A result that is not documented is a result that is lost, and the next provider or the next encounter may administer the standard dose to the variant metabolizer, with the harm that the result would have prevented.

## Common Traps

### Filing The Result Without Acting On It

The pharmacogenomic result is filed in the chart, and the medication is administered at the standard dose, without the adjustment the result indicates. The mechanism of harm is that the filed result does not change the administration, so the variant metabolizer receives the dose that assumes the normal metabolism, and the toxicity or the therapeutic failure that the variant predicts occurs, because the result was not acted on. The harm is the toxicity or the therapeutic failure. The defense is to treat the result as a safety instruction and to act on it.

### Administering The Standard Dose To The Variant Metabolizer

The standard dose is administered to the patient whose pharmacogenomic result identifies them as a variant metabolizer, because the result is not recognized or the significance is not understood. The mechanism of harm is that the standard dose assumes the normal metabolism, and the variant metabolizer does not have it, so the poor metabolizer accumulates the drug to the toxic level, and the ultrarapid metabolizer fails to achieve the therapeutic level, and the harm that the variant predicts occurs. The harm is the toxicity or the therapeutic failure. The defense is to adjust the administration for the variant metabolizer.

### Not Recognizing The Drug-Gene Interactions In The Polypharmacy Patient

The pharmacogenomic result is checked against one drug, but not against the full medication list, and the interactions with the other drugs that share the metabolic pathway are missed. The mechanism of harm is that the enzyme variants are shared across many drugs, so the interaction that is recognized for one drug is missed for the others, and the patient who is the variant metabolizer of several drugs receives the standard doses of all of them, with the cumulative harm. The harm is the unrecognized interactions and the cumulative toxicity. The defense is to review the full medication list against the result.

### Not Consulting Pharmacy When The Result Changes The Plan

The result changes the plan, but the pharmacy and the provider are not consulted, because the nurse attempts the adjustment without the expertise or the authority, or the consultation is deferred. The mechanism of harm is that the adjustment without the expertise may be wrong (the dose too high or too low, the alternative contraindicated), and the deferred consultation leaves the patient on the standard dose until the harm occurs. The harm is the wrong adjustment or the unaddressed risk. The defense is to consult pharmacy and the provider when the result changes the plan.

### Administering The Drug While Awaiting Consultation When The Result Indicates A Risk

The result indicates a risk, but the drug is administered at the standard dose while the consultation is awaited, because the urgency is not recognized. The mechanism of harm is that the administration during the wait exposes the patient to the harm the result predicted, so the consultation that would have prevented the harm arrives after the harm, because the drug was not held. The harm is the toxicity or the therapeutic failure during the wait. The defense is to hold the drug and to communicate the urgency when the result indicates a risk.

### Not Educating The Patient Or Providing The Documentation To Carry

The result is not explained to the patient, and the documentation is not provided for them to carry, so the result does not travel with the patient to the next encounter. The mechanism of harm is that the result that does not travel is the result that is not available in the next encounter, so the next provider administers the standard dose to the variant metabolizer, with the harm that the result would have prevented. The harm is the harm in the future encounter. The defense is to educate the patient and to provide the documentation they can carry.
