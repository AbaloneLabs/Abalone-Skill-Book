# Mental Health and Substance Use in Corrections

The intersection of mental illness, substance use, and incarceration is the defining clinical reality of correctional nursing: jails and prisons are the largest mental health and substance use treatment facilities in most jurisdictions, the suicide rate in custody is many times the community rate, and the withdrawal from substances is a leading cause of in-custody death. The judgment problem is that the security culture of the facility, the behavioral dysregulation of the population, and the sheer volume of mental health and substance use presentations push nurses toward two equally dangerous responses: normalizing the distress (the suicidal statements are "attention-seeking," the withdrawal is "just discomfort," the psychosis is "behavioral") and dismissing the clinical signal, or over-pathologizing the behavior (every rule violation is a mental health crisis) and eroding the clinical credibility. Both miss the patient who is actually deteriorating. A suicide risk assessment that relies on the patient's denial rather than the risk factors misses the patient who hangs themselves in the cell; a withdrawal managed by the officer's report rather than the nurse's assessment misses the patient in the delirium tremens; a trauma-informed approach that is absent produces the re-traumatization and the non-engagement that drive the poor outcomes. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether mental health and substance use care in custody is safe and clinically sound, including the suicide risk and watch, the psychiatric medication, the withdrawal, and the trauma-informed care. Given the life-safety and legal stakes, this guidance is conservative: assess the suicide risk on the factors and the observation, not the denial; manage the withdrawal by the protocol and the tool; use the trauma-informed approach; and escalate the crises beyond the facility's capacity.

## Core Rules

### Assess the Suicide Risk on the Factors and the Observation, Not the Denial

The suicide risk assessment in custody must be based on the validated risk factors (the prior attempts, the current ideation and plan, the mental illness, the substance use, the recent loss, the isolation, the first 24 hours and the first weeks of the incarceration, the recent sentencing or the bad news) and the observation (the behavior, the mood, the withdrawal, the giving away of the possessions, the statements), not on the patient's verbal denial, because the denial is unreliable in the custody setting (the fear of the observation, the loss of the privileges, the shame), and the suicide is the leading cause of the in-custody death. The assessment must use the validated tool and the clinical interview, and the positive findings must trigger the suicide watch (the constant or the frequent observation, the safe cell, the removal of the means), the mental health referral, and the documentation. The assessment must be repeated at the high-risk transitions (the admission, the sentencing, the bad news, the isolation).

The trap is the reliance on the denial ("I'm not suicidal"), which dismisses the risk factors and the observation, and which misses the patient who completes the suicide. The defense is to assess the risk on the factors and the observation, to use the validated tool, and to place the positive findings on the watch.

### Manage the Psychiatric Medication With the Continuity and the Monitoring

The psychiatric medication management in custody must ensure the continuity (the verification and the continuation of the community medications at the admission, to prevent the destabilization and the relapse), the monitoring (the adherence at the pill line, the side effects, the therapeutic response, the laboratory for the specific medications), and the provider coordination (the psychiatric provider for the initiation, the adjustment, the discontinuation), because the interruption of the psychiatric medication produces the relapse, the crisis, and the suicide risk, and the unmonitored medication produces the side effects and the non-adherence. The continuity requires the verification of the community medications (the bottles, the pharmacy, the provider, the prescription drug monitoring program), the continuation per the provider order, and the monitoring at the pill line and the follow-up visits. The controlled-substance psychiatric medications (the stimulants, the benzodiazepines) require the additional security and the diversion prevention.

The trap is the psychiatric medication interruption at the admission (the verification delay) or the non-monitoring during the stay, which destabilizes the patient. The defense is to verify and continue the psychiatric medications, to monitor the adherence and the side effects, and to coordinate with the psychiatric provider.

### Implement the Withdrawal Protocol With the Validated Tool and the Frequent Monitoring

The substance withdrawal in custody must be managed with the validated tool (the CIWA-Ar for the alcohol and the benzodiazepine, the COWS for the opioid), the protocol-driven medication (the tapering schedule per the facility protocol and the provider order), the frequent monitoring (the scheduled reassessment, the vital signs), and the escalation for the severe or the complicated withdrawal, because the alcohol and the benzodiazepine withdrawal can be fatal (the seizures, the delirium tremens), and the opioid withdrawal, while not typically fatal, drives the severe distress, the dehydration, and the post-release overdose. The protocol must begin at the intake screening (the last use, the withdrawal history), with the initial assessment and the first medication, and continue through the scheduled reassessment. The severe or the complicated withdrawal (the history of the seizures, the delirium tremens, the concurrent illness) must be escalated to the provider and the outside care.

The trap is the non-protocol management (the PRN medication, the officer's report), which allows the progression to the dangerous withdrawal. The defense is to assess the withdrawal at the intake, to use the validated tool, to follow the protocol, and to escalate the severe cases.

### Deliver the Trauma-Informed Care to the Detained Population

The trauma-informed care is essential in the correctional setting, because the incarcerated population has a very high prevalence of the trauma (the childhood abuse, the domestic violence, the community violence, the prior incarceration trauma), and the custody environment (the searches, the restraints, the isolation, the loss of the autonomy) can re-traumatize, so the care that is not trauma-informed produces the non-engagement, the dysregulation, and the poor outcomes. The trauma-informed care requires the awareness of the trauma prevalence, the safety (the physical and the psychological, the predictability, the choice), the trust and the collaboration (the clear communication, the shared decision-making within the custody limits), the empowerment (the patient's strengths, the self-management), and the avoidance of the re-traumatization (the gender-responsive care, the minimized restraints, the trauma-informed searches and the procedures). The trauma-informed approach does not compromise the security or the clinical accuracy, but it delivers the care in the way that respects the trauma history.

The trap is the punitive or the dismissive approach (the behavior as the non-compliance rather than the trauma response), which re-traumatizes and disengages. The defense is to deliver the trauma-informed care, to recognize the trauma response, and to minimize the re-traumatization.

### Differentiate the Behavioral, the Medical, and the Psychiatric Presentations

The correctional nurse must differentiate the behavioral presentations (the rule violations, the malingering, the manipulation, the situational distress), the medical presentations (the delirium, the intoxication, the withdrawal, the metabolic disturbance, the head injury), and the psychiatric presentations (the psychosis, the depression, the mania, the anxiety, the personality disorder), because the custody environment and the population produce all three, and the misattribution (the psychosis treated as the behavior, the delirium treated as the psychiatric, the withdrawal treated as the malingering) produces the wrong intervention and the harm. The differentiation requires the systematic assessment (the vital signs, the mental status, the history, the timeline, the toxicology), the consideration of the medical causes (the delirium, the intoxication, the withdrawal, the metabolic), and the psychiatric evaluation, and the documentation of the reasoning.

The trap is the snap attribution (the behavior, the malingering) without the assessment, which misses the medical or the psychiatric cause. The defense is to assess systematically, to consider the medical and the psychiatric causes, and to document the reasoning.

### Escalate the Mental Health and the Substance Use Crises Beyond the Facility

The mental health and the substance use crises (the acute psychosis, the severe depression with the suicide risk, the severe withdrawal, the overdose, the acute medical complication of the substance use) that exceed the facility's capacity must be escalated to the psychiatric provider and the outside healthcare system (the emergency department, the psychiatric hospital, the detoxification facility), because the facility cannot manage them, and the failure to escalate endangers the patient and violates the standard of care. The escalation requires the recognition of the crisis, the communication (the provider, the security, the outside facility), the transport (the security transport), and the documentation. The nurse must advocate for the escalation, even against the operational resistance, because the delay is the harm.

The trap is attempting to manage the crisis in-house, because the escalation is difficult, which endangers the patient. The defense is to recognize the crisis, to escalate to the provider and the outside system, and to document the escalation.

## Common Traps

### The Suicide Risk Dismissed on the Denial

The patient denies the suicidal ideation on the assessment, despite the risk factors (the prior attempt, the recent loss, the first 24 hours, the mental illness), and the risk is dismissed, so the patient is placed in the general population and completes the suicide. The mechanism of the trap is that the denial is unreliable in the custody setting, and the reliance on the denial dismisses the risk factors and the observation, so the patient at the high risk is treated as the low risk, and the suicide occurs. The false signal is the denial, which suggests safety but masks the risk. The harm is the in-custody suicide. The defense is to assess the risk on the factors and the observation, to use the validated tool, and to place the positive findings on the watch.

### The Psychiatric Medication Interrupted at the Admission

The community psychiatric medications are not verified or continued at the admission, because the verification takes time and the provider order is pending, so the patient goes without the medication and destabilizes. The mechanism of the trap is that the psychiatric medication continuity is the critical safety function, and the interruption produces the relapse, the crisis, and the suicide risk, because the patient goes without the antipsychotic, the mood stabilizer, or the antidepressant during the verification. The false signal is the eventual verification, which suggests continuity but masks the destabilizing gap. The harm is the relapse, the crisis, and the suicide risk. The defense is to verify and continue the psychiatric medications at the admission, and to monitor the adherence and the response.

### The Withdrawal Managed by the Officer's Report Without the Protocol

The withdrawal is managed based on the officer's report (the patient "looks okay") and the PRN medication, without the validated tool and the protocol, so the withdrawal progresses to the seizures or the delirium tremens. The mechanism of the trap is that the withdrawal must be assessed by the nurse with the validated tool and managed by the protocol, and the reliance on the officer's report and the PRN medication misses the progression, because the officer is not trained to assess the withdrawal and the PRN approach is not the protocol-driven management. The false signal is the officer's reassurance and the PRN medication, which suggest management but mask the progression. The harm is the seizures, the delirium tremens, and the death. The defense is to assess the withdrawal with the validated tool, to follow the protocol, and to escalate the severe cases.

### The Punitive Approach to the Trauma Response

The patient's dysregulation (the agitation, the withdrawal, the non-compliance) is treated as the behavior or the non-compliance, with the punitive response (the discipline, the isolation), rather than the trauma response, so the patient is re-traumatized and disengages from the care. The mechanism of the trap is that the incarcerated population has a high trauma prevalence, and the punitive approach to the trauma response re-traumatizes and disengages, because the behavior is the symptom of the trauma, not the willful non-compliance, and the punitive response worsens the symptom. The false signal is the behavior, which suggests the non-compliance but masks the trauma response. The harm is the re-traumatization, the dysregulation, and the non-engagement. The defense is to deliver the trauma-informed care, to recognize the trauma response, and to respond with the safety and the support.

### The Snap Attribution Without the Assessment

The patient's presentation (the agitation, the confusion, the bizarre behavior) is attributed to the behavior or the malingering without the assessment, so the underlying medical cause (the delirium, the intoxication, the withdrawal, the head injury) or the psychiatric cause (the psychosis, the mania) is missed. The mechanism of the trap is that the custody environment and the population produce the behavioral, the medical, and the psychiatric presentations, and the snap attribution without the assessment misses the medical or the psychiatric cause, because the symptom is attributed to the willful behavior rather than the clinical condition. The false signal is the behavioral appearance, which suggests the non-compliance but masks the clinical cause. The harm is the missed diagnosis, the delayed treatment, and the deterioration. The defense is to assess systematically, to consider the medical and the psychiatric causes, and to document the reasoning.

### The Crisis Managed In-House Beyond the Facility's Capacity

The acute psychosis, the severe depression with the suicide risk, or the severe withdrawal is managed in-house, beyond the facility's capacity, because the escalation is difficult (the transport, the security, the cost) or the operational resistance, so the patient does not receive the psychiatric hospital or the emergency department care. The mechanism of the trap is that the facility has a limited mental health and substance use capacity, and managing the crisis beyond it endangers the patient and violates the standard of care, because the delay and the substandard care produce the harm. The false signal is the in-house management, which suggests competence but masks the capacity exceedance. The harm is the deterioration, the suicide, and the liability. The defense is to recognize the crisis, to escalate to the provider and the outside system, and to document the escalation.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess the suicide risk on the validated factors (the prior attempts, the ideation and plan, the mental illness, the substance use, the recent loss, the high-risk period) and the observation, not the denial, and place the positive findings on the watch?
- Did I verify and continue the community psychiatric medications at the admission, monitor the adherence and the side effects at the pill line, and coordinate with the psychiatric provider?
- Did I implement the withdrawal protocol with the validated tool (CIWA-Ar, COWS), the protocol-driven medication, the frequent monitoring, and the escalation for the severe cases?
- Did I deliver the trauma-informed care, recognize the trauma response, and minimize the re-traumatization through the safety, the trust, the collaboration, and the empowerment?
- Did I differentiate the behavioral, the medical, and the psychiatric presentations through the systematic assessment, the consideration of the medical causes, and the documentation of the reasoning?
- Did I recognize the mental health and the substance use crises that exceed the facility's capacity, escalate to the psychiatric provider and the outside healthcare system, and document the escalation?
- Did I document the suicide risk assessment, the psychiatric medication management, the withdrawal protocol, and the crisis escalation, with the findings, the interventions, the response, and the rationale?
- Did I maintain the patient's dignity, the confidentiality within the custody limits, and the constitutional standard of care, and practice within the scope of correctional nursing?
