# Preeclampsia and Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy are a leading cause of maternal and perinatal mortality, and they progress unpredictably from mild hypertension to multi-organ failure, seizure, and stroke. The judgment problem is that preeclampsia is a multisystem disease that announces itself through subtle, scattered signs — a slight rise in liver enzymes, a flicker of headache, a half-kilogram of edema — and the nurse who monitors only the blood pressure misses the trajectory. Magnesium sulfate, the cornerstone of seizure prophylaxis, is a narrow-therapeutic-window drug that can cause respiratory arrest, so the nurse administering it carries a direct life-safety responsibility. The obstetric nurse must integrate blood pressure, neurologic status, laboratory evidence of end-organ damage, and fetal well-being into one continuous risk assessment.

## Core Rules

### Confirm the Diagnosis Thresholds and Treat Severe Range Pressures Urgently

Know the diagnostic blood pressure thresholds and treat them as urgent, not routine. Severe hypertension in pregnancy (systolic 160 or higher, or diastolic 110 or higher) is a hypertensive emergency that requires treatment within thirty to sixty minutes to prevent stroke, and it is the single most preventable cause of maternal death in preeclampsia. When a severe-range pressure is confirmed (repeat within fifteen minutes, correct cuff size, patient seated and resting), escalate immediately and administer the ordered antihypertensive (commonly IV labetalol, IV hydralazine, or oral nifedipine) per protocol. Do not allow severe-range pressures to persist while waiting for the next routine vital sign check. The risk of intracerebral hemorrhage rises sharply and quickly in this range.

### Administer Magnesium Sulfate with Rigorous Toxicity Monitoring

Magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis is given as a loading dose followed by a maintenance infusion, and it is excreted renally, so toxicity accumulates in any patient with renal impairment. Before and during the infusion, assess and document the four toxicity checkpoints every hour: patellar deep tendon reflexes (hyporeflexia is the first sign of toxicity), respiratory rate (depression follows loss of reflexes), urine output (target at least 25-30 mL/hr, since magnesium is renally cleared), and level of consciousness. Have magnesium antagonist calcium gluconate immediately available at the bedside, know its dose and route, and know where it is stored. If reflexes are absent, respiratory rate is below twelve, or urine output drops, stop the infusion, notify the provider, and prepare to administer calcium gluconate. Draw magnesium levels per protocol, especially in renal impairment.

### Assess Neurologic and End-Organ Status Continuously

Preeclampsia is a multisystem disease, and the blood pressure is only one manifestation. Assess for severe features and impending eclampsia at every encounter: persistent headache unresponsive to analgesics, visual disturbances (scotomata, blurred vision, photophobia), epigastric or right upper quadrant pain (liver involvement), nausea and vomiting, and any altered mental status. These are not "pregnancy complaints" — they are warning signs of progression to eclampsia or HELLP syndrome (hemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, low platelets). Track laboratory trends (platelets, liver enzymes, creatinine, hemolysis markers) and treat a worsening trajectory as urgent even if the blood pressure is controlled. Preeclampsia can worsen for days after delivery; do not assume the postpartum patient is safe.

### Manage Magnesium Sulfate Interactions and Concomitant Drugs

Magnesium potentiates neuromuscular blockade and respiratory depression, so any concurrent neuromuscular blocker (used in intubation or cesarean anesthesia) has a prolonged and intensified effect. Magnesium also potentiates calcium channel blockers like nifedipine, causing profound hypotension; coordinate timing with the provider when both are given. Avoid combining magnesium with other respiratory depressants without explicit awareness of the additive risk. When caring for a patient on magnesium who is going to surgery or who may need intubation, flag the magnesium status to the anesthesia team so they anticipate prolonged blockade.

### Coordinate Fetal Assessment with Maternal Management

The fetus is affected by both the disease and the treatment. Magnesium sulfate crosses the placenta and can cause neonatal depression, so notify the neonatal team before delivery if magnesium is running. Severe hypertension and uteroplacental insufficiency can cause fetal distress, intrauterine growth restriction, and abruptio placentae. Perform continuous fetal monitoring during severe hypertension or magnesium administration in a viable gestation, and correlate any fetal tracing change with maternal status. The decision to deliver balances maternal and fetal interests, and the nurse's role is to provide the continuous data (maternal vitals, labs, neurologic status, fetal tracing) that informs that decision.

### Plan for Postpartum Surveillance and Patient Education

Preeclampsia can develop for the first time or worsen in the postpartum period, peaking around three to six days after delivery, and postpartum preeclampsia is a leading cause of late maternal death. Continue blood pressure monitoring and magnesium per protocol after delivery, and educate the patient before discharge about the warning signs (severe headache, vision changes, epigastric pain, shortness of breath) that require emergency return. Provide clear written instructions, confirm understanding with teach-back, and ensure a follow-up blood pressure check is scheduled within days. A patient who is told to "watch for headaches" without understanding the urgency may die at home.

## Common Traps

### Treating a Severe-Range Blood Pressure as a Number to Recheck Later

The trap is that the nurse sees a blood pressure of 168/112, charts it, and plans to recheck in an hour at the next vital sign interval, treating it as a data point rather than an emergency. The mechanism of harm is that stroke risk in severe-range pregnancy hypertension rises within hours, and the standard antihypertensive window is thirty to sixty minutes. The harm is an intracerebral hemorrhage that occurs while the nurse was "going to recheck." Any severe-range pressure (160 systolic or 110 diastolic) requires immediate confirmation within fifteen minutes and, if confirmed, immediate escalation and treatment, not routine scheduling.

### Assuming Magnesium Sulfate Is Safe Because It Is Commonly Used

The trap is that magnesium sulfate is given routinely on the obstetric unit, so it feels like a routine infusion, and the hourly toxicity checks get abbreviated or skipped when the unit is busy. The mechanism of harm is that magnesium has a narrow therapeutic window and is renally excreted, so toxicity can develop insidiously in a patient with falling urine output or occult renal impairment. The harm is loss of reflexes progressing to respiratory depression and arrest, sometimes detected only when the patient stops breathing. Perform the four toxicity checks (reflexes, respiratory rate, urine output, mental status) every hour without exception, have calcium gluconate at the bedside, and treat a falling urine output as a magnesium-stop signal pending provider notification.

### Dismissing Headache or Visual Symptoms as Normal Pregnancy Complaints

The trap is that headaches, blurred vision, and epigastric pain are common in pregnancy, so the nurse charts them as "patient reports headache, acetaminophen given" without connecting them to preeclampsia progression. The mechanism of harm is that these symptoms are the prodrome to eclamptic seizure in a patient with preeclampsia, and dismissing them delays escalation, magnesium initiation or continuation, and delivery planning. The harm is a seizure that could have been prevented. In any patient with preeclampsia or new hypertension, treat new or worsening neurologic symptoms as severe features requiring immediate provider notification, not as routine discomfort.

### Relaxing Monitoring After Delivery Because the Baby Is Out

The trap is that delivery is the definitive treatment for preeclampsia, so the team assumes the patient is safe once the baby is delivered and steps down monitoring. The mechanism of harm is that preeclampsia and eclampsia frequently worsen in the first 48 to 72 hours postpartum, and postpartum preeclampsia can develop de novo days after an uncomplicated delivery in a patient who was discharged. The harm is a postpartum seizure, stroke, or HELLP crisis that occurs when monitoring has been reduced and the patient may even be home. Continue blood pressure monitoring, magnesium per protocol, and neurologic assessment through the postpartum risk window, and educate the patient on warning signs before discharge.

### Failing to Have Calcium Gluconate Immediately Available

The trap is that calcium gluconate is "on the unit somewhere" or in the code cart down the hall, so the nurse assumes it is accessible and does not verify its presence at the bedside before starting magnesium. The mechanism of harm is that magnesium toxicity can progress from hyporeflexia to respiratory arrest in minutes, and the antidote must be given promptly to reverse it. The harm is a patient who arrests while the nurse runs to find the antidote. Before initiating a magnesium infusion, physically confirm calcium gluconate is in the patient's room, know the dose and route, and ensure every nurse caring for the patient knows where it is.

## Self-Check

- For any blood pressure 160 systolic or higher or 110 diastolic or higher, did I confirm within fifteen minutes and escalate and treat within the protocol window rather than rechecking at the next routine interval?
- For a patient on magnesium sulfate, did I assess and document reflexes, respiratory rate, urine output, and mental status every hour, and do I know the calcium gluconate dose and location?
- In a patient with preeclampsia or new hypertension, did I treat new headache, visual disturbance, or epigastric pain as severe features requiring immediate escalation rather than routine complaints?
- Am I trending the relevant labs (platelets, liver enzymes, creatinine, hemolysis markers) and treating a worsening trajectory as urgent even if blood pressure is controlled?
- Did I coordinate with anesthesia about the magnesium status before any procedure requiring neuromuscular blockade, and am I alert to magnesium-nifedipine potentiation?
- Am I performing continuous fetal monitoring during severe hypertension or magnesium in a viable gestation, and have I notified the neonatal team if magnesium is running at delivery?
- After delivery, did I continue blood pressure monitoring, magnesium per protocol, and neurologic assessment through the postpartum risk window rather than stepping down prematurely?
- Did I educate the patient on postpartum warning signs, confirm understanding with teach-back, and ensure a scheduled follow-up blood pressure check?
- If urine output dropped below threshold, did I stop the magnesium, notify the provider, and draw a magnesium level?
- Do I treat severe-range hypertension as a stroke-prevention emergency, not a data point to trend?
