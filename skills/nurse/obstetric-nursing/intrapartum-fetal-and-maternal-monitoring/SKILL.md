# Intrapartum Fetal and Maternal Monitoring

Intrapartum nursing is the continuous, simultaneous surveillance of two patients who cannot both be directly examined at once. The judgment problem is that the fetal heart rate strip is an indirect, physiologic proxy for fetal oxygenation, and it is noisy, intermittent, and easy to over-read or under-read. Meanwhile the laboring patient is experiencing intense physical and emotional stress, her hemodynamics shift with each contraction and position change, and the window to act on a deteriorating fetus is measured in minutes, not hours. The nurse must interpret the tracing in context, distinguish reassuring from non-reassuring patterns, intervene with intrauterine resuscitation, and escalate decisively without crying wolf so often that real warnings are ignored.

## Core Rules

### Establish a Reliable Baseline and Context Before Judging Any Strip

Before classifying a tracing, identify the gestational age, the clinical context (spontaneous vs induced labor, oxytocin or epidural in use, prior cesarean), and confirm the equipment is tracking the correct signal. Determine the baseline fetal heart rate over a ten-minute window, identify baseline variability (moderate variability is the most reassuring single feature), and note the presence or absence of accelerations. Confirm you are reading fetal, not maternal, heart rate — maternal tachycardia picked up by an external transducer is a classic and dangerous misread. Only after the baseline and variability are established should you interpret decelerations and categorize the strip. A strip read in fragments, without context and baseline, invites both false alarm and false reassurance.

### Interpret Decelerations by Type, Timing, and Pattern

Decelerations mean different things depending on their relationship to contractions. Early decelerations mirror contractions and reflect vagal response to head compression; they are generally benign. Variable decelerations are abrupt drops that indicate cord compression, and their severity depends on depth and duration — persistent severe variables warrant intervention (position change, amnioinfusion). Late decelerations, which begin after the contraction peak and recover after the contraction ends, reflect uteroplacental insufficiency and are the most ominous pattern when repetitive. Prolonged decelerations lasting more than two minutes require immediate action. Categorize the tracing using the standardized three-tier system (Category I reassuring, Category II indeterminate, Category III abnormal), and remember that Category II is the majority of strips and demands ongoing reassessment and corrective measures, not passive watching.

### Apply Intrauterine Resuscitation Promptly and Systematically

When a non-reassuring pattern appears, initiate intrauterine resuscitation immediately while you escalate. The standard maneuvers are: reposition the patient (left or right lateral, never flat supine which compresses the vena cava), increase intravenous fluid if hypotensive, apply oxygen only if maternal oxygenation is low (routine oxygen is no longer recommended and may be harmful), discontinue oxytocin if running, and consider a bolus to correct tachysystole. Identify and treat the reversible cause: tachysystole from oxytocin or prostaglandins, hypotension from epidural, cord compression, or maternal fever. Document the intervention, the rationale, and the fetal response. Intrauterine resuscitation is not a substitute for escalation — it buys time while the team mobilizes for possible operative delivery.

### Monitor Maternal Hemodynamics and Contraction Pattern Continuously

The laboring patient is not just the vessel for the monitor. Track maternal blood pressure, heart rate, temperature, and pain at the interval the acuity demands, and more frequently after epidural placement, oxytocin titration, or any intervention. Watch for hypotension after regional anesthesia, which reduces uteroplacental perfusion and can cause late decelerations within minutes. Quantify contraction frequency, duration, and intensity, and recognize tachysystole (more than five contractions in ten minutes) — a leading iatrogenic cause of fetal distress that is often caused by over-titration of oxytocin. Palpate or use the tocodynamometer to assess resting tone; a uterus that does not relax between contractions is starving the placenta of oxygen.

### Escalate with Structured Communication and a Clear Threshold

Define the escalation threshold before the strip deteriorates: which patterns require provider notification within minutes, which require the provider at the bedside, and which trigger operating room mobilization. Use a structured tool (SBAR) and communicate the entire picture, not just the number: "I have a Category III tracing with absent variability and recurrent late decelerations for the last twelve minutes, unresponsive to intrauterine resuscitation, oxytocin has been stopped, I need you at the bedside now and I am mobilizing the OR." Avoid minimizing language ("the strip is a little off"). Chain-of-command escalation exists for exactly these moments — if the primary provider does not respond appropriately, escalate to the charge nurse, the obstetric supervisor, or the on-call attending per policy.

### Document Continuously and Objectively

Intrapartum documentation is both a clinical and a legal record, and it must reflect the timeline of events, interventions, and responses. Document the strip interpretation at defined intervals (commonly every thirty minutes for low-risk, every fifteen for high-risk), every intervention with its rationale and the fetal response, provider notifications with the time and content of the communication, and any deviation from standard practice with justification. Never alter, back-date, or delete entries. If a late entry is needed, mark it clearly as such. Objective, contemporaneous documentation protects the patient, the team, and the nurse.

### Maintain Therapeutic Presence for the Laboring Patient

Behind every tracing is a frightened, exhausted person in pain. Explain what the monitor shows in plain language, narrate interventions before performing them, involve the partner or support person, and advocate for the patient's birth plan within safety limits. Epidural requests, pushing urges, and the emotional arc of a long labor all require nursing presence that the monitor cannot replace. A patient who feels informed and supported tolerates labor better and reports the experience more positively even when complications arise.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Monitor Strip as the Patient Instead of Reading It in Context

The trap is that the electronic fetal monitor produces a continuous, seductive tracing that feels like ground truth, so the nurse optimizes the strip rather than assessing the whole clinical picture. The mechanism of harm is that a strip can look reassuring on paper while the fetus is deteriorating, or look ominous due to artifact (signal loss, maternal heart rate pickup, transducer displacement). A nurse who stares at the screen and never palpates the uterus, checks maternal temperature, or asks about bleeding may miss chorioamnionitis, placental abruption, or uterine rupture that the strip only hints at. The harm is delayed escalation of a catastrophic process because the numbers "looked okay." Always correlate the tracing with maternal assessment and the clinical context.

### Calling Every Category II Strip an Emergency or, Conversely, Dismissing All Category II

Category II tracings are indeterminate and constitute the majority of strips, which makes them the hardest to manage. The trap operates in two directions. The over-reader escalates every Category II to a crash cesarean, normalizing operative delivery and eroding trust in the nurse's judgment so that when a true emergency arises, the response is slower. The under-reader dismisses Category II as "normal labor" and watches a strip that is sliding toward Category III without intervening. The mechanism of harm in both cases is failure to apply the structured algorithm: identify the underlying pattern within Category II (recurrent variables, minimal variability, late decelerations), apply intrauterine resuscitation, reassess in a defined window, and escalate if it does not improve. Treat Category II as a call to active management with a reassessment clock, not as a label to file and forget.

### Missing Tachysystole Because the Contraction Pattern Is Not Quantified

The trap is that the tocodynamometer shows wavy lines that "look like contractions," so the nurse eyeballs them without counting frequency or measuring the resting tone. The mechanism of harm is that tachysystole — more than five contractions in ten minutes — starves the placenta of the relaxation time needed for fetal oxygenation, and it is most often iatrogenic, caused by oxytocin over-titration. The fetus then shows late decelerations or loss of variability, and the team chases the fetal heart rate while the actual cause (too much oxytocin) goes unaddressed. The harm is a distressed fetus and an avoidable operative delivery. Count contractions formally every thirty minutes during oxytocin, reduce or stop the infusion at the first sign of tachysystole, and document the contraction frequency objectively.

### Assuming Maternal Vital Signs Are Stable Because the Patient Looks Focused on Labor

The trap is that a laboring patient who is breathing through contractions appears to be coping, so the nurse under-monitors maternal hemodynamics, especially after epidural placement. The mechanism of harm is that epidural analgesia causes sympathetic blockade and hypotension in a substantial fraction of patients, and the hypotension reduces uteroplacental perfusion, producing late decelerations that are then misattributed to the fetus rather than to the maternal blood pressure. The harm is a fetal distress workup while the correctable maternal hypotension goes untreated. Take maternal blood pressure every five to fifteen minutes after epidural initiation and bolus, treat hypotension promptly with fluids and position change, and correlate any new fetal tracing change with the most recent maternal vital signs.

### Failing to Escalate Because the Provider Was "Already Notified Once"

The trap is that the nurse notified the provider of a non-reassuring tracing, received a verbal order to "continue to monitor," and then did not re-escalate when the strip worsened, assuming the single notification fulfilled the obligation. The mechanism of harm is that labor is dynamic and a strip that warranted watching thirty minutes ago may now warrant an operating room. The nurse who treats the first call as the only call allows a deteriorating fetus to decompensate without the provider's updated awareness. The harm is a baby born with hypoxic ischemic encephalopathy that timely escalation could have prevented. Re-escalate whenever the clinical picture changes meaningfully, document each notification, and use the chain of command if the response is inadequate.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm I am reading fetal, not maternal, heart rate, and have I established a true ten-minute baseline and variability before categorizing the strip?
- For any deceleration I am charting, can I state its type (early, variable, late, prolonged), its relation to contractions, and what physiologic process it represents?
- If the strip is Category II, have I identified the specific sub-pattern, applied intrauterine resuscitation, and set a defined reassessment window rather than passively watching?
- Have I quantified contraction frequency in the last thirty minutes and ruled out tachysystole, especially if oxytocin is running?
- When did I last take maternal blood pressure, and if there is a new fetal tracing change, have I correlated it with the most recent maternal vital signs?
- For the most recent non-reassuring pattern, did I escalate with SBAR, state the full picture, and document the time and content of the provider communication?
- If the provider's response did not match the urgency, did I use the chain of command rather than accepting "continue to monitor"?
- Is my documentation contemporaneous, objective, and free of back-dating, with each intervention paired to its rationale and the fetal response?
- Have I physically laid hands on the patient, palpated the uterus, and assessed for bleeding, fever, or pain in the last hour, rather than relying only on the monitor?
- Have I explained the current situation to the laboring patient and her support person in language they understand?
