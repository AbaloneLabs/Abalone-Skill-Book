# Suicide Risk Assessment and Safety Management

Suicide risk assessment is a high-stakes judgment that cannot be reduced to a screening score. A patient who endorses passive thoughts on a screening tool may be at low risk, or may be minimizing active intent and a specific plan; a patient who denies ideation may be concealing intent to avoid intervention. The nurse's job is to assess the full picture — ideation, intent, plan, means, access, protective factors, prior attempts, and acute stressors — and to match the level of observation and intervention to the risk, erring toward safety when uncertain. The environment must be made safe (means restriction), and the patient must be observed at a frequency that matches their risk. The catastrophic failure is a completed suicide on an inpatient unit, and almost all such events involve a lapse in observation, environment, or escalation.

## Core Rules

### Assess Comprehensively, Not Just With a Screening Tool

Validated tools (e.g., Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale, Ask Suicide-Screening Questions) are entry points, not endpoints. For any positive screen, conduct a fuller assessment: ideation (passive vs. active, frequency, intensity, duration), intent (does the patient want to act), plan (specificity, lethality, timing), means access (firearms, medications, ligatures), prior attempts (the strongest single risk factor), acute stressors (loss, conflict, financial, legal), substance use, and protective factors (family, faith, reasons for living, treatment engagement). Document the assessment in detail.

### Match Observation Level to Risk, and Escalate Upward Generously

Use the least restrictive level that is safe, but err toward more observation when uncertain. Levels include: q15-minute observation, constant in-person observation (one-to-one), and constant observation with enhanced environmental safety (ligature-risk room, removal of all means). A patient with active intent, a plan, means access, or recent attempt requires one-to-one observation at minimum. A patient whose risk is unclear, who is intoxicated (and cannot be fully assessed), or who is acutely agitated requires enhanced observation until assessed.

### Make the Environment Safe: Means Restriction Is Treatment

Search the patient and belongings on admission and after any return (pass, bathroom, etc.). Remove ligature risks (belts, shoelaces, strings, chargers, plastic bags, bedsheet risks), sharp objects, medications (including the patient's own), glass, and access to means of jumping or hanging. Use ligature-resistant fixtures where available. In bathrooms, monitor the patient (do not allow unsupervised bathroom use for high-risk patients). The environment is a clinical intervention, not just a physical space.

### Develop a Collaborative Safety Plan

For patients able to participate, develop a written safety plan: warning signs, internal coping strategies, social contacts and settings for distraction, people to ask for help, professionals and crisis lines, and means restriction. The safety plan is a tool the patient uses after discharge; engage them in creating it. A safety plan is not a "no-suicide contract" — such contracts have no proven efficacy and can create false reassurance.

### Reassess With Any Change in Status

Suicide risk fluctuates. Reassess with any change: increased agitation or despair, command hallucinations, news of a loss, a bad outcome, intoxication or withdrawal, medication change, or before any pass or discharge. A patient who was low-risk can become high-risk within hours.

### Coordinate Care and Communication

Communicate risk status and observation level clearly at handoff. Ensure the treatment team (psychiatry, social work, the patient's support people) are aligned. At discharge, ensure warm handoff to outpatient care, means restriction at home (lethal means counseling, especially firearm removal), and crisis resources. The highest-risk periods are the days to weeks after discharge and after a psychiatric hospitalization.

### Know the Limits of Your Scope and When to Escalate

Nurses assess and manage risk; psychiatry and the treatment team direct the overall plan. If you believe the assigned observation level is inadequate, escalate — do not silently accept a plan you judge unsafe. If a patient attempts to leave against advice and is at imminent risk, follow your facility's hold and security procedures.

## Common Traps

**Treating a negative screen or a denial as definitive.** A patient denies suicidal ideation on screening, or endorses only passive thoughts, and the nurse records "no risk" and reduces observation. The mechanism of harm is that patients frequently minimize or conceal ideation to avoid hospitalization, observation, or stigma, and a single denial does not rule out risk, especially in a patient with recent attempt, major loss, or command hallucinations. The harm is a completed suicide by a patient who was cleared on a screening question. Integrate the screen with the full clinical picture; when the picture and the screen conflict, weight the picture and observe more, not less.

**Reducing observation because the patient "looks better" or "engaged in group."** A suicidal patient appears brighter, attends group, and is conversational, and the team reduces observation. The mechanism of harm is that apparent improvement can be genuine, but it can also reflect the patient's decision that they have a plan and feel relieved by it — a well-documented phenomenon before some suicides. The harm is reduced observation at the moment of highest risk. Confirm sustained improvement over time and through stressors before reducing observation; a brief brightening is not sufficient evidence.

**Inadequate environmental search, especially after returns from pass or bathroom.** The patient is searched on admission, but after returning from a supervised pass, after using the bathroom, or after a visit, the environment is not re-checked. The mechanism of harm is that means are acquired during these gaps — a visitor brings medication, the patient finds a ligature in an unsupervised bathroom, or contraband is introduced. The harm is access to means during a period of risk. Search the patient and environment after every transition and return, and monitor bathroom use for high-risk patients.

**Over-relying on "no-suicide contracts."** A patient signs a no-suicide contract and the team feels reassured. The mechanism of harm is that no-suicide contracts have no demonstrated efficacy in preventing suicide, can create false reassurance that reduces vigilance, and have no legal force. The harm is a false sense of safety leading to reduced observation. Use collaborative safety planning instead, and never substitute a contract for observation and means restriction.

**Failing to escalate when the assigned plan feels unsafe.** The nurse at the bedside judges that q15-minute observation is inadequate for a patient with active intent, but the order stands and the nurse does not escalate, rationalizing that "the doctor ordered it." The mechanism of harm is that the bedside nurse has the most direct information about the patient's current state, and failing to escalate a known concern allows an unsafe plan to continue. The harm is a preventable suicide attempt or completion. Escalate respectfully and persistently; if the response is inadequate, use the chain of command. Patient safety overrides deference to an order you judge unsafe.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess suicide risk comprehensively (ideation, intent, plan, means, prior attempts, stressors, protective factors), not just with a screening tool?
- Did I match the observation level to the risk, erring toward more observation when uncertain?
- Did I make the environment safe by removing ligature risks, sharps, medications, and other means?
- Did I search the patient and belongings on admission and after every return or transition?
- Did I monitor bathroom use for high-risk patients rather than allowing unsupervised access?
- Did I develop a collaborative safety plan (not a no-suicide contract) with the patient?
- Did I reassess risk with any change in status, including apparent improvement?
- Did I communicate risk status and observation level clearly at handoff?
- Did I escalate when the assigned plan felt unsafe, using the chain of command if needed?
- At discharge, did I ensure warm handoff to outpatient care, lethal means counseling, and crisis resources?
