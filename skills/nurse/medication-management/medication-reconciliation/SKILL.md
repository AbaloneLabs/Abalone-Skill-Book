# Medication Reconciliation

The judgment problem in medication reconciliation is that it is performed at transitions — admission, transfer, discharge — when time pressure is highest and information is most fragmented, and the temptation is to complete the form rather than to resolve the discrepancies. A reconciliation that lists the patient's home medications and the ordered medications side by side, without resolving the differences, is documentation, not reconciliation. The discrepancies — the stopped statin, the duplicated anticoagulant, the omitted inhaler, the over-the-counter drug that interacts — are exactly where the harm is, and resolving them requires calling the prescriber, calling the pharmacy, and sometimes calling the family, not checking a box.

This skill covers the best-possible medication history (BPMH), discrepancy resolution, and the specific challenges of obtaining histories from patients who cannot or will not provide accurate information.

## Core Rules

### Obtain The Best Possible Medication History, Not The First Answer

The first medication list a patient or family provides is almost always incomplete. A best-possible medication history uses multiple sources and explicit probing.

- Ask about prescription medications, over-the-counter drugs, vitamins, supplements, herbal remedies, inhalers, eye drops, topical agents, patches, and PRN medications — patients often do not mention the last several.
- Use at least two sources: the patient/family interview, the community pharmacy fill history, the prior discharge summary, the primary care record, and the medication list brought in with the patient (the brown bag method).
- Ask specifically about recently stopped medications, "as needed" medications taken regularly, and medications the patient takes differently than prescribed.

### Resolve Every Dis discrepancy, Do Not Just Document It

A discrepancy between the home medication list and the ordered list is a question that must be answered: was the medication intentionally continued, intentionally stopped, unintentionally omitted, or unintentionally duplicated?

- For each home medication not on the order list, determine whether it was intentionally held (and why) or unintentionally omitted.
- For each ordered medication not on the home list, determine whether it is new and intentional or an error.
- Flag duplications (two drugs in the same class, e.g., two PPIs, two anticoagulants) and interactions for prescriber review.
- Document the resolution: "home metformin held — patient NPO pre-procedure" is a resolution; "metformin not on order list" is not.

### Recognize When The History Is Unreliable And Escalate The Search

Patients who are confused, sedated, intubated, non-verbal, or who have limited language concordance cannot provide a reliable history, and accepting "no medications" or "I don't know" as the answer produces a dangerously incomplete reconciliation.

- When the patient cannot provide the history, obtain it from family, the community pharmacy, the primary care provider, the prior facility, and any brought-in medication.
- An empty "no regular medications" entry for a confused elderly patient with multiple comorbidities is almost certainly wrong and must be investigated, not accepted.
- Use professional interpreter services for language barriers; do not rely on family members for clinical translation of medication names and doses.

### Reconcile At Every Transition, Not Just Admission

Reconciliation is required at admission, at every internal transfer (ICU to floor, floor to rehab), and at discharge. Each transition is a point where medications are lost, added, or changed, and each requires a fresh reconciliation.

- At transfer, verify that medications are continued, held, or stopped intentionally, and that the receiving unit has the complete and current list.
- At discharge, reconcile the discharge list against the inpatient list and the home list, provide the patient with a clear written discharge medication list, and explicitly counsel on what to take, what to stop, and what is new.

### Communicate Discrepancies To The Prescriber Specifically

When you identify a discrepancy, communicate it as a specific question requiring a decision, not as a vague flag.

- "The patient's home warfarin 5 mg is not on the admission order list; should it be continued, held, or converted?" is actionable.
- "There may be a discrepancy" is not.

### Provide Clear Discharge Counseling And A Written List

The discharge medication list is the patient's primary tool for safe self-management. A verbal handoff is insufficient.

- Provide a written, patient-readable list: drug name, dose, route, frequency, purpose, and whether it is new, continued, changed, or stopped.
- Counsel specifically on high-risk changes: new anticoagulants, insulin regimens, corticosteroid tapers, and any medication with a narrow window or significant side effects.
- Confirm the patient can access and afford the medications; a prescription the patient cannot fill is an omission.

## Common Traps

### Accepting The First List As Complete

The patient or family provides a list of five medications, the nurse records five, and the reconciliation is complete — except the patient also takes an over-the-counter NSAID that interacts with the newly prescribed anticoagulant, an inhaler they do not think of as a "medication," and a recently stopped beta-blocker whose abrupt cessation will cause rebound. The mechanism is that the first list feels sufficient because it answers the immediate question, and the nurse does not probe the categories that patients systematically omit (OTC, supplements, inhalers, eye drops, topicals, recently stopped). The harm is that the reconciliation is built on an incomplete foundation, and the omitted medications cause interactions, withdrawals, or therapeutic gaps that are discovered only when harm occurs. The defense is to use a structured probing approach that explicitly asks each category, to cross-check against at least one independent source (pharmacy fill history, prior records), and to treat a short list for a patient with multiple comorbidities as a signal to probe harder, not as a satisfactory answer.

### Documenting A Discrepancy Without Resolving It

The reconciliation form has columns for home medications and ordered medications; the nurse fills both columns, notes that the home statin is absent from the orders, and moves on, treating the documentation as the task. The mechanism is that the form's structure makes documentation feel like completion, so the discrepancy is recorded but never brought to the prescriber for a decision, and the medication remains omitted for the admission. The harm is that the patient misses days of a chronic disease medication (statin, antihypertensive, antiplatelet, antiepileptic), with consequences ranging from therapeutic setback to withdrawal seizure to thrombotic event. The defense is to treat every discrepancy as an unresolved question requiring a prescriber decision, to communicate it specifically, and to document the resolution (continued, held with reason, stopped with reason), because a documented-but-unresolved discrepancy is a failure dressed as success.

### Accepting "No Medications" From A Patient Who Cannot Reliably Report

A confused or sedated elderly patient answers "no" to the medication question, or the family is unavailable, and the nurse records "no regular medications," closing the reconciliation. The mechanism is that the patient's inability to provide the history is mistaken for the absence of medications, and the path of least resistance — recording the negative answer — is taken over the harder work of obtaining the history from other sources. The harm is that a patient who is almost certainly on multiple chronic medications is admitted with an empty list, and the omissions (antihypertensives, anticoagulants, antiepileptics, insulin) produce harm during the admission. The defense is to treat "no medications" from a patient who cannot reliably report as a data gap requiring external sources (family, pharmacy, prior records, primary care), not as a finding, and to escalate the search until a credible history is obtained or the gap is explicitly documented as unresolved and flagged for follow-up.

### Reconciling At Admission But Not At Transfer Or Discharge

The admission reconciliation is performed carefully, but at transfer to a step-down unit the medication list is carried forward without re-reconciliation, and medications that were held for the ICU (sedation, paralysis, certain antibiotics) are inadvertently continued, or medications that should have resumed are not restarted. The mechanism is that admission feels like the "real" reconciliation and transfers feel like information transfer, so the transfer reconciliation is treated as clerical. The harm is that medications are lost or inappropriately continued across transitions, and the receiving team inherits an inaccurate list that they trust because it came with the patient. The defense is to perform a fresh reconciliation at every transition, to verify each medication's status (continue, hold, stop, change) against the clinical context, and to recognize that every handoff is a fresh opportunity for error that requires a fresh check.

### Discharging With A List The Patient Cannot Use

The discharge reconciliation produces an accurate list, but it is handed to the patient as a printout in clinical language, without counseling, and the patient does not understand which medications to take, which to stop, or that a dose has changed. The mechanism is that the accuracy of the list is treated as the deliverable, and the patient's ability to act on it is assumed rather than confirmed. The harm is that the patient continues a stopped medication, misses a new one, double-doses a changed one, or cannot afford a prescribed one, and the careful inpatient reconciliation is undone at home. The defense is to provide the list in patient-readable language, to counsel specifically on changes and high-risk medications, to use teach-back to confirm understanding, and to address access and affordability, because a list the patient cannot or will not follow is functionally no list at all.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I obtain the medication history from at least two sources, and explicitly probe OTC drugs, supplements, inhalers, eye drops, topicals, patches, and recently stopped medications?
- [ ] For each discrepancy between the home list and the ordered list, did I determine whether it was intentional or unintentional, and document the resolution?
- [ ] Did I flag duplications (same-class drugs) and interactions for prescriber review with specific questions?
- [ ] If the patient could not reliably provide the history, did I obtain it from family, pharmacy, prior records, or primary care rather than recording "no medications"?
- [ ] Did I reconcile at transfer and discharge, not just at admission, verifying each medication's status against the clinical context?
- [ ] Did I communicate each discrepancy to the prescriber as a specific question requiring a decision?
- [ ] Did I provide a patient-readable written discharge list and counsel specifically on new, changed, and high-risk medications?
- [ ] Did I confirm the patient can access and afford the prescribed medications, and use teach-back to verify understanding?
