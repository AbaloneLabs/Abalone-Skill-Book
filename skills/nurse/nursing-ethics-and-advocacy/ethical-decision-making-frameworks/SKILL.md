# Ethical Decision Making Frameworks

An ethical framework is the structured lens that turns a tangled, emotionally charged clinical conflict into a deliberation that can be reasoned through rather than resolved by power, inertia, or the loudest voice. The judgment problem is that no single framework fits every case, and the framework that is applied without awareness of its limits produces conclusions that feel rigorous and miss what a different lens would reveal — principlism can reduce a relational conflict to a checklist of competing principles, care ethics can lose sight of the justice dimension, and a framework chosen for convenience rather than fit produces an analysis that is defensible and incomplete. The recurring failures are defaulting to one framework for every case, applying the framework as a formula that outputs the answer rather than a structure that supports the deliberation, skipping the fact-gathering that the framework depends on, and treating the framework's output as the decision rather than the input to a decision that also requires the patient's values and the institutional context. The skilled user knows multiple frameworks, selects the one that fits the case, and uses it as a structure for deliberation rather than a formula for answers.

This is a domain where the nurse or ethics facilitator's role is to select and apply the ethical framework that fits the case, to use it as a structure for deliberation, and to integrate its output with the patient's values and the institutional context.

## Core Rules

### Know Multiple Frameworks And Their Strengths And Limits

No single ethical framework fits every case, and the skilled deliberator knows several and their tradeoffs. Principlism (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) is the dominant clinical framework: it is clear, teachable, and widely understood, but it can reduce a relational conflict to competing principles and can struggle when principles genuinely conflict with no lexical priority. Care ethics centers the relational responsibilities and the particularity of the patient, and it excels in the conflicts that are fundamentally about relationships and trust, but it can lose sight of the justice and policy dimensions. Narrative ethics attends to the patient's story and the meaning of the decision within it, and it excels in the complex, values-laden cases, but it is less structured and harder to apply under time pressure. Virtue ethics asks what the virtuous practitioner would do, and it excels in the character and integrity questions, but it is less decisive in the rights and duties conflicts. Know the strengths and limits, and select the framework that fits the case.

### Match The Framework To The Case, Not The Case To The Framework

The selection of the framework should be driven by the nature of the case: a conflict between the patient's autonomy and the team's beneficent concern fits principlism; a conflict rooted in a ruptured relationship or a trust failure fits care ethics; a complex, values-laden case with multiple stakeholders and a rich history fits narrative ethics; a question about the practitioner's integrity or the team's character fits virtue ethics. Match the framework to the case, and when the case spans multiple dimensions (as many do), use the frameworks in combination, letting each illuminate what the others obscure. The framework that is chosen for familiarity rather than fit produces an analysis that addresses the dimensions the framework sees and misses the dimensions it does not.

### Gather The Facts Before Applying The Framework

An ethical framework is only as good as the facts it is applied to, and a deliberation that begins with the principles before the facts produces conclusions that are logically sound and factually wrong. Gather the facts first: the clinical situation (the diagnosis, the prognosis, the options), the patient's preferences and values (the stated wishes, the advance directive, the surrogate's knowledge), the legal and institutional context (the policy, the law, the precedent), and the stakeholders (who is involved, who is affected, who decides). Apply the framework to the gathered facts, not to the assumed facts, and revisit the facts as the deliberation reveals gaps.

### Use The Framework As A Structure For Deliberation, Not A Formula For Answers

A framework is a structure that organizes the deliberation — it identifies the principles at stake, the stakeholders affected, the options and their consequences — but it does not output the answer, and treating it as a formula produces a mechanical conclusion that misses the judgment the case requires. Use the framework to ensure that the deliberation is complete (all the relevant principles considered, all the stakeholders identified, all the options explored), and then exercise the judgment that integrates the analysis with the patient's values and the specific context. The framework that is applied as a formula produces a defensible and shallow conclusion; the framework that is used as a structure produces a deliberation that is both rigorous and responsive to the case.

### Integrate The Framework's Output With The Patient's Values And The Context

The framework's output — the principles at stake, the options, the tradeoffs — is the input to a decision that must also integrate the patient's values (what matters to this patient, in this situation) and the institutional context (the policy, the law, the precedent, the resources). A decision that applies the framework's output without the patient's values is a decision that is ethically analyzed and ethically incomplete, because the patient's values are not an input to the framework but a determinant of the decision within it. Integrate the analysis with the values and the context, and when they conflict, name the conflict explicitly and use the framework to reason through it rather than resolving it by default.

### Document The Framework, The Analysis, And The Decision

Document the ethical deliberation — the framework used, the facts gathered, the principles at stake, the options explored, the patient's values, the decision and its rationale — so the reasoning is visible, the decision is defensible, and the deliberation is communicated to the team. An ethical decision that is reached through deliberation but not documented is invisible and vulnerable, because the reasoning that supported it cannot be reconstructed, and the decision appears arbitrary to those who were not present. Document the framework, the analysis, and the decision, with the rationale that connects them.

### Use The Framework To Facilitate, Not To Dictate, The Team's Deliberation

When facilitating a team's ethical deliberation, the framework is a tool to structure the discussion — to ensure all the dimensions are considered, to surface the principles in conflict, to keep the discussion from devolving into power or inertia — but it is not a tool to dictate the conclusion. Use the framework to facilitate the team's reasoning, to give every voice a structure to contribute within, and to keep the deliberation centered on the patient, and resist the temptation to use the framework's authority to impose a conclusion the team has not reached. A facilitated deliberation produces a decision the team owns; a dictated deliberation produces a decision the team resists.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To One Framework For Every Case

The deliberator defaults to principlism (or another familiar framework) for every case, regardless of the case's nature, and the dimensions that the framework does not see are missed. The mechanism of harm is that the single framework illuminates some dimensions and obscures others, so the conflicts that are fundamentally relational (missed by principlism) or justice-oriented (missed by care ethics) or narrative (missed by both) are analyzed through a lens that does not fit, and the conclusion addresses the visible dimensions and misses the decisive one. The harm is an analysis that is rigorous within the framework and wrong for the case. The defense is to know multiple frameworks and to match the framework to the case.

### Applying The Framework As A Formula That Outputs The Answer

The framework is applied mechanically — the principles listed, the options enumerated, the "winner" selected by the principle that appears to override — and the deliberation is reduced to a calculation that produces a conclusion without judgment. The mechanism of harm is that the formula application produces a defensible conclusion that misses the judgment the case requires, because the framework is a structure for deliberation, not a formula for answers, and the mechanical conclusion can be ethically wrong when the patient's values and the context, which the framework does not fully capture, point the other way. The harm is a conclusion that is logically sound and ethically incomplete. The defense is to use the framework as a structure and to exercise the judgment that integrates the analysis with the values and the context.

### Skipping The Fact-Gathering And Applying The Framework To Assumed Facts

The deliberation begins with the principles, applied to the facts as they are assumed or initially reported, and the gaps in the facts are not discovered until the conclusion is challenged or the case deteriorates. The mechanism of harm is that the framework applied to assumed facts produces a conclusion that is logically sound and factually wrong, because the clinical situation, the patient's preferences, or the legal context that was assumed differs from the reality, and the conclusion that fit the assumption does not fit the case. The harm is a decision that is wrong for the actual case. The defense is to gather the facts before applying the framework, and to revisit them as the deliberation reveals gaps.

### Treating The Framework's Output As The Decision

The framework's output — the principles at stake, the overriding principle — is treated as the decision, without the integration of the patient's values and the context that the decision requires. The mechanism of harm is that the framework's output is the analysis, not the decision, and treating it as the decision produces a conclusion that is ethically analyzed and ethically incomplete, because the patient's values are a determinant of the decision within the framework, not an input to it, and the decision that ignores them violates the autonomy the framework was meant to protect. The harm is a decision that violates the patient's values. The defense is to integrate the framework's output with the patient's values and the context.

### Using The Framework's Authority To Dictate Rather Than Facilitate

The facilitator uses the framework's authority — the principles, the structure — to impose a conclusion the team has not reached, rather than to facilitate the team's own deliberation. The mechanism of harm is that the dictated conclusion is not owned by the team, so the team resists or undermines it in practice, and the deliberation that was meant to produce a shared decision produces a contested one, because the framework's authority was used to override the team's reasoning rather than to structure it. The harm is a decision that is not implemented and a team that is alienated from the ethics process. The defense is to use the framework to facilitate, not to dictate, the team's deliberation.

### Not Documenting The Framework Or The Analysis

The ethical decision is reached through a structured deliberation, but the framework, the analysis, and the rationale are not documented, and the reasoning is invisible. The mechanism of harm is that the undocumented deliberation cannot be reconstructed, so the decision appears arbitrary to those who were not present, and it is vulnerable to reversal or misinterpretation, because the reasoning that supported it is lost. The harm is a decision that is undone or misread. The defense is to document the framework, the analysis, the patient's values, the decision, and the rationale.
