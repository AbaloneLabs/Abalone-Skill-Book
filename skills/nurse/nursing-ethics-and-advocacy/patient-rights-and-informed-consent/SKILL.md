# Patient Rights And Informed Consent

Patient rights — the codified entitlements to dignity, autonomy, information, privacy, and non-discrimination that define the floor of ethical care — are not a single consent event but a continuous obligation that spans the encounter, and the nurse is often the first and sometimes the only line of defense when they are violated. The judgment problem is that rights are easily honored in the abstract and eroded in the operational reality, where the press of the workflow, the institutional convenience, and the unconscious bias produce the quiet compromises (the privacy not afforded, the information not offered, the dignity not protected) that the patient experiences as a violation even when no single act was malicious. The recurring failures are treating the Patient Bill of Rights as a posted document rather than a practiced obligation, honoring consent at the signature and neglecting the ongoing consent that the care requires, failing to operationalize the advance directive and POLST into the real decisions, and not recognizing the rights violations that occur through bias or workflow rather than intent. The skilled advocate treats rights as a continuous obligation, operationalizes the directives into the decisions, and recognizes the violations that occur quietly.

This is a domain where the nurse's role is to protect and advocate for patient rights as a continuous obligation across the encounter, to operationalize the advance directives and the preferences into the real decisions, and to recognize and address the violations that occur through workflow, bias, or convenience.

## Core Rules

### Treat Patient Rights As A Continuous Obligation, Not A Posted Document

The Patient Bill of Rights — the right to respectful and dignified care, to information about the diagnosis and treatment, to participate in the decisions, to privacy and confidentiality, to refuse treatment, to access records, to non-discrimination — is a continuous obligation that spans the encounter, not a document that is posted and honored by its posting. Treat each right as a practiced obligation: the dignity is afforded in every interaction, the information is offered proactively and not only on request, the participation is invited in every decision, the privacy is protected in every conversation and every exposure. A right that is posted and not practiced is a right that is violated in the operational reality, and the patient experiences the gap between the poster and the practice as a betrayal.

### Honor Ongoing Consent, Not Only The Signature

Consent is not a single event at the signature; it is an ongoing process that continues through the care, and the patient who consented to the surgery may not have consented to the additional procedure, the resident's participation, the photography, or the change in the plan. Honor the ongoing consent: inform the patient of the changes, the additions, the participants, and obtain consent for each significant deviation from what was originally agreed. A consent that is obtained once and assumed to cover everything that follows is a consent that is overstretched, and the care that proceeds beyond the consent is a violation, however well-intentioned.

### Operationalize The Advance Directive And POLST Into The Real Decisions

The advance directive and the Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST) are the documents that translate the patient's values into the specific decisions about resuscitation, intubation, and the level of care, and they must be operationalized — translated into the actual orders, the actual care decisions, and the actual communication with the team — not merely filed in the chart. Verify that the directive is current and applicable, that the orders match it, that the team is aware of it, and that the care reflects it. A directive that is filed and not operationalized is a directive that is violated at the moment it matters most, when the default (full resuscitation) is applied because the directive was not translated into the order.

### Protect Privacy And Confidentiality In The Operational Reality

Privacy and confidentiality — the right to control the information about one's care and one's body — are protected not only by the policy but by the operational practices: the conversations that are held out of earshot, the exposures that are minimized, the records that are accessed only by those with a need to know, the information that is shared only with the patient's authorization. Protect privacy in the operational reality: lower the voice in the hallway, close the curtain or the door, limit the exposure to what is necessary, challenge the unauthorized access to the record. A privacy that is protected by policy and violated in the hallway is a privacy that is not protected, and the patient who overhears their case discussed at the nurses' station experiences the violation directly.

### Recognize And Address Rights Violations Through Bias Or Workflow

Rights violations often occur not through malice but through bias (the unconscious assumptions about the patient's preferences, their capacity, their worth) or workflow (the routine that does not afford the time or the structure for the right to be honored). Recognize the violations that occur through these mechanisms: the pain that is undertreated because of a bias about the patient's drug-seeking, the information that is not offered because the workflow does not include the time, the dignity that is not afforded because the patient is perceived as difficult. Address the violation at both levels — the individual interaction and the system that produced it — because the violation that is addressed only at the individual level recurs through the system.

### Advocate For The Right To Non-Discrimination And Equitable Care

The right to non-discrimination — to care that is provided without regard to race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, religion, age, disability, socioeconomic status, or source of payment — is a right that is violated through the biases, the assumptions, and the resource allocations that produce inequitable care. Advocate for the equitable care: challenge the bias when you observe it, ensure the resources are allocated equitably, and address the disparities that the data reveals. A right to non-discrimination that is honored in the policy and violated in the allocation is a right that is not honored, and the patient who receives lesser care because of who they are experiences the violation in the outcome.

### Use The Institutional Resources To Address Rights Violations

When a rights violation is identified — by the patient, the family, or the nurse — use the institutional resources to address it: the patient advocate, the ombudsman, the ethics consultation, the risk management, the compliance office. These resources exist to investigate and resolve the violations, and the nurse who encounters a violation they cannot resolve at the bedside should escalate to them, with a warm handoff that does not abandon the patient. A violation that is not escalated festers, and the patient who is not supported in raising it experiences the violation compounded by the institution's indifference.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Patient Bill Of Rights As A Posted Document

The Bill of Rights is posted in the lobby or the patient room, and its posting is treated as the fulfillment of the obligation, while the operational practices violate the rights it enumerates. The mechanism of harm is that the posting honors the right in the abstract while the practice violates it in the reality, so the patient who reads the poster and experiences the gap between the poster and the practice experiences a betrayal that compounds the violation. The harm is the violation of the right and the erosion of the trust. The defense is to treat each right as a practiced obligation in every interaction.

### Overstretching A Single Consent To Cover Everything That Follows

The consent obtained for the initial procedure is treated as covering the additional procedures, the participants, the photography, and the changes in the plan, without obtaining consent for each significant deviation. The mechanism of harm is that the single consent is overstretched beyond what the patient agreed to, so the care that proceeds beyond the consent is a violation of the patient's autonomy, however well-intentioned, and the patient who did not agree to the addition experiences it as a violation. The harm is the violation of autonomy and the erosion of trust. The defense is to honor the ongoing consent and to obtain consent for each significant deviation.

### Filing The Advance Directive Without Operationalizing It

The advance directive or POLST is filed in the chart, and it is not translated into the actual orders, the care decisions, or the team communication, so the default (full resuscitation) is applied when the moment comes. The mechanism of harm is that the filed directive is not operationalized, so the care that the patient did not want is provided because the directive was not translated into the order, and the patient's values are violated at the moment they mattered most. The harm is the provision of unwanted care and the violation of the patient's values. The defense is to operationalize the directive into the orders, the decisions, and the communication.

### Protecting Privacy By Policy While Violating It In The Hallway

The privacy policy is robust, but the operational practices — the hallway conversations, the uncurtained exposures, the unauthorized record access — violate the privacy in the reality. The mechanism of harm is that the policy protects the privacy in the abstract while the practice violates it in the specific, so the patient who overhears their case discussed at the nurses' station, or who is exposed without need, experiences the violation directly, and the trust is eroded. The harm is the violation of privacy and confidentiality. The defense is to protect privacy in the operational practices, not only the policy.

### Recognizing Only The Intentional Violations And Missing The Bias And Workflow Violations

The nurse recognizes the rights violations that occur through malice or intent, but misses the violations that occur through bias (the undertreated pain, the unoffered information) or workflow (the routine that does not afford the time for the right). The mechanism of harm is that the bias and workflow violations are invisible to the clinician who shares the bias or operates within the workflow, so they recur unaddressed, and the patient who experiences them is not advocate for, because the violation was not recognized. The harm is the systematic violation of the rights of the patients who are subject to the bias or the workflow. The defense is to recognize the violations through bias and workflow, and to address them at both the individual and the system level.

### Not Escalating A Violation That Cannot Be Resolved At The Bedside

A rights violation is identified that the nurse cannot resolve at the bedside — it involves a policy, a provider, or a level of authority beyond the nurse — and it is not escalated to the institutional resources. The mechanism of harm is that the un-escalated violation festers, because the nurse lacks the authority or the perspective to resolve it, so the patient experiences the violation compounded by the institution's indifference, and the system that produced the violation is not addressed. The harm is a compounded violation and an unaddressed system. The defense is to escalate to the institutional resources, with a warm handoff and follow-up.
