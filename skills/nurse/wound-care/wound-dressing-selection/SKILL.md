# Wound Dressing Selection

The judgment problem in dressing selection is that the correct dressing depends on the wound's characteristics — exudate level, tissue type, depth, infection status, the periwound condition — and the failure mode is to default to a familiar dressing regardless of the wound, or to apply a principle (moist wound healing) without recognizing its limits (infected and ischemic wounds require different management). The skilled nurse matches the dressing to the wound's current state, reassesses as the wound changes, and understands the specific indications and contraindications of each dressing category. The goal is to create the environment the wound needs to heal: neither desiccated nor macerated, neither too dry nor too wet, with the bacteria managed and the tissue supported.

This is a high-stakes domain. The wrong dressing can delay healing, cause maceration or desiccation, trap infection, or damage the wound bed on removal. The nurse's role is to select and apply the dressing appropriate to the wound, to reassess and change the selection as the wound evolves, and to escalate wounds that require modalities beyond basic dressing care.

## Core Rules

### Apply Moist Wound Healing Principles, But Recognize Their Limits

Moist wound healing — maintaining a moist environment that supports cell migration and autolysis — is the standard for most chronic wounds, but it is not universal. The wound's condition determines whether moisture is appropriate.

- For granulating wounds with adequate blood supply, maintain a moist environment: hydrocolloids, hydrogels, alginates, foams depending on exudate.
- For dry, necrotic wounds (dry eschar), moisture (hydrogel) supports autolytic debridement, unless the wound is a dry ischemic eschar on a heel or toe, where dryness (eschar preservation) may be preferred until vascular evaluation.
- For infected wounds, moisture-retentive occlusive dressings may trap infection; use dressings that manage exudate and allow assessment, with antimicrobial agents where indicated.
- For heavily exuding wounds, the priority is exudate management to prevent maceration; absorptive dressings (foams, alginates, superabsorbents) and frequent changes.

### Match Dressing Type To Exudate Level

Exudate level is a primary determinant of dressing selection, because the dressing must manage the moisture without macerating the periwound or desiccating the bed.

- Heavy exudate: foams, alginates, hydrofibers, superabsorbent dressings; change frequency driven by saturation; protect periwound with barrier films.
- Moderate exudate: foams, hydrocolloids, alginates.
- Light exudate: hydrocolloids, thin foams, non-adherent dressings.
- Dry or minimal exudate: hydrogels to add moisture (for dry wounds needing autolysis) or non-adherent dressings for protection.

### Match Dressing Type To Tissue Type And Depth

The wound bed's tissue determines what the dressing should do: protect granulation, support autolysis of slough, manage necrosis, or fill dead space.

- Granulating wounds: non-adherent dressings (silicone, non-adherent contact layers) to protect the fragile tissue; moisture-retentive cover.
- Slough-covered wounds: moisture (hydrogels) or enzymatic debriders to support autolysis; or refer for debridement.
- Necrotic eschar: depends on location and perfusion — dry ischemic eschar may be kept dry (eschar preservation); moist eschar or eschar on well-perfused tissue may be debrided.
- Cavity and tunneling wounds: loosely pack dead space with appropriate dressing (alginate or hydrofiber rope), avoiding over-packing that collapses the wound or under-packing that leaves dead space.

### Protect The Periwound And Manage Maceration

Periwound maceration from exudate delays healing and enlarges the wound. Managing exudate and protecting the periwound are as important as the wound bed dressing.

- Use skin barrier films (no-sting barrier films) on the periwound to protect from exudate and adhesive damage.
- Select a dressing with adequate absorptive capacity for the exudate; if the periwound is macerated, the dressing is inadequate or the change frequency insufficient.
- Avoid adhesive dressings on fragile skin (elderly, steroid-treated) where they cause skin tears on removal; use silicone or non-adherent alternatives.

### Use Antimicrobial Dressings Judiciously

Antimicrobial dressings (silver, iodine, honey, chlorhexidine, PHMB) have a role in wounds with or at risk of infection or heavy bioburden, but they are not for routine use and their overuse contributes to resistance and cost.

- Use antimicrobial dressings for infected wounds, wounds with heavy bioburden delaying healing, or wounds at high risk of infection, for a defined period with reassessment.
- Reassess after 2-4 weeks; if no improvement, reconsider the treatment and obtain specialty consultation.
- Do not use antimicrobial dressings as a substitute for debridement, offloading, compression, or treatment of the underlying cause.

### Reassess And Change The Dressing Plan As The Wound Evolves

A wound that was heavily exuding may become lightly exuding as it heals; a wound that was dry may become moist; the dressing plan must evolve with the wound.

- Reassess the wound and the dressing's adequacy at each change.
- A wound that is not progressing requires reassessment of the plan: is the etiology addressed, is the dressing appropriate, is there infection, ischemia, or unrelieved pressure?

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To The Same Dressing For Every Wound

The nurse applies the same dressing — often a dry gauze or a familiar foam — to every wound regardless of exudate, tissue, depth, or etiology, because it is familiar and available, and the wound's specific needs are not matched. The mechanism is that the familiar dressing is quick and the principle of matching the dressing to the wound requires assessment that is skipped under time pressure, so a single dressing becomes the default and the wounds that need something different receive the wrong management. The harm is delayed healing (a dry gauze on a granulating wound desiccates the bed; an occlusive dressing on a heavy-exudate wound macerates the periwound), wasted product, and wounds that fail to progress because the dressing does not meet their needs. The defense is to assess the wound's exudate, tissue, and depth at each change and select the dressing accordingly, to know the indications for each dressing category, and to recognize that a single default dressing is appropriate for none of the wounds it is applied to except the one it suits.

### Using A Dry Dressing On A Wound That Needs Moisture

A granulating or slough-covered wound is dressed with dry gauze, which desiccates the wound bed, adheres to the granulation tissue, and causes bleeding and tissue damage on removal, reversing the healing. The mechanism is that dry gauze is the traditional default and the principle of moist wound healing is not applied, so the wound is managed in a way that actively impairs the cell migration and autolysis that moisture supports. The harm is delayed healing, pain and tissue damage on removal, and a wound that regresses under a dressing meant to protect it. The defense is to apply moist wound healing principles for wounds that benefit from moisture, to use non-adherent and moisture-retentive dressings for granulating and slough-covered wounds, and to reserve dry gauze for wounds where dryness is the goal (eschar preservation) or for packing heavily exuding cavities, recognizing that a dry dressing on a wound that needs moisture is harmful.

### Occluding An Infected Or Ischemic Wound

An infected wound is covered with an occlusive hydrocolloid that traps the exudate and bacteria, worsening the infection, or an ischemic wound with dry gangrene is occluded, accelerating deterioration. The mechanism is that occlusive dressings are effective for clean granulating wounds but trap moisture and bacteria in infected wounds, and the principle of occlusion is applied without assessing for infection or ischemia. The harm is worsening infection under the occlusion, rapid deterioration of ischemic tissue, and a wound that worsens under a dressing that would help a different wound. The defense is to assess for infection and ischemia before selecting an occlusive dressing, to avoid occlusive dressings on infected wounds (use exudate-managing and antimicrobial dressings with assessment access), to keep dry ischemic eschar dry until vascular evaluation, and to recognize that occlusion is contraindicated in specific wound states that must be ruled out before it is used.

### Over-Packing Or Under-Packing A Cavity Wound

A cavity or tunneling wound is packed too tightly, collapsing the wound edges and preventing healing from the base, or packed too loosely, leaving dead space that collects fluid and breeds infection. The mechanism is that packing requires judgment about the amount and the technique — enough to contact the surfaces and absorb exudate, not so much that it exerts pressure — and the default is to pack tightly to "fill" the wound, which applies pressure and collapses the healing space. The harm is delayed healing from collapsed edges, pain, and dead-space fluid collection that infects, or a wound that heals at the surface before the base (pocketing) and requires reopening. The defense is to pack loosely enough to contact all surfaces and absorb exudate without exerting pressure, to count and document packing pieces to ensure complete removal, to use appropriate packing materials (rope alginates or hydrofibers for cavities), and to recognize that the goal is to support healing from the base up, not to fill the wound tightly.

### Failing To Protect The Periwound, Causing Maceration And Enlargement

The wound bed is dressed well, but the exudate leaks onto the periwound and macerates the skin, which breaks down and enlarges the wound, because the dressing's absorptive capacity was inadequate or the periwound was not protected. The mechanism is that attention focuses on the wound bed and the periwound is treated as the area where the dressing is taped, not as skin at risk from the exudate, so the exudate macerates the periwound and the wound enlarges at the margins. The harm is wound enlargement, delayed healing, and pain, caused not by the wound's pathology but by the dressing's inadequacy. The defense is to match the dressing's absorptive capacity to the exudate, to apply skin barrier films to the periwound, to increase change frequency if saturation is occurring, and to recognize that periwound maceration is a dressing-failure sign requiring a change in plan, not a wound deterioration to accept.

### Keeping An Antimicrobial Dressing Indefinitely Without Reassessment

A silver dressing is applied to a wound and continued for weeks without reassessment, long after any benefit, contributing to cost and potentially to resistance, while the wound fails to progress because the underlying cause (pressure, ischemia, unmanaged exudate, untreated etiology) is unaddressed. The mechanism is that the antimicrobial dressing feels active and the wound's non-progression is attributed to ongoing bioburden rather than to the unaddressed cause, so the dressing continues while the real problem is not treated. The harm is wasted resource, potential resistance, and a wound that does not heal because the dressing is a substitute for addressing the etiology. The defense is to use antimicrobial dressings for a defined period with reassessment (typically 2-4 weeks), to discontinue if no improvement, to address the underlying cause (offloading, compression, vascular evaluation, pressure relief), and to recognize that an antimicrobial dressing without etiological treatment is not a wound care plan.

- [ ] Did I assess the wound's exudate level, tissue type, depth, infection status, and periwound condition before selecting the dressing?
- [ ] Did I match the dressing type to the exudate (absorptive for heavy, moisture-retentive for light/dry) and to the tissue (non-adherent for granulation, moisture for slough)?
- [ ] Did I apply moist wound healing principles for wounds that benefit, while keeping dry ischemic eschar dry and avoiding occlusion of infected wounds?
- [ ] Did I protect the periwound with barrier films and ensure the dressing's absorptive capacity prevents maceration?

- [ ] Did I use antimicrobial dressings judiciously, for a defined period with reassessment, and not as a substitute for treating the underlying cause?
- [ ] Did I avoid adhesive dressings on fragile skin, using silicone or non-adherent alternatives to prevent skin tears?
