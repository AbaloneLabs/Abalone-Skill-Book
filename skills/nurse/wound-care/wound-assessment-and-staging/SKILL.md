# Wound Assessment And Staging

The judgment problem in wound assessment is that accurate description requires disciplined, repeatable measurement and observation, and the failure modes are multiple: staging a wound that is not a pressure injury (staging applies only to pressure injuries, yet is often misapplied to arterial, venous, and diabetic ulcers), describing the wound vaguely ("healing well") rather than with the measurements that allow the next clinician to detect change, and assessing only what is visible while the deterioration is hidden under the dressing or in the periwound. The skilled nurse uses a structured assessment framework, measures consistently each time, documents precisely enough to track change, and knows when a wound's appearance signals infection, ischemia, or deterioration that requires escalation.

This is a high-stakes domain. Misassessment leads to inappropriate treatment, missed deterioration, and incorrect attribution of harm. The nurse's role is to assess accurately, stage only pressure injuries correctly, describe all wounds precisely, and escalate wounds that exceed nursing management (ischemic, infected, deteriorating).

## Core Rules

### Use A Structured, Repeatable Assessment Framework

A consistent framework ensures the assessment is complete and comparable across time and assessors. Use the same elements each time: location, size (length, width, depth), wound bed (tissue types and percentages), exudate (amount, color, odor), periwound skin, wound edges, and pain.

- Location: anatomical site, specified precisely (medial malleolus, not "left ankle").
- Size: length x width x depth in centimeters, measured consistently (head-to-toe for length, side-to-side for width); for undermining and tunneling, clock-face position and depth.
- Wound bed: percentages of granulation (red), slough (yellow), eschar (black), epithelialization (pink); the percentages should sum to 100.
- Exudate: amount (none, scant, moderate, heavy), color (serous, sanguineous, serosanguineous, purulent), and odor (none, malodorous).
- Periwound: color, temperature, intactness, maceration, induration, erythema.
- Edges: attached, rolled, undermined, epibole.
- Pain: presence, character, and relation to the wound and dressing change.

### Stage Only Pressure Injuries, And Stage Them Correctly

Staging (Category 1-4, plus unstageable and deep tissue pressure injury) applies only to pressure injuries. It does not apply to arterial, venous, diabetic, surgical, or traumatic wounds, which are described by their etiology and characteristics, not staged.

- Stage 1: non-blanchable erythema of intact skin.
- Stage 2: partial-thickness loss of skin (epidermis and part of dermis); shallow open ulcer with red-pink wound bed, no slough.
- Stage 3: full-thickness loss; subcutaneous fat may be visible, but bone, tendon, muscle not exposed; may have undermining.
- Stage 4: full-thickness loss with exposed bone, tendon, or muscle; often includes undermining and tunneling.
- Unstageable: full-thickness loss in which the base is covered by slough or eschar, preventing staging.
- Deep tissue pressure injury: persistent non-blanchable deep red, maroon, or purple intact skin, or blood-filled blister.
- Mucosal membrane pressure injury cannot be staged by this system.

### Differentiate Wound Types By Etiology Because Treatment Differs Fundamentally

Wound treatment depends on etiology; misidentifying the type leads to harmful treatment.

- Arterial ulcers: distal, painful, punched-out edges, pale, minimal exudate; often on toes, feet; require vascular evaluation; compression is contraindicated.
- Venous ulcers: medial lower leg, shallow, irregular edges, moderate to heavy exudate; compression is the primary treatment.
- Diabetic/neuropathic ulcers: plantar pressure points, painless, often deep with callus, may have underlying infection; require offloading and vascular and neurological evaluation.
- Surgical wounds: classify by closure (primary, secondary, delayed primary) and complication (dehiscence, infection).
- Moisture-associated skin damage is not a pressure injury and should not be staged.

### Assess For Infection Using Beyond The Surface Signs

Wound infection is assessed by the signs of infection (increased pain, erythema, edema, heat, purulent exudate, odor) and by systemic signs (fever, leukocytosis), but chronic wounds may be colonized without infected, and the distinction requires clinical judgment.

- Increasing pain in a previously painless wound is a significant sign of infection.
- Cellulitis (spreading erythema, warmth) in the periwound requires provider notification.
- Systemic signs (fever, confusion in elderly, rising white count) indicate systemic infection requiring urgent treatment.
- Do not culture a wound surface routinely; surface cultures reflect colonization. Culture is taken from clean debrided tissue or by biopsy when infection is suspected and after debridement.

### Remove The Dressing And Assess The Actual Wound, Not The Impression

A common failure is to chart the wound based on the prior assessment or the dressing's appearance, without removing the dressing and observing the wound bed. The wound must be observed directly at each assessment.

- Remove the dressing, clean the wound per protocol, and observe the bed, the periwound, and the exudate on the old dressing.
- The old dressing's saturation (amount, color, odor) is data about exudate.
- Document the assessment based on direct observation, not on the prior note.

### Recognize Deterioration And Escalate

Wounds that are enlarging, deepening, showing new necrosis, developing cellulitis, or producing systemic signs require escalation, not continued routine care.

- A wound that increases in size or depth, develops new eschar, or shows exposed bone requires provider notification and often specialty consultation (wound care, vascular, surgery, infectious disease).
- Ischemic wounds (cold, pale, painful) require urgent vascular evaluation.
- Signs of sepsis from a wound source require immediate escalation.

## Common Traps

### Staging Wounds That Are Not Pressure Injuries

A venous ulcer on the medial malleolus is documented as a "Stage 3," applying the pressure injury staging to a wound of different etiology, because the staging system is familiar and the wound looks deep. The mechanism is that the staging system is well-known and the nurse defaults to it for any open wound, but staging is etiology-specific and misapplies the classification to wounds whose treatment and prognosis differ fundamentally. The harm is that the wound is misclassified in the record, the wrong treatment may follow (compression for a venous ulcer, not pressure-injury care), quality data is corrupted (pressure injury rates inflated), and the wound's actual etiology is obscured. The defense is to stage only pressure injuries, to describe other wounds by etiology and characteristics, and to recognize that staging is a pressure-injury tool, not a general wound-depth scale.

### Vague Description That Prevents Detection Of Change

The wound is documented as "healing well," "improving," or "2 cm wound, clean" without the measurements, tissue percentages, exudate description, and periwound condition that would allow the next clinician to detect improvement or deterioration. The mechanism is that vague description is quick and feels safe (it cannot be wrong because it asserts little), but it strips the record of the information needed to track change, and the wound's trajectory becomes invisible. The harm is that deterioration is missed because the prior notes do not establish a precise baseline, treatment effectiveness cannot be evaluated, and the record cannot support the clinical decisions that depend on detecting change. The defense is to document precise, structured measurements at each assessment — size, tissue types and percentages, exudate, periwound, pain — using the same method each time, so that change is detectable and the record supports clinical judgment.

### Assessing The Impression Rather Than The Actual Wound

The nurse charts the wound based on the prior note ("2 cm, granulating") or on the dressing's appearance, without removing the dressing and observing the wound bed, and the actual wound has enlarged, developed slough, or shows signs of infection that the impression-based note does not capture. The mechanism is that removing the dressing, cleaning, and observing takes time and the prior note feels likely to still be accurate, so the assessment is carried forward without direct observation, and the record asserts findings that were not verified. The harm is that deterioration is not detected because the wound was not observed, and the record falsely asserts stability, delaying the response to real change. The defense is to remove the dressing and observe the wound directly at each assessment, to clean per protocol before observing, to use the old dressing's saturation as exudate data, and to document only what was directly observed, because a carried-forward assessment is a falsified assessment.

### Missing Periwound Deterioration Or Maceration

The wound bed is documented carefully, but the periwound skin — macerated from exudate, cellulitic, or breaking down at the edges — is not assessed, and the periwound deterioration that often precedes wound enlargement is missed. The mechanism is that attention focuses on the wound bed (the obvious target) and the periwound is treated as background, but the periwound reflects the wound's environment and the dressing's adequacy, and maceration and cellulitis there signal problems that will affect the wound. The harm is that periwound maceration leads to wound enlargement, cellulitis signals infection, and these are missed because the periwound was not assessed, and the treatment continues unchanged while the wound deteriorates at the margins. The defense is to assess the periwound at every dressing change — color, temperature, intactness, maceration, induration, erythema — and to recognize that the periwound is part of the wound assessment, not background.

### Misidentifying Wound Etiology And Applying The Wrong Treatment

A painful, distal, punched-out ulcer on the toe is treated with compression as a venous ulcer, when it is an arterial ulcer, and the compression worsens the ischemia. The mechanism is that the wound's appearance is interpreted without the vascular and etiological assessment that distinguishes arterial from venous from diabetic, and a familiar treatment (compression) is applied to a wound for which it is contraindicated. The harm is that the wrong treatment worsens the wound — compression on an arterial ulcer can precipitate critical ischemia — and the underlying vascular disease goes unaddressed. The defense is to assess the etiology (vascular status, sensation, pressure points, history) before selecting treatment, to obtain vascular evaluation for lower-extremity wounds with uncertain etiology, and to recognize that wound treatment is etiology-specific and that applying a generic treatment without etiological assessment can cause harm.

### Under-Recognizing Infection In Chronic Wounds

A chronic wound is assumed to be "just colonized," and increasing pain, new erythema, and increased exudate are attributed to the wound's chronicity rather than to infection, so the infection progresses to cellulitis or sepsis before it is treated. The mechanism is that chronic wounds are often colonized, so the signs of infection are normalized and attributed to the baseline, and the change that signals clinical infection is missed because the wound was "always like that." The harm is that infection progresses, cellulitis develops, and in diabetic or immunocompromised patients the infection can become limb-threatening or systemic before treatment begins. The defense is to assess for change from the wound's baseline at each assessment — increasing pain (especially new pain in a previously painless wound), new or spreading erythema, increased exudate or change to purulent, new odor, systemic signs — and to escalate suspected infection with provider notification, recognizing that chronic wounds can become infected and that change from baseline is the signal.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I use a structured, repeatable framework (location, size L/W/D, tissue types and percentages, exudate, periwound, edges, pain) and measure consistently each time?
- [ ] Did I stage only pressure injuries, using the correct stage (1-4, unstageable, deep tissue), and describe other wounds by etiology and characteristics?
- [ ] Did I differentiate the wound's etiology (arterial, venous, diabetic, surgical, moisture-associated) before selecting treatment, and obtain vascular evaluation for uncertain lower-extremity wounds?
- [ ] Did I remove the dressing, clean the wound, and observe the bed and periwound directly, rather than charting from the prior note or the dressing's appearance?
- [ ] Did I assess the periwound for maceration, cellulitis, induration, and breakdown, treating it as part of the wound assessment?
- [ ] Did I assess for infection by change from baseline (new pain, erythema, purulent exudate, odor, systemic signs) rather than normalizing signs as chronic colonization?
- [ ] Did I document precisely enough to allow detection of change — measurements, percentages, exudate, periwound — rather than vague impressions?
- [ ] Did I escalate wounds that are enlarging, deepening, showing new necrosis or cellulitis, ischemic, or producing systemic signs, with provider notification and specialty consultation?
