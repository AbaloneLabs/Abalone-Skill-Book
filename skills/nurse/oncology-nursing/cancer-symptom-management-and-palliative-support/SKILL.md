# Cancer Symptom Management and Palliative Support

Cancer and its treatment produce a constellation of symptoms that erode quality of life long before they become life-threatening. The judgment problem is that these symptoms are often undertreated because they are normalized as "part of cancer," because patients underreport to avoid bothering the team, and because aggressive symptom management is sometimes confused with hastening death. The oncology nurse is the primary advocate for the patient's comfort and function throughout the trajectory — from diagnosis, through treatment, to survivorship or end of life — and must assess, treat, and escalate symptoms with the same rigor applied to the cancer itself.

## Core Rules

### Assess Pain Systematically and Treat It Aggressively, Including Opioid-Tolerant Patients

Cancer pain is undertreated in a significant proportion of patients, and the consequences are suffering, immobility, depression, and loss of function. Assess pain at every encounter using a validated scale, characterize it (somatic, visceral, neuropathic — the type determines the treatment), and treat according to the WHO analgesic ladder with around-the-clock dosing for persistent pain plus breakthrough doses. For opioid-tolerant patients (those on chronic opioids, with a history of substance use, or with high acute tolerance), do not underdose out of fear — calculate the baseline opioid need, add appropriate breakthrough dosing (10-20% of the total daily dose), and titrate to effect. Use adjuvants for neuropathic pain (gabapentinoids, antidepressants) and for bone pain (NSAIDs, bisphosphonates, radiation). Reassess after every intervention. Pain that is not controlled is a failure of management, not an expected outcome.

### Manage Nausea and Vomiting by Mechanism, Not by Habit

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV) has distinct mechanisms — acute (serotonin-mediated, within 24 hours), delayed (substance P-mediated, 24 hours to days), anticipatory (conditioned, before treatment) — and each requires different agents. Match the antiemetic regimen to the emetogenic potential of the regimen and to the patient's risk factors (young, female, history of motion sickness or nausea in pregnancy are higher risk). For highly emetogenic regimens, use a four-drug combination (5-HT3 antagonist, NK1 antagonist, dexamethasone, olanzapine). Do not rely on a single agent or on pro re nata (PRN) dosing for predictable CINV — give antiemetics on a schedule. For breakthrough nausea, add agents from different classes. Assess bowel function (constipation from opioids and antiemetics worsens nausea) and treat prophylactically.

### Address Cancer-Related Fatigue and Cachexia as Treatable, Not Inevitable

Cancer-related fatigue is the most common and most distressing symptom, and it is often dismissed as unavoidable. Assess it specifically (it is not the same as tiredness, and it is not relieved by rest), identify reversible contributors (anemia, hypothyroidism, depression, dehydration, sleep disturbance, medication side effects), and intervene — exercise (even modest activity reduces fatigue), energy conservation, sleep hygiene, and treatment of underlying causes. Cancer cachexia (involuntary weight loss with muscle wasting, distinct from starvation) is driven by tumor metabolism and inflammation; involve a dietitian early, consider appetite stimulants and nutritional supplements, and set realistic goals (stabilization rather than reversal in advanced disease). The nurse who accepts fatigue and weight loss as inevitable misses opportunities to improve function and quality of life.

### Manage Dyspnea as Both a Physiologic and a Subjective Symptom

Dyspnea in cancer (from pleural effusion, lymphangitic spread, pulmonary embolism, anemia, COPD, heart failure, or anxiety) is terrifying, and the terror worsens the breathlessness. Treat the reversible causes (drain the effusion, anticoagulate the embolism, transfuse for anemia, treat heart failure), but also treat the symptom directly — opioids at low dose reduce the sensation of breathlessness without significantly depressing respiration, positioning (upright, leaning forward) improves mechanics, fans and cool air reduce the perception, and anxiety management (breathing techniques, anxiolytics, presence) breaks the dyspnea-anxiety cycle. Oxygen helps only if the patient is hypoxemic — do not assume oxygen is the treatment for breathlessness. Reassess and adjust.

### Screen for and Treat Distress, Depression, and Anxiety Throughout the Trajectory

Cancer carries a high burden of psychological distress — depression, anxiety, fear of recurrence, existential distress, body image changes, relationship strain — and untreated distress worsens symptoms, reduces treatment adherence, and increases suffering. Screen at every visit with a brief validated tool (Distress Thermometer, PHQ-9, GAD-7), normalize the experience (distress is expected, not a failure), and intervene — supportive counseling, psycho-oncology referral, support groups, mindfulness, and medication when indicated. Be alert to suicidal ideation, which is elevated in cancer patients, and assess directly. The nurse who attends only to the physical disease treats half the patient.

### Coordinate Palliative Care Early, Not Only at End of Life

Palliative care is the specialty of symptom management and quality of life, and early involvement (ideally at diagnosis of advanced or metastatic disease, concurrent with disease-directed treatment) improves symptoms, reduces hospitalizations, and in some studies extends survival. Refer early, not as a "last resort" or as synonymous with hospice. Palliative care teams address pain and symptom management, goals of care, advance care planning, psychosocial and spiritual support, and care coordination. The oncology nurse and the palliative team work together: the nurse provides continuity and day-to-day management, the palliative team provides specialized expertise. Advocate for the referral and explain to the patient that palliative care is an added layer of support, not a sign of giving up.

### Provide Compassionate, Skilled End-of-Life Care When the Time Comes

When disease-directed treatment is no longer effective or desired, the focus shifts entirely to comfort, dignity, and support for the patient and family. Manage symptoms aggressively (pain, dyspnea, agitation, secretions, nausea), discontinue non-comfort interventions (routine vitals, labs, monitoring), provide presence and reassurance, support the family (explain what they are seeing, what to expect, how to be present), and facilitate the transition to hospice if not already in place. Use the nursing presence — the time at the bedside, the skilled assessment, the human connection — as a therapeutic intervention in itself. The goal of end-of-life care is a good death: as comfortable, as conscious or peaceful as the patient wishes, with the people they want present, in the place they choose.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Pain as Expected and Opioids as Too Risky

The trap is that cancer pain is normalized ("of course they're in pain, they have cancer") and opioids are feared (addiction, respiratory depression, regulatory scrutiny), so the patient is undermedicated and suffers. The harm is unnecessary suffering, immobility with its complications, and erosion of trust in the care team. In the context of active cancer and palliative symptom management, the risk of addiction is low, and respiratory depression at appropriate doses is rare. Assess pain, treat it adequately, use the ladder, and escalate to specialists (pain, palliative) when standard approaches are insufficient. A cancer patient in pain deserves the same aggressive treatment as any other emergency.

### Using a Single Antiemetic for All CINV

The trap is that one agent (often ondansetron) is given for all chemotherapy nausea, regardless of the emetogenic potential of the regimen or the timing of the symptoms, so the patient with delayed vomiting (needing an NK1 antagonist) or with anticipatory nausea (needing an anxiolytic) continues to suffer. The harm is dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, treatment refusal, and eroded quality of life. Match the antiemetic regimen to the mechanism and the emetogenic risk. For highly emetogenic regimens, use a multi-drug combination on a schedule. Assess the timing of the nausea to identify the mechanism.

### Dismissing Fatigue and Weight Loss as the Natural Course

The trap is that fatigue and cachexia are attributed to the cancer itself, so no reversible contributors are sought and no interventions are offered, and the patient declines functionally. The harm is loss of independence, reduced tolerance of treatment, and worsened outcomes. Assess fatigue specifically, check for anemia and hypothyroidism, treat depression, encourage activity, and involve a dietitian for nutritional support. Even when the cancer is the primary driver, symptom management can stabilize function and improve quality of life.

### Withholding Opioids for Dyspnea Because the Patient Is Not in Pain

The trap is that opioids are perceived as pain medications, so they are not considered for the dyspneic patient who reports no pain, and the patient continues to struggle for breath with only oxygen (which may not help) and reassurance (which does not). The harm is prolonged, terrifying breathlessness. Low-dose opioids reduce the sensation of dyspnea in most patients, including those without pain, without significantly depressing respiration in the appropriate dose. Assess dyspnea with the same rigor as pain, and treat it with the same aggressiveness.

### Treating Palliative Care as Synonymous with Hospice or Giving Up

The trap is that palliative care is introduced only when the patient is actively dying, or not at all, because the team or the patient perceives it as "giving up" on disease-directed treatment. The harm is that the patient misses months of symptom relief, psychosocial support, and care coordination that would have improved quality of life — and, in some cases, extended it. Refer palliative care early, at diagnosis of advanced disease, concurrent with oncology treatment. Explain clearly that palliative care is an added layer of support focused on quality of life, not a withdrawal of treatment.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed pain at every encounter, characterized it by type, and treated it to a tolerable level — or am I accepting pain as expected?
- For CINV, is the antiemetic regimen matched to the emetogenic potential and the mechanism, given on a schedule, not just PRN?
- For fatigue and weight loss, have I sought reversible contributors and intervened, or am I attributing them to the cancer?
- For the dyspneic patient, have I treated the symptom directly (opioids, positioning, anxiety) as well as the underlying cause?
- Have I screened for distress, depression, and anxiety at this visit, and intervened or referred?
- Has palliative care been involved early — at diagnosis of advanced disease — not only at end of life?
- For the patient approaching end of life, are symptoms aggressively managed, non-comfort interventions discontinued, and the family supported?
- Am I using my presence and skilled assessment as a therapeutic intervention, especially at the end of life?
- Have I had the goals-of-care conversation before the crisis, and does the care plan reflect the patient's values?
- For the patient I am sending home, do they have a symptom management plan and a number to call for worsening symptoms?
