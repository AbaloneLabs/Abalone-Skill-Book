# Oncologic Emergencies and Complication Management

Cancer and its treatment can produce a distinct set of acute, life-threatening complications that progress over hours. The judgment problem is that these emergencies often present with subtle, nonspecific symptoms — back pain, constipation, mild confusion, a low-grade fever — that are easy to attribute to the patient's underlying illness or to treatment side effects. The nurse who recognizes an oncologic emergency early and initiates the right response can prevent irreversible harm; the nurse who waits for clear deterioration is often too late. Every oncology nurse must carry a mental catalog of these emergencies and a low threshold to act on them.

## Core Rules

### Treat Fever in the Neutropenic Patient as a Medical Emergency

Febrile neutropenia (oral temperature ≥ 38.3°C once, or ≥ 38.0°C sustained over an hour, with an absolute neutrophil count < 500) is a true emergency — the mortality from untreated sepsis in this population is high. When a neutropenic patient reports fever, act immediately: draw blood cultures (peripheral and from each lumen of any central catheter), obtain a CBC and basic labs, perform a focused physical exam looking for sources (oral mucosa, perirectal area, skin, catheter site, lungs), and administer the first dose of broad-spectrum antibiotics within one hour of presentation — do not wait for culture results. Document the temperature, the ANC, the cultures drawn, the antibiotic given, and the time. The door-to-antibiotic time is a quality metric because it saves lives.

### Recognize Tumor Lysis Syndrome Before It Causes Renal Failure

Tumor lysis syndrome (TLS) occurs when rapid tumor cell breakdown — usually at the start of treatment for hematologic cancers with high tumor burden (leukemia, lymphoma) — releases intracellular contents, causing hyperuricemia, hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, and hypocalcemia, leading to acute kidney injury and arrhythmias. Identify patients at risk before treatment starts, initiate hydration (to maintain high urine output), administer uric acid-lowering therapy (rasburicase or allopurinol) per protocol, and monitor labs (potassium, phosphorus, calcium, uric acid, creatinine) every 4-6 hours during the risk window. Recognize the signs of evolving TLS — weakness, cramping, arrhythmias, oliguria — and escalate immediately. TLS is predictable and preventable when anticipated; it is lethal when missed.

### Suspect Spinal Cord Compression in Any Cancer Patient with New Back Pain

Malignant spinal cord compression (MSCC) is a neurological emergency — the window to preserve function is hours, not days. Any cancer patient (especially with breast, lung, prostate, or myeloma) who reports new or worsening back pain, particularly with radicular features, needs urgent evaluation. The red flags are progressive back pain, pain worse on lying or coughing, weakness or sensory changes in the legs, bowel or bladder dysfunction (retention or incontinence), or gait changes. Assess neurologically (motor, sensory, reflexes, sphincter tone), keep the patient flat if cord compression is suspected, notify the oncologist immediately, and facilitate urgent MRI. The patient who walks into the hospital may not walk out if the diagnosis is delayed — motor function lost before treatment is often permanent.

### Identify Superior Vena Cava Syndrome and Monitor for Airway Compromise

Superior vena cava syndrome (SVCS), usually from a mediastinal mass (lung cancer, lymphoma), causes facial and upper extremity swelling, distended neck and chest veins, dyspnea, and cough. While often subacute, it can become an airway emergency if the mass compresses the trachea. Assess the patient's airway and breathing continuously, elevate the head of the bed, avoid upper extremity IV placement (the venous return is compromised), and facilitate urgent imaging and oncology evaluation for radiation or stenting. The danger sign is stridor or worsening dyspnea — escalate to the emergency response team immediately.

### Manage Hypercalcemia of Malignancy as a Cause of Altered Mental Status

Cancer-related hypercalcemia (from bone metastases or paraneoplastic PTHrP production) presents with confusion, constipation, polyuria, fatigue, and dehydration — symptoms easily attributed to the cancer or to medications. Check calcium (corrected for albumin, or ionized) in any cancer patient with new neurological or gastrointestinal symptoms. Treat with aggressive isotonic saline hydration, then a bisphosphonate or denosumab per protocol, and monitor for rebound. Untreated hypercalcemia progresses to coma and arrhythmia. The nurse who recognizes that the "confused" cancer patient has a correctable metabolic emergency saves a life.

### Recognize Neutropenic Enterocolitis (Typhlitis) in the Neutropenic Patient with Abdominal Pain

Neutropenic enterocolitis, an inflammation of the cecum and bowel wall during severe neutropenia, presents with fever, abdominal pain (often right lower quadrant), distension, and diarrhea — and can progress to perforation and sepsis. Any neutropenic patient with abdominal pain needs urgent evaluation: NPO, IV fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics covering gut flora, surgical consultation, and imaging (CT). Do not give antispasmodics or antimotility agents, which can worsen the condition. The nurse who attributes the pain to "a stomach bug" and delays evaluation may face a perforated bowel hours later.

### Coordinate Rapid Response and Palliative Involvement for Oncologic Emergencies

Oncologic emergencies require coordinated, rapid response: the oncologist, the nurse, the pharmacist, often the intensivist, and sometimes radiation oncology or surgery. Use structured communication (SBAR) to convey the situation. At the same time, involve palliative care early — not only for end-of-life, but for symptom management, goals-of-care conversations, and support through acute complications. For the patient with advanced cancer experiencing an emergency, the question of "what would the patient want if this gets worse?" should be addressed before the crisis, not during it. The nurse who advocates for these conversations ensures that the response to the emergency aligns with the patient's values.

## Common Traps

### Attributing Fever in the Neutropenic Patient to a Non-Infectious Cause

The trap is that the neutropenic patient with a low-grade fever looks well, or that the fever is attributed to a recent blood transfusion, dehydration, or tumor fever, so antibiotics are delayed pending further workup. The harm is that in neutropenia, infection can progress to septic shock within hours, and the lack of neutrophils means the usual signs (pus, infiltrate on x-ray) are absent — the only sign may be fever. Treat every fever in a neutropenic patient as infectious until proven otherwise. Give antibiotics within the hour. The workup can continue, but the antibiotics cannot wait.

### Missing TLS Because the Labs Were "Normal Yesterday"

The trap is that TLS risk is assessed once at treatment start, and if the first set of labs is fine, the monitoring frequency is reduced, so the potassium or uric acid that spikes at 24 or 48 hours is missed. The harm is acute kidney injury and fatal arrhythmia from a predictable, preventable complication. TLS risk persists for 48-72 hours after treatment initiation in high-risk patients. Monitor labs on the schedule the protocol requires, not less frequently because the first values were reassuring. The trend matters as much as the absolute value.

### Dismissing Back Pain as Degenerative or Musculoskeletal

The trap is that back pain is extremely common, and in a cancer patient it is easy to attribute to immobility, age, or treatment side effects, so the neurological exam is deferred or the MRI is scheduled routinely rather than urgently. The harm is irreversible paraplegia from cord compression that could have been treated. Any new or progressive back pain in a patient with a cancer known to metastasize to spine is cord compression until proven otherwise. Assess neurologically, escalate urgently, and facilitate same-day imaging. The cost of over-triaging is an MRI; the cost of under-triaging is a wheelchair.

### Treating Hypercalcemia Symptoms Without Checking Calcium

The trap is that confusion, constipation, and fatigue are attributed to the cancer, to opioids, or to depression, and are treated symptomatically without a metabolic workup, so the hypercalcemia progresses. The harm is coma, arrhythmia, and death from a treatable condition. Include calcium (corrected for albumin) in the routine metabolic panel for any cancer patient with new neurological or gastrointestinal symptoms. The treatment is straightforward and effective when the diagnosis is made.

### Delaying Antibiotics for a "Better" Culture in the Neutropenic Patient

The trap is that the nurse or team wants to repeat the blood cultures, or wait for a peripheral IV to place a new line for culture, before giving antibiotics, to avoid "contaminating" the culture results. The harm is that the delay in antibiotics increases mortality in febrile neutropenia. Cultures are important, but they are not more important than timely antibiotics. Draw what cultures you can, then give the antibiotics within the hour. The infectious disease team can interpret cultures taken around the antibiotic dose; they cannot reverse septic shock from a delay.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- For any neutropenic patient with fever, have I drawn cultures and administered broad-spectrum antibiotics within one hour?
- For patients at risk for TLS, am I monitoring labs at the protocol frequency for the full 48-72 hour risk window, not just the first day?
- For any cancer patient with new back pain, have I performed a neurological exam and escalated for urgent imaging?
- For the cancer patient with new confusion, constipation, or fatigue, have I checked corrected calcium?
- For the patient with facial swelling and distended veins, have I assessed the airway and avoided upper-extremity IV access?
- For the neutropenic patient with abdominal pain, am I keeping them NPO and escalating for surgical and imaging evaluation?
- In an oncologic emergency, am I using structured communication and coordinating the team — oncology, pharmacy, ICU, radiation, palliative?
- Have I addressed goals of care and involved palliative care before the next crisis, not during it?
- For the patient I am sending home, do they know which symptoms are red flags and which number to call?
- Am I carrying a mental catalog of oncologic emergencies and a low threshold to act on them?
