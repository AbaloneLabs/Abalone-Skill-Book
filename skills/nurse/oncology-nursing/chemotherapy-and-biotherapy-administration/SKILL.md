# Chemotherapy and Biotherapy Administration

Systemic anticancer therapy is one of the highest-risk medication classes in nursing. The judgment problem is that these drugs have narrow therapeutic windows, are lethal to healthy tissue on extravasation, require precise dose calculations often based on body surface area, follow complex multi-agent regimens with specific sequencing, and produce side effects that can range from mild to fatal. The nurse is the final safety check before a drug that can cure cancer also maims or kills the patient. Compounding the risk, oncology patients are often immunocompromised, nutritionally depleted, and psychologically vulnerable, so errors compound quickly.

## Core Rules

### Verify the Order, the Dose, and the Patient with Independent Double Checks

Every chemotherapy order requires independent double verification by two qualified nurses before administration. Verify the drug name, dose, route, rate, sequence within the regimen, cycle number, and date against the protocol. Confirm the dose calculation against the patient's current body surface area (recalculate, do not trust the label), and check that renal and hepatic function support the dose. Verify the patient identity with two identifiers, and confirm informed consent for this cycle is documented. The double check must be independent — two nurses calculating separately, then comparing — not one nurse watching the other sign. Document the verification. The nurse who skips or rushes this step is one calculation error away from a fatal overdose.

### Know the Drug's Mechanism, Toxicity Profile, and Extravasation Risk

Each systemic anticancer agent has a distinct toxicity profile that dictates monitoring and precautions. Anthracyclines (doxorubicin) are vesicants with cumulative cardiotoxicity — track the lifetime dose and watch for heart failure. Platinum agents (cisplatin) are nephrotoxic and ototoxic — ensure hydration and monitor creatinine. Taxanes (paclitaxel) cause hypersensitivity reactions — premedicate and have rescue available. Immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors) causes immune-related adverse events that can affect any organ and may emerge weeks after the infusion. Know whether the drug is a vesicant (causes tissue necrosis on extravasation), an irritant, or non-vesicant, and know the specific antidote. A nurse who administers chemotherapy without knowing the toxicity profile cannot recognize the early signs of a life-threatening complication.

### Establish Reliable Access Before Infusion and Monitor the Site Continuously

For vesicants and many cytotoxic agents, a centrally placed catheter (port, PICC, tunneled line) is the access of choice because peripheral extravasation causes severe tissue injury. Verify patency with a brisk saline flush and confirm blood return before starting the infusion. For peripheral administration of vesicants, use a newly placed, well-anchored IV in the forearm (not the antecubital, wrist, or hand), and monitor the site every 5-10 minutes during the infusion. Educate the patient to report any pain, burning, swelling, or change in sensation immediately. If extravasation is suspected, stop the infusion immediately, leave the catheter in place, aspirate residual drug, disconnect the tubing, and initiate the extravasation protocol (cold or warm compress, antidote per drug) — then document thoroughly and notify the oncologist within minutes.

### Premedicate, Monitor, and Respond to Infusion Reactions

Many anticancer agents cause infusion reactions ranging from mild flushing to anaphylaxis. Premedicate per protocol (antihistamines, corticosteroids, antipyretics for taxanes, rituximab, platins), start the infusion at a slow rate with a test dose where indicated, and stay at the bedside for the first 15-30 minutes when most reactions occur. Have emergency equipment and medications (epinephrine, oxygen, IV fluids, airway supplies) immediately accessible. Distinguish a mild reaction (flushing, mild rash — slow the rate, treat symptoms) from a severe one (hypotension, bronchospasm, angioedema — stop the infusion, initiate emergency response). Document the reaction, the intervention, and the outcome, and communicate with the oncologist about whether re-challenge (often with a slower rate and more aggressive premedication) is appropriate.

### Manage the Side-Effect Cascade Proactively, Not Reactively

Chemotherapy side effects are predictable by drug class, and proactive management reduces severity and prevents complications. Assess and manage nausea and vomiting with a regimen matched to the emetogenic potential of the drugs (5-HT3 antagonist, NK1 antagonist, dexamethasone for highly emetogenic regimens). Monitor for myelosuppression — neutropenia (fever is an emergency), thrombocytopenia (bleeding risk), anemia (fatigue, transfusion need) — with CBCs timed to the nadir. Assess for mucositis, diarrhea, constipation, peripheral neuropathy, skin changes, and fatigue at every visit, and intervene early. Teach patients to take their temperature, to recognize the signs of infection, and to call immediately for fever during neutropenia. The nurse who waits for the patient to report severe symptoms has missed the window for prevention.

### Educate the Patient and Caregiver as Partners in Safety

Oncology treatment happens mostly at home, between visits, and the patient and caregiver are the front line of safety. Teach them, in writing and verbally, about the regimen schedule, the expected side effects and their management, the red-flag symptoms that require immediate contact (fever, uncontrolled vomiting, bleeding, new pain, rash, shortness of breath), safe handling of bodily fluids for 48 hours after treatment (double-flush toilets, wear gloves for spills, separate laundry), and the contact numbers for questions and emergencies. Use teach-back: ask the patient to repeat when to call and what number to call. A patient who does not know that fever during chemotherapy is an emergency may wait hours before seeking care, and those hours can be fatal.

### Coordinate Care Across the Oncology Team and the Patient's Life

Cancer treatment is a multi-disciplinary, longitudinal process involving oncologists, nurses, pharmacists, social workers, dietitians, palliative care, and the patient's primary care provider. Coordinate proactively: ensure lab results are reviewed before each cycle, that imaging is scheduled and results are communicated, that supportive care medications are refilled, that psychosocial needs are addressed, and that the patient understands the overall plan. Communicate changes in the regimen, side effects, or functional status to the whole team. Advocate for the patient's quality of life throughout treatment — the goal is not just tumor response but a person who can live through the treatment.

## Common Traps

### Trusting the Label Without Recalculating the Dose

The trap is that the pharmacy label feels authoritative, and recalculating body-surface-area-based doses feels redundant, so the nurse administers based on the label alone. But pharmacy errors happen — a wrong weight, a wrong height, a decimal error — and in chemotherapy a ten-fold error can be fatal. The harm is overdose causing severe myelosuppression, organ failure, or death, or underdose causing treatment failure. Always recalculate the dose from the current BSA and verify against the order as part of the independent double check. The few minutes of math is the difference between safe and lethal.

### Administering a Vesicant Through a Peripheral Line That Is Not Fresh and Patent

The trap is that a working peripheral IV is convenient and a central line placement takes time, so the nurse proceeds with a peripheral line that has been in for days or shows sluggish blood return. The harm is that vesicant extravasation through a compromised line causes severe tissue necrosis that can require skin grafting or amputation, and the injury may not be apparent until hours after the infusion. The convenience is not worth the risk. For vesicants, use a central line, or place a fresh, well-anchored peripheral IV and confirm brisk blood return and easy flush before starting. If there is any doubt about the line, stop and get better access.

### Dismissing Early Infusion Reaction Signs as Anxiety

The trap is that the first signs of a reaction — flushing, a tickle in the throat, mild chest tightness, restlessness — are nonspecific and easily attributed to anxiety, especially in a nervous first-time patient. The harm is that the reaction progresses to anaphylaxis before the nurse recognizes it as a medical emergency. Take any new symptom during the first infusion of a high-risk agent seriously: slow or stop the infusion, assess vital signs, and stay with the patient. It is far better to stop a non-reaction and restart slowly than to miss an evolving anaphylaxis.

### Waiting for the Patient to Report Severe Symptoms

The trap is that the nurse assesses side effects by asking "how are you feeling?" and accepts "fine" at face value, missing the gradual onset of dehydration from diarrhea, the developing infection during neutropenia, or the progressive neuropathy that will limit function. The harm is that preventable complications become emergencies — the neutropenic patient who reports fever at 39°C has already been infected for hours. Assess proactively with specific questions and objective measures: temperature, oral intake, output, bowel pattern, sensation, weight. Schedule labs at the nadir. Call the patient between visits if indicated. Do not rely on patient-initiated reporting for predictable, dangerous complications.

### Failing to Recognize Immune-Related Adverse Events from Immunotherapy

The trap is that immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors) is perceived as gentler than chemotherapy, with fewer traditional side effects, so the nurse and patient do not connect a new rash, diarrhea, fatigue, or endocrine symptom with the infusion — especially when the symptom appears weeks later. The harm is that immune-related adverse events (colitis, pneumonitis, hepatitis, thyroiditis, hypophysitis) can be fatal if not recognized and treated with corticosteroids and immunosuppression. Teach patients that immunotherapy side effects are different and can appear any time, assess for them at every visit, and have a low threshold to evaluate and involve the oncologist.

## Self-Check

- Have two qualified nurses independently verified the drug, dose, BSA calculation, route, rate, sequence, and cycle against the protocol?
- For each drug I am giving, can I state the toxicity profile, the extravasation risk, and the antidote?
- Is the access appropriate for the drug (central for vesicants, fresh and patent peripheral otherwise), and have I confirmed brisk blood return?
- Am I at the bedside for the first 15-30 minutes, with emergency equipment available, watching for infusion reaction?
- For the expected side effects of this regimen, have I assessed proactively and provided the patient with written guidance on management and red flags?
- Does the patient know that fever during chemotherapy is an emergency, and do they have the contact number?
- Have I taught safe handling of bodily fluids, and confirmed understanding with teach-back?
- If this patient is on immunotherapy, am I assessing for immune-related adverse events at every visit, even weeks after the infusion?
- Have I coordinated with the team — labs reviewed, imaging scheduled, supportive care refilled, psychosocial needs addressed?
- If extravasation were suspected right now, do I know the immediate steps and the specific antidote for this drug?
