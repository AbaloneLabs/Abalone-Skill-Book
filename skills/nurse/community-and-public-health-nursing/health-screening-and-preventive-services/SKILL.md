# Health Screening and Preventive Services

Screening is the testing of asymptomatic people to find disease early, and it is judged by whether the early detection improves outcomes for the population screened, not by how many people are tested or how many cases are found. The judgment problem is that screening feels inherently beneficial (finding disease early must be good), so nurses tend to offer screening broadly, celebrate the cases found, and underweight the harms of false positives, overdiagnosis, and the cascade of follow-up that follows an abnormal result. A screening test with poor positive predictive value in a low-prevalence population produces mostly false positives; a screening program with no referral pathway produces abnormal results that go nowhere; a screening offered without informed consent sends people into a cascade they did not choose. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether screening helps or harms, including the screening criteria, the test selection, the target population, the referral and follow-up system, and the consent and communication. Given the potential for harm, this guidance is conservative: screen only where evidence supports benefit, ensure the referral pathway exists before screening, communicate results and next steps clearly, and refer diagnosis and treatment to the appropriate clinicians.

## Core Rules

### Screen Only When the Evidence Shows Net Benefit for the Target Population

Screening is appropriate only when there is evidence that early detection improves outcomes (morbidity, mortality) for the target population, that the benefits outweigh the harms (false positives, overdiagnosis, anxiety, the cascade of follow-up), and that the screening test is accurate and acceptable in the target population. The criteria for a screenable condition (the Wilson and Jungner criteria and their modern updates) include that the condition is an important health problem, that it has a recognizable early stage, that there is an acceptable and accurate test, that there is an effective treatment for the early stage, that the cost of screening is balanced against the benefit, and that screening will be a continuing process, not a one-time event. The nurse must base the screening decision on current evidence and guidelines (such as the USPSTF or the relevant national authority), not on intuition or on the availability of a test, and must recognize that a test that is appropriate in a high-risk population may cause net harm in a low-risk population.

The tradeoff is the benefit of finding disease early versus the harm of false positives, overdiagnosis, and the cascade. The resolution is to screen only where the evidence shows net benefit, to target the population in whom the benefit holds, and to communicate the benefits and the harms honestly to the participant.

### Select the Screening Test for Accuracy, Acceptability, and Feasibility

The screening test must be selected for its accuracy (sensitivity, specificity, positive and negative predictive value in the target population), its acceptability (will the target population undertake it), and its feasibility (can it be delivered in the screening setting). Sensitivity (the proportion of true cases the test detects) matters for not missing cases; specificity (the proportion of true non-cases the test correctly identifies) matters for not generating false positives; and the positive predictive value (the proportion of positive results that are true cases) depends on the prevalence in the screened population, so the same test has a lower positive predictive value in a low-prevalence population and will generate mostly false positives there. The test must be acceptable to the target population (a test they will not take is useless), and it must be feasible to deliver (the equipment, the staff, the time, the cost) in the screening setting, which may be a clinic, a workplace, a school, or a community site.

The false signal is a test that is available and easy to administer, which suggests a good screening test but may have poor accuracy or poor acceptability in the target population. The defense is to evaluate the test's accuracy in the target population (not just the manufacturer's data), to consider the positive predictive value given the prevalence, and to verify the test is acceptable and feasible.

### Target the Right Population and Avoid Screening Those With No Net Benefit

The target population for screening is defined by the evidence (age, sex, risk factors, family history, the population in whom the benefit has been demonstrated), and screening outside the target population often produces net harm. Screening too young, too old, or too low-risk increases the false positive rate (because the prevalence is lower) and the overdiagnosis rate (finding disease that would never have caused symptoms), with the cascade of follow-up and the anxiety, and without the offsetting benefit. Screening those with a limited life expectancy (from age or comorbidity) for a slow-growing condition produces harm (the follow-up, the anxiety) without benefit (the patient will die of something else first). The targeting must be explicit, based on the evidence and the guidelines, and the screening must decline or redirect those outside the target, with an explanation of why.

The trap is offering screening to everyone who presents, on the assumption that more screening is better, which produces net harm in those outside the target population. The defense is to define the target population from the evidence, to screen only those in the target, and to explain the reasoning to those who are outside it.

### Establish the Referral Pathway and Follow-Up System Before Screening

A screening program is only as good as the pathway that follows an abnormal result, and the referral and follow-up system must be established before the screening begins. Every abnormal result must have a defined next step (diagnostic confirmation, referral to a clinician, a specialist appointment), a responsible provider, a timeline, and a way to track the participant through the follow-up to ensure it happens. The system must handle the participant who has no primary care provider (connecting them to one), the participant who is uninsured or underinsured (connecting them to coverage or to a safety-net provider), and the participant who does not follow up (a system to re-contact and to re-engage). A screening program that produces abnormal results with no follow-up is worse than no screening, because it identifies a problem and then abandons the participant.

The false signal is a screening event that finds cases and celebrates the count, which suggests success but masks the absence of follow-up, so the cases found may not receive the care that justifies the screening. The defense is to establish the referral pathway and the follow-up tracking before screening, to connect each abnormal result to a defined next step, and to track the participant to completion.

### Obtain Informed Consent and Communicate Results and Next Steps Clearly

Screening requires informed consent, which means the participant understands the purpose, the potential benefits, the potential harms (false positives, overdiagnosis, the cascade of follow-up, the anxiety), the accuracy of the test, and the next steps after a result, and they choose to participate. The consent must be in the participant's language and at an appropriate literacy level, must allow questions, and must document the participant's choice, including the choice to decline. The results must be communicated clearly and promptly, with the meaning of the result (a positive result is not a diagnosis, it requires confirmation), the recommended next steps, the timeline, and the contact for questions. The communication must be culturally and linguistically appropriate, must avoid stigmatizing language, and must support the participant through the anxiety of an abnormal result.

The trap is treating screening as a routine test that does not require consent, on the assumption that it is beneficial, which sends participants into a cascade they did not choose and may not want. The defense is to obtain informed consent in the participant's language, to communicate the benefits and the harms honestly, and to support the participant through the result and the follow-up.

### Measure the Program's Effect on Outcomes, Not Just Process

A screening program must be evaluated on its effect on health outcomes (morbidity, mortality, the stage at diagnosis, the treatment success), not just on process measures (the number screened, the number of abnormal results, the number of referrals), because the process measures can look good while the outcomes are unchanged or worsened. The evaluation must track the follow-up to completion (did the abnormal result lead to diagnosis and treatment), the stage at diagnosis (did screening find disease earlier), the treatment outcomes (did the earlier detection improve the outcome), and the harms (the false positives, the overdiagnosis, the anxiety, the cascade). The evaluation must also monitor the equity (did the screening reach the populations most at risk, or only the easy-to-reach), because a screening program that reaches the worried well and misses the at-risk produces inequity.

The false signal is a high screening count and a high referral count, which suggests a successful program but may mask poor follow-up, unchanged outcomes, or inequitable reach. The defense is to measure the outcomes and the follow-up, to monitor the harms, and to track the equity of the reach.

## Common Traps

### Screening a Low-Prevalence Population and Generating Mostly False Positives

A screening test is offered broadly to a low-prevalence population (a workplace, a community event), and the test finds many abnormal results, but because the prevalence is low, most of the positives are false, triggering a cascade of follow-up, anxiety, and cost, with little true disease found. The mechanism of the trap is that the positive predictive value of a test depends on the prevalence, and in a low-prevalence population even a specific test generates mostly false positives, so the screening produces more harm (the cascade, the anxiety) than benefit. The false signal is the count of abnormal results, which looks like successful case-finding but is mostly false. The harm is anxiety, unnecessary follow-up procedures (some of which carry their own harm), cost, and erosion of trust in screening. The defense is to target the population in whom the prevalence and the evidence support net benefit, and to communicate the positive predictive value honestly.

### A Screening Event With No Referral Pathway for Abnormal Results

A screening event is held (a health fair, a workplace screening), and abnormal results are found and handed to the participants, but there is no defined referral pathway, no responsible provider, and no follow-up tracking, so the abnormal results go nowhere. The mechanism of the trap is that screening is only beneficial if the abnormal result leads to diagnosis and treatment, and without a referral pathway the cases found are identified and then abandoned, which is worse than not screening because it raises a concern and provides no resolution. The false signal is the event's success in finding abnormal results, which suggests impact but masks the absence of follow-up. The harm is participants with identified risks who receive no care, anxiety, and erosion of trust. The defense is to establish the referral pathway and the follow-up tracking before screening, and to connect each abnormal result to a defined next step.

### Screening Outside the Evidence-Based Target Population

A screening test is offered to participants outside the evidence-based target (too young, too old, too low-risk, or with limited life expectancy), on the assumption that more screening is better or at the participant's request. The mechanism of the trap is that the benefit of screening has been demonstrated in a specific population, and outside that population the benefit decreases (lower prevalence, shorter life expectancy) while the harms persist or increase (false positives, overdiagnosis, the cascade), so the screening produces net harm. The false signal is the participant's request or the clinician's offer, which suggests patient-centered care but ignores the evidence. The harm is unnecessary follow-up, anxiety, overdiagnosis, and cost. The defense is to define the target population from the evidence and the guidelines, to screen only those in the target, and to explain the reasoning to those outside it.

### Treating Screening as Routine Without Informed Consent

A screening test is performed as part of a routine visit, without a discussion of the purpose, the benefits, the harms, and the alternatives, on the assumption that screening is beneficial and consent is implied. The mechanism of the trap is that screening carries real harms (false positives, overdiagnosis, anxiety, the cascade), and performing it without informed consent sends the participant into consequences they did not choose, which is both an ethical failure and a practical one (the participant may not follow up on a result they did not understand). The false signal is the routine nature of the test, which suggests it does not require consent but ignores the harms. The harm is autonomy violation, anxiety, and the cascade without understanding. The defense is to obtain informed consent in the participant's language, to communicate the benefits and the harms honestly, and to document the choice, including the choice to decline.

### Celebrating the Number Screened Without Measuring Outcomes

A screening program reports the number of people screened and the number of abnormal results as its success metrics, and the numbers look impressive, but the outcomes (the stage at diagnosis, the treatment success, the mortality) are not measured. The mechanism of the trap is that process measures (the count screened) are easy to report and look good, but they do not capture whether the screening improved health, and a program can screen many people with no outcome improvement (or with net harm) if the follow-up, the targeting, or the test are wrong. The false signal is the high screening count, which suggests a successful program but masks the unmeasured outcomes. The harm is a program that continues based on process metrics while producing no benefit (or harm), wasting resources. The defense is to measure the outcomes (the follow-up, the stage, the treatment success, the harms) and the equity, not just the process.

### Reaching the Worried Well and Missing the At-Risk Population

A screening program is delivered in a setting (a workplace, a clinic, a health fair) that reaches the health-engaged, the insured, and the worried well, but it misses the populations most at risk (the uninsured, the isolated, the distrustful, those with barriers to access). The mechanism of the trap is that screening reach follows the paths of least resistance, and the easy-to-reach are often the lower-risk, while the at-risk are the harder-to-reach, so a program that does not actively target the at-risk produces inequity, screening the lower-risk and missing the higher-risk. The false signal is the high screening count, which suggests broad reach but masks the inequitable distribution. The harm is a program that widens the health gap by reaching the lower-risk and missing the higher-risk. The defense is to target the at-risk populations actively, to bring the screening to the settings they use, and to monitor the equity of the reach.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm that the screening has evidence of net benefit for the target population (per current guidelines such as the USPSTF), and that the benefits outweigh the harms of false positives, overdiagnosis, and the cascade?
- Did I select the screening test for its accuracy (sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value in the target population), its acceptability, and its feasibility in the screening setting?
- Did I define the target population from the evidence (age, sex, risk factors, life expectancy) and screen only those in the target, with an explanation for those outside it?
- Did I establish the referral pathway and the follow-up tracking before screening, so every abnormal result has a defined next step, a responsible provider, a timeline, and a way to track the participant to completion?
- Did I obtain informed consent in the participant's language and at an appropriate literacy level, communicate the benefits and the harms honestly, and document the choice, including the choice to decline?
- Did I communicate the results clearly and promptly, explain that a positive result requires confirmation (it is not a diagnosis), and provide the next steps, the timeline, and a contact for questions?
- Did I measure the program's effect on outcomes (follow-up completion, stage at diagnosis, treatment success, harms) and the equity of the reach (did the screening reach the at-risk populations), not just the process count?
- Did I refer diagnosis, treatment, and complex management to the appropriate clinicians, and practice within the scope of nursing while supporting the participant through the screening and follow-up?
