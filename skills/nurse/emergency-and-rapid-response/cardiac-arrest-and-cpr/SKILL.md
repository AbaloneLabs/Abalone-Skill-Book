# Cardiac Arrest And CPR

The judgment problem in cardiac arrest is that the interventions that determine survival — immediate high-quality CPR with minimal interruption, early defibrillation for shockable rhythms — are time-critical and degrade rapidly with delay and with poor technique, and the chaos of the arrest creates conditions where these interventions are performed poorly: compressions that are too slow or too shallow or interrupted for procedures, defibrillation delayed while the team organizes, role confusion that leaves tasks undone or duplicated. The skilled nurse performs high-quality CPR without interruption, ensures early analysis and defibrillation, operates within a clear team role, and recognizes that the arrest is a team event whose outcome depends on each role performed well and coordinated. The nurse also understands the limits of resuscitation and the criteria for termination, and the importance of post-arrest care and family communication.

This is a high-stakes domain. CPR quality and defibrillation timing are the primary determinants of survival. The nurse's role is to perform high-quality CPR, to defibrillate early in shockable rhythms, to function within the team structure, and to participate in post-arrest care and debriefing.

## Core Rules

### Perform High-Quality CPR: Rate, Depth, Recoil, Minimal Interruption

The quality of CPR is the single largest modifiable determinant of outcome. The parameters are specific and must be maintained throughout the arrest.

- Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute.
- Depth: at least 2 inches (5 cm) in adults, avoiding excessive depth; allow full chest recoil between compressions.
- Minimal interruption: interruptions in compressions (for pulse checks, intubation, line placement) reduce survival and must be minimized. Limit pulse checks to when the rhythm is analyzed, and keep interruptions under 10 seconds.
- Rotate compressors every 2 minutes (or sooner if fatigued) to maintain quality, because compression quality degrades rapidly with fatigue.
- Use feedback devices (metronome, depth feedback) where available to maintain parameters.

### Defibrillate Early For Shockable Rhythms

For shockable rhythms (ventricular fibrillation, pulseless ventricular tachycardia), the probability of successful defibrillation declines with each minute, and early defibrillation is the highest-yield intervention.

- Analyze the rhythm as soon as the defibrillator arrives; for a witnessed arrest with a defibrillator immediately available, defibrillate before CPR if the rhythm is shockable.
- Deliver the shock, then resume CPR immediately without a pulse check (the rhythm check follows the next two-minute cycle).
- Continue the cycle of CPR and defibrillation per the algorithm, with epinephrine and antiarrhythmic per protocol.

### Function Within A Clear Team Role

The arrest is a team event; role clarity prevents tasks from being missed or duplicated. Know your role and perform it.

- Team leader: runs the algorithm, directs interventions, monitors time and cycles, ensures quality.
- Compressor: performs high-quality CPR, rotates on schedule.
- Airway: manages the airway and ventilation (bag-mask initially, advanced airway when available).
- Access and medications: establishes IV/IO access, administers medications per algorithm.
- Defibrillator operator: analyzes rhythm, delivers shocks.
- Recorder: documents the timeline, interventions, and medications.

If roles are unassigned or unclear, the team leader assigns them; if you are the first nurse on scene, begin CPR and call for help, and assume the team leader role until one is designated.

### Manage The Airway And Ventilation Without Compromising Compressions

Airway management supports oxygenation but must not interrupt compressions. Bag-mask ventilation is the initial approach; advanced airway placement (when available) occurs during a planned pause or with minimal interruption.

- Bag-mask ventilation: good mask seal, head tilt/chin lift or jaw thrust, squeeze the bag to produce visible chest rise, at a rate of 10 per minute (one breath every 6 seconds) during CPR with an advanced airway, or 30 compressions to 2 breaths without.
- Avoid hyperventilation, which increases intrathoracic pressure and reduces venous return and cardiac output; one breath every 6 seconds is sufficient during CPR.
- Advanced airway (endotracheal tube or supraglottic airway) placement should not cause prolonged interruption; if the first attempt fails, resume CPR and retry later.

### Know The Reversible Causes (Hs And Ts) And Address Them

Cardiac arrest often has a reversible cause, and identifying and addressing it (the Hs: hypovolemia, hypoxia, hydrogen ion/acidosis, hypokalemia/hyperkalemia, hypothermia; the Ts: tension pneumothorax, tamponade, thrombosis pulmonary or coronary, toxins) may restore circulation.

- Consider the reversible causes throughout the arrest, based on the history and presentation.
- Hypovolemia (give fluid), hypoxia (optimize oxygenation and ventilation), tension pneumothorax (needle decompression), tamponade (pericardiocentesis), thrombosis (thrombolytics), toxins (antidote) — each is addressed as identified.

### Recognize The Limits And Participate In Termination Decisions

Resuscitation is not indefinite. Termination is based on the algorithm, the downtime, the reversible causes, the patient's baseline, and the response, and is a clinical decision made by the team leader (often the physician) with the team's input.

- Asystole or PEA unresponsive to the algorithm over the defined duration, with reversible causes addressed, supports termination.
- The nurse contributes observations and supports the decision; the nurse does not independently terminate resuscitation.

### Provide Post-Arrest Care And Family Communication

After return of spontaneous circulation, the patient requires intensive monitoring and care, and the family requires communication.

- Post-arrest: optimize oxygenation (avoid hyperoxia), ventilation, hemodynamics, and consider targeted temperature management per protocol; arrange ICU transfer.
- Family communication: a designated team member communicates honestly and empathetically about the arrest, the outcome, and the next steps, and supports the family through the event.

## Common Traps

### Poor-Quality CPR: Slow, Shallow, Or Interrupted Compressions

Compressions are performed at 70 per minute instead of 100-120, or at 1 inch depth instead of 2, or are interrupted for 30 seconds during intubation, and the coronary and cerebral perfusion that CPR is meant to provide is inadequate, and the arrest is not resuscitable. The mechanism is that the parameters are easy to miss without feedback (the compressor feels they are compressing adequately when they are not), and interruptions feel necessary for procedures, so the quality erodes without anyone noticing, and the CPR provides little perfusion. The harm is that an arrest that could have been resuscitable is lost to poor-quality CPR, and the survival that quality CPR would have produced does not occur. The defense is to maintain the parameters (100-120 rate, 2-inch depth, full recoil), to use feedback devices, to minimize interruptions to under 10 seconds, to rotate compressors every 2 minutes, and to recognize that poor-quality CPR is no CPR, because it provides inadequate perfusion.

### Delaying Defibrillation For A Shockable Rhythm

The rhythm is ventricular fibrillation, but the team waits for IV access, for epinephrine, for the full team to assemble, before analyzing and shocking, and the probability of successful defibrillation declines with each minute of delay. The mechanism is that the algorithm's order (shock first for shockable rhythms) is subverted by the team's instinct to "prepare" before acting, and the defibrillation is delayed by activities that should follow it, not precede it. The harm is that the shockable rhythm degates into asystole, and the patient who was defibrillatable becomes unsalvageable. The defense is to analyze and defibrillate as soon as the defibrillator is available for a shockable rhythm, to resume CPR immediately after the shock, and to recognize that for ventricular fibrillation the shock is the treatment and everything else supports it.

### Hyperventilating During CPR

The patient is bagged rapidly — one breath every 2-3 seconds — during CPR, the increased intrathoracic pressure reduces venous return and cardiac output, and the CPR that is being performed is rendered less effective by the ventilation. The mechanism is that the instinct in an arrest is to ventilate fast (the patient is not breathing), and the rate exceeds the recommended 10 per minute, so the ventilation compromises the circulation the compressions are trying to provide. The harm is that the coronary and cerebral perfusion is reduced by the hyperventilation, and the arrest is harder to resuscitate. The defense is to ventilate at 10 per minute (one breath every 6 seconds) during CPR with an advanced airway, or 30:2 without, to avoid hyperventilation, and to recognize that during CPR the circulation is the priority and excessive ventilation undermines it.

### Role Confusion In A Chaotic Arrest

Multiple people attempt to compress, no one is managing the airway, the defibrillator is unattended, and the medications are not given, because roles are unassigned and the team is acting as a crowd rather than a coordinated unit. The mechanism is that the arrest is high-stress and the team assembles ad hoc, so without explicit role assignment the tasks cluster (everyone compresses) or gap (no one documents), and the algorithm's coordination is lost. The harm is that critical interventions are missed or duplicated, the algorithm is not run cleanly, and the arrest's outcome suffers. The defense is for the team leader to assign roles explicitly and early, for each member to perform their role and not drift, and to recognize that a coordinated team with clear roles outperforms a crowd of capable individuals, because the arrest is a system event whose components must be distributed and synchronized.

### Prolonged Interruption For Intubation Or Line Placement

The compressions stop for a minute while the airway is intubated, or while the central line is placed, and the perfusion that CPR provides is lost for the duration, and the arrest's reversibility declines. The mechanism is that the procedure feels important enough to justify the pause, and the compressor stops while the proceduralist works, but the interruption exceeds the 10-second limit and the perfusion is lost. The harm is that the arrest becomes harder to resuscitate because of the interruption, and the procedure that caused it may not have been immediately necessary. The defense is to minimize interruptions to under 10 seconds, to attempt intubation during the rhythm check pause or with a brief planned pause, to resume CPR immediately if the first attempt fails, and to recognize that no procedure during CPR justifies a prolonged interruption in compressions, because the compressions are the perfusion.

### Continuing Resuscitation Beyond The Point Of Futility Without Recognizing It

The arrest has persisted for the defined duration, the rhythm is asystole, the reversible causes have been addressed, the downtime was prolonged, and the resuscitation continues because no one is willing to call the termination, and the team and the family are subjected to a prolonged futile effort. The mechanism is that termination is an uncomfortable decision and the default (continue) is easier than the decision (stop), so the resuscitation continues past the point where it could succeed, and the resources and the emotional toll extend. The harm is a prolonged futile effort that does not serve the patient and that may complicate family grieving and team wellbeing. The defense is to follow the algorithm and the termination criteria, for the team leader to make the decision with the team's input at the appropriate point, and to recognize that continuing past futility is not more caring than a timely, compassionate termination.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I perform high-quality CPR at 100-120 per minute, 2-inch depth, with full recoil and minimal interruption (under 10 seconds), rotating compressors every 2 minutes?
- [ ] For a shockable rhythm, did I ensure early analysis and defibrillation, resuming CPR immediately after the shock without a pulse check?
- [ ] Did I function within a clear team role (or assign roles as team leader), ensuring tasks were distributed and synchronized rather than clustered or gapped?
- [ ] Did I manage the airway and ventilation without compromising compressions, ventilating at 10 per minute and avoiding hyperventilation?
- [ ] Did I consider and address the reversible causes (Hs and Ts) throughout the arrest based on the history and presentation?
- [ ] Did I avoid prolonged interruptions for procedures (intubation, line placement), resuming CPR immediately if an attempt failed?
- [ ] Did I contribute observations to the termination decision and support the team leader's decision when the criteria were met, rather than continuing past futility?
- [ ] After return of spontaneous circulation, did I support post-arrest care (oxygenation, ventilation, hemodynamics, targeted temperature management, ICU transfer) and honest, empathetic family communication?
