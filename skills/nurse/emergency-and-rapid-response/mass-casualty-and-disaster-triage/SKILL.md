# Mass Casualty And Disaster Triage

The judgment problem in mass casualty response is that the principles of individual patient care — do everything possible for each patient — must be inverted, because the resources are insufficient for all, and the goal shifts to doing the greatest good for the greatest number. The failure modes are predictable: nurses default to the individual-care mindset and pour resources into the first or most visible patient, exhausting capacity on one while others die; or the response is uncoordinated, with individuals acting on their own initiative rather than within the incident command structure, producing duplication and gaps. The skilled nurse performs rapid objective triage (the START method or equivalent), categorizes patients by survivability and resource need, works within the incident command structure, and accepts the painful discipline of passing the unsalvageable to treat the salvageable. This is emotionally and ethically demanding, and the nurse must recognize the psychological toll and the support required.

This is a high-stakes domain. Mass casualty triage decisions determine who lives and dies under resource constraints. The nurse's role is to triage objectively, to work within the command structure, and to provide care within the assigned role and category.

## Core Rules

### Shift From Individual To Population Triage: Greatest Good For Greatest Number

In a mass casualty incident, resources are insufficient for all, and the goal is to maximize the number of survivors. This inverts the usual imperative to do everything for each patient.

- Triage categorizes patients by survivability and resource need, not by severity alone: a patient with a devastating injury requiring extensive resources to survive may be lower priority than a patient with a treatable injury who will survive with prompt care.
- The discipline is to allocate resources where they will save the most lives, which means passing the unsalvageable (expectant category) to treat those who can survive.
- This is emotionally difficult and requires explicit recognition that the standard of care has shifted under the resource constraint.

### Use A Rapid Objective Triage Tool (START Or Equivalent)

Simple Triage And Rapid Treatment (START) categorizes patients in under a minute each, based on ability to walk, respiratory rate, perfusion, and mental status, into four categories.

- Walking (green, minor): patients who can walk to a designated area; least urgent.
- Respiratory rate: not breathing after airway repositioning — expectant (black); over 30 per minute — immediate (red).
- Perfusion: capillary refill over 2 seconds or no radial pulse — immediate (red).
- Mental status: unable to follow commands — immediate (red); otherwise — delayed (yellow).
- The categories direct the order and intensity of care: immediate (red) first, delayed (yellow) next, minor (green) last, expectant (black) comfort care only.

### Work Within The Incident Command Structure

The incident command system coordinates the response; individuals acting outside it produce chaos. Know your role and your reporting line.

- Report to the designated staging area and to your assigned role; do not self-deploy to the scene.
- The medical command structure assigns triage, treatment, transport, and staging roles; perform your assigned role.
- Communicate through the chain of command; do not bypass it with individual initiatives.

### Re-Triage Continuously

Patient conditions change, and categories shift: a delayed patient may deteriorate to immediate, an immediate patient may stabilize. Continuous re-triage prevents misallocation.

- Re-triage at intervals and when conditions change.
- Upgrade or downgrade categories based on current findings.

### Allocate Scarce Resources By Category, Not By Visibility

Resources (time, personnel, equipment, transport) are allocated by triage category, not by who is loudest, most visible, or most familiar. This is the discipline that the population triage requires.

- Immediate (red) patients receive priority for life-saving interventions and transport.
- Delayed (yellow) patients receive care when immediate patients are stabilized.
- Minor (green) patients receive care last and may self-care or be held.
- Expectant (black) patients receive comfort care; resources are not spent on patients who will not survive, because those resources will save others.

### Recognize The Psychological Toll And The Support Required

Mass casualty triage involves decisions (passing the dying, allocating scarce resources) that conflict with the nurse's usual ethics and that produce moral distress and post-traumatic stress. The toll is real and must be acknowledged.

- Debrief after the event; participate in formal debriefing.
- Access peer support and employee assistance; recognize that the distress is expected and that support is required, not optional.
- The decisions were made under resource constraints, not individual choice; the framework, not the nurse, allocated the resources.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Individual Care And Exhausting Resources On One Patient

The nurse encounters a dramatic, visible injury and pours all available resources — time, personnel, equipment — into that one patient, while other patients who could have been saved die unattended, because the individual-care instinct overrode the population-triage discipline. The mechanism is that the individual-care mindset is deeply ingrained and the dramatic patient captures attention, so the nurse defaults to maximum effort for one, and the resource expenditure on that one exceeds what the population framework would allow. The harm is that multiple salvageable patients die because the resources were consumed by one, and the greatest-good principle is violated by the default to individual care. The defense is to triage objectively before treating, to allocate resources by category, to recognize that the mass casualty standard inverts the individual standard, and to accept the discipline of moving past a patient who cannot be saved to treat those who can.

### Treating The Visible Or Vocal Rather Than The Triage Category

The patients who are screaming, bleeding visibly, or demanding attention receive care first, while the quiet patient in respiratory failure or the unconscious patient with a treatable injury is overlooked, because visibility and volume drove the allocation rather than the triage category. The mechanism is that the environment rewards response to the salient stimulus, and the objective triage is bypassed by the pull of the visible, so the immediate (red) patients who are quiet are missed while the minor (green) patients who are loud are treated. The harm is that salvageable immediate patients die while resources are spent on less urgent but more visible patients. The defense is to perform rapid objective triage (START) on all patients before treating, to allocate by category rather than by visibility, and to recognize that in mass casualty the quiet patient is often the more urgent one.

### Operating Outside The Incident Command Structure

A nurse self-deploys to the scene, begins treating patients independently, and is uncoordinated with the rest of the response, duplicating effort in one area while another area is unstaffed, because the individual initiative bypassed the command structure. The mechanism is that the urgency of the scene and the nurse's competence drive self-deployment, and the incident command structure feels like a delay, so the nurse acts independently and the coordination is lost. The harm is duplication, gaps, and a response that is less than the sum of its parts because the coordination that multiplies effectiveness is absent. The defense is to report to staging and to the assigned role, to work within the chain of command, to communicate through the structure, and to recognize that a coordinated response multiplies effectiveness while independent action, however competent, fragments it.

### Failing To Re-Triage As Conditions Change

A patient triaged as delayed (yellow) deteriorates to immediate (red) but remains in the delayed category because no one re-triaged, and the patient dies awaiting care at the lower priority. The mechanism is that the initial triage category is treated as fixed, and the continuous re-triage that the dynamic conditions require is not performed, so a patient whose status changed is managed at the wrong priority. The harm is that a salvageable patient deteriorates and dies because the category was not updated. The defense is to re-triage at intervals and when conditions change, to upgrade or downgrade categories based on current findings, and to recognize that the initial category is a starting point that must be updated as the patient's status evolves.

### Spending Resources On Expectant Patients At The Expense Of Salvageable Ones

A patient with devastating injuries incompatible with survival under the available resources is treated with prolonged effort, while salvageable patients wait, because the team cannot accept passing the dying, and the resources are spent on the unsalvageable. The mechanism is that the individual-care ethics resist the expectant categorization, and the team continues treatment that cannot save the patient, consuming resources that would save others. The harm is that salvageable patients die because the resources were spent on a patient who could not survive. The defense is to apply the expectant category objectively based on survivability and resources, to provide comfort care to expectant patients, to allocate resources to the salvageable, and to recognize that the painful discipline of triage is what maximizes survival under constraint, and that continuing futile care is not compassion but misallocation.

### Ignoring The Psychological Toll Until It Becomes Pathology

The nurse makes triage decisions that conflict with their ethics (passing the dying, allocating scarce resources), suppresses the distress to continue functioning, and does not access debriefing or support afterward, and the moral injury develops into post-traumatic stress or burnout. The mechanism is that the operational demands suppress the emotional response in the moment, and the stigma around distress prevents access to support afterward, so the moral injury is internalized and compounded. The harm is that a skilled nurse is impaired or lost, and the distress that support would have addressed becomes pathology. The defense is to participate in formal debriefing after the event, to access peer support and employee assistance, to recognize that the distress is an expected response to decisions made under constraint and not a personal failing, and to treat the psychological support as required, not optional.
