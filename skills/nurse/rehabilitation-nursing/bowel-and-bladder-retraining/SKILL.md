# Bowel and Bladder Retraining

Bowel and bladder dysfunction is one of the most common, most distressing, and most undertreated problems in rehabilitation. Patients arrive incontinent from acute illness, medication, immobility, neurological injury, or cognitive impairment, and the loss of continence carries shame, social isolation, skin breakdown, and a significant barrier to discharge. Retraining is possible for many patients, but it requires patience, consistency, and a willingness to address problems that patients are reluctant to discuss and staff are tempted to manage with pads rather than restore.

The judgment problem is that incontinence is often treated as a hygiene problem to be managed rather than a functional problem to be solved. The nurse who focuses on keeping the patient clean and dry — changing pads, managing skin, responding to episodes — has addressed the symptom but not the cause. The nurse who assesses the type and cause of incontinence, implements a retraining program, and tracks outcomes can restore continence for patients who would otherwise be discharged to facilities because they "could not be managed at home."

## Core Rules

### Diagnose the Type and Cause Before Choosing a Strategy

Incontinence is not a single problem. Urge incontinence from detrusor overactivity, stress incontinence from pelvic floor weakness, overflow incontinence from retention, functional incontinence from inability to reach the toilet in time, and reflex incontinence from neurogenic bladder all require different interventions. Begin with a thorough assessment — voiding history, fluid intake patterns, medication review (diuretics, anticholinergics, alpha-blockers, sedatives), cognitive status, mobility, and a bladder diary if possible — and identify the likely mechanism before implementing a program.

For patients with neurological injury, determine whether the bladder is spastic (reflex incontinence, high-pressure storage, risk of kidney damage) or flaccid (overflow incontinence, retention, risk of infection). This distinction, often confirmed by urodynamic testing, determines whether the strategy is to suppress reflex contractions, facilitate emptying, or use intermittent catheterization. A retraining program built on the wrong assumption will fail and may cause harm — anticholinergics given to a patient with retention will worsen the retention and the overflow.

### Implement a Structured Toileting Program Based on the Pattern

Timed voiding, habit training, and bladder training are the foundations of retraining, and the choice depends on the patient's cognition and the incontinence pattern. For cognitively intact patients with urge incontinence, bladder training involves gradually increasing the interval between voids, using urge suppression techniques (pelvic floor contractions, distraction, deep breathing), and building capacity. For cognitively impaired patients, timed voiding — offering the toilet at fixed intervals based on the bladder diary pattern — is more effective than urge-based training because the patient cannot reliably interpret or act on the urge.

Consistency is the entire intervention. A timed voiding program that is implemented on day shift, partially on evening shift, and not at all on night shift will fail, and the failure will be attributed to the patient rather than to the inconsistency. The program must be written in the care plan, communicated at handoff, and executed by every nurse and aide every shift. Track every void and every incontinent episode on a record that is visible to the team, and adjust the interval based on the pattern that emerges.

### Manage Neurogenic Bowel with a Predictable Routine

Neurogenic bowel — common after spinal cord injury, spina bifida, and other lower motor neuron injuries — requires a structured bowel program that the patient will perform for life. The goal is predictable, complete emptying at a scheduled time, preventing incontinence between programs and avoiding impaction. A typical program combines a stool softener daily, a stimulant laxative the night before, and a rectal intervention (digital stimulation, suppository, or enema) at a consistent time every day or every other day.

The timing matters. Schedule the bowel program at a time that will be feasible at home — not at 5 AM in the hospital if the patient will not maintain that schedule after discharge. Teach the patient to perform the program independently if they have the hand function, including digital stimulation technique, positioning on a commode or in bed, and recognition of when the program is complete. Autonomic dysreflexia can be triggered by bowel distention or digital stimulation in patients with injuries above T6; monitor blood pressure during the program and have a plan to respond.

### Use Intermittent Catheterization Correctly and Teach It for Self-Management

Intermittent catheterization is the preferred management for patients with urinary retention who cannot empty completely, because it reduces infection risk compared to indwelling catheters and preserves bladder function. Establish the catheterization schedule based on post-void residual volumes and fluid intake — typically every four to six hours — and adjust to keep catheterized volumes below 400 mL to prevent overdistention.

Teach clean technique thoroughly, as the patient or a family member will perform this at home. The teaching includes hand hygiene, catheter preparation, insertion technique, lubrication, volume measurement, and storage of supplies. For patients who will self-catheterize, assess for the manual dexterity and visual ability to perform the task; patients with limited hand function from spinal cord injury may need adaptive equipment or a caregiver. Do not discharge a patient on intermittent catheterization who has not demonstrated independent technique.

### Address the Skin Consequences of Incontinence Proactively

Incontinence-associated dermatitis develops within hours of exposure to urine or stool and progresses to skin breakdown, infection, and pressure injury if not managed aggressively. The strategy is prevention, not reaction: use barrier creams on intact skin in at-risk patients, change soiled products immediately, cleanse with pH-balanced no-rinse cleansers rather than soap and water, and avoid friction during cleansing.

Distinguish incontinence-associated dermatitis (which is moisture-related and appears in the perineal and buttock areas) from pressure injury (which is pressure-related and appears over bony prominences) because the prevention and treatment differ. Document the location and characteristics precisely so the team can track whether the skin is improving or deteriorating. A patient who develops a stage 2 pressure injury over an incontinence-affected area has two problems compounding each other, and the healing will be slow without addressing both.

### Investigate New or Worsening Incontinence as a Potential Symptom

Incontinence that is new or worsening in a patient who was previously continent, or who was progressing in retraining, is a symptom, not a setback. The differential includes urinary tract infection (the most common cause of sudden incontinence in older adults), medication change, new neurological event, constipation or impaction causing overflow, delirium, and deconditioning. Obtain a urinalysis and review the medication list before assuming the incontinence is behavioral or motivational.

The trap of treating new incontinence as a behavioral problem — "the patient stopped trying" — is that it misses the underlying cause and delays treatment of a potentially serious condition. A patient who suddenly becomes incontinent may have a UTI that will progress to sepsis, a new stroke, or an impaction that will lead to bowel obstruction. Investigate before adjusting the behavioral program.

### Prepare the Patient and Family for Home Management

Bowel and bladder management at home requires supplies, equipment, privacy, and a routine that fits the patient's life. Begin discharge planning early: identify the supplies the patient will need (catheters, barrier cream, pads, commode, raised toilet seat), verify insurance coverage and supply sources, and arrange for delivery before discharge. Teach the family the program they will continue, with return demonstrations, and provide written instructions.

Discuss the social and emotional dimensions honestly. The patient who is going home with incontinence or an ostomy faces changes in intimacy, social activities, and self-image that they may not raise unless invited. Create space for these conversations, refer to support resources, and ensure the patient knows that continence management is a normal part of recovery, not a personal failing. The patient who goes home ashamed of their bowel and bladder management will isolate, skip their program, and return with complications.

## Common Traps

### Managing Incontinence with Pads Rather Than Retraining

The patient is incontinent, and the response is to apply an incontinence pad, change it when soiled, and protect the skin. The trap is that this approach manages the symptom while allowing the underlying dysfunction to persist and often worsen — the bladder that is never challenged to hold volume loses capacity, the patient who is never offered the toilet loses the habit and the skill, and the patient is discharged incontinent to a setting that could have received them continent if retraining had been attempted. Pad management is appropriate when retraining is not a goal; it is not a substitute for retraining when restoration of continence is possible.

### Inconsistent Implementation Across Shifts

Day shift implements the timed voiding program every two hours, evening shift does it when they remember, and night shift does not do it at all because "the patient is sleeping." The trap is that the bladder does not know what shift it is, and a program that is implemented 50 percent of the time produces 0 percent of the retraining effect while consuming 50 percent of the effort. The inconsistency is often the reason retraining "fails," and the failure is attributed to the patient when it belongs to the team. The program must be written, communicated, and executed consistently or not attempted at all.

### Treating New Incontinence as Behavioral Regression

The patient who was making progress in retraining suddenly becomes incontinent again, and the team attributes it to "not trying" or "being lazy." The trap is that new or worsening incontinence is a symptom of a medical problem — UTI, impaction, medication effect, delirium, new neurological event — until proven otherwise, and treating it as behavioral delays the diagnosis and treatment of a condition that may be serious. The patient who is called lazy may be septic from an unrecognized UTI.

### Failing to Teach Intermittent Catheterization Before Discharge

The patient has been catheterized by nursing throughout the stay, the discharge date arrives, and no one has taught the patient or family the technique because "there wasn't time." The trap is that a patient discharged on intermittent catheterization without demonstrated independent technique will either not catheterize (leading to retention, infection, and renal damage) or will do it incorrectly (leading to trauma and infection). The teaching must begin days before discharge, with return demonstrations, because this is a life-sustaining skill the patient will perform thousands of times.

### Ignoring the Emotional and Social Impact

The nurse focuses on the mechanics of the bowel and bladder program — the schedule, the volumes, the skin — and does not address the shame, grief, and social withdrawal that accompany continence loss. The trap is that untreated emotional distress directly undermines adherence to the program; the patient who is ashamed of their incontinence will hide episodes, avoid the program, and isolate rather than participate in their own recovery. Continence is intimate, and the nurse who treats it as a purely mechanical problem has missed the dimension that determines whether the patient will sustain the program at home.

### Discharging Without Supplies or a Plan

The patient is discharged with a prescription for catheters and a verbal instruction to "get supplies at the pharmacy," and arrives home to find that the pharmacy does not stock the right catheters, insurance requires prior authorization, and the patient has nothing to manage their bladder. The trap is that supply planning is treated as an afterthought rather than a prerequisite for discharge, and the patient who goes home without supplies will improvise dangerously or return to the emergency department. Verify supplies, coverage, and delivery before the discharge order is written.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed the type and likely cause of incontinence before implementing a program, rather than applying a generic approach?
- Is the toileting program written in the care plan, communicated at handoff, and executed consistently across all shifts?
- For patients with neurogenic bowel, is there a scheduled program at a time feasible for home, with the patient taught to perform it independently if possible?
- If the patient is on intermittent catheterization, has the patient or family demonstrated independent clean technique before discharge?
- Am I protecting skin proactively with barrier care, and distinguishing incontinence-associated dermatitis from pressure injury?
- When incontinence is new or worsening, have I investigated medical causes (UTI, impaction, medication, delirium) before adjusting the behavioral program?
- Have I verified that supplies, equipment, insurance coverage, and delivery are arranged before discharge?
- Have I created space for the patient to discuss the emotional, social, and intimacy dimensions of continence management?
