# Work-Related Injury and Illness Management

Workplace injuries and exposure incidents are time-critical, legally consequential events in which the occupational health nurse must simultaneously deliver immediate clinical care, preserve the evidence and the documentation, protect the workforce from secondary harm, and initiate the workers' compensation and regulatory process. The judgment problem is that the urgency and the operational pressure push the nurse toward treating the single incident as an isolated clinical event, when in fact each injury and exposure is also a regulatory event, a legal event, and a sentinel event that may indicate a systemic hazard. An exposure handled as a simple wound care, without the source patient testing and the post-exposure prophylaxis decision, becomes a life-altering infection; an injury documented casually, without the mechanism and the witnesses, becomes an unwinnable workers' compensation dispute; a chemical exposure treated symptomatically, without the specific antidote and the environmental follow-up, becomes a preventable recurrence. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether workplace injuries and exposures are managed safely, compliantly, and defensibly, including triage and first aid, exposure incident management, workers' compensation documentation, and referral. Given the clinical and legal stakes, this guidance is conservative: stabilize and assess before treating, follow the exposure protocols exactly, document contemporaneously and factually, and refer beyond the scope of occupational health first aid.

## Core Rules

### Triage on Mechanism and Severity, Not on the Employee's Presentation

Workplace injury triage must be based on the mechanism of injury (the force, the height, the chemical, the duration of exposure) and the severity of the findings, not on the employee's presentation or their desire to return to work, because adrenaline, the desire to avoid a recordable incident, and the delay of symptom onset can mask serious injury. A fall from height, a struck-by with significant force, a chemical splash to the eye, or a laceration with a contaminated object requires a systematic assessment for the injuries the mechanism can produce, even if the employee feels fine. The triage must include the primary survey (airway, breathing, circulation, disability), the secondary survey (the mechanism-based assessment), and the decision to treat in-house (for minor injuries within the scope of first aid), to refer (for injuries requiring medical evaluation), or to activate the emergency services (for life-threatening or limb-threatening conditions). The triage must be documented, including the mechanism, the findings, the decision, and the rationale.

The tradeoff is a liberal referral pattern (safe, but may over-refer and inflate the recordable rate) versus a conservative in-house treatment (efficient, but risks missing a serious injury). The resolution is to triage on the mechanism and the severity, to refer when the injury is beyond first aid or when the mechanism suggests a serious possibility, and to document the decision and the rationale.

### Manage Bloodborne Pathogen Exposures by the Protocol and the Clock

Bloodborne pathogen exposures (needlesticks, sharps injuries, mucous membrane splashes, non-intact skin exposures) are time-critical events that must be managed by the established protocol and the clock, because the post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) for HIV has a narrow window (hours, not days), and the source patient testing and the exposed worker's baseline and follow-up testing must follow the schedule. The protocol requires immediately washing the wound and the exposure site, reporting the exposure, identifying and testing the source patient (with consent) for HIV, hepatitis B, and hepatitis C, assessing the exposure's severity and the source's status, and deciding on the PEP (HIV PEP, hepatitis B vaccine and immune globulin) per the current guidelines. The exposed worker must receive baseline testing, counseling, the PEP decision, and the follow-up testing schedule (typically six weeks, three months, six months), and the exposure must be documented in the confidential medical record and the sharps injury log.

The trap is treating the exposure as a simple wound care, without the source testing and the PEP decision, which misses the window for the prophylaxis and the follow-up. The defense is to follow the bloodborne pathogen exposure protocol immediately, to test the source patient, to make the PEP decision per the guidelines within the window, and to complete the testing and the counseling.

### Manage Chemical Exposures With the Safety Data Sheet and the Specific Protocol

Chemical exposures (skin contact, inhalation, ingestion, eye exposure) must be managed with the safety data sheet (SDS) for the specific chemical, which provides the hazards, the first aid measures, the specific antidotes, and the required medical attention, and with the specific protocol for the chemical class. The management requires removing the employee from the exposure, decontaminating (the skin wash, the eye flush for the required duration, often 15 minutes), providing the first aid per the SDS, assessing the severity, and deciding on the referral and the specific treatment (for example, the cyanide antidote kit for cyanide exposure, the specific chelation for heavy metals). The exposure must be reported, the environment must be secured and evaluated to prevent recurrence, and the incident must be documented with the chemical, the duration, the SDS first aid, and the medical evaluation.

The false signal is the employee feeling better after the decontamination, which suggests resolution but may mask a delayed or a systemic effect. The defense is to manage the chemical exposure per the SDS and the specific protocol, to decontaminate for the required duration, to refer per the SDS, and to follow up.

### Document the Workers' Compensation Case Contemporaneously and Factually

Workers' compensation documentation must be completed contemporaneously (at the time of the event, from memory and the notes), factually (the mechanism, the findings, the treatment, the witnesses, the time, the location), and completely (the first report of injury, the medical documentation, the OSHA recordkeeping), because the documentation is the evidence in the claim, the dispute, and the regulatory inspection. The documentation must record the mechanism of injury (how it happened, in the employee's words and the objective findings), the objective findings (the observable injuries, the vital signs), the treatment provided, the work-relatedness assessment, and the disposition (returned to work, referred, restricted). The documentation must avoid speculation about fault or pre-existing conditions (which are legal determinations, not clinical), and it must be consistent across the clinical record, the first report of injury, and the OSHA log.

The trap is casual or delayed documentation, which loses the detail, the witnesses, and the accuracy, and which produces a record that fails in the claim or the dispute. The defense is to document contemporaneously, factually, and completely, to record the mechanism and the objective findings, and to keep the records consistent.

### Assess Work-Relatedness on the Evidence, Not on the Operational Preference

The work-relatedness of an injury or illness (whether it arose out of and in the course of employment) is a determination that drives the recordability, the workers' compensation eligibility, and the OSHA log, and it must be assessed on the evidence (the mechanism, the temporal relationship to work, the objective findings, the medical opinion), not on the operational preference to avoid a recordable incident or a claim. The assessment considers whether the injury resulted from a work activity or a work condition, whether it occurred during work hours and at the work site, and whether there is a plausible causal relationship. Some conditions (the cumulative trauma, the mental health claims, the conditions with pre-existing components) require a careful medical and legal assessment, and the nurse should document the assessment and refer the complex determinations to the appropriate medical and legal channels.

The trap is adjusting the work-relatedness determination to avoid a recordable incident, which misrepresents the case, violates the recordkeeping rules, and exposes the nurse and the employer to liability. The defense is to assess the work-relatedness on the evidence, to document the assessment, and to follow the recordkeeping rules.

### Provide First Aid Within the Scope and Refer Beyond It

Occupational health first aid is the immediate care provided for minor injuries within the defined scope of first aid (the OSHA first aid list: bandaging, wound cleaning, cold packs, non-prescription medications at the package dose, eye flushing, blisters, minor scratches), and the conditions beyond that scope require referral to the appropriate medical provider. The scope distinction matters because first aid cases are not OSHA-recordable, while medical treatment beyond first aid is recordable, and because providing care beyond the scope of occupational health first aid risks missing the diagnosis and the appropriate treatment. The nurse must know the first aid list, provide the care within it, and refer the lacerations requiring sutures, the fractures, the injuries requiring prescription medication, the eye injuries beyond the flush, and the conditions requiring diagnostic evaluation.

The false signal is the convenience of the in-house treatment, which suggests efficiency but may exceed the scope and miss the diagnosis. The defense is to provide the first aid within the scope, to refer the conditions beyond it, and to document the care and the referral.

## Common Traps

### Treating a Bloodborne Pathogen Exposure as Simple Wound Care

The needlestick or the sharps injury is treated as a simple wound care (washed, bandaged), without the source patient testing, the PEP decision, and the follow-up testing schedule, because the wound looks minor and the urgency is not apparent. The mechanism of the trap is that bloodborne pathogen exposures require the protocol management within the window, and treating them as simple wound care misses the PEP window (for HIV, hours), the source testing, and the follow-up, which can result in a life-altering infection. The false signal is the minor wound, which suggests a minor injury but masks the pathogen risk. The harm is HIV, hepatitis B, or hepatitis C infection, regulatory non-compliance, and a devastating outcome for the employee. The defense is to follow the bloodborne pathogen exposure protocol immediately, to test the source, to make the PEP decision within the window, and to complete the follow-up.

### Triage on the Employee's Presentation Rather Than the Mechanism

The employee who feels fine after a fall or a struck-by is returned to work without the mechanism-based assessment, because the presentation seems benign and the employee wants to continue working. The mechanism of the trap is that adrenaline and the delayed symptom onset can mask a serious injury, and triaging on the presentation rather than the mechanism misses the internal injury, the fracture, or the head injury that the mechanism can produce. The false signal is the employee feeling fine, which suggests a minor injury but masks the serious possibility. The harm is a missed serious injury, a delayed diagnosis, a deteriorating employee, and a life-threatening or limb-threatening outcome. The defense is to triage on the mechanism and the severity, to conduct the mechanism-based assessment, and to refer when the mechanism suggests a serious possibility.

### Casual or Delayed Workers' Compensation Documentation

The injury is treated, the employee is returned to work, and the documentation is completed later, from memory, without the witnesses and the detail, or it is completed casually, without the mechanism and the objective findings. The mechanism of the trap is that the documentation is the evidence in the claim and the dispute, and the casual or delayed documentation loses the detail, the witnesses, and the accuracy, producing a record that fails in the claim, the dispute, or the regulatory inspection. The false signal is the completed form, which suggests documentation but masks the inaccuracy and the incompleteness. The harm is a lost or disputed claim, a weakened legal position, and regulatory liability. The defense is to document contemporaneously, factually, and completely, with the mechanism, the objective findings, and the witnesses.

### Managing a Chemical Exposure Without the SDS or the Specific Protocol

The chemical exposure is managed with the general first aid (wash the skin, flush the eye), without consulting the SDS for the specific chemical, the required flush duration, the specific antidote, and the required medical attention. The mechanism of the trap is that the chemicals differ in their hazards and their first aid, and managing a chemical exposure without the SDS risks insufficient decontamination (too short a flush), a missed specific antidote, and a delayed or a systemic effect. The false signal is the employee feeling better after the general first aid, which suggests resolution but masks a delayed or a systemic effect. The harm is a missed specific treatment, a delayed effect, and a preventable recurrence. The defense is to manage the chemical exposure per the SDS and the specific protocol, to decontaminate for the required duration, and to refer per the SDS.

### Adjusting Work-Relatedness to Avoid a Recordable Incident

The injury's work-relatedness is questioned, or the case is classified as first aid rather than medical treatment, to avoid a recordable incident and to protect the recordable rate, under operational pressure. The mechanism of the trap is that the work-relatedness and the recordability are evidence-based determinations, and adjusting them to avoid a recordable incident misrepresents the case, violates the recordkeeping rules, and exposes the nurse and the employer to liability in an inspection or a claim. The false signal is the avoided recordable incident, which suggests a good outcome but masks the misrepresentation. The harm is regulatory citations, penalties, a damaged record, and legal liability. The defense is to assess the work-relatedness and the recordability on the evidence, to document the assessment, and to follow the recordkeeping rules.

### Providing Treatment Beyond the Scope of Occupational Health First Aid

The laceration is sutured, the fracture is splinted long-term, or the prescription medication is provided in-house, beyond the scope of occupational health first aid, on the assumption that the in-house treatment is sufficient. The mechanism of the trap is that occupational health first aid has a defined scope, and providing treatment beyond it risks missing the diagnosis (a laceration that needs a tendon repair, a fracture that needs reduction), delaying the appropriate care, and exceeding the nursing scope of practice. The false signal is the convenient in-house treatment, which suggests efficiency but masks the scope exceedance. The harm is a missed diagnosis, delayed care, and scope-of-practice liability. The defense is to provide the first aid within the scope, to refer the conditions beyond it, and to document the care and the referral.

## Self-Check

- Did I triage the workplace injury on the mechanism and the severity, conduct the primary and the secondary survey, and decide to treat in-house, refer, or activate the emergency services based on the findings?
- Did I manage the bloodborne pathogen exposure by the protocol and the clock, wash the exposure site, test the source patient, make the PEP decision within the window, and schedule the follow-up testing?
- Did I manage the chemical exposure with the SDS and the specific protocol, decontaminate for the required duration, provide the first aid per the SDS, and refer per the SDS?
- Did I document the workers' compensation case contemporaneously, factually, and completely, with the mechanism, the objective findings, the treatment, the work-relatedness assessment, and the disposition?
- Did I assess the work-relatedness on the evidence, document the assessment, and follow the OSHA recordkeeping rules for recordability?
- Did I provide the first aid within the OSHA first aid scope, refer the conditions beyond that scope, and document the care and the referral?
- Did I secure the environment and report the incident to prevent recurrence, and coordinate the follow-up and the return-to-work with the treating provider and the workers' compensation case?
- Did I maintain the confidentiality of the employee's clinical information, share only the need-to-know with the employer, and practice within the scope of occupational health nursing?
