# Sodium and Dysnatremia Management

Sodium disorders are among the most dangerous electrolyte disturbances because sodium governs the osmotic movement of water across the blood-brain barrier, and the brain is exquisitely vulnerable to both the disturbance and the correction. The judgment problem is that the brain adapts to dysnatremia over hours to days, so a "normal" sodium achieved too quickly is not a success — it is a catastrophe. Correcting hyponatremia too rapidly causes osmotic demyelination syndrome, a permanent and often fatal neurologic injury. Correcting hypernatremia too rapidly causes cerebral edema, seizures, and herniation. The nurse administering sodium-altering fluids is not passively following an order; the nurse is the continuous guardian of the correction rate, and the difference between safe and fatal correction is measured in milliequivalents per hour.

## Core Rules

### Determine the Time Course and Symptom Severity Before Anything Else

The first question in any dysnatremia is how fast it developed and whether the patient is symptomatic, because both determine the safe correction rate. Acute hyponatremia (developing in less than 48 hours) and symptomatic hyponatremia (seizure, coma, severe lethargy) require rapid partial correction with hypertonic saline to prevent herniation. Chronic, asymptomatic hyponatremia requires slow, controlled correction to avoid osmotic demyelination. The same logic applies to hypernatremia: acute hypernatremia can be corrected faster, while chronic hypernatremia must be corrected slowly. Obtain the history (when did symptoms start, what was the baseline sodium, what fluids and medications have been given) and the neurologic exam before deciding on the urgency. Document the time course explicitly, because the safe correction limit depends on it.

### Know and Enforce the Correction Rate Limits

The safe correction limits are non-negotiable and the nurse must know them and monitor against them. For hyponatremia, the classic limit is a rise of no more than 8 to 12 mEq/L in any 24 hours (more conservative limits of 4 to 6 mEq/L are increasingly recommended for high-risk patients — those with malnutrition, alcoholism, liver disease, hypokalemia). For hypernatremia, the limit is a fall of no more than 8 to 10 mEq/L in 24 hours. Check the sodium every two to four hours during active correction, calculate the actual change per hour, and if the rate exceeds the limit, stop or slow the correcting fluid and notify the provider immediately. Do not wait for the next scheduled lab; the brain is being remodeled with every change.

### Administer Hypertonic Saline with Precision and Strict Monitoring

Hypertonic saline (commonly 3%) for symptomatic hyponatremia is a high-alert medication that must be given via a pump, on a dedicated line, with frequent sodium monitoring. The goal is a small, immediate rise (commonly 4 to 6 mEq/L) to relieve the threat of herniation, not a normal number. After the initial bolus or infusion, reassess the sodium and the neurologic exam, and stop the hypertonic saline once the symptoms improve or the target rise is achieved. Continuing hypertonic saline past the symptomatic target risks overcorrection and osmotic demyelination. Have desmopressin available in case of overcorrection, and understand that fluid restriction and other measures may be needed to slow correction.

### Identify the Volume Status and the Underlying Cause

Sodium disorders are managed differently depending on volume status and etiology. Hypovolemic hyponatremia is treated with isotonic saline to restore volume. Euvolemic hyponatremia (SIADH) is managed with fluid restriction, salt tablets, urea, or vasopressin antagonists. Hypervolemic hyponatremia (heart failure, cirrhosis) is managed with fluid and sodium restriction plus diuresis. Hypernatremia is almost always a water problem — either inadequate intake (often in elderly or intubated patients) or excessive loss (diabetes insipidus, osmotic diuresis) — and is treated with free water replacement, treating the cause, and adjusting the maintenance fluids. The nurse must understand the etiology to anticipate complications and to administer the right fluid, because giving the wrong fluid (e.g., isotonic saline to a SIADH patient) worsens the sodium.

### Assess Neurologic Status Continuously and Correlate with the Sodium

Neurologic symptoms in dysnatremia range from subtle (headache, nausea, mild confusion) to severe (seizure, coma, brainstem herniation). Perform and document a focused neurologic exam at frequent intervals during active correction, and correlate changes with the sodium trend. Worsening neurologic status during correction may indicate overcorrection (cerebral edema in hypernatremia correction, osmotic demyelination in hyponatremia correction) or undercorrection (ongoing cerebral edema in acute hyponatremia). Either way, the sodium and the neurologic exam must be interpreted together, and any new or worsening symptom requires immediate notification and sodium recheck.

### Manage the Patient at Risk for Dysnatremia Proactively

Many dysnatremias are iatrogenic, caused by the fluids and medications the hospital gives. Prevent hospital-acquired hyponatremia by avoiding hypotonic maintenance fluids in patients at risk (postoperative, elderly, on thiazides, with SIADH), and by monitoring sodium in any patient on significant fluid therapy. Prevent hypernatremia by ensuring adequate free water intake in patients who cannot drink (intubated, sedated, elderly with impaired thirst), and by recognizing and treating diabetes insipidus and high-output renal failure. The nurse who anticipates and prevents dysnatremia practices better nursing than the one who manages it after it occurs.

### Educate the Patient and Coordinate Care at Transitions

Sodium disorders often recur after discharge, especially SIADH of chronic cause and hypernatremia of inadequate intake. Educate the patient and family on fluid restrictions or intake goals, on the medications used (and their side effects), on the symptoms of recurrence, and on when to seek care. Communicate the sodium trajectory, the correction limits used, and the residual risk at handoff and discharge, because the next team must continue to monitor. A patient who was overcorrected in the hospital may develop osmotic demyelination days later, after discharge, and the outpatient team must know to watch for it.

## Common Traps

### Correcting Hyponatremia Too Rapidly Because the Number Looks Low

The trap is that a sodium of 118 looks alarming, so the team pushes for rapid normalization, and the nurse administers hypertonic saline or liberal saline without monitoring the hourly correction rate. The mechanism of harm is that the brain, having adapted to chronic hyponatremia by extruding osmolytes, cannot re-accumulate them fast enough to keep up with a rapid sodium rise, so brain cells shrink and oligodendrocytes die, producing osmotic demyelination syndrome. The harm is a permanent, often fatal neurologic injury (quadriparesis, pseudobulbar palsy, coma) that appears days after the "successful" correction. Know the correction limit, check the sodium every two to four hours during active correction, and stop or slow the correcting fluid if the rate exceeds the limit.

### Treating the Sodium Number Instead of the Patient's Symptoms and Volume Status

The trap is that the team targets a "normal" sodium regardless of whether the patient is symptomatic, hypovolemic, or hypervolemic, applying the same fluid strategy to all. The mechanism of harm is that the correct fluid depends on the volume status and cause: isotonic saline helps hypovolemic hyponatremia but worsens SIADH; fluid restriction helps SIADH but worsens hypovolemic hyponatremia. The harm is iatrogenic worsening of the sodium and the underlying condition. Identify the volume status and etiology, and administer the fluid that matches them, not the fluid that "should raise the sodium."

### Using Hypotonic Maintenance Fluids in Hospitalized Adults at Risk

The trap is that hypotonic fluids (such as 0.45% sodium chloride or D5W) were historically standard maintenance fluids, so the nurse hangs them by default, especially postoperatively or in elderly patients. The mechanism of harm is that hospitalized patients frequently have non-osmotic ADH release (from stress, pain, nausea, opioids), which retains free water and produces hospital-acquired hyponatremia, and hypotonic fluids provide the free water that drives the sodium down. The harm is a preventable, sometimes fatal, hyponatremia. Use isotonic maintenance fluids in most hospitalized adults, monitor the sodium, and reserve hypotonic fluids for specific indications.

### Missing Hypernatremia in the Patient Who Cannot Complain of Thirst

The trap is that hypernatremia is a water problem, and the patients most at risk (intubated, sedated, elderly with impaired thirst, cognitively impaired) cannot ask for water, so the sodium creeps up unnoticed until the patient seizes or the routine labs catch it. The mechanism of harm is that hypernatremia causes cerebral shrinkage, which can rupture bridging veins and cause subdural hemorrhage, and chronic hypernatremia corrected too fast causes cerebral edema. The harm is neurologic injury from a preventable and treatable disturbance. Provide adequate free water (enterally or via appropriate maintenance fluids) to patients who cannot drink, monitor the sodium in high-risk patients, and respond to a rising trend before symptoms occur.

### Assuming a "Normal" Sodium After Correction Means the Patient Is Safe

The trap is that the team corrects the sodium to 135, declares success, and stops monitoring, missing the delayed onset of osmotic demyelination or the recurrence of the underlying disorder. The mechanism of harm is that osmotic demyelination typically presents two to six days after the overcorrection, often after discharge, and recurrence of SIADH or diabetes insipidus can swing the sodium again. The harm is a patient who deteriorates days later without the team connecting it to the recent correction. Continue to monitor the sodium and neurologic status for several days after correction, communicate the risk at transitions, and educate the patient on symptoms to report.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I determine the time course of the dysnatremia (acute vs chronic) and the symptom severity before initiating or continuing correction?
- Do I know the correction rate limit (typically 8 to 12 mEq/L per 24 hours for hyponatremia, 8 to 10 for hypernatremia), and am I checking the sodium every two to four hours during active correction?
- For hypertonic saline, is it on a pump, on a dedicated line, with a defined symptomatic target, and stopped once symptoms improve or the target rise is achieved?
- Did I identify the volume status and underlying cause, and am I giving the fluid that matches them (isotonic for hypovolemic, restriction for SIADH, free water for hypernatremia)?
- Am I performing and documenting a focused neurologic exam at frequent intervals and correlating changes with the sodium trend?
- For maintenance fluids, am I using isotonic solutions in at-risk hospitalized adults and avoiding default hypotonic fluids?
- For the patient who cannot drink, am I providing adequate free water and monitoring the sodium to prevent hypernatremia?
- If the correction rate exceeded the limit, did I stop or slow the correcting fluid and notify the provider immediately?
- Did I educate the patient and family on fluid restrictions, symptoms of recurrence, and when to seek care?
- At handoff and discharge, did I communicate the sodium trajectory, the correction limits used, and the risk of delayed osmotic demyelination or recurrence?
