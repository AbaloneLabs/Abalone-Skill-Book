# IV Fluid Therapy and Volume Status Assessment

Intravenous fluids are among the most common interventions in hospitalized patients, and they are widely treated as routine when in fact they are prescription drugs with specific indications, compositions, and harms. The judgment problem is that fluids are easy to start and easy to continue, so they accumulate insidiously, and the patient who needed a bolus six hours ago now needs diuresis for iatrogenic pulmonary edema. Volume status assessment is one of the hardest skills in nursing because the physical signs are indirect and often contradictory, and the wrong conclusion — fluid-depleted when actually overloaded, or vice versa — drives therapy in the opposite direction from what the patient needs. The skilled nurse treats every fluid order as a hypothesis to be tested against the patient's physiology, not a bag to be hung and forgotten.

## Core Rules

### Assess Volume Status Dynamically, Using Multiple Converging Signs

No single sign confirms volume status; the nurse must integrate history, exam, and trends. For hypovolemia, look for thirst, dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor, oliguria with high urine sodium and osmolality, tachycardia, orthostatic hypotension, and a rising blood urea nitrogen-to-creatinine ratio. For hypervolemia, look for edema, jugular venous distension, crackles on lung auscultation, an S3 gallop, weight gain, and low urine sodium. Use trends aggressively — daily weights, intake and output, urine output per kilogram per hour, and the response to a fluid challenge. When signs conflict (a patient with edema who is intravascularly dry, as in heart failure or cirrhosis), recognize the limitation of the exam and escalate for dynamic assessment (passive leg raise, point-of-care ultrasound, fluid challenge). Volume status is a moving target, not a static label.

### Match the Fluid Composition to the Indication

Different fluids do different things, and the choice matters. Isotonic crystalloids (0.9% sodium chloride, balanced crystalloids such as lactated Ringer's or Plasma-Lyte) are the mainstay of volume resuscitation. Balanced crystalloids are increasingly preferred over normal saline in large-volume resuscitation (especially sepsis) because large volumes of normal saline cause hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis and acute kidney injury. Hypotonic fluids (0.45%, D5W) are for free water replacement, not volume resuscitation, and carry hyponatremia risk in hospitalized patients. Hypertonic saline is for symptomatic hyponatremia and severe traumatic brain injury with elevated intracranial pressure. Colloids (albumin) have a limited role (ascites, specific shock states). Know the composition of every fluid you hang, the indication, and the risk, and question orders that do not match the physiology.

### Resuscitate to Goals and Reassess Responsiveness Before Each Bolus

Fluid resuscitation in shock is not "give fluids until the pressure is normal"; it is "give fluids in boluses, reassess responsiveness, and stop when the patient is no longer fluid-responsive." Give a defined bolus (commonly 30 mL/kg of crystalloid in sepsis, per guidelines), then reassess: did the mean arterial pressure, heart rate, urine output, lactate, and mental status improve? Is the patient still fluid-responsive (assessed by passive leg raise, pulse pressure variation in a ventilated patient, or point-of-care ultrasound)? If yes, continue; if no, escalating fluids will not help and will cause harm (pulmonary edema, abdominal compartment syndrome, worsened outcomes in sepsis). Document the bolus, the response, and the decision to continue or stop. The reflexive "blood pressure low, give more fluid" without reassessment is poor practice.

### Anticipate and Prevent Fluid Overload in Vulnerable Patients

Certain patients tolerate fluids poorly and develop overload at much lower volumes: heart failure (especially with reduced ejection fraction), renal failure, cirrhosis with ascites, the elderly, and the postoperative patient with capillary leak. In these patients, use smaller, more frequent boluses with closer reassessment, prefer balanced crystalloids, monitor for early overload signs (rising oxygen requirement, new crackles, falling urine output paradoxically), and have a low threshold to escalate to vasopressors rather than additional fluid. Daily weights, intake and output, and lung exam are the nurse's main tools. The goal is to resuscitate enough without drowning the patient, and that balance is the core of fluid stewardship.

### Manage Maintenance Fluids Deliberately, Not by Default

Maintenance fluids are often started and then continued indefinitely "because the patient is NPO," accumulating sodium, chloride, and water that cause hypernatremia, hyperchloremic acidosis, and overload. Reassess the indication for maintenance fluids daily: does the patient still need them, can they take enteral intake, are the electrolytes being monitored and corrected, and is the composition appropriate? Avoid hypotonic maintenance fluids in most hospitalized adults (hyponatremia risk). Consider the sodium and potassium content of all sources — maintenance fluids, potassium additives, medications in saline, enteral water — because the cumulative load drives the electrolyte picture. The daily fluid plan should be a deliberate decision, not an inherited default.

### Recognize and Respond to Third-Spacing and Capillary Leak

In sepsis, pancreatitis, burns, major surgery, and anaphylaxis, fluid leaks from the vascular space into the interstitium (third-spacing), so the patient is intravascularly dry while appearing grossly overloaded (edema, ascites, weight gain). This is one of the most confusing volume situations in nursing. The treatment is to support the intravascular volume (often with albumin or blood products in addition to crystalloid) while the underlying condition resolves and the fluid mobilizes back, which can then cause overload as the third space re-enters the vasculature. Recognize the pattern, anticipate the mobilization phase (often days later, with diuresis and weight loss), and adjust monitoring and therapy as the patient transitions from third-spacing to re-entering.

### Coordinate with Diuretics and Ultrafiltration When Overload Develops

When fluid overload develops, the response is diuresis (loop diuretics typically first-line), ultrafiltration (in renal failure or refractory overload), or simply stopping the fluids and allowing the kidneys to handle it. Monitor the response with daily weights, urine output, oxygenation, and exam. In heart failure and renal failure, the diuretic response may be blunted, requiring escalation of dose or combination therapy. Communicate the volume trajectory at handoff, because overload is a leading cause of respiratory failure and prolonged hospitalization, and the next shift must continue the stewardship.

## Common Traps

### Giving Fluid Boluses Reflexively for Low Blood Pressure Without Reassessing Responsiveness

The trap is that a low blood pressure triggers an automatic fluid bolus, and when the pressure does not improve, another bolus is given, and another, without ever assessing whether the patient is fluid-responsive. The mechanism of harm is that once the heart is operating on the flat part of the Frank-Starling curve, additional fluid does not improve cardiac output but does raise hydrostatic pressure, causing pulmonary edema, abdominal compartment syndrome, and worsened tissue oxygenation — large trials show excess fluid harms septic patients. The harm is iatrogenic overload that worsens oxygenation and extends the ICU stay. Reassess responsiveness (passive leg raise, pulse pressure variation, ultrasound) before each bolus, and escalate to vasopressors when the patient is no longer fluid-responsive.

### Defaulting to Normal Saline for Large-Volume Resuscitation

The trap is that 0.9% sodium chloride is the familiar default, so the nurse hangs bag after bag in sepsis or hemorrhage, accumulating chloride. The mechanism of harm is that large volumes of normal saline cause hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis, renal vasoconstriction, and acute kidney injury, and balanced crystalloids (lactated Ringer's, Plasma-Lyte) have better outcomes in large trials. The harm is preventable kidney injury and prolonged hospitalization. Use balanced crystalloids for large-volume resuscitation, and reserve normal saline for specific indications (hyponatremia, hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis, limited volumes).

### Continuing Maintenance Fluids Indefinitely Because the Patient Is NPO

The trap is that the patient is NPO, so maintenance fluids are started, and they continue day after day without reassessment, with the composition unchanged and the electrolytes unmonitored. The mechanism of harm is that prolonged maintenance fluids accumulate water and electrolytes that cause hyponatremia (if hypotonic), hypernatremia (if isotonic with inadequate free water), hyperchloremic acidosis, and overload, and they delay the resumption of enteral intake. The harm is iatrogenic electrolyte and volume disturbance. Reassess the indication daily, prefer enteral intake when possible, monitor electrolytes, and adjust the composition to the patient's evolving needs.

### Misreading Peripheral Edema as Hypervolemia in a Third-Spacing Patient

The trap is that the patient with sepsis or pancreatitis has gross edema and weight gain, so the nurse concludes they are overloaded and restricts fluids. The mechanism of harm is that the edema is interstitial (third-spaced), while the intravascular volume may be depleted, and restricting fluids worsens the intravascular hypovolemia and organ perfusion. The harm is worsened shock and renal failure. Assess the intravascular volume separately from the peripheral exam (heart rate, blood pressure, lactate, urine output, dynamic measures), and treat the intravascular deficit even in the edematous patient, anticipating the eventual mobilization phase.

### Trusting the Intake and Output or Daily Weight Without Context

The trap is that the intake and output record shows a positive balance and the weight is up, so the nurse concludes the patient is overloaded, missing that the patient is third-spacing or that the urine output is low because of poor perfusion rather than overload. The mechanism of harm is that the I&O and weight are aggregates that do not distinguish intravascular from interstitial fluid or perfusion from overload, and treating them as ground truth leads to the wrong therapy. The harm is diuresis of an intravascularly dry patient or fluid restriction of a patient who needs volume. Interpret the I&O and weight in the context of the exam, the vitals, the labs (BUN, creatinine, lactate), and the underlying condition, not in isolation.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess volume status using multiple converging signs (history, exam, vitals, urine output, labs, weight), or am I relying on a single sign?
- For the fluid I am hanging, do I know the composition, the indication, and the risk, and does it match the patient's physiology?
- During resuscitation, did I reassess fluid responsiveness before each bolus, and did I escalate to vasopressors when the patient was no longer responsive?
- Am I using balanced crystalloids for large-volume resuscitation rather than defaulting to normal saline?
- For the patient at risk of overload (heart failure, renal failure, elderly), am I using smaller boluses with closer monitoring and a low threshold for vasopressors?
- Did I reassess the maintenance fluid indication today, and am I monitoring electrolytes and adjusting composition?
- For the third-spacing patient, am I distinguishing intravascular volume from peripheral edema and treating accordingly?
- Are daily weights and intake and output being interpreted in the context of the exam and labs, not in isolation?
- When overload developed, did I coordinate diuresis or ultrafiltration and monitor the response with weights, urine output, and oxygenation?
- At handoff, did I communicate the volume trajectory, the fluid responsiveness, and the plan for the next shift?
