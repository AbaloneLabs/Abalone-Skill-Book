# Potassium and Dyskalemia Management

Potassium is the electrolyte most likely to kill a patient without warning, because it directly determines myocardial excitability, and both excess and deficiency can produce lethal arrhythmias within minutes. The judgment problem is that the serum potassium is a single number that lags behind total body potassium and is influenced by acid-base status, renal function, medications, and cellular shifts, so the nurse must interpret the number in context, anticipate who will swing, and act on the ECG as much as on the lab. Hyperkalemia is a leading cause of preventable death in renal failure, and hypokalemia from diuretics and GI loss causes avoidable arrhythmias. The nurse administering potassium — raising it or lowering it — is managing a myocardial excitability drug, and the margin between therapeutic and lethal is narrow.

## Core Rules

### Treat the ECG and the Patient, Not Just the Number

The serum potassium tells you the concentration; the ECG tells you what it is doing to the heart. In hyperkalemia, look for the progressive pattern of peaked T waves, loss of P waves, prolonged PR, widening QRS, and ultimately sine-wave ventricular fibrillation. In hypokalemia, look for flattened T waves, prominent U waves, ST depression, and premature ventricular contractions. Obtain a 12-lead ECG and continuous cardiac monitoring for any significant dyskalemia, and treat the patient with ECG changes or symptoms immediately, even before the lab results back. Conversely, a borderline number with a normal ECG in an asymptomatic patient may allow more measured management. The ECG is the bridge between the lab and the clinical urgency.

### Stabilize the Membrane First in Severe Hyperkalemia

Severe hyperkalemia (commonly potassium above 6.5 with ECG changes, or above 7.0 regardless) requires a staged approach, and the first step is membrane stabilization with intravenous calcium (calcium gluconate or calcium chloride) to prevent ventricular fibrillation. Calcium does not lower the potassium; it stabilizes the myocardium for thirty to sixty minutes, buying time for the potassium-lowering interventions to work. After calcium, shift potassium into cells with insulin and dextrose (the most reliable rapid shift, onset in fifteen minutes, lasting hours), with nebulized or inhaled beta-agonist as an adjunct, and with sodium bicarbonate in acidotic patients. Then remove potassium from the body with loop diuretics, gastrointestinal binders (patiromer, sodium zirconium, or kayexalate), or — definitively — dialysis. Know the sequence, have the medications drawn up, and verify the insulin dose meticulously (insulin errors in hyperkalemia protocols are common and dangerous).

### Replace Potassium Safely and Slowly in Hypokalemia

Potassium replacement must respect the rate at which the heart can tolerate a rise. Intravenous potassium is irritating to veins and dangerous if given too fast (peak serum concentration can cause arrhythmia), so the peripheral rate is typically limited to 10 mEq per hour (20 mEq per hour via central line with cardiac monitoring in severe deficit). Dilute potassium adequately (never give undiluted), use a pump, and monitor the insertion site for infiltration and phlebitis. Oral replacement is safer and preferred when the patient can tolerate it, but it is slow and may cause GI irritation. Recheck the potassium after replacement to confirm response, because ongoing losses (diarrhea, diuresis) may outpace the replacement. Replete magnesium concurrently, because hypomagnesemia causes refractory hypokalemia — the kidney wastes potassium when magnesium is low.

### Anticipate Potassium Shifts in the Patients at Risk

Dyskalemia is rarely random; it follows predictable patterns the nurse can anticipate. Renal failure, acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, tumor lysis syndrome, and potassium-sparing drugs cause hyperkalemia. Diuretics (especially loop and thiazide), diarrhea, vomiting, nasogastric suction, corticosteroids, and amphotericin cause hypokalemia. Insulin and beta-agonists shift potassium into cells (used therapeutically in hyperkalemia, but a hazard in a hypokalemic patient on insulin for diabetic ketoacidosis). Acidosis shifts potassium out of cells; alkalosis shifts it in. Identify the at-risk patients at the start of the shift, monitor the potassium proactively, and intervene before the number reaches the dangerous range. Prevention is safer than rescue.

### Correct Magnesium Concurrently in Any Dyskalemia

Magnesium is required for the cellular uptake of potassium, and hypomagnesemia causes renal potassium wasting and refractory hypokalemia — repleting potassium without correcting magnesium is futile. In any patient with hypokalemia, check and replete magnesium. In hyperkalemia, magnesium status matters less for the potassium itself but is relevant to arrhythmia risk. Make magnesium part of the routine electrolyte panel interpretation in dyskalemia, and do not consider potassium correction complete until magnesium is adequate.

### Coordinate with Dialysis for Refractory or Renal-Failure Hyperkalemia

In a patient with renal failure, hyperkalemia will recur after medical management because the underlying problem (the kidney cannot excrete potassium) persists. Medical therapy buys time; dialysis removes potassium. For severe or recurrent hyperkalemia in renal failure, contact the nephrology team early, prepare for dialysis (vascular access, dialysis orders), and continue temporizing measures (calcium, insulin-dextrose, binders) until dialysis is underway. Do not assume one round of medical therapy has solved the problem in a dialysis-dependent patient; the potassium will rise again.

### Monitor and Document the Trend and the Response

Trend the potassium, the ECG, and the interventions, and document the response. A potassium that does not rise after appropriate replacement suggests ongoing loss (reassess for diarrhea, diuresis, magnesium deficiency); a potassium that does not fall after medical therapy for hyperkalemia suggests severe renal failure or ongoing release (rhabdomyolysis, tumor lysis). Communicate the trend at handoff, because the next nurse inherits both the number and the trajectory, and the trajectory predicts the next crisis.

## Common Traps

### Treating a Single Potassium Number Without the ECG or the Clinical Context

The trap is that the lab reports a potassium of 6.8, the nurse notifies the provider, and the team treats the number with binders or insulin without first looking at the ECG. The mechanism of harm is that the urgency is determined by the ECG, not the number — a patient with peaked T waves and a widening QRS needs calcium immediately to prevent arrest, while a patient with a normal ECG may tolerate slower management. The harm is either fatal arrhythmia from undertreated severe hyperkalemia or unnecessary risky therapy for a number that was a lab artifact (hemolysis). Always obtain an ECG and continuous monitoring for significant dyskalemia, and treat the patient with ECG changes first and fastest.

### Giving IV Potassium Too Fast or Undiluted Because the Number Is Low

The trap is that a potassium of 2.9 with ectopy looks dangerous, so the nurse pushes IV potassium faster than the safe rate to "fix it now." The mechanism of harm is that rapid IV potassium creates a transient high serum concentration at the heart that can cause ventricular fibrillation and cardiac arrest — the treatment becomes the cause of death. The harm is a fatal arrhythmia from iatrogenic rapid replacement. Limit peripheral IV potassium to 10 mEq per hour (20 via central line with monitoring), dilute it, use a pump, and recheck the potassium. Use oral replacement when possible.

### Forgetting to Replete Magnesium, Causing Refractory Hypokalemia

The trap is that the patient's potassium keeps dropping despite repeated replacement, and the team escalates the potassium dose without checking the magnesium. The mechanism of harm is that hypomagnesemia causes the kidney to waste potassium, so no amount of potassium replacement sticks until the magnesium is corrected. The harm is ongoing, treatment-resistant hypokalemia and arrhythmia risk. Always check and replete magnesium in any patient with hypokalemia, and consider hypomagnesemia when potassium replacement fails to correct the level.

### Assuming Medical Therapy Has Solved Hyperkalemia in Renal Failure

The trap is that the team gives insulin-dextrose and a binder, the potassium drops from 7.1 to 5.8, and the team declares the hyperkalemia resolved. The mechanism of harm is that the insulin shift is temporary (hours), and the binders work slowly, while the underlying renal failure continues to retain potassium; the potassium will rebound within hours. The harm is recurrent severe hyperkalemia, often overnight or after discharge from the emergency department, with arrhythmia or arrest. In renal failure, medical therapy buys time for dialysis — it does not solve the problem. Arrange definitive removal (dialysis) and continue temporizing measures and monitoring until it is underway.

### Missing the Patient on Insulin for DKA Whose Potassium Is Plummeting

The trap is that the diabetic ketoacidosis patient presents with normal or high potassium (from acidosis-driven cellular shift), so the team does not worry, then starts the insulin drip, and the potassium crashes as insulin drives it into cells and the underlying total-body deficit is revealed. The mechanism of harm is that total-body potassium in DKA is profoundly depleted despite a normal or high serum level, and insulin therapy unmasks the deficit, producing severe hypokalemia and arrhythmia. The harm is a cardiac arrest during what looked like a straightforward DKA treatment. Check the potassium frequently during the insulin drip (every one to two hours initially), replete aggressively as it falls, and anticipate the drop rather than reacting to it.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- For any significant dyskalemia, did I obtain a 12-lead ECG and initiate continuous cardiac monitoring, and am I treating the ECG as much as the number?
- In severe hyperkalemia, did I give calcium first to stabilize the membrane, then insulin-dextrose to shift potassium, with binders or dialysis for removal?
- For IV potassium replacement, am I within the safe rate (10 mEq/hr peripheral, 20 central), diluted, on a pump, with the site monitored?
- Did I check and replete magnesium in this patient with hypokalemia, and is the potassium refractory because of untreated hypomagnesemia?
- Have I identified the at-risk patients on my assignment (renal failure, diuretics, insulin, acidosis) and am I monitoring their potassium proactively?
- For the insulin-dextrose in hyperkalemia, did I verify the insulin dose meticulously and ensure dextrose is given to prevent hypoglycemia?
- For the renal failure patient with hyperkalemia, did I arrange dialysis and continue temporizing measures rather than assuming medical therapy solved it?
- For the DKA patient on insulin, am I checking potassium every one to two hours and repleting as it falls?
- Did I recheck the potassium after replacement or therapy to confirm response, and am I treating ongoing losses?
- At handoff, did I communicate the potassium trend, the ECG findings, and the planned next interventions?
