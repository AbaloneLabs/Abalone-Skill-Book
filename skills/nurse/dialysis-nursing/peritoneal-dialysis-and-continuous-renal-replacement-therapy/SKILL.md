# Peritoneal Dialysis and Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy

Peritoneal dialysis (PD) and continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) are the slower, gentler cousins of hemodialysis, and that slowness is exactly why they are easy to mismanage. PD depends on a sterile peritoneal cavity and a permanent catheter that the patient or family often handle at home, so nursing errors in technique or teaching can cause peritonitis, the complication that ends PD as an option. CRRT runs continuously on critically ill patients with tenuous hemodynamics and active anticoagulation, so small errors in fluid balance, anticoagulation, or filter care accumulate over 24 hours into serious harm. This skill covers the judgment needed to perform and manage PD and CRRT safely.

## Core Rules

### Peritoneal dialysis: protect the catheter and the sterile field above all

The PD catheter is a permanent direct line into the peritoneal cavity, and peritonitis is almost always a contamination or exit-site event. Before any exchange, perform hand hygiene and prepare a clean, dry workspace; don a mask and use sterile or no-touch technique for connections as policy requires. Scrub the connection with the approved antiseptic for the full contact time, and never touch the exposed end of the catheter or transfer set to anything non-sterile. Inspect the exit site at every encounter for redness, drainage, tenderness, and tunnel integrity, and teach the patient the same technique, because most peritonitis originates at home.

### Perform exchanges with attention to flow, volume, and dwell

For each exchange, confirm the prescription: dialysate concentration, volume, dwell time, and number of cycles. Warm the dialysate to body temperature with an approved device, never a microwave. Watch the drain for volume, color, and clarity; cloudy effluent is peritonitis until proven otherwise and must be reported and cultured immediately. Track ultrafiltration per exchange and per day, because the prescription depends on it, and persistent negative or excessive ultrafiltration signals a problem with the prescription, the membrane, or the catheter. Confirm the patient tolerates the dwell without pain, shortness of breath, or hernia stress.

### Recognize and respond to PD complications

Be alert to peritonitis, exit-site and tunnel infection, catheter malposition or omentum capture, inflow or outflow failure, leakage, hernia, hyperglycemia from dextrose absorption, and constipation that impairs flow. For cloudy effluent, abdominal pain, or fever, obtain a PD fluid cell count and culture, notify the nephrology team, and expect intraperitoneal or systemic antibiotics; do not wait for confirmation to escalate a clearly infected patient. For inflow or outflow failure, troubleshoot position, check for kinks or constipation, and escalate for imaging or intervention if it persists.

### CRRT: verify the prescription and the patient before starting

CRRT is prescribed by mode (CVVH, CVVHD, CVVHDF), with specific blood flow, effluent or dose target, replacement fluid composition, dialysate composition, fluid removal goal, anticoagulation, and chemistry replacement plan. Before connecting, confirm each parameter against the order and against the patient's current status: weight, electrolytes, ionized calcium, magnesium, phosphorus, acid-base, and coagulation. A CRRT prescription that is not individualized to today's labs will cause progressive dyselectrolytemia over the next 24 hours. Confirm the access is patent and adequate for the prescribed blood flow.

### Manage anticoagulation to balance clotting and bleeding

CRRT circuits tend to clot, and anticoagulation is used to extend filter life, but the critically ill patient often bleeds. Follow the prescribed anticoagulation, whether systemic heparin, regional citrate, or no anticoagulation. For regional citrate, monitor ionized calcium closely, because citrate toxicity causes hypocalcemia with prolonged QT and hypotension; follow the protocol for calcium replacement and watch total-to-ionized calcium ratio as a marker of accumulation. For heparin, monitor for bleeding and circuit clotting and adjust per protocol. Never run a citrate CRRT line without confirming the calcium infusion is running and correctly traced.

### Monitor fluid balance hourly and reconcile continuously

CRRT fluid balance is the difference between large inflow and outflow volumes, and small programming or documentation errors compound over hours. Check the machine's net fluid removal against the prescribed goal at least hourly, and reconcile the patient's total intake and output, including CRRT, infusions, oral intake, drains, and urine. If the net removal is wrong, the patient develops occult volume overload or insidious hypovolemia over the shift. Investigate any discrepancy between the prescribed and actual fluid balance immediately rather than waiting for the next vital signs.

### Prevent and manage circuit clotting and pressure alarms

Circuit clotting ends treatments early, wastes blood, and exposes the patient to access manipulation. Maintain the prescribed blood flow, ensure good access function, manage anticoagulation proactively, and avoid interruptions. Respond to pressure alarms by identifying the cause, whether access recirculation, kinking, clotting, or patient position, rather than silencing. When a circuit clots, follow the protocol for return or non-return of blood, change the circuit promptly, and document the cause and downtime so treatment is not silently under-dosed.

### Coordinate chemistry replacement and nutrition with CRRT

CRRT removes water-soluble substances continuously, including potassium, magnesium, phosphorus, and many medications, in particular antibiotics. Monitor these electrolytes at the prescribed frequency and replace per protocol, because depletion is rapid. Review antibiotic dosing with pharmacy, because standard doses are often subtherapeutic on CRRT. Ensure nutrition, including parenteral nutrition if used, accounts for the glucose load from dialysate or replacement fluid and for the continuous amino acid loss.

## Common Traps

### Breaks in sterile technique during PD connections

Because PD exchanges become routine, the temptation is to streamline the connection and shorten the antiseptic contact time. The mechanism of harm is that even momentary contamination of the exposed transfer set introduces organisms into the peritoneal cavity, and peritonitis is the leading reason patients transfer off PD to hemodialysis. The false signal is that most sloppy connections do not immediately cause infection. The harm is a peritonitis episode that scars the peritoneal membrane and ends PD as a therapy for that patient.

### Ignoring cloudy effluent as a minor finding

Cloudy effluent can be dismissed as fibrin or a cold sample, particularly if the patient feels well. The mechanism of harm is that cloudy effluent is the cardinal sign of peritonitis, and delay in culturing and treating allows progression to sepsis and membrane damage. The false signal is the absence of fever or pain early in the course. The harm is a more severe infection, longer membrane scarring, and a higher chance of catheter loss.

### Citrate accumulation causing unrecognized hypocalcemia

Regional citrate anticoagulation is safe when monitored, but in hepatic dysfunction or high citrate flow, citrate accumulates and complexes calcium. The mechanism of harm is that ionized calcium falls, causing prolonged QT, hypotension, and arrhythmia, while total calcium may look normal or high because it is bound to citrate. The false signal is a reassuring total calcium. The harm is hemodynamic collapse or arrhythmia from profound ionized hypocalcemia that the total calcium hid.

### Hourly fluid balance errors accumulating over a shift

Because CRRT runs continuously and the numbers are large, a small net removal error, such as 50 mL per hour off, becomes over a liter across a shift. The mechanism of harm is that the patient is slowly over-resuscitated or over-diuresed without any single dramatic event, and the problem is only recognized at the end of the shift or the next weight. The false signal is that the machine is running and the vitals are stable. The harm is occult volume overload or hypovolemia that contradicts the apparent precision of the therapy.

### Treating CRRT circuit clotting as inevitable

Frequent clotting can be normalized as just how this patient's blood behaves, so the nurse changes circuits repeatedly without investigating. The mechanism of harm is that each clotting episode shortens treatment time, under-delivers the dialysis dose, and exposes the patient to blood loss and access manipulation, while a fixable cause, such as inadequate blood flow, low anticoagulation, or access recirculation, goes unaddressed. The false signal is that treatment resumes after each circuit change. The harm is cumulative under-dialysis and anemia.

### Forgetting CRRT-driven electrolyte and drug depletion

Because CRRT removes solutes continuously, potassium, magnesium, phosphorus, and antibiotics fall faster than on intermittent dialysis. The mechanism of harm is that standard replacement intervals and antibiotic doses are insufficient, and the patient develops arrhythmogenic hypokalemia or refractory hypophosphatemia, or an infection worsens on subtherapeutic antibiotics. The false signal is that labs were acceptable at the start of treatment. The harm is electrolyte instability and treatment failure driven by the therapy itself.

## Self-Check

- For every PD exchange, did I perform hand hygiene, use a mask and no-touch or sterile technique, scrub the connection for the full contact time, and inspect the exit site?
- Did I check effluent for clarity and volume every exchange, and did I culture and escalate immediately for any cloudiness, abdominal pain, or fever?
- For CRRT, did I verify the mode, blood flow, dose, fluids, anticoagulation, and removal goal against today's labs and patient status before starting?
- If using regional citrate, did I confirm the calcium infusion is running and correctly traced, and am I monitoring ionized calcium and the total-to-ionized ratio?
- Did I check net fluid removal against the prescription at least hourly and reconcile total intake and output, investigating any discrepancy immediately?
- Did I respond to pressure and clotting alarms by identifying cause rather than silencing, and did I document downtime and circuit changes so treatment dose is not silently lost?
- Did I monitor potassium, magnesium, phosphorus, and acid-base at the prescribed frequency and replace per protocol, and did I confirm antibiotic dosing with pharmacy for CRRT clearance?
- Did I account for CRRT-related glucose and amino acid effects when reviewing the patient's nutrition plan?
