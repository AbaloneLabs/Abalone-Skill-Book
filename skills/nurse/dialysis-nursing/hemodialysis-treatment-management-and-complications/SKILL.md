# Hemodialysis Treatment Management and Complications

A hemodialysis treatment is a controlled physiologic insult. Over three to four hours the machine removes fluid, solutes, and electrolytes that accumulated over days, and the patient's hemodynamics must absorb that change in real time. The nurse's job is not simply to run the machine; it is to anticipate which patients will not tolerate the prescribed treatment, to detect complications within the first minutes when they are reversible, and to advocate for prescription changes when the ordered dose or fluid removal is unsafe for the patient in the chair. This skill covers the judgment needed before, during, and after a hemodialysis session.

## Core Rules

### Assess and individualize before connecting the patient

Before starting treatment, reconcile the prescription with the patient's current status. Confirm the dry or target weight, the treatment time, the blood and dialysate flow rates, the dialyzer, the anticoagulation plan, and the ultrafiltration goal. Weigh the patient and calculate the actual fluid to remove, accounting for any intake since the last treatment. Review the most recent labs, particularly potassium, phosphorus, calcium, hemoglobin, and coagulation parameters, because these change what dialysate composition and medications are safe today. Confirm the access type and assess it before needling. If the prescription does not match the patient in front of you, hold and clarify with the nephrology team.

### Assess and protect the vascular access first

The access is the patient's lifeline, and a single compromised treatment can damage it permanently. For an arteriovenous fistula or graft, assess thrill, bruit, pulse, temperature, and skin integrity before cannulation, and follow unit cannulation technique to avoid infiltration. For a tunneled catheter, inspect the exit site for infection and confirm line patency. Never cannulate an access that lacks the expected thrill or bruit, and never force flush against resistance. Confirm needle direction and positioning, and secure the needles to prevent dislodgement during treatment.

### Initiate treatment with deliberate monitoring

At treatment initiation, set the blood flow rate progressively rather than jumping to target, and watch the venous and arterial pressures, the patient's hemodynamics, and the access site for the first minutes. This is when access failure, needle infiltration, hypotension, and dialyzer reactions most often declare themselves. Stay at the chairside during initiation until pressures and the patient are stable. Confirm the dialysate conductivity and temperature are correct and that the air detector and venous pressure alarms are active and within range.

### Manage ultrafiltration against tolerance, not just against target

The prescribed ultrafiltration volume is a goal, not a contract. If the patient becomes hypotensive, crampy, dizzy, or develops chest pain or arrhythmia during fluid removal, slow or stop ultrafiltration, place the patient supine, reduce the blood flow rate if needed, and give fluid as indicated. Re-evaluate the target weight with the team, because a patient who repeatedly drops their blood pressure at the same target weight may need the dry weight raised. Squeezing the full prescribed volume into a hypotensive patient causes harm that outweighs the value of the extra fluid removal.

### Monitor throughout and respond to alarms and symptoms

Throughout the treatment, assess vital signs at the prescribed interval and whenever the patient reports symptoms. Investigate every alarm: venous pressure changes can signal needle dislodgement, kinking, or clotting; arterial pressure changes can signal access compromise; conductivity or temperature alarms can signal dialysate problems that endanger all patients on the same machine. Do not silence recurring alarms without finding the cause. Watch for dialyzer reactions, which range from mild itching and chest or back pain to anaphylaxis, particularly in the first minutes of treatment.

### Anticipate and manage common intra-treatment complications

Be prepared to recognize and respond to intradialytic hypotension, muscle cramping, arrhythmia, chest pain, shortness of breath, fever, itching, and seizures. Have a clear plan for each: stop ultrafiltration, reposition, give normal saline or albumin as ordered, obtain an electrocardiogram for chest pain, and escalate to the nephrology team and rapid response as the picture requires. For suspected anaphylactoid or pyrogenic reactions, stop the treatment, clamp the lines without returning the blood, and treat and escalate immediately.

### Terminate safely and reconcile the session

At termination, follow the correct rinse-back procedure and confirm hemostasis at the needle sites before the patient sits up or leaves. Recheck vital signs and weight, and compare actual fluid removed to the goal. Document the treatment, any complications, the access assessment, any medications given, and the patient's tolerance. Communicate any access concerns, hypotensive episodes, or prescription problems to the team and to the next treatment. Confirm follow-up labs and the next treatment plan.

### Maintain anticoagulation and bleeding balance

If heparin or regional citrate is used, follow the anticoagulation plan and monitor for both clotting of the circuit and bleeding at the access or other sites. For patients at high bleeding risk, confirm whether the treatment is ordered without heparin or with a regional anticoagulation strategy, and watch the circuit for clotting that can shorten the treatment.

## Common Traps

### Rigidly chasing the target weight into hypotension

The prescribed ultrafiltration volume is calculated from an estimated dry weight, and that estimate is often wrong or stale. The mechanism of harm is that the nurse continues removing fluid to reach the ordered target even as the patient becomes hypotensive, crampy, or dizzy, treating the number as more important than the patient. The false signal is the orderly progress of the ultrafiltration curve toward the goal. The harm is repeated intradialytic hypotension, which causes myocardial stunning, access thrombosis, and cumulative end-organ injury, and which often means the dry weight itself is too low.

### Silencing recurring alarms without finding the cause

Pressure alarms fire often, and the easy response is to silence and re-start. The mechanism of harm is that a recurring venous pressure change can be the only warning of needle dislodgement, clotting, or access compromise, and silencing masks it. The false signal is that the alarm usually resets and the treatment appears to continue normally. The harm is a venous needle dislodgement causing rapid blood loss, or a clotted circuit ending the treatment early, either of which the alarm was trying to prevent.

### Initiating treatment and walking away

The first minutes of a treatment are the highest-risk window, but the temptation once the machine is running is to move to the next patient. The mechanism of harm is that access failure, infiltration, hypotension, and dialyzer reactions declare themselves during initiation, and an unattended patient can deteriorate before anyone returns. The false signal is the stable-looking machine. The harm is a preventable complication, such as a large infiltration or an anaphylactoid reaction, that worsens because no one was at the chairside.

### Cannulating a compromised access out of schedule pressure

When a patient has a marginal access and the schedule is tight, there is pressure to cannulate anyway to avoid canceling a treatment. The mechanism of harm is that needling an access without a thrill or with an abnormal exam risks infiltration, thrombosis, or failure of the only lifeline the patient has. The false signal is that the access has worked before. The harm is permanent loss of the access, which can require a catheter and months of reconstruction.

### Trusting the conductivity display without questioning dialysate

The machine displays dialysate conductivity as a proxy for composition, and nurses may trust it without recognizing that a wrong concentrate, a depleted bicarbonate cartridge, or a machine error can produce a normal-looking conductivity with a dangerous composition. The mechanism of harm is that all patients on the affected machine receive the wrong dialysate, causing hemolysis, dysequilibrium, or arrhythmia. The false signal is the normal conductivity reading. The harm is a cluster of injured patients traced back to one machine setup error.

### Underestimating the risk of venous needle dislodgement

A partially dislodged venous needle can bleed into the linens or chair without a dramatic alarm, because the blood flow is continuous and the venous pressure may not drop sharply. The mechanism of harm is that the blood loss is hidden and ongoing, and the patient can exsanguinate before the alarm fires. The false signal is a treatment that looks like it is running normally. The harm is catastrophic hemorrhage, which is why access site visibility and securement are non-negotiable.

## Self-Check

- Before connecting the patient, did I reconcile the prescription, weight, labs, access assessment, and ultrafiltration goal with the patient in front of me today?
- Did I assess thrill, bruit, pulse, and skin before cannulation, and refuse to cannulate an access with an abnormal exam?
- During initiation, did I stay at the chairside until blood flow, pressures, and the patient's hemodynamics were stable?
- When the patient became hypotensive, crampy, or symptomatic, did I slow or stop ultrafiltration and re-evaluate the target weight rather than continuing to the prescribed volume?
- For every alarm, did I identify the cause rather than silencing and restarting?
- Did I monitor for dialyzer reactions in the first minutes and throughout, and was I prepared to stop the treatment and clamp the lines for a suspected reaction?
- Did I confirm hemostasis, recheck vital signs and weight, and reconcile actual versus prescribed fluid removal at termination?
- Did I document and communicate access concerns, hypotensive episodes, and prescription problems so the next treatment can be adjusted?
