# Vascular Access Management for Dialysis

The dialysis vascular access is the single point of failure for a patient's renal replacement therapy. An arteriovenous fistula, graft, or tunneled catheter is the patient's lifeline, and each treatment is an opportunity either to preserve it for years or to damage it irreversibly in minutes. Access complications, including thrombosis, infection, stenosis, and infiltration, are the leading cause of hospitalization and death in dialysis patients, and most are preventable with disciplined nursing assessment and technique. This skill covers the judgment needed to assess, cannulate, maintain, and protect dialysis vascular access.

## Core Rules

### Assess the access before every cannulation or use

Before touching a needle or catheter to the access, perform a full assessment. For a fistula or graft, palpate for thrill throughout the access length, auscultate for bruit, assess pulse quality, skin temperature, color, and integrity, and look for edema, redness, drainage, pseudoaneurysm, or new bruising. Map the access direction and identify the arterial and venous segments based on the most recent access map, not memory. For a tunneled catheter, inspect the exit site and tunnel for redness, drainage, tenderness, swelling, and integrity of the cuff and sutures. A change in thrill, bruit, or pulse from the prior treatment is a signal to stop and escalate before needling.

### Cannulate with deliberate, individualized technique

Cannulation is a skill that determines access survival. Use the correct needle gauge for the access maturity and flow needs, and choose sites using a rotation plan to avoid aneurysm formation and segmental stenosis. Insert needles at the correct angle for the access type, with the arterial needle pointing toward the arterial anastomosis and the venous needle toward the venous return, unless unit practice or the access map dictates otherwise. Advance slowly and feel for the pop of entry. If you meet resistance, pain out of proportion, or a sudden hematoma, stop immediately, do not advance or flush against resistance, and manage the infiltration.

### Recognize and respond to infiltration immediately

If a needle infiltrates, stop, clamp, and remove the needle, apply firm pressure to achieve hemostasis without occluding the access, and assess the extent of the infiltration. Mark the borders of any hematoma, monitor the distal limb for perfusion, and document the infiltration grade. Do not re-cannulate an infiltrated segment, and re-evaluate whether the treatment can proceed at other sites or must be held. Notify the nephrology team and document thoroughly, because an infiltration can compromise future access use and may require imaging.

### Manage tunneled catheters with strict asepsis

Tunneled catheters carry a high risk of bacteremia and metastatic infection, so aseptic technique is non-negotiable. Perform hand hygiene and don mask and sterile gloves for any catheter manipulation, scrub the hub and catheter connections with the approved antiseptic for the required contact time, and never leave a catheter lumen open to air; use a cap or saline-filled syringe. Assess the exit site at every treatment and culture promptly if there is purulent drainage, new tenderness, or unexplained fever. Do not use a catheter that appears infected for dialysis without explicit direction from the nephrology team.

### Detect dysfunction early through pressure and flow monitoring

Access dysfunction often shows itself first in the treatment parameters. A rising venous pressure over successive treatments, difficulty achieving the prescribed blood flow rate, recirculation, prolonged bleeding from needle sites after removal, or a weakened thrill all suggest stenosis or partial thrombosis. Track these trends across treatments and report them, because early referral for angioplasty or thrombectomy can save the access, while late detection usually loses it. Do not dismiss a gradual pressure change as machine variation without investigating.

### Protect the access and the limb between and during treatments

Educate the patient continuously about access protection: no blood pressure cuffs, venipuncture, or IV insertion on the access arm; no heavy sleeping on the arm; no tight clothing or jewelry restricting flow. During treatment, secure needles and tubing to prevent dislodgement, keep the access visible, and ensure the patient cannot inadvertently pull the lines. After treatment, confirm hemostasis with the correct pressure and duration before the patient leaves, because premature pressure release causes hematoma and access damage.

### Prevent and recognize access-related infection and bacteremia

For fistulas and grafts, watch for exit-site, puncture-site, or tunnel infection with redness, warmth, swelling, pain, or drainage, and obtain cultures and notify the team promptly. For catheters, suspect catheter-related bloodstream infection with any new fever, chills, or rigors, particularly during or after treatment, and escalate immediately. Teach the patient to report fever, redness, or drainage at home, because delayed treatment of access infection leads to sepsis and endovascular infection.

### Coordinate access surveillance and intervention

Maintain and update the access map at the interval required by policy, participate in monthly access surveillance with flow studies and pressure trends, and ensure referrals for imaging or intervention are tracked to completion. A patient who misses a scheduled angioplasty may arrive for the next treatment with a thrombosed access.

## Common Traps

### Cannulating against an abnormal exam under schedule pressure

When the schedule is tight and the access feels different, the temptation is to cannulate anyway and see if it works. The mechanism of harm is that a weakened thrill, absent bruit, or new edema often signals stenosis or early thrombosis, and needling a compromised access can precipitate thrombosis, infiltration, or failure. The false signal is that the access has been usable for months. The harm is loss of the access, which may force a catheter and its infection risk for months.

### Repeatedly using the same cannulation sites

Cannulating the easiest, most familiar segment each treatment feels efficient and is less painful for the patient, so the path of least resistance is to reuse the same sites. The mechanism of harm is that repeated needling of one segment causes aneurysm formation, skin thinning, and stenosis at adjacent segments, ultimately shortening access life and risking catastrophic bleed. The false signal is the easy cannulation and patient preference. The harm is an access that fails earlier than it should and a bleed risk at the thinned aneurysm wall.

### Advancing or flushing a needle that meets resistance

When a needle does not flow well, the instinct is to flush or advance to improve position. The mechanism of harm is that resistance usually means the needle is against the wall, in a side branch, or infiltrating, and forcing flush expands the infiltration and damages the access wall. The false signal is that flushing sometimes temporarily improves flow. The harm is a larger infiltration, hematoma, and compromised future access use.

### Underestimating catheter hub contamination risk

Because catheter manipulations are frequent and routine, strict aseptic discipline can erode, particularly the contact time for antiseptic scrub and the practice of capping lumens instantly. The mechanism of harm is that even brief exposure of the hub to non-sterile technique introduces organisms that seed the bloodstream, and catheter-related bacteremia is among the most dangerous complications in dialysis. The false signal is that most manipulations do not immediately cause infection. The harm is a metastatic infection, including endocarditis and spinal abscess, traced back to a moment of lax technique.

### Dismissing a gradual rise in venous pressure as machine variation

Venous pressure creeps up over several treatments, and the easy interpretation is machine calibration or needle position. The mechanism of harm is that a progressive venous pressure rise is a classic early sign of venous anastomotic stenosis, and ignoring the trend delays referral until the access thromboses. The false signal is that any single treatment still runs. The harm is loss of a salvageable access that angioplasty could have rescued.

### Inadequate hemostasis pressure or duration after needle removal

To keep the schedule moving, staff or patients may release pressure too quickly after needle removal. The mechanism of harm is that incomplete hemostasis causes hematoma at the puncture site, which compresses and damages the access and predisposes to stenosis. The false signal is that bleeding appears to have stopped at the surface. The harm is subcutaneous bleeding that compromises the access and shortens its life.

## Self-Check

- Before every cannulation or catheter use, did I assess thrill, bruit, pulse, skin, and any change from the prior treatment, and did I stop if the exam was abnormal?
- Did I cannulate using a site rotation plan, correct needle gauge and angle, and stop immediately on resistance, pain, or hematoma?
- For any infiltration, did I stop, remove the needle, achieve hemostasis, mark the hematoma, monitor perfusion, document the grade, and notify the team?
- For catheter manipulation, did I use mask, sterile gloves, hand hygiene, the full antiseptic contact time, and never leave a lumen open to air?
- Did I track venous pressure, blood flow difficulty, recirculation, and prolonged bleeding across treatments, and escalate trends suggesting stenosis?
- Did I keep the access visible and secured during treatment, and confirm hemostasis with correct pressure and duration before the patient left?
- Did I assess for and culture any signs of access infection, and escalate any new fever or rigors during or after treatment?
- Did I update the access map, participate in surveillance, and ensure any imaging or intervention referral was tracked to completion?
