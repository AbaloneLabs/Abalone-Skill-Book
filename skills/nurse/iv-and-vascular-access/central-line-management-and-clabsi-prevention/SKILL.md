# Central Line Management and CLABSI Prevention

Central venous catheters (CVCs) deliver life-sustaining therapy — vasopressors, chemotherapy, parenteral nutrition, long-term antibiotics — but they also carry the highest device-related infection risk in the hospital. A central line-associated bloodstream infection (CLABSI) can extend a hospital stay by weeks, require ICU-level care, and kill the patient. The nurse's moment-to-moment decisions about access technique, dressing care, flushing, and line handling are the primary determinant of whether a CVC remains a lifeline or becomes a lethal portal for pathogens. This is a domain where "close enough" asepsis produces catastrophic, delayed, and invisible harm.

## Core Rules

### Maximal Barrier Precautions During Insertion and Access

CVC insertion requires maximal sterile barriers: sterile gloves and gown, mask, cap, and full-body sterile drape — for the operator and all assistants. This is not negotiable and is not equivalent to the precautions used for a PIVC. During any access of the line — drawing blood, administering medications, changing the cap — perform rigorous hand hygiene and apply sterile gloves and antiseptic for port cleaning per protocol. The hub and port are the most common entry points for skin flora that cause CLABSI.

### Scrub the Hub, Every Time

Every catheter port, needleless connector, and access point must be disinfected before access. Use the appropriate antiseptic (chlorhexidine/alcohol preferred) with active friction for the specified contact time (commonly 5–15 seconds) and allow it to dry. Passive "wipe and go" does not disinfect. This single practice is among the highest-yield CLABSI prevention measures, and skipping or shortening it is a leading cause of contamination.

### Dressing Care: Regimen, Inspection, and Change Triggers

Use a sterile transparent semipermeable dressing (chlorhexidine-impregnated patch where indicated) and change it every seven days, or immediately if it is damp, loose, or visibly soiled. Gauze dressings are changed every 48 hours. Inspect the site at every shift for redness, drainage, tenderness, swelling, and integrity. A compromised dressing is a breach in the barrier; do not reinforce it — replace it using aseptic technique.

### Access Only When Necessary, De-access Immediately

Each entry into the system is a contamination opportunity. Do not routinely open the line to draw blood if peripheral draws are feasible and the clinical need does not require central sampling. When access is complete, cap, clean, and secure the line promptly. Avoid leaving the line open or uncapped between med passes.

### Line Designation and Dedication

Each lumen should have a designated purpose (medication, nutrition, blood draws) and be labeled. Do not administer incompatible substances through the same lumen without adequate flushing. Dedicated lumens reduce contamination and medication error risk. Parenteral nutrition ideally runs through a dedicated lumen and is handled with the highest level of asepsis.

### Daily Review of Line Necessity

Every day, ask whether the central line is still needed. The strongest predictor of CLABSI is duration of dwell; lines that remain after the clinical indication ends are pure risk. Advocate for removal when the line is no longer essential, and document the daily necessity review. This is a nurse-driven safety function, not solely a physician decision.

### Recognize and Escalate Line Malfunction and Infection Signs

Signs of malfunction: inability to draw blood, resistance to flush, sluggish infusion, or patient reports of discomfort or hearing a "squirting" sound (possible catheter fracture/pinch-off). Signs of infection: fever without another source, redness or drainage at the exit site, tenderness along the tunnel, rigors during or after line access (a red flag for bacteremia from the line). Escalate promptly — do not "watch" a CVC with new fever and no other source.

## Common Traps

**Assuming a sterile-looking port is clean.** Needleless connectors and hubs appear clean and dry, which creates a false sense that they do not require disinfection before each access. The mechanism of harm is biofilm: microbial colonization on the internal and external surfaces of the connector is invisible to the eye, and each access without proper scrub-and-dry pushes skin flora and biofilm fragments directly into the bloodstream. The nurse who skips the scrub because "I just accessed it an hour ago" is inoculating the patient with bacteria every time. The harm is a bloodstream infection that presents days later as fever and sepsis, with no obvious localizing sign at the line, making it easy to miss the source. Scrub the hub with friction for the full contact time, every access, no exceptions.

**Reinforcing a damp or loose dressing instead of replacing it.** When a transparent dressing lifts at the edge or absorbs a small amount of drainage, the temptation is to tape it down to save time and avoid disturbing the site. The mechanism of harm is that a non-intact dressing no longer provides an occlusive microbial barrier, and the moist, warm environment under a partially detached dressing is ideal for bacterial proliferation at the exit site. Reinforcing with extra tape traps moisture and bacteria against the skin rather than removing them. The harm is a localized exit-site infection that can ascend into the bloodstream. Replace compromised dressings using full aseptic technique; never reinforce.

**Drawing blood routinely from the central line for convenience.** Central lines spare the patient repeated peripheral sticks, so it feels patient-centered to draw labs from the line. The mechanism of harm is that each blood draw requires opening the system, discarding or wasting blood, and manipulating the catheter — all of which increase contamination risk and catheter lumen damage. Frequent drawing also increases the discard volume and can cause iatrogenic anemia in critically ill patients. The harm is a CLABSI risk that accumulates with each access and anemia that complicates recovery. Draw from the line only when peripheral access is not feasible or when central sampling is clinically required, and follow the discard/flush protocol precisely.

**Ignoring rigors during or immediately after line access.** A patient who suddenly shakes with chills during a flush or medication administration is exhibiting a classic sign of bacteremia seeded from the line, but it is often attributed to a "rapid push" or "cold fluid." The mechanism of harm is that the rigors reflect an acute bloodstream microbial challenge that can progress to septic shock within hours. Dismissing it as a benign reaction delays blood culture collection, delays line removal or lock therapy, and allows bacteremia to deepen. The harm is a preventable ICU admission or death. Treat new rigors with line access as a potential line infection: stop, notify the provider, draw cultures (including from the line), and prepare for line management per protocol.

**Leaving the line in because "the team might need it."** Central lines are often left in place beyond their indication out of hypothetical future need — "in case we need pressors again" or "in case the patient crashes." The mechanism of harm is that each additional day of dwell multiplies CLABSI risk, and the hypothetical need rarely materializes while the infection risk is certain and accumulating. The harm is a CLABSI that develops in a line that was no longer serving a purpose. Conduct the daily necessity review honestly: if the indication has resolved, advocate for removal and document the conversation. A new line can be placed if the clinical situation changes.

## Self-Check

- Did I perform hand hygiene and scrub the hub/port with appropriate antiseptic and full contact time before every access?
- Did I inspect the dressing this shift and replace it (not reinforce) if it was damp, loose, or soiled?
- Did I review whether the central line is still clinically necessary today and advocate for removal if not?
- Did I access the line only when necessary and de-access/cap promptly?
- Did I administer parenteral nutrition and other high-risk fluids through a dedicated, labeled lumen?
- Did I recognize and escalate fever without another source, exit-site drainage, tunnel tenderness, or rigors during access?
- Did I use maximal barrier precautions if I assisted with insertion?
- Did I follow the blood-draw discard and flush protocol precisely when central sampling was required?
- Did I label each lumen's designated purpose and avoid mixing incompatible infusions?
- Did I escalate line malfunction (resistance, inability to draw, patient-reported abnormal sensation) rather than forcing or ignoring it?
