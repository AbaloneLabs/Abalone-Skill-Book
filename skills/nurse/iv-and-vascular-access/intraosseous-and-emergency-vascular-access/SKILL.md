# Intraosseous and Emergency Vascular Access

Intraosseous (IO) access is the rescue bridge when peripheral veins collapse and central access is not immediately available. In cardiac arrest, profound shock, severe trauma, or status epilepticus, the patient's perfusion is failing and veins constrict, making PIVC insertion nearly impossible. IO access provides a route into the non-collapsible medullary cavity, where infusion rates can approach those of a large peripheral line. The nurse's role is to know when IO is indicated, assist or insert per scope, and — critically — manage the IO site correctly afterward, because the same properties that make IO fast also make it painful and capable of causing compartment syndrome, osteomyelitis, and fracture if mismanaged. IO is a temporary measure, not a substitute for definitive vascular access.

## Core Rules

### Recognize the Indication and Act Without Delay

IO is indicated when emergent vascular access is needed and peripheral access cannot be rapidly established — typically within 90 seconds to a few minutes of failed attempts in a critical patient. Indications include cardiac arrest, hemodynamic collapse, severe burns or trauma with difficult veins, status epilepticus needing IV medications, and pediatric arrest where time to access is decisive. Do not spend prolonged time on repeated PIVC attempts in an arrest; move to IO.

### Site Selection and Insertion

The preferred adult site is the proximal humerus (fast flow, less pain during infusion, preferred for pressors and rapid volume); alternatives are the proximal tibia and distal tibia. Pediatric sites depend on age and device. Avoid fractured bones, sites over infection, prosthetic joints, and previously attempted IO sites at the same bone. Confirm placement by: loss of resistance, stable needle position, ability to aspirate marrow/blood, and free flow of infusion without extravasation. Secure the needle per device instructions.

### Pain Management for IO Infusion

The medullary cavity is richly innervated. Needle insertion is tolerable in an unconscious patient, but infusion through a conscious patient's IO is sharply painful. After confirming placement in an alert patient, administer local anesthetic (e.g., preservative-free lidocaine) into the IO before infusion per protocol, and provide systemic analgesia or sedation as the patient's status allows. Do not omit this in a responsive patient.

### Flow Management: Syringe and Pressure

IO infusion meets resistance from the marrow; gravity flow is inadequate. Use a syringe bolus or a pressure bag/infusion pump to achieve therapeutic flow rates. Flush between medications just as with any IV. All medications and fluids that can be given IV can be given IO, at the same doses.

### Treat IO as a Bridge, Establish Definitive Access

IO is designed for short-term emergency use (typically hours, not days). As soon as the patient is stabilized and perfusion improves (making peripheral veins accessible), establish definitive IV access and remove the IO. Document the reason for IO, time of insertion, site, and time of removal.

### Monitor for Complications Continuously

Assess the IO site every shift and before each use: stable needle position, no swelling/induration behind or around the site (suggesting extravasation), intact distal pulses and perfusion, no signs of infection. Compartment syndrome is the most dangerous mechanical complication — extravasation into a tight fascial compartment can compromise distal blood flow and threaten the limb. Osteomyelitis is the most dangerous infectious complication and risk increases with dwell time.

### Know Your Scope and Device

IO insertion may be within nursing scope in many settings (especially emergency and critical care with specific training and protocol). Know your facility policy, the devices available, and your competency. If insertion is outside your scope, your role is to prepare the device, position the patient, and assist — and to manage the line afterward.

## Common Traps

**Repeated peripheral attempts before going to IO in an arrest.** The instinct to "get one more IV" is strong, especially for nurses skilled at PIVC insertion, but in cardiac arrest or profound shock, peripheral veins are collapsed and each failed attempt consumes the narrow window during which medications and fluids can actually reach the heart. The mechanism of harm is that every minute without vascular access in arrest is a minute without epinephrine, antiarrhythmics, or fluids, and neurological outcome deteriorates with each minute of untreated arrest. The trap is treating IO as a "last resort" when it should be the *next* resort after one or two quick PIVC attempts fail. The harm is a prolonged no-flow or low-flow state and a worse neurologic outcome. Move to IO early in the algorithm.

**Infusing into a conscious patient without analgesia.** The medullary cavity is exquisitely sensitive, and a rapid saline flush or medication push through an IO in an awake patient causes sudden, severe pain. The mechanism of harm is both acute suffering and, in responsive patients, a sympathetic surge that can worsen the underlying emergency (tachycardia, hypertension, agitation). Nurses who assume the patient is "too sick to feel it" or who prioritize speed over analgesia inflict significant pain and can provoke a destabilizing response. Administer IO lidocaine per protocol before infusion in any responsive patient and provide systemic analgesia as the situation allows.

**Assuming gravity flow is adequate.** Because an IO is "a line," nurses sometimes hang fluids and expect them to run by gravity as with a PIVC. The mechanism of harm is that the marrow cavity resists passive flow, so gravity-drip fluids barely move and the patient does not receive the volume or medication intended. The trap is a line that looks like it is working (fluid in the chamber) but delivers subtherapeutic flow while the patient remains hypovolemic or under-resuscitated. Use pressure infusion (pressure bag, pump) or syringe boluses to achieve therapeutic rates, and verify flow at the site.

**Missing extravasation and compartment syndrome.** The IO needle is anchored in bone, so it feels stable, and nurses may assume a stable needle means a working line. The mechanism of harm is that the needle can dislodge from the medullary space or fluid can leak around the cortex, especially with high-pressure infusion, and infusate accumulates in the soft tissue or fascial compartment. Because the limb is already compromised by the emergency, the swelling of compartment syndrome can be attributed to the underlying condition rather than the IO, delaying recognition. The harm is ischemic injury to muscle and nerve that can result in permanent disability or amputation. Assess for firmness, swelling, coolness, and diminished distal pulses at the IO site, and stop infusion immediately if extravasation is suspected.

**Leaving the IO in too long.** Once the patient is stable and definitive access is established, the IO should be removed, but it is sometimes left in "just in case" because it is already placed and functioning. The mechanism of harm is that IO dwell time is the primary driver of osteomyelitis risk, and a line left for convenience converts a short-term rescue device into a long-term infection source in bone — an infection that is painful, difficult to treat, and can seed the bloodstream. The harm is a bone infection that requires weeks of antibiotics and sometimes surgical debridement. Establish definitive access and remove the IO as soon as clinically appropriate; document the dwell time.

**Overlooking the edge case or exception.** The mechanism of harm is that the agent analyzes the typical scenario thoroughly but skips the unusual case. The false signal is that the common path is well-handled; in reality the exception silently produces the wrong outcome because the boundary was never tested. The harm is a failure that only appears in the case nobody checked. The fix is to always ask what the edge case is and whether the conclusion still holds there.

## Self-Check

- Did I move to IO after one or two failed rapid PIVC attempts in a critical patient, rather than persisting with peripheral attempts?
- Did I select an appropriate site (proximal humerus/tibia) avoiding fracture, infection, prosthetic joints, and prior IO attempts at the same bone?
- Did I confirm placement by stable needle position, aspiration, and free flow without extravasation?
- Did I administer IO lidocaine and systemic analgesia as indicated before infusion in a responsive patient?
- Did I use pressure infusion or syringe boluses to achieve therapeutic flow rather than relying on gravity?
- Did I flush between medications and use IV-equivalent doses for all IO medications?
- Did I assess the IO site every shift for stability, extravasation, distal perfusion, and infection?
- Did I establish definitive vascular access and remove the IO as soon as the patient stabilized?
- Did I document the indication, site, time of insertion, and time of removal?
- Did I operate within my scope, device competency, and facility protocol?
