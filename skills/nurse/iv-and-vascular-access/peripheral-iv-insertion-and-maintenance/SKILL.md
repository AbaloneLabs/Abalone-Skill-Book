# Peripheral IV Insertion and Maintenance

Peripheral intravenous catheter (PIVC) management is one of the most common invasive procedures nurses perform, and its apparent simplicity is exactly why complications proliferate. A PIVC breaches skin and vein wall, deposits a foreign body in a flowing vessel, and creates a sustained entry route for microbes, irritants, and fluid overload. Decisions about site, gauge, dwell time, dressing, and replacement are not mechanical — they carry infection, thrombosis, infiltration, and nerve-injury risk, and they directly affect whether therapy can be delivered at all. The nurse's job is to make the first attempt the right attempt, recognize failure early, and know when a peripheral line is no longer adequate for the clinical need.

## Core Rules

### Assess Before You Stick: Vessel, Therapy, and Patient Factors Together

Gauge and site selection must match the *therapy*, not just the easiest vein. Large-bore (14–16G) catheters in antecubital veins are needed for rapid volume resuscitation, massive transfusion, or contrast bolus. Smaller gauges (20–22G) are adequate for routine maintenance fluids and most medications; 24G is reserved for fragile veins, pediatrics, or when no better option exists. A mismatch — a 22G in a patient needing rapid transfusion, or a large-bore line in a tiny fragile vein — creates either failed therapy or vessel injury. Match catheter length to the intended dwell: short peripheral catheters are not meant for long-term access.

Site selection priorities, from most to least preferred: forearm veins (stability, comfort, lower complications), dorsal hand, antecubital fossa (only when necessary — flexion interferes with dwell and patient mobility). Avoid:
- the dominant arm only if an alternative exists (preserve patient function);
- areas of flexion, joint creases, and areas near arteries or nerves;
- limbs with mastectomy, dialysis fistula/graft, paralysis, or existing vascular compromise;
- infected, bruised, or previously infiltrated areas.

### Vein Preservation Is a Long Game

For patients likely to need repeated access — oncology, chronic illness, IV drug users, dialysis candidates — every stick damages future options. Use the most distal appropriate site and work proximally only when needed. Do not cannulate veins you expect to need for fistula creation. Communicate preservation plans across shifts.

### The First Attempt Should Be Deliberate

Before insertion, gather all supplies, position the patient, apply warm compresses for difficult veins, and use adequate lighting. Assess by palpation as well as sight — a palpable, resilient vein is better than a visible but fragile one. For difficult access, consider ultrasound-guided PIVC by a trained clinician rather than repeated blind attempts. Limit attempts: after two failed attempts by one nurse, escalate to a more experienced clinician or vascular access team. Repeated blind attempts cause bruising, patient distress, and loss of viable sites.

### Secure, Dress, and Document the Standard

Use a sterile transparent semipermeable dressing (or sterile gauze if the site is bleeding or diaphoretic). Secure the hub to prevent motion but do not occlude the ability to inspect the site. Label with date, time, gauge, site, and initials. Document the number of attempts.

### Maintenance: Assess Every Shift, Every Time You Use It

Assess the PIVC site at minimum every shift and before administering any vesicant, irritant, or bolus. Check for: pain, tenderness, swelling, redness, streaking, warmth, firmness, dressing integrity, and patency. Flush with preservative-free normal saline; do not force against resistance — resistance means obstruction, malposition, or infiltration and forcing can rupture the vessel or dislodge a clot.

### Know When to Replace and When to Remove

Follow your facility protocol on routine replacement (clinical indication-based replacement is increasingly favored over fixed dwell schedules, but know which policy governs your unit). Replace immediately for: phlebitis, infiltration, extravasation, occlusion, suspected infection, or dressing failure with contamination risk. Remove as soon as the PIVC is no longer clinically necessary — every day of unnecessary dwell is infection and thrombophlebitis risk.

### Vesicant and Irritant Medications Require Heightened Vigilance

Before administering a known vesicant (e.g., certain chemotherapy, concentrated potassium, vasopressors peripherally), confirm blood return, patency, and absence of pain or swelling. Administer slowly and monitor continuously. Peripheral administration of continuous vesicants or vasoactive infusions is high-risk; advocate for central access when therapy duration or vesicancy warrants it.

## Common Traps

**The "easy antecubital vein" trap.** The antecubital fossa offers large, visible veins that are tempting for first attempts, especially under time pressure. The trap is that these veins sit directly over a joint, so every elbow flexion tugs the catheter, irritates the intima, and accelerates phlebitis and infiltration. A line placed there for convenience will fail sooner, leak vesicant medications more readily, and tether the patient's arm during eating, hygiene, and ambulation. The harm is a shorter dwell, a wasted site, and often a patient who cannot receive the next dose because the only working line infiltrated. Prefer forearm veins even when they require slightly more skill; the antecubital should be a deliberate choice for short-term large-bore need, not a default for convenience.

**Assuming a "good flash" confirms correct placement.** A brisk blood return at insertion confirms the needle bevel entered the vessel lumen at that instant, but it says nothing about whether the catheter tip is correctly positioned after advancement, whether the vein is patent downstream, or whether the catheter will hold position with movement. Nurses who trust the flash and skip the post-insertion flush-with-observation, or who begin vesicant infusion without re-confirming patency and blood return, can infuse into a partially extravasated or malpositioned line. The harm ranges from painful infiltration to devastating tissue necrosis requiring surgical intervention, particularly with vesicants. Always verify patency with a smooth saline flush and observe the site before relying on the line.

**Forcing a flush that meets resistance.** When saline will not flow easily, the instinct is to push harder to "clear" the line. The mechanism of harm is that resistance means the catheter tip is against a valve, the vessel wall, a kink, or a clot — and increasing pressure can either rupture the vessel (causing infiltration), embolize a fragment, or drive fluid into tissue. The resistance itself is the diagnostic signal that something is wrong, and overriding it converts a manageable problem into tissue injury. Stop, reposition the arm, attempt gentle aspiration, and if patency cannot be restored, remove and replace rather than force.

**Treating routine replacement intervals as the only removal trigger.** Policies that call for replacement every 72 or 96 hours create a false sense that a line is safe until that clock expires. Phlebitis, infiltration, and catheter-related infection can develop within hours of insertion, and a line that is tender, red, or swollen on day one must come out regardless of the schedule. The harm of schedule-bound thinking is a nurse who leaves a clearly inflamed line in place "because it's not due yet," allowing phlebitis to progress to thrombophlebitis or a bloodstream infection. Clinical indication always overrides the calendar.

**Overlooking the dominant-arm or restricted-limb rule under pressure.** When access is difficult, a nurse may reach for the first usable vein without checking the chart for mastectomy, lymphedema risk, dialysis access, paralysis, or prior stroke. Cannulating a limb with compromised lymphatic drainage or vascular integrity risks worsening edema, infection, and in dialysis patients, jeopardizes future fistula viability. The harm is both immediate (poor healing, infiltration) and long-term (loss of a limb that the patient needs for future life-sustaining access). Always verify limb restrictions before site selection, even when the vein looks perfect.

**Overlooking the edge case or exception.** The mechanism of harm is that the agent analyzes the typical scenario thoroughly but skips the unusual case. The false signal is that the common path is well-handled; in reality the exception silently produces the wrong outcome because the boundary was never tested. The harm is a failure that only appears in the case nobody checked. The fix is to always ask what the edge case is and whether the conclusion still holds there.

## Self-Check

- Did I select gauge and site based on the prescribed therapy and anticipated duration, not just the easiest vein?
- Did I check for limb restrictions (mastectomy, fistula/graft, paralysis, vascular compromise) before choosing a site?
- Did I limit insertion attempts and escalate after two failed attempts?
- Did I verify blood return and a smooth, resistance-free flush before relying on the line?
- Did I label the site with date, time, gauge, site, and my initials?
- Did I assess the site this shift and before any vesicant or irritant administration?
- Did I remove or replace the line based on clinical indication (phlebitis, infiltration, occlusion, suspected infection) rather than waiting for the scheduled interval?
- Did I remove the PIVC as soon as it was no longer clinically necessary?
- Did I advocate for central access when therapy (continuous vesicant, prolonged vasoactive infusion) warranted it?
- For vesicant administration, did I confirm patency, absence of pain/swelling, and blood return before and during infusion?
