# Interdisciplinary Care Conference Facilitation

The interdisciplinary care conference is the setting where the patient's complex, contested, or high-stakes plan is supposed to come together — where medicine, nursing, social work, therapy, pharmacy, ethics, and the patient and family align on the goals and the plan. The judgment problem is that these conferences easily become either a series of discipline-specific reports that no one synthesizes, or a conflict between divergent goals that is not resolved but papered over, and the patient leaves the meeting with a "plan" that no one owns and that does not actually align the team. The recurring failures are holding the conference without the right people (or without the patient and family), letting the loudest voice or the most senior physician set the plan rather than the patient's goals, failing to name and resolve the conflict, and producing decisions that are not documented, not assigned, and not communicated — so the conference changes nothing.

This is a domain where the nurse's role is often to facilitate the synthesis, to ensure the patient and family voice is present and heard, to surface and work through the conflict rather than around it, and to convert the discussion into a documented, accountable, communicated plan.

## Core Rules

### Convene The Right People, Including The Patient And Family

A care conference that excludes a key discipline, the patient, or the family produces a plan that the absent party will not support or that does not reflect the patient's goals. Identify who must be present — the primary team and key specialists, nursing, social work, the relevant therapies, pharmacy, palliative or ethics where indicated, and the patient and their surrogate or family — and convene them. For the patient and family, prepare them in advance: explain the purpose, the attendees, the questions to be decided, and what to expect, so they can participate rather than be overwhelmed. A conference held without the people who will execute or be affected by the plan is a conference that produces a plan no one owns.

### Define The Purpose And The Questions To Be Decided Before The Meeting

A care conference without a defined purpose becomes a status update rather than a decision-making meeting, and the team disperses without resolving the issue that prompted it. Before the meeting, define the purpose and the specific questions to be decided (the goals of care, the disposition, the treatment escalation or limitation, the discharge barriers, the conflict to resolve), share them with the attendees, and structure the meeting around them. A clear agenda keeps the meeting focused on the decisions and prevents it from drifting into reports.

### Anchor The Discussion In The Patient's Goals And Values

The plan that emerges from a care conference should serve the patient's goals and values, not the convenience of the system or the preference of the most assertive participant. Elicit and center the patient's and family's understanding of the patient's condition, their goals, their priorities, and their concerns, and structure the discussion around how the clinical options serve those goals. When the team's recommendation diverges from the patient's goals, surface the divergence and work through it explicitly rather than overriding the patient's voice. The patient's goals are the anchor; the clinical expertise is in service of them.

### Synthesize The Disciplinary Input Into An Integrated Picture

Each discipline brings a perspective — medicine on diagnosis and treatment, nursing on function and response, social work on the social and financial context, therapy on functional potential, pharmacy on the medication regimen — and the conference's value is in synthesizing these into an integrated picture of the patient and the plan, not in hearing each in isolation. The facilitator actively synthesizes: drawing out the connections, identifying the conflicts between disciplinary recommendations, summarizing the integrated assessment, and checking that the team shares the understanding. A conference that hears each discipline report without synthesis leaves the team with fragments, not a plan.

### Surface And Work Through Conflict, Do Not Paper Over It

Care conferences are often convened because there is conflict — between the team and the family, between disciplines, between the patient's goals and the clinical reality — and the temptation is to avoid the conflict to keep the meeting comfortable, which produces a superficial agreement that does not hold. Name the conflict explicitly, invite each perspective, explore the values and concerns underlying each position, and work toward a resolution that the parties can accept, even if not everyone's first choice. Use ethics consultation, palliative care, or other resources when the conflict is deep or recurring. A conflict that is surfaced and worked through can be resolved; a conflict that is avoided festers and re-emerges at the next crisis.

### Convert The Discussion Into Specific, Accountable Decisions

A care conference that ends with a general sense of agreement but no specific decisions has produced a feeling, not a plan. Before the meeting ends, articulate the specific decisions made — the goals of care, the treatments to pursue or limit, the disposition, the services to arrange, the changes to the plan — and confirm each with the team and the family. Assign each action to a named owner with a timeframe, and define the reassessment and the next conference if needed. A decision that is not specific and not owned is a decision that does not happen.

### Document And Communicate The Decisions

Document the conference — the attendees, the purpose, the discussion, the decisions, the rationale, the assigned actions and owners, and the patient's and family's understanding and agreement — and communicate the plan to the team members who were not present, to the receiving settings, and to the patient and family in writing. A conference whose decisions are not documented and communicated is invisible to the team that must execute the plan, and the decisions are lost or reversed. The documentation is the handoff that carries the conference's work into the care.

### Follow Up And Reassess

A care plan decided in conference evolves as the patient's condition and circumstances change, and the decisions should be revisited at defined points or triggers. Schedule the reassessment or the next conference for complex or unstable plans, and revisit the goals and the plan when the patient's condition changes, when an intervention is not working, or when the family's perspective shifts. A plan decided once and never revisited becomes stale, and the patient's evolving needs go unaddressed.

## Common Traps

### Holding The Conference Without The Right People

A care conference is held without a key discipline, without the patient or family, or without the decision-maker, and the plan is made by a partial group that the absent party will not support. The mechanism of harm is that the absent party — whether a specialist whose input changes the plan, or the family who must consent and execute it — is not bound by a decision they did not participate in, so the plan is reversed, delayed, or ignored when they are informed. The harm is a plan that does not hold, a delayed decision, and a loss of trust. The defense is to identify and convene the right people before the meeting, including the patient and the decision-maker, and to reschedule rather than proceed with a partial group when a key party is missing.

### Letting The Loudest Or Most Senior Voice Set The Plan

In a care conference, the plan is set by the most assertive family member, the most senior physician, or the most vocal team member, rather than by the patient's goals and the integrated clinical judgment. The mechanism of harm is that decisions made by power rather than by patient-centered synthesis may violate the patient's autonomy, may not reflect the integrated clinical picture, and may be resented by the team members whose input was overridden. The harm is a plan that is ethically or clinically suboptimal and that does not have the team's commitment. The defense is to anchor the discussion in the patient's goals, to actively elicit each perspective including the quieter ones, to synthesize rather than defer, and to use ethics or palliative care when the power dynamic distorts the decision.

### Failing To Name And Resolve The Conflict

A care conference is convened because of a conflict — over goals of care, over disposition, over the treatment plan — and the conflict is avoided to keep the meeting comfortable, producing a vague agreement that does not resolve the underlying disagreement. The mechanism of harm is that the unaddressed conflict does not disappear; it re-emerges at the next crisis, the plan is not executed as agreed, and the family and team remain misaligned. The harm is a plan that does not hold and a conflict that festers. The defense is to name the conflict explicitly, to invite and explore each perspective, to work toward a resolution the parties can accept, and to bring in ethics or palliative care for deep or recurring conflict.

### Producing Decisions That Are Not Specific Or Accountable

The conference ends with a general sense of agreement but no specific decisions, and the team disperses without knowing who will do what by when. The mechanism of harm is that general agreement without specific, owned actions is a feeling, not a plan, so the decisions are not executed, the plan drifts, and the conference's work is lost. The harm is a plan that changes nothing. The defense is to articulate the specific decisions before the meeting ends, to assign each action to a named owner with a timeframe, and to define the reassessment.

### Not Documenting Or Communicating The Decisions

The conference's decisions are not documented, or the documentation is delayed, and the team members who were not present, the receiving settings, and the patient and family do not receive the plan. The mechanism of harm is that undocumented or uncommunicated decisions are invisible to those who must execute them, so the plan is not followed, the decisions are reversed, and the conference's work is wasted. The harm is care that does not reflect the decisions, and confusion among the team and family. The defense is to document the conference promptly and completely, and to communicate the plan to all who need it, including the patient and family in writing.

### Treating The Conference As A One-Time Event Rather Than A Process

The care plan is decided in one conference and never revisited, and as the patient's condition and the family's perspective evolve, the plan becomes stale. The mechanism of harm is that complex and unstable patients' situations change, and a plan decided once does not track the changes, so the goals and interventions become inappropriate. The harm is care that does not meet the patient's current needs. The defense is to schedule the reassessment or the next conference, to define the triggers for revisiting the plan, and to keep the plan current.
