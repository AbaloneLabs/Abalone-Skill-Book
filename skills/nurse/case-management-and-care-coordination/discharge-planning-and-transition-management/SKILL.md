# Discharge Planning And Transition Management

The discharge from hospital to the next setting is the most dangerous transition in care, because it moves the patient from a high-surveillance environment with on-site providers to one with far less support, at a moment when they are still recovering, on new medications, with new diagnoses, and often with reduced functional capacity. The judgment problem is that discharge planning treated as a paperwork step — filling out the discharge summary, printing prescriptions — leaves the patient without the services, equipment, understanding, or follow-up they need to survive and recover at home, and the result is readmission, a fall, a medication error, or a deterioration that presents late. The recurring failures are starting the discharge plan on the day of discharge rather than at admission, sending a patient home without the home services or equipment they need, failing to reconcile medications across the admission and communicate the changes, delivering teaching the patient cannot act on, and not scheduling or confirming the follow-up that catches the deteriorating patient early.

This is a domain where the nurse's role is to plan the transition from admission, to assess and arrange for the post-discharge needs, to reconcile and communicate the medications, to teach for understanding, and to ensure the follow-up is in place — because the discharge is only safe when the receiving setting can actually support the patient.

## Core Rules

### Start Discharge Planning At Admission, Not On The Day Of Discharge

Discharge planning that begins on the day of discharge is too late to arrange the services, equipment, and disposition that a complex patient needs, because home health, skilled nursing facility placement, durable medical equipment, and prior authorizations take days to arrange. Begin the discharge assessment at admission: identify the anticipated discharge destination, the home situation and caregiver support, the functional and cognitive status, the likely post-discharge needs (home health, rehab, equipment, wound care, infusion), and the barriers (transportation, finances, housing, caregiver capacity). Initiate the arrangements early, so that the discharge is not delayed by logistics and the patient is not sent home without what they need.

### Assess The Post-Discharge Needs Comprehensively

Assess what the patient will need after discharge across the domains that determine whether they can safely recover at home: functional status and the assistance needed for activities of daily living, the medical services required (home health for wound care, infusion, monitoring; skilled nursing for rehabilitation), the durable medical equipment (walker, hospital bed, oxygen, suction), the medications and the ability to obtain and manage them, the follow-up appointments and the ability to get to them, the caregiver capacity and the caregiver's own needs, and the social and financial barriers. A patient discharged without the walker they need, or the home wound care, or the caregiver who has left, is set up for a fall, a wound complication, or a readmission.

### Identify And Intervene For The High Readmission-Risk Patient

Use a validated readmission risk assessment (or clinical judgment where one is not in place) to identify the patients at highest risk of readmission: advanced age, multiple comorbidities, recent prior admission, heart failure, COPD, complex discharge (new diagnoses, multiple medication changes, infusion therapy, wound care), poor social support, low health literacy, limited finances, and substance use or behavioral health conditions. Intervene more intensively for these patients: a follow-up phone call within days, an early clinic appointment, enhanced teaching, a home health referral, a pharmacist medication review, and connection to community resources. The high-risk patient is the one for whom the standard discharge is insufficient, and the additional support is what prevents the readmission.

### Reconcile Medications Across The Admission And Communicate The Changes

Medication reconciliation at discharge is the step that prevents the most common and most dangerous discharge error: the patient who continues a home medication that was held in hospital, who stops a hospital medication that should continue, who duplicates a medication under a different name, or who cannot afford or obtain a new prescription. Reconcile the discharge list against the home list and the hospital list, explicitly identify what was started, stopped, and changed, and communicate the changes to the patient, the caregiver, the next provider, and the pharmacy. Provide the patient with a clear, updated medication list, and teach the changes specifically — not "continue your medications" but "stop the ibuprofen, start the furosemide, continue the metoprolol." A patient who does not understand the medication changes cannot execute them safely.

### Deliver Transition Teaching The Patient And Caregiver Can Act On

Discharge teaching is the patient's manual for recovery at home, and it must be delivered and confirmed, not handed over. Teach the expected course, the warning signs that require a call or a return (fever, increasing shortness of breath, bleeding, swelling, new pain, weight gain in heart failure, signs of infection), the medications and their changes, the activity and diet, the wound or site care, the equipment use, and the follow-up. Use teach-back — ask the patient or caregiver to explain the plan and the warning signs back — rather than assuming the handout was understood. Tailor the teaching to the patient's literacy, language, and circumstances, with an interpreter or simplified materials as needed. A patient who cannot articulate what to watch for cannot act on it, and the warning sign becomes a readmission.

### Confirm The Follow-Up Is Scheduled And Accessible

Confirm that the follow-up appointment with the primary provider and any specialists is scheduled, that the patient has the date and time and location, that they can get there (transportation, distance), and that the appointment is timely for the patient's risk (early, within days, for high-risk patients). A patient discharged without a follow-up appointment, or with one they cannot reach, is a patient whose deterioration will present to the emergency department instead of the clinic. Provide the appointment, the contact numbers, and the instructions for what to do if they cannot attend.

### Communicate The Transition To The Receiving Setting And Provider

The discharge is a handoff, and the receiving setting and provider must receive a clear, complete handoff: the summary of the admission, the diagnoses and the course, the procedures and their findings, the discharge medications and the changes, the pending results and follow-up items, the functional and social status, the services arranged, and the goals of care and the advance directives. A discharge to home health, to a skilled nursing facility, or to a primary provider without a complete handoff leaves the receiving team without the information to care for the patient, and the gaps produce errors and missed follow-up. Send the handoff, confirm it was received, and include the pending items that need follow-up.

## Common Traps

### Starting Discharge Planning On The Day Of Discharge

The discharge plan is begun on the day of discharge, and the home health, the equipment, the placement, or the prior authorization cannot be arranged in time, so the discharge is delayed or the patient is sent home without what they need. The mechanism of harm is that the logistics of a safe discharge take time, and beginning them late forces a choice between a delayed discharge and an unsafe one, and the unsafe option is often chosen under pressure. The harm is a patient sent home without services or equipment, leading to a fall, a complication, or a readmission. The defense is to begin the discharge assessment at admission, to identify the anticipated needs and barriers early, and to initiate the arrangements in time.

### Sending A Patient Home Without The Services Or Equipment They Need

A patient is discharged home without the home health, the wound care, the oxygen, the walker, or the caregiver support they need, because the arrangement was not made or was assumed. The mechanism of harm is that the patient goes home to an environment that cannot support their recovery, so the wound is not cared for, the oxygen is not there, the patient falls without the walker, and the deterioration or complication presents late. The harm is a preventable readmission, a fall, or a complication. The defense is to assess the post-discharge needs comprehensively, to arrange the services and equipment before discharge, and to refuse to discharge a patient to a setting that cannot support them.

### Failing To Reconcile And Communicate The Medication Changes

The discharge medications are printed without explicit reconciliation against the home list, and the patient continues a held medication, stops a new one, or duplicates under a different name. The mechanism of harm is that medication discrepancies at discharge are common and dangerous, and a reconciliation that is not explicit leaves the changes unclear to the patient, the pharmacy, and the next provider, so the patient takes the wrong regimen. The harm is a medication error — toxicity from a duplicated drug, a withdrawal from a stopped drug, a relapse from an omitted drug, a bleeding event from an anticoagulant error. The defense is to reconcile explicitly, to identify the started, stopped, and changed medications, to provide a clear updated list, and to teach the changes specifically with teach-back.

### Providing Teaching The Patient Cannot Act On

The discharge instructions are handed over as a paper sheet, in clinical jargon or a language the patient does not speak, and the patient signs without understanding, because teach-back was not used. The mechanism of harm is that teaching not understood is functionally no teaching, so the patient goes home without the knowledge to recognize or respond to problems, and the signed form provides false assurance. The harm is a delayed return for a treatable complication, a medication error, a missed follow-up. The defense is to use teach-back, plain language, materials in the patient's language, and an interpreter, and to confirm understanding before discharge.

### Not Scheduling Or Confirming The Follow-Up

A patient is discharged with an instruction to "follow up with your doctor" but no appointment is made, or the appointment is too far out for the patient's risk, or the patient cannot get there. The mechanism of harm is that the follow-up is the safety net that catches the deteriorating patient early, and without it, the deterioration presents to the emergency department as a readmission. The harm is a preventable readmission. The defense is to schedule the follow-up before discharge, to make it timely for the patient's risk, to confirm the patient can attend, and to provide the contact numbers.

### An Incomplete Handoff To The Receiving Setting

The discharge summary is incomplete or delayed, and the receiving provider, home health, or skilled nursing facility does not receive the diagnoses, the medication changes, the pending results, or the functional and social status. The mechanism of harm is that the receiving team cares for the patient without the information they need, so they miss the pending culture, continue the wrong medication, or fail to provide the ordered wound care. The harm is errors and missed follow-up in the receiving setting. The defense is to send a complete, timely handoff, to confirm it was received, and to include the pending items and the follow-up needs.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I begin the discharge assessment at admission, identifying the anticipated destination, needs, and barriers early?
- [ ] Did I assess the post-discharge needs comprehensively — functional, medical services, equipment, medications, follow-up, caregiver, and social?
- [ ] Did I identify the high readmission-risk patient and intervene more intensively (early follow-up, call, enhanced teaching, home health, pharmacist review)?
- [ ] Did I reconcile the medications across the admission, identify the started, stopped, and changed drugs, and teach the changes specifically with teach-back?
- [ ] Did I deliver transition teaching the patient and caregiver can act on, including the warning signs, using teach-back and tailored to literacy and language?
- [ ] Did I confirm the follow-up is scheduled, timely for the patient's risk, and accessible, with contact numbers?
- [ ] Did I send a complete, timely handoff to the receiving setting and provider, including pending results and follow-up items?
- [ ] Did I document the discharge plan, the services and equipment arranged, the medication reconciliation, the teaching and teach-back, and the follow-up?
