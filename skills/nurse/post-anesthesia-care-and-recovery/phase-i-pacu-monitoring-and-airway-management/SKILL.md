# Phase I PACU Monitoring And Airway Management

Phase I post-anesthesia care is the highest-acuity recovery environment outside the operating room and intensive care unit. In the first minutes after admission, the patient is pharmacologically depressed, cannot reliably protect their own airway, may still be paralyzed or sedated, and is at peak risk for the three immediately lethal complications of emergence: airway obstruction, hypoventilation, and hemodynamic collapse. The judgment problem in Phase I monitoring is that the patient looks deceptively stable — the oxygen saturation reads well, the blood pressure is acceptable — while residual paralysis, opioid-induced hypoventilation, bleeding, or evolving airway edema silently progress. The recurring failures are accepting the handoff report as complete without an independent airway and breathing assessment, trusting the pulse oximeter while ignoring the respiratory rate and tidal volume, treating residual neuromuscular blockade as lingering sedation, and failing to recognize that the patient who arrives "stable" can obstruct within minutes as the anesthetic redistributes.

This is a high-stakes domain. The nurse's role is structured, frequent reassessment; airway vigilance as the first and continuous priority; recognition of the difference between residual drug effect and a surgical complication; and decisive escalation to anesthesia when the patient is not emerging or protecting their airway as expected.

## Core Rules

### Perform A Structured Admission Assessment Before Accepting The Handoff

The moment the patient arrives, the receiving nurse performs an independent, rapid assessment in parallel with the anesthesia handoff, rather than transcribing the report first. The assessment follows a fixed sequence: airway patency and airway adjuncts, spontaneous respiratory rate and tidal volume, oxygen saturation and delivery device, heart rate and rhythm, blood pressure, temperature, level of consciousness, pain, surgical site and dressings, drains, IV access and infusions, and estimated blood and fluid loss. Confirm the airway is open and the patient is ventilating before attending to the monitor numbers, because a patent airway and adequate ventilation are prerequisites for every other value to be meaningful.

### Make Airway And Breathing The First And Continuous Priority

Residual general anesthesia, opioids, and residual neuromuscular blockade depress ventilatory drive and airway tone. Position the patient to maintain airway patency — the recovery position for the patient without a protected airway, head tilt or jaw thrust if obstructed, and a nasopharyngeal or oropharyngeal airway when indicated. Monitor respiratory rate and observed tidal volume, not the oxygen saturation alone, because supplemental oxygen delays the desaturation that would otherwise reveal hypoventilation, and a patient can be severely hypercapnic while the saturation reads ninety-eight percent. Snoring, stridor, paradoxical or see-saw breathing, retractions, tracheal tug, and a falling respiratory rate are urgent signs of obstruction or hypoventilation and require immediate intervention — jaw thrust, airway adjunct, stimulation, reversal of opioids or neuromuscular blockade, and anesthesia call before the situation deteriorates to bradycardia and arrest.

### Differentiate Residual Neuromuscular Blockade From Lingering Sedation

A patient who is not moving, has weak or absent grip, cannot lift the head for five seconds, has shallow breathing or diminished cough, and appears poorly perfused may have residual neuromuscular blockade rather than ongoing sedation, and the two are managed very differently. Residual paralysis is reversed with pharmacologic reversal per anesthesia direction and confirmed by objective monitoring where available, while treating a paralyzed patient as merely sleepy and waiting for emergence leaves them at risk of airway obstruction, aspiration, and the profound distress of being aware but unable to move. Assess neuromuscular recovery with head lift, grip strength, cough, and tidal volume; use a peripheral nerve stimulator where available; and escalate to anesthesia for any patient not meeting recovery of motor strength, rather than attributing immobility to sleep.

### Recognize The Specific Emergence Airway Threats

Certain airway threats are specific to the surgical and anesthetic context and recognizing the pattern accelerates the response. After thyroid, anterior cervical spine, carotid, or head and neck surgery, evolving airway edema or hematoma can produce stridor, audible airway noise, and progressive obstruction that can become complete within minutes; do not wait, call anesthesia and the surgeon immediately and keep the head of the bed elevated. After oral, airway, or gastrointestinal surgery, blood or secretions in the oropharynx threaten obstruction and aspiration; suction promptly and position to drain. After neuraxial anesthesia (spinal or epidural), monitor the sensory level ascending, as a high or rising block can impair respiratory muscles and produce hypotension from sympathetic blockade; treat high-block hypotension with fluids, positioning, and vasopressors per protocol, and escalate a block rising above T4 or any respiratory compromise.

### Monitor Hemodynamics For The Causes Of Instability In Context

Hemodynamic instability in the first minutes of Phase I has a focused differential tied to the anesthetic and surgical context: residual anesthetic vasodilation and myocardial depression, bleeding from the surgical site, third-spacing, pre-existing hypovolemia, pain, and the sympathetic blockade of neuraxial anesthesia. Treat hypotension with the right intervention for the cause — fluids or vasopressors for vasodilation, blood for bleeding, analgesia for pain-driven hypertension and tachycardia — and investigate rather than reflexively giving fluid, because the hypotensive patient who is bleeding needs surgical intervention, not crystalloid that dilutes the hematocrit and worsens coagulopathy. Compare every reading to the patient's baseline and trend the trajectory; a blood pressure that is acceptable in isolation but falling from baseline with rising heart rate and cool extremities is early shock, not stability.

### Reassess At The Defined Frequency And Escalate Non-Emergence

Phase I assessment is performed at a defined frequency, commonly every fifteen minutes in the early phase and extending as the patient stabilizes per the unit protocol and the anesthesia orders. Reassess airway, breathing, circulation, consciousness, pain, and the surgical site at every interval, and trend the trajectory across assessments. A patient who is not emerging as expected — who remains deeply sedated, who is not maintaining adequate ventilation, whose hemodynamics are not stabilizing, or who is not meeting the expected recovery trajectory — warrants anesthesia notification, not extended waiting, because delayed emergence has reversible causes (residual anesthetic, opioid, hypothermia, hypoglycemia, hypercapnia, metabolic derangement, stroke) that require evaluation rather than passive observation.

### Use A Validated Recovery Score And Document Before Phase Transfer

Use a validated recovery scoring system (commonly the Aldrete score) to objectify readiness for progression from Phase I to Phase II or the floor, and meet the score criteria before transfer. Do not transfer a patient who has not met recovery criteria — even under bed pressure — because the criteria exist to catch patients who are not yet safe to be in a lower-surveillance environment, and transferring an incompletely recovered patient moves them to a setting where airway obstruction or hypoventilation will not be caught in time. Document the score, the criteria met, the airway and ventilation status, pain and nausea control, and the anesthesia orders at the time of transfer.

## Common Traps

### Trusting The Pulse Oximeter While Ignoring Respiratory Rate And Tidal Volume

A postoperative patient on supplemental oxygen has a saturation of ninety-eight percent, and the nurse is reassured, while the respiratory rate has drifted to eight, the tidal volume is shallow, and the patient is becoming hypercapnic. The mechanism of harm is that supplemental oxygen fills the functional residual capacity and delays the desaturation that would otherwise reveal hypoventilation, so the pulse oximeter reads as reassuring long after effective ventilation has been lost, and the nurse who monitors the saturation rather than the respiratory rate is monitoring the lagging indicator. The harm is undetected opioid-induced or residual-anesthetic respiratory depression progressing to hypercapnic coma, bradycardia, and arrest in a patient whose saturation looked fine minutes earlier. The defense is to monitor respiratory rate, observed tidal volume, and sedation level as the primary indicators of ventilation, and to treat supplemental-oxygen saturation as supportive rather than sufficient evidence of adequate breathing.

### Treating Residual Paralysis As Lingering Sleep

A patient who is not moving and breathing shallowly is described as sleepy and left to emerge, when the actual problem is residual neuromuscular blockade from an incompletely reversed non-depolarizing agent. The mechanism of harm is that immobility and hypoventilation are attributed to sedation — a plausible and reassuring explanation — so the specific intervention (pharmacologic reversal confirmed by objective monitoring) is not pursued, and the patient remains at risk of airway obstruction, aspiration, and the experience of awareness-with-paralysis. The harm is prolonged unsafe airway status and, in the worst case, respiratory arrest in a patient being watched rather than treated. The defense is to assess neuromuscular recovery objectively (head lift for five seconds, grip, cough, tidal volume, peripheral nerve stimulator where available), to distinguish residual blockade from sedation, and to escalate to anesthesia for any patient not meeting motor recovery criteria rather than waiting for emergence.

### Accepting The Anesthesia Handoff As Complete Without Independent Airway Assessment

The receiving nurse transcribes the anesthesia report — procedure, drugs, fluids, estimated blood loss — and accepts the patient, then discovers minutes later that the airway is obstructed or the patient is hypoventilating. The mechanism of harm is that the handoff is treated as a documentation transfer rather than a clinical handoff, so the nurse's own independent assessment of airway patency and ventilation is deferred until after the report, and the patient is unattended at the highest-risk moment. The harm is delayed recognition of airway obstruction or hypoventilation during the window when the nurse was focused on the paperwork rather than the patient. The defense is to perform the independent airway and breathing assessment in parallel with the handoff, confirming a patent airway and adequate ventilation before attending to the monitor or the documentation.

### Waiting To Call Anesthesia For Delayed Or Incomplete Emergence

A patient remains sedated or poorly ventilating longer than expected, and the nurse continues to wait, believing the patient will emerge with time, rather than notifying anesthesia, because calling feels like overreacting. The mechanism of harm is that delayed emergence has reversible causes — residual anesthetic or opioid, hypothermia, hypoglycemia, hypercapnia, electrolyte derangement, or stroke — and passive waiting both prolongs the unsafe airway status and delays the evaluation that would identify and reverse the cause. The harm is prolonged airway and ventilation risk and, in the case of a metabolic or neurologic cause, progression of a treatable condition. The defense is to treat non-emergence as a finding that warrants anesthesia evaluation, to check temperature, glucose, and capnography where available, and to escalate rather than to wait beyond the expected recovery trajectory.

### Missing Evolving Airway Edema Or Hematoma After Neck Surgery

After thyroid, carotid, anterior cervical spine, or head and neck surgery, a patient develops mild stridor or a change in voice, and the nurse attributes it to irritation from the endotracheal tube rather than to evolving airway edema or hematoma. The mechanism of harm is that a plausible benign explanation (intubation irritation) is available and reassuring, so the early sign of a surgical airway emergency is not escalated, and the edema or hematoma progresses to complete obstruction that is far harder to manage. The harm is airway compromise that can become complete and lethal within minutes, in a patient whose early sign was attributed to a benign cause. The defense is to treat new stridor, audible airway noise, change in voice, neck swelling, or progressive work of breathing after neck surgery as a surgical emergency, to keep the head of the bed elevated, and to call anesthesia and the surgeon immediately rather than waiting to see if it resolves.

### Transferring To Phase II Before Recovery Criteria Are Met

Under pressure to free the PACU bed for an incoming case, a patient is transferred to Phase II or the floor before meeting Aldrete criteria, with pain not controlled or ventilation marginal. The mechanism of harm is that the recovery criteria exist precisely to catch patients who are not yet safe for a lower-surveillance environment, and bypassing them moves a patient who is still at risk of airway obstruction or hypoventilation to a setting where deterioration will not be recognized in time. The harm is respiratory depression, uncontrolled bleeding, or readmission to a higher level of care. The defense is to apply the recovery score rigorously, to document the criteria met before transfer, and to escalate bed pressure to the charge nurse or anesthesia rather than compromising the patient's safety.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I perform an independent airway and breathing assessment in parallel with the anesthesia handoff, before attending to the monitor or documentation?
- [ ] Did I monitor respiratory rate, observed tidal volume, and sedation level as primary indicators, rather than relying on the pulse oximeter alone?
- [ ] Did I assess neuromuscular recovery objectively (head lift, grip, cough, tidal volume, nerve stimulator) and distinguish residual blockade from lingering sedation?
- [ ] Did I recognize the specific emergence airway threats for the surgical context (airway edema or hematoma after neck surgery, blood or secretions after oral or GI surgery, high neuraxial block)?
- [ ] Did I investigate the cause of hemodynamic instability in context rather than reflexively giving fluid, and compare readings to baseline and trend the trajectory?
- [ ] Did I reassess at the defined Phase I frequency and escalate delayed or incomplete emergence to anesthesia rather than waiting?
- [ ] Did I apply a validated recovery score (Aldrete) and meet criteria before transfer to Phase II or the floor, resisting bed-pressure?
- [ ] Did I document the airway and ventilation status, recovery score, criteria met, pain and nausea control, and anesthesia orders at the time of transfer?
