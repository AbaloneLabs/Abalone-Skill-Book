# Discharge Readiness Assessment And Criteria

Discharge from the post-anesthesia or post-procedure setting is a transition from a high-surveillance environment to one with far less monitoring, and the decision to discharge is the decision that the patient can now be safe with less watching. The judgment problem is that discharge readiness is not the absence of obvious instability — it is the positive demonstration that the patient meets objective criteria for airway, breathing, circulation, consciousness, pain and nausea control, surgical-site stability, and safe home arrangements, and that no finding suggests a complication that will progress after the patient leaves the monitored setting. The recurring failures are discharging on time-elapsed or bed-pressure rather than on criteria, accepting that the patient "looks okay" without applying the scoring system, sending a patient home without verifying a responsible adult or adequate home-care arrangements, and discharging a patient whose pain or nausea is not controlled because the bed is needed. Each of these moves a patient at residual risk into an environment where deterioration will not be recognized in time.

This is a high-stakes transition. The nurse's role is to apply the discharge criteria rigorously, to verify the home-care and escort arrangements, to teach the patient and caregiver what to watch for and when to return, and to delay discharge — and escalate the pressure — when the patient is not yet safe.

## Core Rules

### Apply A Validated Discharge Scoring System And Meet Criteria Before Discharge

Use a validated scoring system appropriate to the setting — the Aldrete score for progression from Phase I PACU to Phase II or the floor, and a same-day surgery discharge score (such as PADSS or the Postanesthetic Discharge Scoring System) for discharge home — and meet the criteria before discharge. The criteria cover the domains that determine safe transition: stable vital signs within an acceptable range of baseline, intact protective reflexes and adequate ventilation, controlled pain and nausea, ability to ambulate or void as required, minimal surgical-site drainage or bleeding, and normothermia. Apply every criterion; a patient who meets most but not all is not ready, and the unmet criterion is often the one that would have caught a developing problem.

### Do Not Discharge On Time Elapsed Or Bed Pressure

The patient is ready to discharge when the criteria are met, not when a fixed time has elapsed or when the bed is needed. Time-elapsed discharge assumes that recovery is uniform, but it is not — patients vary in their response to anesthesia, opioid sensitivity, bleeding risk, and comorbidities, and a patient who needs more time is not served by a clock. Bed pressure is a real operational constraint, but it is never a clinical criterion, and discharging a patient who has not met criteria to free a bed moves the risk to the patient. When bed pressure conflicts with readiness, escalate to the charge nurse, anesthesia, or the surgeon — the pressure is to be resolved at the operational level, not by compromising the patient's safety.

### Verify The Safe Home-Care And Escort Arrangements Before Same-Day Discharge

Same-day discharge requires that the patient go home with a responsible adult who will stay with them, that someone is available to call for help or to bring the patient back, and that the home environment can support the recovery (stairs, bathroom access, refrigeration for medications, a phone). Verify the escort by name and contact, confirm they will stay the night, and do not discharge a patient who plans to drive themselves, take a taxi alone, or go home to an empty house. The residual effects of anesthesia and opioids impair judgment, reaction time, and balance for many hours, and the patient who goes home alone cannot reliably recognize or respond to their own deterioration. The arrangement is part of the discharge criteria, not a formality.

### Confirm Pain And Nausea Are Controlled To A Discharge-Acceptable Level

Discharge requires that pain and nausea are controlled to a level the patient can manage at home with oral medication, not merely that they are improved. A patient whose pain is eight out of ten on oral analgesia, or who is still vomiting, is not safe for discharge — they will not be able to ambulate, hydrate, or recover, and they will likely return. Achieve control in the monitored setting with the multimodal regimen that will be used at home, confirm the patient tolerates oral intake as required, and provide the prescriptions and the teaching before discharge. Discharging uncontrolled pain or nausea to meet a schedule sets up a readmission.

### Inspect The Surgical Site And Confirm Hemodynamic And Output Stability

Before discharge, inspect the surgical site and dressings for drainage amount and type, confirm drain output is within expected range and not bloody or increasing, and confirm the vitals are stable and the urine output is adequate. The discharge assessment is a clinical assessment, not a paperwork step, and the surgical site and hemodynamics are where late bleeding and complications often first show. A patient whose drain output has increased, whose dressing is dampening, or whose heart rate is creeping up is not ready, even if the score adds up, and warrants surgeon notification rather than discharge.

### Provide Discharge Teaching That The Patient And Caregiver Can Act On

Discharge teaching is not reading a list; it is confirming that the patient and caregiver understand what to expect, what to watch for, what to do, and when to return. Teach the warning signs that require a call or a return (bleeding that soaks a dressing, fever, increasing pain or swelling, shortness of breath, inability to void, new numbness or weakness), the medications and their schedule and side effects, the activity and driving restrictions, the wound care, and the follow-up appointment. Use teach-back — ask the patient or caregiver to explain the plan back — rather than assuming that handing over the sheet means the teaching was received. A patient who cannot articulate what to watch for cannot act on it.

### Document The Criteria, The Teaching, And The Arrangements

Document the discharge score and the criteria met, the vital signs and surgical-site status at discharge, the pain and nausea control, the escort and home arrangements verified, the teaching provided and the teach-back confirmation, and the prescriptions and follow-up. This communicates the discharge status to the next setting, supports continuity, and creates the record that the discharge was appropriate. Undocumented discharge criteria and teaching are invisible and vulnerable to challenge, and the documentation is often the most important information in a later review of an adverse event.

## Common Traps

### Discharging On Time Elapsed Or Bed Pressure Rather Than Criteria

A patient is discharged after a fixed recovery time or because the bed is needed for an incoming case, before the discharge criteria are fully met. The mechanism of harm is that time-elapsed and bed-pressure discharge substitute an operational trigger for a clinical one, so a patient who has not demonstrated readiness is moved to a lower-surveillance environment where the residual risk (airway obstruction, hypoventilation, bleeding, uncontrolled pain) will not be recognized in time. The harm is respiratory depression, uncontrolled bleeding, readmission, or an unsafe discharge home. The defense is to discharge on criteria, to apply the scoring system, and to escalate bed pressure to the operational level rather than compromising the patient.

### Accepting That The Patient Looks Okay Without Applying The Scoring System

A patient who looks comfortable is discharged on clinical impression, without applying the discharge score, and a criterion that was not met (marginal ventilation, uncontrolled nausea, dampening dressing) is missed. The mechanism of harm is that clinical impression is reassuring but incomplete — it weights what is visible and under-weights what requires systematic assessment — so the structured criteria that would have caught the gap are bypassed. The harm is discharge of a patient who is not yet safe, with the missed criterion becoming the problem after discharge. The defense is to apply the scoring system every time, to assess every domain, and to treat the impression as a prompt to the assessment rather than a substitute for it.

### Sending A Patient Home Without Verifying A Responsible Escort

A same-day surgery patient is discharged to a taxi or to an empty home, without a responsible adult verified to stay with them, because the arrangement was treated as a formality. The mechanism of harm is that the residual effects of anesthesia and opioids impair judgment, reaction time, and balance for many hours, and a patient alone cannot reliably recognize or respond to their own deterioration, cannot call for help if they cannot reach a phone, and is at risk of falls and injury. The harm is an unattended deterioration, a fall, or an unsafe return to activity (such as driving). The defense is to verify the escort by name and contact, to confirm they will stay the night, and to refuse discharge to a patient without a responsible adult, arranging a longer stay or a social-work consult if needed.

### Discharging Uncontrolled Pain Or Nausea Because The Bed Is Needed

A patient whose pain is still severe or who is still vomiting is discharged to free the bed, with oral analgesia and antiemetics that have not been shown to control the symptoms. The mechanism of harm is that uncontrolled pain and nausea prevent ambulation, hydration, and recovery, and the patient who cannot tolerate oral intake or move will likely deteriorate at home and return. The harm is a preventable readmission and a patient who suffers unnecessarily. The defense is to achieve control in the monitored setting with the home regimen before discharge, to confirm oral tolerance as required, and to delay discharge until pain and nausea are manageable.

### Providing Written Instructions Without Confirming Understanding

A patient is handed discharge instructions and signs the form, but cannot articulate the warning signs or the medication schedule, because teach-back was not used. The mechanism of harm is that handing over a sheet does not equal teaching, and a patient who cannot explain the plan cannot act on it — they will not recognize the warning sign, will not take the medication correctly, and will not know when to return. The harm is a delayed return for a treatable complication, a medication error, or a missed follow-up. The defense is to use teach-back, to ask the patient or caregiver to explain the plan, and to re-teach and document until understanding is confirmed.

### Missing A Late Surgical-Site Finding Because The Site Was Not Inspected

A patient is discharged without the surgical site or drain output being inspected at the time of discharge, and an increasing drain output or dampening dressing is missed. The mechanism of harm is that the discharge assessment is treated as paperwork rather than a clinical assessment, so the site and output that would have revealed late bleeding are not checked, and the patient is sent home with an evolving complication. The harm is delayed recognition of bleeding or a surgical complication after discharge. The defense is to inspect the site and dressings, confirm drain output and vitals, and notify the surgeon for any concerning finding before discharge.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I apply a validated discharge scoring system (Aldrete, PADSS) and meet every criterion before discharge?
- [ ] Did I discharge on criteria rather than on time elapsed or bed pressure, escalating operational pressure to the charge nurse or anesthesia?
- [ ] For same-day discharge, did I verify a responsible adult escort by name and contact who will stay the night?
- [ ] Did I confirm pain and nausea are controlled to a discharge-acceptable level on the home oral regimen, with oral tolerance confirmed?
- [ ] Did I inspect the surgical site and dressings, confirm drain output and urine output, and check the vitals at the time of discharge?
- [ ] Did I provide discharge teaching and use teach-back to confirm the patient or caregiver can articulate the warning signs, medications, restrictions, and when to return?
- [ ] Did I document the discharge score, criteria met, vitals and site status, escort and arrangements, teaching and teach-back, prescriptions, and follow-up?
- [ ] Did I delay discharge and escalate when the patient did not meet criteria, rather than compromising safety?
