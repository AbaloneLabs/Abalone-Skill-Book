# Microbiology and Culture Specimen Collection

Culture results drive antibiotic selection, isolation decisions, and sometimes the diagnosis itself, so the quality of the specimen collected by the nurse determines the quality of every downstream decision. The judgment problem is that a contaminated or poorly collected specimen produces misleading results — a false-positive urine culture treated with unnecessary antibiotics, a mixed respiratory culture that obscures the true pathogen, a superficial wound swab that grows colonizers rather than the cause of infection — and the team acts on these results as if they were true. The nurse who collects cultures with the right technique, at the right time, from the right site, and with clinical judgment about whether to culture at all is the primary determinant of microbiology quality and antimicrobial stewardship.

## Core Rules

### Obtain Cultures Before Starting Antibiotics When Possible

The timing of culture collection relative to antibiotic administration dramatically affects the yield. Whenever clinically feasible, draw blood cultures and collect the relevant site cultures before the first antibiotic dose, because antibiotics can sterilize cultures within hours and leave the team treating a serious infection blind. In sepsis, the guideline is to draw cultures and then give antibiotics within one hour; do not delay antibiotics for hours to obtain cultures, but do obtain them first if it can be done rapidly. If the patient is already on antibiotics, note this on the specimen, because some culture methods include antibiotic-neutralizing media, and the lab needs to interpret the result in context. Communicate with the provider about the timing, and document when cultures were drawn relative to the antibiotic dose.

### Collect from the Correct Anatomic Site with the Correct Technique

Each culture type has a specific correct site and technique, and shortcuts produce misleading results. Blood cultures require aseptic preparation of the site and the bottles, the appropriate volume (more volume increases yield, especially in adults), and typically two sets from two separate sites. Sputum cultures should be a deep-cough specimen, not saliva; induced sputum or suctioning may be needed for patients who cannot cough. Lower respiratory cultures (bronchoalveolar lavage) require procedural collection. Wound cultures should be from clean, debrided tissue or deep tissue or fluid, not a superficial swab of necrotic material or colonized exudate; the superficial swab grows colonizers and misleads. Urine cultures should be from a clean-catch midstream specimen, a freshly inserted catheter, or a straight catheter — never from a urinary bag, and a culture from a catheter that has been in place for days reflects the catheter biofilm as much as the urine. Stool cultures should be from a fresh sample, not contaminated with urine. Know the correct technique for each specimen type, and use it every time.

### Avoid Contamination with Strict Aseptic Technique and Site Preparation

Contamination introduces organisms that are not part of the true infection, producing mixed cultures that obscure the pathogen and false-positive results that drive unnecessary treatment. For blood cultures, prepare the site with chlorhexidine or iodine and allow it to dry completely, prepare the bottle septum, do not re-palpate the prepared site, and use sterile gloves. For other sterile site cultures (cerebrospinal, joint, pleural), the provider uses sterile technique and the nurse assists with sterile field maintenance. For urine cultures, clean the perineum appropriately and collect the midstream specimen, or use a freshly inserted catheter. For wound cultures, debride and clean the wound before sampling to remove colonizers. The goal is to collect the organism causing the infection, not the organisms on the skin or in the environment.

### Apply Clinical Judgment to Whether Culturing Is Indicated at All

Not every fever, every cloudy urine, and every wound needs a culture, and over-culturing drives antibiotic overuse through the treatment of colonization rather than infection. Asymptomatic bacteriuria in a catheterized patient should not be cultured or treated; a chronic wound without signs of infection does not need a culture; a viral upper respiratory infection does not need a sputum culture. Use the clinical picture (signs of infection — fever, leukocytosis, localizing signs, systemic instability) to decide whether culturing is indicated, and advocate for stewardship by questioning reflex culturing. When a culture is indicated, collect it well; when it is not, advocate for restraint. The nurse is often the first to ask whether the culture is truly needed.

### Document the Source, the Time, and the Clinical Context

The laboratory interprets the culture in the context of the source and the clinical information, so the specimen must be accompanied by accurate documentation. Note the exact anatomic source (right foot wound, midstream urine, sputum from suctioning), the collection time, the patient's symptoms and antibiotic status, and any relevant clinical context (postoperative, immunocompromised, indwelling device). A specimen labeled simply "wound" or "urine" is less useful than one labeled with the specific site and the clinical question. The documentation supports the microbiology interpretation and the stewardship decisions.

### Coordinate Preliminary Results, Source Control, and Antibiotic Adjustment

Microbiology results come in stages — gram stain within hours, preliminary growth and identification in one to two days, sensitivities in two to three days — and each stage drives decisions. Communicate preliminary results to the provider promptly, because the gram stain and the early growth pattern may narrow the antibiotic choice and the isolation status. Advocate for source control (drainage of an abscess, removal of an infected line, debridement of necrotic tissue), because antibiotics without source control fail. When final sensitivities result, ensure the antibiotic is adjusted (de-escalated to the narrowest effective agent, or escalated if resistant) per stewardship principles. The nurse is the coordinator who ensures the culture result translates into the right action.

### Recognize the Limitations of Culture and Use Adjunctive Tests

Cultures have limitations: they take days, they require viable organisms, they are affected by prior antibiotics, and some organisms are difficult to grow. Use adjunctive tests appropriately — molecular tests (PCR) for rapid detection of specific pathogens, antigen tests, serology, and inflammatory markers (procalcitonin, C-reactive protein) — and understand what each can and cannot tell. A negative culture does not exclude infection if the clinical picture is consistent, especially in a patient on antibiotics. Integrate the microbiology results with the clinical assessment rather than treating the culture as the sole arbiter.

## Common Traps

### Culturing a Chronic Wound Superficially and Treating the Colonizers

The trap is that the nurse swabs the surface of a chronic wound with exudate or necrotic tissue, sends the swab, and the culture grows a polymicrobial mix of skin and environmental organisms, which the team then treats with broad antibiotics. The mechanism of harm is that chronic wounds are colonized by a range of organisms that are not causing infection, and a superficial swab grows the colonizers rather than the true pathogen (if infection exists), leading to unnecessary antibiotics, resistance selection, and missed diagnosis of the actual problem (poor perfusion, unrelieved pressure, non-viable tissue). The harm is antibiotic overuse and failure to address the wound's real needs. Culture only wounds with signs of infection (erythema, warmth, increased pain, purulent drainage, systemic signs), and culture clean, debrided deep tissue or fluid, not superficial exudate.

### Culturing Urine from a Urinary Bag or an Old Catheter

The trap is that the nurse obtains a urine culture from the drainage bag or from a catheter port of a catheter that has been in place for days, because it is faster than a fresh specimen. The mechanism of harm is that the drainage bag and the catheter biofilm are colonized with bacteria that do not reflect bladder urine, producing a false-positive culture that drives unnecessary antibiotic treatment of asymptomatic bacteriuria or colonization, with resistance selection and Clostridioides difficile risk. The harm is antibiotic overuse and the masking of the true source of fever (which may be elsewhere). Culture urine from a fresh midstream clean-catch, a freshly inserted catheter, or a straight catheter specimen, never from the bag, and aspirate from the sampling port (after cleaning it) of a catheter that must remain in place.

### Drawing Only One Blood Culture Set, Reducing Yield and Complicating Interpretation

The trap is that the nurse draws a single blood culture set (one bottle or one bottle pair from one site) to save time or sticks, rather than the recommended two sets from two sites. The mechanism of harm is that a single set has lower yield (missing some bacteremias) and makes interpretation of skin flora (coagulase-negative staphylococci) impossible — a single positive bottle of skin flora could be contamination or true bacteremia, and without a second set to compare, the team cannot tell, leading to either unnecessary treatment or missed diagnosis. The harm is undertreated or overtreated bacteremia. Draw the recommended number of sets (commonly two sets, two bottles each, from two separate sites) at the indicated intervals, and document the sites and times.

### Sending Saliva Instead of a Deep Sputum Specimen

The trap is that the patient is asked to cough into a cup, and the specimen is mostly oral secretions rather than lower respiratory material, but it is sent for culture anyway. The mechanism of harm is that the culture grows oral flora (which is not the cause of pneumonia), and the team either treats the oral organisms unnecessarily or dismisses the culture as unhelpful, missing the opportunity to identify the true pathogen. The harm is misleading results that drive either unnecessary antibiotics or a missed diagnosis. Ensure the specimen is a deep-cough production from the lower airway; use induced sputum, suctioning, or bronchoscopy for patients who cannot produce a deep specimen, and inspect the specimen for saliva (which should prompt recollection).

### Culturing and Treating Asymptomatic Bacteriuria in a Catheterized Patient

The trap is that the catheterized patient has cloudy urine or a positive culture without urinary symptoms, and the team cultures and treats it as a urinary tract infection. The mechanism of harm is that catheterized patients are commonly colonized (asymptomatic bacteriuria), and treating it does not improve outcomes but does select for resistant organisms and increase Clostridioides difficile risk; the patient's fever or decline is usually from another source that gets missed while the urine is treated. The harm is antibiotic overuse and a missed alternative diagnosis. Do not screen or culture urine in asymptomatic catheterized patients, and reserve treatment for symptomatic infection (systemic signs, new urinary symptoms, no other source) — and when treating, obtain a fresh specimen first.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I obtain cultures before the first antibiotic dose when clinically feasible, and document the timing relative to antibiotics?
- For each culture, did I collect from the correct anatomic site with the correct technique (deep sputum, clean debrided wound, midstream or fresh-catheter urine)?
- For blood cultures, did I draw the recommended number of sets from separate sites with strict aseptic preparation of site and bottles?
- Did I apply clinical judgment about whether culturing is indicated, and advocate for stewardship rather than reflex culturing?
- Did I document the exact source, the collection time, the clinical context, and the antibiotic status on the specimen?
- For a wound culture, did I clean and debride before sampling, rather than swabbing superficial exudate or necrotic tissue?
- For a urine culture, did I avoid the drainage bag and an old catheter, and use a fresh specimen?
- Am I communicating preliminary results (gram stain, early growth) promptly to the provider and advocating for source control?
- When final sensitivities result, is the antibiotic being de-escalated or escalated per stewardship principles?
- For a negative culture in a patient with strong clinical evidence of infection, am I integrating the picture rather than excluding infection on the culture alone?
