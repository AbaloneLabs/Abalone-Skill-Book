# Blood Specimen Collection and Preanalytical Error Prevention

The majority of laboratory errors occur before the sample ever reaches the analyzer, in the preanalytical phase that is entirely under nursing control. The judgment problem is that blood drawing is treated as a routine, mechanical task, so the steps that determine accuracy — patient identification, tube selection, order of draw, technique to avoid hemolysis, labeling at the bedside, and transport — are abbreviated under time pressure, producing results that are wrong, misleading, or uninterpretable. A hemolyzed sample produces a falsely elevated potassium that triggers unnecessary treatment; a mislabeled sample produces a result attached to the wrong patient, with potentially catastrophic consequences. The nurse who draws blood is the first and most important determinant of laboratory quality.

## Core Rules

### Identify the Patient and Label the Specimen at the Bedside, Every Time

Specimen misidentification — attaching a result to the wrong patient — is among the most dangerous laboratory errors, and it is preventable with two-step bedside verification. Identify the patient using two identifiers (full name and date of birth, matched to the wristband and the order), and label the specimen tubes at the bedside, in the patient's presence, immediately after the draw — never in the nursing station, never from memory, never prelabeled. Use the barcode label generated from the patient's wristband when available. If the patient cannot state their identifiers (unconscious, pediatric, confused), verify with the wristband and a second source. A mislabeled specimen can lead to a wrong transfusion, a wrong diagnosis, or a wrong procedure, and the bedside labeling step is the barrier that prevents it.

### Draw in the Correct Order of Fill to Prevent Cross-Contamination

The order of draw — the sequence in which tubes are filled from a single venipuncture — prevents cross-contamination of additives between tubes, which can falsely alter results. The standard order is: blood cultures (sterile tubes first, to prevent contamination from other tubes), sodium citrate (light blue, for coagulation), serum tubes (red or gold, for chemistry and serology), heparin (green, for chemistry), EDTA (lavender, for hematology), and fluoride (gray, for glucose). The additive in one tube carried over to the next can corrupt the result (EDTA contaminating a chemistry sample falsely elevates potassium and calcium). Know the order, follow it, and invert each tube the appropriate number of times after filling rather than shaking. The order of draw is not trivia; it is the foundation of accurate results.

### Use Technique That Minimems Hemolysis and Other Preanalytical Errors

Hemolysis — the rupture of red cells releasing intracellular contents — is the most common reason for sample rejection, and it falsely elevates potassium, magnesium, phosphate, lactate dehydrogenase, and aspartate aminotransferase, potentially triggering unnecessary and harmful treatment. Prevent hemolysis with proper technique: use the appropriate needle gauge (commonly 20 to 22 for routine draws, larger for rapid draws), avoid drawing from a catheter that has been running fluids or through a small or fragile vein, avoid forceful pulling on a syringe or vigorous shaking, avoid frothing, and limit tourniquet time to under a minute. For draws from a central line or PICC, follow the discard protocol precisely to avoid dilution or contamination from the line. Other preanalytical errors — underfilling citrate tubes (alters the anticoagulant ratio), clotted samples, wrong tube, prolonged tourniquet time (hemoconcentration) — each corrupt specific results. Technique is not optional; it is the difference between a usable and a misleading sample.

### Collect Blood Cultures with Strict Asepsis to Prevent Contamination

Blood culture contamination — a false-positive culture from skin flora introduced during the draw — is a major problem, because it leads to unnecessary antibiotics, additional testing, longer stays, and the missed diagnosis of true bacteremia. Draw blood cultures with strict aseptic technique: prepare the site with the appropriate antiseptic (chlorhexidine or iodine) and allow it to dry completely, do not re-palpate the prepared site, prepare the septum of the culture bottles, inject the required volume into the aerobic then anaerobic bottles, and avoid drawing through a contaminated line unless the line itself is the suspected source. Draw cultures before starting antibiotics when possible, and from two separate sites if the protocol requires. A contaminated culture is not a trivial inconvenience; it drives antibiotic overuse and obscures the real diagnosis.

### Time Specimen Collection Correctly for Therapeutic and Diagnostic Tests

Many tests are time-sensitive, and the timing of the draw determines the value of the result. Therapeutic drug levels (vancomycin trough before the fourth dose, aminoglycoside peaks and troughs) must be drawn at the precise time relative to the dose to be interpretable; a "trough" drawn after the dose starts is meaningless and misleads dosing. Cardiac biomarkers and troponin have defined serial timing. Cortisol and certain hormones have diurnal variation. Pre-meal and bedtime glucose for insulin titration. Specimens for lactate, blood gases, and ammonia must be transported and analyzed rapidly (ammonia especially, as levels rise in the tube). Coordinate the draw with the medication timing, the order, and the lab requirements, and document the exact time of draw relative to the relevant event. A perfectly drawn sample at the wrong time is as useless as a poorly drawn one.

### Handle and Transport Specimens Appropriately

Specimen integrity depends on handling after the draw. Transport specimens rapidly to the laboratory, especially time-sensitive ones (blood gas, lactate, ammonia, potassium, which rises in the tube over time). Maintain the required temperature (some on ice, some room temperature, some refrigerated) per the lab manual. Ensure tubes are properly sealed to prevent leakage and exposure. Use the pneumatic tube system only for specimens approved for it (some, like blood gases or body fluids, require hand delivery). If there is any delay, communicate with the lab. A specimen that arrives hemolyzed, clotted, underfilled, warm when it should be cold, or delayed is rejected, requiring a redraw, delaying care, and wasting the patient's blood and the team's time.

### Communicate Critical Values and Document the Collection

Document the specimen collection in real time, including the time, the site, the patient's tolerance, and any difficulty. When a critical value results, follow the critical value protocol: read back the value, document the notification and the provider's response, and ensure the indicated intervention is initiated. The nurse is the bridge between the laboratory result and the clinical action, and the integrity of that bridge depends on accurate collection, accurate documentation, and prompt communication.

## Common Traps

### Labeling Specimens at the Nursing Station Rather Than at the Bedside

The trap is that the nurse draws multiple patients' blood, carries the unlabeled tubes to the nursing station, and labels them there from memory or from the paperwork, to save time. The mechanism of harm is that labeling away from the patient introduces the possibility of attaching the label to the wrong patient's tubes, and the result is then attributed to the wrong patient, potentially triggering a wrong transfusion, a wrong diagnosis, or a wrong procedure. The harm is catastrophic misattribution of a result. Label every specimen at the bedside, in the patient's presence, immediately after the draw, using two patient identifiers and the barcode when available.

### Drawing Through a Running IV or From a Site with Fluids, Causing Dilution or Hemolysis

The trap is that the nurse draws from the arm with the running IV or from a catheter to avoid a fresh stick, and the sample is diluted by the fluid or hemolyzed by the catheter trauma. The mechanism of harm is that the diluted sample shows falsely low values (a hemoglobin of 7 that is really 10) or the hemolyzed sample shows falsely high potassium, and the team acts on the wrong numbers — withholding a transfusion, treating a non-existent hyperkalemia, or missing a true abnormality. The harm is unnecessary or harmful treatment based on a corrupt result. Draw from the opposite arm, stop the infusion briefly if needed, or use a fresh venipuncture; avoid drawing through a running line unless the protocol strictly permits it.

### Filling Tubes in the Wrong Order, Cross-Contaminating Additives

The trap is that the nurse fills tubes in whatever order is convenient, not the standard order of draw, assuming the additives do not matter. The mechanism of harm is that the additive in one tube carried over to the next corrupts the result — EDTA from a lavender tube falsely elevates potassium and calcium in a subsequent chemistry tube, citrate from a blue tube alters coagulation in subsequent samples. The harm is falsely abnormal results that trigger unnecessary workup and treatment. Follow the standard order of draw (blood cultures, citrate, serum, heparin, EDTA, fluoride), and invert each tube appropriately after filling.

### Underfilling Citrate Tubes and Invalidating Coagulation Results

The trap is that the nurse fills a light blue citrate tube partially, especially from a difficult draw, and sends it anyway. The mechanism of harm is that the citrate anticoagulant ratio depends on a precise fill volume, and an underfilled tube has excess citrate relative to plasma, which prolongs the prothrombin time and activated partial thromboplastin time falsely, suggesting a bleeding tendency that does not exist. The harm is unnecessary workup, delayed procedures, and inappropriate correction (fresh frozen plasma, vitamin K) for a non-existent coagulopathy. Fill citrate tubes to the exact required volume; if a full draw is not possible, use a smaller-volume citrate tube designed for partial fills, or redraw.

### Drawing "Trough" Levels After the Dose Has Started

The trap is that the nurse draws the vancomycin trough after hanging the dose, or just before but with the dose already running, and labels it a trough. The mechanism of harm is that a trough must reflect the lowest concentration immediately before the next dose, and a sample drawn during or after the infusion reflects a rising or peak level, not the trough, making the result uninterpretable and misleading the dosing — the team may increase a dose that is already adequate or miss accumulation. The harm is subtherapeutic or toxic dosing based on a meaningless level. Draw troughs within the defined window before the dose (commonly 30 minutes before, before the dose is hung), and document the exact time relative to the dose.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I identify the patient with two identifiers and label every specimen at the bedside, immediately after the draw, using the barcode when available?
- Did I fill the tubes in the correct order of draw (cultures, citrate, serum, heparin, EDTA, fluoride) and invert each appropriately?
- Did I use technique that minimizes hemolysis (appropriate gauge, no forceful pulling, short tourniquet time, no frothing), and did I avoid drawing through a running IV?
- For blood cultures, did I prepare the site and the bottles with strict asepsis, allow the antiseptic to dry, avoid re-palpating the site, and inject the required volume?
- For therapeutic drug levels, did I draw the trough within the correct window before the dose, and document the exact time relative to the dose?
- Did I fill each tube to the required volume, especially citrate tubes for coagulation?
- Are the specimens being transported rapidly and at the correct temperature, with time-sensitive samples prioritized?
- Did I document the collection, the time, the site, and any difficulty, and follow up on critical values with read-back and provider notification?
- For central line draws, did I follow the discard and flush protocol precisely to avoid dilution and contamination?
- If a specimen was rejected, did I identify the preanalytical cause and redraw with corrected technique rather than repeating the same error?
