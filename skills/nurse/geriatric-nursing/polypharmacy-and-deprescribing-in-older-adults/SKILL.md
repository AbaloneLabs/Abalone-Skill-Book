# Polypharmacy and Deprescribing in Older Adults

Older adults are the most medicated population and the most vulnerable to medication harm. The judgment problem is that each additional medication was prescribed for a reason, so every drug looks justified in isolation, but the cumulative burden creates a cascade of interactions, side effects, and adverse events that mimic disease and trigger still more prescriptions. Polypharmacy is associated with falls, delirium, cognitive decline, hospitalization, and death, and the nurse — who administers the medications, observes the patient across shifts, and reconciles the list at every transition — is the primary sentinel for medication harm and the strongest advocate for deprescribing. The skilled geriatric nurse treats every medication on the list as a hypothesis to be questioned, not an order to be executed.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile the Medication List Thoroughly at Every Transition

Medication reconciliation is not a clerical task; it is a high-yield safety intervention. At admission, obtain the complete home medication list from multiple sources (the patient, the family, the pharmacy, the prior records, the brown bag of bottles), because patients underreport and one source is never sufficient. At every transfer and at discharge, reconcile again: confirm what is being continued, stopped, started, or changed, and verify the dose, route, and frequency. The reconciliation should be done with the pharmacist when possible, and the discrepancies should be resolved with the prescriber before the patient takes the next dose. Most medication errors occur at transitions, and thorough reconciliation is the single most effective prevention.

### Apply the Beers Criteria and Prescribing Cascade Awareness to Every List

Use the AGS Beers Criteria for Potentially Inappropriate Medications in Older Adults as a routine screen on every geriatric medication list. Common high-risk drugs include benzodiazepines, first-generation antihistamines, certain muscle relaxants, long-acting sulfonylureas, and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatories, all of which carry excess risk in older adults. Beyond the list, watch for the prescribing cascade: a drug causes a side effect that is misdiagnosed as a new condition and treated with another drug. The classic example is a calcium channel blocker causing ankle edema that is then treated with a diuretic. Recognizing the cascade is the nurse's strength, because the nurse sees the timeline of drugs and symptoms across shifts. Flag suspected cascades to the prescriber and pharmacist.

### Recognize That Older Adults Present Differently to Adverse Drug Events

Adverse drug events in older adults frequently present atypically — as delirium, falls, fatigue, anorexia, or functional decline — rather than as classic side effects. A new confusion in a hospitalized older adult should prompt a review of recently added anticholinergics or opioids. A new fall should prompt a review of sedatives, hypotensives, and diuretics. Constipation may be opioid- or anticholinergic-induced. Treat any new symptom in an older adult as potentially drug-related until proven otherwise, and advocate for a medication review as part of the workup. The nurse's longitudinal view of the patient makes this pattern recognition possible.

### Advocate for Deprescribing as an Active, Evidence-Based Intervention

Deprescribing is the planned, supervised reduction of inappropriate or unnecessary medications, and it is a core component of geriatric care, not an act of therapeutic nihilism. Advocate for deprescribing when medications have no current indication, when the harms outweigh the benefits (especially in limited life expectancy), when they duplicate therapy, or when they are on the Beers list with safer alternatives. Use the appropriate deprescribing protocols (e.g., for benzodiazepines, proton pump inhibitors, statins in limited life expectancy), which involve tapering rather than abrupt cessation to avoid withdrawal. Frame deprescribing as a positive, patient-centered intervention that reduces pill burden and adverse events, and engage the patient and family in shared decision-making about which medications are worth continuing.

### Use the Beers and STOPP/START Frameworks as Decision Support, Not as Rigid Rules

The Beers Criteria, the STOPP/START (Screening Tool of Older Persons' Prescriptions / Screening Tool to Alert to Right Treatment) tools, and similar frameworks are decision support, not automatic overrides of clinical judgment. A Beers-listed drug may be the best choice in a specific patient (e.g., a benzodiazepine for alcohol withdrawal), and a START-indicated drug may be inappropriate in a specific context (e.g., a statin in end-stage disease). Use the tools to flag candidates for review, then apply the clinical context, the patient's goals of care, and the life expectancy to decide. The nurse's role is to surface the flags and advocate for the conversation, not to unilaterally decide.

### Educate the Patient and Family on Safe Medication Management

Polypharmacy harm does not stop at discharge; it often worsens at home, where the patient manages a complex regimen without supervision. Educate the patient and family using teach-back: have them name each medication, its purpose, how and when to take it, and what side effects to watch for. Address common hazards — taking multiple drugs with the same active ingredient (especially acetaminophen), sharing medications, taking expired drugs, and using over-the-counter and herbal supplements that interact with prescriptions. Provide a written, updated medication list, recommend a single pharmacy and a pill organizer, and arrange a follow-up medication review. A reconciliation that is not understood at home has failed the patient.

### Coordinate with Pharmacy and the Interprofessional Team

Medication review in older adults is a team sport. Engage the clinical pharmacist for comprehensive medication review on complex patients, especially at admission and discharge. Coordinate with the prescriber to resolve discrepancies and discuss deprescribing candidates. Involve the patient's primary care provider at discharge, since they will manage the regimen long-term. Document the medication decisions and the rationale, and communicate the reconciled list clearly at handoff. The nurse is the connective tissue that ensures the medication plan is coherent across the team and across transitions.

## Common Traps

### Continuing Every Admission Medication Because Each Was "Prescribed for a Reason"

The trap is that each medication on the list was ordered by a clinician for an indication, so the nurse assumes all are necessary and continues them without question. The mechanism of harm is that the cumulative burden grows with each admission and each new consultant, and the original indications are often no longer present (the statin started ten years ago for primary prevention in a patient now in end-stage disease). The harm is progressive polypharmacy with mounting interaction and adverse-event risk, and a patient who takes fifteen drugs when five would do. Treat every medication as a hypothesis: is the indication still present, do the benefits still outweigh the harms in this patient at this time, and is deprescribing appropriate?

### Adding a New Drug to Treat the Side Effect of Another (Prescribing Cascade)

The trap is that a patient develops a new symptom — ankle swelling, constipation, tremor — and the team prescribes a new medication to treat it, without recognizing that the symptom is a side effect of an existing drug. The mechanism of harm is that each new drug in the cascade adds its own side effects and interactions, and the underlying cause (the original drug) is never addressed. The harm is a spiraling medication burden and worsening symptoms. When a new symptom appears, ask first whether it could be drug-induced, and advocate for adjusting or stopping the offending drug rather than adding another to suppress the side effect.

### Treating a New Symptom as a New Disease Rather Than a Possible Adverse Drug Event

The trap is that an older adult develops delirium, a fall, or fatigue, and the team works up a new diagnosis (UTI, stroke, depression) while skipping the medication review. The mechanism of harm is that adverse drug events are among the most common causes of these presentations in older adults, and a workup that ignores medications misses the actual cause and exposes the patient to still more diagnostic and therapeutic interventions. The harm is a delayed or missed diagnosis of an iatrogenic cause and continued exposure to the offending drug. Make a medication review part of the workup for any new geriatric symptom, especially delirium, falls, and functional decline.

### Reassuring Yourself That Over-the-Counter and Herbal Products "Don't Count"

The trap is that the nurse reconciles prescription medications but does not ask about over-the-counter drugs, herbal supplements, or as-needed products, assuming they are benign. The mechanism of harm is that over-the-counter products are a major source of polypharmacy harm in older adults — first-generation antihistamines cause anticholinergic delirium, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatories cause gastrointestinal bleeding and renal injury, and multiple acetaminophen-containing products cause hepatotoxicity. Herbal products interact with anticoagulants and other prescriptions. The harm is an adverse event from a product the chart never recorded. Ask explicitly about all over-the-counter, herbal, and as-needed products at every reconciliation, and document them.

### Discharging the Patient Without Ensuring They Understand the Regimen

The trap is that the nurse performs the discharge reconciliation, hands the patient a printed list, and considers the education complete, without verifying comprehension. The mechanism of harm is that older adults frequently cannot recall or correctly execute a complex new regimen, especially after a hospitalization that may have caused delirium or fatigue, and the regimen at home diverges from the plan within days. The harm is readmission for an adverse event or worsening of the underlying condition. Use teach-back, involve a family caregiver, provide a simplified written list and a pill organizer, and arrange a follow-up medication review. The reconciliation is complete only when the patient or caregiver can demonstrate the plan.

## Self-Check

- Did I reconcile the complete medication list from multiple sources (patient, family, pharmacy, records, bottles) at admission, transfer, and discharge?
- Did I screen the list against the Beers Criteria and look for prescribing cascades and duplication?
- For any new symptom in this older adult (delirium, fall, fatigue, constipation), did I consider an adverse drug event and advocate for a medication review?
- Did I advocate for deprescribing of drugs with no current indication, excess harm, or duplication, using appropriate tapering protocols?
- Did I ask explicitly about over-the-counter, herbal, and as-needed products, and document them as part of the regimen?
- Did I use teach-back to confirm the patient or caregiver can name each medication, its purpose, the schedule, and the side effects to watch for?
- Did I engage the clinical pharmacist for a comprehensive review on complex patients?
- Is the reconciled list communicated clearly at handoff and to the primary care provider at discharge?
- For each medication, can I state the current indication and whether the benefits still outweigh the harms given the patient's goals and life expectancy?
- Did I provide a simplified written medication list and arrange follow-up review to support adherence at home?
