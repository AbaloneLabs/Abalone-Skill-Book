# Falls and Immobility in Older Adults

Falls are the most common inpatient safety event, and in older adults they cause fractures, head injury, functional decline, loss of independence, and death. At the same time, the response to fall risk — keeping the patient in bed — causes its own catastrophic harm through immobility: pressure injuries, deconditioning, hospital-acquired pneumonia, venous thromboembolism, and further loss of function. The judgment problem is that the nurse must simultaneously prevent falls and prevent immobility, two goals that are in tension. Defaulting to bed rest and bed alarms feels safe but causes the very functional decline that drives future falls. The skilled geriatric nurse mobilizes the patient safely, matches the intervention to the actual risk, and treats immobility as a harm to be prevented, not a default to be accepted.

## Core Rules

### Perform a Validated Multifactorial Fall Risk Assessment on Admission and When Status Changes

Fall risk is not a global attribute of "being old"; it is multifactorial and dynamic. Use a validated tool (such as the Morse Fall Scale or Johns Hopkins Fall Risk Assessment) on admission, after any fall, after any change in condition (new medication, post-procedure, altered mental status), and at defined intervals. The assessment should capture fall history, gait and balance, cognition, continence, medications (sedatives, hypotensives, diuretics), sensory deficits, and environmental factors. Document the score and the specific risk factors, because the interventions must target the factors, not the score. A patient who fell because of orthostatic hypotension needs different interventions than one who fell because of impulsive behavior in dementia.

### Match Interventions to the Specific Risk Factors, Not a Generic Bundle

A fall risk score alone does not tell you what to do; the risk factors do. For orthostatic hypotension, teach slow position changes and measure orthostatic vital signs. For urinary frequency or urgency, implement a scheduled toileting program, since most inpatient falls occur on the way to the bathroom. For gait instability, provide the appropriate assistive device and ensure it is within reach and that footwear is non-skid. For cognitive impairment, use a sitter or family presence and orient frequently. For high-risk medications, advocate for medication review and deprescribing of sedatives and hypnotics. Generic interventions applied to all patients (yellow wristband, bed alarm) without targeting the actual factors create alert fatigue and false security without reducing falls.

### Treat Mobilization as Treatment, and Prevent Immobility Harm Actively

Bed rest is not a safety intervention; it is a harm. Older adults lose one to five percent of muscle strength per day of bed rest, and immobility drives pressure injuries, deconditioning, constipation, aspiration risk, venous thromboembolism, and mood decline. Mobilize the patient out of bed to the chair for meals and to walk (with assistance as needed) at least three times a day, starting early in the admission. Use progressive mobility protocols, involve physical therapy early, and document the mobility achieved. The goal is to discharge the patient at or above their admission functional level, and that requires active mobilization throughout the stay, not just at the end.

### Use Bed Alarms and Restraints Judiciously, Not as a Substitute for Presence

Bed and chair alarms are widely used but have limited evidence for preventing falls, and they create alarm fatigue and a false sense of security. They may have a role for specific patients (acute high-risk period, post-fall monitoring), but they are not a substitute for direct observation, toileting schedules, and environmental safety. Physical restraints increase, not decrease, fall-related serious injury, and they should be used only as a last resort for imminent safety risk, with an order, time limit, and ongoing reassessment. The safer alternative is usually one-to-one observation (a sitter or family member), which addresses the underlying behavior without restraint harm.

### Respond to Every Fall with a Standardized Post-Fall Protocol

When a patient falls, do not simply help them back to bed and chart it. Follow the post-fall protocol: assess for injury before moving the patient (especially head, spine, hips), obtain vital signs and neurologic checks, notify the provider, and consider imaging per protocol (any head strike in a patient on anticoagulants requires head imaging). Implement increased monitoring for 24 to 48 hours, since complications (subdural hematoma, occult fracture) can present delayed. Conduct a root-cause analysis of the fall: what time, what was the patient doing, what were the contributing factors, and what interventions need to change? A fall is a signal that the prevention plan failed and must be revised, not an isolated accident.

### Optimize the Environment for Safety

The physical environment contributes to falls and is within nursing control. Keep the bed in the lowest position with brakes locked, ensure the call light is within reach, keep the path to the bathroom clear and the floor dry, use non-skid footwear (not just hospital socks for high-risk patients), ensure adequate lighting (a nightlight at night), and keep personal items within reach. For patients with cognitive impairment, consider a room closer to the nurses' station. These environmental measures are unglamorous but high-yield, and they are the nurse's responsibility to maintain every shift.

### Coordinate with the Interprofessional Team for Functional Recovery

Falls and immobility are not solved by nursing alone. Involve physical therapy for gait training and mobility progression, occupational therapy for activities of daily living and assistive devices, pharmacy for medication review, and the medical team for treating reversible contributors (anemia, dehydration, infection, arrhythmia). Communicate the functional goals at interdisciplinary rounds, and ensure the plan is updated as the patient's status changes. The nurse is the coordinator who sees the whole patient across shifts and ensures the team's interventions align.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to Bed Rest and a Bed Alarm Because It Feels Safe

The trap is that a patient in bed with the alarm on cannot fall, so the nurse interprets immobility as safety. The mechanism of harm is that bed rest causes rapid deconditioning, pressure injuries, and functional decline in older adults, and the weakened patient who finally does get up — to toilet, to transfer, or at discharge — is at higher fall risk, not lower. The harm is a patient discharged to a skilled nursing facility instead of home because they lost function during the stay. Mobilize safely out of bed from early in the admission, matched to the patient's ability, rather than defaulting to bed rest as a one-size-fits-all "safety" measure.

### Applying Generic Fall Precautions Without Targeting the Specific Risk Factors

The trap is that the unit applies the same interventions to every fall-risk patient — a yellow band, a sign on the door, a bed alarm — and the nurse checks the boxes without addressing why this specific patient is at risk. The mechanism of harm is that generic interventions do not modify the actual risk drivers (orthostatic hypotension, urinary urgency, sedating medication, impulsive behavior), so the patient falls anyway despite the "precautions." The harm is a fall that the chart says was "prevented" by precautions that never addressed the cause. Identify the specific risk factors from the assessment and target them directly: scheduled toileting for urgency, orthostatic vitals and slow transfers for hypotension, medication review for sedatives.

### Assuming a Quiet, Uninjured Patient After a Fall Is Fine Without Monitoring

The trap is that the patient fell, the nurse helped them up, they report no pain, vital signs are stable, so the nurse concludes there is no injury and resumes routine care. The mechanism of harm is that occult injuries — subdural hematoma (especially in anticoagulated patients), occult hip or vertebral fracture, and slow intracranial bleeding — present hours later, and the patient who seemed fine deteriorates in the next shift. The harm is a delayed diagnosis of a serious injury that earlier imaging and monitoring would have caught. Follow the full post-fall protocol, implement increased monitoring and neurologic checks for 24 to 48 hours, and have a low threshold for imaging, especially with head strike or anticoagulant use.

### Using Physical Restraints Because the Patient "Keeps Getting Up"

The trap is that a patient with dementia repeatedly tries to get out of bed, the nurse fears a fall, and a vest or wrist restraint is applied to keep them in bed. The mechanism of harm is that restraints do not prevent serious falls (patients climb over bed rails or struggle out of restraints and fall from a greater height), they cause restraint-related injury (asphyxiation, bruising, deconditioning), and they worsen agitation and delirium. The harm is a more serious fall, a restraint injury, or an accelerated functional and cognitive decline. Use least-restrictive alternatives first: a sitter or family member, frequent toileting, pain and elimination management, low bed with mats, and treatment of delirium. Reserve restraints for imminent safety risk only, with order, time limit, and reassessment.

### Missing the Contribution of Medications to Fall Risk

The trap is that the nurse focuses on the patient's gait and environment but does not flag the new benzodiazepine for sleep, the diuretic causing nocturia, or the antihypertensive causing orthostasis, all of which are driving the fall risk. The mechanism of harm is that sedating and hypotensive medications are among the strongest, most reversible fall risk factors in older adults, and they accumulate insidiously during a hospitalization. The harm is a patient who falls because of an iatrogenic medication effect that a pharmacist-nurse review could have caught. Advocate for a medication review in any fall-risk older adult, especially for sedatives, hypnotics, anticholinergics, and polypharmacy, and deprescribe where possible.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I perform a validated multifactorial fall risk assessment on admission and after any change in condition, and did I document the specific risk factors, not just the score?
- Are my interventions targeted to the specific risk factors (toileting for urgency, orthostatic vitals for hypotension, medication review for sedatives) rather than a generic bundle?
- Am I mobilizing this older adult out of bed at least three times a day, or have I defaulted to bed rest that will cause deconditioning?
- After the most recent fall, did I follow the full post-fall protocol, including injury assessment before moving, provider notification, and 24 to 48 hours of increased monitoring?
- If restraints are in use, are they truly a last resort with an order, time limit, and reassessment, or are they a convenience substitute for presence and toileting?
- Is the environment optimized (bed low and locked, call light in reach, non-skid footwear, clear path, lighting)?
- Did I advocate for a medication review of sedatives, hypnotics, and antihypertensives in this fall-risk patient?
- Did I involve physical and occupational therapy early and coordinate functional goals at interdisciplinary rounds?
- For the anticoagulated patient who struck their head, did I obtain head imaging and not just reassure myself that they "feel fine"?
- Is the discharge plan addressing fall prevention at home, including assistive devices, home safety, and follow-up?
