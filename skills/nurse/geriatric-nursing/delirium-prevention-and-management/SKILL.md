# Delirium Prevention and Management

Delirium is an acute, fluctuating disturbance of attention and cognition that affects up to half of hospitalized older adults, and it is one of the most underrecognized and mismanaged conditions in acute care. The judgment problem is that delirium is repeatedly mistaken for dementia, " sundowning," or "agitation," leading to either dismissal or inappropriate sedation — both of which worsen outcomes. Delirium is not a benign confusional state: it is associated with longer stays, higher mortality, accelerated cognitive decline, and new nursing home placement. The nurse, who sees the patient across shifts and is positioned to notice the first fluctuation, is the primary sentinel for delirium, and the quality of prevention and early response depends almost entirely on nursing vigilance.

## Core Rules

### Establish and Document the Cognitive Baseline at Admission

Delirium is defined by a change from baseline, so you cannot recognize it without knowing the baseline. On admission, obtain a collateral cognitive history from family or caregivers: what is the patient's usual mental status, how independent are they in daily activities, do they have a prior dementia diagnosis, and what is their baseline communication pattern? Administer a brief cognitive screen (such as the Mini-Cog or Montreal Cognitive Assessment) and document the result. Without this anchor, every subsequent fluctuation is interpreted in a vacuum, and acute change is attributed to "the patient is old" or "they have dementia." The baseline is the reference frame that makes delirium visible.

### Screen for Delirium Every Shift with a Validated Tool

Use a validated screening tool (the Confusion Assessment Method, CAM, is the most widely used) every shift on every older adult, not only when you "suspect" delirium. The CAM requires acute onset and fluctuating course, inattention, and either disorganized thinking or altered level of consciousness. Inattention is the cardinal feature — a patient who cannot follow a conversation, track a question, or perform a simple attention task (squeeze my hand whenever I say the letter A) is delirious until proven otherwise. Document the screen results, and treat a positive screen as a clinical alarm requiring workup, not a charting checkbox.

### Treat Delirium as a Medical Emergency and Search for the Cause

Delirium is a symptom, not a diagnosis, and it almost always has a reversible precipitant. Work through the common causes systematically using a framework such as the DELIRIUM or PINCH ME mnemonic (Drugs, Electrolytes, Lack of drugs, Infection, Reduced sensory input, Intracranial, Urinary retention, Myocardial/metabolic, Environmental). The most common culprits are infection (urinary tract, pneumonia), medications (especially anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, opioids), electrolyte disturbances, hypoxia, urinary retention, fecal impaction, pain, and sensory deprivation (missing glasses or hearing aids). Notify the provider of a new delirium with your differential, advocate for the workup, and address every reversible factor you can identify. Treating delirium without finding the cause is treating the smoke while the fire burns.

### Implement Multicomponent Nonpharmacologic Prevention for Every At-Risk Patient

Delirium prevention is more effective than treatment, and it is overwhelmingly a nursing intervention. For every older adult, implement the multicomponent bundle: orient and reorient frequently (clock, calendar, whiteboard with the date and the nurse's name), restore sensory input (glasses on, hearing aids in and working), promote sleep hygiene (lights down at night, cluster activities, minimize nighttime awakenings, reduce noise), encourage mobility out of bed three times a day, ensure adequate hydration and nutrition, and address pain and constipation proactively. Family presence reduces delirium, so facilitate it. These interventions are not optional niceties; they are the evidence-based standard of care.

### Manage the Delirious Patient Safely Without Defaulting to Sedation

A delirious patient may pull at lines, climb out of bed, or become agitated, and the reflex is to reach for a sedative. Resist this reflex. Sedatives, especially benzodiazepines, prolong and worsen delirium and increase mortality; they are indicated only for specific situations such as alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal. Use least-restrictive measures first: a family member or sitter at the bedside, reorientation, addressing pain and elimination needs, reducing noise, and ensuring glasses and hearing aids are in place. Use physical restraints only as a last resort for imminent safety risk, with an order, time limit, and ongoing reassessment — restraints themselves worsen delirium and increase injury. If pharmacologic management is genuinely required (e.g., severe agitation risking harm), it should be provider-ordered, low-dose, and reassessed frequently.

### Differentiate Delirium from Dementia and Depression

These three conditions coexist and overlap, but they require different management. Delirium is acute (hours to days), fluctuating, with inattention and often altered level of consciousness. Dementia is chronic, progressive, with preserved level of consciousness until late stages and prominent memory loss. Depression is subacute to chronic, with normal level of consciousness and a depressed mood, and is frequently missed in older adults who present with somatic complaints. Critically, a patient with dementia can and often does develop superimposed delirium, and the acute change is the emergency even if the chronic state is present. Never attribute acute new confusion to "just dementia" without ruling out delirium.

### Support the Family and Document the Trajectory

Delirium is frightening for families, who see a loved one become a different person. Explain that delirium is common, often temporary, and not the same as permanent dementia; describe what they are seeing and what to expect; and invite them to help with reorientation and presence. Document the cognitive trajectory across shifts so the team can track improvement or worsening, and ensure that delirium history is communicated at handoff and discharge, since delirium may persist for weeks to months and affects post-discharge care needs.

## Common Traps

### Labeling Acute Confusion as "Underlying Dementia" Without a Baseline

The trap is that the patient is confused, the chart mentions a dementia history, and the nurse concludes the confusion is "their baseline," so no further workup occurs. The mechanism of harm is that without a documented baseline, the nurse cannot distinguish chronic dementia from acute delirium superimposed on dementia — and the acute change is the emergency. The harm is that an infection, metabolic derangement, or medication effect goes untreated, the delirium deepens, and the patient suffers prolonged hospitalization, functional decline, or death. Always obtain a collateral baseline and treat any acute or fluctuating change from that baseline as delirium requiring workup, regardless of any chronic dementia diagnosis.

### Reaching for an Antipsychotic or Benzodiazepine at the First Sign of Agitation

The trap is that an agitated older adult is frightening, disruptive, and time-consuming, and a sedative feels like the fastest way to restore order on a busy unit. The mechanism of harm is that sedatives, particularly benzodiazepines, prolong delirium, increase the risk of falls and aspiration, and carry a black-box mortality warning in older adults with dementia-related psychosis. The harm is a patient who is chemically restrained into a longer, more dangerous delirium rather than treated for the underlying cause. Use nonpharmacologic measures and address reversible causes first; reserve pharmacologic management for genuine safety crises, at the lowest effective dose, with frequent reassessment.

### Dismissing Inattention as "the Patient Is Just Tired"

The trap is that the patient cannot answer questions coherently, drifts off, or loses the thread, and the nurse attributes this to fatigue, pain, or personality rather than testing attention. The mechanism of harm is that inattention is the cardinal, defining feature of delirium, and it is easy to overlook if you do not formally test it. The harm is that the earliest, most treatable stage of delirium is missed, and the condition progresses to overt agitation or hypoactive withdrawal before it is recognized. Formally test attention every shift in at-risk older adults using a simple task (days of the week backward, letter recitation), and treat new inattention as delirium until proven otherwise.

### Missing Hypoactive Delirium Because the Patient Is Quiet

The trap is that delirium is stereotyped as agitated and loud, so the quiet, withdrawn, sleepy patient is considered "stable" and "comfortable," and their delirium goes unrecognized. The mechanism of harm is that hypoactive delirium — characterized by lethargy, reduced motor activity, and withdrawn affect — is actually the more common and more dangerous form, with worse outcomes than the hyperactive type, precisely because it is missed. The harm is that a patient in quiet delirium receives no workup, no reversible-cause correction, and no family education, and they deteriorate silently. Screen every older adult every shift regardless of how calm they appear, and recognize that the quiet patient may be the sickest.

### Leaving Glasses and Hearing Aids in the Bedside Drawer

The trap is that sensory aids are small, easily misplaced, and a hassle to put on a confused patient, so they sit unused in the drawer, and the nurse does not see this as clinically significant. The mechanism of harm is that sensory deprivation is a major, reversible driver of delirium, and a patient who cannot see or hear cannot orient, cannot follow conversation, and slides further into confusion. The harm is preventable delirium that persists and worsens for lack of a ten-second intervention. Ensure glasses and hearing aids are on, clean, and working every shift, label them, and involve family in bringing them from home if they were left behind.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I document a collateral cognitive baseline at admission, and can I distinguish the patient's current state from that baseline?
- Did I screen for delirium every shift with a validated tool (CAM), including formal attention testing, not just observation?
- For any new or worsening delirium, did I work through a systematic cause list (infection, medications, electrolytes, hypoxia, retention, impaction, pain, sensory) and advocate for workup?
- Did I implement the multicomponent prevention bundle (orientation, sensory aids, sleep hygiene, mobility, hydration, family presence) for this older adult?
- Before considering any sedative, did I exhaust nonpharmacologic measures and address reversible causes, and is any restraint use ordered, time-limited, and reassessed?
- Am I alert to hypoactive delirium in the quiet, withdrawn patient, and screening regardless of apparent calm?
- Did I differentiate delirium from chronic dementia and depression, and treat the acute change as the priority?
- Are the patient's glasses on and hearing aids in and working, or are they in the bedside drawer?
- Did I educate the family about delirium, its typical course, and how they can help, and is the cognitive trajectory documented across shifts?
- At handoff and discharge, did I communicate the delirium history and residual cognitive risk to the next team?
