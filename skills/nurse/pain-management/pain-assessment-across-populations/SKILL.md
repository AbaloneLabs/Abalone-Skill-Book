# Pain Assessment Across Populations

Pain assessment is often treated as a single number on a flowsheet, but pain is a subjective, multidimensional experience that cannot be measured directly. The nurse's job is to construct the most accurate estimate of the patient's pain by combining self-report (the gold standard when available), behavioral indicators, physiologic signals, and knowledge of the patient's condition and likely pain sources. The most dangerous errors are not in measurement but in interpretation: dismissing pain because the vital signs are normal, assuming a sleeping patient is pain-free, or under-treating populations who cannot self-report. Poor assessment directly causes under-treatment, which impairs healing, drives delirium and immobility, and erodes trust.

## Core Rules

### Self-Report Is the Gold Standard — When the Patient Can Give It

For alert, communicative patients, the patient's reported pain is the pain. Use a validated scale appropriate to the patient: 0–10 numeric rating for adults who understand it, the Visual Analog Scale, a faces scale for children or adults with literacy barriers, and the FLACC or revised FLACC for preverbal or cognitively impaired children. Document the score, the scale used, and the quality (burning, aching, sharp, cramping) and location. A patient reporting 8/10 who is lying still with normal vitals has 8/10 pain; do not discount it.

### Nonverbal and Cognitively Impaired Patients Need Behavioral Tools

When self-report is impossible — dementia, delirium, sedation, intubation, intellectual disability — use a validated observational tool: the Behavioral Pain Scale (BPS) or Critical-Care Pain Observation Tool (CPOT) for critically ill, the Pain Assessment in Advanced Dementia (PAINAD) for dementia, and the Non-communicative Adult Pain Checklist for intellectual disability. These tools score behaviors such as facial grimacing, restlessness, muscle rigidity, vocalization, and consolability. Use them consistently across shifts so trends are interpretable.

### Integrate Behavior, Physiology, and Likely Pain Sources

Pain assessment is not one number; it is a composite. Observe for behavioral cues (guarding, grimacing, moaning, agitation, refusal to move), physiologic cues (tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis, tearing), and contextual cues (recent surgery, known pathology, procedures performed). A grimacing, postoperative patient who tenses when you approach the wound is in pain regardless of vital signs. Synthesize all sources rather than relying on any single indicator.

### Assess Pain at the Right Times

Assess pain on admission, at the start of each shift, before and after procedures, before and after interventions (within a defined window, e.g., 30–60 minutes after oral analgesia, 15–30 minutes after IV), and whenever the patient's status changes. Unassessed pain is untreated pain. Establish a reassessment rhythm that captures both baseline and response.

### Distinguish Acute, Procedural, Breakthrough, and Chronic Pain

Each has a different assessment and management approach. Acute postoperative or traumatic pain demands frequent assessment and rapid intervention. Procedural pain requires pre-procedural planning. Breakthrough pain on a chronic baseline requires recognizing the baseline and the spike separately. Chronic pain often shows minimal physiologic signs and can be missed by nurses expecting tachycardia or hypertension — the absence of vital sign changes does not rule out chronic pain.

### Consider the Whole Person: Culture, Cognition, Fear, and Prior Experience

Pain expression is shaped by culture, prior pain experiences, fear, anxiety, depression, and meaning (e.g., cancer pain carries different meaning than postoperative pain). Patients from stoic cultural backgrounds may underreport; patients with chronic pain may have developed tolerance and need higher doses; anxious patients may amplify. None of these justify under-treatment. Assess the patient's baseline, their goals (what pain level lets them function), and their concerns (fear of addiction, side effects).

## Common Traps

**Dismissing pain because vital signs are normal.** Nurses are taught that pain causes tachycardia, hypertension, and diaphoresis, and then invert this into "if the vitals are normal, the pain is not severe." The mechanism of harm is that vital sign response to pain is variable and blunts rapidly — a patient in chronic pain, a patient who has received partial analgesia, or a patient who is exhausted may have severe pain with completely normal vital signs. Using vital signs to validate or invalidate a self-reported pain score leads directly to under-treatment. The harm is prolonged suffering, impaired mobilization, sleep deprivation, and a cascade of complications (atelectasis, DVT, delirium) driven by uncontrolled pain. Trust the self-report; use vital signs as one data point, never as an override.

**Assuming a sleeping patient is comfortable.** A patient who sleeps between assessments may be exhausted from uncontrolled pain and finally sleeping through it, or may have found one tolerable position and fear moving. The mechanism of harm is that nurses document "resting comfortably" based on sleep without assessing, then withhold or delay the next analgesic dose, allowing pain to resurge and disrupt recovery. Sleep is not evidence of analgesia. The harm is a patient who wakes in severe pain, loses trust in the care team, and refuses mobilization. When in doubt, gently assess before assuming comfort, and follow the scheduled analgesic plan unless the patient's respiratory status contraindicates it.

**Under-assessing cognitively impaired patients.** Patients with dementia, delirium, or intellectual disability often cannot report pain numerically, and their behavioral expressions (agitation, resistance to care, moaning, combativeness) are frequently attributed to the dementia rather than to pain. The mechanism of harm is that the actual pain source (a fracture, a full bladder, constipation, a wound) goes untreated while the patient receives sedatives for "agitation," which further obscures pain assessment and increases fall and delirium risk. The harm is untreated pain masked by sedation, plus iatrogenic complications from the sedatives. Use a validated observational tool, investigate pain sources systematically, and treat suspected pain with a trial of analgesia before reaching for sedatives.

**Equating calm demeanor with low pain.** Some patients — due to stoicism, cultural conditioning, fear of bothering staff, fear of addiction, or a desire to appear strong — present as calm and pleasant while reporting a high number, or while clearly in pain on close observation. The mechanism of harm is that nurses discount the self-report or the behavioral cues because the patient "looks fine," and under-medicate. The harm is silent suffering and a patient who stops asking for help. A pleasant, smiling patient who reports 8/10 has 8/10 pain. Treat the reported experience, not the projected demeanor.

**Treating the number without the function.** A patient may report a pain score that sounds high (7/10) but be functioning well, ambulating, and sleeping, while another reports 4/10 but cannot move or cough. The mechanism of harm is chasing a number with escalating opioids rather than assessing what the pain prevents the patient from doing. The harm is over-sedation, respiratory depression, and delirium from unnecessary opioid escalation, while the functional problem (e.g., a splinting chest, a rigid posture) goes unaddressed. Always pair the score with a functional question: "What does the pain stop you from doing?" and treat toward functional goals, not toward zero.

## Self-Check

- Did I use a validated pain scale appropriate to the patient's cognitive and communicative abilities?
- For a nonverbal or cognitively impaired patient, did I use a validated observational tool (PAINAD, CPOT, BPS) rather than guessing?
- Did I integrate self-report, behavior, physiology, and likely pain sources into my assessment?
- Did I assess pain before and after interventions within the appropriate reassessment window?
- Did I trust the patient's self-report even when vital signs were normal or the patient appeared calm?
- Did I avoid assuming a sleeping or quiet patient was pain-free without assessment?
- Did I distinguish acute, breakthrough, procedural, and chronic components of the pain?
- Did I ask what the pain prevents the patient from doing, and treat toward functional goals?
- Did I consider cultural, emotional, and experiential factors that shape pain expression?
- Did I document the scale used, the score, quality, location, and response to intervention?
