# Multimodal Analgesia and Opioid Stewardship

Pain management is a balance between relieving suffering and avoiding harm. Opioids are powerful and fast, which makes them both effective and dangerous — respiratory depression, sedation, delirium, constipation, falls, and dependence are all dose- and duration-dependent. Multimodal analgesia uses combinations of agents with different mechanisms to achieve better pain control at lower opioid doses, reducing harm. The nurse's role is to operationalize this: give the right drugs in the right sequence, monitor for the specific dangers of each, titrate to functional goals, and act as a steward who prevents both under-treatment and over-sedation. Neither pole is safe — untreated pain harms, and excessive opioid harms.

## Core Rules

### Build a Multimodal Plan, Not an Opioid-Only Plan

Effective analgesia layers agents: scheduled acetaminophen for baseline, NSAIDs (where not contraindicated) for inflammatory pain, regional or neuraxial techniques where appropriate, adjuvants (gabapentinoids, muscle relaxants, antidepressants) for neuropathic or spasm components, and opioids reserved for breakthrough and severe pain. Give non-opioids around the clock so they maintain therapeutic levels; relying on PRN opioids alone produces a rollercoaster of pain and sedation. The opioid becomes the rescue, not the foundation.

### Titrate to Function, Not to Zero

The goal of acute pain management is not a pain score of zero; it is a pain level that allows the patient to deep-breathe, cough, ambulate, and participate in care — often 3–4/10 for surgical patients. Chasing zero with escalating opioids causes sedation and respiratory depression. Set functional goals with the patient ("What number lets you walk to the bathroom?") and titrate toward those.

### PCA and Continuous Opioid Infusions: Heightened Monitoring

Patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) is safe only when the patient — not a family member or nurse — activates it; proxy activation is a leading cause of PCA-related respiratory depression. Continuous opioid infusions require defined sedation monitoring. Use a validated sedation scale (e.g., PAS, RASS, POSS) and respiratory monitoring. The combination of opioid plus benzodiazepine, or opioid plus other sedatives, multiplies respiratory depression risk and demands intensified monitoring.

### Recognize and Respond to Opioid-Induced Respiratory Depression (OIRD)

OIRD presents as decreasing respiratory rate, shallow breathing, increasing sedation, and (late) falling oxygen saturation and rising CO2. Pulse oximetry alone is inadequate — it lags, especially in patients on supplemental oxygen, and can remain normal while CO2 climbs. Capnography is more sensitive for hypoventilation. If sedation is deeper than expected or respiratory rate is below threshold, stop the opioid, stimulate the patient, and administer naloxone per protocol. Naloxone should be titrated to restore adequate respiratory rate, not to full alertness, to avoid precipitating severe pain and withdrawal.

### Manage Side Effects Proactively, Not Reactively

Opioids predictably cause constipation, nausea, sedation, and pruritus. Prevent rather than chase: bowel regimens should start with the first opioid dose; antiemetics should be available; pruritus often responds to low-dose naloxone or antihistamines. Untreated side effects lead patients to refuse necessary analgesia.

### Opioid Stewardship: Right Drug, Right Dose, Right Duration

Stewardship is not opioid denial; it is appropriate use. Match opioid potency to pain severity (don't use morphine for mild pain that acetaminophen would control; don't use oral codeine for severe acute pain). Adjust for renal/hepatic function, age, opioid tolerance, and concurrent sedatives. Convert doses accurately when switching agents or routes. Plan the taper from the start for patients on continuous or high-dose opioids. Identify patients at risk for opioid use disorder and arrange appropriate transition and follow-up.

### Know When to Escalate for Uncontrolled or Neuropathic Pain

If scheduled multimodal therapy plus breakthrough opioids is insufficient, escalate to the pain service or provider for regional techniques, ketamine infusions, or adjuvant adjustment. Severe neuropathic pain often does not respond to opioids and needs gabapentinoids or other neuropathic agents. Persistent severe pain is not a nursing failure to "tough out" — it is a signal to change the plan.

## Common Traps

**Chasing the pain score to zero with opioids.** A postoperative patient reports 4/10 pain and the nurse, aiming for complete relief, administers additional opioid until the score drops to zero. The mechanism of harm is that the escalating dose produces progressive sedation and respiratory depression, often peaking after the patient appears "comfortable," because peak respiratory depression lags behind peak analgesic effect. The harm is a sedated, hypoventilating patient at risk of aspiration, atelectasis, and arrest — the very complications pain control was meant to prevent. Titrate to functional goals (deep breathing, ambulation), accept a low residual pain score, and resist the reflex to eliminate all pain chemically.

**Allowing PCA proxy activation.** Family members or nurses, wanting to spare a sleeping or struggling patient, press the PCA button for them. The mechanism of harm is that the safety mechanism of PCA — the patient self-limits when sedated and stops pressing — is bypassed, and the patient receives opioid they would not have requested, accumulating to respiratory depression. The harm is overdose in a patient who cannot signal their own sedation. Only the patient activates the PCA; educate family and staff, and intervene immediately if proxy activation is observed.

**Relying on pulse oximetry alone to detect respiratory depression.** Because pulse oximeters are ubiquitous and easy, nurses may monitor SpO2 and assume a normal reading excludes respiratory depression. The mechanism of harm is that supplemental oxygen masks hypoventilation — the patient retains CO2 and becomes somnolent while SpO2 remains normal, and the nurse is falsely reassured until the patient is deeply sedated or airway-compromised. The harm is delayed detection of OIRD, sometimes to the point of arrest. Use sedation scoring and respiratory rate as primary monitors; add capnography for high-risk patients (older adults, sleep apnea, concurrent sedatives, high-dose opioids), and do not let a normal SpO2 override signs of sedation.

**Treating opioid side effects only when the patient complains.** Constipation, nausea, and pruritus are treated as expected and addressed only when reported. The mechanism of harm is that these side effects are nearly universal with opioids, and waiting for the patient to suffer through them before treating leads to preventable distress — constipation can progress to ileus and bowel obstruction, nausea prevents eating and mobilization, and pruritus disrupts sleep. The harm is prolonged recovery, patient refusal of needed analgesia, and avoidable complications. Start the bowel regimen with the first opioid dose, provide antiemetics proactively, and treat pruritus early.

**Failing to convert or adjust opioid doses across agents, routes, and patient factors.** A patient is switched from IV to oral opioid, or from one agent to another, and the ordered dose is not equivalent, or renal/hepatic impairment is not accounted for. The mechanism of harm is that equianalgesic conversion is imperfect and patient-specific — too much causes overdose, too little causes withdrawal and pain crisis. The harm ranges from breakthrough pain to respiratory depression. Always verify conversions, apply cross-tolerance reduction when switching agents, adjust for organ function and age, and double-check high-risk conversions with pharmacy or the provider.

**Overlooking the edge case or exception.** The mechanism of harm is that the agent analyzes the typical scenario thoroughly but skips the unusual case. The false signal is that the common path is well-handled; in reality the exception silently produces the wrong outcome because the boundary was never tested. The harm is a failure that only appears in the case nobody checked. The fix is to always ask what the edge case is and whether the conclusion still holds there.

## Self-Check

- Did I administer scheduled non-opioid analgesics (acetaminophen, NSAIDs) around the clock as the foundation, reserving opioids for breakthrough?
- Did I titrate analgesia toward functional goals (deep breathing, ambulation) rather than a pain score of zero?
- For PCA, did I confirm that only the patient activates it and educate family/staff against proxy activation?
- Did I monitor sedation level (validated scale) and respiratory rate, not just pulse oximetry, for patients on opioids?
- For high-risk patients (older adults, sleep apnea, concurrent sedatives), did I use capnography or intensified monitoring?
- Did I start a bowel regimen and provide prophylactic antiemetics with the first opioid dose?
- Did I adjust opioid doses for renal/hepatic function, age, tolerance, and concurrent sedatives?
- Did I verify equianalgesic conversions when switching agents or routes, applying cross-tolerance reduction?
- Did I titrate naloxone to restore respiratory rate (not full alertness) when treating OIRD?
- Did I escalate to the pain service for persistent severe pain rather than continuing an ineffective plan?; did I plan the opioid taper and identify patients needing addiction-medicine or follow-up support?
