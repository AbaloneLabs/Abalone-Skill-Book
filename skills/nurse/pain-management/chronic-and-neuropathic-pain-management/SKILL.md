# Chronic and Neuropathic Pain Management

Chronic pain is fundamentally different from acute pain. It is not a symptom to be eliminated but a condition to be managed, often alongside depression, sleep disruption, deconditioning, and loss of identity and role. Neuropathic pain — caused by nerve injury or dysfunction — frequently does not respond to opioids at all and requires adjuvant agents and different expectations. The nurse who applies acute-pain logic (escalate opioids until the score drops) to chronic pain causes harm: escalating doses, hyperalgesia, dependence, and loss of function. The nurse's job is to support a long-term, multimodal, function-oriented plan, recognize when pain is neuropathic and needs different therapy, and balance compassion with opioid-risk vigilance.

## Core Rules

### Reframe the Goal: Function and Quality of Life, Not Zero Pain

In chronic pain, the goal is improved function — sleep, mobility, mood, social participation, return to role — at an acceptable pain level, not elimination of pain. Patients who have lived with chronic pain for months or years may never reach a low score, and chasing it chemically causes harm. Establish functional goals collaboratively and track them: days worked, hours slept, distance walked, mood. Celebrate functional gains even when the pain number does not move.

### Distinguish Neuropathic from Nociceptive Pain

Neuropathic pain has distinctive features: burning, electric, shooting, tingling, numbness, allodynia (pain from non-painful stimuli like light touch), and hyperalgesia. It is caused by diabetes, post-herpetic neuralgia, radiculopathy, chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy, phantom limb, spinal cord injury, and trigeminal neuralgia. Neuropathic pain responds poorly to opioids and NSAIDs and well to gabapentinoids (gabapentin, pregabalin), certain antidepressants (SNRIs, tricyclics), topical agents (lidocaine, capsaicin), and neuromodulation. Recognizing the neuropathic pattern prompts escalation for the right therapy rather than opioid escalation.

### Build a Multimodal, Long-Term Plan

Chronic pain management layers pharmacologic and non-pharmacologic strategies: scheduled adjuvants, topical agents, physiotherapy, exercise, cognitive behavioral therapy for pain, sleep optimization, mood treatment, and interventional procedures (nerve blocks, spinal cord stimulators). Opioids, when used, are one component with strict boundaries, not the centerpiece. Non-pharmacologic approaches are not adjuncts to dismiss — they are evidence-based core therapy.

### Long-Term Opioid Therapy: Risk Stratification and Boundaries

If chronic opioids are part of the plan, apply risk mitigation: screen for opioid use disorder risk before initiation, use prescription drug monitoring programs, set a written agreement with the patient defining expectations (one prescriber, one pharmacy, no early refills, no unauthorized dose escalation), and monitor with periodic urine drug screens. Watch for aberrant behaviors (running out early, lost prescriptions, seeking from multiple providers). These are not accusations; they are safety structures that protect the patient and the therapeutic relationship.

### Manage the Whole Person: Depression, Sleep, and Deconditioning

Chronic pain, depression, and insomnia are tightly linked and amplify each other. Treating pain without addressing depression and sleep is rarely effective. Screen for depression and anxiety, support sleep hygiene and sleep medication where appropriate, and address the deconditioning spiral (pain leads to inactivity, which worsens pain and mood). The biopsychosocial model is not optional here — it is the only effective framework.

### Tapering and Transition

Patients on long-term high-dose opioids may need tapering when risk exceeds benefit, when there is no functional improvement, or when aberrant use emerges. Taper slowly (5–10% per month for long-term patients) to avoid withdrawal and pain crisis, and coordinate with the prescriber and addiction medicine where indicated. Buprenorphine may be appropriate for patients with chronic pain and opioid use disorder. Never abruptly stop long-term opioids — this is dangerous and cruel.

### Cancer Pain Has Different Rules

Cancer pain and end-of-life pain are managed more aggressively, with opioids as a mainstay and fewer restrictions, because the prognosis and goals differ. Do not apply chronic non-cancer opioid restrictions to palliative and hospice patients; the priority is comfort. Distinguish, however, between curative-intent cancer treatment (more like chronic pain) and end-of-life comfort (more aggressive).

## Common Traps

**Applying acute-pain escalation logic to chronic pain.** A patient with chronic low back pain reports persistent 7/10 pain, and the nurse or team responds by escalating the opioid dose repeatedly. The mechanism of harm is that chronic pain involves central sensitization and opioid-induced hyperalgesia — higher doses can actually worsen pain perception while escalating dependence, sedation, and overdose risk. The harm is a patient on massive opioid doses with no functional improvement, now at risk of overdose and difficult to taper. Recognize chronic pain as a condition managed for function, escalate by changing the plan (adjuvants, therapy, interventional approaches) rather than by raising the opioid dose.

**Assuming chronic pain patients reporting high scores are drug-seeking.** Because of legitimate concern about opioid misuse, nurses may approach chronic pain patients — especially those on long-term opioids — with suspicion, interpreting a request for medication as drug-seeking. The mechanism of harm is that this bias leads to under-treatment of genuine pain, damages the therapeutic relationship, and drives the patient toward defensive or adversarial behavior. The harm is untreated suffering, loss of trust, and sometimes the patient abandoning care. Assess each request on its merits, use the treatment agreement and monitoring data rather than gut suspicion, and distinguish genuine pain from aberrant use through objective patterns, not stereotypes.

**Treating neuropathic pain with escalating opioids.** A patient with diabetic neuropathy reports burning foot pain, and because opioids are the familiar tool, the dose is increased. The mechanism of harm is that neuropathic pain arises from nerve dysfunction and responds poorly to opioids — escalating doses produce sedation and dependence without relief, and can worsen pain through hyperalgesia. The harm is a patient sedated, dependent, and still in pain. Recognize the neuropathic pattern (burning, electric, allodynia) and advocate for adjuvant therapy (gabapentinoids, SNRIs, topicals) and referral to pain management or neurology.

**Dismissing non-pharmacologic therapy as secondary.** Nurses and patients may view physiotherapy, CBT, exercise, and mindfulness as "not real treatment" compared to medication. The mechanism of harm is that these approaches are the evidence-based foundation of chronic pain management, and sidelining them leaves the patient reliant on medications that lose effectiveness and carry escalating risk. The harm is a patient who never develops self-management skills, deconditions further, and has no tools when medications fail. Actively support and reinforce non-pharmacologic strategies as core therapy, not optional add-ons.

**Abruptly stopping or holding long-term opioids.** When a chronic pain patient is admitted for an unrelated issue, or when a dose is questioned, the opioids may be held or stopped abruptly. The mechanism of harm is that physical dependence develops within weeks of continuous use, and abrupt cessation causes severe withdrawal (pain crisis, agitation, tachycardia, hypertension, diarrhea, insomnia) that can destabilize the patient and complicate the admission. The harm is acute suffering and sometimes cardiovascular instability. Continue the home opioid regimen (verified and reconciled) during admission, adjust for acute needs, and coordinate any taper with the prescriber rather than stopping abruptly.

**Overlooking the edge case or exception.** The mechanism of harm is that the agent analyzes the typical scenario thoroughly but skips the unusual case. The false signal is that the common path is well-handled; in reality the exception silently produces the wrong outcome because the boundary was never tested. The harm is a failure that only appears in the case nobody checked. The fix is to always ask what the edge case is and whether the conclusion still holds there.

## Self-Check

- Did I set and track functional goals (sleep, mobility, mood, role participation) rather than chasing a pain score of zero?
- Did I identify whether the pain is neuropathic (burning, electric, allodynia) and advocate for adjuvant therapy rather than opioid escalation?
- Did I support non-pharmacologic strategies (physiotherapy, CBT, exercise, sleep optimization) as core therapy?
- For long-term opioid therapy, did I verify the regimen, check the monitoring program, and watch for aberrant behaviors without stereotyping?
- Did I screen for and address depression, anxiety, and sleep disruption as part of the pain plan?
- Did I distinguish chronic non-cancer pain (restrictive opioid approach) from cancer/end-of-life pain (comfort-focused approach)?
- Did I avoid abruptly stopping or holding long-term opioids during admission?
- Did I coordinate tapers slowly (5–10% per month) with the prescriber and addiction medicine where indicated?
- Did I document functional outcomes, not just pain scores, to track meaningful progress?
- Did I escalate to specialty pain management for refractory or complex pain rather than continuing an ineffective plan?
