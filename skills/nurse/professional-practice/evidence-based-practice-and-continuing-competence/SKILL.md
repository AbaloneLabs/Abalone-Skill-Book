# Evidence-Based Practice and Continuing Competence

Nursing is a profession grounded in evidence, but the bedside is also full of practices handed down by tradition — rituals that "have always been done this way" and persist long after the evidence has moved on, sometimes causing harm. The nurse's professional duty is to ground practice in current evidence (the best available research, integrated with clinical expertise and patient values), to recognize and abandon outdated practices, to maintain and expand competency throughout their career, and to speak up when a practice lacks evidence or contradicts it. This is not academic work; it is the difference between care that helps and care that is at best useless and at worst harmful. The nurse who never questions practice, never updates knowledge, and never challenges ritual becomes progressively more dangerous as the evidence advances without them.

## Core Rules

### Ground Practice in Current Evidence, Integrated With Expertise and Patient Values

Evidence-based practice (EBP) integrates three components: the best available external evidence (research, guidelines, systematic reviews), clinical expertise (your assessment and judgment of this patient), and patient values and circumstances (what matters to this individual). No single component is sufficient. A guideline applied without considering the patient's context is rote; expertise without evidence is opinion; patient preference without evidence may be misinformed. Integrate all three.

### Recognize and Challenge Ritual Practices

Many nursing practices persist by tradition rather than evidence: aspirating before IM injections (no longer recommended for vaccines), routine sequential compression without assessment, restrictive visiting hours, certain wound care rituals, extended NPO periods without justification, and many others. When you encounter a practice, ask: what is the evidence? When a practice seems ritualistic, investigate, raise it with the team, and advocate for the evidence-based approach. Do not perpetuate a practice simply because "that's how we do it here."

### Stay Current With Guidelines in Your Practice Area

Guidelines evolve: sepsis bundles, resuscitation guidelines, infection control recommendations, heart failure and diabetes management, ventilator protocols, and many others are updated regularly. Commit to ongoing learning: read the key guidelines in your area, attend in-services and conferences, use reputable continuing education, and engage with professional organizations. Knowledge that was current at licensure is not current five years later without active maintenance.

### Maintain and Expand Clinical Competency

Competency is not permanent; it decays without use and must be validated and updated. Maintain competency in your core skills, seek validation (skills checklists, simulations, peer review), and expand into new skills and knowledge as your role evolves. If you have not performed a skill in a long time, refresh before performing it on a patient. Acknowledge competency gaps honestly and address them rather than performing unvalidated skills.

### Question Practices That Lack Evidence or May Cause Harm

When you encounter a practice, order, or tradition that lacks evidence, contradicts current guidelines, or may cause harm, raise it. Ask the team: "What is the evidence for this?" Consult the literature, pharmacy, the clinical nurse specialist, or the educator. Advocate for the evidence-based approach, and document your concern if the practice continues. Silent compliance with a harmful practice is a professional failure.

### Contribute to Practice Improvement and Inquiry

EBP is not only consuming evidence; it is generating and applying it. Participate in unit-based practice councils, quality improvement projects, journal clubs, and research where possible. Identify practice gaps (a recurring problem, a variation in care, an outdated policy) and work with the team to address them with evidence. Mentor newer nurses in questioning and inquiry.

### Distinguish Strong Evidence From Weak or Absent Evidence

Not all evidence is equal. Strong evidence (systematic reviews, large randomized trials, well-developed guidelines) warrants confident practice change. Weak evidence (single small studies, expert opinion, tradition) warrants caution and may not justify deviating from current practice. Understand levels of evidence and apply accordingly. The absence of evidence is not evidence of absence — some practices lack research because they have not been studied, and clinical judgment still applies.

### Integrate Patient Values Into Evidence-Based Decisions

Evidence tells you what works on average; it does not tell you what is right for this patient. Integrate the patient's values, goals, preferences, and circumstances into the evidence-based decision. A guideline that recommends aggressive treatment may not fit a patient prioritizing comfort; a standard follow-up interval may not fit a patient with transportation barriers. EBP includes the patient.

## Common Traps

**Perpetuating ritual practices because "that's how we've always done it."** A practice is handed down through generations of nurses and performed without question, even after the evidence has changed. The mechanism of harm is that outdated practices may be ineffective (wasting resources and time), may cause harm (restrictive practices, unnecessary interventions, prolonged NPO, certain wound rituals), and crowd out evidence-based care. The harm is suboptimal or harmful care, and a culture that resists improvement. Question practices; investigate the evidence; advocate for change.

**Treating guidelines as rigid rules rather than evidence integrated with judgment.** A nurse applies a guideline mechanically to every patient, regardless of context, because "the guideline says so." The mechanism of harm is that guidelines are developed for populations and may not fit individual patients whose circumstances, values, or comorbidities differ; rigid application can produce inappropriate or harmful care. The harm is care that is "by the book" but wrong for the patient. Integrate guidelines with clinical expertise and patient values; document the reasoning when you deviate.

**Letting competency decay without addressing it.** A nurse has not performed a skill in years but performs it when needed, without refresh, because it is "in their scope." The mechanism of harm is that competency decays without practice, and performing an unrefreshed skill increases error and harm risk. The harm is a procedure-related complication or error. Acknowledge competency gaps, refresh before performing, and seek validation; honesty about gaps is professional, not embarrassing.

**Relying on outdated knowledge from initial training.** A nurse's foundational knowledge was current at licensure but has not been updated, and they continue to practice based on what they learned years or decades ago. The mechanism of harm is that evidence and guidelines evolve, and practice based on outdated knowledge deviates from current standards, sometimes causing harm (e.g., outdated resuscitation techniques, outdated glucose targets, outdated wound care). The harm is substandard care. Commit to ongoing learning; read the guidelines in your area; engage with continuing education and professional organizations.

**Silent compliance with a practice you believe is wrong.** A nurse encounters a practice, order, or policy they believe lacks evidence or may cause harm, but does not raise it because of hierarchy, inertia, or fear of conflict. The mechanism of harm is that silence allows the practice to continue, and the patient is harmed by a practice that one nurse's question could have changed. The harm is preventable adverse events and a culture that suppresses inquiry. Raise concerns respectfully, consult the evidence and resources, and document if the practice continues.

**Overlooking the edge case or exception.** The mechanism of harm is that the agent analyzes the typical scenario thoroughly but skips the unusual case. The false signal is that the common path is well-handled; in reality the exception silently produces the wrong outcome because the boundary was never tested. The harm is a failure that only appears in the case nobody checked. The fix is to always ask what the edge case is and whether the conclusion still holds there.

## Self-Check

- Did I ground my practice in the best available evidence, integrated with my clinical expertise and the patient's values?
- Did I recognize and challenge ritual practices, investigating the evidence rather than defaulting to tradition?
- Did I stay current with the guidelines and evidence in my practice area through ongoing learning?
- Did I maintain and validate my competency in core skills and refresh before performing skills I have not done recently?
- Did I question practices, orders, or traditions that lack evidence or may cause harm, raising concerns respectfully?
- Did I distinguish strong evidence from weak or absent evidence when applying findings to practice?
- Did I integrate the patient's values, goals, and circumstances into evidence-based decisions?
- Did I participate in practice improvement, inquiry, or mentorship to advance unit practice?
- Did I acknowledge and address my own competency gaps honestly?
- Did I document my concerns and reasoning when practices deviated from evidence?
