# Scope of Practice and Delegation

Scope of practice and delegation are the boundaries that protect patients from care delivered by people not qualified to provide it, and protect nurses from being pushed beyond their legal authority and competency. These boundaries are defined by law (nurse practice acts), regulation, institutional policy, and individual competency, and they shift by setting and circumstance. The nurse's judgment is to know what they may and may not do, what they may and may not delegate, to match the delegated task to the right person and the right patient situation, and to refuse — respectfully and firmly — to practice outside scope. The most dangerous errors are silent overreach (doing something you are not qualified to do) and unsafe delegation (assigning a task to someone who cannot safely perform it, or to a patient situation that requires nursing judgment).

## Core Rules

### Know Your Legal Scope and Your Personal Competency — Both Must Be Satisfied

Legal scope (what the law permits a licensed nurse to do) is the ceiling; personal competency (what you have been trained, validated, and maintained proficiency to do) is the practical limit. A task may be legally within RN scope but outside your competency (e.g., a procedure you were never trained on), and you should not perform it. Conversely, competency does not override legal scope. Both conditions must be met.

### Delegation Requires the Five Rights

Delegation is not handing off a task; it is a clinical judgment that this task, with this patient, in this circumstance, can be safely performed by this person while you retain accountability. The Five Rights of Delegation: right task (within the scope of the delegate, does not require nursing judgment), right circumstance (patient condition is stable and predictable), right person (the delegate is competent and licensed/certified appropriately), right direction and communication (clear, specific instructions), and right supervision and evaluation (appropriate monitoring and follow-up). The nurse remains accountable for the delegation decision and the outcome.

### Reserve Nursing Judgment for the Nurse

Tasks that require nursing assessment, judgment, evaluation, or care plan modification cannot be delegated to unlicensed assistive personnel (UAP). UAP may perform routine tasks (vital signs on stable patients, ambulation, hygiene, feeding, toileting, basic measurements) but may not assess, interpret, triage, administer medications (in most settings), perform sterile procedures, or make clinical decisions. When a UAP reports a concern, the nurse must assess and interpret — the UAP's report is data, not the assessment.

### Match Delegation to Patient Stability

A task that is safe to delegate for a stable patient is unsafe for an unstable one. Taking vital signs can be delegated for a stable postoperative patient; it cannot be safely delegated for a patient on titrated vasoactive drips, where the nurse must observe and interpret the values in real time. Reassess the patient's stability before delegating, and pull the task back to the nurse when the patient's status changes.

### Floating: Practice Only Within Competency in the New Setting

When floated to an unfamiliar unit, you practice within your competency, not the unit's expectations. Orientation to the unit, the patient population, the equipment, and the emergency procedures is required; if it is not provided, ask for it and escalate if patient safety is at risk. Take a modified assignment appropriate to your familiarity. You cannot refuse a float in most circumstances, but you can and must decline specific tasks or patients for which you lack competency, and request support.

### Accepting Provider Orders: Verify Scope, Safety, and Clarity

When a provider orders a task, verify it is within your scope and competency, that the order is complete and clear, and that it is safe for this patient. If an order is unclear, incomplete, outside your scope, or appears unsafe, clarify with the provider before acting. Do not implement an order you do not understand or that seems wrong — clarify first.

### Refusing to Practice Outside Scope Is Professional Duty

If asked (or pressured) to perform a task outside your legal scope or competency, refuse — respectfully, with explanation, and with an offer to find the right person. Practicing outside scope exposes the patient to harm and exposes you to legal and professional liability, and it is never the right answer to a staffing or convenience problem. Document the request, the refusal, and the rationale.

### Document Delegation and Supervision

Document significant delegation decisions, the instructions given, the delegate's report-back, and your follow-up assessment and actions. The nurse's accountability for delegated care is reflected in the record.

## Common Traps

**Delegating a task that requires nursing judgment to UAP.** A nurse, pressed for time, asks a UAP to "check on the patient and let me know if they're okay," delegating what is actually an assessment. The mechanism of harm is that assessment and interpretation require nursing judgment, and a UAP may not recognize the significance of subtle findings (a slightly lower blood pressure, a change in mental status, a new symptom), reporting "they're fine" when the patient is deteriorating. The harm is a missed deterioration. Reserve assessment and interpretation for the nurse; delegate only data collection (vital signs, observations) with clear parameters for reporting, and always interpret the data yourself.

**Delegating to a stable patient circumstance and not revisiting when the patient destabilizes.** A task is delegated for a stable patient, the patient deteriorates, and the delegation continues because no one reassessed. The mechanism of harm is that delegation appropriateness depends on patient stability, and a task that was safe to delegate (vital signs, ambulation) becomes unsafe when the patient destabilizes, because the delegate cannot interpret the findings or respond. The harm is deterioration that goes unrecognized or unmanaged. Reassess the patient's stability throughout the shift and pull delegated tasks back to the nurse when the status changes.

**Performing a task outside competency because "someone has to do it."** A nurse is asked to perform a procedure or manage a device they were never trained on, and does it because no one else is available. The mechanism of harm is that lack of training and competency directly increases the risk of error and patient harm, and performing the task anyway exposes the patient to that risk and the nurse to liability. The harm is a procedure-related complication or error. Decline, explain, and escalate to obtain the right person — patient safety overrides the staffing gap.

**Floating and accepting a full assignment in an unfamiliar setting without orientation.** A nurse floats to a different unit, is given a full assignment, and proceeds without orientation to the unit, population, or equipment because "they need the help." The mechanism of harm is that even an experienced nurse lacks critical unit-specific knowledge (where supplies are, the patient population's specific risks, the unit's emergency procedures, the equipment), and full assignment in an unfamiliar setting increases error risk. The harm is errors, missed unit-specific risks, and patient harm. Request orientation, take a modified assignment, ask for support, and escalate if safety is at risk.

**Implementing an unclear or questionable provider order without clarifying.** A provider order is ambiguous or seems wrong, and the nurse implements it as interpreted rather than clarifying. The mechanism of harm is that ambiguity and error in orders are common, and implementing a misunderstood or wrong order directly causes harm (wrong drug, wrong dose, wrong route, contraindicated intervention). The harm is a medication or treatment error. Clarify any unclear, incomplete, or questionable order with the provider before acting; do not guess.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm that the task is within both my legal scope and my personal competency before performing it?
- Did I apply the Five Rights of Delegation (right task, circumstance, person, direction, supervision) before delegating?
- Did I reserve assessment, interpretation, and care plan modification for the nurse, delegating only data collection with clear reporting parameters?
- Did I reassess patient stability throughout the shift and pull delegated tasks back when the patient destabilized?
- Did I refuse to perform tasks outside my scope or competency, with explanation and escalation?
- When floating, did I request orientation, take a modified assignment, and escalate if safety was at risk?
- Did I clarify unclear, incomplete, or questionable provider orders before implementing them?
- Did I match the delegated task and the delegate to the patient's acuity and stability?
- Did I document significant delegation decisions, instructions, report-back, and my follow-up?
- Did I retain accountability for delegated care and follow up on the outcomes?
