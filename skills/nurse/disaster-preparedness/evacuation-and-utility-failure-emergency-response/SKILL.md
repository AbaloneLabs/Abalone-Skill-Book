# Evacuation and Utility Failure Emergency Response

Hospital infrastructure failures and internal disasters — fires, power outages, water and oxygen failures, IT downtimes, and the need to evacuate patients — place nurses in situations where the environment that normally supports care has failed, and the patient's survival depends on the nurse's emergency execution. The judgment problem is that evacuation and utility failure are rare, so the protocols are unfamiliar, and under stress the nurse must rapidly triage which patients need immediate manual support (ventilators without power, critical infusions, oxygen-dependent patients), execute the evacuation in the correct order, and maintain care standards with degraded resources. The skilled emergency nurse knows the protocols before the event, prioritizes by acuity, and executes calmly under conditions of uncertainty.

## Core Rules

### Know the Emergency Codes, the Evacuation Plan, and Your Role Before the Event

Emergency response depends on pre-existing knowledge, not improvisation. Know your facility's emergency codes (commonly color-coded: red for fire, gray for security, pink for infant abduction, orange for hazardous materials, and so on, though systems vary), the evacuation plan (the evacuation routes, the staging areas, the receiving units or facilities, the priority order), the shelter-in-place protocol, and your specific role in each. Know the location of fire alarms, extinguishers, oxygen shutoff valves, emergency power outlets, and evacuation equipment (evacuation chairs, sleds, stretchers). Walk the evacuation routes during orientation and periodically, because in smoke, darkness, or crowd, familiarity saves lives. The time to learn the plan is not during the emergency.

### Execute the Fire Response (RACE) Immediately and Correctly

For a fire, the standard response is RACE: Rescue anyone in immediate danger, Alarm (pull the alarm and call the code), Contain (close doors and windows to limit smoke and fire spread, shut off oxygen and medical gases if directed), and Extinguish (only if the fire is small and you are trained, with an appropriate extinguisher using PASS: pull, aim, squeeze, sweep) or Evacuate. The priority is life safety, then property. Close all doors to patient rooms to create compartments, move patients horizontally away from the fire first (to a smoke compartment on the same floor, which is faster and safer than vertical evacuation), and evacuate vertically only if horizontal relocation is insufficient. Do not use elevators during a fire. Know the oxygen shutoff valve location, because oxygen feeds fires, and shutting it off (per protocol, after ensuring dependent patients are managed) is a critical containment step.

### Prioritize Evacuation by Acuity and Dependency

When evacuation is required, prioritize by a system that accounts for acuity, dependency on life-sustaining equipment, and mobility. A common approach is to evacuate in this order: ambulatory patients first (they can move themselves quickly, freeing staff for the dependent), then less acute non-ambulatory patients, then the most critical and equipment-dependent patients last (because they require the most staff time and manual support, and they are best managed in their room with the door closed as a compartment until staff can fully extract them) — though the exact order depends on the threat (for an immediate threat like fire at the door, the order may invert). For each patient, ensure continuity of critical therapies: hand-ventilate the intubated patient with a bag-valve mask, secure critical infusions, bring portable oxygen, and bring the medications needed during the evacuation. Document the evacuation (who, when, to where) as it progresses.

### Manage Power Outages and Equipment Failure

Power outages, even with backup generators, place equipment-dependent patients at risk. Know which outlets are on emergency power (commonly red or marked), and ensure critical equipment (ventilators, IV pumps, monitors, dialysis machines) is plugged into emergency outlets so it survives the transfer to generator power. For equipment that does not have backup (some older devices, certain monitors), have a manual plan: bag-valve mask for ventilator failure, manual blood pressure for monitor failure, manual rate calculation for IV pump failure (count drops, document). Generator power takes seconds to transfer, during which equipment may reset — be ready to restart equipment and reconfirm settings. Report outages and equipment failures to the appropriate systems, and monitor generator fuel for prolonged outages.

### Respond to Water, Oxygen, Medical Gas, and IT Failures

Each utility failure has a specific response. Water failure affects hydration, hygiene, dialysis, equipment processing, and fire suppression — implement water conservation, arrange alternative water sources, and adjust care (postpone dialysis, use hand sanitizer for hand hygiene if water is unavailable, though this is suboptimal). Oxygen and medical gas failure affects ventilators, nebulizers, and patients on supplemental oxygen — switch to portable oxygen tanks, hand-ventilate as needed, and prioritize the most oxygen-dependent patients. IT and electronic health record downtime requires reverting to paper (downtime forms, manual orders, paper medication administration records) while maintaining safety standards (correct patient identification, allergy verification, the rights of medication administration); ensure the paper downtime kit is accessible and that staff know how to use it. For each failure, communicate with the incident command or engineering, document the impact on care, and revert to normal when systems are restored.

### Maintain Patient Safety and Care Standards During Degraded Operations

An emergency does not suspend the duty of care; it modifies the conditions. Maintain core safety standards even during degraded operations: correct patient identification (especially without the electronic record), medication safety (the rights of medication administration, even on paper), infection control (hand hygiene, even with water constraints), and documentation (on paper if necessary, transferred to the electronic record later). Communicate clearly with patients and families about the situation and the care plan, manage fear and confusion, and ensure vulnerable patients (confused, agitated, pediatric) are supervised during evacuation and sheltering. A patient harmed by a preventable error during an emergency is a second victim of the event.

### Participate in Drills, Debriefs, and System Improvement

Emergency response is a skill that degrades without practice. Participate actively in drills (fire drills, evacuation drills, downtime drills), treating them as real and identifying gaps. After any real event, participate in the debrief: what happened, what worked, what failed, what to change. Document your observations, advocate for system improvements (better-marked emergency outlets, clearer evacuation routes, more accessible downtime kits), and engage with the institutional learning. The drill and the debrief are how rare events become manageable.

## Common Traps

### Using the Elevator During a Fire or Evacuation

The trap is that the elevator is the fastest way to move a patient vertically, so the nurse, under pressure, uses it during a fire. The mechanism of harm is that elevators fail, recall to the fire floor, or trap occupants in smoke during a fire, and they are explicitly prohibited in fire response. The harm is death of patients and staff in an elevator that stops at the fire floor or loses power. Use stairs, evacuation sleds, or carry techniques for vertical evacuation, never the elevator, during a fire.

### Failing to Plug Critical Equipment into Emergency Power Outlets

The trap is that the nurse plugs the ventilator or IV pump into the nearest outlet, not the marked emergency outlet, because they look the same and the distinction seems trivial. The mechanism of harm is that during a power outage, only the emergency outlets transfer to generator power, and equipment on regular outlets loses power immediately — the ventilator stops, the infusion stops, the monitor goes dark. The harm is a life-threatening interruption in critical therapy during the outage. Plug all critical equipment into emergency (red or marked) outlets, and verify this during orientation and periodically.

### Evacuating the Most Critical Patients First, Slowing the Overall Evacuation

The trap is that the nurse, applying routine prioritization, evacuates the most critical and equipment-dependent patients first, spending extensive staff time on each while other patients wait. The mechanism of harm is that critical patient evacuation is slow and resource-intensive (hand ventilation, multiple staff, equipment), and committing all resources to the first critical patient delays the evacuation of many other patients who are exposed to the threat. The harm is preventable injury or death among the patients who waited. Use the disaster prioritization (ambulatory first, then less acute, then critical last, unless the immediate threat inverts the order), and move the population efficiently.

### Forgetting the Downtime Procedures and Paper Forms During IT Failure

The trap is that the electronic health record fails, and the nurse continues to try to use it or improvises documentation without the formal downtime forms, because the downtime kit is unfamiliar or inaccessible. The mechanism of harm is that care proceeds without a reliable record (medications given without allergy verification, orders missed, assessments undocumented), and the reconstruction after downtime is unreliable, leading to errors and omissions. The harm is patient harm from undocumented and uncoordinated care. Know the location of the downtime kit, the paper forms and processes, and the medication administration and order verification procedures during downtime, and use them from the first minute of the failure.

### Shutting Off Oxygen Without Considering Oxygen-Dependent Patients

The trap is that, during a fire, the nurse shuts off the zone oxygen valve to contain the fire, without checking for oxygen-dependent patients on the unit. The mechanism of harm is that shutting off oxygen abruptly endangers patients who depend on it (COPD on supplemental oxygen, ventilated patients on blended oxygen), and the fire-containment benefit may be outweighed by the immediate patient harm if patients are not first transitioned to portable oxygen. The harm is hypoxic injury or death of oxygen-dependent patients. Follow the protocol (which may require checking for dependent patients, transitioning to portable tanks, and notifying staff before shutoff), and coordinate the oxygen shutoff rather than acting unilaterally.

## Self-Check

- Do I know the emergency codes, the evacuation plan, the evacuation routes, and my role in each, before an event occurs?
- For a fire, can I execute RACE (rescue, alarm, contain, extinguish or evacuate), close doors to compartment, move patients horizontally first, and avoid the elevator?
- For evacuation, am I prioritizing by the disaster order (ambulatory first, then less acute, then critical last, unless the threat inverts), and ensuring continuity of critical therapies?
- Is all critical equipment plugged into emergency (marked) outlets, and do I have a manual plan for each device that lacks backup?
- For oxygen or medical gas failure, am I switching to portable tanks and prioritizing the most oxygen-dependent patients?
- For IT or EHR downtime, do I know where the downtime kit is and how to use the paper forms, medication administration, and order verification processes?
- During degraded operations, am I maintaining core safety standards (identification, medication rights, infection control, documentation)?
- Am I communicating clearly with patients and families about the situation and the care plan, and supervising vulnerable patients?
- For a fire, am I following the oxygen shutoff protocol and not acting unilaterally without considering oxygen-dependent patients?
- After the event or drill, will I participate in the debrief and advocate for system improvements?
