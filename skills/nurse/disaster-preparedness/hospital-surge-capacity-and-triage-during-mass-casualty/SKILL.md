# Hospital Surge Capacity and Triage During Mass Casualty

A mass casualty incident (MCI) or surge event overwhelms the normal capacity of the hospital, forcing the team to shift from individualized, maximum-care-for-each-patient thinking to population-based, greatest-good-for-the-greatest-number thinking. The judgment problem is that this shift is psychologically and ethically difficult for nurses trained to advocate for each patient, and the failure to make the shift — by over-resourcing the first critical patient at the expense of ten salvageable ones — costs lives in aggregate. Surge response also depends on systems (bed expansion, staffing, discharge, supply management) that the nurse must execute under pressure. The skilled disaster nurse triages by objective criteria, expands capacity systematically, and coordinates the population-level response while maintaining clinical care standards as far as possible.

## Core Rules

### Understand and Apply Disaster Triage Principles Distinct from Routine Triage

Disaster triage is fundamentally different from routine emergency triage. Routine triage prioritizes the sickest patients first (those who need immediate life-saving intervention). Disaster triage, when resources are overwhelmed, prioritizes patients who can survive with available intervention and assigns minimal resources to those unlikely to survive — the goal is to maximize the number of survivors, not to save every individual. Use a validated disaster triage system (such as START — Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment — which categorizes patients by ability to walk, respiratory rate, perfusion, and mental status into immediate, delayed, minimal, and expectant categories). Triage is rapid (under a minute per patient), objective, and reassessed as conditions and resources change. Understand the ethical framework (maximizing survival, distributive justice, duty to care) and the psychological weight of the decisions, and debrief afterward.

### Activate and Follow the Hospital Incident Command System

Every hospital has an incident command system (commonly the Hospital Incident Command System, HICS) that defines roles, authority, and communication during a disaster. Know your role in the system, the activation process, and your reporting chain. When an MCI is declared, the hospital shifts to incident command leadership, and routine reporting and decision-making structures are suspended — you report through the incident command chain, not the usual nursing hierarchy. Follow the activation steps: report to your assigned role, receive your assignment, communicate through the established channels, and document your actions. Clear command and communication prevent the chaos that kills in disasters.

### Expand Bed and Staffing Capacity Systematically

Surge capacity expansion is a planned, phased process, not an improvisation. Implement the surge plan: open additional units or convert existing spaces (post-anesthesia care units, procedural areas, hallways as needed) to inpatient capacity, discharge or transfer patients who can safely leave (elective admissions postponed, stable patients discharged early), and adjust staffing through extended shifts, called-in staff, agency staff, and cross-deployment of nurses to areas of need within their competency. Track bed availability in real time and communicate it to incident command. Know your institution's surge plan, your role in it, and the trigger criteria for each level of activation. Capacity expansion that is improvised rather than planned produces gaps and errors.

### Manage Patient Flow and Rapid Discharge to Create Capacity

Creating capacity requires aggressive patient flow management: rapid assessment and disposition of emergency patients, accelerated discharge of inpatients (with discharge teams mobilized to clear barriers), postponement of elective admissions and procedures, and transfer of stable patients to lower-acuity facilities or home with support. The nurse drives much of this by identifying patients ready for discharge, completing the discharge process efficiently, and communicating bed availability. During surge, the discharge threshold may shift (patients discharged slightly earlier than usual, with close follow-up), and the nurse must balance the individual patient's readiness against the population need for the bed. Document the discharge decisions and the follow-up arrangements.

### Conserve, Adapt, and Allocate Scarce Supplies and Equipment

During a severe surge, supplies and equipment (ventilators, oxygen, medications, personal protective equipment, staff time) may become scarce, and the institution will activate its scarce-resource allocation plan. Follow the institutional allocation framework (which is based on objective clinical criteria and ethical principles, not individual advocacy), conserve resources where possible (reuse according to protocol, minimize waste, substitute where safe), and adapt practices (alternative care models, scope-of-practice expansion within competency). Do not hoard supplies (which deprives other patients and staff), and do not unilaterally allocate scarce resources based on personal judgment — follow the institutional framework and report shortfalls to incident command. Scarce-resource situations are ethically fraught, and the framework exists to distribute the burden fairly.

### Maintain Clinical Care Standards Within the Constraints

A surge does not eliminate the duty of care; it modifies how care is delivered. Maintain core safety standards (correct patient identification, medication safety, infection control, documentation) even under pressure, because errors and infections compound the disaster. Adapt where necessary (extended intervals between assessments, group care activities, delegation to qualified team members) while preserving the elements that prevent harm. Communicate clearly with patients and families about the situation and the care plan, and maintain dignity and compassion even when care is constrained. The patient who is harmed by a preventable error during a surge is a second victim of the disaster.

### Coordinate Communication with Patients, Families, and the Public

During a disaster, communication is both a clinical and a public-safety function. Communicate with patients and families about the situation, the care plan, and the constraints honestly and compassionately, and manage expectations (wait times, visitor restrictions, altered care). Coordinate with the public information officer and incident command for consistent messaging, and avoid spreading unverified information. For families seeking loved ones, follow the institutional family reunification process. Document the communications, and recognize that clear, honest communication reduces fear and conflict during a chaotic event.

### Participate in the Debrief and the After-Action Review

After the event, participate in the debrief and the after-action review: what went well, what failed, what was learned, and what changes are needed. Document your observations and recommendations, and engage with the system improvements that follow. Disasters reveal both strengths and vulnerabilities, and the after-action process is how the institution learns and prepares for the next event. The nurse's frontline perspective is essential to a meaningful review.

## Common Traps

### Applying Routine Triage (Sickest First) During a Mass Casualty Event

The trap is that the nurse, trained in routine emergency triage, continues to prioritize the sickest patient first during an MCI, devoting extensive resources to a patient with minimal survival chance while salvageable patients wait. The mechanism of harm is that disaster triage inverts the routine priority — the goal is to maximize total survivors, and over-resourcing one critical patient at the expense of many salvageable ones reduces the total saved. The harm is preventable deaths among the patients who waited. Apply disaster triage principles (START or equivalent) when an MCI is declared, prioritize the immediate (salvageable with intervention) and delayed categories, and assign minimal resources to the expectant category with comfort care.

### Operating Outside the Incident Command Structure

The trap is that the nurse, accustomed to the routine nursing hierarchy, continues to report through the usual charge nurse and manager during a disaster, bypassing or duplicating the incident command structure. The mechanism of harm is that incident command depends on a single, clear chain of communication and authority to coordinate the response, and parallel reporting creates confusion, conflicting instructions, and gaps. The harm is a disorganized response that delays care and wastes resources. When incident command is activated, report through the disaster chain, follow the assigned role, and document through the established channels.

### Hoarding Supplies "Just in Case" During a Scarcity

The trap is that the nurse, anticipating scarcity, stockpiles supplies (PPE, medications, ventilator circuits) in their unit or at the bedside "just in case," beyond the immediate need. The mechanism of harm is that hoarding deprives other patients and units of the supplies they need now, and it disrupts the institutional allocation that distributes resources based on objective criteria. The harm is preventable harm to other patients and staff due to artificial scarcity created by hoarding. Use supplies per protocol, conserve where possible, request what you need through the system, and trust the institutional allocation framework rather than personal stockpiling.

### Discharging Patients Unsafely to Create Capacity

The trap is that the surge creates intense pressure for beds, so patients are discharged before they are clinically ready, without adequate follow-up, to free capacity. The mechanism of harm is that premature discharge causes adverse events (readmission, deterioration, complications) that compound the disaster by adding more patients to an already overwhelmed system. The harm is iatrogenic harm to the discharged patient and additional burden on the system. Discharge only patients who are clinically safe to go, with appropriate follow-up and clear instructions, even under pressure — communicate bed constraints to incident command rather than discharging unsafely.

### Failing to Document During the Chaos

The trap is that the pace and chaos of the surge make documentation feel impossible, so the nurse charts minimally or not at all, planning to reconstruct later. The mechanism of harm is that contemporaneous documentation is essential for patient safety (knowing what was given, what was done), for continuity across handoffs, and for the after-action review and any legal scrutiny. The harm is errors from poor information transfer, missed care, and an inability to reconstruct what happened. Document contemporaneously, even briefly (use disaster documentation tools if available, including triage tags and abbreviated records), and complete the documentation as soon as feasible.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- During a mass casualty event, am I applying disaster triage principles (START or equivalent) that maximize total survivors, rather than routine triage?
- Do I know my role in the hospital incident command system, the activation process, and my reporting chain?
- Is the surge plan being implemented systematically (opening additional capacity, accelerating discharge, adjusting staffing within competency)?
- Am I managing patient flow aggressively but safely, discharging only patients who are clinically ready with appropriate follow-up?
- For scarce resources, am I following the institutional allocation framework, conserving and adapting, and reporting shortfalls rather than hoarding?
- Am I maintaining core safety standards (identification, medication safety, infection control, documentation) even under pressure?
- Am I communicating honestly and compassionately with patients and families, and coordinating with the public information function?
- Am I documenting contemporaneously, even briefly, and completing the documentation as soon as feasible?
- For families seeking loved ones, am I following the institutional reunification process?
- After the event, will I participate in the debrief and after-action review, and contribute observations for system improvement?
