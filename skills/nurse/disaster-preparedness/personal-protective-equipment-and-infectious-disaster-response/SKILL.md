# Personal Protective Equipment and Infectious Disaster Response

An infectious disaster — a novel respiratory pandemic, a viral hemorrhagic fever, a deliberately released biological agent — turns personal protective equipment (PPE) from a routine precaution into the barrier between life and death for nurses, their families, and their other patients. The judgment problem is that PPE is uncomfortable, time-consuming, and easy to misuse, and the highest-risk step is not wearing PPE but removing it (doffing), when self-contamination is most likely. During an infectious surge, the nurse must select the correct PPE for the pathogen, don and doff with meticulous technique, sustain the discipline over long shifts and many encounters, and manage the broader infection-control response — all while providing care to frightened, isolated patients.

## Core Rules

### Match the PPE to the Pathogen's Transmission Route

Different pathogens spread by different routes, and the PPE must match the route. Contact transmission (norovirus, C. difficile, multidrug-resistant organisms, viral hemorrhagic fevers in body fluids) requires gown and gloves, with meticulous environmental cleaning. Droplet transmission (influenza, pertussis, meningococcal) requires a surgical mask and eye protection within the close-contact range. Airborne transmission (tuberculosis, measles, varicella, novel respiratory viruses like SARS-CoV-2 in aerosol-generating situations) requires a fit-tested N95 or higher respirator (or powered air-purifying respirator for high-risk procedures), eye protection, gown, and gloves, in a negative-pressure room when possible. For high-consequence pathogens (Ebola and other viral hemorrhagic fevers), full coverage with no skin exposure (PAPR or N95 with full hood, double gloves, impermeable gown, boot covers) and a trained observer for donning and doffing are required. Know the pathogen's route, the institutional protocol, and the correct PPE, and do not improvise.

### Don and Doff with Meticulous, Observed Technique

The donning and doffing sequence is where contamination happens, and it must be deliberate and correct. Don in the correct order (commonly gown, mask or respirator, goggles or face shield, gloves), ensuring the respirator is fit-checked (the user seal check) each time, the gown covers fully, and the gloves cover the gown cuffs. Doff in the correct order (commonly gloves, gown, eye protection, respirator last, because it is the most protective layer and must be removed outside the room or at the exit), performing hand hygiene between steps, never touching the front of any contaminated item (the front is contaminated; remove by the ties, straps, or inside). For high-consequence pathogens, a trained observer guides and watches every donning and doffing, because self-contamination is so easy and so dangerous. Practice the sequence until it is automatic, and never rush it under pressure.

### Perform Fit Testing and the User Seal Check for Respirators

An N95 respirator protects only if it fits and seals to the face, and facial hair, weight changes, dental work, and the wrong model all break the seal. Undergo fit testing at the required interval and when conditions change (new respirator model, facial changes). Perform the user seal check (positive and negative pressure check) every time you don an N95, before entering the patient area — if the seal fails, re-adjust or replace the respirator. A poorly fitting N95 gives a false sense of protection while allowing airborne pathogens in around the edges. Know your size and model, and do not substitute without fit testing.

### Manage Aerosol-Generating Procedures with Heightened Precautions

Aerosol-generating procedures (intubation, extubation, suctioning, nebulizer treatment, sputum induction, non-invasive ventilation, high-flow oxygen, resuscitation) create aerosols that transmit airborne pathogens far more efficiently than routine breathing, and they are high-risk moments for staff infection. Perform aerosol-generating procedures in a negative-pressure room when possible, with the minimum necessary staff present, all wearing N95 or PAPR and full PPE, and use closed-suction systems and ventilator filters where available. Schedule and cluster aerosol-generating procedures when possible, and avoid nebulizers (use metered-dose inhalers with spacers where feasible) in patients with airborne respiratory infections. Plan the procedure, prepare the equipment and PPE, and limit exposure time.

### Sustain PPE Discipline Over Long Shifts and Many Encounters

The challenge of PPE is not the single encounter but the sustained discipline over a shift with dozens of encounters, fatigue, heat, dehydration, and the temptation to cut corners. Plan your care to minimize entries (cluster activities, bring all needed supplies in one entry), stay hydrated and take breaks (which require doffing and re-donning, but are essential), monitor yourself and your colleagues for breaches and fatigue, and resist the normalization of deviation ("just this once without the eye protection"). PPE fatigue is a real phenomenon that drives infections in healthcare workers, and the response is system design (adequate supplies, rest breaks, buddy checks) plus individual discipline.

### Handle Contaminated Waste and Linen Safely

Waste and linen from patients with high-consequence pathogens are contaminated and must be handled and disposed of per protocol. For routine isolation, waste goes in standard regulated waste streams with appropriate containment. For high-consequence pathogens (viral hemorrhagic fevers), waste may require special handling, on-site treatment (autoclaving), and dedicated packaging, because the waste itself can transmit infection. Train on the waste protocol before the encounter, do not create unnecessary waste, double-bag securely at the point of generation, and avoid overfilling containers. Linen is handled with the same precautions, bagged at the point of use, and processed per protocol. Mishandled waste extends the contamination zone and endangers environmental services staff.

### Care for the Patient Behind the PPE

The PPE that protects the nurse also distances them from the patient, who is frightened, isolated, and often unable to see the nurse's face or hear them clearly. Compensate for the barrier: introduce yourself clearly, explain what you are doing before you do it, use touch deliberately (gloved touch still communicates presence), maintain eye contact (the only part of the face visible), and address the patient's fear and isolation. Patients in isolation for high-consequence pathogens experience significant psychological harm, and the nurse's presence and communication are part of the care. Document the care provided and the patient's response, and ensure the isolation does not become abandonment.

## Common Traps

### Touching the Front of the PPE During Doffing

The trap is that the front of the gown, gloves, mask, and eye protection is contaminated, and during doffing the nurse touches the front to remove or adjust an item, transferring the pathogen to their hands and then to their face or clean clothing. The mechanism of harm is that the front-contaminated surfaces are the source of the pathogen, and the careful doffing sequence (removing by ties, straps, rolling the gown inside out) is designed specifically to avoid touching the front; one touch breaks the protection. The harm is self-contamination and infection. Remove PPE by the clean surfaces only (ties, straps, inside), perform hand hygiene between steps, and use a trained observer for high-consequence pathogens.

### Skipping the User Seal Check on the N95 Because "It Was Fit-Tested"

The trap is that the nurse was fit-tested for the N95 model, so they assume each donning is sealed, and they skip the user seal check before entering. The mechanism of harm is that fit testing confirms the model fits under test conditions, but each individual donning can have a poor seal due to strap placement, facial movement, or a shifted mask, and the user seal check catches the failed seal at the moment of entry. The harm is exposure to airborne pathogens through an unsealed respirator that the nurse believes is protecting them. Perform the user seal check (positive and negative pressure) every time you don an N95, before entering the patient area.

### Using an N95 When a PAPR Is Required for a High-Consequence Pathogen

The trap is that the nurse, more comfortable with the N95, uses it for a high-consequence pathogen (such as a viral hemorrhagic fever) when the protocol requires a PAPR with full coverage and no skin exposure. The mechanism of harm is that high-consequence pathogens require no skin exposure and a higher level of respiratory protection than an N95 provides, and using inadequate PPE for a lethal, low-infectious-dose pathogen is a death-risking error. The harm is fatal occupational infection. Follow the institutional protocol for the specific pathogen, use the required PPE (PAPR, full coverage, trained observer) for high-consequence pathogens, and do not substitute down based on comfort.

### Performing Aerosol-Generating Procedures Without Heightened Precautions

The trap is that the nurse or team performs an intubation, suctioning, or nebulizer treatment on a patient with an airborne respiratory infection using only routine precautions, underestimating the aerosol risk. The mechanism of harm is that aerosol-generating procedures produce far more infectious aerosols than routine breathing, and they are the procedures during which healthcare workers most commonly become infected during respiratory outbreaks. The harm is occupational infection that spreads to colleagues, families, and other patients. Perform aerosol-generating procedures in a negative-pressure room when possible, with the minimum staff in full airborne PPE, using closed systems and filters, and plan the procedure to limit exposure.

### Cutting Corners on PPE Due to Fatigue, Heat, or Time Pressure

The trap is that the shift is long, the PPE is hot and uncomfortable, the unit is busy, and the nurse skips the eye protection, reuses a mask beyond its intended life, or enters without full PPE "just for a minute." The mechanism of harm is that these shortcuts, repeated over many encounters and many shifts, accumulate exposure, and a single unprotected exposure in a high-prevalence environment can transmit infection. The harm is occupational infection. Plan care to minimize entries, take breaks with proper doffing and re-donning, monitor yourself and colleagues for fatigue and breaches, and sustain the discipline — the system should support the discipline with adequate supplies, rest, and staffing.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- For the pathogen I am managing, do I know the transmission route and the correct PPE (contact, droplet, airborne, full-coverage for high-consequence)?
- Did I don in the correct order, fit-check the respirator with the user seal check, and ensure full coverage with no exposed skin?
- Will I doff in the correct order, removing by clean surfaces only, with hand hygiene between steps and a trained observer for high-consequence pathogens?
- For aerosol-generating procedures, am I in a negative-pressure room with minimum staff in full airborne PPE, using closed systems and filters?
- Am I clustering care to minimize entries, taking breaks with proper doffing and re-donning, and sustaining discipline over the shift?
- For high-consequence pathogens, am I using the required PAPR and full coverage, not substituting an N95 based on comfort?
- Am I handling waste and linen per protocol, double-bagging at the point of generation, and avoiding overfilling?
- Am I communicating clearly with the isolated patient, compensating for the PPE barrier, and addressing their fear and isolation?
- Did I document the care provided and the patient's response, ensuring isolation does not become abandonment?
- Am I monitoring myself and my colleagues for PPE fatigue and breaches, and reporting any exposure promptly?
