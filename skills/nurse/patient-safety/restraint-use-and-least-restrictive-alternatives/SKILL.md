# Restraint Use And Least Restrictive Alternatives

The judgment problem in restraint use is that restraints are sometimes genuinely necessary to prevent imminent harm (a delirious patient pulling at a life-sustaining line), but they are also easily misused for staff convenience or as a default response to agitation, and the harm of the restraint — injury, dignitary harm, prolonged agitation, death from asphyxiation or strangulation — can exceed the harm it was meant to prevent. The skilled nurse treats restraint as a last resort of last resorts, exhausts the least-restrictive alternatives first, secures proper authorization and monitoring when restraint is used, and continuously re-evaluates to discontinue at the earliest moment. The nurse also recognizes that chemical restraint (sedating medication given for behavior, not for a diagnosed condition) carries the same ethical and legal weight as physical restraint and is often less scrutinized.

This is a high-stakes domain. Restraint-related deaths and injuries are well-documented, and restraint use is heavily regulated. The nurse's role is to prevent the need for restraint, to use it correctly when indicated, to monitor continuously, and to advocate for discontinuation.

## Core Rules

### Treat Restraint As A Last Resort And Exhaust Alternatives First

Before any restraint, the least-restrictive interventions must be attempted and documented. Restraint is reserved for imminent danger that alternatives cannot manage.

- Identify and treat the underlying cause of agitation or behavior: pain, hypoxia, hypoglycemia, urinary retention, fecal impaction, medication effect, withdrawal, infection, sleep deprivation. Agitation is often a symptom, not a behavior to suppress.
- Try non-pharmacological alternatives: reorientation, presence of family, calming environment, distraction, sensory aids (glasses, hearing aids), one-to-one observation, bed in low position with mat, covering lines or devices to reduce pulling.
- Try pharmacological alternatives only when indicated for a diagnosed condition (e.g., treating delirium), not as a chemical restraint for convenience.

### Distinguish Physical From Chemical Restraint And Apply The Same Scrutiny

A physical restraint is any device or method that restricts the patient's freedom of movement and that the patient cannot easily remove. A chemical restraint is medication used to control behavior or restrict freedom of movement and not a standard treatment for the patient's condition.

- Both require the same clinical justification, authorization, monitoring, and time-limited use.
- Chemical restraint is more easily overlooked because it is "just a medication," but it carries the same ethical and legal weight and the same risks (oversedation, respiratory depression, falls, prolonged delirium).

### Secure Proper Authorization, Time Limits, And Monitoring

Restraints are not nursing decisions alone; they require a provider order with a specified timeframe, and they require continuous monitoring per policy.

- The provider order must specify the type, the indication, and the duration; restraints cannot be ordered PRN indefinitely.
- Time limits: restraints must be renewed per regulation (often every 4 hours for adults, more frequently for children), with face-to-face reassessment by the provider.
- Monitoring during restraint: circulation, skin integrity, range of motion, hydration, toileting, psychological status, and continued need — at the frequency policy requires (often every 15 minutes for physical restraints).
- One-to-one observation is required for the highest-risk restraints and for any patient in seclusion.

### Use The Least Restrictive Effective Restraint And Release Regularly

When restraint is necessary, use the least restrictive device that prevents the harmful behavior, and release for range of motion, toileting, hydration, and reassessment at defined intervals.

- The least restrictive effective device: mitts may suffice where wrist restraints would be excessive; a waist restraint may be needed only if limb restraints are inadequate.
- Release at least every two hours (per policy) for range of motion, skin check, toileting, and nutrition, and document the release.
- Never tie a restraint to a movable bed part (side rail), because movement can tighten it and cause injury or asphyxiation.

### Discontinue At The Earliest Moment And Re-Evaluate Continuously

Restraints tend to persist beyond the acute indication because they reduce staff burden, so active effort is required to discontinue them as soon as the danger resolves.

- Reassess the continued need at every renewal; if the patient is no longer pulling at lines or endangering themselves, discontinue.
- A trial of release with close observation often reveals that the restraint is no longer necessary.
- Document the discontinuation and the alternative plan that maintains safety.

### Recognize That Restraints Can Cause The Harm They Prevent

Restraints cause injury (bruising, skin breakdown, asphyxiation, strangulation), increase agitation and delirium, and are associated with longer lengths of stay and higher mortality. A patient restrained to prevent a fall may be injured more severely by the restraint than by a fall.

- The decision to restrain must weigh the harm of the restraint against the harm it prevents, and the restraint must be discontinued as soon as that balance shifts.

## Common Traps

### Using Restraints For Staff Convenience Or As A Default

A patient is restless at night, pulling at sheets but not at lines, and wrist restraints are applied so the staff can attend to other patients, framed as "for safety." The mechanism is that the restraint reduces the immediate staffing burden, so it is applied without a genuine imminent-danger indication, and the convenience is rationalized as protection. The harm is that the patient suffers the dignitary and physical harms of restraint without a clinical indication, the agitation often worsens, and the restraint persists because it is easier than the alternative (one-to-one observation, treating the cause). The defense is to require a genuine imminent-danger indication for restraint, to exhaust and document alternatives, to treat convenience as an explicit non-indication, and to escalate for additional staffing (one-to-one observation) rather than defaulting to restraint when the load is heavy.

### Failing To Identify And Treat The Underlying Cause Of Agitation

An agitated patient is restrained and sedated without investigation, when the agitation is caused by hypoxia, hypoglycemia, urinary retention, pain, or infection — each treatable — and the underlying physiological crisis continues under the restraint. The mechanism is that the behavior is treated as the problem rather than as a symptom, so the restraint suppresses the manifestation while the cause worsens, and the patient deteriorates from the untreated condition while appearing "calmer." The harm is that a reversible cause of agitation is missed, the patient is harmed by the untreated condition and by the restraint, and the deterioration is attributed to the original presentation rather than to the missed cause. The defense is to treat new agitation as a symptom requiring investigation — vital signs, glucose, oxygenation, pain, bladder, bowel, infection screen, medication review — before and alongside any restraint, and to recognize that sedating an agitated patient without identifying the cause can mask a life-threatening deterioration.

### Overlooking Chemical Restraint Because It Is "Just Medication"

A sedating medication is given to control behavior without a diagnosed condition, and it is not documented or managed as a restraint because it was "for anxiety" or "for sleep," when the actual purpose was behavioral control. The mechanism is that chemical restraint is less visible than physical restraint and is easily rationalized as treatment, so it escapes the scrutiny, authorization, and monitoring that physical restraints receive, while carrying the same risks and ethical weight. The harm is oversedation, respiratory depression, falls, prolonged delirium, and dignitary harm, all without the safeguards that would have been applied to a physical restraint. The defense is to apply the same scrutiny to sedating medications given for behavior: distinguish treatment of a diagnosed condition from chemical restraint, secure authorization and monitoring when the purpose is behavioral control, and recognize that a medication is a restraint if its purpose is to restrict movement or control behavior rather than to treat a condition.

### Continuing Restraints Beyond The Acute Indication

A patient was appropriately restrained for pulling at a central line during delirium, the delirium resolves, but the restraints continue because they are in place and no one has actively trialed discontinuation, and the patient remains restrained for days. The mechanism is that restraints reduce staff burden and their continuation is the path of least resistance, so the active effort required to discontinue (trial of release, close observation, alternative plan) is not undertaken, and the restraint persists beyond the acute indication. The harm is prolonged restraint with its associated complications (deconditioning, skin breakdown, psychological harm, prolonged delirium) in a patient who no longer needs it. The defense is to reassess the continued need at every renewal, to trial discontinuation with close observation at defined intervals, to treat continuation as an active decision requiring justification rather than a default, and to recognize that a restraint that is not being actively discontinued is a restraint that is being over-used.

### Improper Tying Or Monitoring That Causes Injury

A wrist restraint is tied to a movable side rail, the rail is raised or lowered, and the restraint tightens, causing injury or asphyxiation; or a restraint is applied too tightly and the circulation is compromised without detection because monitoring was inadequate. The mechanism is that the physical application of the restraint (the knot, the attachment point, the tightness) determines whether it is safe, and errors in application combined with inadequate monitoring produce injury that the restraint was meant to prevent. The harm is direct physical injury — nerve damage, ischemia, skin breakdown, asphyxiation — from a device intended to protect. The defense is to apply restraints per technique (quick-release knot, secure to a non-movable bed frame, two-finger tightness), to never tie to a movable part, to monitor circulation, skin, and range of motion at the required frequency, and to recognize that the application and monitoring are as safety-critical as the decision to restrain.

### Documenting Restraint Care As A Form Rather Than Genuine Monitoring

The 15-minute monitoring is charted as a series of checkmarks without genuine assessment, and a circulation compromise, skin breakdown, or psychological crisis goes undetected because the monitoring was a documentation task rather than an assessment. The mechanism is that the frequency of required monitoring makes it feel burdensome, so it is performed as a charting ritual rather than as assessment, and the complications that the monitoring was meant to detect develop between the checkmarks. The harm is restraint-related injury in a patient whose chart shows compliant monitoring. The defense is to perform genuine assessment at each monitoring interval — circulation, skin, range of motion, hydration, toileting, psychological status, continued need — and to document the findings, recognizing that monitoring is the safeguard that makes time-limited restraint acceptable and that ritual monitoring provides no safety.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Before any restraint, did I identify and treat the underlying cause of the agitation (pain, hypoxia, glucose, retention, infection, medication) and exhaust least-restrictive alternatives, documenting what was tried?
- [ ] Did I distinguish physical from chemical restraint and apply the same scrutiny, authorization, and monitoring to sedating medications given for behavioral control?
- [ ] Did I secure a provider order specifying the type, indication, and duration, and renew within regulatory time limits with face-to-face provider reassessment?
- [ ] Did I use the least restrictive effective device and release at least every two hours for range of motion, skin check, toileting, and nutrition?
- [ ] Did I apply the restraint correctly (quick-release knot, non-movable bed frame, two-finger tightness) and never tie to a movable side rail?
- [ ] Did I monitor circulation, skin, range of motion, hydration, toileting, and psychological status at the required frequency (often every 15 minutes), performing genuine assessment rather than ritual charting?
- [ ] Did I reassess the continued need at every renewal and trial discontinuation with close observation, treating continuation as an active decision rather than a default?
- [ ] Did I provide one-to-one observation for the highest-risk restraints and seclusion, and escalate for additional staffing rather than defaulting to restraint?
