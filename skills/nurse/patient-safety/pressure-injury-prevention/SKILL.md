# Pressure Injury Prevention

The judgment problem in pressure injury prevention is that the injury develops where it is not looked at — under devices, on the sacrum and heels of patients who are not turned, beneath the occiput of a supine patient — and the prevention is often reduced to a turning schedule that is documented but not genuinely performed, or performed without attention to the surfaces, the devices, and the nutrition that determine whether pressure is actually relieved. The skilled nurse understands that pressure injury prevention is a bundle: surface, offloading, repositioning, skin care, moisture management, and nutrition, and that any one element performed well does not compensate for the others being neglected. They also understand that staging is a judgment that determines whether an injury is attributed to the facility or was present on admission, and that incorrect staging has clinical, financial, and legal consequences.

This is a high-stakes domain. Pressure injuries are largely preventable harm, and hospital-acquired pressure injuries are a quality indicator. The nurse's role is to assess risk, implement the prevention bundle, inspect all high-risk areas, stage accurately, and escalate wounds that exceed nursing management.

## Core Rules

### Assess Risk With A Validated Tool And Implement The Bundle For At-Risk Patients

Validated tools (Braden, Norton) stratify risk based on sensory perception, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, and friction/shear. A patient at risk receives the full prevention bundle, not a single intervention.

- Score at admission and when condition changes; a patient who becomes immobile, hypotensive, or poorly nourished moves up the risk scale.
- The bundle: pressure-redistribution surface (appropriate mattress), scheduled repositioning (individualized frequency), offloading of heels, skin and moisture management, and nutritional support.

### Inspect All High-Risk Areas At Every Assessment, Including Under Devices

Pressure injuries develop over bony prominences and under medical devices. Inspecting only what is visible misses the highest-risk areas.

- High-risk areas: sacrum/coccyx, heels, ischial tuberosities, greater trochanters, occiput, ears, elbows, and under devices (oxygen masks, splints, casts, compression devices, catheters, endotracheal tube securement).
- Turn the patient to inspect the back and sacrum; lift the heels and inspect them; remove or reposition devices to inspect underneath.
- Documenting "skin intact" without inspecting these areas is a falsification that hides developing injury.

### Offload Heels Specifically

Heel pressure injuries are common and severe because the heel has a small contact area over the calcaneus and little soft tissue. Offloading the heels is distinct from general repositioning.

- Use pillows or heel-elevating devices to float the heels off the mattress.
- Heel offloading must be verified each time the patient is repositioned; pillows shift.

### Reposition With Attention To Surface, Shear, And Frequency

Repositioning is not just turning; it is relieving pressure on the at-risk areas while minimizing shear (the sliding friction that injures tissue).

- Frequency is individualized: every 2 hours is a default, but high-risk patients may need more frequent repositioning, and the position must genuinely relieve pressure on the previously loaded area, not just rotate through positions that all load the sacrum.
- Use draw sheets and proper technique to lift rather than drag the patient, reducing shear.
- Even small shifts in position (micro-shifts) relieve pressure; teach the mobile patient to shift frequently.

### Manage Moisture And Skin Integrity

Moisture from incontinence, perspiration, or wound drainage macerates skin and accelerates breakdown. Dry, intact skin resists injury.

- Manage incontinence promptly with appropriate containment and skin barriers; do not leave the patient on wet linens or incontinent briefs.
- Use barrier creams on at-risk areas; avoid over-drying or harsh soaps.
- Inspect skin under incontinence products and devices, where moisture and pressure combine.

### Address Nutrition Because Pressure Injury Prevention Requires Tissue Integrity

Malnutrition, dehydration, and protein deficit impair tissue tolerance and healing. A patient who is repositioned perfectly but starved will still develop pressure injuries.

- Assess nutritional status and advocate for nutritional support (dietitian referral, oral supplements, enteral feeding) for at-risk patients.
- Hydration supports skin turgor and perfusion; dehydration increases risk.

### Stage Accurately And Distinguish Present-On-Admission From Hospital-Acquired

Staging (Category/Stage 1 through 4, plus unstageable and deep tissue pressure injury) describes the depth and tissue involvement. Correct staging guides treatment and determines whether the injury is attributed to the facility.

- A Stage 1 is non-blanchable erythema of intact skin; a Stage 2 is partial-thickness loss; Stage 3 is full-thickness loss; Stage 4 is full-thickness with bone, tendon, or muscle visible; unstageable is obscured by slough or eschar; deep tissue pressure injury is persistent non-blanchable deep red or purple.
- Document a baseline skin assessment on admission so that injuries found later can be correctly classified as present-on-admission or hospital-acquired.
- Do not reverse-stage a healing pressure injury; it does not heal through the stages in reverse but fills with granulation tissue.

## Common Traps

### Documenting "Skin Intact" Without Inspecting High-Risk Areas

The nurse documents "skin intact" based on the visible anterior surfaces, without turning the patient to inspect the sacrum, without lifting the heels, and without removing devices to inspect underneath, and a developing sacral or heel pressure injury goes undetected. The mechanism is that the high-risk areas require effort and assistance to inspect, so the documented assessment reflects only the easily visible surfaces, and the injuries develop exactly where no one looked. The harm is that the earliest, most treatable stage (non-blanchable erythema) is missed, the injury progresses to deeper stages, and it is discovered late when it is harder to treat and is classified as hospital-acquired harm. The defense is to inspect all high-risk areas at every assessment, to turn the patient and lift the heels, to remove and reposition devices to inspect underneath, and to document only what was actually inspected, because a "skin intact" entry for an uninspected area is a falsification that hides harm.

### Treating The Turning Schedule As Documentation Rather Than Care

The turning schedule is documented every two hours, but the turns are performed hurriedly, incompletely, or not at all, and the documentation asserts care that did not genuinely relieve pressure. The mechanism is that the documentation requirement is satisfied by the chart entry, while the actual care is compressed or skipped under time pressure, and the record falsely asserts that pressure was relieved on the schedule it claims. The harm is that the patient develops a pressure injury despite a chart that shows perfect turning, and the injury is both a clinical harm and a quality failure that the documentation concealed. The defense is to perform the turn genuinely — repositioning to relieve the loaded area, offloading heels, managing shear — and to document only what was done, to recognize that the schedule is a floor not a ceiling for high-risk patients, and to treat the documentation as a record of care rather than a substitute for it.

### Ignoring Device-Related Pressure

A patient on bilateral sequential compression devices, a tight oxygen mask, a splinted limb, or an endotracheal tube develops pressure injuries under the device, because the device was not removed or repositioned to inspect and relieve the underlying skin. The mechanism is that the device is treated as part of the treatment rather than as a pressure source, so the skin beneath it is not inspected, and the pressure and moisture accumulate unseen. The harm is device-related pressure injuries that are preventable with routine inspection and repositioning, often at sites (ears, chin, nasal bridge, calves) that are not on the standard turning checklist. The defense is to inspect under all medical devices at every assessment, to reposition or pad devices to relieve pressure, to remove devices when clinically possible for skin checks, and to recognize that any device in contact with skin is a pressure source requiring active management.

### Failing To Offload Heels Distinctly

The patient is turned side-to-side but the heels remain in contact with the mattress throughout, because general repositioning is assumed to address the heels, and heel pressure injuries develop because the calcaneus was never offloaded. The mechanism is that the heels have a small contact area and the supine and lateral positions both load them, so turning the body does not relieve heel pressure unless the heels are specifically floated, and the assumption that turning addresses the heels leaves them continuously loaded. The harm is severe heel pressure injuries, which are difficult to heal and often progress to bone, in patients whose sacral care was excellent. The defense is to offload the heels specifically with pillows or devices, to verify the offloading at every repositioning, and to recognize that heel care is a distinct element of the bundle, not a byproduct of turning.

### Under-Nutrition Hidden Behind Good Surface And Turning Care

A patient on a pressure-redistribution surface with a perfect turning schedule develops a pressure injury because their protein intake is inadequate and their tissue cannot withstand or repair pressure, but the nutritional deficit was not assessed or addressed. The mechanism is that the visible elements of the bundle (surface, turning) are attended to while the metabolic element (nutrition, hydration) is neglected, and the tissue breaks down because it lacks the substrate to tolerate pressure. The harm is a pressure injury in a patient who received excellent mechanical prevention but poor nutritional support, and the injury is attributed to the surface or the turning when the actual gap was nutritional. The defense is to assess nutritional status and hydration as part of the risk assessment, to advocate for dietitian referral and nutritional support for at-risk patients, and to recognize that pressure injury prevention is metabolic as well as mechanical.

### Reverse-Staging A Healing Pressure Injury

A Stage 4 pressure injury is healing with granulation tissue, and the nurse re-stages it as a Stage 2 as it fills in, because it appears shallower, creating an inaccurate record that confuses tracking and billing. The mechanism is that the healing pressure injury fills with granulation tissue from the base, so it appears to move through the stages in reverse, but the original tissue loss (muscle, fat) is replaced by scar, not by the original tissue, so the numerical stage does not reverse. The harm is inaccurate staging that confuses the clinical record, misrepresents the injury's severity to subsequent caregivers, and creates documentation errors with quality and billing implications. The defense is to never reverse-stage a healing pressure injury, to document the healing progress descriptively (granulation, reduction in depth, closure) rather than by changing the numerical stage, and to recognize that a healing Stage 4 remains a Stage 4 until closed.
