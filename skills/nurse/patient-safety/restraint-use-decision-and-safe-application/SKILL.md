# Restraint Use Decision And Safe Application

The decision to restrain a patient is a clinical judgment with two failure modes that point in opposite directions: restraining too readily, which inflicts dignitary and physical harm for staff convenience, and restraining too late, which allows a delirious patient to pull out a life-sustaining line. The judgment problem is that the same behavior — a patient pulling at their central line — may require restraint in one case (genuine imminent danger, alternatives exhausted) and prohibit it in another (untreated pain or hypoxia driving the agitation, restraint applied to suppress the symptom while the cause worsens). The skilled nurse treats restraint as a time-limited, last-resort intervention with a specific clinical indication, applies it with technique that prevents the restraint itself from causing asphyxiation or ischemia, monitors the patient for the harms that restraint produces, and works actively to discontinue it at the earliest moment rather than allowing it to persist because it reduces staffing burden. The decision and the application are equally safety-critical: a correctly indicated restraint tied to a movable bed rail can kill, and an incorrectly indicated restraint applied perfectly still harms the patient without clinical justification.

This is a high-stakes domain. Restraint-related deaths from asphyxiation and strangulation are well documented, and restraint use is heavily regulated. The nurse's role is to make the indication explicit, to apply the restraint safely, to monitor continuously, and to advocate for discontinuation.

## Core Rules

### Make The Clinical Indication Explicit Before Applying Any Restraint

Restraint requires a specific clinical indication — imminent danger to self or others that less-restrictive interventions have not managed — documented and ordered. The absence of an explicit indication is the most common failure, because restraint is often applied under the vague framing of "for safety" when the actual driver is staffing load or the patient's disruptiveness.

- The indication must be a concrete, imminent danger: pulling at a life-sustaining line (central line, endotracheal tube, chest tube), striking staff, climbing out of bed in a way that risks injury. "Agitation" or "confusion" alone is not an indication.
- Distinguish the indication that justifies restraint from the underlying cause that requires treatment. A patient pulling at lines because of hypoxia, hypoglycemia, pain, urinary retention, or medication effect has a treatable cause; restraining them suppresses the behavior while the cause worsens.
- Secure a provider order specifying the type, the indication, and the duration, before or immediately after application in an emergency; restraints cannot be ordered PRN or indefinitely.

### Exhaust And Document Least-Restrictive Alternatives First

Restraint is reserved for the situation where alternatives have been tried and failed, or where the danger is too immediate to permit them. Documenting what was tried is what distinguishes a justified restraint from a default response.

- Investigate and treat reversible causes first: vital signs, glucose, oxygenation, pain, bladder, bowel, infection screen, medication review, withdrawal.
- Try non-pharmacological alternatives: reorientation, family presence, calming environment, sensory aids (glasses, hearing aids), one-to-one observation, bed in low position with a mat, covering lines or devices to reduce pulling, diversion.
- Try pharmacological alternatives only when indicated for a diagnosed condition (treating delirium, treating anxiety as a condition), not as a chemical restraint for behavior.

### Select The Least Restrictive Effective Device

When restraint is indicated, use the least restrictive device that prevents the specific dangerous behavior. Over-restraining — using a more restrictive device than necessary — inflicts harm without additional safety.

- Match the device to the behavior: mitts or hand-control mitts may suffice where wrist restraints would be excessive; a vest restraint may be needed only if limb restraints are inadequate; a waist or belt restraint only when the patient is at risk of falling or climbing out.
- Avoid full limb restraint when a single limb or mitt would prevent the behavior.
- For each device, know the specific risk: vest restraints carry asphyxiation risk, wrist and ankle restraints carry ischemia and nerve-injury risk, mitten restraints carry less risk but may not prevent determined line-pulling.

### Apply The Restraint With Technique That Prevents Injury

The physical application of the restraint determines whether it is safe, and errors in application cause the injuries and deaths that restraints are meant to prevent. Apply per technique, every time.

- Use a quick-release knot, never a knot that cannot be released instantly in an emergency.
- Secure the restraint to a non-movable part of the bed frame, never to a side rail or any part that moves when the bed is adjusted, because movement can tighten the restraint and cause asphyxiation or strangulation.
- Apply with two-finger tightness: tight enough that the patient cannot slip out, loose enough that two fingers fit between the restraint and the skin, preserving circulation.
- Position to preserve circulation and range of motion, and check the restraint cannot ride up into the neck or axilla (vest restraints especially).

### Monitor At The Frequency Policy Requires, With Genuine Assessment

Monitoring during restraint is the safeguard that makes time-limited use acceptable, and it must be genuine assessment, not ritual charting. The frequency is set by policy and regulation (often every 15 minutes for physical restraints, continuously for the highest-risk patients and seclusion).

- At each monitoring interval, assess circulation (color, warmth, capillary refill, pulses distal to the restraint), skin integrity under and around the restraint, range of motion, hydration and nutrition, toileting needs, psychological status, and the continued need for the restraint.
- Release at least every two hours (per policy) for range of motion, skin care, toileting, hydration, and reassessment, and document the release.
- Provide one-to-one observation for the highest-risk restraints and for any patient in seclusion.

### Discontinue At The Earliest Moment, Treating Continuation As An Active Decision

Restraints persist beyond their indication because they reduce staffing burden, and the active effort required to discontinue (trial of release, close observation, alternative plan) is not undertaken. Treat continuation as a decision requiring justification, not as a default.

- Reassess the continued need at every renewal; if the patient is no longer pulling at lines or endangering themselves, discontinue.
- Trial release with close observation at defined intervals; a trial often reveals the restraint is no longer necessary.
- When the underlying cause of the agitation is treated (the delirium resolves, the pain is controlled), discontinue the restraint as part of treating the cause.

### Apply The Same Scrutiny To Chemical Restraint

A chemical restraint — sedating medication given to control behavior rather than to treat a diagnosed condition — carries the same ethical and legal weight as a physical restraint and is often less scrutinized because it is "just a medication." Apply the same indication, authorization, monitoring, and time-limited use.

- Distinguish treatment of a diagnosed condition (treating delirium with an antipsychotic, treating anxiety as a condition) from chemical restraint (sedating a patient for behavior without a diagnosed indication).
- When the purpose is behavioral control, secure the same authorization and monitoring as a physical restraint.
- Monitor for the specific risks: oversedation, respiratory depression (especially combined with opioids), falls, prolonged delirium, hypotension.

## Common Traps

### Applying Restraint For Staff Convenience Framed As Safety

A patient is restless at night, pulling at sheets but not at lines, and wrist restraints are applied so the nurse can attend to other patients, framed in the chart as "for safety," when the actual driver is staffing load and the patient is not in imminent danger. The mechanism is that the restraint reduces the immediate staffing burden, so it is applied without a genuine imminent-danger indication, and the convenience is rationalized retrospectively as protection. The harm is that the patient suffers the dignitary and physical harms of restraint — skin breakdown, deconditioning, worsened agitation, psychological trauma, and in rare cases asphyxiation — without a clinical indication, and the restraint tends to persist because it is easier than the alternative of one-to-one observation. The defense is to require a concrete imminent-danger indication, to exhaust and document alternatives, to treat staff convenience as an explicit non-indication, and to escalate for additional staffing rather than defaulting to restraint when the load is heavy, because a restraint applied for convenience is an injury inflicted to solve a staffing problem.

### Restraining To Suppress Agitation While The Underlying Cause Worsens

An agitated patient is restrained and sedated without investigation, and the agitation — caused by hypoxia, hypoglycemia, urinary retention, pain, infection, or medication effect — continues to worsen under the restraint while the patient appears calmer, until the untreated physiological crisis declares itself as a deterioration or arrest. The mechanism is that the behavior is treated as the problem rather than as a symptom, so the restraint and sedation suppress the manifestation while the cause progresses, and the patient's apparent calm is read as improvement rather than as the masking of deterioration. The harm is that a reversible, treatable cause of agitation is missed, the patient is harmed by the untreated condition and by the restraint, and the deterioration is attributed to the original presentation rather than to the missed cause. The defense is to treat new agitation as a symptom requiring investigation — vital signs, glucose, oxygenation, pain, bladder, bowel, infection screen, medication review — before and alongside any restraint, and to recognize that sedating an agitated patient without identifying the cause can mask a life-threatening deterioration that the agitation was signaling.

### Tying To A Movable Bed Part Causing Asphyxiation Or Strangulation

A wrist or vest restraint is tied to a side rail, the rail is raised or lowered, or the bed is repositioned, and the restraint tightens or shifts into the neck, producing asphyxiation or strangulation in a patient who cannot free themselves and whose distress may be attributed to agitation rather than to the restraint. The mechanism is that the attachment point determines whether the restraint is safe, and a movable part can change tension unpredictably under routine bed adjustment, so a restraint that was safe when applied becomes lethal when the bed moves. The harm is a restraint-related death from a device intended to protect, in a patient whose monitoring may not distinguish restraint asphyxiation from the agitation that prompted the restraint. The defense is to secure every restraint to a non-movable part of the bed frame with a quick-release knot, never to a side rail or adjustable part, to verify the attachment point after any bed adjustment, and to recognize that the attachment point is as safety-critical as the decision to restrain, because the physics of the tie, not the clinical indication, is what kills restrained patients.

### Ritual Monitoring That Charts Without Assessing

The required 15-minute monitoring is charted as a series of checkmarks without genuine assessment, and a circulation compromise, skin breakdown, or psychological crisis develops between the checkmarks and goes undetected, because the monitoring was a documentation task performed from the doorway rather than an assessment performed at the bedside. The mechanism is that the frequency of required monitoring makes it feel burdensome, so it is performed as a charting ritual rather than as assessment, and the complications that the monitoring was meant to detect develop in the gaps between compliant-looking checkmarks. The harm is restraint-related injury — ischemia, skin breakdown, nerve damage, psychological trauma — in a patient whose chart shows compliant monitoring. The defense is to perform genuine assessment at each interval — circulation, skin, range of motion, hydration, toileting, psychological status, continued need — to document the findings rather than the checkmarks, and to recognize that ritual monitoring provides no safety because it does not perform the assessment that would catch the complication.

### Continuing The Restraint Beyond The Acute Indication

A patient was appropriately restrained for pulling at a central line during delirium, the delirium resolves over the next day, but the restraints continue because they are in place and no one has actively trialed discontinuation, and the patient remains restrained for days, accumulating deconditioning, skin breakdown, and psychological harm. The mechanism is that restraints reduce staffing burden, so their continuation is the path of least resistance, and the active effort required to discontinue — trial of release, close observation, alternative plan — is not undertaken because the restraint is not causing an acute problem visible to the staff. The harm is prolonged restraint with its associated complications in a patient who no longer meets the indication, and a documented duration that far exceeds the clinical justification. The defense is to reassess the continued need at every renewal, to trial discontinuation with close observation at defined intervals, to treat continuation as an active decision requiring current justification rather than a default, and to recognize that a restraint that is not being actively discontinued is a restraint that is being overused.

### Overlooking Chemical Restraint Because It Is "Just Medication"

A sedating medication is given to control behavior without a diagnosed condition, and it is not documented or managed as a restraint because it was charted as "for anxiety" or "for sleep," when the actual purpose was behavioral control, and the patient suffers oversedation, respiratory depression, or a fall without the safeguards that would have been applied to a physical restraint. The mechanism is that chemical restraint is less visible than physical restraint and is easily rationalized as treatment, so it escapes the authorization, monitoring, and time-limiting that physical restraints receive, while carrying the same ethical weight and comparable physical risk. The harm is oversedation, respiratory depression (especially combined with opioids), falls, prolonged delirium, and dignitary harm, all without the safeguards, because the intervention was framed as medication rather than as restraint. The defense is to apply the same scrutiny to sedating medications given for behavior: distinguish treatment of a diagnosed condition from chemical restraint, secure authorization and monitoring when the purpose is behavioral control, and recognize that a medication is a restraint if its purpose is to restrict movement or control behavior rather than to treat a condition.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Before applying any restraint, did I make the clinical indication explicit (concrete imminent danger) and distinguish it from the underlying cause requiring treatment?
- [ ] Did I investigate and treat reversible causes of agitation (hypoxia, glucose, pain, retention, infection, medication) and exhaust and document least-restrictive alternatives?
- [ ] Did I select the least restrictive effective device, matching the device to the specific behavior rather than over-restraining?
- [ ] Did I apply the restraint with a quick-release knot, secured to a non-movable part of the bed frame (never a side rail), at two-finger tightness, positioned to preserve circulation?
- [ ] Did I monitor at the required frequency with genuine assessment of circulation, skin, range of motion, hydration, toileting, psychological status, and continued need — not ritual checkmarks?
- [ ] Did I release at least every two hours for range of motion, skin care, toileting, and reassessment, and provide one-to-one observation for the highest-risk restraints and seclusion?
- [ ] Did I reassess the continued need at every renewal, trial discontinuation with close observation, and treat continuation as an active decision rather than a default?
- [ ] Did I apply the same scrutiny, authorization, and monitoring to sedating medications given for behavioral control (chemical restraint), distinguishing them from treatment of a diagnosed condition?
