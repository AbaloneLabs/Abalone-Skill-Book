# Fall Risk Assessment And Prevention

The judgment problem in fall prevention is that the population at risk is broad (most hospitalized adults score as fall risk), which degrades the screening tool into a checkbox that triggers the same generic interventions for everyone, while the patients who actually fall are a specific subset whose risk factors — new orthostasis, delirium, recent sedation, urgency to toilet alone at night — require individualized, targeted interventions. The second failure mode is the opposite: the patient who does not look like a fall risk (young, mobile, "independent") is under-protected because their specific risk (post-anesthesia, new medication, sleep deprivation) is not recognized. The skilled nurse uses the validated tool as a starting point, then individualizes the intervention to the actual modifiable risk factors, and resists both the uniform "everyone is a fall risk" approach that protects no one and the "they seem fine" approach that misses the hidden risk.

This is a high-stakes domain. Falls cause injury, prolonged stays, and death in the elderly. The nurse's role is to assess risk accurately, implement individualized prevention, and balance fall prevention against the harm of restricted mobility and inappropriate restraint.

## Core Rules

### Use The Validated Tool As A Starting Point, Then Individualize

Validated fall risk tools (Morse, Hendrich II, Schmid) stratify risk using factors such as history of falls, mobility, mental status, and medications. They are screening tools, not care plans.

- Score every patient at admission and when condition changes, but do not stop at the score.
- Identify the specific, modifiable risk factors for this patient: orthostatic hypotension, delirium, frequent toileting needs, new sedating medications, impaired vision, weak lower extremities, IV tubing and lines that tangle.
- Target interventions to the specific factors: scheduled toileting for urgency, orthostatic precautions for hypotension, delirium management for confusion, line management for entanglement.

### Address The Modifiable Risk Factors Specifically

Generic interventions (bed alarm, nonslip socks, bed in low position) are baseline; the high-yield interventions target the specific factor driving the risk.

- Medications: review for sedatives, antihypertensives, diuretics, opioids; flag interactions and recent additions; advocate for deprescribing where possible.
- Toileting: unmet toileting need is a leading cause of falls; implement scheduled toileting, ensure the call bell and a clear path are available, and respond promptly to toileting requests — the patient who waits too long gets up alone.
- Mobility aids: ensure the correct aid (walker, cane) is at the bedside and the patient knows how to use it.
- Vision and hearing: ensure glasses and hearing aids are in place; sensory impairment increases fall risk and is easily corrected.
- Environment: clear path to the bathroom, adequate lighting (especially at night), bed in low position, brakes on.

### Balance Fall Prevention Against The Harm Of Immobility

Excessive fall prevention — keeping a patient in bed, restricting movement, overusing bed alarms — causes harm: deconditioning, pressure injuries, delirium, and loss of function. The goal is safe mobility, not immobility.

- Encourage and assist mobility at the level the patient can manage; a patient who mobilizes safely is at lower long-term fall risk than one who is kept in bed.
- Bed alarms do not prevent falls; they alert after the patient is already up. Use them judiciously, not as a substitute for supervision and scheduled care.
- Avoid restraints (including "convenience" restraints like bed rails used to keep a patient in bed) unless clinically indicated and ordered; restraints increase injury and are a last resort.

### Recognize That Fall Risk Fluctuates And Reassess

Fall risk is not static. It rises after sedation, after a new medication, during delirium, at night, during toileting urgency, and after a procedure. A patient who was low-risk in the morning may be high-risk that evening.

- Reassess fall risk after any change in condition, after sedation or anesthesia, after starting a new high-risk medication, and at each shift.
- The night shift carries particular risk: patients wake disoriented, the environment is dark, staffing is lower, and toileting needs are unmet.

### Manage The Post-Fall Patient Correctly

When a patient falls, the immediate priorities are to assess for injury before mobilizing, to notify the provider, and to investigate the cause to prevent recurrence.

- Assess head, spine, and limbs before moving the patient; a head strike or possible spine injury requires immobilization and imaging, not standing the patient up.
- Notify the provider with a structured report including the circumstances of the fall and the assessment findings.
- Investigate the cause (orthostasis, new medication, delirium, environmental hazard) and modify the care plan; a fall is a sentinel event that predicts future falls.

### Engage The Patient And Family In The Plan

Fall prevention requires the patient's participation; a plan imposed without explanation is less effective.

- Explain the specific risks and the specific interventions to the patient and family.
- Teach the patient to call for help before getting up, and ensure the help actually comes promptly — a patient who calls and waits learns to stop calling.

## Common Traps

### Applying Generic Fall Precautions To Every Patient

Every patient scores as a fall risk, so every patient gets the same interventions — nonslip socks, bed alarm, "call for help" sign — and the specific factors that drive each patient's actual risk are not addressed. The mechanism is that the broad sensitivity of the screening tool produces a low specificity, so the response becomes a uniform default that consumes attention without targeting the real risk, and the staff develop alert fatigue to the "all patients are fall risk" environment. The harm is that the patient whose specific risk is orthostatic hypotension does not get orthostatic precautions, the patient whose risk is toileting urgency does not get scheduled toileting, and the generic interventions fail to prevent the fall they were meant to address. The defense is to use the score as a trigger for individualized assessment, to identify and target the specific modifiable factors for each patient, and to recognize that a generic precaution applied to everyone protects no one particularly well.

### Under-Protecting The Patient Who "Seems Independent"

A young, mobile patient who "is independent" is not put on fall precautions, and then falls after receiving a sedating medication or post-anesthesia, because the specific situational risk was not recognized. The mechanism is that the appearance of independence anchors the assessment, so the dynamic risks (new medications, recent procedures, sleep deprivation, orthostasis from fluid shifts) that apply even to independent patients are not factored in, and the patient is left unprotected during the window when their risk is actually elevated. The harm is a fall in a patient who was deemed safe based on a static assessment that ignored the acute risk factors. The defense is to reassess fall risk after any acute change — sedation, anesthesia, new medication, fluid shifts, prolonged bed rest — regardless of the patient's baseline independence, and to recognize that independence is a baseline that acute events can override.

### Relying On Bed Alarms Instead Of Supervision And Scheduled Care

A bed alarm is installed and the nurse relies on it to catch the patient getting up, but the alarm sounds after the patient is already out of bed and moving, and the fall occurs in the interval between the alarm and the nurse's arrival. The mechanism is that the alarm provides a feeling of surveillance that substitutes for the actual supervision and scheduled care (toileting, turning, reassurance) that would have met the patient's needs before they attempted to get up alone. The harm is that the alarm becomes a substitute for the preventive care that addresses the cause (unmet toileting, unaddressed restlessness), and the fall happens despite the alarm because the alarm is reactive, not preventive. The defense is to use alarms judiciously as an adjunct, to implement scheduled toileting and rounding that meet needs before the patient acts, to respond promptly to calls, and to recognize that an alarm that fires frequently is a signal that the patient's needs are unmet, not that the alarm is working.

### Restricting Mobility In The Name Of Fall Prevention

In an effort to prevent falls, the patient is kept in bed, discouraged from walking, and confined, and the resulting immobility causes deconditioning, pressure injuries, delirium, and venous thromboembolism — harms that exceed the fall risk they were meant to prevent. The mechanism is that the visible harm (a fall) is weighted more heavily than the invisible harm (deconditioning), so the intervention over-corrects toward immobility, and the patient leaves the hospital weaker and at higher long-term fall risk than they entered. The harm is functional decline and new complications from the immobility that was meant to protect. The defense is to encourage and assist safe mobility at the level the patient can manage, to treat the goal as safe movement rather than no movement, to avoid restraints and over-restriction, and to recognize that a patient who is kept still is being harmed even if no fall occurs.

### Failing To Respond Promptly To Toileting Calls

The patient calls for help to the bathroom, the call is delayed because the nurse is busy, and the patient — unable to wait — gets up alone and falls. The mechanism is that the unmet toileting need is the most common precipitant of inpatient falls, and a delayed response teaches the patient that calling does not work, so they attempt to toilet alone, and the fall occurs in the gap between the need and the response. The harm is a fall that was directly caused by the system's failure to meet a basic need promptly. The defense is to treat toileting requests as high-priority, to implement scheduled toileting for at-risk patients so the need is met before urgency drives unsafe behavior, to ensure a clear path and assistance, and to recognize that a patient who has learned not to call is a patient at high risk of falling.

### Standing A Post-Fall Patient Up Without Assessing For Injury

After a fall, the patient is helped to stand and returned to bed without assessment, and an unrecognized head injury, hip fracture, or spine injury is worsened by the movement. The mechanism is that the urgency to restore the patient to a safe position and the absence of obvious deformity lead to mobilization before the injury is assessed, and an occult fracture or a head strike with intracranial injury is aggravated. The harm is that the fall causes a minor injury that the subsequent movement converts into a major one. The defense is to assess head, spine, and limbs before mobilizing any post-fall patient, to immobilize and image when a head strike or spine injury is possible, to notify the provider with the circumstances and findings, and to recognize that the absence of obvious deformity does not exclude serious injury.
