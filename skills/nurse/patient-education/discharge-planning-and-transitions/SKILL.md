# Discharge Planning And Transitions

The judgment problem in discharge planning is that the discharge is often driven by the institution's timeline (bed needed, length-of-stay pressure) rather than by the patient's readiness, and the failure mode is to complete the paperwork and the medication list while the actual barriers to a safe discharge — no caregiver at home, no money for medications, no follow-up appointment, an inaccessible home, an unresolved clinical issue — remain unaddressed, and the patient returns within days with a preventable complication. The skilled nurse treats discharge as a transition the patient must be equipped to survive: they identify the barriers early, they involve the team (social work, care management, pharmacy, the family) to resolve them, they confirm the patient has the knowledge, the resources, and the follow-up to manage at home, and they escalate when the discharge is premature. The nurse also recognizes that the discharge destination (home, rehab, skilled nursing) must match the patient's needs, not the institution's preference.

This is a high-stakes domain. Poor discharge planning is a leading cause of readmission and post-discharge adverse events. The nurse's role is to assess readiness, identify and escalate barriers, coordinate the plan, and verify the patient is equipped before discharge.

## Core Rules

### Assess Discharge Readiness Early And Continuously, Not At The Last Moment

Discharge planning begins at admission, not at the discharge order. Barriers identified early can be resolved; barriers identified at discharge cannot.

- At admission, identify the anticipated barriers: home environment, caregiver availability, finances, functional capacity, cognitive status, prior self-management, and social determinants.
- Involve care management and social work early for patients with complex needs (no caregiver, unsafe home, financial barriers, complex regimens).
- Reassess readiness daily; a clinical setback, a new functional limitation, or a change in caregiver availability changes the plan.

### Identify And Resolve The Specific Barriers To Safe Discharge

The barriers are concrete and must be addressed individually; "discharge ready" without resolving them is a fiction.

- Caregiver: who will be at home, what can they do, are they trained (teach-back the caregiver as well as the patient)?
- Home environment: stairs, bathroom access, refrigeration for medications, safety hazards, distance to services.
- Finances and access: can the patient afford and obtain the medications, the equipment, the follow-up? A prescription the patient cannot fill is an omission.
- Functional capacity: can the patient manage the activities of daily living, the wound care, the medications, the devices at the expected level of independence?
- Cognitive capacity: can the patient manage the regimen, or is a caregiver or supervised setting required?
- Follow-up: is the appointment scheduled, does the patient have the means and the understanding to attend, are the pending results accounted for?

### Match The Discharge Destination To The Patient's Needs

The destination — home, home with services, inpatient rehab, skilled nursing facility, hospice — must match the patient's clinical and functional needs and the available support.

- A patient who cannot manage at home safely (no caregiver, complex regimen, limited mobility) requires services or a higher level of care, not discharge home with a wish.
- Advocate for the destination that meets the patient's needs; resist pressure to discharge to a lower level of care than the patient requires.

### Equip The Patient With Knowledge, Resources, And A Written Plan

The patient leaves with what they carry: the knowledge, the written instructions, the medications, the equipment, the appointments, and the warning signs.

- Provide a written, patient-readable discharge plan: medications (with what to take, what to stop, what is new), follow-up appointments, warning signs and who to call, activity and diet instructions.
- Use teach-back to verify understanding of the medications, the warning signs, and the follow-up.
- Confirm the patient has the medications in hand or a plan to obtain them, the equipment, and the services arranged.
- Provide the contact information for questions and for the warning signs that require a call or a return.

### Ensure Pending Results And Follow-Up Are Accounted For

Tests pending at discharge and follow-up appointments are common sources of post-discharge harm when they are lost in the transition.

- Document pending results and who will follow them up; the patient should know which results are pending and how they will be notified.
- Confirm follow-up appointments are scheduled and the patient can attend (transportation, timing, understanding of the purpose).
- Communicate the pending results and the follow-up plan to the receiving provider or primary care.

### Escalate A Premature Discharge

When the discharge is ordered but the patient is not ready (unresolved barriers, clinical instability, no caregiver), the nurse escalates rather than executes.

- Communicate the specific barriers to the provider and to care management.
- Document the concerns and the escalation.
- A discharge that proceeds over unresolved safety concerns is a system failure; the nurse's role is to raise the concern clearly and to advocate for the patient's readiness.

## Common Traps

### Treating Discharge As Paperwork Rather Than A Transition

The nurse completes the discharge instructions, the medication reconciliation, and the follow-up paperwork, and the patient is discharged, while the actual barriers (no caregiver, unaffordable medications, unsafe home) remain unaddressed because the task was the paperwork, not the readiness. The mechanism is that the discharge is experienced as a documentation and order-execution task, so the completion of the paperwork substitutes for the verification of readiness, and the patient leaves with a complete packet and an unmanageable situation. The harm is a readmission, a fall, a missed medication, or a complication that the complete paperwork concealed. The defense is to treat discharge as a transition the patient must be equipped to survive, to identify and resolve the concrete barriers, to verify the patient has the knowledge, resources, and follow-up, and to recognize that completed paperwork without resolved barriers is a premature discharge dressed as readiness.

### Discharging Before The Barriers Are Resolved

Under length-of-stay or bed pressure, the discharge proceeds despite unresolved barriers — the caregiver is not confirmed, the medications are unaffordable, the home is unsafe — because the pressure to discharge overrides the readiness assessment, and the patient returns within days. The mechanism is that the institutional pressure (bed needed, length-of-stay exceeded) is weighted more heavily than the patient's readiness, and the unresolved barriers are rationalized as manageable, so the discharge proceeds over the safety concerns. The harm is a preventable readmission, a complication, or an injury that the premature discharge caused. The defense is to escalate the specific barriers to the provider and care management, to document the concerns, to advocate for the destination and timing that meet the patient's needs, and to recognize that discharging over unresolved safety concerns transfers the risk to the patient and often back to the institution via readmission.

### Failing To Identify Social Determinants That Will Defeat The Plan

The clinical discharge plan is sound, but the patient has no money for the medications, no transportation to the follow-up, no refrigerator for the insulin, or no caregiver to assist with the wound care, and the plan fails at home because these social determinants were not assessed. The mechanism is that the clinical focus (medications, wound, diagnosis) dominates and the social context (finances, environment, support) is not assessed, so the plan is clinically correct but socially unworkable. The harm is that the patient cannot execute the plan — they cannot fill the prescription, cannot attend the appointment, cannot store the medication, cannot perform the care — and they return with the complication the plan was meant to prevent. The defense is to assess the social determinants explicitly at admission (finances, environment, caregiver, transportation, health literacy), to involve care management and social work for patients with barriers, and to recognize that a clinically correct plan that the patient cannot execute is a failed plan.

### Discharging With Pending Critical Results Unaccounted For

A culture or biopsy is pending at discharge, the result returns after the patient leaves, and no one follows it up because the pending result was not communicated or tracked, and the patient returns with progressed disease that the result would have caught. The mechanism is that pending results are common at discharge and the tracking responsibility is ambiguous (is it the discharging team, the primary care, the patient?), so the result falls through the gap and is discovered only when harm occurs. The harm is a delayed diagnosis or treatment (a positive blood culture, a cancer biopsy) that the pending result would have prompted. The defense is to document pending results at discharge, to specify who will follow them up and how the patient will be notified, to communicate the pending results to the receiving provider, and to recognize that a pending result without a tracking plan is a hazard that will materialize after discharge.

### Teaching Only The Patient When A Caregiver Will Provide The Care

The discharge teaching is delivered to the patient, but the family caregiver who will actually perform the wound care, administer the medications, and manage the devices is not present or not taught, and the care at home fails because the caregiver was not equipped. The mechanism is that the patient is the nominal recipient of the teaching, but the actual performer at home is a family member who was not included, so the teach-back was done with the wrong person and the care fails. The harm is that the caregiver performs the care incorrectly (wrong technique, wrong dose, missed warning signs) and the patient suffers a complication. The defense is to identify who will provide the care at home, to include and teach that caregiver with teach-back, to schedule the teaching when the caregiver is present, and to recognize that teaching the patient when a caregiver will perform the care is teaching the wrong person.

### Providing Instructions Without Verifying The Patient Can Act On Them

The discharge instructions are comprehensive and correct, but the patient cannot read them (low literacy, language barrier, visual impairment), cannot afford the prescribed regimen, or cannot physically perform the required care, and the instructions are useless because the patient cannot act on them. The mechanism is that the instructions are delivered as a packet and the patient's ability to read, understand, afford, and perform is assumed rather than verified, so the instructions exist but are not actionable. The harm is that the patient cannot follow the plan and returns with a complication. The defense is to use teach-back to verify understanding, to provide instructions in the patient's language and at an accessible literacy level, to confirm the patient can obtain and afford the medications and equipment, to verify the patient or caregiver can perform the required skills, and to recognize that instructions the patient cannot act on are no instructions at all.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I assess discharge readiness and barriers at admission and daily, involving care management and social work early for complex needs?
- [ ] Did I identify and resolve the specific barriers — caregiver, home environment, finances, functional and cognitive capacity, follow-up — rather than treating discharge as paperwork?
- [ ] Did I match the discharge destination to the patient's clinical and functional needs, advocating for the level of care required?
- [ ] Did I provide a written, patient-readable plan and use teach-back to verify understanding of medications, warning signs, and follow-up?
- [ ] Did I confirm the patient has the medications, equipment, services, and means to attend follow-up, and that prescriptions are affordable and obtainable?
- [ ] Did I account for pending results — documenting them, specifying who follows up, and notifying the receiving provider?
- [ ] Did I teach the caregiver who will perform the care, not just the patient, using teach-back with the right person?
- [ ] If the discharge was premature over unresolved safety concerns, did I escalate the specific barriers, document the concerns, and advocate for readiness?
