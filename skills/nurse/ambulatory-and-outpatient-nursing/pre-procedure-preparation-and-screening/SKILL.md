# Pre-Procedure Preparation And Screening

Ambulatory and outpatient procedures assume that the patient is healthy enough to undergo the procedure and recover without the resources of an inpatient stay, which means that the pre-procedure screening is the gatekeeping step that determines whether that assumption holds. The judgment problem is that screening is often treated as a paperwork exercise — checking boxes on a form — when it is actually a clinical risk assessment that must catch the patient whose undiagnosed or under-reported condition (uncontrolled diabetes, untreated sleep apnea, recent stent, anticoagulation, pregnancy, dehydration, upper respiratory infection) makes the procedure unsafe in the ambulatory setting. The recurring failures are accepting the patient's verbal history without verification, missing the medication that must be held or continued, failing to confirm NPO status, proceeding with an incomplete or unsigned consent, and not escalating the patient whose screening reveals a red flag because the schedule is full and the slot would be lost.

This is a domain where the nurse's role is to be the patient's safety gatekeeper before the procedure begins, to verify rather than assume, to recognize the red flags that warrant delay or additional workup, and to escalate when proceeding would be unsafe.

## Core Rules

### Treat Pre-Procedure Screening As A Clinical Risk Assessment, Not Paperwork

The pre-procedure assessment is a clinical evaluation of whether the patient is safe for the planned procedure and anesthesia in the ambulatory setting. It includes the history (comorbidities, prior anesthesia problems, family history of adverse reactions such as malignant hyperthermia, allergies, medications, substance use, pregnancy possibility, recent illness), the physical findings relevant to airway and cardiovascular risk, the functional status and exercise tolerance, and the psychosocial factors that affect recovery (home support, transportation, distance from the facility). Weight each finding against the planned procedure and anesthesia type, and recognize that ambulatory procedures tolerate less physiologic reserve than inpatient ones, because the patient will go home without monitoring.

### Verify NPO Status Explicitly And Do Not Accept Ambiguity

Confirm NPO status by asking what the patient last ate or drank and the exact time, not by asking whether they are NPO, because patients often misunderstand the instruction, have had a sip of water with medications, or have chewed gum. The NPO duration required depends on the substance (clear fluids, light meal, fatty meal) and the facility protocol, and applies to the anesthesia type. A patient who is not properly NPO is at increased aspiration risk, and proceeding requires anesthesia judgment about the risk-benefit of delay versus proceeding with modified technique — not a nursing decision to proceed. Document the last intake precisely, and escalate any deviation to anesthesia before proceeding.

### Reconcile Medications With Attention To Hold And Continue Decisions

Reconcile the patient's home medications with specific attention to the agents that affect procedural risk: anticoagulants and antiplatelets (bleeding risk, and the hold decision depends on the agent, the procedure, and often cardiology guidance), antihypertensives (some continued, some held), insulin and oral hypoglycemics (adjusted to prevent hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia while NPO), inhalers (continued and brought), and opioids and benzodiazepines (continued to avoid withdrawal). Do not assume the patient has held or continued correctly; verify what they actually took that morning. A patient who took their metformin the morning of a contrast procedure, or who held their beta-blocker, or who double-dosed their insulin while NPO is at risk, and the discrepancy must be caught and addressed before the procedure.

### Confirm Consent Is Informed, Signed, And For The Correct Procedure

Verify that the consent is present, signed by the patient (or authorized surrogate), dated, for the correct procedure and side (where applicable), and that the patient can articulate what they are consenting to and the alternatives and risks. A patient who cannot explain the procedure has not given informed consent, regardless of the signature, and warrants re-consent by the provider. Confirm the correct site marking where applicable, and never proceed with a consent that is missing, for the wrong procedure, or for the wrong side. The consent is a patient-rights safeguard, and a procedural shortcut around it is a serious failure.

### Screen For The Red Flags That Warrant Delay Or Additional Workup

Recognize the screening findings that should pause or redirect the procedure, rather than proceeding because the slot is scheduled. These include uncontrolled comorbidities (poorly controlled diabetes, uncontrolled hypertension, decompensated heart failure, recent cardiac event or stent within the high-risk window), untreated or severe sleep apnea (especially with opioids planned), an abnormal airway assessment suggesting difficult intubation, active upper respiratory infection or fever, suspected or confirmed pregnancy, recent anticoagulant dose that was not held as required, an acute change in condition, or a patient who cannot comply with post-procedure instructions or lacks a safe escort. Each of these warrants anesthesia or provider evaluation and a decision to proceed, delay, reschedule, or move to a higher-acuity setting — not a nursing decision to push forward.

### Complete The Pre-Procedure Safety Checklist As A Verification, Not A Form

Use the pre-procedure time-out and safety checklist (consistent with the surgical safety checklist) to verify identity, procedure, site, side, consent, allergies, medications, NPO status, equipment, implants, antibiotics, and the team. The checklist is a structured verification that catches the errors that kill patients — wrong patient, wrong procedure, wrong side, missed allergy, missing implant — and it must be performed as an active confirmation, not a box-check. Stop the process if any element cannot be confirmed, and resolve the discrepancy before proceeding. The checklist is the last systematic check before an irreversible step, and a perfunctory checklist provides false assurance without the safety.

### Verify The Safe Discharge And Escort Arrangements Before Sedation Or Anesthesia

For procedures involving sedation or anesthesia, verify before starting that the patient has a responsible adult escort and home-care arrangement, because the patient will not be safe to leave alone or drive. A patient without an escort is not a candidate for sedation in the ambulatory setting, and discovering this after the procedure leaves the patient stranded. Confirm the escort by name and contact at the pre-procedure screening, and reschedule if the arrangement is not in place.

## Common Traps

### Accepting The Patient's Verbal History Without Verification

A patient states they have no allergies, take no medications, and have been NPO since midnight, and the nurse records this without probing, when the patient takes an anticoagulant they do not consider a "medication," has a latex sensitivity they forgot to mention, and had a sip of juice that morning. The mechanism of harm is that verbal history is incomplete or misinterpreted by the patient, and recording it without verification builds the care plan on an unreliable foundation, so the allergy is discovered at exposure, the anticoagulant causes bleeding, and the NPO deviation increases aspiration risk. The harm is a preventable adverse event from an undetected discrepancy. The defense is to ask specific, concrete questions (what did you take this morning, what did you eat or drink and when, have you ever had a reaction to latex, tape, or medications), to reconcile against a medication list where available, and to verify rather than to accept.

### Proceeding With An Incomplete Or Unsigned Consent Because Of Schedule Pressure

A patient arrives without a signed consent, or cannot explain the procedure, and the team proceeds because the slot is scheduled and the provider is ready. The mechanism of harm is that the consent is a patient-rights safeguard that ensures the patient understands and agrees to the procedure, and bypassing it — by proceeding without a signature, or by treating a signature as sufficient without understanding — violates the patient's autonomy and removes the safeguard that would have caught a patient who did not want or did not understand the procedure. The harm is an ethically and legally compromised procedure, and the rare but serious wrong-procedure error. The defense is to verify consent is present, signed, correct, and informed before proceeding, and to re-consent the patient who cannot explain the procedure, regardless of schedule pressure.

### Missing The Anticoagulant Or Antiplatelet That Was Not Held

A patient on warfarin, a direct oral anticoagulant, or an antiplatelet agent presents for a procedure with bleeding risk, and the medication was not held as required, but the screening does not catch it because the patient does not list it or the nurse does not reconcile it. The mechanism of harm is that the anticoagulated patient has markedly increased bleeding risk during and after the procedure, and proceeding without recognizing the active anticoagulation leads to uncontrolled bleeding, hematoma, or the need for emergent reversal. The harm is a bleeding complication that could have been prevented by holding the agent or rescheduling. The defense is to reconcile medications specifically for anticoagulants and antiplatelets, to verify the hold was followed, and to escalate to the provider and anesthesia when an anticoagulant is active and the procedure has bleeding risk.

### Failing To Confirm NPO Status By Asking The Right Question

A patient is asked "are you NPO" and answers yes, but had a sip of water with medications, a piece of gum, or a light snack, because they did not understand the instruction. The mechanism of harm is that the closed question elicits the answer the patient thinks is wanted, not the factual intake, so the NPO deviation is not detected, and the patient proceeds with increased aspiration risk. The harm is aspiration pneumonitis during sedation or anesthesia. The defense is to ask what the patient last ate or drank and the exact time, to document the specific intake, and to escalate any deviation to anesthesia for a risk-benefit decision rather than proceeding.

### Pushing Forward With A Red Flag Because The Schedule Is Full

A patient has an untreated severe sleep apnea, a recent cardiac stent, an active upper respiratory infection with fever, or no escort, and the team proceeds because rescheduling would lose the slot and inconvenience the patient. The mechanism of harm is that the red flag indicates a real increase in procedural risk, and proceeding because of operational pressure substitutes the schedule's priority for the patient's safety, so the patient undergoes a procedure they were not optimized for and suffers the complication the screening was meant to prevent. The harm is a preventable adverse event, an unsafe discharge, or an unplanned admission. The defense is to recognize the red flags, to escalate to anesthesia or the provider for a decision to proceed or delay, and to treat the schedule as subordinate to safety.

### Discovering The Missing Escort After The Procedure

A patient undergoes sedation and, on recovery, has no responsible adult to escort them home, because the escort arrangement was not verified at pre-procedure screening. The mechanism of harm is that the patient is impaired from sedation and cannot safely leave alone or drive, and discovering the gap after the procedure leaves the patient stranded, delays recovery discharge, and pressures an unsafe discharge. The harm is an unsafe discharge, a fall, or a prolonged stay. The defense is to verify the escort by name and contact at the pre-procedure screening, before sedation, and to reschedule the patient who has no escort.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I treat the pre-procedure assessment as a clinical risk assessment for ambulatory safety, not paperwork?
- [ ] Did I verify NPO status by asking what the patient last ate or drank and the exact time, rather than a closed yes-or-no question?
- [ ] Did I reconcile medications with attention to anticoagulants, antiplatelets, antihypertensives, insulin, inhalers, and opioids, and verify what was actually taken that morning?
- [ ] Did I confirm the consent is present, signed, for the correct procedure and side, and that the patient can explain the procedure?
- [ ] Did I screen for the red flags (uncontrolled comorbidity, severe sleep apnea, difficult airway, fever or URI, pregnancy, active anticoagulant, no escort) and escalate rather than proceed?
- [ ] Did I complete the pre-procedure safety checklist as an active verification of identity, procedure, site, side, allergies, and equipment?
- [ ] Did I verify the responsible adult escort and home-care arrangement before sedation or anesthesia, by name and contact?
- [ ] Did I document the assessment, the verifications, the consent, and any escalation or delay with the rationale?
