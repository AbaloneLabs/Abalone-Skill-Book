# Nutrition Assessment and Malnutrition Screening

Malnutrition is one of the most underdiagnosed and harmful conditions in hospitalized patients, associated with longer stays, more complications, worse wound healing, higher infection rates, and higher mortality — and it is overwhelmingly a nursing detection problem. The judgment problem is that malnutrition is invisible in the standard vital signs and labs, it is easily dismissed as "the patient is just thin" or "old," and the window to intervene closes as the patient loses lean mass that is slow to regain. The nurse who screens every patient on admission, performs a focused nutrition assessment, and advocates for early referral is the primary defense against the silent harm of hospital malnutrition.

## Core Rules

### Screen Every Patient for Malnutrition Risk on Admission and Reassess

Malnutrition screening is not optional and not reserved for obviously wasted patients; it should be performed on every admission using a validated tool (such as the Malnutrition Screening Tool, MUST, or NRS-2002). The screen captures recent unintentional weight loss, reduced intake, and underlying disease burden, and a positive screen triggers a full assessment and dietitian referral. Reassess during the stay, especially after a week of poor intake, after surgery, during a prolonged ICU stay, or in any patient with a new wound or infection. The screen is fast, and it is the gateway to the interventions that prevent harm. Document the screen result and the action taken.

### Perform a Nutrition-Focused Physical Exam and History

When the screen is positive or the patient is at risk, perform a nutrition-focused assessment. Quantify the weight loss (percentage over what time period — five percent in a month or ten percent in six months is significant), assess the dietary intake (how much of meals are eaten, for how long, any changes), and examine for physical signs of malnutrition: loss of subcutaneous fat (triceps, chest), muscle wasting (temporalis, deltoids, quadratus, interosseous), edema, and dull or sparse hair. Combine the history and exam to characterize the malnutrition (starvation-related, chronic disease-related, acute disease-related) and its severity. The physical exam often reveals wasting that the weight alone (distorted by edema or obesity) misses. Document the findings objectively to support the diagnosis and the referral.

### Interpret Labs in Context, Not as Standalone Markers

There is no single lab that diagnoses malnutrition, and the classic markers (albumin, prealbumin, transferrin) are acute-phase reactants that fall with inflammation, infection, and stress regardless of nutritional status, so they conflate nutrition with illness severity. Use them as trend markers and as part of the picture, not as definitive evidence. A low albumin in sepsis reflects inflammation, not necessarily malnutrition; a falling prealbumin over a week of poor intake in a stable patient supports malnutrition. Combine the labs with the weight loss, the intake history, and the physical exam to form the assessment. Avoid the trap of either dismissing a low albumin as "just inflammation" or diagnosing malnutrition on the albumin alone.

### Identify the Patients at Highest Risk Proactively

Certain patients are at disproportionate risk and warrant heightened surveillance: the elderly (especially with dental issues, social isolation, or polypharmacy), oncology patients (especially on chemotherapy or with head and neck or GI cancers), surgical patients (especially major abdominal or thoracic), critically ill and intubated patients (who depend entirely on the team to feed them), patients with chronic diseases (heart failure, renal failure, liver disease, inflammatory bowel disease, COPD), patients with wounds or burns (high metabolic demand), and patients with psychiatric or substance use conditions. Identify these patients at the start of the shift and ensure their nutrition is on the care plan, because they are the ones who slide into malnutrition unnoticed.

### Advocate for Early Dietitian Referral and an Individualized Plan

A positive screen or assessment warrants a dietitian referral, and the nurse is often the one who triggers it. Refer early rather than waiting for overt malnutrition, because prevention and early intervention are far more effective than rehabilitation of a wasted patient. Provide the dietitian with the full picture (weight trend, intake, labs, comorbidities, functional status, goals of care), and collaborate on the plan (oral nutrition supplements, enteral, parenteral, appetite stimulants, modified textures). Advocate for the plan at interdisciplinary rounds, and ensure it is updated as the patient's status changes. The nurse is the coordinator who ensures nutrition is treated as a treatment, not an afterthought.

### Match Nutrition to the Clinical Context and Goals

Nutrition must be matched to the patient's physiology and goals, not delivered as a standard calorie target. A patient with wound healing or burns needs higher protein and calories; a patient with renal failure needs restricted potassium, phosphorus, and sometimes protein; a patient with heart failure needs sodium and fluid restriction; a patient with liver failure needs controlled protein and adequate calories; a patient at the end of life may benefit from comfort-focused, lower-intensity nutrition that prioritizes enjoyment and dignity over calorie targets. Understand the rationale for each modification, advocate for adjustments as the patient changes, and respect the patient's preferences and goals of care in the plan.

### Address the Social and Functional Determinants of Nutrition

Malnutrition is often driven by factors outside the hospital: poverty, food insecurity, social isolation, dental problems, difficulty cooking or shopping, depression, cognitive impairment, and substance use. Screen for these determinants, involve the social worker, and address them in the discharge plan, because a perfect hospital nutrition plan fails if the patient goes home to an empty refrigerator. For the elderly, address dentition, swallowing, polypharmacy-induced anorexia, and loneliness. Nutrition care that ignores the social context treats the symptom and misses the cause.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Weight Loss in the Elderly or Chronically Ill as Expected

The trap is that the elderly or chronically ill patient has lost weight, and the nurse attributes it to "aging," "the disease," or "poor appetite," charting it without further assessment. The mechanism of harm is that unintentional weight loss in these populations is a red flag for malnutrition (and sometimes for occult cancer, depression, or thyroid disease), and dismissing it as expected delays the assessment, the dietitian referral, and the intervention. The harm is progressive malnutrition, functional decline, worse outcomes from the underlying disease, and avoidable complications. Treat any unintentional weight loss (five percent in a month, ten percent in six months) as a signal requiring full assessment, regardless of age or chronic illness.

### Diagnosing or Excluding Malnutrition on Albumin Alone

The trap is that the team sees a low albumin and diagnoses malnutrition, or sees a normal albumin and excludes it, treating the marker as the disease. The mechanism of harm is that albumin is an acute-phase reactant that falls with inflammation, infection, surgery, and stress independent of nutritional status, so a low albumin in sepsis reflects inflammation, not malnutrition, and a normal albumin can coexist with significant malnutrition in a stable patient. The harm is misdiagnosis — overdiagnosis that leads to unnecessary feeding, or underdiagnosis that misses a wasted patient. Use the albumin as one data point in the context of weight loss, intake, and physical exam, not as the answer.

### Assuming an Obese Patient Cannot Be Malnourished

The trap is that the patient is obese, so the nurse assumes nutrition is adequate and does not screen or assess. The mechanism of harm is that obese patients, especially those with acute illness, surgery, or prolonged ICU stays, can be sarcopenically malnourished — losing lean mass while retaining fat — and the standard weight and body mass index mask the muscle wasting and the poor intake. The harm is missed malnutrition in a patient who looks well-nourished, with worse wound healing, infections, and outcomes. Screen every patient regardless of body size, and assess for muscle wasting and intake even in the obese patient.

### Waiting for Overt Malnutrition Before Referring to the Dietitian

The trap is that the nurse waits until the patient is visibly wasted or has lost significant weight before referring to the dietitian, treating the referral as a last resort. The mechanism of harm is that malnutrition prevention and early intervention are far more effective than rehabilitation, and by the time the patient is overtly malnourished, lean mass has been lost that takes months to regain, and complications have accumulated. The harm is preventable malnutrition that progresses because the referral came too late. Refer on a positive screen, on a week of poor intake, on the start of a critical illness — early, not late.

### Focusing Only on Calories and Ignoring Protein and Micronutrients

The trap is that the team focuses on total calories (or even just "the patient is eating") and ignores the protein, vitamin, and mineral adequacy of the intake. The mechanism of harm is that wound healing, immune function, and muscle preservation depend on adequate protein (often 1.2 to 2 g per kg per day in stressed patients) and specific micronutrients (vitamin C, zinc for wounds; thiamine, folate, B12 in refeeding; iron, vitamin D in chronic deficiency), and a calorie-adequate but protein-poor intake fails to meet these needs. The harm is poor wound healing, infections, and ongoing deficiency despite "adequate" intake. Assess and address the full nutrient profile, not just the calories.

## Self-Check

- Did I screen every patient for malnutrition risk on admission using a validated tool, and reassess after a week of poor intake or a new complication?
- For a positive screen, did I perform a nutrition-focused history and physical exam (weight loss quantified, intake assessed, fat and muscle wasting examined)?
- Did I interpret the labs (albumin, prealbumin) as trend markers in context, not as standalone diagnostic evidence?
- Did I identify the high-risk patients on my assignment (elderly, oncology, surgical, critically ill, chronic disease, wounds) and ensure their nutrition is on the care plan?
- Did I refer to the dietitian early, with the full picture, and collaborate on an individualized plan?
- Is the nutrition plan matched to the patient's disease state (renal, cardiac, hepatic, wound healing) and goals of care?
- Did I screen for and address the social determinants of nutrition (food access, dentition, isolation, function) and involve the social worker?
- For the obese patient, did I assess for sarcopenic malnutrition rather than assuming nutrition is adequate?
- Am I monitoring protein and micronutrient adequacy, not just total calories?
- At discharge, did I communicate the nutrition plan, the residual risk, and the follow-up needs to the patient, family, and primary care provider?
