# Parenteral Nutrition Management

Parenteral nutrition (PN) is life-saving nutrition delivered intravenously for patients whose gut cannot meet their needs, and it is one of the highest-risk therapies a nurse manages. The judgment problem is that PN is a concentrated, hypertonic, nutrient-rich solution that is also an ideal growth medium for bacteria and fungi, so it carries simultaneous risks of metabolic derangement (hyperglycemia, electrolyte shifts, refeeding syndrome), infectious catastrophe (central line-associated bloodstream infection), and access complications. Every aspect of PN management — the line, the rate, the monitoring, the handling — is a safety-critical decision, and the difference between therapeutic and harmful PN is almost entirely in nursing execution.

## Core Rules

### Dedicate a Central Line and Manage It with the Highest Asepsis

PN is hypertonic and must be delivered through a central venous catheter (with the tip in the superior vena cava) to allow dilution; peripheral administration is limited to lower-osmolarity formulations and short duration. The PN lumen should be dedicated to PN alone — no blood draws, no other medications, no pressure tubing — to reduce contamination and incompatibility. Manage the line with maximal aseptic technique: hand hygiene, sterile gloves and mask for access and dressing changes, scrub the hub with friction for the full contact time before every access, and inspect the dressing every shift. PN is the highest-risk fluid for CLABSI, because its nutrient content feeds any organism that enters the line, and CLABSI in a PN patient is often fatal. Treat the PN line as the most important infection-prevention surface on the patient.

### Verify the Order, the Formulation, and the Compatibility Before Hanging

PN is compounded individually for each patient based on a complex order (amino acids, dextrose, lipids, electrolytes, trace elements, vitamins, sometimes insulin), and errors in compounding or delivery are dangerous. Verify the PN order against the bag label (patient identity, components, concentration, rate, any added insulin), inspect the bag for particulate matter, separation, or cracks, and confirm the bag has not been out of refrigeration too long. Confirm compatibility with any other therapy running through adjacent lumens, and never add medications to the PN bag or piggyback into the PN line. If the formulation or the rate seems wrong for the patient, do not hang it — clarify with pharmacy and the prescriber. PN is too concentrated and too individualized for assumptions.

### Titrate the Rate Gradually and Never Bolus or Catch Up

PN must be started at a low rate (commonly half the goal rate) and titrated up over 24 to 48 hours to allow the pancreas to upregulate insulin and the cells to adapt to the glucose load, preventing hyperglycemia and osmotic diuresis. Similarly, never "catch up" a PN infusion that has been behind — the rate is set, and a backlog is not made up by running faster, because a rapid bolus of concentrated dextrose causes hyperglycemia, osmotic diuresis, and potentially hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state. If the PN has been off or behind, resume at the prescribed rate, do not accelerate, and notify pharmacy if the bag will run out before the next scheduled delivery. PN is a continuous, rate-controlled therapy, and any deviation requires deliberate decision-making.

### Monitor Glucose, Electrolytes, and Liver Function Regularly

PN causes predictable metabolic shifts that require scheduled monitoring. Check blood glucose every four to six hours initially (and use a protocol for hyperglycemia, often with insulin added to the PN or given separately), because the dextrose load can cause significant hyperglycemia, especially in diabetic or stressed patients. Check electrolytes (sodium, potassium, phosphate, magnesium, calcium), triglycerides (for lipid tolerance), and liver function tests regularly (commonly daily initially, then a few times a week), because PN affects all of these. Watch especially for refeeding syndrome in the first week (falling phosphorus, potassium, magnesium), and for PN-associated liver dysfunction with long-term use (rising liver enzymes, fatty liver). Treat each derangement promptly, and adjust the PN formulation based on the trends in coordination with pharmacy and the prescriber.

### Prevent Refeeding Syndrome in At-Risk Patients

Refeeding syndrome is a particular risk when starting PN in a malnourished or depleted patient, and the concentrated dextrose in PN makes it more dangerous than enteral refeeding. Identify at-risk patients (more than five days of minimal intake, low body mass index, recent weight loss, alcoholism, oncology patients), start PN at a low rate (commonly 10 to 20 kcal per kg per day, even less in severe risk), give thiamine before the first bag, check phosphorus potassium and magnesium daily and replace aggressively, and watch for fluid overload and arrhythmias. The nurse is the sentinel who catches the falling phosphorus and the rising weight before they become emergencies. Slow initiation and aggressive electrolyte replacement prevent most refeeding harm.

### Handle and Store PN Correctly to Prevent Infection and Degradation

PN is a growth medium and degrades at room temperature. Refrigerate PN until shortly before hanging, hang it within the time limit after compounding (commonly 24 hours for the combined formulation, per pharmacy), and never use a bag that has been at room temperature beyond the limit. Use a dedicated PN administration set, change the tubing per protocol (commonly every 24 hours with each new bag), and never reuse tubing between bags without a proper change. Do not disconnect and reconnect the PN repeatedly, because each manipulation is a contamination opportunity; if the patient must leave the unit, coordinate to maintain the infusion or follow the protocol for safe interruption. Lipids, whether separate or in a three-in-one bag, have additional handling requirements to prevent bacterial growth.

### Coordinate Weaning and Transition to Enteral or Oral Intake

PN is a bridge to enteral or oral nutrition, and the goal is to wean it as the gut resumes function. As enteral or oral intake increases, decrease the PN rate gradually (commonly by half, then off over 24 to 48 hours) to prevent rebound hypoglycemia from the continued pancreatic insulin output, and monitor glucose during the transition. Never abruptly stop PN that has been running at significant rate, because the sudden withdrawal of the dextrose causes hypoglycemia. Coordinate the transition with the dietitian, the prescriber, and the team, and document the tolerance of enteral or oral intake as PN is weaned. A successful PN course ends with the patient nourished enterally or orally, not with prolonged dependence on the central line.

## Common Traps

### Using the PN Line for Other Medications or Blood Draws for Convenience

The trap is that the central line has multiple lumens and the PN line is "right there," so the nurse draws blood or gives medications through the PN lumen to save a stick or a line change. The mechanism of harm is that each access of the PN lumen is a contamination opportunity, and PN is the ideal growth medium for any organism introduced; additionally, many medications are incompatible with PN and can precipitate in the line. The harm is a CLABSI or a line occlusion from precipitate. Dedicate the PN lumen to PN alone, use other lumens for other therapies, and never compromise the dedication for convenience.

### Catching Up a Behind PN Infusion by Increasing the Rate

The trap is that the PN infusion fell behind (pump issue, transport, line problem) and the nurse increases the rate to deliver the prescribed volume before the bag runs out. The mechanism of harm is that a rapid bolus of concentrated dextrose causes acute hyperglycemia, osmotic diuresis, dehydration, and potentially hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state, and the electrolytes in PN given rapidly can cause acute shifts. The harm is metabolic destabilization from a preventable rate error. Resume the PN at the prescribed rate, do not accelerate to catch up, and notify pharmacy if the bag will run short — they will adjust the next bag, not the current rate.

### Starting PN at Goal Rate in a Malnourished Patient

The trap is that the patient is malnourished and the team wants to reverse the depletion quickly, so PN is started at the full goal rate. The mechanism of harm is that rapid refeeding in a depleted patient drives phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium into cells (refeeding syndrome), causes fluid retention and heart failure, and produces severe hyperglycemia, all of which can be fatal. The harm is iatrogenic metabolic and cardiovascular collapse. Start PN at a low rate in at-risk patients, titrate up over 24 to 48 hours, give thiamine, and monitor electrolytes and glucose closely during the first week.

### Abruptly Stopping PN and Causing Rebound Hypoglycemia

The trap is that the patient is now eating or has enteral feeding, so the PN is turned off abruptly when the order changes. The mechanism of harm is that the pancreas has been secreting insulin in response to the continuous dextrose load, and abrupt withdrawal of the dextrose causes rebound hypoglycemia, which can cause seizures, altered mental status, and injury. The harm is hypoglycemic events during what should be a routine transition. Wean PN gradually (reduce the rate over 24 to 48 hours), monitor glucose during the transition, and never abruptly stop PN that has been running at a significant rate.

### Hanging a PN Bag That Has Been Out Too Long or Reusing Tubing

The trap is that the PN bag was hung late or the tubing was not changed, and the nurse continues the existing setup past the time limit to avoid waste or delay. The mechanism of harm is that PN at room temperature beyond the compounding limit supports bacterial and fungal growth, and the administration set accumulates organisms over time; infusing a contaminated bag causes bloodstream infection and sepsis. The harm is a fatal CLABSI from a preventable handling error. Hang PN within the time limit, change the tubing per protocol with each new bag, and discard any bag or tubing that has exceeded the limit — the cost of a new bag is trivial compared to a CLABSI.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Is the PN running through a dedicated central line lumen, managed with maximal asepsis, scrub the hub, and dedicated to PN alone?
- Did I verify the order against the bag label, inspect the bag, and confirm compatibility before hanging?
- Did I start (or continue) PN at the prescribed rate, titrate gradually, and never catch up a behind infusion by increasing the rate?
- Am I checking glucose every four to six hours initially, electrolytes and liver function per protocol, and treating derangements promptly?
- For the malnourished patient, did I start at a low rate, give thiamine, and monitor phosphorus potassium and magnesium daily for refeeding syndrome?
- Is the PN bag within its time limit, refrigerated until shortly before hanging, and is the tubing changed per protocol with each new bag?
- Am I avoiding accessing the PN line for other medications or blood draws?
- When transitioning to enteral or oral, am I weaning PN gradually and monitoring glucose to prevent rebound hypoglycemia?
- Did I inspect the central line site every shift and treat any sign of infection or malfunction as urgent?
- Did I coordinate the formulation adjustments with pharmacy and the prescriber based on the lab trends?
