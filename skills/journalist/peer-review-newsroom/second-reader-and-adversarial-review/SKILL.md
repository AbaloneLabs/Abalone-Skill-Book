# Second Reader And Adversarial Review

The reporting team and primary editor become blind to problems in their own story. They know what the story is supposed to say, they have invested in the reporting, and they have read the draft many times, and all of this familiarity hides gaps, unsupported leaps, unfair framing, and alternative explanations. The judgment problem is that a story can survive every internal check and still fail on contact with a skeptical reader who was not part of the team. Second-reader and adversarial review insert that skeptical reader before publication, when problems can still be fixed.

Use this skill when conducting or designing second-reader and adversarial review of stories before publication, especially investigations, exposés, and high-stakes pieces. The main risk is that an agent may treat review as a courtesy sign-off rather than a genuine challenge, or may staff it with reviewers too close to the team to doubt the story. This skill is editorial guidance; review structures and authority rest with editorial leadership.

## Core Rules

### Make The Review Genuinely Independent

A second reader who shares the team's assumptions, who is invested in the story's success, or who fears the team's reaction cannot perform the function. Independence requires a reviewer outside the reporting team, ideally outside the immediate desk, with permission to doubt the story and authority to require changes. The reviewer's value comes from not being part of the consensus that produced the draft. Choose reviewers senior enough to challenge and detached enough to see what the team cannot.

For the highest-stakes stories, consider a reviewer from a different beat, a standards editor, or an outside expert, to maximize independence.

### Give The Reviewer A Skeptical Mandate

The reviewer's job is not to praise the story or confirm the decision to publish; it is to find what is wrong. Frame the mandate explicitly: stress-test the central claims, probe the evidence, look for unfair framing, identify missing perspectives, consider alternative explanations, and find the weakest points. A reviewer who approaches the story as a friendly reader will miss what a hostile or skeptical reader would catch. Make skepticism the instructed posture, not an optional stance.

Encourage the reviewer to articulate the strongest case against the story, not merely to confirm that it holds.

### Stress-Test The Central Claims And The Evidence

The reviewer should examine whether the central claims are supported by the evidence cited, whether the evidence is as strong as the framing implies, whether attribution is precise, and whether the headline implication is earned by the body. Particular attention goes to the leap between what is proven and what is asserted, because that gap is where stories fail. The reviewer asks: what would a careful critic say is not supported, and is the critic right?

Document the reviewer's challenges and the team's responses, so the basis for each surviving claim is clear.

### Examine Fairness And Alternative Explanations

A story can be accurate and still unfair if it privileges one interpretation, omits a plausible alternative, or frames a subject without adequate context. The reviewer asks whether the story considers the strongest alternative explanation, whether subjects were given fair opportunity to respond, and whether the framing follows the evidence or precedes it. Fairness review catches problems that claim-checking misses, because fairness is about emphasis and context, not just literal accuracy.

If a reasonable person could read the story and think a material perspective was missing, the reviewer should flag it.

### Check For Confirmation Bias In The Reporting

Reporting teams can drift toward confirmation, seeking evidence that supports the thesis and underweighting evidence that contradicts it. The reviewer asks what the team did to seek disconfirming evidence, whether contrary sources were consulted, and whether inconvenient facts were pursued or set aside. A story built only on confirmation is fragile, and the reviewer's job is to find the disconfirmation the team may have missed.

If the team cannot point to a genuine effort to disprove the thesis, the reviewer should require more reporting.

### Empower The Reviewer To Require Changes

Review without authority is toothless. The reviewer must be able to require changes, additional reporting, narrowing of claims, or holding the story. This does not mean the reviewer edits the story directly; it means the reviewer's challenges must be resolved by the team and the responsible editor before publication. Establish that unresolved challenges block publication, so the review cannot be ignored.

Distinguish between challenges the team must address and suggestions the team may accept or reject, so authority is clear.

### Time The Review Before Publication, Not After

Second-reader review must occur before publication, when changes are still possible, not after, when it becomes a post-mortem. Build the review into the timeline with enough lead time that the team can respond to challenges with additional reporting if needed. A review conducted the hour before publication, with no time to act on findings, is a ritual, not a safeguard.

For complex stories, schedule the review early enough that major challenges can redirect the reporting.

### Apply Review Depth To Story Stakes

Not every story needs deep adversarial review. A brief may need only a quick accuracy and fairness check. An investigation, an exposé, a story naming individuals, or a story with legal exposure needs thorough second-reader review, often by multiple reviewers covering different dimensions: claims, fairness, legal, and sensitivity. Calibrate review depth to stakes, concentrating the strongest challenge where the consequences of error are highest.

Under-reviewing high-stakes stories lets avoidable failures through; over-reviewing routine briefs wastes scarce senior attention.

## Common Traps

### Review As Courtesy Sign-Off

A reviewer who confirms the decision to publish rather than challenging the story adds no value and creates false assurance.

### Reviewer Too Close To The Team

A reviewer who shares the team's assumptions or fears their reaction cannot see what an independent skeptic would catch.

### Friendly Rather Than Skeptical Mandate

Without an explicit skeptical mandate, reviewers default to support and miss the weaknesses a critic would find.

### Claim-Checking Without Fairness Review

Verifying literal accuracy while missing unfair framing, omitted perspectives, or unconsidered alternatives leaves the story vulnerable.

### No Search For Disconfirming Evidence

A story built on confirmation alone is fragile, and review that does not probe for disconfirmation misses the gap.

### Review Without Authority

A reviewer whose challenges can be ignored provides no real safeguard; unresolved challenges must block publication.

### Review Timed Too Late

A review conducted with no time to act on findings is a ritual that cannot prevent the failure it identifies.

### Uniform Review Depth

Treating a brief and an investigation the same either over-invests in routine work or under-invests in high-stakes stories.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the reviewer genuinely independent of the reporting team, senior enough to challenge, and detached enough to doubt the story?
- [ ] Does the reviewer have an explicit skeptical mandate to find what is wrong, including articulating the strongest case against the story?
- [ ] Have the central claims been stress-tested against the cited evidence, with the gap between proof and assertion examined?
- [ ] Has fairness review examined alternative explanations, missing perspectives, response opportunities, and whether framing follows the evidence?
- [ ] Has the reviewer probed for confirmation bias, asking what disconfirming evidence the team sought and whether it was adequate?
- [ ] Does the reviewer have authority to require changes, additional reporting, or holding, with unresolved challenges blocking publication?
- [ ] Is the review timed before publication with enough lead time to act on findings, including additional reporting if needed?
- [ ] Is review depth calibrated to story stakes, with the strongest challenge concentrated where consequences of error are highest?
- [ ] Has the review outcome been documented and confirmed by the responsible editor, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that review authority rests with editorial leadership?
