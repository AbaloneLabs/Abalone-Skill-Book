# Post Publication Review And Learning

When a story goes wrong, the immediate work is correction, but the deeper work is understanding why it went wrong and changing the conditions that allowed it. The judgment problem is that newsrooms under pressure treat errors as one-off mistakes to be corrected and moved past, when they are usually symptoms of process failures, and a failure to learn guarantees repetition. Post-publication review is the discipline of turning a correction or a failure into institutional learning, so that the next story is stronger rather than a repeat of the same error.

Use this skill when conducting post-mortems on errors or failures, reviewing what went wrong after publication, and designing learning that changes process. The main risk is that an agent may conduct review as blame or as a formality, producing conclusions that do not change practice. This skill is editorial guidance; post-mortem processes and authority rest with editorial leadership and standards.

## Core Rules

### Separate Correction From Learning

The correction fixes the public record; the post-mortem fixes the process. Both are necessary, and they operate on different timescales. The correction is urgent and outward-facing; the learning is deliberate and inward-facing. Do not let the urgency of correction consume the attention needed for learning, and do not let the learning become an excuse to delay correction. Run both tracks, with the correction first and the review as soon as the story is stable.

Conflating them produces either a slow correction or a shallow review.

### Focus On Process, Not Blame

Effective post-mortems examine the conditions that allowed the error, not the individual who made it. Blame-oriented review drives problems underground, discourages disclosure, and misses the systemic causes. Process-oriented review asks which checks failed, which signals were missed, which pressures distorted judgment, and which structures allowed the error through. Individuals make mistakes, but processes are what the institution can change.

This does not mean ignoring individual responsibility; it means locating the fixable causes at the level of process, training, and structure.

### Reconstruct The Timeline And Decision Points

Map how the story moved from reporting to publication, identifying the points where the error entered or survived. Which claim was unsupported and when was it added? Which check should have caught it and why did it not? Which pressure, deadline, or assumption shaped the decision? A timeline reconstruction reveals where the defenses failed and which interventions would have caught the error. Without reconstruction, lessons remain abstract.

Use the reporting record, drafts, edits, and communications to reconstruct accurately, not from memory.

### Identify The Specific Failure, Not A Generic Lesson

"We need to be more careful" is not a lesson; it is a wish. A specific lesson identifies the failure mode and the change that prevents it: "Anonymous claims of criminal conduct now require a second independent source and senior editor sign-off," or "Late edits after fact-check must be re-checked, with a checklist confirming it." Translate the finding into a concrete change to a specific check, standard, or process. Generic lessons change nothing.

Test each proposed change against whether it would have prevented this specific error; if not, it is not the right lesson.

### Distinguish Error Types And Match The Response

Errors have different causes and deserve different responses. A factual slip despite a sound process may need only a correction and a note. A process failure, where a check was missing or skipped, needs a process change. A standards failure, where the wrong call was made, needs standards clarification or training. A cultural failure, where pressure or norms distorted judgment, needs leadership and structural change. Match the response to the type, rather than applying a one-size response.

Misdiagnosing the error type leads to changes that do not address the actual cause.

### Examine Near-Misses, Not Just Published Errors

Some of the best learning comes from near-misses, errors caught before publication by a check that worked. Reviewing what nearly went wrong, and which check caught it, reveals both the vulnerabilities and the defenses that are working. Near-miss review is lower-stakes than error review and can surface process issues before they produce a public failure. Build a culture where near-misses are reported and reviewed, not hidden.

Near-misses that are caught only by luck, with no systematic check, are warnings that the next similar case may not be lucky.

### Close The Loop With Follow-Through

A post-mortem that produces recommendations but no implementation is a ritual. Assign owners and deadlines to each change, track implementation, and verify that the change is actually in practice, not just documented. Return to the change after a reasonable interval to confirm it is working and has not produced new problems. Learning that is not implemented and verified does not improve the institution.

The most common failure of post-mortems is excellent analysis with no follow-through.

### Share Lessons Appropriately Across The Newsroom

Lessons that stay with the team that made the error help only that team. Where appropriate and consistent with source protection and privacy, share the lessons across the newsroom, so that other teams can prevent the same failure. Generalize the lesson without exposing sensitive detail, and integrate it into training, standards, and checklists. Institutional learning requires institutional dissemination.

Balance transparency with the need to protect individuals from disproportionate exposure; the goal is learning, not public blame.

## Common Traps

### Correction Consuming The Learning

The urgency of fixing the public record leaves no attention for the process review that prevents recurrence.

### Blame Over Process

Focusing on who erred rather than what failed drives problems underground and misses systemic causes.

### Generic Lessons

"We must be more careful" changes nothing; lessons must specify the concrete change to a check or process.

### Misdiagnosed Error Type

Applying a process fix to a standards failure, or vice versa, does not address the actual cause.

### Ignoring Near-Misses

Learning only from published errors misses the warnings that near-misses provide before a public failure.

### No Follow-Through

Recommendations without owners, deadlines, and verification become a ritual that changes nothing.

### Lessons Kept Siloed

Learning that stays with one team does not prevent the same failure elsewhere in the newsroom.

### Reconstruction From Memory

Reconstructing the timeline from recollection rather than records produces an inaccurate basis for lessons.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the correction been handled first and urgently, with the post-mortem run as a separate, deliberate track?
- [ ] Does the review focus on process and conditions rather than individual blame?
- [ ] Has the timeline been reconstructed from records, identifying where the error entered and which checks failed?
- [ ] Are the lessons specific, naming the concrete change to a check, standard, or process that would have prevented this error?
- [ ] Has the error type, factual slip, process failure, standards failure, or cultural failure, been diagnosed and matched to the appropriate response?
- [ ] Are near-misses reviewed as well as published errors, with a culture that encourages reporting?
- [ ] Do recommendations have owners, deadlines, and verification, with a return check after implementation?
- [ ] Are lessons shared appropriately across the newsroom and integrated into training and standards?
- [ ] Has the post-mortem been reviewed and its changes endorsed by editorial leadership and standards, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that institutional learning processes are established by editorial leadership?
