# Legal Review Coordination

Legal review is where the journalism meets the law, and it is also where the relationship between newsroom and counsel can either strengthen or strangle a story. The judgment problem is that lawyers are trained to minimize risk, while journalism often requires accepting risk in service of the public interest, and an editor who treats legal advice as a veto will kill newsworthy reporting, while an editor who ignores legal advice will publish into avoidable liability. The discipline is to extract the genuine legal analysis, weigh it against the public-interest case, and make an editorial decision that respects both.

Use this skill when coordinating legal review, deciding what to send, interpreting counsel's advice, and weighing risk against public interest. The main risk is that an agent may either over-defer to counsel's caution or dismiss counsel's concerns, in both cases failing to integrate legal and editorial judgment. This skill is editorial guidance; publication decisions rest with editorial leadership, and legal advice must come from qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Send The Right Material With The Right Questions

Effective legal review depends on sending counsel what they need: the final draft, the central claims, the evidence supporting them, the subjects' status, the response received, and the specific questions, such as defamation risk, privacy, intellectual property, or national-security exposure. Vague referrals produce vague advice. Frame the editorial concerns as specific legal questions so counsel can address the actual risks rather than offering generic caution.

Include the reporting record or a summary of it, because counsel's assessment depends on what the outlet can prove, not just what the draft says.

### Translate Editorial Concerns Into Legal Questions

Editors and lawyers use different vocabularies. An editor's concern that a claim "outruns the evidence" is a legal question about whether the statement is provable and defensible. A concern about "fairness" may map to the right of response and to framing that affects defamation implication. Translate the editorial instinct into the legal question, so counsel can advise precisely rather than generally. This translation is a core coordination skill.

Equally, translate counsel's advice back into editorial terms, so the newsroom understands what changes would reduce risk and why.

### Distinguish Risk Advice From A Veto

Counsel advises on risk; the editor decides whether to accept it. Conflating the two turns counsel into a de facto publisher, which serves caution over journalism. A lawyer's role is to identify the legal exposure, the strength of defenses, and the options for reducing risk; the editor's role is to weigh that against the public interest and decide. Maintain the distinction clearly, even when counsel's advice is strongly cautionary.

This does not mean ignoring counsel; it means integrating their analysis into an editorial decision rather than substituting it for one.

### Weigh Risk Against Public Interest Explicitly

When counsel flags risk, the editorial response is to weigh that risk against the public-interest value of publication, using the tools available to reduce risk without abandoning the story. Can the claim be narrowed to what is provable? Can attribution be tightened? Can a sensitive detail be removed? Can framing be adjusted? Often the public interest can be served with materially less risk through targeted changes. Make the weighing explicit and documented, so the decision is defensible.

Sometimes the right decision is to accept the risk and publish, because the public interest justifies it; sometimes it is to narrow or hold. Both are legitimate outcomes of a genuine weighing.

### Match Legal Review Depth To Story Risk

Not every story needs legal review. Routine reporting with no allegations, no private individuals at risk, and no sensitive material may not require it. Investigations, exposés, stories naming individuals in a damaging light, national-security material, and intellectual-property questions require review, often detailed. Calibrate legal review to the actual risk profile, sending high-risk stories for thorough review and not burdening counsel with low-risk material that delays publication.

Define the triggers for legal review in advance, so the decision is consistent rather than ad hoc.

### Involve Counsel Early For High-Risk Stories

For high-risk stories, involve counsel early, during the reporting or drafting stage, not just at the final gate. Early involvement lets counsel flag risks while there is still time to gather additional evidence, adjust the approach, or build defenses. Late involvement, at the moment of publication, leaves only the options of publish-as-is, cut, or hold, with no time for the reporting that could resolve the risk.

Early involvement also helps the reporting team understand the legal dimensions as they work, producing a more defensible draft.

### Protect Source Identity During Legal Review

Legal review must not become a channel for exposing confidential sources. Share with counsel what is needed for the legal assessment, including the basis for claims, without unnecessarily revealing source identities. Use summaries and descriptions rather than identifying detail where possible. Coordinate with counsel on privilege and confidentiality so that the review process does not create discoverable material that endangers sources.

For source-sensitive stories, discuss with counsel how to conduct review without creating records that could be subpoenaed.

### Preserve The Editorial Decision And Document It

The final publication decision is editorial, informed by legal advice but not dictated by it. Document the legal review, the risks identified, the options considered, the risk-reduction measures taken, and the editorial rationale for the final decision. This documentation supports the outlet's defensibility, clarifies the basis for the decision internally, and creates an institutional record. It also protects against later claims that risks were ignored.

Documentation should capture the weighing, not just the outcome, so the reasoning is reviewable.

## Common Traps

### Treating Counsel's Advice As A Veto

Deferring to caution as if it were a publication decision kills newsworthy reporting and surrenders editorial authority.

### Ignoring Genuine Legal Risk

Dismissing counsel's concerns publishes into avoidable liability and can endanger the outlet and sources.

### Vague Referrals

Sending counsel a draft without specific questions produces generic caution that does not address the actual risks.

### Late Involvement Only

Bringing counsel in at the final gate leaves no time for the reporting that could resolve identified risks.

### Over-Reviewing Low-Risk Stories

Sending routine material for legal review delays publication and burdens counsel without value.

### Exposing Sources Through Review

Sharing identifying detail unnecessarily during review creates records that could endanger confidential sources.

### Undocumented Decisions

Without a record of the legal advice and the editorial weighing, the decision cannot be defended or reviewed later.

### Failure To Translate Between Editor And Lawyer

Different vocabularies lead to misunderstanding, with editors missing legal nuance and lawyers missing editorial purpose.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has counsel received the final draft, central claims, evidence, subject status, response, and specific legal questions?
- [ ] Have editorial concerns been translated into precise legal questions, and counsel's advice translated back into editorial terms?
- [ ] Is counsel's risk advice distinguished from a publication veto, with the editorial decision reserved to the editor?
- [ ] Has legal risk been weighed explicitly against public interest, with risk-reduction options, narrowing, attribution, and framing, considered before deciding?
- [ ] Is legal review depth matched to the story's risk profile, with consistent triggers defined in advance?
- [ ] Is counsel involved early for high-risk stories, while reporting can still address identified risks?
- [ ] Is source identity protected during review, with summaries used and privilege coordinated to avoid discoverable records?
- [ ] Is the legal review and editorial decision documented, capturing the risks, options, measures, and rationale?
- [ ] Has the final publication decision been made by editorial leadership on the basis of qualified legal advice, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that legal review must be conducted by qualified counsel?
