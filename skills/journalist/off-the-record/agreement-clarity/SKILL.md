# Agreement Clarity

Agreement clarity is the practice of ensuring that the journalist and the source share an exact, mutual understanding of the terms under which information is provided and how it may be used. The harm this skill prevents is the breach of trust, the burned source, and the reputational damage that follow when a source feels betrayed because the journalist used information differently than the source expected. Most sourcing disputes are not bad faith; they are failures of clarity. A source says "this is off the record" meaning one thing, the journalist hears another, and publication produces a rupture that cannot be repaired. The discipline of agreement clarity is to make the invisible contract between journalist and source explicit, documented, and unambiguous before any risk materializes.

## Core Rules

### Make the agreement explicit and verbalized
Do not rely on implication, assumption, or shared convention. State the terms aloud or in writing: "This is on background, meaning I can use the information but describe you only as a city official, not by name." Verbalizing forces both parties to confront and confirm the terms.

### Confirm the source's understanding back
After stating the terms, confirm the source's understanding by asking them to acknowledge or restate them. Misunderstanding often survives a one-way statement; it rarely survives a two-way confirmation. A simple "does that match your understanding?" surfaces divergences before they cause harm.

### Define the scope of the agreement precisely
Agreements have multiple dimensions: what information is covered, how it can be used, how the source can be described, whether it can be pursued with other sources, how long the terms last. Define each dimension that matters. Vague agreements leave gaps that become disputes.

### Document the agreement contemporaneously
Note the terms in your records at the time of the conversation, including what was agreed and any qualifications. Memory degrades and disputes arise later; contemporaneous documentation is the authoritative record of what was promised. This protects both the source and the journalist.

### Distinguish the agreement from the information
Be clear about what the agreement covers. A source may provide some information on the record and other information on background in the same conversation. Track which is which, so you do not apply the wrong terms to the wrong material.

### Address edge cases before they arise
Anticipate the questions that cause disputes: Can the information be used if another source confirms it independently? Can the source's description be narrowed under legal pressure? Can the information be used after the source leaves their position? Address these where foreseeable, rather than discovering the gap under pressure.

### Renegotiate explicitly, never by drift
If circumstances change and the terms need to change with them, renegotiate explicitly with the source. Do not let terms drift through assumption that the source "wouldn't mind." Drift is the silent path to breach.

### Treat the agreement as binding on the outlet, not just the reporter
Sourcing agreements bind the outlet, not only the individual reporter who made them. Ensure that editors and colleagues who handle the information honor the terms. A breach by a colleague is still a breach.

## Common Traps

### Assuming shared convention
Assuming the source knows what "on background" means, or shares your definition, is the most common source of dispute. Define explicitly.

### One-way statement without confirmation
Stating terms without confirming the source's understanding lets misunderstanding persist. Confirm back.

### Vague scope
Agreements that specify attribution but not whether information can be pursued independently, or that cover some statements but not others, leave gaps that become disputes. Define scope precisely.

### Reliance on memory
Failing to document the agreement contemporaneously leaves disputes to be resolved by degraded memory. Document at the time.

### Drift through assumption
Letting terms shift because the journalist assumes the source would consent is a breach by increments. Renegotiate explicitly.

### Mixing attribution levels without tracking
When a conversation spans on-the-record and background material, failing to track which is which leads to applying the wrong terms. Track attribution level per statement.

### Colleague breach
Assuming the agreement binds only you, not your editors or co-reporters, leads to breaches when others handle the information. Ensure the outlet honors the terms.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I state the sourcing agreement explicitly and verbally, rather than relying on implication?
- [ ] Did I confirm the source's understanding back, surfacing any divergence?
- [ ] Did I define the scope precisely, including use, attribution, independent pursuit, and duration?
- [ ] Did I document the agreement contemporaneously in my records?
- [ ] Am I tracking which information in a mixed conversation is on which terms?
- [ ] Did I address foreseeable edge cases, such as independent corroboration or legal pressure, before they arise?
- [ ] Where circumstances changed, did I renegotiate explicitly rather than letting terms drift?
- [ ] Have I ensured editors and colleagues handling the information honor the agreement?
- [ ] Would the source, reviewing how I used the information, agree the terms were honored exactly?
- [ ] Is there a contemporaneous record I could point to if the agreement were later contested?
