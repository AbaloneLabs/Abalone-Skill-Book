# On-Background Vs Off-Record

Sourcing agreements define the terms under which information is provided and how it can be used, and they sit at the center of source relationships and journalistic ethics. The harm this skill prevents is the confusion and breach of trust that arise when sourcing terms are ambiguous, mismatched, or applied inconsistently: sources who believed they were off the record seeing their words published, journalists bound by terms they did not understand, information that could have been used lost to a vague agreement, and the corrosion of source relationships when expectations diverge. The terms on the record, on background, deep background, and off the record have no universal definitions; they mean only what the journalist and source agree they mean. The discipline is to negotiate terms explicitly, ensure shared understanding, and apply them rigorously.

## Core Rules

### Negotiate sourcing terms before the information is provided
Agree on the attribution level before the source discloses sensitive information, not after. Once information is on the table, negotiating terms becomes reactive and contested. Establish terms up front so both parties know the rules governing what follows.

### Define terms explicitly because they have no universal meaning
Because on background, deep background, and off the record mean different things to different people, define what you and the source mean by them in concrete terms: what can be used, how it can be attributed, whether it can be pursued with other sources, whether it is for the journalist's understanding only. Shared definition prevents later breach.

### Use the standard taxonomy as a starting point
While definitions vary, a common working taxonomy: on the record means usable with name and title; on background means usable with an agreed description (e.g., "a senior official"); deep background means usable without any attribution, woven into the journalist's own reporting; off the record means not usable at all in publication, for the journalist's understanding only. Confirm the source shares these meanings.

### Reserve off the record for genuine need
Off the record, where information cannot be used at all, has limited journalistic value and should be reserved for cases where it genuinely serves understanding or relationship, such as a source explaining context they will not let you publish. Do not default to off the record; it forecloses use of the information.

### Prefer on background where attribution can be negotiated
On background often preserves the ability to use information while protecting the source's identity, and is preferable to off the record where the source will agree. Negotiate the attribution description as specifically as the source will allow, to maximize reader understanding of the source's basis of knowledge.

### Pursue on-background information on the record with other sources
Information learned on background from one source can and should be pursued with other sources who may provide it on the record or on better terms. Do not let a background agreement with one source prevent corroboration through independent reporting, so long as the original source's identity and the fact that they provided it are protected.

### Honor the strictest interpretation when in doubt
If there is ambiguity about what was agreed, apply the interpretation most favorable to the source's privacy. The cost of under-using information is small; the cost of breaching an agreement is a damaged relationship and a damaged reputation. When in doubt, protect the source.

### Revisit and renegotiate terms as circumstances change
If a source later wants to go on the record, or wants to tighten terms, or if publication circumstances change, revisit the agreement explicitly. Terms are not immutable, but changes must be negotiated, not assumed.

## Common Traps

### Assuming shared definitions
The most common trap is assuming the source means the same thing by off the record that you do. Sources often mean "don't use my name" while journalists mean "don't use at all." Define explicitly.

### Agreeing to off the record by default
Defaulting to off the record forecloses use of information and is often unnecessary. Negotiate the least restrictive terms the source will accept.

### Failing to pursue background information independently
Treating on-background information as locked when it could be corroborated and published through other sources wastes reporting. Pursue it independently while protecting the original source.

### Applying terms inconsistently
Using information more broadly than agreed, or attributing differently than promised, breaches the agreement even subtly. Apply agreed terms rigorously.

### Negotiating terms after disclosure
Waiting until information is on the table to negotiate terms leads to contested agreements and breaches. Establish terms first.

### Misunderstanding deep background
Deep background, usable without any attribution, is often confused with off the record. Confirm whether the source means "use it but don't cite me at all" or "don't use it."

### Breaching by inference
Inferring that a source "wouldn't mind" a use slightly beyond the agreement, or that they would consent if asked, is a breach. When in doubt, ask; do not assume.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I negotiate the sourcing terms before the sensitive information was provided?
- [ ] Did I define terms explicitly with the source, given they have no universal meaning?
- [ ] Did I confirm the source shares my understanding of the agreed attribution level?
- [ ] Did I reserve off the record for genuine need rather than defaulting to it?
- [ ] Did I negotiate on background with the most specific attribution the source would allow?
- [ ] Am I pursuing on-background information independently through other sources while protecting the original?
- [ ] Where terms were ambiguous, am I applying the interpretation most favorable to the source?
- [ ] Have I revisited and renegotiated terms explicitly where circumstances changed?
- [ ] Am I applying the agreed terms rigorously, without subtle expansion or different attribution?
- [ ] Would the source, reviewing how I used the information, agree I honored our agreement?
