# Source-Protection Pledges

A pledge of confidentiality is among the most serious commitments a journalist can make, because it asks the source to trust the journalist with their safety, livelihood, or freedom. The harm this skill prevents is the exposure of a source who relied on a pledge of protection: the whistleblower fired or prosecuted, the insider ostracized or sued, the leak traced back through operational failures, and the destruction of the fragile trust on which accountability reporting depends. Source protection is not a promise made at the moment of disclosure; it is an ongoing operational, legal, and ethical duty that extends from first contact through publication and indefinitely beyond. A pledge broken, whether through carelessness, legal capitulation, or technical failure, cannot be repaired and endangers every future source.

## Core Rules

### Make protection pledges deliberately and sparingly
Confidentiality is a serious commitment with real costs, including legal exposure and constraints on the reporting. Grant it where the source genuinely needs it to speak and the information justifies it, not as a default or a courtesy. A pledge made lightly is harder to uphold.

### Match the protection to the risk
Assess the realistic risk to the source: Could they be fired? Prosecuted? Physically harmed? Harassed? The level of protection must match the level of risk. A low-risk source may need only standard care; a high-risk source may require encrypted communications, records destruction, legal contingency planning, and operational discipline.

### Implement operational security from first contact
Protection begins before the pledge is made. Use secure communication for sensitive contacts, minimize identifying records, separate source-identifying information from reporting materials, and consider physical meeting security for high-risk sources. Operational failures, not legal demands, expose most sources.

### Avoid creating identifiable records
Do not create records that link the source to the information unnecessarily: avoid naming the source in notes that could be subpoenaed, avoid metadata that identifies them, destroy or minimize records of source identity consistent with the reporting needs. The fewer records that exist, the less there is to compel or steal.

### Plan for legal demands before they arise
Subpoenas, court orders, and searches seeking source identity are foreseeable risks in accountability reporting. Plan in advance: know the outlet's legal posture, whether shield laws apply, whether the outlet will resist or comply, and what the source should expect. A source surprised by legal process they were not warned about has been failed.

### Honor the pledge absolutely and indefinitely
Once made, the pledge binds the journalist and the outlet absolutely, across time. It is not voided by the source's death, by the passage of years, by a change of assignment, or by the journalist's departure. Do not reveal a source's identity, even to colleagues, beyond what is strictly necessary.

### Protect against inadvertent disclosure through the reporting
Even when the journalist protects the source, the reporting itself can identify them through distinctive details, timing, or access patterns. Review the published story for "jigsaw identification," where details combined could identify the source to a knowledgeable reader, and generalize or omit details that risk exposure.

### Have a contingency and escalation plan
For high-risk sources, plan for contingencies: what if the source is suspected? What if legal process targets the journalist? What if the outlet's leadership wavers? Identify who within the outlet knows the source's identity, who advocates for protection, and what the fallback positions are.

## Common Traps

### Casual or default confidentiality
Granting confidentiality without assessing need creates unnecessary commitments and operational burden. Grant it deliberately.

### Protection mismatched to risk
Applying low protection to a high-risk source exposes them; applying extreme protection to a low-risk source wastes effort and signals distrust. Match protection to assessed risk.

### Operational security failures
Most sources are exposed not by legal process but by careless records, insecure communication, or metadata. Implement operational discipline from first contact.

### Creating subpoena-able records
Notes, emails, and drafts that name or identify the source become evidence if compelled. Minimize and destroy identifying records.

### Surprising the source with legal risk
Failing to warn a source about foreseeable legal process leaves them unprepared and exposed. Plan and communicate legal contingencies.

### Treating the pledge as time-limited
The duty of protection does not expire. Revealing a source years later, after the story fades, still betrays the pledge.

### Jigsaw identification in the story
Details that seem harmless individually can identify the source when combined. Review the published story for cumulative identification risk.

### Outlet wavering under legal pressure
If the outlet's leadership considers complying with demands for source identity, the pledge is at risk. Establish the outlet's posture in advance and advocate for resistance.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I grant confidentiality deliberately, based on genuine need and justification, rather than as a default?
- [ ] Have I assessed the realistic risk to the source and matched the level of protection to that risk?
- [ ] Have I implemented operational security from first contact, including secure communication and minimized records?
- [ ] Have I avoided creating records that link the source to the information and that could be compelled?
- [ ] Have I planned for foreseeable legal demands and communicated the legal posture to the source?
- [ ] Am I prepared to honor the pledge absolutely and indefinitely, regardless of time or circumstance?
- [ ] Have I reviewed the published story for jigsaw identification risk from combined details?
- [ ] Do I have a contingency and escalation plan for high-risk sources, including who knows the identity?
- [ ] Would the source, knowing how I handled their protection, trust me with their identity again?
- [ ] Have I ensured the outlet, not just me, is bound to uphold the pledge?
