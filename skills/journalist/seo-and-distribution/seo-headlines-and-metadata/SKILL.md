# SEO Headlines and Metadata

A headline written for search is read by two audiences with different needs: the human reader deciding whether to click, and the algorithm deciding whether to surface the story at all. Metadata, the title tag, meta description, URL slug, image alt text, and structured data, is even less visible to readers but heavily shapes whether the story is found. The central judgment problem is that the techniques that help a story rank (keyword repetition, search-friendly phrasing, clear topic signaling) can pull against the techniques that make a headline honest, fair, and faithful to the reporting. An optimized headline that overstates the story to win a click betrays the reader; an honest headline that no one can find serves no one. This skill covers how to write search-aware headlines and metadata without sacrificing accuracy, attribution, or editorial integrity.

## Core Rules

### Write the Headline for the Reader First, Search Second

The headline's primary job is to tell the truth about the story clearly enough that a reader can decide whether to read it. Begin with accuracy and clarity: does the headline reflect what the reporting actually shows, with appropriate attribution and hedging? Only then consider search: can a reader searching for this topic recognize and find the story? When accuracy and optimization conflict, accuracy wins. A headline that ranks well but misleads readers about the content damages trust and can create legal and ethical exposure. The best SEO headlines serve both audiences by being clear, specific, and truthful.

### Use Precise, Searchable Language Without Keyword Stuffing

Search engines reward clarity about a story's topic, so using the concrete nouns and proper nouns a reader would search for (names, places, events, institutions) helps discovery. But keyword stuffing, repeating terms unnaturally or cramming in tangential keywords, harms readability and is penalized by modern search algorithms. Write natural headlines that include the key search terms once, in a way a human would actually speak. Prefer specific terms ("city council," "flood," "election results") over vague ones ("officials," "the situation"), both for readers and for search.

### Make the Title Tag and Meta Description Work as a Team

The title tag is what appears as the clickable link in search results; the meta description is the snippet beneath it. Together they are the story's storefront. Write a title tag that is accurate, specific, and within the length that search engines display fully (roughly 50 to 60 characters), so it is not truncated. Write a meta description that summarizes the story's value in roughly 150 to 160 characters, accurately and without hype. Do not let the meta description promise something the story does not deliver; it is a factual summary, not ad copy. If no meta description is written, search engines will generate one from page content, which may be misleading, so write it deliberately.

### Keep URLs Clean, Stable, and Descriptive

The URL slug is a permanent identifier and a minor search signal. Keep it short, lowercase, hyphen-separated, and descriptive of the story ("city-council-votes-on-budget" rather than "?p=48291" or "article10"). Avoid changing the URL after publication; a changed URL breaks links and shares and loses search equity. If a URL must change, set up a redirect from the old to the new. Do not pack the URL with keywords; clarity and stability matter more than optimization. URLs often outlast headlines in the reader's memory of how to find a story.

### Ensure Structured Data Accurately Describes the Content

Structured data (schema markup) tells search engines what kind of content a page is: a news article, an opinion piece, a review, a recipe, a fact-check. Accurate structured data helps the story appear in the right features (news boxes, rich results) and helps readers understand what they are getting. Mislabeling an opinion piece as a news article, or a sponsored post as editorial, misleads readers and can violate platform policies. Ensure the structured data matches the actual content type and that author, date, and section labels are correct.

### Preserve Attribution and Hedging in Search-Facing Text

The pressure to write punchy, keyword-rich headlines can strip out the attribution and uncertainty that make a story honest. "Mayor accused of fraud, council says" should not become "Mayor committed fraud" in the title tag to save characters or add a keyword. Carry appropriate attribution ("police say," "report finds," "allegedly") into headlines and metadata when the underlying claim is attributed or contested. A search-optimized headline that converts a sourced allegation into a bare assertion is both unethical and legally risky.

### Write Alt Text That Is Accurate and Useful

Image alt text serves accessibility for readers using screen readers and provides a minor search signal. Write alt text that accurately describes what the image shows and its relevance to the story, in natural language. Do not stuff alt text with keywords unrelated to the image, and do not use alt text to make claims the image does not support. Alt text is a factual description, not a marketing channel.

### Test How the Story Appears Before Publishing

Before publishing, preview how the headline, URL, meta description, and lead image will appear in a search result and in a social link card. Check that nothing is truncated, that the image is legible at small size, and that the combination accurately represents the story. A story can be accurate in its body but misleading in its search-facing elements; the elements are what most people see, so they deserve the same editorial care.

## Common Traps

### Writing Clickbait Headlines the Body Cannot Support

Headlines that tease, exaggerate, or mislead to win clicks create a gap between promise and delivery. This is a trap because the headline itself becomes misinformation; readers who click expecting one thing and find another lose trust, and the gap can distort public understanding. The headline must be supportable by the reporting.

### Stripping Attribution to Make Headlines Punchier

Removing "police say" or "allegedly" to create a cleaner, more clickable headline converts a sourced claim into the publication's own assertion. This is a trap because it increases legal exposure and misleads readers about the strength of the claim. Carry attribution into search-facing text when the claim is attributed.

### Keyword Stuffing That Harms Readability and Ranking

Unnatural repetition of search terms reads poorly to humans and is discounted or penalized by modern search engines. This is a trap because it sacrifices the reader experience for an optimization that no longer works. Use key terms naturally, once or twice, in context.

### Ignoring the Meta Description and Letting Search Generate It

Leaving the meta description blank lets the search engine pull a snippet that may be misleading or out of context. This is a trap because the generated snippet is the reader's first impression of the story. Write the meta description deliberately as an accurate summary.

### Changing URLs and Breaking Links

Renaming a URL after publication breaks every existing share and link and discards search equity. This is a trap because the short-term reason for the change is usually minor and the long-term cost is significant. Keep URLs stable; use redirects if a change is unavoidable.

### Mislabeling Content Type in Structured Data

Marking an opinion piece, sponsored content, or satire as a straight news article misleads readers and platforms. This is a trap because it can trigger policy violations and erodes the distinction between news and other content. Ensure structured data matches the actual content type.

### Over-Optimizing Alt Text With Unrelated Keywords

Using alt text as a dumping ground for keywords unrelated to the image harms accessibility and misleads search engines. This is a trap because it abuses an accessibility feature for a marginal gain. Alt text should accurately describe the image.

## Self-Check

- Does the headline accurately reflect what the reporting shows, without exaggeration, teasing, or unsupported claims?
- Is attribution and appropriate hedging preserved in the headline and title tag for any attributed or contested claim?
- Is the title tag within display length, and is the meta description an accurate summary rather than hype?
- Is the URL clean, descriptive, lowercase, and stable, with no plan to change it after publication?
- Does the structured data correctly identify the content type (news, opinion, sponsored, etc.) and accurately label author, date, and section?
- Does image alt text accurately describe the image without keyword stuffing or unsupported claims?
- Have you previewed how the story will appear in search results and social link cards before publishing?
- For headlines and metadata involving legal risk, accusations, or sensitive identifications, has an editor reviewed the search-facing text, and where defamation risk exists, has counsel been consulted?
