# Audience Discovery and Evergreen Content

Not every story is a one-day event. Some of the most valuable journalism serves readers over months or years: the explainer that gets rediscovered every time the topic returns to the news, the resource page that readers return to for reliable information, the authoritative coverage that establishes a publication as the place to go for a subject. Building for discovery and evergreen value is different from chasing daily news; it requires anticipating what readers will search for, deciding which stories deserve long-term maintenance, and committing to keep accurate information available over time. The judgment problem is that the techniques that build durable audience value (topic authority, updated resources, comprehensive coverage) can pull against the daily-news rhythm and can tempt shortcuts, shallow "SEO content," that fool search engines but fail readers. This skill covers how to plan for discovery and evergreen value while maintaining the accuracy, depth, and editorial standards that earn lasting trust.

## Core Rules

### Build Topic Authority Through Sustained, Genuine Coverage

Search engines and readers both reward publications that demonstrably know a subject deeply, through a body of real reporting over time. Topic authority is earned by covering a beat consistently, breaking real stories, and demonstrating expertise, not by publishing a flurry of shallow keyword-targeted articles. Before investing in evergreen or discovery content on a topic, ask whether your publication has, or is building, genuine authority through original reporting on that subject. If not, the right first step is more reporting, not more optimization. Authority that is performed without substance eventually collapses.

### Identify Genuine Reader Need, Not Just Search Volume

Search data can reveal what readers are looking for, but search volume alone is not a mandate to cover a topic. Distinguish genuine reader need, questions that matter to people's lives, decisions, or understanding, from ephemeral curiosity or low-quality demand. Prioritize discovery content that serves a real informational need your publication can meet with authority and accuracy. Avoid manufacturing content to chase search volume on topics where you add no value; that crowds out better work and erodes the publication's identity.

### Decide Deliberately Which Stories Become Evergreen

Not every story should be maintained indefinitely. Decide deliberately which stories have lasting value and deserve to be treated as evergreen resources: explainers of recurring topics, authoritative accounts of significant events, resource guides, and reference material. For these, commit to reviewing and updating them on a schedule, keeping facts current, adding new developments, and clearly showing the last-reviewed date. For stories without lasting value, let them age naturally with clear dates. Treating every story as evergreen dilutes the effort; treating none as evergreen wastes durable value.

### Maintain Evergreen Content With Real Editorial Care

An evergreen resource is only valuable if it stays accurate. Assign ownership for maintaining key evergreen pieces, schedule regular reviews, and update them when the underlying facts change. When you update, follow the same standards as original publication: verify new facts, attribute claims, and note what changed. A stale evergreen page that contradicts current reality is worse than no page at all, because it misleads readers who trust it as a reliable reference. Maintenance is not optional; it is the cost of evergreen value.

### Link Evergreen and Daily Coverage Into a Coherent Whole

The most powerful discovery strategy connects daily news to evergreen context. When a breaking story relates to an existing explainer or resource, link them prominently in both directions: the news story points readers to context, and the evergreen page notes the latest development. This serves readers (who get context and news together), signals topic authority, and keeps evergreen pages fresh through related updates. Build the habit of asking, for every news story, "what existing context should this link to, and what context should we create?"

### Avoid Shallow "SEO Content" That Fails the Reader

The temptation in discovery work is to produce content engineered to rank, keyword-targeted, formulaic, shallow, that satisfies an algorithm without satisfying a reader. This is a failure of purpose. Every piece of content should clear the bar of genuinely informing a reader who arrives via search. If a topic does not warrant real reporting or genuine expertise, do not cover it. Reader-trusting, substantive content ranks better in the long run anyway, because search engines increasingly reward quality and penalize thin content.

### Use Search Data as a Signal, Not a Command

Search and audience data are valuable signals about what readers want and how they look for it. Use them to understand reader needs, to find gaps in your coverage, to choose the language readers actually use, and to identify topics where your authority is underused. But do not let data override editorial judgment about what matters. Some of the most important journalism serves needs readers do not yet know to search for. Balance data-driven discovery with mission-driven coverage that the data would never surface.

### Be Honest About What Evergreen Content Is and Isn't

Label evergreen content accurately. A resource page should be clearly a resource; an explainer should be clearly an explainer; an opinion piece should never be framed as a neutral reference. Readers who arrive via search often cannot tell what kind of content they have found, so make the nature of the content explicit. Do not present an outdated story as current, or a partial account as comprehensive. Honesty about what the content is protects readers and sustains the trust that discovery depends on.

## Common Traps

### Producing Shallow Content to Chase Search Volume

Manufacturing keyword-targeted articles without genuine reporting or expertise. This is a trap because it fails readers, erodes the publication's identity, and is increasingly penalized by search engines. Every piece must genuinely inform.

### Letting Evergreen Content Go Stale

Treating a resource page as "done" and never updating it until it contradicts reality. This is a trap because stale evergreen content misleads the readers who trust it most. Schedule and own maintenance.

### Confusing Search Volume With Reader Need

Covering a topic because it has high search volume rather than because it serves a genuine need your publication can meet. This is a trap because it produces low-value content that crowds out better work. Prioritize genuine need and authority.

### Performing Authority Without Substance

Publishing a volume of coverage to appear authoritative on a topic without the underlying reporting. This is a trap because performed authority eventually collapses and damages credibility. Build real authority through original reporting first.

### Failing to Connect Daily News and Evergreen Context

Treating news and evergreen as separate streams rather than linking them. This is a trap because readers lose the context that would make both more valuable, and topic authority is underbuilt. Link them in both directions.

### Mislabeling Content Type for Discovery Advantage

Framing an opinion piece, sponsored content, or partial account as a neutral, comprehensive resource to win search traffic. This is a trap because it misleads readers about what they are reading. Label content type honestly.

### Letting Data Override Editorial Mission

Covering only what the data shows readers searching for, at the expense of important stories readers do not yet know to seek. This is a trap because it abandons the journalistic mission that creates lasting value. Balance data with mission.

## Self-Check

- Does any discovery or evergreen content clear the bar of genuinely informing a reader, rather than being shallow "SEO content"?
- Have you identified which stories have lasting value and committed to maintaining them with scheduled reviews and updates?
- Is evergreen content kept accurate, with last-reviewed dates visible and updates following the same standards as original publication?
- Are daily news and evergreen context linked in both directions so readers get news and context together?
- Is topic authority built through genuine, sustained reporting rather than performed through volume of shallow coverage?
- Is search data used as a signal to understand reader needs and coverage gaps, without overriding editorial judgment about what matters?
- Is the content type (news, explainer, opinion, resource) labeled honestly so search-arriving readers know what they have found?
- For evergreen or discovery content involving legal risk, medical or financial guidance, or sensitive topics, has an editor reviewed the content for accuracy and appropriate caveats, and where specialized risk exists, has appropriate expert or legal review been sought?
