# Digital Ethics and Corrections Online

The digital record is permanent, searchable, and copied. An error published online does not stay where it was published: it is indexed by search engines, cached, screenshotted, aggregated, and quoted by other outlets, often within minutes. Correcting an online error is therefore not a single act of editing a page; it is an attempt to catch and repair information that has already escaped the page. At the same time, the digital medium offers tools print never had: the ability to fix errors instantly, to explain what changed, to maintain transparent version history, and to reach the readers who saw the original. This skill covers the judgment involved in handling accuracy, corrections, updates, and the permanent online record in ways that build rather than erode trust.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Digital Story as a Permanent, Searchable Record

Unlike yesterday's newspaper, a digital story will be found by readers for years through search, and it will be read as a current statement of fact unless signaled otherwise. Build and maintain every story with this permanence in mind. Ensure facts are accurate at publication, keep the story updated as circumstances change, and make the publication date and last-update date visible. When a story becomes substantially outdated, decide deliberately whether to update it, label it as archived, unpublish it with explanation, or leave it with a clear note that it reflects the situation as of a specific date. Never leave a stale, misleading story standing as if current.

### Correct Errors Immediately, Visibly, and With Explanation

When an online error is identified, fix it at once and add a visible correction note. The note should state what was wrong, what the correct information is, and, where useful, how the error occurred. Place the correction prominently enough that a reader returning to the page will see it, not buried at the bottom. For significant errors, consider a standalone correction or editor's note and, where appropriate, a notification to readers who may have seen the original through newsletters or social posts. The goal is not only to fix the page but to reach the audience that encountered the error.

### Never Silently Edit a Published Story

Silent edits, changing a published fact without any sign that the story changed, are among the most damaging practices in digital journalism. They make the publication appear honest only to readers who never saw the original, and they destroy trust among readers who notice. Any material change, a corrected fact, a removed quote, a changed headline, a retracted claim, must be accompanied by a visible note explaining the change. Routine copy edits and typo fixes generally do not require notes, but any change to meaning, attribution, or substance does. When unsure whether a change is material, default to noting it.

### Manage the Spread of Errors Beyond Your Own Page

Because online errors are copied and aggregated, correcting your own page is necessary but not sufficient. Consider where else the error may have traveled: search engine caches, social posts from your official accounts, syndication partners, newsletters already sent, aggregators. Where feasible, correct or delete erroneous social posts, request that syndication partners run the correction, and add structured correction markup that helps search engines surface the corrected version. Acknowledge that you cannot catch every copy, but take reasonable steps to limit the spread of the harm you caused.

### Distinguish Corrections, Updates, and Clarifications

These are different acts and should be labeled differently. A correction fixes a factual error. An update adds new information that did not exist or was not known at publication. A clarification refines or expands ambiguous reporting that was not wrong but was incomplete or easily misunderstood. Using these labels precisely helps readers understand what changed and why, and it protects the credibility of the correction system itself. A change labeled "update" that actually fixes an error conceals a mistake; a change labeled "correction" that merely adds context devalues the correction label.

### Handle Unpublishing With Care and Transparency

Sometimes a story should be removed entirely: it contained a serious error, it named a vulnerable person who should not have been named, or it was published in error. Unpublishing is a serious step because it alters the public record and can itself look like concealment. When you unpublish, replace the story with a transparent note explaining that it was removed, why, and when, rather than leaving a dead link or a 404. Consult an editor before unpublishing, and document the decision. The right to be forgotten and source-safety considerations may also justify removal in specific cases; handle these with clear editorial criteria, not ad hoc.

### Maintain Archive Integrity

Old stories are part of the historical record. Resist the temptation to retroactively "improve" archives by rewriting old stories to match current standards or knowledge. When an old story contains an error, add a correction note rather than rewriting history. When an old story reflects outdated language or framing, consider an editor's note explaining the context rather than silently modernizing it. The archive should be an honest record of what was published, with transparent annotations where understanding has changed.

### Be Accountable in Public

Digital accountability means explaining your journalism to your readers, not only when you err. Consider publishing methodology notes for complex investigations, responding substantively to reader questions and critiques, and being reachable when readers spot problems. A publication that is visibly responsive to error and open about its process earns trust that pays off when serious mistakes do occur. Treat reader corrections as a resource, not an annoyance; readers who catch your errors are often your most engaged audience.

## Common Traps

### Silent Edits That Conceal Material Changes

Changing facts, quotes, or headlines without a correction note makes the publication look clean only to new readers. This is a trap because readers who saw the original notice and lose trust, and because the uncorrected version continues to spread elsewhere. Any material change requires a visible note.

### Burying Corrections Where Readers Won't See Them

Placing correction notes at the very bottom of a long story, or in a separate corrections page no one visits, effectively hides the fix. This is a trap because it satisfies a form of accountability without achieving its substance; readers who return to the page never learn it was wrong. Place corrections prominently enough to be seen.

### Using "Update" to Conceal an Error

Labeling a factual fix as an "update" softens the appearance of a mistake but misleads readers about what happened. This is a trap because it erodes the meaning of both labels and prevents honest accounting. Reserve "correction" for errors and "update" for genuinely new information.

### Leaving Stale Stories Standing as Current

A story that was accurate at publication can become misleading if circumstances change and it is never updated. This is a trap because future readers find it via search and treat it as current fact. Maintain important stories, or label them clearly with their date and context.

### Unpublishing Without Explanation

Removing a story and leaving a dead link or 404 looks like concealment and breaks the record. This is a trap because the disappearance itself becomes the story and invites suspicion. Replace removed stories with a transparent note explaining the removal.

### Treating the Page as the Only Place the Error Exists

Correcting only the original page ignores that the error has been copied, cached, and shared. This is a trap because the harm continues wherever the error traveled. Take reasonable steps to correct social posts, notify partners, and limit spread.

### Rewriting Archive History to Match Current Standards

Retroactively rewriting old stories erases the honest record of what was published and when understanding changed. This is a trap because it makes the publication look consistently correct at the cost of historical truth. Annotate rather than rewrite.

## Self-Check

- For any material change to a published story, is there a visible note explaining what changed and why, rather than a silent edit?
- Are corrections placed prominently enough that a returning reader will see them, and labeled correctly as correction, update, or clarification?
- Have you taken reasonable steps to address the spread of any error beyond the original page (social posts, syndication, caches)?
- Are publication and last-update dates visible on every story, so readers can judge currency?
- For stories that are outdated, have you decided deliberately whether to update, archive with a note, or remove with explanation?
- Have you avoided rewriting archive history, using annotations and notes instead of retroactive edits?
- Is there a clear editorial process for deciding when unpublishing is justified, with transparency about the removal?
- For corrections involving legal risk, defamation, or significant harm, has an editor approved the correction and, where appropriate, has counsel reviewed the handling before publication?
