# Digital Newsgathering And Online Research

A large and growing share of newsgathering happens online: search, social platforms, public records databases, archived material, satellite and geospatial data, and the open-source intelligence techniques that combine them. This digital newsgathering is powerful because it makes evidence accessible that no previous generation of reporters could reach, and it enables investigations built substantially from open sources. But it carries distinctive judgment problems. Online material is abundant, mutable, and often of uncertain provenance; platforms shape what is visible through algorithms that reporters do not control; digital evidence can be fabricated, manipulated, or stripped of context; and the ease of gathering online material can substitute for the verification that establishes whether it is true. The harm this skill prevents is the construction of reporting on digital material that was abundant and convincing but unverified, manipulated, or misleadingly decontextualized, failures that are particularly damaging because online-sourced stories often travel fast and far before any correction can catch them.

Use this skill when gathering news or conducting research online, using search, social platforms, databases, or OSINT techniques, evaluating online source reliability, managing digital evidence, or building reporting from online sources. The main risk is that an agent treats the abundance and accessibility of online material as a substitute for the verification disciplines that establish its accuracy, provenance, and context.

## Core Rules

### Treat Online Material As Leads Requiring Verification, Not As Established Fact

The abundance of online material creates a false sense of sufficiency: if a claim appears in many sources, if a document is easy to find, if a social media post is widely shared, the material can feel verified by its accessibility. But online material is often unverified, repeated from a common origin, fabricated, or stripped of context. Treat every piece of online material as a lead to be verified, not as established fact, regardless of how widely it circulates. Apply the same verification disciplines to digital material as to any other source: trace provenance, confirm authenticity, establish context, and corroborate against independent evidence. See the source-verification and verifying-user-generated-content skills.

The ease of finding material is not evidence of its truth; a viral fact is not a verified fact.

### Evaluate The Reliability And Provenance Of Online Sources

Online sources vary enormously in reliability, and evaluating them requires examining who produced the material, why, and with what basis for knowledge. Distinguish among primary sources, original documents, official records, and direct witnesses; secondary reporting and aggregation; and tertiary repetition and commentary. A claim sourced to an official database is different from one sourced to a blog that cites a tweet that cites an unknown origin. Trace the provenance of online material to its source, and weigh it according to the reliability of that source and the independence of the evidence. Be alert to sources that appear independent but share a common origin, and to platforms and accounts that exist to inject material into news cycles.

For databases and records, understand how the data was produced, what it includes and excludes, and how current it is, because a dataset's limitations are often invisible.

### Preserve And Document Digital Evidence And Its Provenance

Digital material is mutable: web pages change, posts are deleted, accounts disappear, and documents are edited. When you encounter material relevant to reporting, preserve it immediately: archive pages, capture screenshots with timestamps and URLs, save documents with their metadata, and record where and when you obtained each item. Document the provenance of each piece of digital evidence, the source, the date of access, the method of discovery, so that the material can be authenticated and the chain of custody established. Digital evidence without preserved provenance is far weaker, because it cannot be re-examined or defended if challenged.

Use archiving tools that create durable, timestamped records, because a screenshot alone may not establish when or from where the material was captured.

### Understand How Platforms Shape Visibility

Search engines and social platforms do not present the neutral totality of online material; they rank, filter, and amplify according to algorithms that shape what is visible. Understand that what you find through search or social is a curated subset, shaped by the platform's logic, your own query and history, and the dynamics of virality. A topic that appears prominent in search results may reflect algorithmic amplification rather than inherent significance; an absence of results may reflect suppression, poor indexing, or your query rather than genuine absence. Do not infer the state of the world from platform visibility without accounting for the curation that produced it.

Diversify your discovery methods: use multiple search tools, multiple platforms, direct source contact, and offline methods, to avoid building a picture from a single curated stream.

### Apply OSINT Techniques Rigorously And Honestly

Open-source intelligence techniques, geolocation, chronolocation, satellite and aerial imagery analysis, social media cross-referencing, and database correlation, are powerful tools for digital investigation, and they enable reporting that would otherwise be impossible. Apply them rigorously: document each step of the analysis, preserve the evidence at each stage, distinguish what the technique establishes from what it infers, and subject OSINT findings to the same corroboration as any other evidence. Be honest about the limits: geolocation establishes where an image was taken, not who took it or why; a satellite image shows what was visible, not the intent behind it. OSINT findings are strongest when they converge with other evidence and weakest when a single technique is stretched beyond what it establishes.

For complex OSINT, consult specialists and use established methodologies, because the techniques are easy to apply incorrectly and the errors are hard for lay reviewers to detect.

### Navigate The Ethical Boundaries Of Digital Investigation

Digital newsgathering raises ethical boundaries that offline reporting also faces but that take specific forms online. Accessing material that is public is not always ethical to use; de-anonymizing individuals from digital traces can expose them to harm; scraping or accessing material in ways that violate platform terms or law creates legal and ethical exposure; and investigating private individuals' digital lives without public-interest justification is intrusive. Distinguish between scrutiny of public officials and institutions, where the public interest is strong, and exposure of private individuals, where it is weak. Apply the same harm and public-interest weighing to digital investigation as to any reporting, and do not let the technical ease of digital access override the ethical judgment about whether the investigation is justified.

See the harm-minimization and privacy-law skills for the ethical and legal frameworks that govern digital investigation.

### Corroborate Online Findings With Offline Reporting Where Possible

The strongest digital investigations are corroborated by offline reporting: contacting the people and institutions the digital material concerns, obtaining documents through official channels, and verifying online findings against real-world evidence. Online material can be wrong, manipulated, or misleadingly decontextualized in ways that only offline corroboration can reveal, and a story built entirely from online sources is more vulnerable than one tested against the world it describes. Where possible, take the digital findings into the field: contact sources, request records, and seek the response of subjects. The combination of digital breadth and offline depth produces the most defensible reporting.

Where offline corroboration is impossible, as in some international or OSINT investigations, be explicit about the reliance on online sources and the verification performed.

## Common Traps

### Treating Abundance As Verification

Widely circulated material feels verified by consensus. This is a trap because circulation measures spread, not truth. Verify online material against its provenance and independent evidence.

### Failing To Preserve Mutable Digital Evidence

Web pages and posts change or disappear. This is a trap because unpreserved evidence cannot be re-examined or defended. Archive and document provenance at the point of discovery.

### Inferring Reality From Platform Visibility

Algorithms curate what is visible. This is a trap because prominence may reflect amplification, not significance. Diversify discovery and account for platform curation.

### Stretching OSINT Beyond What It Establishes

A technique proves one thing and not another. This is a trap because over-reading the finding produces confident error. Distinguish what OSINT establishes from what it infers.

### Using Public Material Without Ethical Judgment

Accessible does not mean ethical to use. This is a trap because digital ease overrides harm and privacy judgment. Weigh public interest and harm for digital investigation as for any reporting.

### Building Only From Online Sources Without Offline Corroboration

Purely online sourcing is more vulnerable to manipulation and decontextualization. This is a trap because offline corroboration reveals what online material conceals. Corroborate where possible.

### Trusting Apparent Source Independence

Sources that look independent may share an origin. This is a trap because false independence manufactures corroboration. Trace provenance to actual origins.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is online material treated as leads requiring verification, with the same verification disciplines applied as to any other source, regardless of how widely it circulates?
- [ ] Is the reliability and provenance of each online source evaluated, tracing material to its origin and weighing it according to source reliability and evidence independence?
- [ ] Is digital evidence preserved and its provenance documented at the point of discovery, using archiving tools that create durable, timestamped records?
- [ ] Is the role of platform algorithms in shaping visibility understood, with discovery diversified to avoid building a picture from a single curated stream?
- [ ] Are OSINT techniques applied rigorously and honestly, with each step documented, limits acknowledged, and findings distinguished from inferences?
- [ ] Are the ethical boundaries of digital investigation navigated, distinguishing scrutiny of public officials from exposure of private individuals, and weighing public interest against harm?
- [ ] Are online findings corroborated with offline reporting, contacting sources and obtaining records, where possible?
- [ ] Before publication, is there any online-sourced claim whose accuracy rests on accessibility or circulation rather than on verified provenance and independent corroboration?
- [ ] For complex OSINT or digital investigations, have specialists and established methodologies been used, with the analysis documented for review?
- [ ] Where reliance is substantially on online sources, is that reliance and the verification performed made explicit to editors and, where relevant, to readers?
