# Armed Actor Engagement

Engaging armed actors, whether state military, insurgents, militias, or organized criminal groups, is often necessary to report a conflict but introduces risks that distinguish it from ordinary interviewing: the actors may target or detain journalists, may use the engagement for propaganda, may impose conditions that compromise independence, and may react unpredictably to questions or coverage. The judgment problem is that access requires some relationship with the armed actor, but the relationship can compromise the reporting, endanger the reporter, or serve the actor's aims at the expense of the truth. A defensible engagement treats access as conditional and reviewable, maintains independence throughout, and plans for the specific risks of dealing with people who have the means and willingness to use force.

Use this skill when planning to engage armed actors for reporting, negotiating access or safe passage, managing checkpoint and detention interactions, or interviewing combatants. The main risk is that an agent pursues access without a clear assessment of the actor's motives, accepts conditions that compromise independence, or fails to plan for detention, kidnapping, or propaganda exploitation.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Actor's Motives For Granting Access

Armed actors grant or deny access for reasons that serve their aims, and understanding those motives is the foundation of safe and independent engagement. An actor may grant access to shape coverage in their favor, to use the journalist's presence as legitimacy, to gather intelligence on the journalist or their contacts, to detain or kidnap, or, more rarely, because they believe accurate coverage serves their cause. Assess the actor's likely motives before seeking access, using intelligence from security advisers, local contacts, other journalists who have dealt with them, and the actor's track record with the press.

Access that seems generous may be a trap, and access that is grudging may be more honest. Do not assume that an actor who is friendly to the press is safe, or that one who is hostile is the greater danger. The question is what the actor gains from the engagement and whether that gain is compatible with independent reporting. If the actor's motive is to use the engagement for propaganda or intelligence, the access is compromised regardless of how it is framed.

### Negotiate Access And Conditions Without Compromising Independence

Access to armed actors often comes with conditions: minders who accompany the reporter, pre-approved questions, limits on whom the reporter may speak to, or editorial review of the coverage before publication. Some conditions are logistical and acceptable, such as traveling in a convoy for safety; others compromise independence, such as agreeing to submit copy for approval or to avoid certain subjects. Negotiate access while holding the line on independence, and be prepared to walk away if the conditions would make the coverage propaganda rather than journalism.

Be explicit about what is and is not acceptable, with the editor's backing, and do not agree to conditions in the moment that the editor has not authorized. A reporter on the ground, under pressure from an armed actor, is poorly positioned to negotiate editorial independence, and the terms should be set before deployment. Where access requires accepting a minder or limits, report those constraints transparently to the reader, so the coverage is not presented as more independent than it is.

### Plan For Checkpoints, Detention, And Unpredictable Encounters

Interactions with armed actors at checkpoints, during patrols, and in detention are among the highest-risk moments in conflict reporting, because they are unpredictable, because the reporter has little control, and because a wrong word or document can escalate. Plan for these encounters before deploying: know what identification and credentials to carry and in what language, whether press identification provides protection or increases risk in this specific context, how to conduct oneself at checkpoints, and what to do if detained.

Different conflicts have different dynamics around press identification. In some, clear press credentials provide protection; in others, they make the reporter a target. The decision to display or conceal press identification is context-specific and should be made with security advisers based on the specific threat. Carry redundant identification, and ensure the team and editor know the plan if someone is detained. See the incident-response-and-extraction skill.

### Interview Combatants Without Becoming A Propaganda Tool

Interviewing combatants is necessary to report their perspective, but the interview can be used as propaganda if the combatant controls the setting, the questions, and the framing, and if the resulting coverage presents their claims without context or verification. Conduct interviews that preserve the reporter's control over questions and framing, that allow follow-up and challenge, and that do not present the combatant's statements as unverified fact.

Be transparent with the reader about the conditions of the interview: whether it was conducted under supervision, whether questions were restricted, whether the combatant reviewed or approved the coverage. An interview conducted under the armed actor's control is not the same as an independent interview, and presenting it as such misleads the reader and serves the actor's propaganda aims. Verify claims made in the interview against other sources, and contextualize them within the conflict's facts.

### Maintain Independence And Avoid Embedding's Distortions

Embedding with an armed actor, traveling with and depending on them for security and access, provides proximity but distorts coverage in ways that are often invisible to the reporter. The reporter who depends on the armed actor for food, shelter, security, and access tends, often unconsciously, to adopt the actor's framing, to see the conflict from the actor's perspective, and to underreport the actor's misconduct. This is a documented pattern, not a failure of individual character, and it must be actively countered.

If embedding is necessary, counter its distortions deliberately: maintain contact with independent sources outside the armed actor's control, seek perspectives from the actor's adversaries and from civilians affected by all sides, and remain alert to the framing the embedding imposes. Disclose the embedding arrangement to the reader, so the coverage is understood in context. Where possible, balance embedding with independent movement, even if more dangerous, to avoid total dependence on one side's perspective.

### Protect Sources And Contacts From Armed Actor Retaliation

Engaging armed actors creates risk not only for the reporter but for the sources and contacts who facilitate the engagement, especially local staff, fixers, and civilians who speak with the reporter. An armed actor may monitor the reporter's contacts, interrogate or punish those seen as collaborating, or use the reporter's presence to identify and target critics. Protect these contacts through the source-protection practice: do not reveal their identities in notes or coverage, be discreet about who the reporter meets, and plan for the contacts' safety after the engagement.

A reporter who leaves a trail of identified contacts who are then targeted by the armed actor has caused serious harm, regardless of the coverage's quality. The duty of protection extends to everyone the engagement exposes, and the engagement plan must account for their risk. See the source-identity-protection skill.

### Have A Clear Plan For When To Disengage

Engagement with an armed actor should have a clear disengagement plan: the conditions under which the reporter will end the engagement, leave the area, or cease contact, and how that will be done safely. An engagement that has no exit becomes open-ended, with the reporter dependent on the actor's continued tolerance and vulnerable to changing conditions. Define the triggers for disengagement in advance, with the editor, and execute them when met, even if the story is incomplete.

Disengagement may itself carry risk, as the actor may resent the departure or suspect the reporter of hostile intent. Plan the disengagement as carefully as the initial access, with attention to how the reporter will leave, what they will say, and how they will manage the actor's reaction. An engagement that cannot be safely ended should not be begun.

## Common Traps

### Pursuing Access Without Assessing The Actor's Motives

Access that seems generous may serve the actor's propaganda, intelligence, or targeting aims. Assess motives before seeking access.

### Accepting Conditions That Compromise Independence

Agreeing to submit copy for approval, avoid subjects, or accept minders without disclosure makes the coverage propaganda. Negotiate access with independence and disclose constraints.

### Displaying Or Concealing Press Identification Without Context

Press identification provides protection in some conflicts and increases risk in others. Make the decision with security advisers based on the specific threat.

### Presenting A Controlled Interview As Independent

An interview under the armed actor's control is not independent. Disclose the conditions and verify and contextualize the claims.

### Underestimating Embedding's Distortions

Embedding distorts coverage toward the armed actor's framing. Counter the distortions deliberately and disclose the arrangement.

### Leaving A Trail Of Contacts For The Actor To Target

Local staff, fixers, and civilians who facilitate engagement face retaliation. Protect their identities and plan for their safety.

### Engaging Without A Disengagement Plan

An engagement with no exit becomes open-ended and vulnerable. Define disengagement triggers and plan the departure.

## Self-Check

Before treating armed actor engagement as sound, verify:

- The armed actor's motives for granting access are assessed before seeking it, using intelligence from security advisers, local contacts, other journalists, and the actor's track record.
- Access and conditions are negotiated without compromising independence, with unacceptable conditions rejected and acceptable logistical constraints disclosed to the reader.
- The plan accounts for checkpoints, detention, and unpredictable encounters, including identification, conduct, and the team and editor's response if someone is detained.
- Interviews with combatants preserve the reporter's control over questions and framing, with conditions disclosed and claims verified and contextualized.
- Embedding's distortions are countered through independent sources, adversary and civilian perspectives, and active alertness to framing, with the arrangement disclosed.
- Sources and contacts exposed by the engagement are protected, with identities concealed and safety planned for after the engagement.
- A clear disengagement plan defines the triggers and method for ending the engagement safely, executed when met even if the story is incomplete.
- The engagement plan is reviewed by a responsible editor and a security adviser before deployment, with clear decision authority.
- The reporter is not the sole decision-maker on continuing or disengaging, given the pressures of the ground.
- Armed actor engagement supports editorial judgment; engagement decisions should involve a senior editor, a security adviser, and, where detention or legal threat is a risk, legal counsel, recognizing that access is never worth becoming a propaganda tool or a target and that the engagement may need to be declined.
