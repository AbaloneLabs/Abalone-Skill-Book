# Conflict Risk Assessment

Conflict reporting carries risks that ordinary reporting does not: armed actors who may target or detain journalists, unpredictable violence, degraded medical and communications infrastructure, and psychological harm that accumulates over time. The judgment problem is that the pressure to be on the ground, the professional rewards for proximity to danger, and the adrenaline of the moment push reporters toward risk that is neither necessary nor justified, while the story value of presence may be real and significant. A defensible assessment separates the story value of the coverage from the heroics of the reporter, quantifies the risks honestly, and makes the go or no-go decision against a threshold that treats the team's safety as a precondition of the work, not a trade-off against it.

Use this skill when assessing whether to report from a conflict zone, civil unrest, or volatile situation, planning team composition and equipment, or deciding whether continued presence is justified. The main risk is that an agent overvalues presence and proximity, underestimates the risk, or makes the go decision under pressure without a clear threshold or exit plan.

## Core Rules

### Separate Story Value From Reporter Heroics

The first question in conflict assessment is what coverage the story requires and whether ground presence is necessary to achieve it. Some stories require a reporter on the ground to bear witness, to interview people directly, and to convey the reality of the situation in ways that wire copy and remote reporting cannot. Other stories can be reported as well or better from a distance, using local stringers, satellite imagery, open-source investigation, and remote interviews, without exposing the team. Distinguish the two honestly.

The professional culture of conflict reporting rewards proximity and presence, and reporters may push for ground deployment because it is expected, prestigious, or personally compelling, not because the story requires it. Assess the story value independently of the reporter's drive to be there. If the story does not require ground presence, the risk is not justified, regardless of the reporter's willingness or the competitive pressure.

### Quantify The Risks Honestly, Including The Invisible Ones

Conflict risks include direct violence, targeting by armed actors, detention, kidnapping, injury from crossfire or crowd weapons, and the degradation of medical, communications, and evacuation infrastructure. They also include risks to local staff, fixers, drivers, and translators, who often face greater danger than the visiting reporter because they cannot leave, who may be targeted for collaboration, and whose risk outlasts the assignment. And they include psychological harm, trauma, and post-traumatic stress, which accumulate across assignments and which reporters and editors chronically underestimate.

Quantify these risks honestly, using current intelligence from security advisers, local contacts, other news organizations, and credible open sources. Do not rely on the reporter's sense of the situation, which is distorted by proximity and adrenaline. Identify the specific threats in the specific location over the specific period, and assess the team's ability to mitigate them. See the journalist-safety skill.

### Set A Clear Go Or No-Go Threshold Before The Pressure Hits

The decision to deploy into a conflict zone should be made against a threshold set in advance, before the competitive pressure and the reporter's drive distort judgment. The threshold should define what risks are acceptable for what story value, what conditions trigger a no-go or an extraction, and who has the authority to make the call. A reporter on the ground, in the moment, should not be the sole decision-maker on whether to stay, because the pressures they face distort their assessment.

The threshold should treat the team's safety as a precondition of the work, not a variable to trade against story value. There is no story worth a life, and a threshold that allows safety to be traded for scoop is not a threshold. Where the risk exceeds the threshold, the coverage changes, is done from a distance, or is not done. This is a hard call in a competitive environment, and it requires editorial leadership willing to hold the line.

### Plan Team Composition, Equipment, And Evacuation Before Deploying

Deployment into a conflict zone requires planning the team, the equipment, and the exit before going in. Team composition should match the threat: experienced conflict reporters for high-threat environments, adequate local knowledge through trusted fixers, medical training and kits, and a clear chain of command. Equipment should include personal protective equipment appropriate to the threat, communications gear with redundancy, and identification as press where that provides protection, recognizing that in some conflicts press identification increases risk.

Most importantly, plan the exit before entering. Know how the team will leave if the situation deteriorates, where the nearest safe medical care is, how evacuation will be funded and arranged, and what triggers extraction. A team that goes in without an exit plan is committed to staying regardless of how the situation changes, which is not a plan but an abdication. See the incident-response-and-extraction skill.

### Account For The Risk To Local Staff And Fixers

Local staff, fixers, drivers, and translators face risks that visiting reporters do not: they cannot leave when the assignment ends, they may be targeted for collaboration with foreign media, and their families and communities may face retaliation. The assessment must account for their risk, not just the reporter's, and the duty of care extends to them. This includes fair compensation, protective equipment, training, and a plan for their safety after the assignment, including possible relocation or continued support if they are targeted.

Treating local staff as disposable logistics, paid minimally and left to face the consequences, is both unethical and a practical failure, because trusted local partners are the foundation of conflict coverage and their loss or targeting degrades future work. The assessment of whether to deploy includes whether the team can protect its local members, not just whether the visiting reporter is willing to go.

### Build In Psychological Safety And Trauma Support

Conflict reporting causes psychological harm that accumulates across assignments, manifesting as trauma, post-traumatic stress, burnout, and changed behavior that affects both work and life. This harm is invisible, slow-acting, and chronically underestimated by reporters and editors who treat physical safety as the only safety that counts. Build psychological safety into the assessment: mandatory rest between deployments, access to trauma-informed counseling, peer support, and an editorial culture that treats psychological injury as real and reportable.

A reporter who has been deployed repeatedly without recovery is a higher risk to themselves and the team, and the assessment of whether to deploy them again must account for their cumulative exposure. The macho culture that treats psychological harm as weakness or as the cost of the work is a failure of duty of care and a contributor to the harm.

### Reassess Continuously And Be Willings To Leave

A conflict assessment is not a one-time go decision but a continuous reassessment as the situation evolves. Conditions change rapidly in conflict zones: a safe route becomes dangerous, an armed group advances, a checkpoint changes hands, medical infrastructure is destroyed. The team on the ground, in contact with the editor and security adviser, must reassess continuously against the threshold and be willing to change the coverage, pull back, or extract when conditions deteriorate.

The willingness to leave is the test of whether the threshold is real. A team that commits to staying regardless of conditions has no threshold, only inertia. Editors must support the decision to leave, even when it means losing the story or ceding ground to competitors, because the alternative is accepting risks that were deemed unacceptable at the outset.

## Common Traps

### Equating Presence With Story Value

Ground presence may be prestigious and personally compelling but unnecessary for the story. Assess story value independently of the reporter's drive to be there.

### Underestimating Risk Under Proximity And Adrenaline

The reporter's sense of the situation on the ground is distorted by proximity and adrenaline. Use current intelligence and security advisers, not just the reporter's feel.

### Letting The Reporter On The Ground Make The Stay-Or-Go Call Alone

A reporter in the moment faces pressures that distort their assessment. Set the threshold in advance and have the editor hold it.

### Deploying Without An Exit Plan

A team that goes in without knowing how it will leave is committed to staying regardless of conditions. Plan the exit before entering.

### Ignoring The Risk To Local Staff And Fixers

Local staff face greater danger than visiting reporters and cannot leave. The duty of care extends to them, including their safety after the assignment.

### Treating Psychological Harm As The Cost Of The Work

Conflict reporting causes cumulative psychological harm. Build in rest, counseling, and a culture that treats psychological injury as real.

### Refusing To Leave Once Committed

The willingness to leave is the test of whether the threshold is real. Reassess continuously and extract when conditions deteriorate.

## Self-Check

Before treating a conflict risk assessment as sound, verify:

- The story value of ground presence is assessed independently of the reporter's drive to be there, and remote or local-stringer coverage is used where presence is not required.
- Risks are quantified honestly using current intelligence from security advisers, local contacts, other organizations, and open sources, not just the reporter's sense of the situation.
- A go or no-go threshold is set in advance, defining acceptable risk for story value, extraction triggers, and decision authority, with safety as a precondition rather than a trade-off.
- Team composition, equipment, and identification match the threat, and the exit is planned before entering, including medical care, evacuation funding, and extraction triggers.
- The risk to local staff, fixers, drivers, and translators is accounted for, with fair compensation, equipment, training, and a plan for their safety after the assignment.
- Psychological safety and trauma support are built in, including rest between deployments, access to counseling, peer support, and a culture that treats psychological injury as real.
- The assessment is continuous, with the team and editor reassessing against the threshold as conditions evolve and a willingness to change coverage, pull back, or extract.
- The decision authority is clear, and the reporter on the ground is not the sole decision-maker on whether to stay.
- The assessment is documented and reviewed by a responsible editor and a security adviser before deployment.
- Conflict risk assessment supports editorial judgment; deployment decisions should involve a senior editor, a security adviser, and, where the team faces legal or political threat, legal counsel, recognizing that no story is worth a life and that the team's safety may require foregoing or delaying coverage.
