# Methodology Disclosure And Accountability

When a story rests on data analysis, document review, or a distinctive investigative method, the credibility of the conclusion depends entirely on whether readers can see and evaluate how it was reached. Methodology disclosure is the discipline of showing the work: where the evidence came from, how it was processed, what choices were made, what was included and excluded, and what the limits are. The harm this skill prevents is the presentation of contested or weak findings as settled fact, the use of opaque method to shield sloppy or biased analysis from scrutiny, and the loss of credibility when a hidden methodological flaw later undermines a story. Disclosed methodology is accountable journalism; opaque methodology is assertion dressed as reporting.

Use this skill when writing any story built on data, document analysis, computational methods, or a multi-step investigation. Consult editors on methodology disclosure and, where statistical or technical claims are central, consider expert review before publication.

## Core Rules

### Disclose enough for evaluation and, where feasible, reproduction
The standard is not "mention the data source" but "enable a knowledgeable reader or critic to understand, evaluate, and ideally reproduce the findings." State the source, the time period, the unit of analysis, the inclusion and exclusion criteria, the transformations applied, the statistical methods, the assumptions, and the software or tools used. Where the underlying data can be ethically and legally shared, publish it alongside the story, with code where relevant. Reproducibility is the strongest defense against methodological dispute.

### State the origin and limitations of the data
No dataset is neutral. Disclose who collected it, why, for what purpose, using what definitions, and with what known gaps. Government data may have reporting lags, jurisdictional inconsistencies, or political incentives; corporate data may be self-serving; survey data depends on sampling and question wording; scraped data may be incomplete or biased by the source platform. Name the limitations explicitly rather than letting readers assume the data is complete and authoritative.

### Explain the analytical choices and why they were made
Analysis involves choices: which records to count, how to handle missing values, how to define the population, what denominator to use, what threshold counts as a case, what time window to examine. Each choice can change the result. Disclose the choices and the reasoning, and where reasonable alternatives existed, acknowledge them and explain why this approach was chosen. A reader who disagrees with a choice should at least be able to see what choice was made.

### Distinguish correlation from causation in the presentation
Data often shows association, not causation, and the difference matters enormously. If the analysis shows that two variables move together, say so; do not imply that one causes the other without a causal mechanism, controlled analysis, or expert support. Where you do claim causation, show the basis. The most common methodological failure in data journalism is overclaiming causation from correlational evidence.

### Acknowledge uncertainty and the limits of the findings
Every analysis carries uncertainty: sampling error, measurement error, model assumptions, missing data, and alternative explanations. Quantify uncertainty where possible, with confidence intervals, margins of error, or sensitivity analysis, and describe it qualitatively where it cannot be quantified. A finding presented without uncertainty is overclaimed; a finding presented with honest uncertainty is more credible, not less.

### Pre-register or document the analytical plan where stakes are high
For high-stakes investigations, document the analytical plan before running the final analysis: what hypothesis is being tested, what outcome would confirm or refute it, what subgroups will be examined. This guards against p-hacking, cherry-picking, and post-hoc rationalization, where the analyst tries many approaches and reports only the one that worked. Even informal pre-registration, in internal notes, strengthens the integrity of the finding.

### Subject the method to independent expert review
Before publishing complex data or investigative work, have a methodologist, statistician, domain expert, or outside reviewer examine the approach. Independent review catches errors, challenges assumptions, and stress-tests the conclusions. Treat expert criticism before publication as a safeguard, not an obstacle; the cost of a pre-publication revision is small compared to a post-publication collapse.

### Make the methodology accessible, not just present
A methodology note buried in a sidebar, written in impenetrable jargon, or formatted as an afterthought does not achieve disclosure. Write the methodology clearly enough that an interested non-specialist can follow the logic, link to technical detail for specialists, and place the note where readers will encounter it. Accessibility is part of accountability.

## Common Traps

### Mentioning the data source without explaining the method
Naming the dataset feels like disclosure but tells readers nothing about how it was analyzed or what choices shaped the result. The trap is that source attribution reads as transparent while hiding the analytical decisions that actually determined the finding.

### Overclaiming causation from correlation
Two variables moving together is easy to find and easy to misinterpret as cause and effect. The trap is that causal claims are more dramatic and publishable, so the temptation to overclaim is constant, and the methodological weakness is invisible to most readers.

### Cherry-picking the favorable result
Trying many analyses and reporting only the one that supports the story is a form of fabrication by selection. The trap is that the reported result is "real" in the sense that it came from the data, which masks that it was selected from many non-results.

### Hiding uncertainty to seem authoritative
Presenting a point estimate without a confidence interval or margin of error makes a finding look stronger than it is. The trap is that uncertainty feels like weakness, but its absence is overclaiming that collapses under scrutiny.

### Opaque methodology dressed as rigor
A long, technical-sounding methodology note that does not actually let readers evaluate the choices is theater. The trap is that the length and jargon read as thoroughness while concealing the relevant decisions.

### Skipping independent review under deadline pressure
High-stakes data work published without expert review is vulnerable to errors that specialists would have caught immediately. The trap is that review takes time, and the pressure to publish can override the safeguard.

### Treating data as neutral and authoritative
Data feels objective, but every dataset reflects the choices, incentives, and limits of its collectors. The trap is that "the data shows" reads as definitive, when the data shows only what its collection and analysis allow it to show.

## Self-Check

- Does the methodology disclosure enable a knowledgeable reader to understand, evaluate, and where feasible reproduce the findings?
- Are the origin, collection purpose, definitions, and known limitations of the data stated explicitly?
- Are the analytical choices, including inclusion criteria, denominators, and handling of missing values, disclosed with reasoning?
- Is the presentation careful to distinguish correlation from causation, with causal claims supported by mechanism, controls, or expert analysis?
- Is uncertainty, including sampling error, measurement error, and alternative explanations, acknowledged and where possible quantified?
- For high-stakes work, was the analytical plan documented in advance to guard against cherry-picking and post-hoc rationalization?
- Has the method been reviewed by an independent expert before publication, with criticisms addressed or acknowledged?
- Is the methodology written accessibly and placed where readers will encounter it, and have editors reviewed the disclosure for stories whose conclusions carry significant public weight?
