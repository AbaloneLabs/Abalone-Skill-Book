# Corrections Clarifications And Updates

Publication is not the end of a journalist's obligation to accuracy; it is the beginning of a continuing duty to the public record. Stories change: errors are discovered, new information arrives, official accounts revise, subjects respond, and context shifts. The discipline of corrections, clarifications, and updates is the practice of maintaining the record honestly over time, so that what readers encounter remains as accurate as possible and so that the history of changes is itself visible. The harm this skill prevents is the slow accumulation of error: stories that were wrong staying wrong in search results forever, silent edits that leave earlier readers misinformed, corrections buried so deeply that the original false impression persists, and the loss of trust when readers discover that a newsroom changed a story without acknowledging it.

Use this skill whenever an error is found, when new information changes a published story, when a subject requests a correction or removal, and when deciding whether and how to update. This domain carries legal as well as ethical weight; consult editors on correction decisions and legal counsel where unpublishing, defamation exposure, or subject legal demands are involved.

## Core Rules

### Correct errors promptly and visibly
When an error is confirmed, correct it promptly and make the correction visible. A correction note should state what was wrong, what is correct, when the error was made, and when it was corrected, in plain language. The prominence of the correction should roughly match the prominence of the error: a front-page mistake deserves more than a buried line. Speed matters because false information spreads fast and the longer it sits uncorrected the more people carry the wrong impression.

### Distinguish corrections from clarifications from updates
These are different acts and should be labeled differently. A correction fixes a factual error: something stated was wrong. A clarification adds missing context or refines ambiguous wording without changing the core facts: the original was not wrong but incomplete or unclear. An update adds new developments: the story was accurate at publication but events have moved. Using the right label tells readers what kind of change occurred and why.

### Preserve the history of changes
Readers who saw an earlier version are entitled to know it changed and how. Maintain a visible change log, dated, explaining each significant revision. Do not silently rewrite history. Even when the live text is corrected, the record of what was originally published and when it changed should be accessible, so that anyone tracking the story's evolution can follow it. Silent edits destroy trust when discovered; visible change logs build it.

### Take responsibility without defensiveness
A correction is not an admission of failure to be minimized; it is an act of accountability that strengthens credibility. Write corrections in active, direct voice: "An earlier version of this story incorrectly stated X. The correct figure is Y." Avoid evasive constructions like "some readers may have interpreted" or passive voice that obscures who made the error. Owning the mistake cleanly is more trustworthy than hedging it.

### Apply the correction across all versions and surfaces
A story exists in many places: the article page, the headline, the social post, the push notification, the newsletter blurb, the syndicated wire version, the search snippet. Correcting the body text while leaving a false headline, social post, or push notification standing leaves the error in circulation. Audit every surface where the story or its claims appear and correct each one. The correction obligation extends to the distribution, not only the article.

### Handle unpublishing requests with a strong default against removal
Requests to remove a story, often from subjects who argue it is outdated, harmful, or no longer accurate, should be evaluated against a strong default that the public record should not be silently erased. Removal deprives the public of history and can be used by powerful actors to clean their records. Prefer updating with current context, adding a note explaining subsequent developments, or correcting specific errors, over wholesale deletion. Reserve unpublishing for rare cases: factual error that cannot be corrected in place, legal compulsion, or genuine safety risk, and even then document that the story existed.

### Update as the story develops, with the update visible
When new information arrives that changes the story, update it, and make the update visible rather than folding it silently into the original text. A reader who read the original and a reader who reads the updated version should both be able to understand what changed and when. For developing stories, a timestamped update log at the top or in a dedicated section serves readers tracking the evolution.

### Re-examine the framing, not just the facts, when the picture changes
Sometimes new information does not change a specific fact but changes the overall picture: a denial that seemed weak turns out to be accurate, a source that seemed central turns out to be unreliable, a trend that seemed real turns out to be a statistical artifact. In these cases the correction is not just to a detail but to the framing and emphasis. Have the honesty to revisit the frame, not only the easily checkable facts, when the underlying understanding shifts.

## Common Traps

### Silent edits to avoid embarrassment
Changing a published error without noting the change avoids the discomfort of a visible correction but destroys trust when discovered. The trap is that silent editing feels cleaner in the moment, but it is the more damaging choice.

### Burying the correction
A correction placed at the bottom, in small type, or in a separate corrections page that no one visits fails to reach the readers who saw the error. The trap is technically correcting while practically leaving the false impression in place.

### Defensive or evasive correction language
"This story has been updated to reflect additional information" obscures whether there was an error, a clarification, or an update. The trap is hedged language that protects the newsroom's feelings at the cost of reader understanding.

### Correcting only the article body
Leaving false headlines, social posts, push notifications, and syndicated versions in circulation leaves the error actively spreading even after the body is fixed. The trap is treating the article page as the whole story when the story is distributed across many surfaces.

### Removing stories to satisfy subject pressure
Deleting accurate, newsworthy stories because the subject finds them inconvenient erases the public record and rewards pressure. The trap is that removal resolves the immediate complaint while damaging the historical record and inviting further demands.

### Treating updates as optional once the splash is over
Moving on to the next story while the original story's picture changes leaves readers with an outdated version permanently in search results. The trap is that the splash generated the attention, so the update feels less urgent, but the record persists.

### Refusing to revisit framing when facts shift
Correcting a number while leaving a now-misleading frame intact preserves the original narrative at the cost of accuracy. The trap is that framing changes are harder and more consequential than detail fixes, so they get avoided.

## Self-Check

- Are confirmed errors corrected promptly, visibly, and with prominence matching the original error?
- Are corrections, clarifications, and updates labeled distinctly so readers know what kind of change occurred?
- Is the history of changes preserved in a visible, dated log rather than silently rewritten?
- Is the correction written in active, direct voice that takes responsibility without defensiveness or evasion?
- Has the correction been applied across every surface, including headline, social, push, newsletter, and syndicated versions?
- Are unpublishing requests evaluated against a strong default for preserving the public record, with updates and context preferred over removal?
- Are developing stories updated visibly with timestamped change logs so readers can follow the evolution?
- When the overall picture changes, has the framing been revisited, not just the easily checkable facts, and have editors and, where legal exposure or subject demands exist, legal counsel been consulted?
