# Source Recruitment And First Contact

First contact is the moment a stranger decides whether to trust you, talk to you, or shut the door. Everything later in the relationship, the access, the candor, the willingness to take risks, is shaped by how the first contact is handled. The judgment problem is that reporters are often under time pressure and tempted to lead with the ask, which triggers defensiveness, or to over-promise, which creates expectations that later poison the relationship. A botched first contact can permanently close a source who would otherwise have been invaluable.

Use this skill when approaching anyone you have not worked with before: witnesses, officials, insiders, experts, affected people, accused parties, or potential whistleblowers. The main risk is that an agent treats first contact as a transaction rather than the start of a relationship, or that it uses tactics that work in the moment but destroy trust over time.

## Core Rules

### Lead With Honesty About Who You Are And Why You Are Calling

Do not disguise your identity, your outlet, or the purpose of the contact. State plainly that you are a journalist, who you work for, and the broad subject you are reporting on. Deception, even minor, may yield a short-term conversation but destroys credibility the moment it is discovered, and it can expose the work to legal and ethical challenge. Honesty also respects the source's right to make an informed decision about whether to engage.

You do not need to reveal your full thesis on first contact, but you must not actively mislead about the story's direction. If the subject is sensitive, explain enough for the person to understand the stakes.

### Match The Approach To The Source's Position And Vulnerability

A powerful official, a corporate spokesperson, an expert, an affected community member, a victim, and a potential whistleblower each require a different approach. Officials expect formal outreach and often have media procedures; affected people may need time, context, and reassurance; whistleblowers may be frightened and need to understand protections before saying anything substantive. Calibrate tone, channel, timing, and the amount of detail you share to the source's situation.

For vulnerable sources, prioritize their understanding and safety over your speed. For institutional sources, follow their preferred channels but do not let procedure become a stalling tactic.

### Give The Source A Reason To Talk That Serves Them

People talk to journalists for many reasons: to correct a record, to expose wrongdoing, to advance a cause, to defend a reputation, to be heard, to influence policy, or out of civic duty. Understand what this source might want, and frame the conversation in terms that connect to their genuine motivations, without promising outcomes you cannot deliver. A source who sees no benefit, or only risk, will not engage.

Be honest about what you can and cannot offer. You can offer a fair hearing, accurate representation, and the chance to shape understanding. You cannot promise a headline, favorable framing, legal protection beyond what genuinely exists, or immunity from consequences.

### Establish Ground Rules Before Substantive Information

Do not let a first conversation drift into sensitive territory without clarifying the rules. The default is on the record, but a frightened source may begin sharing before realizing the implications. If the conversation turns sensitive, pause and confirm: is this on the record, on background, not for attribution, or off the record? Define each term plainly, because people use them inconsistently.

Record the agreement immediately. If ground rules are disputed later, your notes are the record. Never accept sensitive information and then negotiate the terms afterward; the source may have already exposed themselves.

### Build Incremental Trust Through Small Exchanges

First contact rarely produces the full story. Treat it as the start of a relationship. Demonstrate, in small ways, that you are prepared, that you listen, that you follow up, and that you respect the source's time and concerns. Share context you can legitimately share. Ask genuine questions about their work or situation. Let them see that engaging with you is not dangerous or wasteful.

Incremental trust is especially important for insiders and whistleblowers, who usually need several interactions before they will share anything that puts them at risk. Rushing this stage closes doors that patience would have opened.

### Prepare Meticulously So The Source Feels Respected

A source can tell within minutes whether you have done your homework. Research their role, their institution, their public statements, the relevant documents, and the context before you make contact. Preparation signals respect and competence, and it lets you ask informed questions rather than generic ones. A source who feels you understand their world is far more likely to engage deeply.

Preparation also protects you: you will not be easily misled, and you will recognize when a source is being evasive or selective.

### Leave The Door Open After A Refusal

Many sources say no on first contact and yes later. Do not burn the bridge. Thank them, leave your contact details, explain that the door remains open, and follow up gently at a reasonable interval if circumstances change. A respectful refusal handled well can convert into cooperation weeks or months later, whereas an aggressive or dismissive response closes the door permanently.

Document the refusal and the reason, so you can demonstrate later that you made a genuine effort and so you know whether to approach again.

### Protect The Source's Decision-Making From Your Pressure

First contact should inform, not coerce. Do not trap the source, corner them, or extract agreement through emotional pressure, especially with vulnerable people. A source who agrees under pressure may retract, distort, or suffer harm they did not fully choose. The strongest sourcing is voluntary and informed.

This is doubly important for people in custody, in hospitals, in grief, under investigation, or in dependent relationships. Their capacity to consent is compromised, and your duty to protect them is higher.

## Common Traps

### Leading With The Ask

Opening with a demand for information or an interview request triggers defensiveness. Open with context and respect; make the ask once the source understands who you are and why it matters.

### Over-Promising To Get The Conversation Started

Promising anonymity, protection, favorable coverage, or story control to secure a first interview creates obligations that distort the work and often cannot be honored. Under-promise and explain the real limits.

### Deception In Service Of Access

Pretending to be a student, a researcher, a customer, or someone else to get a foot in the door may work once and ruin your reputation, your outlet's credibility, and the source's future willingness to trust any journalist. Reserve undercover methods for the rarest cases of overriding public interest with explicit editorial approval.

### Treating Refusal As Failure

A no on first contact is often temporary. Reporters who treat refusal as final and move on lose sources who would have come around with patience and respectful follow-up.

### Cold Approaches That Endanger The Source

A message to a source through a monitored work channel, or a visit that is observed, can expose the source to suspicion before they have decided whether to talk. Consider the channel's safety, especially for insiders and people in repressive contexts.

### Burning The Source In The First Conversation

Pushing too hard, too fast, for the most sensitive material can spook a source who would have shared it after trust was built. Pace the conversation to the relationship's actual depth.

### Generic Pitches To Specialized Sources

A source who receives a clearly generic, copy-pasted request feels disrespected and declines. Personalize every approach to demonstrate that you understand who they are and why they specifically matter.

## Self-Check

Before treating first contact as well-handled, verify:

- You identified yourself, your outlet, and the broad subject honestly, without disguise or misleading framing.
- The approach was calibrated to the source's position, vulnerability, and likely motivations.
- The reason to talk was framed in terms that serve the source's genuine interests, without unkeepable promises.
- Ground rules for the conversation were clarified before any sensitive information was shared.
- The contact was treated as the start of a relationship, with incremental trust built through preparation and respect.
- Preparation was sufficient that the source could tell you understood their role and context.
- A refusal was handled respectfully, with the door left open and contact details provided.
- No pressure was applied that compromised a vulnerable source's ability to consent freely.
- The channel of contact was assessed for the source's safety, especially for insiders or people in monitored environments.
- The conversation was paced to the relationship's depth, not pushed for maximum extraction on first contact.
