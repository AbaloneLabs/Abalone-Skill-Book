# Source Cultivation Long Term

A source network is not built during a breaking story. It is built slowly, through repeated low-stakes contact, demonstrated reliability, and reciprocal trust, long before the moment a source's information becomes decisive. The judgment problem in long-term cultivation is that the work is mostly invisible, yields no immediate story, and can drift into coziness, dependency, or transactional behavior that compromises independence. Reporters who only call sources when they need something find their network collapsing precisely when they need it most.

Use this skill when the task involves maintaining relationships with people who may someday provide information: officials, insiders, experts, community members, dissidents, whistleblowers, and ordinary people close to power or events. The main risk is that an agent treats sources as extractable resources rather than people, or that cultivation slides into inappropriate closeness that distorts coverage.

## Core Rules

### Cultivate Before You Need

The most valuable sources are cultivated when there is no immediate ask. Reach out to share a relevant development, ask a genuine question about their field, attend their public events, or simply check in. The goal is to be a known, predictable, respectful presence so that when a story breaks, the source already trusts you enough to talk. A cold call during a crisis reaches a guarded stranger; a warm call reaches a colleague.

Build a habit of regular, low-pressure contact with the people who matter to your beat. Track who you have spoken with and when, so no relationship goes cold through neglect.

### Demonstrate Reliability In Small Things

Trust is built through small demonstrations that you are accurate, fair, and discreet. When you use a source's information, attribute it exactly as agreed. When you promise to keep something off the record, keep it. When you make a mistake involving them, correct it and tell them. Sources watch how you treat other people's information as a forecast of how you will treat theirs.

Reliability also means following through. If you say you will send a document, send it. If you say you will call back, call back. Small broken promises accumulate into a reputation for being untrustworthy, and that reputation travels.

### Define The Relationship's Boundaries Early

Cultivation can drift into coziness: shared meals, gifts, favors, emotional reliance, or access that depends on favorable coverage. Set boundaries that preserve independence. Be warm and respectful without becoming a friend whose coverage is constrained by loyalty. Decline gifts, hospitality, or perks that would create a sense of obligation, and disclose those you cannot avoid.

Clarify, repeatedly and in plain language, that you are a journalist and that your duty is to the audience, not to the source. A source who understands this from the start is less likely to feel betrayed later, and you are less likely to feel compromised.

### Maintain Reciprocity Without Trading Favors

Healthy source relationships involve a degree of reciprocity: you share what you can, you offer context, you treat the source as a person. But reciprocity must not become transactional. Do not trade favorable coverage for access, do not kill a story to protect a source, do not publish a puff piece to maintain a relationship, and do not accept information on the condition that you ignore other wrongdoing.

The line is clearest when you ask: would I do this for a source I did not know? If the answer is no, the favor is probably too large. Genuine reciprocity is built on professional respect, not obligation.

### Protect Sources From Themselves And From You

Sources often do not foresee the consequences of talking. An official may not realize that a specific detail will identify them inside their agency. A community member may not realize that their quote will travel globally. Part of cultivation is helping sources understand the risks before they take them, and protecting them from pressure you create through careless reporting.

Review every published detail from the source's perspective. Could this phrase, this date, this document, identify them? If so, and if identification is not essential, remove or generalize it. Do not let your need for vividness expose a source to retaliation.

### Rebuild Trust After A Breach

Even careful reporters sometimes publish something a source experiences as a betrayal: a quote used differently than expected, an identifying detail, a story that harmed the source's interests. When this happens, do not avoid the source. Reach out, listen to their concern, explain your reasoning, acknowledge genuine errors, and discuss how to prevent recurrence. Avoid defensiveness, but also do not promise changes you cannot keep.

Some breaches are unfixable, and you must accept the loss. But many sources will continue a relationship with a reporter who is honest about a mistake, whereas they will never trust one who pretends nothing happened.

### Diversify The Network Deliberately

Source networks tend to narrow over time toward people who are accessible, friendly, and ideologically comfortable. This produces coverage that reflects one slice of a community. Deliberately cultivate sources across roles, ranks, ideologies, neighborhoods, languages, and levels of power. Include people who are hard to reach, who disagree with you, and who are not usually heard.

A diverse network is not only more fair; it is more accurate, because it surfaces information that a homogeneous network would miss.

### Keep Records That Survive Handoff

Source relationships outlive individual stories and sometimes individual reporters. Maintain records of who each source is, what they know, what ground rules apply, when you last spoke, and what they care about, stored securely. When you hand off a beat, these records let the next reporter continue without losing years of trust, and they protect sources from being re-contacted in ways that breach prior agreements.

## Common Traps

### Only Calling When You Need Something

Sources can tell when they are being mined. A reporter who vanishes for months and then calls with urgent questions is treated as a liability, not an ally. Regular contact is the price of access in a crisis.

### Drifting Into Coziness

Shared social lives, emotional reliance, and unexamined favors erode the independence that makes a source's information credible. Warmth is professional; coziness is a conflict.

### Promising More Than You Can Deliver

Telling a source you will protect them, keep them anonymous forever, or kill a competing story creates obligations you may be unable or ethically barred from honoring. Under-promise and over-explain the limits.

### Letting One Dominant Source Define The Beat

A single well-placed, friendly source can shape an entire beat's framing, because their perspective becomes the default. Actively seek sources who would contradict that perspective.

### Confusing Access With Insight

A source who returns your calls and speaks fluently is not necessarily more truthful than one who is cautious or hostile. Fluency and availability can mask self-interest. Test even your best sources' claims.

### Burning A Source Through Careless Detail

A vivid anecdote, a precise date, or a uniquely held document can identify a source inside their institution. The most painful breaches are often unintentional, caused by detail the reporter thought was harmless.

### Neglecting The Network During Quiet Periods

When a beat is quiet, cultivation usually stops, and the network decays. The quiet periods are exactly when maintenance matters most, because that is when the next story is germinating.

## Self-Check

Before treating source cultivation as healthy, verify:

- The network includes regular, low-pressure contact, not only calls when information is needed.
- Reliability has been demonstrated through accurate attribution, kept promises, and corrected mistakes.
- Boundaries preserve independence: no favors, gifts, or obligations that would constrain coverage.
- Reciprocity is professional respect, not transactional exchange of access for favorable treatment.
- Sources were helped to understand publication consequences before sharing sensitive information.
- Published details were reviewed from each source's perspective for inadvertent identification.
- A plan exists for rebuilding trust after a perceived breach, including honest acknowledgment.
- The network is deliberately diverse across roles, ranks, ideologies, and communities.
- Records of sources, ground rules, and contact history are kept securely and are usable across handoffs.
- No single dominant source is allowed to define the beat's default framing without challenge.
