# Difficult Interview Management

Some interviews are conversations among people who want to talk. Others are contests in which the subject has strong reasons to evade, minimize, deflect, or mislead. Difficult interviews, with accused parties, evasive officials, crisis communicators, hostile executives, or witnesses with interests to protect, require a different discipline than ordinary reporting. The judgment problem is that the reporter must hold the subject accountable without becoming the story, must press hard without losing the source permanently, and must extract substance from someone actively working to prevent it.

Use this skill whenever the interviewee has a stake in limiting what becomes public and is likely to use evasion, deflection, prepared talking points, or legal constraint. The main risk is that an agent either backs down to preserve access or becomes combative in ways that produce heat but no usable information.

## Core Rules

### Decide The Interview's Purpose Before You Sit Down

A difficult interview can serve different purposes: to put specific allegations on the record, to capture the subject's response for fairness, to test evasions against documents, to surface new information, or to demonstrate accountability publicly. Decide which purpose dominates, because it shapes everything: the question order, the tone, how hard you press, and when you stop. An interview without a clear purpose becomes a meandering argument the subject controls.

Write down the two or three things you must come away with. If you do not get them, the interview's purpose is unmet, regardless of how long it ran.

### Prepare With Documents, Not Just Questions

In a difficult interview, questions are weak; documents are strong. The subject can deny, reinterpret, or talk past a question, but a specific document, with a date and a signature, is harder to evade. Prepare by assembling the records that establish the facts, then frame questions around them: "The contract dated March 4 shows X. Can you explain?" Document-led questioning exposes evasion and creates a record the subject cannot later deny.

Know each document's limits as well as its strengths, so the subject cannot discredit your entire case by finding one weakness in one exhibit.

### Control The Frame Without Becoming The Story

Difficult subjects try to reframe the interview: attacking the reporter, changing the subject, invoking procedure, demanding to know your sources, or shifting to favorable ground. Your job is to return, calmly and persistently, to the questions that matter. Do not take the bait of personal attacks, do not match the subject's aggression, and do not let the interview become a spectacle about you. The audience needs the subject's answers, not your reactions.

Calm persistence is more powerful than confrontation. A reporter who repeatedly, politely, refuses to let a question go unanswered exposes evasion more effectively than one who shouts.

### Use Silence And Follow-Up Against Evasion

Evasive subjects often answer a question that was not asked, deliver a prepared statement, or talk until time runs out. Do not move on after a non-answer. Note what was not answered, and return to it: "I understand your broader point. The specific question was X." Use silence after a partial answer; people often fill it with more, sometimes with the substance you need.

Follow-up is where evasion breaks down. The first question can be deflected; the fourth reformulation of the same question, grounded in a document, is much harder to dodge.

### Pin Down Specifics, Then Lock Them

Vague answers are useless and dangerous, because they can be reinterpreted later. Push for specifics: who, when, how much, which document, which decision. Then lock the answer by summarizing it back: "So you are telling me that on March 4, no one in your department approved this payment. Is that correct?" A locked, specific answer creates a record the subject cannot quietly revise.

If the subject refuses to be specific, the refusal itself is reportable: the official declined to say whether the payment was approved.

### Preserve The Record Meticulously

Difficult interviews are the most likely to be disputed. Record audio or video where legal and agreed, and where not, take detailed contemporaneous notes with exact wording for key statements. Note the date, time, location, who was present, and any constraints on use. If the subject later claims to have been misquoted or taken out of context, your record, not your memory, is the basis for defense.

For legally sensitive interviews, consider a second person present to corroborate the record, and transcribe key exchanges immediately while memory is fresh.

### Give The Subject Genuine Opportunity To Respond

Even an adversarial interview must be fair. Put the specific allegations to the subject clearly, with enough detail and time for a substantive response. Do not spring new material at the last second to prevent a reply. Fairness is both an ethical duty and a practical protection: a subject who was given a real chance to respond and declined is harder positioned to complain later that they were ambushed.

Document the offer of response, the time given, and what the subject did with it.

### Know When To Stop And When To Continue Off The Record

A difficult interview has diminishing returns. Once the subject is repeating themselves, the substantive answers are exhausted, or the conversation has become unproductive confrontation, end the interview cleanly. Thank them, summarize what was established, and leave. Sometimes the most valuable moments come after the formal interview ends, when the subject relaxes; be alert to that, but do not manipulate the close to manufacture candor.

If the subject offers to go off the record to explain something, decide deliberately whether to accept, understanding you cannot publish it but may use it to guide further reporting.

## Common Traps

### Letting The Subject Run Out The Clock

A skilled evader will talk at length, restate prepared points, and consume the available time without answering. Impose structure, interrupt politely but firmly, and return to the unanswered question.

### Becoming Combative And Becoming The Story

Anger and grandstanding feel like accountability but often produce a clip of the reporter, not the subject. The goal is the subject's words on the record, not your performance.

### Accepting A Non-Answer And Moving On

Moving on after a deflection lets the subject escape the question entirely. Note the non-answer and return to it; the pattern of evasion is itself part of the record.

### Over-Reliance On Gotcha Questions

A surprise question the subject cannot answer can look powerful but may reflect preparation gaps rather than guilt. Build questions on documents and established facts so the subject's response, whatever it is, is meaningful.

### Springing Allegations At The Last Moment

Ambushing a subject with serious allegations at the end, with no time to respond, is unfair and undermines the story's defensibility. Provide substance and adequate time.

### Losing The Source Permanently Through Needless Hostility

Some subjects must be pressed hard; few need to be insulted. Hostility that exceeds what the story requires can close a source who might have been valuable later, without adding anything to the record.

### Failing To Lock Specifics

An answer like "we followed all procedures" is meaningless unless pinned to specifics. Without locked specifics, the subject can later claim they meant something different.

## Self-Check

Before treating a difficult interview as well-managed, verify:

- The interview's primary purpose was defined in advance, and the questions served that purpose.
- Preparation centered on documents and established facts, not just a list of questions.
- The frame was controlled calmly, with persistent return to unanswered questions rather than escalation.
- Non-answers were noted and followed up, not accepted and moved past.
- Specifics were pinned down and locked by summary confirmation.
- A robust record exists, with audio, video, or detailed contemporaneous notes, including date, time, location, and attendees.
- The subject received a genuine, specific, and timely opportunity to respond to allegations.
- The interview ended when it stopped producing substance, without manipulation of the close.
- Any off-the-record material was handled according to the agreed terms.
- Tone served accountability without making the reporter the subject of the story.
