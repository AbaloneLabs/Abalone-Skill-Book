# Defamation Risk In Stories

Defamation risk, the risk that a story will expose the journalist or newsroom to liability for damaging a person's reputation through false or unsupported assertions, is one of the most common and serious legal risks in journalism, and it is a domain where the journalistic instinct for strong, clear language collides with the legal requirement for provable accuracy. The judgment problem is that defamatory meaning is determined by how a reasonable reader understands the story, not by what the journalist intended, and that implications, connotations, and the arrangement of true facts can create defamatory meanings the journalist did not explicitly state. A defensible assessment identifies the defamatory meanings a story could bear, verifies the factual basis for each damaging assertion, and frames the story so that it asserts only what it can prove.

Use this skill when assessing defamation risk before publication, identifying defamatory meanings, verifying factual basis, or framing allegations and charged language. The main risk is that an agent focuses on explicit statements while missing defamatory implications, asserts damaging conclusions without verifiable support, or assumes that because individual facts are true the story's overall meaning is defensible.

## Core Rules

### Identify Defamatory Meanings, Not Just Explicit Statements

Defamation turns on the meaning a reasonable reader takes from the publication, and that meaning includes implications and connotations, not just explicit statements. A story that reports true facts in an arrangement or with language that implies a damaging conclusion, such as reporting that a person was investigated alongside others who were guilty, implying guilt by association, can be defamatory even if no explicit accusation is made. The defamatory meaning is what the story communicates, not what it says in its narrowest reading.

Read the story for the meanings a reasonable reader would take, including implications from juxtaposition, headlines, images, and charged language. Identify each meaning that could damage a person's reputation, such as implications of dishonesty, criminality, incompetence, or moral failure. This reading is different from fact-checking, which verifies explicit claims; it asks what the story communicates beyond its explicit claims, and it often catches the defamation risks that fact-checking misses. See the fairness-context-and-newsworthiness skill.

### Verify The Factual Basis For Each Damaging Assertion

Every damaging assertion in a story, whether explicit or implied, must have a factual basis that can be proven if challenged. The strength of the required basis depends on the assertion: statements of fact require verifiable support; opinions based on disclosed facts are protected but must genuinely rest on those facts; allegations must be attributed to a source and framed as allegations, not adopted as the story's assertions. Verify the basis for each damaging assertion before publication, documenting the sources, evidence, and reasoning.

The verification standard is not what the journalist believes to be true but what the journalist can demonstrate through evidence and sources. A story that asserts damaging facts the journalist cannot prove carries defamation risk regardless of the journalist's confidence, because the defense to defamation is the ability to prove truth, not the subjective belief in it. Build the documentation into the reporting, so that the basis for each assertion survives the news cycle and can support a defense. See the reporting-and-source-verification skill.

### Apply The Actual Malice And Negligence Standards To The Subject's Status

The standard for defamation liability depends on the subject's status. Public officials and public figures must prove actual malice, meaning the journalist knew the statement was false or acted with reckless disregard for its truth, a high standard that protects robust reporting on public matters. Private individuals need only prove negligence, meaning the journalist failed to exercise reasonable care, in many jurisdictions. The status of the subject determines the standard that applies, and the assessment should account for it.

Determine the subject's status, recognizing the categories: public officials, all-purpose public figures, limited-purpose public figures who have injected themselves into a particular public controversy, and private individuals. The status affects both the standard of liability and the assessment of risk. A story about a public figure carries lower defamation risk, because of the actual malice standard, than the same story about a private individual, but the actual malice standard is not a license for recklessness, and reckless disregard can be shown by failure to verify, reliance on unreliable sources, or disregard of obvious doubts. See the media-law skill.

### Frame Allegations As Allegations, Not Adopted Assertions

When a story reports allegations made by others, the framing determines whether the story is adopting the allegations as its own assertions or reporting them as attributed claims. A story that says "X committed fraud" adopts the assertion and must be able to prove it. A story that says "Y alleges that X committed fraud, and X denies it" reports the allegation as an attributed claim, which carries less defamation risk, provided the allegation is accurately reported and the subject's response is included.

Frame allegations carefully, attributing them to sources and distinguishing the story's reporting from the allegations themselves. Include the subject's response, both as a matter of fairness and as a defamation risk mitigation, because reporting only one side of a damaging allegation increases the risk. Do not frame allegations as adopted assertions unless the story has independently verified them to the standard required for the assertion. See the fairness-context-and-newsworthiness skill.

### Use Charged Language Deliberately And Defensibly

Charged language, words that carry strong connotations beyond their literal meaning, can create defamatory meanings that the journalist did not intend. Words like "scam," "fraud," "corrupt," "liar," and "cover-up" carry legal and moral weight, and using them loosely can assert meanings the story cannot support. A story that describes conduct as a "scam" asserts dishonesty and must be able to prove it, even if the underlying conduct is accurately described elsewhere.

Use charged language deliberately, choosing it only where the story can support the meaning it carries, and prefer precise, neutral language where the charged term is not necessary. "The company charged customers for services it did not provide" is more defensible than "the company ran a scam," because it asserts provable facts rather than a loaded conclusion. The discipline of precise language serves both legal risk management and journalistic accuracy.

### Distinguish Statements Of Fact, Opinion, And Allegation

Defamation law distinguishes statements of fact, which are actionable if false, from opinions, which are generally protected if they are based on disclosed facts and do not imply undisclosed defamatory facts, and from allegations, which are attributed claims. A statement of fact asserts something that can be proven true or false. An opinion expresses a view based on disclosed facts. An allegation reports a claim made by someone else.

Distinguish these in the story, because the protections differ. An opinion that implies undisclosed defamatory facts, such as "in my opinion, based on what I know, X is corrupt," may be actionable because it implies knowledge of specific facts. An opinion must rest on disclosed facts that the reader can evaluate. A statement presented as opinion but asserting provable facts may be treated as fact. Frame each statement according to its type, and do not use opinion framing to assert facts the story cannot support.

### Assess The Story's Overall Meaning, Not Just Its Sentences

Defamation is assessed based on the publication's overall meaning, including headlines, images, captions, and the arrangement of content, not just the individual sentences. A story whose sentences are individually defensible may be defamatory in its overall meaning, if the headline, image, or arrangement creates a damaging implication the sentences do not. A correction to one sentence may not cure a defamatory overall meaning created by the headline.

Assess the story as a whole, including all its elements, for the meaning a reasonable reader would take. Check that headlines, images, captions, and arrangement do not create defamatory meanings the body does not support. This holistic assessment is essential, because defamation turns on the publication's meaning, and a story defended by its sentences may be condemned by its presentation.

## Common Traps

### Focusing On Explicit Statements And Missing Implications

Defamatory meaning includes implications and connotations. Read for what the story communicates, not just what it explicitly states.

### Asserting Damaging Conclusions Without Verifiable Support

Every damaging assertion must have a provable factual basis. The defense is the ability to prove truth, not subjective belief.

### Ignoring The Subject's Status And The Applicable Standard

Public figures require actual malice; private individuals require negligence. Assess risk against the applicable standard.

### Adopting Allegations As The Story's Own Assertions

Attribute allegations to sources and frame them as claims, with the subject's response, unless independently verified.

### Using Charged Language Loosely

Words like "scam," "fraud," and "corrupt" carry meanings the story must support. Prefer precise, neutral language.

### Confusing Opinion With Fact

Opinions must rest on disclosed facts and not imply undisclosed defamatory facts. Do not use opinion framing to assert unsupported facts.

### Assessing Sentences But Not The Overall Meaning

Defamation turns on the publication's overall meaning, including headlines and images. Assess the story as a whole.

## Self-Check

Before treating a defamation risk assessment as sound, verify:

- The story is read for defamatory meanings a reasonable reader would take, including implications, juxtapositions, headlines, images, and charged language, not just explicit statements.
- Each damaging assertion, explicit or implied, has a factual basis that can be demonstrated through evidence and sources, documented to survive the news cycle.
- The subject's status as public official, public figure, limited-purpose public figure, or private individual is determined, and the applicable standard, actual malice or negligence, is applied to the risk assessment.
- Allegations are attributed to sources and framed as claims, with the subject's response included, not adopted as the story's assertions unless independently verified.
- Charged language is used deliberately and defensibly, with precise, neutral language preferred where the charged term is not necessary or supported.
- Statements of fact, opinion, and allegation are distinguished, with opinions resting on disclosed facts and not implying undisclosed defamatory facts.
- The story's overall meaning, including all elements, is assessed, with headlines, images, captions, and arrangement checked for defamatory implications the body does not support.
- The verification documentation supports each assertion at the level the applicable standard requires.
- The assessment is documented and reviewed by a responsible editor and, for high-risk stories, legal counsel before publication.
- Defamation risk assessment supports editorial judgment; significant risk decisions should involve legal counsel, recognizing that this skill does not provide legal advice, that defamation law is jurisdiction-specific, and that the public interest may justify publishing defamatory material after verification and professional consultation.
