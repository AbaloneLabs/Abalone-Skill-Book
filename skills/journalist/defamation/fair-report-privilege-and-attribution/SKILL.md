# Fair Report Privilege And Attribution

The fair report privilege, also called the section 230 or official proceedings privilege depending on jurisdiction, protects journalists who accurately report on official proceedings, government records, and public accusations, allowing them to report damaging allegations without adopting them as their own assertions, provided they attribute the allegations to the official source and frame them fairly. The judgment problem is that the privilege is conditional, requiring accurate attribution, fair framing, and often that the proceeding be official and public, and that losing the privilege through sloppy attribution or unfair framing exposes the newsroom to defamation liability for the underlying allegations. A defensible use of the privilege understands its conditions, attributes allegations to official sources precisely, and frames reports on proceedings to preserve the privilege rather than inadvertently waiving it.

Use this skill when relying on the fair report privilege, reporting on official proceedings or accusations, attributing statements to official sources, or framing reports on charges and hearings. The main risk is that an agent assumes the privilege covers any report on official matters, fails to attribute accurately, or frames the report in a way that adopts the allegations and waives the privilege.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Privilege's Conditions And Limits

The fair report privilege protects accurate and fair reports of official proceedings, government actions, and public records, but its conditions vary by jurisdiction. Generally, the privilege requires that the report be of an official or public proceeding, that the report be accurate and complete or fair abridgment, that the source be attributed, and that the report not be adopted as the journalist's own assertion through commentary or framing. Some jurisdictions require the proceeding to be public; others extend to official records regardless of public access. Some require the report to be a fair and accurate summary, not a selective or distorted excerpt.

Understand the privilege's conditions in the relevant jurisdiction before relying on it. The privilege is not a blanket protection for any report touching on official matters; it is a conditional protection that must be earned through accurate attribution and fair framing. A report that fails a condition, such as by distorting the proceeding or omitting exculpatory context, may lose the privilege and face defamation liability for the underlying allegations. See the defamation-risk-in-stories skill.

### Attribute Allegations To The Official Source Precisely

The privilege depends on attribution to the official source, and the attribution must be precise enough that the reader understands the allegation comes from the proceeding, not from the journalist's own reporting. A report that says "X committed fraud, according to court filings" attributes the allegation to the filing. A report that says "X committed fraud" without attribution adopts the assertion and must be able to prove it, regardless of whether a filing makes the same allegation.

Attribute allegations to the specific official source: the indictment, the hearing testimony, the regulatory filing, the police report. Make the attribution clear and proximate, so the reader sees it in connection with the allegation, not buried or generic. Precise attribution both preserves the privilege and serves the reader's understanding of the allegation's basis. Vague attribution, such as "according to authorities," may be insufficient in some jurisdictions and should be specific where possible.

### Frame Reports On Proceedings Fairly And Without Distortion

The privilege requires a fair and accurate report, and framing that distorts the proceeding can waive the privilege. Distortion can occur through selective reporting that highlights damaging allegations while omitting exculpatory context, through headlines or framing that sensationalize beyond the proceeding's content, or through arrangement that creates implications the proceeding does not support. A fair report conveys the proceeding's substance accurately, including the significant context a reasonable reader would need to understand it.

Frame reports on proceedings to convey what the proceeding actually says, including charges, denials, and significant context. Report an indictment as an indictment, not as a conviction; report an allegation as an allegation, not as established fact. Include the subject's response or denial where the proceeding contains it or where fairness requires seeking it. The privilege protects fair reports, and fairness is both a legal condition and a journalistic obligation. See the fairness-context-and-newsworthiness skill.

### Distinguish Official Proceedings From Unofficial Allegations

The fair report privilege generally covers official proceedings, government actions, and public records, not unofficial allegations or private accusations. A lawsuit filing is generally an official proceeding; a press release from a political opponent is not. A regulatory action is official; an advocacy group's report may or may not be, depending on its official character. A legislative hearing is official; a campaign rally is not.

Distinguish official from unofficial sources before relying on the privilege. Reporting an unofficial allegation carries defamation risk unless independently verified, regardless of how carefully attributed, because the privilege does not extend to unofficial accusations. Where the source is ambiguous, such as a government official making allegations in a non-official context, assess whether the privilege applies and verify the allegation independently if it does not. See the public-records skill.

### Do Not Adopt Allegations Through Commentary Or Framing

The privilege protects reports of official allegations, but it does not protect commentary or framing that adopts the allegations as the journalist's own assertions. A report that accurately conveys an indictment's allegations and then adds commentary concluding that the subject is guilty waives the privilege as to the commentary, because the commentary asserts the journalist's own conclusion. The privilege protects the report of the proceeding, not the journalist's editorial adoption of its allegations.

Separate the report of the proceeding from any commentary, and ensure the commentary does not adopt unproven allegations as established fact. Opinion based on disclosed facts is protected, but opinion that asserts facts the proceeding does not establish may be actionable. The discipline is to report what the proceeding says and to offer commentary that rests on disclosed facts, not on implications or conclusions the proceeding does not support.

### Verify The Proceeding's Content And Update For Developments

The privilege requires an accurate report of the proceeding, and accuracy requires verifying what the proceeding actually says. Do not rely on summaries, press releases, or secondhand accounts of a filing, indictment, or hearing; obtain the primary document or transcript where possible and report from it. A report based on a distorted summary inherits the distortion and may lose the privilege.

Update reports as the proceeding develops. An initial report of an indictment is privileged, but if the charges are dismissed, the subject is acquitted, or the allegations are retracted, failing to report these developments can create a misleading ongoing publication that may affect the privilege and the fairness of the coverage. Maintain the accuracy of reports on ongoing proceedings by updating them as the facts change.

### Account For Jurisdictional Variation In The Privilege

The fair report privilege varies by jurisdiction in its scope, conditions, and application. Some jurisdictions extend it broadly to any official proceeding or record; others limit it to specific categories. Some require the report to be a fair and accurate summary; others require complete reporting of exculpatory context. Some recognize a privilege for reports of unofficial accusations made in public settings; others do not. The privilege's treatment of reports on accusations in legislative proceedings, which often have absolute legal privilege for the speaker, also varies.

Account for the jurisdictional variation where the report will be published or where liability could arise. A report that is privileged in one jurisdiction may face liability in another with a narrower privilege. For reports crossing jurisdictions, assess the privilege in each relevant jurisdiction and structure the report to satisfy the strictest applicable standard. See the media-law skill.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The Privilege Covers Any Report On Official Matters

The privilege is conditional, requiring accurate attribution, fair framing, and an official proceeding. Earn it through the conditions.

### Failing To Attribute Allegations Precisely

Attribution must be clear, proximate, and specific to the official source. Vague or buried attribution may be insufficient.

### Distorting The Proceeding Through Selective Or Sensationalized Framing

The privilege requires a fair and accurate report. Selective reporting, sensationalized framing, or omitted exculpatory context can waive it.

### Treating Unofficial Allegations As Privileged

The privilege covers official proceedings, not unofficial accusations. Verify unofficial allegations independently.

### Adopting Allegations Through Commentary

Commentary that concludes the subject is guilty waives the privilege as to the commentary. Separate report from opinion.

### Relying On Summaries Rather Than Primary Documents

A report based on a distorted summary inherits the distortion. Verify the proceeding's content from primary sources.

### Ignoring Jurisdictional Variation

The privilege's scope and conditions vary by jurisdiction. Assess in each relevant jurisdiction and satisfy the strictest standard.

## Self-Check

Before treating a fair report privilege assessment as sound, verify:

- The privilege's conditions in the relevant jurisdiction are understood, including the requirement of an official proceeding, accurate attribution, fair framing, and any specific local rules.
- Allegations are attributed to the specific official source, with clear, proximate, and specific attribution that conveys the allegation's basis to the reader.
- The report is framed fairly and without distortion, conveying the proceeding's substance accurately, including charges, denials, and significant context, and avoiding sensationalized framing.
- Official proceedings are distinguished from unofficial allegations, with unofficial accusations verified independently rather than relying on the privilege.
- Commentary and framing do not adopt unproven allegations as the journalist's own assertions, with opinion separated from report and resting on disclosed facts.
- The proceeding's content is verified from primary documents or transcripts, not from summaries or secondhand accounts, and reports are updated as the proceeding develops.
- Jurisdictional variation is accounted for, with the report structured to satisfy the strictest applicable standard where it crosses jurisdictions.
- The subject's response or denial is included where the proceeding contains it or where fairness requires seeking it.
- The assessment is documented and reviewed by a responsible editor and, for high-risk reports, legal counsel before publication.
- Fair report privilege assessment supports editorial judgment; significant privilege questions should involve legal counsel, recognizing that this skill does not provide legal advice, that the privilege is conditional and jurisdiction-specific, and that reliance on the privilege requires professional legal advice and careful editorial framing.
