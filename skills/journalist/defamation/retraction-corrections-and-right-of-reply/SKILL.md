# Retraction, Corrections, And Right Of Reply

Corrections, retractions, and the right of reply are the mechanisms by which journalism addresses errors and gives subjects the opportunity to respond, and they serve both accuracy and legal risk management, because prompt, accurate corrections demonstrate good faith, support defamation defenses in many jurisdictions, and maintain the trust that journalism depends on. The judgment problem is that the pressure to defend the original story can lead to resisting justified corrections, while the pressure to appease a complaining subject can lead to unjustified retractions that distort the record and undermine the reporting. A defensible practice assesses each correction request against the actual accuracy of the story, corrects genuine errors promptly and prominently, resists unjustified demands clearly, and manages the legal dimensions with counsel.

Use this skill when handling correction, retraction, or right-of-reply requests, assessing whether corrections are warranted, drafting corrections, or managing post-publication accuracy. The main risk is that an agent treats correction requests as public relations problems to be managed rather than as accuracy questions to be assessed, resisting justified corrections or capitulating to unjustified demands.

## Core Rules

### Assess Each Request Against The Story's Actual Accuracy

The first question when a correction or retraction request arrives is whether the story is accurate, and this assessment must be made against the actual reporting and evidence, not against the subject's preference for how the story should read. A subject may request a correction because the story is genuinely wrong, because it is accurate but unflattering, or because they wish to reframe accurate reporting. Distinguish these cases by reviewing the reporting against the specific complaint.

Review the specific statements challenged, the reporting behind them, and the evidence supporting them. If the story contains a factual error, correct it. If the story is accurate but the subject disputes the framing or emphasis, the response is different, and may involve clarification, additional context, or no change. Do not conflate accuracy with the subject's approval; a story can be accurate and unflattering, and accuracy is the standard for correction, not satisfaction. See the fairness-context-and-newsworthiness skill.

### Correct Genuine Errors Promptly And Prominently

When the story contains a factual error, correct it promptly and prominently. Promptness serves accuracy and, in many jurisdictions, supports defamation defenses by demonstrating good faith and mitigating damages. Prominence ensures the correction reaches the audience that saw the error, which serves the reader and the credibility of the journalism. A correction buried where few will see it does not fully serve accuracy.

Draft the correction to state the error clearly, provide the accurate information, and explain the correction transparently, without minimizing the error or obscuring it. The correction should be understandable to a reader who did not see the original, conveying what was wrong and what is right. Avoid defensive or minimizing language, which undermines the correction's credibility. For online stories, update the story itself and note the correction, so that the corrected version is what future readers encounter.

### Distinguish Corrections From Clarifications And Editor's Notes

Not every post-publication change is a correction, and distinguishing the types serves accuracy and clarity. A correction fixes a factual error. A clarification addresses a story that was technically accurate but unclear or misleading, adding context or restating information to improve understanding. An editor's note addresses broader concerns about the story's framing, sourcing, or handling, often after editorial review. Each serves a different purpose and should be labeled accurately.

Use the appropriate label for the change, so that readers understand what is being addressed. Calling a clarification a correction implies a factual error where there was none; calling a correction a clarification minimizes an actual error. The label should match the substance, and the substance should address the genuine concern, whether error, clarity, or framing. This precision serves both accuracy and the credibility of the corrections process.

### Resist Unjustified Retraction Demands Clearly

Some retraction demands are unjustified, seeking to suppress accurate reporting rather than to correct genuine errors, and these should be resisted clearly. A demand that the story be retracted because it is unflattering, because the subject disputes accurate facts, or because the subject wishes to reframe the reporting is not a basis for retraction. Assess the demand against the story's accuracy, and where the story is accurate, respond clearly that the story is correct and no retraction is warranted, while offering to consider specific factual disputes.

Resist the pressure to capitulate to unjustified demands, which distorts the record and undermines the reporting, while remaining open to genuine factual disputes. The response to an unjustified demand should be respectful, specific, and firm, addressing the substance and explaining the basis for the decision not to retract. Document the demand and the response, as they may be relevant to subsequent legal action or public discussion.

### Manage The Legal Dimensions With Counsel

Corrections and retractions have legal dimensions, particularly in defamation cases, and they should be managed with counsel where the story carries defamation risk or where the demand is aggressive. In some jurisdictions, a retraction demand triggers specific legal procedures, such as a requirement to respond within a certain period or a cap on damages if a retraction is offered. A correction can mitigate damages in defamation cases by demonstrating good faith, but a hasty or poorly worded correction can also be used against the newsroom.

Involve counsel where the story carries defamation risk, where the demand threatens litigation, or where the legal procedures are complex. Do not handle aggressive retraction demands, particularly those accompanied by legal threats, without counsel review. The correction itself should be accurate and transparent, but the legal strategy around it should be informed by counsel's assessment of the defamation landscape. See the journalist-legal-risk-assessment skill.

### Honor The Right Of Reply Substantively, Not As A Formality

The right of reply, the obligation to seek and present the subject's response to allegations, is both an ethical obligation and a defamation risk mitigation, and it should be honored substantively rather than as a formality. A genuine right of reply means contacting the subject with the specific allegations, giving them a meaningful opportunity to respond, and presenting their response accurately and prominently in the story. A perfunctory contact that does not allow meaningful response, or a buried or distorted presentation of the response, does not honor the obligation.

Honor the right of reply by contacting the subject before publication with the specific allegations, allowing reasonable time for response, and presenting the response accurately and prominently. If the subject declines to respond, note that. If the subject responds, present the response fairly, including denials, explanations, and context. The right of reply serves accuracy, fairness, and legal risk, and honoring it substantively strengthens the story rather than weakening it. See the fairness-context-and-newsworthiness skill.

### Document The Accuracy Assessment And The Response

Document the accuracy assessment, the correction decision, and the response to the subject, so that the process can be reviewed and defended if challenged. The documentation should include the specific complaint, the review of the reporting and evidence, the basis for the decision, and the correction or response provided. This documentation supports accountability, defends against claims that the newsroom ignored or mishandled the complaint, and provides a record for any subsequent legal action.

Documentation is part of the corrections process, not a secondary task. A correction decision that is not documented is harder to defend, and a pattern of well-documented corrections demonstrates a serious accuracy practice. Build documentation into the corrections workflow, so that each request generates a record of the assessment and response. See the reporting-and-source-verification skill.

## Common Traps

### Resisting Justified Corrections To Defend The Original Story

The pressure to defend the original story can lead to resisting genuine errors. Assess each request against accuracy, not against preference.

### Conflating Accuracy With The Subject's Approval

A story can be accurate and unflattering. Accuracy is the standard for correction, not the subject's satisfaction.

### Burying Corrections Where Few Will See Them

Prominence serves accuracy and credibility. A buried correction does not fully serve the reader or mitigate harm.

### Mismatching The Label To The Change

Corrections, clarifications, and editor's notes serve different purposes. Label accurately so readers understand what is being addressed.

### Capitulating To Unjustified Retraction Demands

Suppressing accurate reporting distorts the record and undermines the journalism. Resist unjustified demands clearly.

### Handling Legal Threats Without Counsel

Corrections have legal dimensions, and aggressive demands trigger procedures. Involve counsel where defamation risk or threats are present.

### Treating The Right Of Reply As A Formality

A perfunctory contact or buried response does not honor the obligation. Contact with specific allegations, allow meaningful response, and present fairly.

### Failing To Document The Assessment And Response

Undocumented decisions are hard to defend. Document the complaint, review, decision, and response as part of the workflow.

## Self-Check

Before treating a corrections and right-of-reply practice as sound, verify:

- Each correction or retraction request is assessed against the story's actual accuracy, reviewing the specific statements, the reporting, and the evidence, distinguishing genuine errors from unflattering accuracy.
- Genuine errors are corrected promptly and prominently, with clear, transparent, non-defensive language that conveys the error and the accurate information to readers who did not see the original.
- Corrections, clarifications, and editor's notes are distinguished and labeled accurately, matching the label to the substance of the change.
- Unjustified retraction demands are resisted clearly and specifically, with the response addressing the substance and explaining the basis for the decision.
- Legal dimensions are managed with counsel where the story carries defamation risk, where demands threaten litigation, or where retraction procedures apply.
- The right of reply is honored substantively, with specific allegations communicated before publication, meaningful opportunity for response, and accurate, prominent presentation of the response.
- The accuracy assessment, correction decision, and response are documented, supporting accountability and defense against challenge.
- The corrections process is consistent, prominent, and transparent, serving readers and credibility.
- Post-publication accuracy is maintained through updates as facts change, particularly for ongoing proceedings.
- Corrections and right-of-reply practice supports editorial judgment; legal dimensions should involve counsel, recognizing that this skill does not provide legal advice, that retraction procedures are jurisdiction-specific, and that decisions about corrections and retractions in legally sensitive stories require professional legal advice and senior editorial judgment.
