# Beat Coverage Rhythm

A beat is not a series of isolated stories; it is a sustained rhythm of coverage that must be maintained day after day, year after year. Managing that rhythm, deciding what gets covered now, what gets developed slowly, and what gets declined, is a core beat skill that is easy to undervalue. The harm this skill prevents is the two opposite failures: the beat that drowns in reactive daily coverage and never produces depth, and the beat that chases only big features and misses the routine events where accountability and trends actually live. Rhythm is the discipline of allocating finite attention across the recurring, the breaking, and the long-term, so the beat remains both current and substantive over time.

## Core Rules

### Establish a baseline calendar of recurring coverage
Every beat has predictable cycles: meetings, hearings, earnings calls, legislative sessions, reporting deadlines, seasonal events. Map them and build them into your workflow as baseline obligations. Covering the routine consistently is what keeps you visible, builds source relationships, and positions you to catch the significant moments that occur within routine events.

### Separate the urgent from the important
Breaking events demand immediate response, but not every urgent item is important, and many important developments are slow and non-urgent. Develop the habit of asking, for each incoming item, whether it merely feels pressing or genuinely matters to the beat's significance. Protect time for the important work that has no deadline pushing it forward.

### Allocate time deliberately across routine, breaking, and enterprise
Treat your time as a budget. Decide, in advance and by default, how much goes to routine coverage, how much to breaking news, and how much to longer enterprise. Without a deliberate allocation, the urgent crowds out the important and the beat becomes purely reactive. Revisit the allocation as the beat's demands shift.

### Build a rolling story pipeline with stages
Maintain a living pipeline that tracks stories from raw lead, to reporting in progress, to ready for publication, to follow-up needed. This makes the beat's workload visible, prevents leads from dying in neglect, and ensures that enterprise work advances in the gaps between daily demands. A beat without a pipeline forgets its own best ideas.

### Follow up as a matter of policy
The first story on a development is rarely the last word. Institutions respond, promises are made or broken, consequences unfold. Build follow-up into the rhythm: track what you have published, note what was promised or predicted, and return to it. Accountability lives in the follow-up, not the initial splash.

### Recognize and manage the overload points
Beats have predictable crunch periods, budget seasons, election cycles, anniversary of a major event, when demand spikes. Anticipate them, front-load what can be done early, and negotiate with editors about realistic output. Trying to sustain peak output indefinitely leads to burnout and shallow coverage.

### Use the slow periods for depth and network-building
When the beat is quiet, resist the urge to coast. Use the lulls for enterprise reporting, source cultivation, skills development, and getting ahead of the next cycle. The depth and relationships built in slow periods are what produce the strong coverage when the beat gets busy again.

### Decline or defer coverage that does not serve the beat
Not every press release, event, or tip deserves a story. Develop criteria for what merits coverage and the confidence to defer or decline the rest. Saying yes to everything produces shallow, voluminous coverage; saying no strategically preserves capacity for work that matters.

## Common Traps

### Pure reactivity
If every day is spent chasing whatever broke that morning, the beat never develops depth. Reactivity feels productive but produces coverage that anyone could write. Carve out protected time for enterprise even when the inbox is full.

### Neglecting the routine
The unglamorous recurring events are where institutional decisions are actually made and where sources are maintained. Skipping them to chase flashier stories erodes the foundation of the beat and causes you to miss the significant moments embedded in routine proceedings.

### Letting leads die in the backlog
A promising tip that sits untouched for weeks becomes stale and then forgotten. Without a pipeline and periodic review, the beat's best ideas are lost to inertia. Review the backlog regularly and either advance or consciously kill each lead.

### One-and-done coverage
Publishing a story and never returning to it lets subjects wait out the attention and undoes accountability. Without a follow-up discipline, the beat produces announcements rather than consequences.

### Burnout disguised as dedication
Sustained overwork produces diminishing returns: shallower reporting, more errors, weaker judgment. Treating exhaustion as a badge of honor harms the beat over the long run. Manage workload and advocate for sustainable pacing with editors.

### Over-covering to appear busy
Volume is not value. Producing many thin stories to demonstrate productivity wastes capacity that could go to fewer, stronger pieces. Judge the beat by significance and depth, not by item count.

### Failing to anticipate predictable cycles
Beats have known busy seasons. Discovering them only when they arrive guarantees being overwhelmed. Study the beat's calendar and prepare in advance.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I mapped and built into my workflow the beat's recurring calendar of meetings, cycles, and deadlines?
- [ ] Am I deliberately allocating time across routine, breaking, and enterprise coverage rather than letting the urgent dominate?
- [ ] Do I maintain a rolling story pipeline that tracks leads from raw tip through publication and follow-up?
- [ ] Is follow-up built into my rhythm, with earlier stories revisited for consequences and broken promises?
- [ ] Have I anticipated the beat's predictable overload points and prepared or negotiated realistic output in advance?
- [ ] Am I using slow periods for enterprise, network-building, and skills development rather than coasting?
- [ ] Have I developed and applied criteria for what to decline or defer so capacity goes to work that matters?
- [ ] Am I distinguishing genuine significance from mere urgency when deciding what to cover?
- [ ] Is my pacing sustainable over the long term, or am I running on overwork that will degrade the coverage?
- [ ] When I review my recent output, does it reflect a healthy mix of routine, breaking, and enterprise, or has one type crowded out the others?
