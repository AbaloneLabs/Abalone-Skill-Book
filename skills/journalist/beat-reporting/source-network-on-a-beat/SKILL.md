# Source Network On A Beat

A beat reporter's competitive advantage lives in the breadth, depth, and trust of their source network. Sources provide the tips, context, documents, and reality checks that separate informed coverage from press-release rewriting. The harm this skill prevents is the common pattern where a reporter relies on a narrow set of convenient official sources, loses access the moment those relationships sour, and discovers too late that the real information was held by people they never bothered to know. Building and sustaining a source network is deliberate, ongoing work, and it requires balancing the hunger for access against the independence that gives the coverage credibility.

## Core Rules

### Build a layered network, not a star system
A robust beat has sources at every layer: top officials and spokespeople, mid-level decision-makers, front-line staff, regulators and overseers, academics and analysts, advocates and critics, and affected community members. No single layer is sufficient. A network built around one or two star sources collapses when they leave, get fired, or turn hostile. Diversify deliberately so the loss of any one source does not cripple the beat.

### Recruit sources before you need them
The time to cultivate a source is not when a story breaks. Build relationships in the quiet periods through genuine curiosity, fair prior coverage, and consistent presence. Sources call back the reporters they know and trust. If your first contact is a request for a sensitive favor, you have started too late.

### Offer something of value without trading integrity
Relationships are reciprocal. What you offer is fairness, accuracy, context, and the chance to be heard, never favorable coverage or killed stories. Be honest about what you can and cannot do. A source who understands the rules of the relationship trusts you; one who expects transactional favors will eventually be disappointed or will corrupt the coverage.

### Maintain clear lines between on-record, background, and deep-background
Ambiguity about attribution rules destroys source relationships and creates legal risk. Establish and confirm the terms of each conversation. Honor them scrupulously, even when it would be convenient to blur the lines. A reputation for keeping agreements is the currency that sustains a network over years.

### Cultivate critics and dissidents as deliberately as insiders
Official sources have an institutional interest in the official narrative. Critics, former insiders, and affected communities often hold the counter-narrative and the documents that expose what officials obscure. A network drawn only from the powerful reproduces the powerful's view. Actively recruit those with reason to challenge the institution.

### Protect sources who take risks for you
Some sources risk careers, legal exposure, or retaliation by speaking. Understand the risk each source runs, protect their identity with operational discipline, and honor pledges of anonymity strictly. Burning a source, even once, ends your credibility with the entire network, because sources talk to each other.

### Periodically refresh and prune the network
Networks decay. Sources retire, get promoted, become compromised, or lose access. Continually recruit new sources, especially at lower levels where tomorrow's decision-makers begin their careers. Reassess older relationships for whether they still provide genuine value or have become conduits for spin.

### Keep sources independent of each other
Apparent multiplicity can be illusory if sources share a common origin or coordinate. When corroborating, confirm that sources are genuinely independent. A network where everyone traces back to one well is a single source in disguise.

## Common Traps

### Over-reliance on official spokespeople
Spokespeople are paid to deliver the institution's line. Useful for official response, they are the weakest source for understanding what is actually happening. A beat built on spokespeople produces official-sounding coverage that misses the story.

### Trading access for soft coverage
The temptation to soften coverage to preserve a valuable relationship is constant and corrosive. Access gained at the price of independence is not worth having; it produces coverage that serves the source, not the public. Recognize the trade and refuse it.

### Becoming captive to a single dominant source
When one source provides most of your tips, your coverage reflects that source's interests and blind spots. Diversify aggressively. If one source's departure would gut your beat, the network is too thin.

### Failing to verify what sources say
Familiar, trusted sources can still be wrong, self-serving, or manipulated. Trust lowers the barrier to belief; it does not eliminate the duty to verify. Apply the same standard to a friendly source as to a hostile one.

### Burning a source through carelessness
An identifying detail in a story, a careless metadata trail, or a conversation overheard can unmask a confidential source. The damage is irreversible and spreads through the network. Audit every story and every communication for source-identifying traces.

### Neglecting the network in quiet periods
When nothing is breaking, it is easy to let relationships lapse. Then the crisis comes and no one returns your call. Sustained, low-intensity contact in the quiet times is what makes the network functional under pressure.

### Confusing friendliness with access
A source who is warm and available is not necessarily providing genuine information; warmth can be a strategy to manage you. Evaluate sources by the quality and risk of what they actually disclose, not by how much they seem to like you.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does my source network span multiple layers of the beat, from top officials to front-line staff and affected communities?
- [ ] Am I recruiting and cultivating sources during quiet periods, before I urgently need them?
- [ ] Do I offer sources genuine value, fairness and a hearing, without trading favorable coverage or killing stories?
- [ ] Are the attribution rules for each source explicit and consistently honored?
- [ ] Have I cultivated critics and dissidents as deliberately as insiders, so the network is not captive to the official narrative?
- [ ] Do I understand and protect the risks each confidential source runs, with operational discipline?
- [ ] Am I continually refreshing the network with new sources, especially at rising levels, and pruning relationships that no longer provide value?
- [ ] When I corroborate, have I confirmed that sources are genuinely independent rather than sharing a common origin?
- [ ] Am I applying verification standards to trusted sources, not only to hostile ones?
- [ ] Have I audited my recent stories and communications for any detail that could identify a confidential source?
