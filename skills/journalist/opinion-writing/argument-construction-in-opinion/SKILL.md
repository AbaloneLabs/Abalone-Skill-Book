# Argument Construction In Opinion

Opinion journalism is not the absence of standards; it is a different set of standards. A strong opinion piece does not merely assert a view; it constructs an argument, grounded in evidence, that earns the reader's assent through reasoning rather than demands it through volume. The harm this skill prevents is the degradation of opinion into pure assertion, tribal signaling, or performance: columns that state conclusions without supporting them, that cherry-pick evidence, that strawman opponents, or that present opinion as if it were established fact. Weak opinion journalism fails readers who seek to understand a position and decide whether to adopt it, and it poisons public discourse by modeling argument as combat rather than reasoning. Strong opinion holds itself to evidentiary and logical standards more demanding, not less, than news.

Use this skill when writing or editing any opinion piece, editorial, column, or op-ed. Consult editors on argument structure and, where claims could carry legal risk or factual dispute, ensure opinion is clearly distinguished from news and grounded in checkable fact.

## Core Rules

### Build the argument from evidence, not toward a conclusion
A strong opinion piece begins with a genuine question, examines the evidence, and reaches a conclusion the evidence supports. Beginning with the conclusion and then hunting for support produces cherry-picked, defensive argument that sophisticated readers detect. State the conclusion clearly, but show the reasoning and the evidence that led there, including the evidence that complicates the case. Readers should be able to follow, and to disagree intelligently, because the argument is transparent rather than opaque assertion.

### Distinguish opinion from fact, and label each clearly
Opinion pieces make claims of value, interpretation, and prediction; these differ from factual claims and must be distinguishable. "The policy is unjust" is opinion; "the policy displaced ten thousand families" is a factual claim that must be checkable. Conflating them, presenting contested facts as settled or presenting opinions as facts, misleads readers and exposes the outlet. Where a factual claim underpins the opinion, the claim must be accurate; opinion is not a license to misstate facts.

### Support factual claims with checkable evidence
Every factual assertion in an opinion piece must meet the same accuracy standard as news. Cite sources, link to evidence, and avoid unsourced generalizations. "Studies show" without citation, "many people believe" without basis, and "experts agree" without naming experts are forms of unsupported assertion that weaken the argument and mislead readers. The strength of an opinion depends partly on the strength of the facts it rests on; weak factual foundations undermine even a sound value judgment.

### Engage the strongest counterargument, not the weakest
Honest argument engages the best version of opposing views, steelmanning rather than strawmanning. Address the most serious objection to your position and explain why your argument survives it. Strawmanning, attacking a weak or distorted version of the opposing view, may score points with those who already agree but persuades no one and signals intellectual dishonesty. Readers who hold the opposing view recognize instantly when their position has been misrepresented, and they stop trusting the argument.

### Separate ad hominem from substantive critique
Criticize positions, arguments, decisions, and conduct, not motives, character, or identity. "The senator's policy would harm working families" is substantive; "the senator hates working families" is ad hominem. Even where motive is genuinely relevant, argue it from evidence rather than asserting it as insult. Ad hominem attacks substitute personal attack for reasoning, lower the quality of discourse, and often cross into defamation risk when directed at identifiable individuals.

### Calibrate certainty to the strength of the case
Opinion allows stronger language than news, but certainty should still match the evidence. A well-supported argument can be stated firmly; a speculative or contested argument should acknowledge its limits. Overstating certainty, declaring a position obviously correct or opponents obviously wrong, signals weakness rather than strength and alienates readers who see the complications. Honest calibration, acknowledging where the case is strong and where it rests on judgment calls, builds credibility.

### Disclose relevant interests and perspectives
Opinion writers bring perspectives, affiliations, and sometimes financial or institutional interests that shape their views. Disclose material conflicts, funding sources, and relevant affiliations so readers can weigh the argument in context. A column advocating a policy from which the writer would benefit financially must disclose the benefit. Transparency about perspective is not weakness; it lets readers evaluate the argument honestly rather than discovering hidden interests later.

### Distinguish the writer's voice from the outlet's editorial voice
An individual columnist's opinion is not necessarily the outlet's editorial position, and an unsigned editorial represents the institution's voice. Maintain the distinction clearly so readers know whether they are reading one writer's view or the outlet's collective position. Conflating them misleads readers about the weight and authority behind the argument.

## Common Traps

### Conclusion-first argumentation
Deciding the position and then selecting supportive evidence produces cherry-picked, unconvincing argument. The trap is that the conclusion feels right, but the argument fails to earn assent from unconvinced readers.

### Conflating opinion and fact
Presenting value judgments as facts or factual claims as opinions misleads readers about what is contestable. The trap is that blurring the line strengthens the rhetorical moment while undermining accuracy.

### Unsourced generalization
"Studies show," "experts agree," and "everyone knows" substitute assertion for evidence. The trap is that vague sourcing reads as authoritative while hiding an absent evidentiary basis.

### Strawmanning opponents
Attacking a distorted version of the opposing view scores points with allies and persuades no one. The trap is that the strawman is easier to defeat than the real argument, but defeating it proves nothing.

### Ad hominem attack
Substituting personal attack for substantive critique lowers discourse and risks defamation. The trap is that insult feels forceful, but it replaces reasoning with aggression.

### Certainty inflation
Declaring a contested position obviously correct signals arrogance and alienates readers who see the complications. The trap is that strong language feels confident, but overstatement undermines credibility.

### Undisclosed interests
Hiding affiliations, funding, or benefits that shape the argument lets readers weigh it on false premises. The trap is that disclosure feels like weakness, but its later discovery is far more damaging.

## Self-Check

- Does the argument build from evidence toward a conclusion the evidence supports, showing reasoning rather than merely asserting?
- Are opinions distinguished from factual claims, with each labeled clearly so readers know what is contestable?
- Is every factual claim supported by checkable evidence, cited and free of unsourced generalization?
- Does the piece engage the strongest counterargument rather than strawmanning the weakest version of the opposing view?
- Is critique substantive rather than ad hominem, addressing positions and conduct rather than motives and character?
- Is certainty calibrated to the strength of the case, with limits acknowledged where the argument rests on judgment?
- Are relevant interests, affiliations, and perspectives disclosed so readers can weigh the argument in context?
- Is the writer's individual voice distinguished from the outlet's editorial voice, and have editors reviewed the argument where claims could carry legal risk or factual dispute?
