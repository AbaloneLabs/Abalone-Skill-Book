# Separating News And Opinion

The line between news and opinion is one of the most important distinctions a newsroom maintains, and one of the most often blurred. Readers are entitled to know whether they are encountering verified reporting or interpretation and advocacy, because the two carry different authority and demand different evaluation. The harm this skill prevents is the erosion of that distinction: opinion presented with the authority of news, news slanted to favor a viewpoint, the wall between editorial and reporting sides breached by shared staff or cozy coordination, or readers left unable to tell what they are actually reading. When the line blurs, trust in all the outlet's work declines, because readers can no longer calibrate the credibility of what they encounter.

Use this skill whenever labeling, structuring, or staffing content that sits near the news-opinion boundary, and whenever evaluating whether a piece maintains the distinction. Consult editors on labeling and structural decisions, and where staff cross the boundary, ensure the outlet's policies are followed.

## Core Rules

### Label opinion clearly and prominently
Readers should know they are reading opinion before they are deep into the piece. Label columns, op-eds, editorials, and commentary with clear, visible markers: "Opinion," "Editorial," "Commentary," "Column," in the headline area and on any surface where the piece appears. The label belongs where readers will see it, not buried in metadata or fine print. Vague labels like "Analysis" or "Perspective" can blur the line; use the clearest term and apply it consistently.

### Distinguish analysis from opinion
Analysis, which interprets news using reporting and expertise, sits between straight news and opinion, and it must be labeled and structured to reflect that. Analysis explains what events mean, contextualizes developments, and weighs interpretations, but it does not advocate a position. Opinion advocates, urges, or argues for a conclusion. Confusing them misleads readers: presenting advocacy as analysis inflates its authority, and presenting analysis as advocacy diminishes it. Label each accurately and hold analysis to the standard of even-handed interpretation rather than covert advocacy.

### Maintain structural separation between news and editorial
The credibility of news depends on its independence from the outlet's editorial positions. Maintain a wall between the newsroom and the editorial board, opinion columnists, and the opinion section's editorial decisions. News reporters should not coordinate coverage with opinion writers to advance a shared agenda, opinion writers should not dictate news assignments, and editorial positions should not be presented as the newsroom's reporting. Structural separation, separate leadership, separate meetings, clear policies, makes the independence credible rather than merely asserted.

### Prevent staff conflicts between news and opinion roles
A reporter who covers a beat should not also write opinion about that beat, and an opinion columnist should not also report news on the topics they opine about. Wearing both hats on the same subject blurs the line and undermines the credibility of both roles. Where staff move between news and opinion, manage the transition explicitly, with disclosure and a cooling-off period on the relevant topics. Consultants, contributors, and freelancers who write opinion should not also be reporting news for the outlet on the same subjects.

### Ensure news reporting does not slip into editorializing
News stories can reflect viewpoint through word choice, source selection, emphasis, framing, and omission, even without explicit opinion. Hold news reporting to neutral language, balanced sourcing, and proportionate framing, so that the reporting does the informing rather than the persuading. Editorializing within a news story, through loaded verbs, selective sourcing, or skewed emphasis, blurs the line from the news side and misleads readers who trust the news label.

### Ensure opinion does not misstate facts
Opinion writers have latitude to interpret and advocate, but they do not have license to misstate facts. Factual claims within opinion must meet the same accuracy standard as news. The opinion section's editors should fact-check assertions, require sourcing for empirical claims, and correct errors as visibly as the news side does. Opinion that rests on false facts is not protected by the opinion label; it is misinformation with a column logo.

### Make the distinction survive across surfaces
The news-opinion distinction must hold on every surface: homepage placement, section labeling, social posts, push notifications, search snippets, and syndication. An opinion piece promoted in a news feed without labeling, or a social card that presents a columnist's view as a news headline, misleads readers who encounter the piece out of context. Audit distribution surfaces to ensure the label travels with the content.

### Be transparent about the outlet's editorial positions
Editorials represent the institution's collective view, and that view should be transparently distinguished from the newsroom's reporting. Where the outlet has taken an editorial position on an issue it also covers as news, the separation between the editorial board's advocacy and the newsroom's reporting should be visible and credible. Readers should understand which voice they are hearing and trust that the news coverage is not shaped by the editorial position.

## Common Traps

### Vague or buried opinion labels
"Analysis," "Perspective," or unlabeled commentary presented with news authority blurs the distinction. The trap is that softer labels generate less friction, but they deceive readers about what they are reading.

### Covert advocacy in news reporting
Loaded language, selective sourcing, and skewed emphasis editorialize within news stories. The trap is that the slant is subtle enough to feel like reporting while persuading under the news label.

### Opinion that misstates facts
Factual errors sheltered behind the opinion label spread misinformation with added authority. The trap is that opinion feels protected, but false facts are false regardless of section.

### Shared staff crossing the line
Reporters writing opinion on their beats, or columnists reporting news on their topics, blurs roles. The trap is that versatile staff are convenient, but the role conflict undermines credibility.

### Coordination between news and editorial sides
Newsrooms and editorial boards coordinating to advance shared agendas breaches the wall. The trap is that coordination feels efficient and mission-aligned, but it destroys the independence that gives news its authority.

### Label that does not travel across surfaces
Opinion labeled on the article page but presented as news in social posts or feeds misleads out-of-context readers. The trap is that the label exists somewhere, but distribution surfaces strip it.

### Editorials presented as the newsroom's voice
Conflating the editorial board's institutional position with the newsroom's reporting misleads readers about authority. The trap is that the unified voice feels strong, but it sacrifices the credibility of separation.

## Self-Check

- Is opinion labeled clearly and prominently, with the label visible on every surface where the piece appears?
- Is analysis distinguished from opinion, with analysis held to even-handed interpretation rather than covert advocacy?
- Is structural separation between news and editorial maintained, with separate leadership, meetings, and policies?
- Are staff conflicts between news and opinion roles prevented, with disclosure and cooling-off for transitions?
- Does news reporting avoid editorializing through loaded language, selective sourcing, or skewed emphasis?
- Are factual claims within opinion held to the same accuracy standard as news, with visible correction of errors?
- Does the news-opinion distinction survive across homepage, social, push, search, and syndication surfaces?
- Are the outlet's editorial positions transparently distinguished from newsroom reporting, and have editors reviewed labeling and structural decisions where the boundary is at issue?
