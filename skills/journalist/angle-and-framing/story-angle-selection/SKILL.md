# Story Angle Selection

The same set of facts can yield radically different stories depending on the angle chosen, what is foregrounded, what is subordinated, what question the story sets out to answer. Angle selection is therefore one of the most consequential and least visible editorial judgments. The harm this skill prevents is the selection of an angle that distorts significance, that sensationalizes a peripheral aspect while burying the substantive one, that frames a story to fit a predetermined narrative or ideological preference, or that simply misses what actually matters most to the audience. Choosing an angle well is a discipline of identifying the genuine significance of the facts and finding the honest, most illuminating entry point.

## Core Rules

### Identify the true significance before choosing the angle
Before deciding how to frame the story, determine what is genuinely most significant about the development: who is affected, what is at stake, what is new, what conflicts with prior understanding. The angle should emerge from the significance, not impose a significance onto the facts. Angles chosen before significance is understood often distort.

### Distinguish the most significant angle from the most dramatic
The most newsworthy angle is not always the most sensational. A dramatic detail may be peripheral; a less flashy aspect may be where the real consequence lies. Resist the pull toward the angle that generates clicks if it buries the angle that serves understanding. Calibrate the angle to public significance, not to novelty or outrage.

### Test the angle against the full facts
An angle that holds up only by omitting complicating context is a distortion. Before committing, test whether the chosen frame remains honest when the full set of facts is considered. If the angle requires suppressing relevant information to work, it is the wrong angle.

### Consider the audience's stake and existing understanding
The best angle connects the story to what the audience already cares about or needs to know, without pandering. Consider what readers already understand, what misconceptions prevail, and what framing would most increase their genuine understanding. An angle that ignores the audience's stake fails to land; one that panders to it fails to inform.

### Generate multiple angles before selecting
Force yourself to articulate several plausible angles before choosing. The first angle that occurs is often the conventional or convenient one; alternatives may be more illuminating. Comparing angles reveals what each foregrounds and obscures and leads to a more deliberate choice.

### Guard against angle selection driven by bias or convenience
Angles can be chosen because they confirm a reporter's or outlet's worldview, because they flatter a source, or because they are easy to execute. Audit the angle choice for whether it serves the audience or serves a preference. The honest angle is sometimes harder to report and less congenial; choose it anyway.

### Revisit the angle as reporting develops
The angle that seemed right at the outset may be wrong once reporting reveals what is actually significant. Stay willing to reframe as the story's true center becomes clear. Clinging to an initial angle despite contradictory reporting produces a story that misses its own point.

### Align headline, lede, and framing with the chosen angle consistently
Once an angle is chosen, the headline, lede, structure, and emphasis should cohere with it. Inconsistency, a headline that promises one angle and a body that delivers another, confuses readers and undermines credibility. Ensure the angle is consistently executed throughout.

## Common Traps

### Sensationalizing the peripheral
Choosing the angle that shocks or outrages while the substantive significance is buried misleads readers about what matters. The dramatic angle is tempting and often wrong.

### The angle that requires omission
If an angle only works by leaving out relevant facts, it is a distortion. Test the angle against the complete picture.

### Confirmation-driven angle selection
Choosing the angle that confirms a prior belief or an outlet's editorial lean, rather than the angle that best represents significance, turns reporting into advocacy. Audit for bias in the choice.

### Missing the real story
The most common failure is simply not recognizing the most significant angle, often because it is structural or slow-moving rather than event-driven. Invest in understanding significance before locking the frame.

### Convenience-driven angles
Choosing the angle that is easiest to report, or that relies on readily available sources, rather than the angle that matters most. Significance, not ease, should drive the choice.

### Locking the angle too early
Committing to an angle before reporting is complete, then forcing the story to fit it, ignores what the reporting actually reveals. Hold the angle loosely until the facts are clear.

### Incoherent execution
Choosing an angle but then writing a headline, lede, or structure that serves a different one, leaving readers confused about what the story is. Execute the chosen angle consistently.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I identified the genuine significance of the facts before choosing the angle?
- [ ] Is the chosen angle the most significant, not merely the most dramatic or sensational?
- [ ] Does the angle hold up when tested against the full set of facts, without requiring omission?
- [ ] Does the angle connect to the audience's stake and increase their genuine understanding?
- [ ] Did I generate and compare multiple plausible angles before selecting?
- [ ] Have I audited the choice for bias, convenience, or confirmation driving?
- [ ] Am I willing to reframe if reporting reveals a different center of significance?
- [ ] Do the headline, lede, structure, and emphasis cohere consistently with the chosen angle?
- [ ] Have I avoided burying the substantive angle beneath a more clickable one?
- [ ] Would a knowledgeable observer recognize the chosen angle as the most honest and significant frame?
