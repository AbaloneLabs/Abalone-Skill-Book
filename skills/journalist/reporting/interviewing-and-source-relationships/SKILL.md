# Interviewing And Source Relationships

Interviews are not just a way to collect quotes. They are a structured method for learning what a person knows, how they know it, what they experienced, what they believe, what they want, and what they may be unable or unwilling to say. A good journalist protects accuracy, independence, fairness, and human dignity throughout the relationship with a source.

Use this skill before interviewing a witness, expert, official, victim, whistleblower, accused person, community member, employee, executive, campaign representative, or ordinary person affected by news. The main risk is that an agent may treat a source as a quote generator and miss consent, power, trauma, incentives, ground rules, and the difference between memory and proof.

## Core Rules

### Define The Source's Relationship To The Story

Before the interview, identify why this source matters. A source may be:

- a direct witness;
- a participant;
- an affected person;
- an accused person;
- an advocate;
- an official representative;
- a technical expert;
- a data custodian;
- a whistleblower;
- a critic;
- a historian or context source;
- a person whose private life is being affected by coverage.

Different source roles require different questions. A witness can describe what they saw, not necessarily why it happened. An expert can explain mechanisms, not necessarily facts of a specific event. A spokesperson can give an institutional position, not necessarily the full truth. A victim can describe harm, but should not be pressured to supply emotional detail merely because it makes the story vivid.

### Set Ground Rules Before Sensitive Information

Do not let interview status remain vague. Begin with the assumption that an interview is on the record, then clarify any change before the source shares sensitive information. Many people use "off the record," "background," "not for attribution," and "deep background" inconsistently. Define the terms plainly.

Clarify:

- what can be quoted by name;
- what can be used without the source's name;
- what cannot be used for publication;
- whether the information can be used to guide further reporting;
- whether the source can be described by role;
- whether documents can be referenced;
- whether recording is allowed;
- whether the source can review exact quotes for accuracy, not rewrite the story.

Record the agreement in notes. If the ground rules are disputed later, the reporter should be able to show what was agreed.

### Prepare Questions, Then Listen Past Them

Preparation prevents shallow interviews. Research the source, timeline, public records, prior statements, key documents, terminology, and likely evasions. Write questions that move from factual foundation to explanation to accountability.

Ask:

- What happened?
- How do you know?
- When did you learn it?
- Who else was present?
- What document or record supports that?
- What did you do next?
- What are you unsure about?
- What would someone on the other side say?
- Who else should I speak with?
- What am I missing?

Do not cling to a script when the source reveals a gap, contradiction, or new lead. Follow up with specific, neutral questions. Silence can be useful. Let the source finish, then ask for precision.

### Protect Accuracy In Quotation

Quotes must represent meaning faithfully. Do not clean up a quote so aggressively that it changes voice or substance. Do not preserve confusing grammar if paraphrase would be more accurate. Do not stitch phrases together in a way that creates a sentence the source did not say. Do not omit context that changes meaning.

For each quote, keep:

- exact wording;
- recording or notes basis;
- interview setting;
- date and time;
- whether the conversation was in another language;
- any translation choices;
- relevant nonverbal context only when it affects meaning.

If a quote is unclear, ask again. If the source later claims they were misquoted, compare against notes and recordings rather than memory.

### Interview Vulnerable Sources With Care

Vulnerability may come from age, trauma, grief, immigration status, employment risk, disability, poverty, custody, incarceration, illness, war, abuse, political repression, or lack of media experience. A vulnerable source may not understand publication consequences.

Explain:

- who you are;
- what the story is about;
- where it may appear;
- that publication can spread beyond the immediate audience;
- what name or identifying details may be used;
- that they can decline to answer;
- that you cannot promise outcomes you do not control.

Avoid extracting pain for color. Ask only for details needed to understand the public issue. Consider whether identifying the person could expose them to harassment, legal risk, family conflict, employment loss, deportation, stigma, retaliation, or renewed trauma.

### Handle Experts As Evidence Interpreters, Not Oracles

Experts help interpret complexity, but they are not a substitute for evidence. Check expertise against the precise topic. A doctor is not automatically an epidemiologist. A lawyer is not automatically expert in every jurisdiction. A professor may be prominent but outside the relevant method.

Ask experts:

- What evidence supports this conclusion?
- How strong is the evidence?
- What are the limits or uncertainties?
- What would change your view?
- Is there a mainstream consensus?
- Are there credible disputes?
- Do you have conflicts, funding ties, advocacy roles, or paid work related to this issue?

Use multiple experts when the field is contested or high stakes. Do not quote an expert merely because they are available or colorful.

### Maintain Independence From Sources

Sources have motives. That does not make them useless, but it means the reporter must stay independent. A source may want publicity, revenge, policy change, legal leverage, market movement, protection, sympathy, or control of timing.

Do not let sources preview full drafts unless newsroom policy permits it for narrow technical verification. Do not trade favorable framing for access. Do not promise headlines, story placement, legal help, employment help, anonymity without review, or removal of material after publication. Keep professional boundaries with frequent sources, especially powerful officials and executives.

### Give Subjects A Fair Chance To Respond

When a story includes criticism, allegation, wrongdoing, or a serious negative portrayal, make meaningful efforts to reach the person or organization involved. Provide enough detail for them to respond to the substance, not just a vague request for comment. Give reasonable time based on urgency. Document outreach attempts.

If they do not respond, say that they did not respond by deadline or that attempts were unsuccessful. Do not characterize silence as guilt. If they respond after publication with relevant facts, consider updating the story.

## Common Traps

### Letting A Source Define The Frame

Sources often bring a ready-made story: hero, villain, scandal, crisis, comeback, betrayal, or breakthrough. Listen, but do not adopt the frame without testing it. Ask who benefits from this framing and what evidence is missing.

### Confusing Emotion With Accuracy

A sincere, detailed, emotional account can still contain errors. Memory is shaped by stress, time, repetition, trauma, and suggestion. Treat testimony with respect while still checking dates, sequences, identities, and physical facts.

### Creating Accidental Anonymity

Even if a name is withheld, a combination of role, location, age, workplace, family detail, and event description may identify the person. Review the full story for indirect identification.

### Asking Leading Questions

Questions like "Were you terrified when the company ignored the danger?" tell the source what answer fits the story. Ask open questions first, then precise follow-ups. Use direct accountability questions when needed, but do not plant facts.

### Allowing Quote Approval To Become Story Control

Quote checks can prevent errors, especially with technical or translated material. They should not allow a source to rewrite history, remove accountability, soften criticism, or insert public relations language. Make the boundary clear.

### Forgetting Safety After Publication

Publication can create consequences. A source who was safe during reporting may become exposed afterward. Consider whether the source needs timing awareness, identity protection, or careful removal of unnecessary details.

## Self-Check

Before treating source work as complete, verify:

- The role of each source is clear: witness, participant, expert, affected person, official, critic, or document custodian.
- Interview ground rules were defined before sensitive information was shared.
- Any anonymous source has a documented reason for anonymity and enough credibility context for readers.
- The source's access to knowledge was tested with "how do you know" questions.
- Important claims from interviews were corroborated through documents, data, observation, or independent sources when possible.
- Vulnerable sources were told the realistic publication consequences and were not pressured for unnecessary private details.
- Subjects of criticism received a meaningful chance to respond to the substance of the claims.
- Expert sources were matched to the precise topic and checked for conflicts or limits.
- Quotes preserve meaning, context, language, and uncertainty.
- Translation choices are faithful and noted when relevant.
- Notes record interview date, setting, status, and verification follow-ups.
- Source motives were considered without dismissing evidence solely because the source has a motive.
- The story does not rely on access, friendship, outrage, or sympathy in place of verification.
- No promises were made that the journalist cannot keep.
