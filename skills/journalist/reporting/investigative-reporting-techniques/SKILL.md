# Investigative Reporting Techniques

Investigative reporting is the systematic exposure of matters that powerful people or institutions would prefer to keep hidden. It is not merely aggressive daily reporting. It is a sustained, evidence-led process in which the reporter gathers documents, data, and testimony over weeks or months, tests hypotheses against contrary evidence, and publishes only when the account is defensible. The central judgment problem is that investigations are expensive, slow, and easy to get wrong: a reporter can spend enormous effort confirming a narrative that was never quite true, or can be so cautious that hidden harm continues unchecked.

Use this skill when the work goes beyond a single event or official statement and requires uncovering concealed behavior, mapping relationships, building a document-based case, or sustaining reporting across a long arc. The main risk is that an agent treats investigation as confirmation-seeking rather than disciplined inquiry, or that it underestimates the operational, legal, and safety dimensions of exposing concealed conduct.

## Core Rules

### Begin With A Hypothesis, Not A Conclusion

An investigation needs a working hypothesis that is specific enough to be tested and falsified. "The agency is corrupt" is not a hypothesis; it is an impression. "The agency awarded contracts to firms owned by officials' relatives without competitive bidding in 2023" is a hypothesis that documents can confirm or refute. Write the hypothesis down, then list the evidence that would prove it and, critically, the evidence that would disprove it. If you cannot imagine what would change your mind, you are advocating, not investigating.

Revisit the hypothesis as evidence accumulates. A common failure is to keep the original frame after the documents point somewhere else. Let the evidence redirect the inquiry, even if the new direction is less dramatic.

### Build The Document Trail Before The Narrative

Investigations stand or fall on documents. Testimony can be recalled, denied, or distorted; documents, properly authenticated, create a fixed record. Before writing, assemble the paper trail: contracts, filings, audit reports, court records, regulatory correspondence, expense records, meeting minutes, emails, incorporation records, property records, and databases. Organize it so that each central claim maps to a specific document and citation.

Where documents are missing, note the gap explicitly. Absence is itself information: a missing audit, a gap in the meeting minutes, a contract that should exist but cannot be found may indicate concealment, but it may also indicate poor record-keeping. Do not assert destruction of records without proof.

### Map Power, Money, And Relationships

Investigations often turn on connections that are not visible in any single document. Build a relationship map that tracks who is connected to whom through ownership, employment, family, payments, shell companies, boards, partnerships, loans, and political donations. Follow the money: where funds originate, how they move, who benefits, and what is disclosed versus hidden.

Useful tools include corporate registries, procurement databases, land and property records, court filings, lobbying registers, campaign finance records, sanctions lists, leaked datasets, and public asset declarations. Cross-reference these against each other, because a single registry rarely tells the whole story. A company name may change, an official may use a relative's name, or ownership may be layered through jurisdictions specifically to obscure it.

### Triangulate Across Independent Evidence Types

No single source, however compelling, should carry an investigation. Triangulate across at least three independent evidence types: documents, data, named or anonymous human sources with direct knowledge, public records, and physical or digital observation. Independence matters more than count. Three sources who all read the same leaked memo are one source. Three sources who separately observed different parts of the same system are stronger.

For each central claim, record which evidence types support it and which are absent. A claim supported only by anonymous testimony, with no document and no data, is weaker than the narrative may suggest and should be framed accordingly.

### Pursue The Other Side Actively, Not Perfunctorily

Subjects of investigation must be given a genuine, detailed opportunity to respond before publication. A vague request for comment sent hours before deadline is not meaningful engagement. Provide the specific findings, the documents they rest on, and the questions they raise, with enough time for a substantive reply. Document every attempt at contact: dates, times, methods, and content.

A subject's denial, explanation, or additional context can change the story materially. Sometimes the response reveals that the reporter misunderstood a document, that there is a legitimate explanation, or that the conduct was known and authorized. Treat the response as evidence, not as an obstacle. If the subject does not respond, say so plainly rather than implying guilt from silence.

### Maintain An Investigative Timeline And Master File

Long investigations generate material that no one can hold in memory. Maintain a master timeline that records every dated event, document, payment, communication, and statement, with citations. Maintain a master file that tracks every source, every document, every open question, every dead end, and every verification status. Update it continuously. When the team grows or hands off, the timeline and master file are what allow the work to continue without distortion.

The timeline also exposes gaps and contradictions. Two documents with incompatible dates, or a payment that predates the contract it supposedly fulfills, often reveal the most important parts of the story.

### Assess Legal, Ethical, And Safety Exposure Continuously

Investigations carry legal risk (defamation, privacy, breach of confidence, source protection), ethical risk (harm to innocent parties, exposure of private individuals), and safety risk (threats to sources, reporters, or subjects). Identify these exposures early and revisit them as the story develops. Qualified legal review should improve precision of language and attribution, not be a veto over newsworthy reporting.

Safety planning applies to sources as much as reporters. A source who cooperates may face retaliation, dismissal, prosecution, or physical danger. Consider how identifying details, timing, and publication might expose them, and plan protections before, not after, publication.

### Know When The Investigation Is Ready

An investigation is ready to publish when each central claim is supported by evidence adequate to its weight, the subjects have responded or had fair opportunity to do so, the remaining uncertainties are named rather than hidden, and the public-interest justification outweighs foreseeable harm. It is not ready when it merely feels complete or when competitive pressure mounts. If the strongest claims cannot be supported, narrow the story to what the evidence sustains rather than inflating weak material to match the original ambition.

## Common Traps

### Confirmation Bias Disguised As Tenacity

The hardest-working reporters can become the most biased, because they have invested so much that abandoning a weak hypothesis feels like failure. Build in deliberate disconfirmation: assign someone to argue the strongest version of the subject's defense.

### Falling In Love With The Target

Investigations can become personal. A reporter who comes to dislike or distrust the subject may stop testing evidence and start accumulating grievance. Step back periodically and ask whether the evidence would lead a neutral observer to the same place.

### Over-Relying On A Single Disgruntled Source

A fired employee, a former business partner, or a political rival can provide a genuine lead and a distorted frame. Their documents may be real and their interpretation selective. Verify their material independently and probe their motives openly.

### Treating Leaked Material As Settled Truth

Leaked documents are authentic records of something, but they may be incomplete, cherry-picked, or part of a coordinated disclosure. Establish what the leak shows, what it omits, and who benefits from its release before building a case on it.

### Confusing Pattern With Proof

A pattern of suspicious-looking transactions may indicate corruption, or it may reflect ordinary practice in an industry the reporter does not understand. Test the pattern against a control: how do comparable institutions behave? Without a baseline, pattern is innuendo.

### Burning Sources Through Careless Detail

A specific phrase, a unique document, or a precise date in the published story can identify an internal source even when unnamed. Review every detail from the perspective of someone inside the institution trying to locate the leak.

### Publishing Under Competitive Pressure

When a competitor is about to break the story, the temptation to rush is intense. A rushed investigation produces errors that can discredit years of work. If the evidence is not ready, publish what is defensible and continue reporting, rather than overreaching.

## Self-Check

Before treating the investigation as ready, verify:

- The central hypothesis was written down, tested against disconfirming evidence, and revised or abandoned when evidence required it.
- Each material claim maps to specific authenticated documents, data, or testimony, and gaps are named rather than papered over.
- A relationship and money map traces ownership, payments, and connections, with sources cited for each link.
- Central claims are triangulated across genuinely independent evidence types, not merely repeated from a common origin.
- Subjects received a detailed, specific, and timely opportunity to respond, and their replies were treated as evidence that could change the story.
- A master timeline and source file exist and are current enough that another reporter could continue the work without distortion.
- Legal, ethical, and safety exposures were identified early and revisited, with qualified review sought where stakes require it.
- The story is narrowed to what the evidence sustains rather than inflated to match the original ambition or competitive pressure.
- Identifying details were reviewed for the risk they pose to sources, and protections are in place before publication.
- Uncertainty is stated plainly, and the public-interest justification is explicit and stronger than foreseeable harm.
