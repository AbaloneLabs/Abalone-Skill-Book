# Reporting And Source Verification

Journalistic verification is the discipline of deciding what can be responsibly reported, what must be attributed, what must be held, and what remains unknown. The task is not to collect material that supports a preferred story. The task is to build a defensible account from evidence, source knowledge, direct observation, documents, data, and careful uncertainty.

Use this skill before reporting a factual claim, summarizing an allegation, relying on a document, using user-generated media, writing a fact-check, covering breaking news, or turning interviews and online material into a publishable story. The main risk is that an agent may sound confident because the available material is fluent, recent, emotionally compelling, or repeated by many outlets. Repetition is not verification.

## Core Rules

### Separate The Claim From The Evidence

Break the assignment into individual claims before judging the story as a whole. A story may be directionally true while containing unsupported numbers, misleading timelines, weak attributions, or overstated causation.

For each important claim, identify:

- who is making the claim;
- what exact fact is being asserted;
- whether it is direct observation, interpretation, allegation, estimate, or prediction;
- what evidence supports it;
- what evidence contradicts or qualifies it;
- whether the evidence is primary, secondary, or hearsay;
- what would change the conclusion.

Do not write "reports say" when the actual basis is one outlet, one unnamed official, a social media post, or a document the agent has not examined. Do not let a broad source label hide a narrow evidence base.

### Prefer Original Sources, But Do Not Worship Them

Original sources are usually better than summaries, but they are not automatically true. A court filing contains allegations. A police statement contains an institutional account. A company report may be selective. A government dataset may have collection gaps. A scientific preprint may not have peer review. An eyewitness may be sincere and wrong.

Use original sources to reduce distortion, then ask how the source was created, what incentives shaped it, what it omits, and what independent evidence can test it. If the story depends on a document, read the relevant sections directly. Check dates, signatures, issuing body, version history, metadata when available, and whether later updates changed the document.

### Corroborate Across Different Kinds Of Evidence

Two sources with the same origin are not two independent confirmations. A wire story, local article, blog post, and social post may all trace to one press release. A claim repeated by several commentators may still be one rumor.

Prefer corroboration across different evidence types:

- direct observation;
- documents;
- named expert analysis;
- data;
- public records;
- on-the-record interviews;
- physical evidence;
- photos or video with location and time checks;
- prior reporting with transparent sourcing.

When sources are not independent, say so in the reporting notes and avoid presenting the information as settled.

### Track Attribution Precisely

Attribute information at the level of certainty it deserves. Facts that are directly documented can be stated more firmly than claims made by interested parties. Allegations, estimates, interpretations, and predictions need visible attribution.

Use attribution to help the audience judge reliability. "A person familiar with the matter" is weak unless the story also explains why the person has knowledge and why anonymity is justified. "According to a company spokesperson" tells the audience more than "the company said" when the source role matters.

Never use passive construction to hide responsibility. "Mistakes were made," "it was alleged," and "concerns were raised" may conceal who did what, who said what, and how the reporter knows.

### Treat Anonymous Sources As A Last Resort

Anonymous sourcing can protect vulnerable people and expose important information, but it also weakens audience trust and can be abused by powerful actors. Use it only when the information is newsworthy, factual rather than opinion or speculation, unavailable on the record, and provided by someone with direct knowledge.

Before relying on anonymity, ask:

- What harm could the source face if named?
- Is the source using anonymity to attack others without accountability?
- Does the source have direct access to the information?
- Can the information be confirmed elsewhere?
- Can any portion be moved on the record?
- Can the story describe the source enough for readers to judge credibility without exposing identity?
- Has an editor or responsible reviewer been told the source identity?

Do not grant anonymity casually at the end of an interview. Set ground rules before sensitive information is shared.

### Verify Digital Material Before Repeating It

Images, videos, screenshots, social posts, audio clips, and data visualizations require verification before use. A viral item can be old, staged, edited, translated badly, generated, taken from another event, or framed with false context.

Check:

- original upload or earliest known appearance;
- account history and possible impersonation;
- location clues;
- weather, shadows, landmarks, language, uniforms, license plates, or signage;
- timestamp and time zone;
- whether the same media appeared in older events;
- metadata when available, without over-relying on it;
- whether independent witnesses or records place the event there;
- whether editing altered meaning.

If verification is incomplete but the material is still newsworthy, state what is known, what is not known, and why the material is being discussed.

### Distinguish Speed From Certainty

Breaking news rewards speed, but speed does not excuse inaccuracy. In fast-moving events, publish narrower facts rather than broader guesses. Say "the agency reported an explosion" if that is what is known. Do not say "attack," "accident," "terrorism," "mass casualty," or "confirmed dead" unless the evidence supports those terms.

Keep a running uncertainty log:

- confirmed facts;
- claims under review;
- disputed details;
- source conflicts;
- terms to avoid until confirmed;
- updates needed after official statements, records, or field reporting.

When updating a story, correct the public record rather than silently replacing wrong material.

### Verify Numbers, Dates, Names, And Context

Small factual errors can undermine the entire story. Check names, titles, pronouns, ages, locations, dates, court names, agency names, measurements, currency, percentages, denominators, time spans, and comparisons. A percentage increase without a base number may mislead. A "record high" needs a period and dataset. A "first" needs careful historical checking.

For statistics, ask:

- Who collected the data?
- What is counted and excluded?
- What period is covered?
- Is the data preliminary, revised, sampled, modeled, or complete?
- What denominator makes the number meaningful?
- Are there reporting gaps or jurisdiction differences?
- Does the visual presentation distort the result?

### Preserve Reporting Notes

Maintain notes that would allow another responsible editor or reporter to understand the basis of the story. Keep source names, interview dates, documents consulted, URLs, archived copies when appropriate, data transformations, verification steps, and unresolved questions.

Do not store sensitive source details in insecure locations. Protect raw notes when they could expose vulnerable sources, private individuals, whistleblowers, victims, minors, or people in unsafe political contexts.

## Common Traps

### Mistaking Consensus For Verification

Many outlets repeating the same claim can create false confidence. Trace the claim backward. If the entire chain rests on one anonymous official, one press release, one translated post, or one unreviewed dataset, the story should say that or wait.

### Treating Official Statements As Neutral Fact

Officials can be accurate, mistaken, incomplete, self-protective, or strategic. Police, prosecutors, companies, campaigns, agencies, schools, hospitals, and courts each speak from a role. Report what they say, but verify independently when possible and include the status of the claim.

### Overcorrecting Into False Balance

Verification does not require giving equal weight to a weak denial, bad-faith claim, or unsupported fringe view. Fairness means representing material evidence and meaningful disagreement, not manufacturing symmetry between a documented fact and a baseless counterclaim.

### Letting Language Outrun Evidence

Words like "proved," "admitted," "slammed," "refused," "secret," "corrupt," "chaos," "miracle," "crisis," and "cover-up" can smuggle interpretation into facts. Use precise verbs. If a person did not answer, say they did not respond by deadline, not that they refused, unless refusal is known.

### Confusing Publication Permission With Ethical Use

Public availability does not make material ethically safe to use. A public social post may expose a private person to harassment. A legally accessible document may contain unnecessary personal details. A graphic image may be newsworthy but still require careful framing, warnings, cropping, or non-use.

### Ignoring What Would Disprove The Story

If the reporting plan only seeks confirmation, it will miss serious errors. Identify the strongest contrary evidence and actively look for it. Ask knowledgeable sources what would make the current hypothesis wrong.

## Self-Check

Before treating the reporting as complete, verify:

- Every central claim has a named evidence basis or a clear reason for anonymity.
- The story distinguishes observed facts, documented facts, allegations, estimates, interpretations, and predictions.
- Original documents, data, or recordings were checked directly when the story depends on them.
- Repeated claims were traced to their original source.
- At least one meaningful attempt was made to corroborate major claims through independent evidence.
- Anonymous sourcing is justified by news value, source knowledge, lack of on-record alternatives, and risk of harm or retaliation.
- People or institutions facing criticism were given a fair opportunity to respond when practicable; digital media was checked for origin, time, location, authenticity, and context before use
- Numbers, names, dates, titles, places, and quotes were checked against primary records or notes; the story says what remains unknown instead of filling gaps with confident language
- The wording does not imply causation, intent, guilt, motive, or certainty beyond the evidence; sensitive details are included only when they serve a public-interest purpose
- Notes preserve the verification path without exposing protected sources or private information unnecessarily; any update or correction plan is ready if new facts change the story
