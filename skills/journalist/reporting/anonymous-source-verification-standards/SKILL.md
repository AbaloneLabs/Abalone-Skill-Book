# Anonymous Source Verification Standards

Anonymity is one of the most powerful and most corrosive tools in journalism. It unlocks information the public needs but could never obtain on the record, from whistleblowers exposing corruption to officials revealing policy decisions, and it simultaneously removes the accountability that lets readers judge the source's credibility, motives, and track record. The judgment problem is that anonymity is granted too casually and too often, used as a convenience for sources who simply prefer not to be named rather than as a protection for those who face real risk, and each casual grant erodes the credibility that makes anonymous reporting valuable. The standards for verifying anonymous information must therefore be higher, not lower, than for on-the-record reporting, because the reader is being asked to trust the journalist's unseen sourcing, and that trust is finite and shared across the whole profession. The skill is granting anonymity only when justified, verifying anonymous claims with rigor that compensates for the absent public accountability, and being honest with readers about what they can and cannot assess.

The harm this skill prevents is the credibility collapse that careless anonymous sourcing produces, both for individual stories and for the profession. A story built on a single anonymous source making an uncorroborated claim can be wrong, manipulated, or self-serving, and when it collapses, it damages not just the journalist but every reporter who later relies on anonymity for legitimate reporting. Worse, anonymity is often weaponized by officials to float trial balloons, attack rivals, or shape narratives without accountability, turning journalism into a conduit for unattributed spin. The agent must help the journalist apply a strict justification test before granting anonymity, require corroboration that matches the claim's significance, document the source's reliability and motives, and disclose to readers enough about the source's position and reasons for anonymity to let them judge the claim.

Use this skill when a source requests anonymity, when verifying anonymous information, when deciding whether to publish an anonymous claim, when applying multi-source rules, or when writing disclosures about anonymous sourcing. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating anonymity as a default convenience and to ensure anonymous reporting meets verification standards that earn the reader's trust.

## Core Rules

### Apply A Strict Justification Test Before Granting Anonymity

Anonymity should be the exception, granted for specific, defensible reasons, not the default for any source who asks. The justification test ensures anonymity serves the public interest rather than the source's comfort or the journalist's convenience.

Grant anonymity only when:

- the source faces real danger, such as loss of job, legal action, or physical harm, if named;
- the information cannot be obtained on the record by any other means;
- the source has direct, first-hand knowledge, not hearsay or opinion;
- the public interest in the information clearly outweighs the accountability cost of anonymity;
- no on-the-record source can provide the same information.

Refuse anonymity when the source simply prefers it, when the information is available on the record, when the source is offering opinion rather than fact, or when anonymity would let the source attack others without accountability. The default is on the record; anonymity must be earned.

### Require Corroboration That Matches The Claim's Significance

An anonymous claim carries less inherent credibility than an on-the-record one, because the reader cannot assess the source. The verification standard must compensate by requiring corroboration proportionate to the claim's significance and potential harm.

Match corroboration to significance:

- for routine, low-stakes claims, a second independent source may suffice;
- for significant allegations against individuals or institutions, require multiple independent sources plus documentary evidence where possible;
- for explosive or career-damaging claims, require the highest standard, multiple sources with direct knowledge and corroborating documents;
- for claims that could not be made on the record even with protection, require especially rigorous corroboration given the source's hidden motives;
- never publish a significant anonymous claim from a single source without extraordinary justification.

The more serious the claim and the more hidden the source, the higher the corroboration bar. A single anonymous source is rarely sufficient for a claim that could harm a person's reputation or career.

### Document The Source's Reliability, Access, And Motives

The journalist must understand the source far better than the reader can, because the journalist is vouching for the source. Documenting the source's reliability, access to the information, and motives for speaking allows the journalist to assess whether the information is trustworthy and whether the source has an agenda that colors it.

Document by recording:

- how the source knows the information, first-hand, second-hand, or inferred;
- the source's track record, have they been reliable in the past;
- the source's motive for speaking, including any personal stake in the story's outcome;
- whether the source has an axe to grind against the subject;
- whether the source is authorized to share the information or is leaking;
- any promises made to the source in exchange for the information.

A source with direct knowledge and a track record of reliability is far more credible than a source with second-hand information and a personal grievance. The journalist must assess and weigh these factors, not just relay the claim.

### Give Readers Enough To Judge The Source

Readers cannot evaluate an anonymous source they cannot see, so the journalist must disclose enough about the source's position, knowledge, and reasons for anonymity to let readers assess the claim's credibility. Vague sourcing like "sources said" gives readers nothing; specific, descriptive sourcing gives them the tools to judge.

Disclose to readers:

- the source's relationship to the story, such as "a senior official directly involved in the discussions";
- the source's basis for knowledge, such as "who reviewed the documents";
- the reason for anonymity, such as "who was not authorized to speak publicly";
- the number of sources and whether they are independent;
- any conflicting accounts or limitations on the source's knowledge.

The goal is maximum disclosure consistent with protecting the source. A reader should be able to form a judgment about whether the source is in a position to know and whether they have a reason to mislead, even without knowing the name.

### Distinguish Whistleblowing From Spin

Anonymity protects genuine whistleblowers who expose wrongdoing at personal risk, and it also shields officials who use anonymity to float trial balloons, attack rivals, or shape narratives without accountability. The two uses look similar on the page but have opposite relationships to the public interest, and the journalist must distinguish them.

Distinguish by asking:

- is the source exposing wrongdoing for the public benefit, or advancing an agenda for private benefit;
- is the information being shared because it is hidden from the public, or because the source wants it in the public eye without attribution;
- is the source taking a personal risk, or using anonymity as a communications strategy;
- does the source have a stake in how the story affects a rival, policy, or institution;
- is the journalist being used as a conduit for an official's messaging.

Genuine whistleblowing deserves protection and rigorous verification. Official spin deserves skepticism and, often, refusal, because publishing it makes the journalist a tool of the source's agenda rather than an independent reporter.

### Apply The Right Of Reply To Anonymous Allegations

Even when a source is anonymous, the subject of the allegation has the right to respond before publication. This is both an ethical obligation and a verification step, because the subject's response may confirm, deny, or add context that changes the story.

Apply right of reply by:

- contacting the subject with the specific allegations before publication;
- providing enough detail for a meaningful response, while protecting source identity;
- giving adequate time to respond, proportionate to the story's urgency;
- reporting the response fully, including denials and explanations;
- not publishing anonymous allegations without giving the subject a chance to respond, except in extraordinary circumstances.

The right of reply protects against error and unfairness, and it often produces information that strengthens or qualifies the story. Skipping it to protect a scoop is a serious ethical failure.

### Maintain Records And Be Prepared To Justify The Decision

The decision to grant anonymity and publish anonymous claims must be defensible after publication, especially if the story is challenged. Maintain records of the sourcing, the verification, and the justification, so the decision can be reviewed and explained.

Maintain by:

- keeping detailed notes on each source's identity, knowledge, motive, and corroboration;
- documenting the justification for anonymity and the editor's approval;
- recording the verification steps taken and the evidence obtained;
- being prepared to explain, to editors and to the public, why anonymity was necessary and why the sourcing was sufficient;
- reviewing anonymous sourcing decisions periodically to maintain standards.

Anonymity decisions that cannot be justified after the fact should not have been made. The discipline of documentation keeps the standards honest.

## Common Traps

### Granting Anonymity As A Convenience

Allowing sources to be anonymous because they prefer it or because it is easier than negotiating on-the-record. The trap is eroding the default of accountability.

### Single-Source Significant Claims

Publishing serious allegations from one anonymous source without corroboration. The trap is unverified claims presented as fact.

### Vague Sourcing

Using "sources said" without describing the source's position, knowledge, or reason for anonymity. The trap is asking readers to trust without giving them the basis to judge.

### Conduit For Official Spin

Publishing anonymous claims that advance an official's agenda without recognizing the use. The trap is journalism as a tool of the source.

### Ignoring Source Motives

Failing to assess whether the source has a grievance, stake, or agenda that colors the information. The trap is treating all sources as equally credible.

### Skipping Right Of Reply

Publishing anonymous allegations without contacting the subject. The trap is unfairness and preventable error.

### Anonymity For Opinions Or Analysis

Granting anonymity to sources offering opinion or interpretation rather than first-hand fact. The trap is unaccountable commentary presented as reporting.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Anonymity was granted only after a strict justification test showing real risk, no on-the-record alternative, and clear public interest.
- [ ] Corroboration was required proportionate to the claim's significance, with multiple independent sources and documents for serious allegations.
- [ ] The source's reliability, access to the information, and motives were documented and assessed, including any personal stake or grievance.
- [ ] Readers were given enough description of the source's position, knowledge, and reason for anonymity to judge the claim's credibility.
- [ ] Genuine whistleblowing was distinguished from official spin, and spin was refused or treated with appropriate skepticism.
- [ ] The subject of the allegations was contacted for right of reply before publication, with adequate time and detail.
- [ ] Records of sourcing, verification, and justification were maintained and the decision is defensible if challenged.
- [ ] No significant anonymous claim was published from a single source without extraordinary, documented justification.
- [ ] No anonymity was granted for opinion or analysis rather than first-hand fact.
- [ ] The story discloses its limitations honestly, including what could and could not be independently verified.
