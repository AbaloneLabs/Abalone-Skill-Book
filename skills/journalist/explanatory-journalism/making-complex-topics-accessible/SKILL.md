# Making Complex Topics Accessible

Explanatory journalism takes subjects that are genuinely complicated, policy, science, economics, law, technology, and makes them understandable to readers who lack specialist background, without sacrificing accuracy. The harm this skill prevents is the two opposite failures of explanation: the piece that dumbs down, simplifying so aggressively that it misleads, and the piece that fails to explain, retaining the jargon and assumptions of specialists and leaving general readers lost. Both failures leave the public uninformed about decisions and developments that affect their lives. Strong explanation respects both the reader's intelligence and the subject's complexity, finding the level of simplification that illuminates without distorting.

Use this skill when writing any explainer, background piece, or coverage of a complex topic for a general audience. Consult editors on framing, and where technical accuracy is central, consider expert review before publication to catch simplifications that mislead.

## Core Rules

### Know the subject deeply before simplifying
You cannot explain something clearly that you do not understand deeply. Before writing for general readers, master the subject at a level beyond what you will present, so that your simplifications are chosen rather than forced by your own confusion. Read the primary sources, consult experts, and identify the essential mechanisms, tradeoffs, and uncertainties. Simplification grounded in deep understanding preserves accuracy; simplification grounded in shallow understanding produces distortion. If you cannot explain why the simple version is a simplification, you do not yet understand enough to write it.

### Identify the essential and shed the incidental
Complexity often comes from detail that is incidental to the core mechanism. Distinguish what readers must understand to grasp the subject from what is interesting but non-essential. Build the explanation around the essential, and shed or defer the incidental. Over-inclusion, attempting to convey every qualification and exception, overwhelms readers; ruthless selection of what matters most is the core explanatory skill. You can always link to deeper detail for readers who want more.

### Choose the right level for your actual audience
Accessibility is relative to the reader. An explainer for a general news audience assumes less background than one for an educated lay audience or a specialist adjacent audience. Identify your actual audience and calibrate the level accordingly: define terms they will not know, supply context they will lack, and avoid belaboring what they already understand. Miscalibration in either direction, too basic or too advanced, loses readers. When in doubt, err slightly toward accessibility, since specialists can skim but novices cannot fill gaps.

### Use analogy carefully, and disclose its limits
Analogies make abstract concepts concrete by mapping them to familiar ones, and they are powerful explanatory tools. But every analogy breaks down at some point, and a misleading analogy can implant a wrong mental model that is hard to correct. Choose analogies where the mapping holds for the essential mechanism, and flag where the analogy breaks down: "this is like X in that..., though unlike X, it also..." An analogy that oversimplifies or that maps onto the wrong feature misleads readers about how the system actually works.

### Define jargon or replace it, never just use it
Specialist terms carry precise meanings that insiders rely on, but they exclude general readers. For each piece of jargon, decide whether to define it, where the term is essential and precision matters, or replace it with plain language, where the precision is not necessary for understanding. Never use jargon undefined assuming readers will know it or look it up; each undefined term is a point where readers disengage. When you define, define at first use, briefly, and in terms the reader already understands.

### Show mechanisms, not just conclusions
Explanations that state what happens without showing why leave readers with conclusions they cannot evaluate or apply. Show the mechanism: how the policy produces its effects, how the scientific process works, why the economic incentive operates as it does. Readers who understand the mechanism can reason about new cases; readers who memorize only the conclusion cannot. Mechanism-level explanation is more work to produce but vastly more durable for the reader.

### Make uncertainty and contestation visible
Complex topics are often subjects of genuine expert disagreement, evolving evidence, or unresolved questions. Explanations that present a contested topic as settled, or an evolving one as final, mislead readers about the state of knowledge. Where experts disagree, say so and briefly characterize the disagreement. Where evidence is preliminary, label it. Where the answer is genuinely unknown, say unknown rather than forcing false certainty. Honest uncertainty is more credible than false clarity.

### Use structure and visuals to manage complexity
Long explanatory prose can overwhelm; structure helps readers navigate. Use headings, signposting, step-by-step organization, and summaries to break complexity into manageable pieces. Visuals, diagrams, charts, and timelines can convey relationships and processes more efficiently than prose. Structure and visuals are not decoration; they are explanatory tools that reduce cognitive load and help readers build understanding incrementally.

## Common Traps

### Simplification that misleads
Reducing complexity so aggressively that the simple version is wrong implants mental models that are hard to correct. The trap is that the simple version is clear and memorable, but it distorts the underlying reality.

### Jargon retained without translation
Specialist terms that exclude general readers signal that the writer has not done the translation work. The trap is that the terms feel precise and authoritative, but they lose the audience.

### Analogy that maps the wrong feature
An analogy that clarifies one aspect while misrepresenting another leaves readers with a wrong model. The trap is that the analogy feels illuminating, but it misleads where the mapping breaks.

### Conclusion without mechanism
Stating what happens without showing why leaves readers unable to reason about the topic. The trap is that conclusions are easier to write than mechanisms, but they are less useful.

### False certainty on contested topics
Presenting evolving or disputed knowledge as settled misleads readers about the state of the field. The trap is that certainty reads as authority, but it overstates what is known.

### Over-inclusion of incidental detail
Attempting to convey every qualification overwhelms readers and obscures the essential. The trap is that completeness feels thorough, but it defeats comprehension.

### Miscalibrated audience level
Writing too basic for the actual audience bores them; writing too advanced loses them. The trap is that the writer's own familiarity makes calibration hard to judge.

## Self-Check

- Do you understand the subject deeply enough that your simplifications are chosen rather than forced by confusion?
- Have you distinguished essential from incidental detail, building the explanation around what readers must grasp?
- Is the level calibrated to your actual audience, defining terms they lack and avoiding belaboring what they know?
- Are analogies chosen where the mapping holds, with their limits disclosed where they break down?
- Is jargon either defined at first use or replaced with plain language, with no undefined specialist terms assumed?
- Does the explanation show mechanisms, not just conclusions, so readers can reason about the topic?
- Is uncertainty and expert contestation made visible rather than smoothed into false certainty?
- Do structure and visuals manage cognitive load, and have editors reviewed framing and, where technical accuracy is central, has expert review caught simplifications that mislead?
