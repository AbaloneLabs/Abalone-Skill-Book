# Expert Sourcing In Explanatory Journalism

Explanatory journalism depends heavily on experts: scientists, economists, lawyers, doctors, policy analysts, and practitioners who help readers understand complex subjects. How a journalist selects, represents, and weighs experts determines whether the explanation reflects the actual state of knowledge or distorts it. The harm this skill prevents is the misrepresentation of expert consensus: elevating a fringe view to equal standing with the mainstream, treating a contested debate as settled, presenting an interested party as a neutral expert, or selecting experts whose credentials do not match the question. Weak expert sourcing leaves readers with a false picture of what is known, which is especially dangerous in science, health, economics, and policy where misunderstanding has consequences.

Use this skill when selecting experts for any explanatory piece, representing the state of a field, and weighing competing expert claims. Consult editors on expert selection for high-stakes topics, and where the subject is contested, seek independent methodological review of how consensus is characterized.

## Core Rules

### Represent the actual distribution of expert views
The journalist's job is to convey where the weight of expert opinion lies, not to manufacture symmetry. If a strong consensus exists, say so and represent it, while noting any meaningful minority views and their basis. If the field is genuinely divided, characterize the division honestly: how the views split, what the disagreements are about, and what the different camps rely on. False balance, giving a fringe view equal standing with a consensus, misleads readers about the state of knowledge. So does the opposite, presenting a live debate as settled.

### Match the expert's credentials to the specific question
Expertise is narrow, not general. A Nobel-winning physicist is not necessarily an expert on climate policy; a prominent surgeon is not necessarily an expert on health-system economics; a legal scholar of one field is not necessarily an expert on another. Match each expert's specific credentials to the specific question being asked, and be wary of experts opining far from their actual specialty. The most credentialed expert in the wrong field is less reliable than a less famous expert in the right one. State the expert's relevant expertise, not just their general eminence.

### Identify and weigh conflicts of interest
Experts often have financial, institutional, ideological, or professional interests that shape their views. A researcher funded by an industry may still produce sound work, but the funding is relevant to how readers weigh it. A think-tank scholar's institutional orientation, a practitioner's commercial interests, a partisan affiliation, and a professional stake in a particular position all bear on credibility. Identify material conflicts, disclose them to readers, and weigh conflicted sources accordingly. Do not present an interested party as a neutral authority without disclosure.

### Distinguish consensus, majority, minority, and fringe
These are different states of expert opinion and should be represented differently. A consensus is the overwhelming view of the relevant expert community; a majority view is the most common but contested position; a minority view is a serious but less widely held position with real evidentiary support; a fringe view lacks mainstream standing. Represent each at the level the evidence and the expert community support. Treating a fringe view as a minority, a minority as a majority, or a majority as a consensus misleads readers about how settled the question is.

### Seek independent experts, not only interested parties
Interested parties, industry scientists, government officials, advocates, and practitioners with a stake, can provide valuable information, but their framing reflects their interests. Balance them with independent experts: academic researchers, professional society positions, government scientific bodies, and methodologists without a direct stake. Independent experts help test whether the interested parties' claims hold up against the broader field's understanding. A piece built only on interested-party experts presents a partial picture as the field's view.

### Characterize the basis of expert claims, not just the claims
Readers can evaluate expert views better when they understand the basis: what evidence, what kind of study, what theoretical framework, what professional experience supports the claim. "Experts say X" is less informative than "a meta-analysis of dozens of studies found X" or "the professional society's guideline, based on clinical evidence, recommends X." Characterizing the basis lets readers weigh the claim and distinguishes well-supported views from expert assertion.

### Represent uncertainty and evolving knowledge honestly
Many fields are in active development, with evidence accumulating, positions revising, and yesterday's consensus sometimes overturned. Represent this honestly: where knowledge is solid, say so; where it is evolving, say so; where today's confident claim may revise, signal that. Presenting evolving knowledge as final misleads readers who will be confused when the picture changes; presenting solid knowledge as tentative deprives readers of warranted confidence. Match the characterization of certainty to the actual state of the field.

### Avoid the rotation of the same narrow expert pool
Newsrooms often return to a small pool of quotable experts who are accessible, articulate, and media-trained. This narrows the range of perspectives represented and overweights the views of those skilled at media engagement rather than those most authoritative. Deliberately diversify the expert pool: seek experts of different backgrounds, institutions, methodological approaches, and demographic groups. A broader pool produces more representative and often more accurate explanation.

## Common Traps

### False balance
Giving a fringe or unsupported view equal standing with a consensus misleads readers about the state of knowledge. The trap is that symmetry feels fair, but it misrepresents where the weight of evidence lies.

### Credentials mismatch
Citing eminent experts opining outside their actual specialty lends false authority to views the expert is not well-positioned to evaluate. The trap is that fame reads as expertise, but specialty matters.

### Undisclosed conflicts
Presenting interested parties as neutral experts lets their framing pass without scrutiny. The trap is that the expert sounds authoritative, but the conflict shapes the view.

### Consensus inflation or deflation
Treating a live debate as settled or a settled question as open misleads readers about certainty. The trap is that inflation reads as confidence and deflation reads as caution, but both misrepresent the field.

### Interested-party-only sourcing
Building explanation only on experts with a stake presents a partial picture as the field's view. The trap is that interested parties are accessible and articulate, but independence matters.

### Claim without basis
Stating what experts conclude without characterizing the evidence leaves readers unable to evaluate the claim. The trap is that the assertion reads as authoritative, but the basis is invisible.

### Over-reliance on a narrow media-trained pool
Returning to the same quotable experts narrows perspective and overweights engagement skill over authority. The trap is that familiar experts are convenient, but they under-represent the field.

## Self-Check

- Does the piece represent the actual distribution of expert views, conveying where the weight of opinion lies without manufacturing false symmetry?
- Is each expert's specific credentials matched to the specific question, with relevant expertise stated rather than general eminence?
- Are material conflicts of interest identified, disclosed to readers, and weighed in credibility assessment?
- Are consensus, majority, minority, and fringe views each represented at the level the evidence and expert community support?
- Are independent experts sought to balance interested parties, so the field's broader understanding tests interested-party claims?
- Is the basis of expert claims, the evidence, study type, or framework, characterized rather than only the conclusions asserted?
- Is uncertainty and evolving knowledge represented honestly, with certainty matched to the actual state of the field?
- Has the expert pool been diversified beyond a narrow media-trained rotation, and have editors reviewed expert selection for high-stakes topics where consensus characterization matters?
