# Platform Selection and Audience Fit

Every platform a publication chooses to be on is a commitment: to produce for that platform's format, to serve its audience, to absorb its risks, and to maintain standards in an environment the publication does not control. No publication can do every platform well, and the attempt dilutes effort and quality across channels none of which are served properly. The judgment problem is that platform choices are often made reactively, chasing where audiences seem to be, without assessing whether the platform fits the journalism, whether the publication can sustain quality there, and what the risks of dependence or compromise are. This skill covers how to select platforms deliberately, assess audience fit, weigh reach against risk, and build a platform portfolio that serves the mission without overextending or eroding standards.

## Core Rules

### Choose Platforms to Serve the Mission and Audience, Not the Trend

Platform decisions should start from the publication's mission and audience, not from platform hype. Ask: where is the audience we are trying to serve, and where can we reach them with journalism that meets our standards? A platform that reaches a large but irrelevant audience, or that requires formats that compromise the journalism, may not be worth the investment. A platform that reaches a smaller but highly relevant audience may be valuable. Resist the pressure to be on every new platform; each platform is a real ongoing commitment of production capacity, and spreading thin produces poor work everywhere. Choose deliberately based on mission and audience fit.

### Assess Platform Characteristics Honestly

Each platform has characteristics that shape what journalism can be there: format constraints (character limits, video-first demands, ephemeral content), audience expectations and norms, algorithmic dynamics that determine reach, monetization models, content rules and moderation practices, and risks (takedowns, suppression, manipulation, data exposure). Assess these honestly before committing. A platform whose algorithm rewards sensationalism will pressure the journalism toward sensationalism; a platform with aggressive takedown rules may suppress legitimate documentation; a platform whose audience expects brevity may not suit deep journalism. Know what you are signing up for, and decide whether you can maintain standards within it.

### Match Content Form to Platform, Without Distorting the Journalism

Producing for a platform means adapting content to its form: short posts for one, video for another, audio for a third. Adapt the form to serve the platform's audience, but do not let the adaptation distort the journalism. A story that cannot be told accurately in a platform's format should not be forced into it; link without making the claim, save it for a format that fits, or find a way to adapt honestly. The platform's format constraints are real, but they do not override accuracy, attribution, or fairness. Adapt form; do not distort substance.

### Weigh Reach Against Risk and Dependence

Large platforms offer reach, but reach comes with risks: dependence on a platform that can change rules, suppress reach, or deplatform you; exposure to manipulation and harassment; vulnerability to algorithm changes that can collapse traffic overnight; and data and privacy exposure. Weigh reach against these risks. Diversify so that no single platform is a single point of failure for audience or revenue. Invest in direct channels (newsletters, apps, owned properties) as a hedge against platform dependence. Reach is valuable, but reach you do not control and cannot rely on is fragile.

### Commit Only to Platforms You Can Sustain at Quality

A platform presence done poorly is worse than no presence, because it signals that the publication does not take the channel or its audience seriously. Before committing to a platform, assess whether you can sustain quality production there over time: do you have the staff, skills, and capacity to produce for this platform consistently at your standards? If not, do not commit, or commit at a scope you can sustain. It is better to be excellent on two platforms than mediocre on six. Sustainable quality on fewer channels outperforms unsustainable spread.

### Maintain Consistent Standards Across the Portfolio

Standards do not change by platform. The accuracy, attribution, fairness, independence, and transparency the publication upholds on its site apply on every platform, even those with looser norms. A common failure is to treat certain platforms as informal spaces where standards relax, which produces errors, reputational damage, and erosion of the brand's credibility. Define the standards clearly, train staff on them, and apply them consistently across the portfolio. The platform's norms do not set your standards; your mission does.

### Reassess Platforms Periodically and Exit Deliberately

Platforms change: audiences migrate, algorithms shift, rules tighten, risks grow, and new platforms emerge. Reassess the platform portfolio periodically against mission, audience, and risk. Be willing to exit platforms that no longer serve the audience or that have become too risky, and exit deliberately, with communication to the audience about where to find you. Clinging to a declining or hostile platform out of sunk-cost loyalty wastes capacity. Treat the portfolio as dynamic, not fixed.

### Protect Staff and Audience on Every Platform

Each platform carries specific risks to staff (harassment, doxxing, legal threat) and audience (misinformation, data exposure, manipulation). Assess and address these for every platform in the portfolio: provide staff with safety support and protocols, moderate and protect audience spaces where you control them, and be honest with the audience about platform risks. Do not expose staff or audience to harm as a cost of platform presence. Safety is part of the platform decision.

## Common Traps

### Chasing Every New Platform Without Assessing Fit

Jumping onto new platforms because they are hyped, without assessing mission and audience fit. This is a trap because each is a real commitment that dilutes capacity. Choose deliberately.

### Overestimating Reach and Underestimating Dependence

Pursuing a large platform's reach without accounting for dependence and fragility. This is a trap because the reach can vanish with an algorithm or rule change. Diversify and invest in direct channels.

### Distorting Journalism to Fit Platform Formats

Forcing stories into formats that compromise accuracy or fairness. This is a trap because format constraints do not override standards. Adapt form without distorting substance.

### Committing to More Platforms Than You Can Sustain

Spreading across many platforms and producing poor work on all. This is a trap because mediocre presence signals you do not take the audience seriously. Commit only to what you can sustain at quality.

### Relaxing Standards on "Informal" Platforms

Treating certain platforms as exempt from editorial standards. This is a trap because looser norms produce errors and erode credibility. Maintain consistent standards across the portfolio.

### Clinging to Declining or Hostile Platforms Out of Sunk Cost

Staying on a platform that no longer serves the audience or has become too risky. This is a trap because sunk-cost loyalty wastes capacity. Reassess and exit deliberately.

### Exposing Staff or Audience to Platform Harm Unaddressed

Treating harassment, doxxing, or manipulation risks as costs of doing business. This is a trap because safety is part of the platform decision. Protect staff and audience on every platform.

## Self-Check

- Are platform choices made deliberately based on mission and audience fit, rather than reactively chasing trends?
- Have you assessed each platform's characteristics, format constraints, algorithmic dynamics, rules, and risks honestly before committing?
- Is content adapted to platform form without distorting the accuracy, attribution, or fairness of the journalism?
- Is reach weighed against dependence and risk, with diversification and direct channels as a hedge?
- Have you committed only to platforms you can sustain at quality, rather than spreading thin across many?
- Are editorial standards consistent across the entire platform portfolio, with no platform treated as exempt?
- Do you periodically reassess the portfolio and exit platforms that no longer serve the audience or have become too risky?
- For platform decisions involving significant safety, legal, or reputational risk, has editorial leadership reviewed the decision, and where exposure is significant, has counsel or appropriate expertise been consulted?
