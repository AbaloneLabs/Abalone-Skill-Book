# Consent And Vulnerable Subjects

Some subjects are especially vulnerable to harm from journalism: children, trauma survivors, people in distress or crisis, those with diminished capacity, and people in positions of dependence or powerlessness. With these subjects, the ordinary standards of consent and minimize-harm are not enough; heightened care is required. The harm this skill prevents is the exploitation of vulnerable people's stories, the obtaining of consent that is not truly free or informed, the publication of details that cause lasting harm to people already suffering, and the lasting damage to lives that cannot easily recover from public exposure. Consent with vulnerable subjects is a discipline of care, capacity-assessment, restraint, and ongoing responsibility.

## Core Rules

### Assess the subject's capacity to give meaningful consent
Consent requires the capacity to understand what is being agreed to, the implications, and the freedom to refuse without consequence. Vulnerable subjects, those in shock, grief, intoxication, dependence, or immaturity, may lack full capacity at the moment. Assess capacity honestly, and where it is diminished, decline, defer, or seek additional safeguards. Formal agreement from someone who cannot truly consent is not consent.

### Ensure consent is genuinely informed and voluntary
Explain, in terms the subject can understand, what the story is about, how they will be portrayed, the reach and permanence of publication, and the potential consequences, including unwanted attention or retaliation. Ensure the subject knows they can decline or withdraw, and that refusal will not be held against them. Consent obtained through pressure, deception, or dependence is not voluntary.

### Recognize and counter the power asymmetry
The journalist holds power: the platform, the framing, the decision to publish. Vulnerable subjects hold little. This asymmetry can produce compliance that looks like consent. Act deliberately to reduce the imbalance, give the subject real choices, and avoid leveraging the subject's dependence, hope, or fear.

### Minimize harm through restraint in what is published
Even with consent, ask whether publication of certain details is necessary and whether the harm it may cause is justified by the public interest. With vulnerable subjects, err toward restraint: withhold identifying details, graphic specifics, or private matters whose publication serves no pressing public need. The fact that a subject agreed does not eliminate the duty to minimize harm.

### Protect children with special care
Children require heightened protection. Obtain parental or guardian consent where appropriate, but also assess the child's own willingness and understanding. Avoid publishing information that could endanger, stigmatize, or follow a child harmfully into adulthood. The child's long-term welfare outweighs the story's immediate value.

### Handle trauma with trauma-aware practice
Trauma survivors may be re-traumatized by retelling their experiences, may have fragmented memory, and may consent in the moment and regret it later. Follow trauma-aware practice: explain the process, allow the subject to control the pace and scope, avoid pressuring for graphic detail, and provide information about support resources. The story is not worth compounding the subject's harm.

### Honor withdrawal and changing decisions
Vulnerable subjects may realize, during or after the process, that they do not want to participate. Respect withdrawal requests to the extent possible, especially before publication. Treating consent as irrevocable, or pressuring a subject who has changed their mind, betrays the trust that made the consent meaningful.

### Maintain responsibility after publication
Vulnerable subjects may face consequences from publication, unwanted attention, retaliation, stigma, or emotional distress, that they did not anticipate. Maintain contact where appropriate, respond to concerns, and connect subjects with support. The duty of care does not end when the story is published.

## Common Traps

### Treating formal agreement as meaningful consent
A signature or a "yes" from someone in shock, dependent, or lacking understanding is not meaningful consent. Assess capacity and voluntariness, not just the form of agreement.

### Leveraging vulnerability
Using a subject's hope, dependence, or moment of crisis to obtain participation exploits the power asymmetry. The subject's vulnerability should trigger greater care, not greater access.

### Publishing harmful detail merely because consent was given
Consent permits; it does not require. Even with consent, publishing details that cause unjustified harm to a vulnerable person violates the duty to minimize harm.

### Insufficient protection of children
Children cannot fully anticipate the consequences of public exposure, and errors can follow them for life. Apply the highest standard of protection to minors.

### Re-traumatizing through the interview
Pressuring trauma survivors for graphic detail, or handling their accounts without trauma awareness, can cause real psychological harm. Prioritize the subject's well-being over narrative completeness.

### Refusing to honor withdrawal
Treating consent as a binding contract that cannot be revoked, especially before publication, ignores the subject's evolving understanding and circumstances.

### Abandoning the subject post-publication
Moving on to the next story while the subject lives with the consequences abandons the duty of care that the relationship created.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I assessed the subject's capacity to give meaningful consent, and declined, deferred, or added safeguards where capacity is diminished?
- [ ] Is the consent genuinely informed, with the subject understanding the scope, reach, permanence, and consequences, and knowing they can refuse or withdraw?
- [ ] Have I recognized and actively counteracted the power asymmetry between journalist and vulnerable subject?
- [ ] Have I exercised restraint, publishing only what is justified by public interest and minimizing harm, even where consent was given?
- [ ] Have I applied special protection to children, considering their long-term welfare above the story's value?
- [ ] Did I follow trauma-aware practice, avoiding pressure for graphic detail and providing support information?
- [ ] Will I honor requests to withdraw, to the extent possible, especially before publication?
- [ ] Have I planned to maintain responsibility and respond to consequences after publication?
- [ ] Have I avoided leveraging the subject's vulnerability, dependence, hope, or fear to obtain participation?
- [ ] Would an advocate for the subject recognize my handling as careful, respectful, and protective?
