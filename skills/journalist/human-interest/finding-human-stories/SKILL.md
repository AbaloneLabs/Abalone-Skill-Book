# Finding Human Stories

Human stories, the experiences of individuals, are how readers grasp the reality of abstract issues, statistics, and distant events. A single well-told story can convey what no dataset can. The harm this skill prevents is the misuse of the human-interest form: the unrepresentative anecdote that distorts a systemic reality, the exploitation of a subject's suffering for emotional impact, the selection of subjects who fit a predetermined narrative rather than illustrate the truth, or the human story that stands in for evidence it cannot actually provide. Finding and using human stories well is a discipline of representativeness, consent, context, and honesty about what an individual story can and cannot prove.

## Core Rules

### Ensure the human story represents the larger truth
An individual story is powerful but can mislead if it is unrepresentative. Before featuring a subject, establish that their experience genuinely reflects the broader issue, through data, other cases, or expert confirmation. If the story is illustrative-but-exceptional, say so explicitly rather than implying it is typical. The vivid particular must be anchored to the general reality.

### Find subjects through the issue, not the narrative
Seek subjects whose experiences genuinely illuminate the issue, rather than finding an issue to hang a compelling narrative on. Start from the problem, the policy, the event, the trend, and locate the people living it, then verify that their experience is representative. Selecting subjects to fit a pre-written story distorts both the subject and the issue.

### Distinguish the illustrative from the evidentiary
A human story illustrates; it rarely proves. One person's experience with a system does not establish that the system fails everyone, though it may reveal a failure worth investigating. Use the human story to make the issue real and to generate questions, and use data, documents, and additional reporting to establish the systemic truth. Be clear with readers about which function the story serves.

### Obtain genuine, informed consent
Subjects of human-interest stories, especially those sharing difficult experiences, must understand what participation involves: the scope of the story, how they will be portrayed, the reach and permanence of publication, and the potential consequences. Consent that is uninformed, coerced, or obtained under false pretenses is not consent. Protect the subject's ability to make a free, informed choice.

### Provide context that connects the individual to the systemic
The individual story gains significance from its connection to the larger issue. Provide the data, history, and structural context that show how the subject's experience fits the pattern. Without this context, the story is merely emotional; with it, the story illuminates a truth larger than itself.

### Avoid exploiting suffering for engagement
Human stories often involve hardship, trauma, or loss. The line between illuminating suffering and exploiting it for emotional impact is critical. Pursue and portray difficult experiences only where they serve public understanding, treat subjects with dignity, and avoid gratuitous detail whose only function is to provoke feeling. Engagement is not justification.

### Represent subjects as agents, not merely victims
People living through difficult circumstances are not only their suffering; they have agency, complexity, and lives beyond the issue. Portray subjects as full human beings acting within their circumstances, not as passive victims reduced to their plight. Agency-respecting portrayal is both more accurate and more dignified.

### Follow up and honor ongoing duties
Human stories create relationships and obligations that do not end at publication. Subjects may face consequences from their participation. Follow up, honor any commitments, and be responsive if the subject later wants to discuss the impact. The story is not over for the subject when it is published.

## Common Traps

### The unrepresentative anecdote
Featuring a compelling subject whose experience is atypical, presented as representative, distorts the reader's understanding of the issue. Verify representativeness or contextualize the exception.

### The narrative in search of an issue
Starting from a desired story shape and finding subjects to fit it produces distortion. Start from the issue and let genuine subjects emerge.

### Confusing illustration with proof
A human story illustrates a problem; it does not prove its prevalence or cause. Overstating what the story establishes misleads readers about the systemic reality.

### Exploitative emotionalism
Pursuing or presenting suffering primarily for emotional impact, without serving understanding or treating the subject with dignity, exploits the subject and degrades the journalism.

### Consent failures
Obtaining participation without genuine understanding of the consequences, or from subjects too vulnerable to give free consent, is unethical even if the story is significant.

### Reducing subjects to their plight
Portraying subjects only as victims of their circumstance strips them of agency and complexity, producing a portrait that is both inaccurate and undignified.

### Abandoning subjects after publication
Treating subjects as material to be used and then discarded ignores the consequences they live with and the duties the relationship created.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I established that the subject's experience genuinely represents the larger issue, or explicitly contextualized it as exceptional?
- [ ] Did I find the subject through the issue rather than fitting the issue to a pre-written narrative?
- [ ] Am I clear, with readers and myself, about what the human story illustrates versus what it proves?
- [ ] Did the subject give genuine, informed consent, understanding the scope, reach, and consequences?
- [ ] Have I provided the context that connects the individual story to the systemic truth?
- [ ] Have I avoided exploiting suffering for engagement, including only what serves understanding with dignity?
- [ ] Have I portrayed the subject as a full, agentive person rather than reducing them to their plight?
- [ ] Have I distinguished the emotional power of the story from its evidentiary weight?
- [ ] Will I follow up and honor ongoing duties to the subject after publication?
- [ ] Would the subject, and others who share their experience, recognize the story as fair, representative, and dignified?
