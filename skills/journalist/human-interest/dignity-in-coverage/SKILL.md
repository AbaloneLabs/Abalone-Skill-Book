# Dignity In Coverage

How journalism portrays people in difficult circumstances, poverty, crime, victimization, tragedy, illness, shapes how the public sees them and how they see themselves. The harm this skill prevents is coverage that degrades its subjects: poverty voyeurism that turns hardship into spectacle, tragedy tourism that consumes suffering for engagement, language and images that reduce people to their circumstances, or framing that strips subjects of agency and humanity. Dignity in coverage is a discipline of respect, careful language and image choices, contextualization, and the recognition that subjects are people whose lives extend beyond the story, not material to be consumed.

## Core Rules

### Portray subjects as full human beings, not reductions of their circumstance
A person in poverty is not only poor; a crime victim is not only a victim; a defendant is not only the charge. Represent subjects with the complexity, agency, and full humanity they possess. Avoid framing that defines a person entirely by their circumstance, however central it is to the story. Reduction is distortion, and it degrades.

### Choose language that respects dignity
Word choice signals respect or contempt. Avoid labels that reduce people to categories, "the poor," "illegals," "felons," that strip individuality and invite judgment. Prefer person-first or precise language that describes without stigmatizing. Be conscious of how terminology affects both the subject and the reader's perception.

### Select images that do not exploit or degrade
Photographs of people in distress can be powerful and necessary, but they can also exploit, invade privacy, and reduce subjects to their suffering. Choose images that convey the story without stripping subjects of dignity. Avoid gratuitous depictions of suffering, identifying images of vulnerable subjects without compelling justification, and compositions that demean. The image should serve understanding, not spectacle.

### Avoid poverty and tragedy voyeurism
Coverage that invites readers to gaze at hardship or suffering primarily for emotional experience, rather than to understand its causes and significance, turns subjects into exhibits. Pursue difficult stories because they serve public understanding, and frame them so that the reader is invited to understand and empathize, not to consume. The test is whether the coverage serves the subject and the issue or the audience's appetite for sensation.

### Provide context that explains rather than excuses
Subjects in difficult circumstances are often there because of systemic forces, history, policy, or structural inequality. Providing this context is essential to honest coverage and to representing subjects fairly. Context explains how the subject came to their circumstance without reducing them to a victim or absolving them of agency. It lets readers understand rather than judge.

### Give subjects voice and agency in their own portrayal
Where possible, let subjects speak for themselves, in their own words, about their experience and perspective. Representation that includes the subject's own voice is more accurate and more dignified than portrayal that treats the subject as a silent object of description. Agency includes the subject's right to shape how they are seen.

### Consider the consequences of identification
Naming or visually identifying a person in connection with hardship, crime, victimization, or stigma can have lasting consequences for their life, employment, relationships, and safety. Weigh the public interest in identification against the harm it may cause, especially for private individuals. The fact that identification is possible does not mean it is justified.

### Apply the same dignity standards regardless of the subject's status
Dignity is owed to all subjects, including those accused or convicted of crimes, those whose actions are criticized, and those who are unsympathetic. Standards of fair, non-degrading portrayal should not depend on whether the subject is admired. Consistency is the test of principle.

## Common Traps

### Poverty and tragedy voyeurism
Coverage that aestheticizes or consumes suffering for reader engagement, without serving understanding or the subject's interest, exploits the vulnerable for attention.

### Reductive labeling
Language that defines people by a single characteristic or circumstance strips them of individuality and invites stigma. Prefer precise, respectful description.

### Exploitative imagery
Photographs that emphasize suffering, expose vulnerable subjects, or demean through composition, serve spectacle rather than understanding and degrade the subject.

### Defining subjects by their circumstance
Framing that treats a person only as a victim, a perpetrator, or a case, without the context and complexity of their full life, distorts and diminishes.

### Insufficient voice for the subject
Portrayal that describes subjects without letting them speak for themselves treats them as objects rather than agents. Include their perspective in their own words.

### Unjustified identification
Naming or depicting subjects in connection with stigma or hardship, without compelling public interest, causes lasting harm without serving the story's purpose.

### Inconsistent dignity standards
Withholding dignity from unsympathetic subjects, the accused, the convicted, the criticized, reveals that the standard is convenience rather than principle.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I portrayed subjects as full, complex human beings rather than reducing them to their circumstance?
- [ ] Does my language respect dignity, avoiding stigmatizing labels and preferring precise, respectful description?
- [ ] Do the images I have chosen convey the story without exploiting, degrading, or unnecessarily exposing subjects?
- [ ] Have I avoided poverty and tragedy voyeurism, ensuring the coverage serves understanding rather than sensation?
- [ ] Have I provided the context that explains the circumstance without reducing the subject to a victim or stripping their agency?
- [ ] Does the portrayal include the subject's own voice and perspective in their own words?
- [ ] Have I weighed the consequences of identification, publishing names or images only where justified by public interest?
- [ ] Have I applied consistent dignity standards regardless of whether the subject is sympathetic?
- [ ] Have I avoided framing that invites readers to judge or consume rather than to understand and empathize?
- [ ] Would the subject, and others in similar circumstances, recognize the coverage as respectful of their dignity and humanity?
