# Expert Source Identification

Finding the right expert is a reporting skill, not a lookup task. The expert who appears first in a search, who is most quoted, or who is most eager to talk is often not the most knowledgeable, and may be the most agenda-driven. The judgment problem is that expertise is invisible to a generalist reporter: credentials look similar, titles sound authoritative, and confident commentary reads as competence. Without disciplined identification, a reporter reaches for the visible expert and ends up amplifying a narrow, conflicted, or fringe voice while the genuine specialists go unheard. The result is coverage that is fluent but wrong, especially in scientific, medical, legal, economic, and technical domains where errors travel far and cause real harm.

Use this skill when seeking an expert for a story, evaluating whether a candidate is qualified, building a standing roster of specialists, or deciding which expert fits a specific question. The main risk is that an agent treats discoverability, the ease of finding someone, as a proxy for expertise.

## Core Rules

### Match The Expert To The Specific Question

Expertise is narrow. A cardiologist is not an expert on virology; a constitutional lawyer is not an expert on tax law; a climate modeler is not an expert on adaptation policy; a software engineer is not an expert on hardware security. Before searching for an expert, define the specific question precisely, then identify the specialty that actually maps to it. A vague question, "is this chemical safe?", invites a vague expert; a precise question, "what does the toxicological literature say about chronic low-dose exposure to this compound in drinking water?", invites the right specialist.

When a story spans specialties, assemble more than one expert rather than forcing a single voice to opine outside their domain. An expert speaking outside their field is, for that question, a layperson, and their confidence does not transfer their competence.

### Evaluate Credentials, Not Just Titles And Visibility

Credentials are earned through training, peer-reviewed work, and recognized contribution to a field. Titles, media presence, and institutional affiliation are signals, but they are not the same as credentials, and they can be misleading. A professor at a prestigious institution may be speaking outside their research area; a think-tank fellow may have strong credentials or may be an advocate with a thin academic record; a frequently quoted commentator may be skilled at commentary rather than at the underlying science.

Look at the candidate's actual peer-reviewed publications, their specific research domain, their citations by other specialists, their institutional role, and their standing among peers in the exact subfield. Distinguish between academic experts, practitioners, and advocates, and use each for what they can legitimately offer. A practitioner's hands-on experience may be more relevant than a theorist's publications for some questions; an advocate's framing is input to be weighed, not expertise to be deferred to.

### Check For Conflicts Of Interest

Experts have conflicts: funding from industry, consulting relationships, board seats, equity, ideological commitments, or institutional incentives that shape their positions. A conflict does not invalidate expertise, but it must be known and disclosed so the reporter and the audience can weigh it. Ask experts directly about their funding, consulting, and affiliations, and verify against disclosure registers, conflict-of-interest statements in their publications, and prior reporting.

Be especially careful with experts provided by industry front groups, advocacy organizations, or institutions with a stake in the story's outcome. They may be genuinely credentialed and still selected because their position serves the funder. Diversify your expert pool so that no single interested party controls the voices in your story.

### Distinguish Independent Experts From Institutional Spokespeople

An official from an agency, a corporate spokesperson, or a professional association representative is speaking for an institution, not as an independent expert, even when they hold a doctoral degree. Their role is to advance the institution's position, and their statements should be attributed accordingly. Independent experts, academics without a stake in the outcome, practitioners not employed by the parties, and researchers whose work is not funded by interested parties offer a different kind of authority.

Use both, but label them accurately. Conflating an institutional spokesperson with an independent expert misleads the audience about the basis of the claims. When an institution's expert and an independent expert disagree, the disagreement itself is often the story.

### Seek Diversity Across The Expert Pool

Expert pools have demographic, geographic, institutional, and ideological patterns that, left uncorrected, produce narrow coverage. If every expert on a beat comes from the same handful of universities, the same professional networks, or the same demographic group, the coverage will reflect those patterns even when each expert is individually qualified. Actively seek experts from a range of institutions, regions, career stages, and backgrounds, including early-career researchers who may have the most current knowledge but the lowest media visibility.

Professional societies, peer-reviewed literature's author lists, conference programs, and specialist databases are better sources of diverse experts than the roster of frequent media commentators. Build a standing roster that reflects the actual diversity of the field, not just its most visible members.

### Verify The Expert's Position Against The Field

A single expert, however credentialed, can be an outlier. Before treating one expert's view as the state of knowledge, ask where it sits relative to the field: is it a mainstream position, a respected minority view, or a fringe claim? Look for synthesis documents, professional society statements, systematic reviews, and the distribution of views among multiple independent specialists. An expert whose view contradicts the field may be right, but the audience deserves to know that the view is not representative.

This is especially important in contested scientific areas, where a confident dissenter can be presented as one side of a balanced debate when they represent a tiny fraction of specialist opinion. Represent the weight of evidence honestly, not as a false balance between a consensus and a contrarian. See the fairness-context-and-newsworthiness skill.

### Prepare Before The Conversation

An expert can tell when a reporter has not done the groundwork, and the quality of what they share drops accordingly. Before the interview, read the expert's key papers or prior statements, understand the basic vocabulary and current debates of the field, and prepare specific questions that reflect real engagement. This produces better information and signals respect for the expert's time. It also lets the reporter recognize when an expert is overstating, hedging, or straying outside their evidence.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To The Most Quoted Expert

The expert who appears constantly in media is easy to find and often good on camera, but may be selected for availability and charisma rather than for the most current or rigorous knowledge. Reaching for the same visible voices narrows coverage and reinforces a self-perpetuating media ecosystem. Search the literature and specialist networks, not just prior coverage.

### Treating Confidence As Competence

An expert who speaks with certainty on everything is often speaking beyond their evidence. Genuine specialists tend to be careful about the limits of their knowledge and willing to say "that's outside my area" or "the evidence is mixed." Uncritical deference to confident commentary propagates overstatement.

### Letting An Interested Party Supply The Expert

Industry associations, advocacy groups, and government communications offices often provide experts chosen because their position serves the provider's interest. The expert may be credentialed, but the selection is not neutral. Independently identify experts rather than relying on supplied voices, and disclose supplied experts' affiliations.

### Conflating Advocacy With Expertise

An advocate may have deep knowledge, but their role is to advance a position, not to represent the state of the field. Treat advocates' claims as input to be verified against independent experts and the literature, not as expert assessment to be deferred to. Label their role accurately for the audience.

### Failing To Disclose Conflicts

An expert's funding, consulting, or ideological commitments shape their positions and must be known to the reporter and disclosed to the audience. Failing to ask, or failing to publish what is known, deprives the audience of the context needed to judge the claims.

### Presenting A Fringe View As Mainstream

A confident dissenter can be framed as one half of a balanced debate, giving a fringe position parity with a consensus. Represent the actual distribution of expert opinion, and where a view is marginal, say so rather than implying equivalence.

## Self-Check

Before treating expert identification as sound, verify:

- The specific question was defined precisely, and the expert's specialty actually maps to that question rather than to an adjacent field.
- Credentials were evaluated through publications, research domain, peer recognition, and institutional role, not just titles, visibility, or media presence.
- Conflicts of interest, including funding, consulting, equity, and ideological commitments, were asked about, verified, and disclosed.
- Institutional spokespeople were distinguished from independent experts, and each was labeled accurately for the audience.
- The expert pool is diverse across institution, region, career stage, and background, not drawn from the same visible commentators.
- The expert's position was checked against the weight of field opinion, with mainstream, minority, and fringe views represented accurately rather than as false balance.
- The reporter prepared by reading key work and understanding the field's current debates before the interview.
- No interested party's supplied expert was treated as a neutral voice without independent verification and disclosure.
- Advocates were labeled as advocates, and their claims verified against independent expertise and the literature.
- Expert identification supports editorial judgment; significant reliance on a particular expert, especially in contested or high-stakes domains, should involve a responsible editor, and specialist or legal review where the expert's claims carry significant risk.
