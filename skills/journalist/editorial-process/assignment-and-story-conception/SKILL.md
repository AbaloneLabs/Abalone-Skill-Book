# Assignment And Story Conception

Every story begins with a decision about what it is for, and that decision shapes everything that follows: who is assigned, how much time is given, what evidence is sought, and what the finished piece must prove. The judgment problem is that vague assignments produce vague stories, that an under-resourced investigation produces a confident but thin piece, and that a compelling pitch can hide a reporting question that cannot actually be answered. The work of conception is to turn an interesting idea into a reporting question that is specific, answerable, newsworthy, and proportional to the effort.

Use this skill when commissioning, pitching, scoping, or greenlighting stories, and when reviewing whether a story idea is ready to become an assignment. The main risk is that an agent may approve a story on the strength of a hook or a hypothesis without defining what evidence would satisfy the reporting question, what could disprove it, and whether the resources match the ambition. This skill is editorial guidance; final commissioning decisions rest with editors.

## Core Rules

### Define The Reporting Question Before The Hook

A hook is what makes a story feel timely; a reporting question is what the story must answer. "There is growing concern about X" is a hook. "Did the agency's new policy cause the documented increase in Y, and who knew?" is a reporting question. Before approving an assignment, state the central question in one or two sentences, identify what evidence would answer it, and identify what evidence would refute the presumed answer. A story without a defined question drifts into anecdote and assertion.

The reporting question should be specific enough that finishing the reporting can be recognized. If the question cannot be stated concretely, the assignment is not yet ready.

### Test The Hypothesis For Falsifiability

A strong story hypothesis is falsifiable: there is a conceivable reporting outcome that would prove it wrong. "The company misled regulators" is falsifiable if documents or testimony could show honest disclosure. "The company is bad" is not falsifiable and tends to produce advocacy rather than reporting. Before greenlighting, ask what the reporter would do if the reporting contradicts the premise. If the answer is "find a different angle to keep the thesis," the premise is an agenda, not a hypothesis.

Build the falsifiability test into the assignment: require the reporter to seek the strongest contrary evidence, not only confirmation.

### Match Resources To The Ambition

Ambition without resources produces overreaching. An investigation that needs six months, three reporters, legal review, and data analysis cannot be done responsibly in two weeks by one reporter, no matter how talented. When scoping, estimate the reporting depth required, the number and type of sources, the documents needed, the verification burden, the legal review, and the production time. If the resources are not available, narrow the ambition rather than publishing a thin version of a big claim.

It is better to publish a narrow, fully supported story than a broad, under-supported one. Scope to what can be done well.

### Establish Newsworthiness And Public Interest

Before committing resources, articulate the public-interest value: what does the audience gain, what accountability does it serve, what decision does it inform? Newsworthiness is not the same as interest or traffic. A story may be interesting without serving public understanding, and a story may be important without being viral. Document the public-interest rationale so it can be weighed against the cost, the intrusion, and the risk.

If the public-interest case is weak, the story may still be worth doing as feature or service journalism, but it should not be framed or resourced as accountability reporting.

### Identify The Subjects And The Response Obligation

At conception, identify who the story is about, who will be criticized or affected, and what the right-of-response obligation will require. Stories that accuse or expose need contact with the subjects, response time, and often legal review. Building the response obligation into the timeline prevents last-minute scrambles and fairness failures. Identify vulnerable subjects who need additional protection.

A story that cannot give its subjects fair notice is not ready to be assigned as an accountability piece.

### Define The Standard Of Proof For Central Claims

Different claims deserve different evidentiary standards. A routine fact needs solid sourcing; an accusation of wrongdoing needs strong, corroborated evidence; a claim that implies criminality needs the highest standard. At conception, define what proof the central claims require, so the reporter knows when the reporting is sufficient. This prevents both under-proved publication and endless over-reporting that never publishes.

Tie the standard of proof to the consequences of error: the more damaging the claim, the higher the bar.

### Plan The Verification And Independence Check

Build verification into the assignment from the start: independent corroboration, document review, source-independence analysis, and conflict checks. Confirm the reporter has no conflict with the subject, that sources are not all from one interested party, and that the evidence base is not a single origin in disguise. A story built on one press release, one faction, or one dataset needs broader reporting before it can stand.

### Set A Realistic Timeline With Gates

Break the assignment into gates: initial reporting, evidence review, response contacts, draft, edit, fact-check, legal review, publication. Each gate is a decision point where the story can be narrowed, expanded, held, or killed. A timeline without gates produces deadline-driven publication of unfinished reporting. Build in time for the gates that matter most: response, fact-check, and legal.

## Common Traps

### Approving On Hook Strength Alone

A timely, compelling hook can carry an assignment that has no answerable question, producing a story that is engaging but unsupported.

### Non-Falsifiable Premises

A premise that cannot be disproved produces confirmation-seeking rather than reporting and tends toward advocacy.

### Over-Ambition, Under-Resource

Big claims with small resources yield thin, overreaching stories that fail under scrutiny.

### Vague Public-Interest Rationale

Without a specific public-interest case, the story cannot be weighed against its cost, intrusion, and risk.

### Ignoring The Response Obligation

Failing to build subject contact into the timeline leads to last-minute fairness failures or delayed publication.

### Uniform Standard Of Proof

Treating a minor fact and a criminal accusation as needing the same evidence leads to either under-proved serious claims or wasted effort on trivial ones.

### Single-Origin Evidence

A story resting on one press release, faction, or dataset is fragile and often misleading.

### Timeline Without Gates

A flat timeline forces publication of unfinished reporting when the deadline arrives.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the central reporting question stated in one or two concrete sentences, with the evidence that would answer and refute it identified?
- [ ] Is the hypothesis falsifiable, with a genuine reporting outcome that would disprove the premise?
- [ ] Do the assigned resources, time, and expertise match the ambition, or has the scope been narrowed to what can be done well?
- [ ] Is the public-interest rationale specific and documented, beyond mere interest or traffic?
- [ ] Are the subjects identified, with the right-of-response obligation built into the timeline and vulnerable subjects flagged?
- [ ] Is the standard of proof for each central claim defined and proportionate to the consequences of error?
- [ ] Does the verification plan include independent corroboration, source-independence analysis, and conflict checks?
- [ ] Does the timeline include decision gates for response, fact-check, legal review, and publication?
- [ ] Has the commissioning decision been reviewed by the responsible editor, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that final assignment authority rests with editorial leadership?
