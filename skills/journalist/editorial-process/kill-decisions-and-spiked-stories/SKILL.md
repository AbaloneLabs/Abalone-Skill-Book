# Kill Decisions And Spiked Stories

The decision not to publish a story, to kill or spike it, is one of the most consequential and least transparent decisions in journalism, and it is where editorial integrity is most tested. Killing a story that should run suppresss information the public deserves; running a story that should not publish causes harm that cannot be undone. The judgment problem is that kill decisions are made for many reasons, some legitimate and some improper, and the legitimate reasons, legal risk, insufficient verification, low newsworthiness, resource limits, often shade into improper ones, protecting advertisers, avoiding political pressure, sparing powerful subjects, or favoring institutional interests. Because killed stories are invisible, there is no public check on whether the decision was sound, and the absence of scrutiny makes kill decisions a primary site where pressure and self-interest can override journalism's public purpose. The skill is making kill decisions on legitimate journalistic grounds, documenting the reasoning, resisting improper pressure, and maintaining standards that distinguish a responsible kill from an improper suppression.

The harm this skill prevents is the silent erosion of public information that improper kills produce. When stories are killed to protect advertisers, avoid angering powerful subjects, or serve the outlet's interests, the public loses information it had a right to, and the loss is invisible because the story never appears. This is especially damaging because the stories most likely to face improper pressure are often the most important, investigations of powerful institutions, exposés of advertisers, reporting that challenges the outlet's relationships. The agent must help the journalist and editor evaluate kill decisions against legitimate criteria, recognize and resist improper pressure, document the reasoning so it can be reviewed, and create the conditions for kill decisions to be made on merit rather than on convenience or fear.

Use this skill when deciding whether to kill or spike a story, when pressure is applied to suppress a piece, when evaluating whether a completed story should run, or when reviewing whether past kill decisions were sound. The goal is to prevent the agent from accepting weak reasons to kill and to ensure kill decisions serve the public rather than improper interests.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate Kill Decisions Against Explicit Legitimate Criteria

A kill decision should be evaluated against explicit, legitimate criteria, not made on instinct or convenience. Making the criteria explicit forces the decision to be defensible and exposes decisions driven by improper motives that cannot survive scrutiny.

Evaluate against:

- verification: is the story sufficiently reported and sourced to publish, or does it fall short of the standard;
- newsworthiness: does the public interest justify publication, or is the story minor, stale, or without impact;
- legal risk: is there a defamatory or legal exposure that cannot be managed, or can the risk be addressed through editing;
- harm: would publication cause harm disproportionate to the public interest, to subjects or third parties;
- resources: has the story consumed resources without reaching publishable quality, and is further investment justified.

A story that fails verification, lacks newsworthiness, carries unmanageable legal risk, or would cause disproportionate harm may be legitimately killed. A story that fails none of these but is killed anyway demands scrutiny of the real reason.

### Distinguish Legitimate From Improper Kill Reasons

The core integrity test is distinguishing legitimate reasons, which serve the public, from improper reasons, which serve the outlet or powerful interests at the public's expense. Many improper reasons masquerade as legitimate ones, and the distinction must be made honestly.

Legitimate reasons include:

- insufficient verification despite reasonable effort;
- low newsworthiness that does not justify the harm or resource cost;
- unmanageable legal risk that cannot be addressed through editing;
- disproportionate harm to subjects or third parties without countervailing public interest;
- the story has been overtaken by events or superseded by better reporting.

Improper reasons include:

- protecting an advertiser or revenue source;
- avoiding political or institutional pressure;
- sparing a powerful subject or personal connection;
- serving the outlet's commercial or reputational interests over the public's;
- fear of consequences rather than journalistic judgment.

When the stated reason is legitimate but the real reason is improper, the decision is corrupt regardless of the framing. The editor must be honest about which is driving the decision.

### Resist Pressure To Kill For Improper Reasons

Kill decisions are a primary target for pressure, because suppressing a story is often easier for powerful interests than fighting a published one. Advertisers threaten to pull spending, subjects threaten lawsuits or consequences, political actors apply leverage, and institutional interests favor suppression. Resisting this pressure is essential to editorial integrity.

Resist by:

- identifying the source and nature of pressure when it appears;
- distinguishing legitimate concerns, like genuine legal risk, from pressure to suppress;
- involving senior editors and, where appropriate, legal counsel, to evaluate pressure on merit;
- documenting the pressure and the response, so the decision is reviewable;
- being willing to publish despite pressure when the journalism is sound and the public interest warrants it.

Pressure to kill is a signal that the story matters, not a reason to kill it. The response to pressure is rigorous evaluation, not capitulation.

### Document The Reasoning For Review

Because killed stories are invisible, the only check on kill decisions is internal documentation and review. Documenting the reasoning allows the decision to be reviewed by other editors, revisited if circumstances change, and defended if later questioned.

Document by:

- recording the specific criteria that drove the decision, verification, newsworthiness, legal, harm;
- noting what additional reporting would be needed to make the story publishable, if any;
- recording any pressure applied and how it was evaluated;
- identifying who made the decision and who was consulted;
- setting a review trigger, if circumstances change, should the decision be revisited.

A kill decision that cannot be documented or defended after the fact is suspect. The discipline of documentation keeps the decision honest.

### Apply A Higher Bar For Killing Investigations

Investigative stories represent a large investment of resources and often address significant public interests, so killing them carries a higher cost and should face a higher bar. The temptation to kill an investigation that is close to publication, because of pressure, fatigue, or institutional risk-aversion, is strong and must be resisted.

Apply a higher bar by:

- requiring investigation kills to be decided at a senior editorial level, not unilaterally;
- demanding that the reason be strong, not merely convenient;
- considering whether the investigation can be published with additional reporting or editing rather than killed;
- recognizing the chilling effect of killing investigations on the reporters and the culture;
- being especially vigilant about pressure when investigations target powerful subjects.

Investigations that are killed under pressure send a message that the outlet will not protect its reporters' work, which undermines future reporting. The bar for killing them must be high and the process rigorous.

### Consider Partial Publication Or Alternative Outcomes

A story that cannot be published as planned may still have legitimate partial outcomes that serve the public without the risks of full publication. Binary kill-or-publish decisions sometimes miss options that preserve the public interest while managing the concerns.

Consider:

- publishing with additional caveats, context, or right-of-reply that addresses the concerns;
- publishing a portion of the story that is verified while holding the unverified portion;
- continuing reporting to reach publishable quality rather than killing;
- referring the story to another outlet if your outlet cannot publish for legitimate reasons;
- using the reporting to inform future coverage even if this specific piece does not run.

Partial publication is not always appropriate, but it should be considered before a binary kill, especially when the public interest is significant and the concerns are addressable.

### Maintain A Culture That Supports Publishing

Kill decisions do not happen in a vacuum; they happen in an editorial culture that either supports or undermines publishing difficult stories. A culture that default-kills under pressure, that penalizes reporters whose stories face resistance, or that treats advertiser or political concerns as overriding will produce improper kills routinely. Building a culture that supports publishing is preventive.

Build by:

- making clear that the default is publication when journalism is sound and public interest warrants;
- protecting reporters whose stories face pressure, rather than abandoning them;
- separating editorial from commercial and political influence in kill decisions;
- reviewing kill decisions periodically to identify patterns of improper suppression;
- celebrating difficult stories that were published despite pressure, to set cultural norms.

Culture determines outcomes. A healthy culture produces sound kill decisions; an unhealthy one produces improper suppression dressed up as judgment.

## Common Traps

### Killing For Advertiser Or Revenue Protection

Suppressing stories that would displease advertisers or revenue sources. The trap is commercial interest overriding public interest.

### Killing Under Political Or Institutional Pressure

Suppressing stories because of pressure from political actors or institutions. The trap is fear overriding journalism.

### Vague Or Undocumented Reasons

Killing stories on instinct or convenience without explicit, documented criteria. The trap is unreviewable decisions that hide improper motives.

### Killing Investigations Close To Publication

Abandoning investigations near the finish line due to fatigue, risk-aversion, or pressure. The trap is wasting investment and chilling future reporting.

### Binary Kill-Or-Publish Thinking

Treating kill as the only option when partial publication or continued reporting could serve the public. The trap is missing legitimate alternatives.

### Defaulting To Caution

Killing stories reflexively to avoid risk or controversy rather than evaluating on merit. The trap is risk-aversion that suppresses journalism.

### Improper Reasons Framed As Legitimate

Presenting advertiser or political pressure as verification or legal concerns. The trap is corrupt decisions disguised as journalistic judgment.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The kill decision was evaluated against explicit legitimate criteria: verification, newsworthiness, legal risk, harm, and resources.
- [ ] Legitimate reasons were distinguished from improper ones, and no decision was driven by advertiser, political, or institutional interest.
- [ ] Any pressure to kill was identified, evaluated on merit, and documented, rather than capitulated to.
- [ ] The reasoning is documented well enough to be reviewed by other editors and revisited if circumstances change.
- [ ] Investigations faced a higher bar for killing and required senior editorial decision, not unilateral action.
- [ ] Partial publication, continued reporting, or alternative outcomes were considered before a binary kill.
- [ ] The decision was made on journalistic merit, not on default caution or convenience.
- [ ] The editorial culture supports publishing sound journalism and protects reporters whose stories face pressure.
- [ ] No story was killed to protect a powerful subject, personal connection, or the outlet's commercial interests.
- [ ] The decision, if challenged, can be defended on legitimate journalistic grounds that would withstand review.
