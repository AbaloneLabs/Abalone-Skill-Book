# Editing And Revision Cycles

Editing is where reporting becomes publishable, and it is also where many failures originate: structural problems left unfixed, claims that outran the evidence, fairness gaps, and language that distorts. The judgment problem is that revision is not just polishing prose; it is a re-examination of the reporting against the claims, and an editor who treats it as line-editing will miss the substantive problems, while an editor who rewrites the reporter loses the reporting's voice and accuracy. The discipline is to run revision in stages that each catch a different class of problem.

Use this skill when editing drafts, structuring revision cycles, giving feedback to reporters, and deciding when a story is ready to move to fact-check and publication. The main risk is that an agent may do one undifferentiated pass that catches style errors but misses unsupported claims, structural incoherence, or fairness failures. This skill is editorial guidance; final editing authority rests with the responsible editor.

## Core Rules

### Edit In Stages, Not In One Pass

Different problems live at different layers, and a single pass blurs them. A useful sequence moves from large to small: first the structure and thesis, then the evidence and claims, then fairness and framing, then language and style, then mechanics. Fixing a sentence-level issue before resolving whether the section belongs wastes effort, because the section may be cut. Start with the questions that, if answered differently, would change the most.

The first edit should ask whether the story's central claim is supported and whether the structure serves it. Only when the spine is sound does line-level editing add value.

### Pressure-Test The Central Claim First

Before touching prose, confirm what the story actually claims and whether the reporting supports it. Identify the thesis sentence, the strongest evidence, and the weakest important claim. Ask whether the headline implication is earned by the body. Many drafts imply more than they prove, and the edit's first job is to close that gap, either by strengthening the reporting or by narrowing the claim to what is proven.

If the central claim cannot be supported, the edit must escalate to the editor responsible for the story rather than quietly softening language to mask the gap.

### Check Fairness And Framing In A Dedicated Pass

Fairness and framing deserve their own review, separate from claim-checking. Examine whose voices are present and absent, whether subjects were given a meaningful response opportunity, whether the framing privileges one interpretation, whether loaded language smuggles in judgment, and whether the story distinguishes fact, allegation, and opinion. A fairness pass catches problems that claim-checking misses, because a claim can be technically supported yet unfairly framed.

Look especially at the treatment of subjects who cannot easily defend themselves: private individuals, the accused, the dead, and the absent.

### Separate Substantive Feedback From Line Edits

Reporters cannot act on a mix of structural critique and comma corrections. Give substantive feedback first, the structural, evidentiary, and fairness issues, and resolve them before moving to line-level editing. This respects the reporter's time, preserves their voice, and prevents the demoralizing experience of having prose polished in a section that will be rewritten.

Substantive feedback should be specific: name the problem, explain why it matters, and propose a direction, rather than issuing vague instructions to "tighten" or "sharpen."

### Preserve The Reporter's Voice And Reporting Integrity

The editor's job is to make the reporter's work clearer and stronger, not to replace it with the editor's voice. Heavy rewriting risks introducing errors, because the editor lacks the reporter's knowledge of the material, and it erodes the reporter's accountability for the words. When rewriting is necessary, do it collaboratively, confirm factual implications with the reporter, and ensure the reporter stands behind the final version.

The reporter must recognize and own every published sentence. If they do not, the editing has overreached.

### Match Revision Depth To Story Risk

Not every story needs the same revision depth. A routine brief needs a quick accuracy and clarity check. An investigation, an exposé, an obituary, or a story naming individuals needs deep, multi-pass revision with senior review. Calibrate the revision process to the stakes: the more damaging the claims, the more vulnerable the subjects, or the more legal exposure, the more rigorous and layered the editing must be.

Under-editing high-stakes stories is a common cause of failure; over-editing low-stakes stories wastes scarce editorial time.

### Build In A Fresh-Eyes Review

After several revision cycles, both reporter and editor become blind to problems they have read many times. A fresh-eyes read by an editor who has not been immersed in the story catches residual gaps, confusing passages, and fairness issues the team has stopped seeing. Schedule a fresh-eyes review for significant stories before they move to fact-check.

The fresh-eyes reader should be senior enough to challenge the story and independent enough to doubt it.

### Connect Editing To Fact-Check And Legal Review

Editing does not end revision; it hands off to fact-check and, where needed, legal review. Ensure the edited draft reflects the reporting accurately, that no edit introduced an unsupported claim, and that the fact-checker works from the final edited version. Edits made after fact-check must be re-checked, because late changes are a frequent source of error.

## Common Traps

### One Undifferentiated Pass

Mixing structural, evidentiary, fairness, and line-level edits in a single pass lets the small problems crowd out the large ones.

### Polishing Prose Over Unsupported Claims

Spending effort on style while leaving the central claim unsupported produces a fluent but vulnerable story.

### Quietly Softening Language To Mask Gaps

Softening a claim to hide that the reporting does not support it evades the real decision, which is whether to publish, narrow, or hold.

### Editor Rewriting Over The Reporter

Heavy rewriting introduces errors the reporter cannot catch and erodes accountability for the words.

### Skipping The Fairness Pass

A technically supported claim can still be unfairly framed, and fairness problems are invisible to pure claim-checking.

### No Fresh-Eyes Review

Familiarity blindness lets residual problems survive many revision cycles.

### Late Edits After Fact-Check

Changes made after fact-check, often under deadline, introduce errors because they bypass the checking process.

### Uniform Revision Depth

Treating a brief and an investigation the same either over-invests in trivia or under-invests in high-stakes work.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the revision move in stages from large to small, starting with structure and thesis before line-level editing?
- [ ] Has the central claim been pressure-tested against the reporting, with unsupported claims escalated rather than quietly softened?
- [ ] Has a dedicated fairness and framing pass examined voices present and absent, response opportunities, loaded language, and the fact-opinion distinction?
- [ ] Is substantive feedback given and resolved before line edits, with feedback specific about the problem, the reason, and the direction?
- [ ] Does the editing preserve the reporter's voice and ensure the reporter recognizes and owns every published sentence?
- [ ] Is the revision depth calibrated to the story's stakes, with deeper, layered review for high-damage, high-vulnerability, or high-exposure stories?
- [ ] Has a fresh-eyes review by a senior, independent editor been scheduled for significant stories?
- [ ] Does the edited draft hand off cleanly to fact-check and legal review, with any post-fact-check changes re-checked?
- [ ] Has the final version been approved by the responsible editor, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that final editing authority rests with editorial leadership?
