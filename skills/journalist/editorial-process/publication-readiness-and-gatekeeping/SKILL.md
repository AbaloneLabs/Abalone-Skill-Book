# Publication Readiness And Gatekeeping

The decision to publish is a gate, not a formality. Once a story is public, it can inform decisions, damage reputations, expose sources, and become part of the permanent record, and correcting it after the fact is always harder than getting it right before. The judgment problem is that deadline pressure, competitive pressure, and the momentum of a finished draft all push toward publication, while the conditions that make a story safe to publish, full verification, fair response, proper framing, legal review, and editorial sign-off, are often incomplete. Gatekeeping is the discipline of holding stories that are not ready, even when holding is uncomfortable.

Use this skill when running a final pre-publication review, signing off on a story, deciding to hold or publish, and managing the tension between speed and readiness. The main risk is that an agent may treat a polished draft as a ready story, or may publish because the deadline has arrived rather than because the story is complete. This skill is editorial guidance; final publication authority rests with the responsible editor, and high-risk stories require legal review.

## Core Rules

### Define What "Ready" Means For This Story

Readiness is not a single standard; it is proportionate to the story's stakes. A brief needs accuracy, clarity, and basic sourcing. An exposé needs strong corroboration, documented response, fairness review, fact-check, and legal sign-off. Before the final gate, define what readiness requires for this specific story: the claims at stake, the subjects affected, the legal exposure, and the verification depth. A story is ready when it meets the standard its stakes demand, not when the draft is clean.

Document the readiness criteria so the gate is a checkable review, not a feeling.

### Verify That Central Claims Meet Their Evidentiary Standard

At the final gate, re-examine each central claim against its required standard of proof. The most damaging claims need the strongest evidence; routine facts need solid sourcing. Confirm that the headline implication is earned by the body, that attribution is precise, and that no claim outruns its evidence. If a central claim cannot meet its standard, the story must be narrowed, held, or killed, not softened to disguise the gap.

This is the last chance to catch the gap between what the story says and what the reporting proves.

### Confirm The Response Obligation Is Satisfied

For any story that criticizes, accuses, or affects identified subjects, confirm that meaningful response opportunities were offered, that responses were reflected or fairly considered, and that the subjects' positions are represented where relevant. Document the requests, deadlines, and responses. A story that publishes criticism without a fair response opportunity is both ethically weak and legally more vulnerable.

If a response is still owed and the story cannot wait, narrow the criticism to what can be published responsibly without the response, or hold.

### Check Framing, Fairness, And Language

The final gate reviews framing as well as facts. Examine whether the framing privileges one interpretation, whether loaded language smuggles in judgment, whether the headline and subhead match the body, whether images and captions distort, and whether vulnerable subjects are protected. A story can be factually supported yet unfairly framed, and the final gate is where framing problems are caught before they reach the audience.

Read the headline and social copy as the audience will encounter them, often without reading the full piece.

### Ensure Fact-Check And Legal Review Are Complete

For stories that warrant them, confirm that fact-check is complete and that any changes made after fact-check have been re-checked. Confirm that legal review has occurred where the story involves allegations, private individuals, national-security material, or other exposure, and that legal's concerns have been addressed. Fact-check and legal review are not optional stages for high-stakes stories; they are part of readiness.

If fact-check or legal review is pending, the story is not ready, regardless of the deadline.

### Review All Publication Surfaces

Errors and distortions travel through surfaces beyond the article body: headline, subhead, dek, image, caption, chart, pull quote, push alert, social post, newsletter blurb, and video thumbnail. The final gate reviews these surfaces for accuracy and framing, because they often reach a larger audience than the body and are harder to correct. A misleading headline on an accurate story misleads the audience.

Confirm that corrections to any surface can be made if the article changes.

### Assess Harm And Public Interest Together

At the final gate, weigh the foreseeable harm of publication against the public interest one more time. Harm includes reputational damage, safety risk to sources or subjects, legal exposure, and the risk of misleading the audience. Public interest includes accountability, informing decisions, and correcting the record. If the harm is disproportionate to the public interest, or if the public interest can be served with less harm through redaction or narrowing, adjust before publishing.

This is not a veto on newsworthy reporting; it is a final proportionality check.

### Hold When The Story Is Not Ready

The most important gatekeeping decision is to hold. A story that is incomplete, unfair, under-proved, or unsafe should be held until it is ready, even under deadline or competitive pressure. Holding is not failure; publishing an unfinished story is. Document the reasons for holding and the conditions for release, so the story can move when it is ready rather than dying in limbo.

Competitors publishing first does not make an unfinished story ready. Attribution, narrowing, and holding preserve credibility that speed would destroy.

### Plan Post-Publication Monitoring

Readiness includes a plan for what happens after publication. Identify who monitors for corrections, official responses, source safety, and follow-up developments, and how corrections will be handled across all surfaces. A story is not done when it publishes; the newsroom remains accountable for it.

## Common Traps

### Equating A Clean Draft With A Ready Story

A polished draft can still be under-proved, unfairly framed, or missing legal review. Readiness is about evidence and process, not prose quality.

### Publishing Because The Deadline Arrived

Deadlines create pressure, but they do not make a story ready. Publishing unfinished work to meet a deadline causes avoidable harm and corrections.

### Softening Claims To Pass The Gate

Quietly weakening language to disguise an evidentiary gap evades the real decision and leaves a misleading impression.

### Ignoring Publication Surfaces

Focusing on the body while letting headline, social copy, or caption distort reaches more readers with the error.

### Skipping Fact-Check Or Legal Under Pressure

Treating fact-check or legal review as optional for high-stakes stories removes the safeguards that catch serious problems.

### Competitive Publication As Justification

Competitors publishing first does not make unverified material safe to publish. Attribute, hold, or narrow instead.

### No Post-Publication Plan

Publishing without a monitoring and correction plan leaves the story unaccountable after release.

### Gatekeeping As A Rubber Stamp

A final gate that only confirms the decision already made adds no value. The gate must be a genuine review with authority to hold.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are the readiness criteria for this story defined and proportionate to its stakes, and is each criterion checkable rather than impressionistic?
- [ ] Does every central claim meet its evidentiary standard, with the headline implication earned by the body?
- [ ] Is the response obligation satisfied, with requests, deadlines, and responses documented and reflected?
- [ ] Have framing, fairness, loaded language, headline-body alignment, images, and vulnerable-subject protection been reviewed?
- [ ] Are fact-check and legal review complete for stories that warrant them, with post-fact-check changes re-checked?
- [ ] Have all publication surfaces, headline, social copy, caption, image, push alert, been reviewed for accuracy and framing?
- [ ] Has the foreseeable harm been weighed against the public interest, with narrowing or redaction applied where proportionality requires?
- [ ] If the story is not ready, is it being held with documented reasons and release conditions, regardless of deadline or competitive pressure?
- [ ] Is there a post-publication monitoring and correction plan, and has the responsible editor given final sign-off, with legal review where the story warrants it, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that final publication authority rests with editorial leadership?
