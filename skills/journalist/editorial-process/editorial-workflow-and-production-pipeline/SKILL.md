# Editorial Workflow And Production Pipeline

The editorial workflow is the sequence of stages a story passes through from pitch to publication, and the production pipeline is the machinery that moves it through those stages. Together they are the system that catches errors, enforces standards, and ensures that what publishes meets the publication's quality, and their failures are the source of a large share of published mistakes. The judgment problem is that the workflow is easy to treat as bureaucracy, a series of handoffs and sign-offs that slow the story, when in fact each stage exists to catch a specific category of problem that the previous stage cannot. When stages are skipped, compressed, or performed by the wrong role, the problems they would have caught reach the reader. A story that publishes with a misattributed quote, an unverified claim, a misleading headline, or a legal exposure almost always did so because a stage that would have caught it was bypassed, usually under deadline or resource pressure. Managing the pipeline is therefore not administration; it is the active defense of accuracy and standards.

Use this skill when managing the editorial workflow, sequencing review stages, coordinating the roles that touch a story, deciding how many passes it needs, handling handoffs and version control, or preventing the process failures that let errors through. The main risk is that an agent treats the workflow as a formality to be compressed under pressure, removing the safeguards whose absence produces the errors the system exists to prevent.

## Core Rules

### Match The Workflow To The Story's Stakes

Not every story needs the same pipeline. A brief needs accuracy review and a clean edit; a complex investigation needs reporting review, fact-check, legal review, sensitivity review, and senior editorial sign-off. Define the workflow a story requires based on its stakes: the claims at issue, the subjects affected, the legal exposure, and the verification depth required. A workflow that applies the same shallow process to everything under-checks high-stakes work, while one that applies the full process to everything strangles daily news. Match the pipeline to the risk, and document the required stages so that the process is a checkable plan rather than an impression.

The definition of required stages should happen at assignment or pitch, not at the end when pressure to publish is highest.

### Preserve The Distinct Function Of Each Stage

Each stage in the pipeline exists to catch a different category of problem, and conflating stages removes the catch. Reporting review examines whether the reporting supports the claims; editing sharpens structure, clarity, and framing; fact-checking verifies each claim against its sources; copyediting enforces grammar, style, and consistency; legal review assesses exposure; sensitivity review examines harm to vulnerable subjects. When one role performs another's function, the independent check is lost: an editor who fact-checks their own edit has no independent verification, and a fact-checker who reframes the story has displaced the editor. Preserve the distinct function of each stage, and assign each to the role equipped to perform it.

This is especially important when resources are thin and roles blur; the discipline of preserving distinct functions is what prevents the gaps that errors exploit.

### Sequence Stages So Each Builds On The Previous

The order of stages matters. Fact-checking a story whose structure is still changing wastes effort, because the checked text will be revised. Legal review of a story whose facts are not yet confirmed reviews the wrong material. Sequence the pipeline so that each stage operates on a stable input: reporting review before substantive editing, editing before fact-check, fact-check before legal review, and all substantive stages before copyedit and production. Late changes that occur after a stage has run require that stage to be rerun on the change, because the change may introduce exactly the problem the stage would have caught.

Build the sequence into the workflow as a rule, and resist the temptation to run stages in parallel to save time when the stages depend on each other.

### Manage Handoffs And Version Control Explicitly

Errors enter stories at handoffs, when one role passes the work to another and context is lost. Manage handoffs explicitly: communicate what stage the story is in, what has been checked and what has not, what open questions remain, and what changes were made and why. Maintain version control so that there is a single authoritative current version and a record of changes, because competing versions are a common source of published errors, where an earlier, uncorrected version reaches production. A story whose version history is unclear is a story whose verification status is unclear, because late changes may not have passed through the stages that verified the earlier text.

Late changes are the most dangerous moment in the pipeline, because they occur under deadline and after verification stages have run. Require that any substantive change after fact-check or legal review be re-checked and re-reviewed before publication.

### Protect Time For The Stages That Catch Serious Errors

The stages that catch the most serious errors, fact-check and legal review, are also the stages most often compressed or skipped under pressure, because they occur late and slow publication. Protect time for them deliberately. Build them into the schedule from the start, so that they are not competing for time at the end. A story that cannot accommodate fact-check or legal review in the time available is a story that is not ready to publish on that timeline, and the choice is to delay, narrow, or accept the risk of the uncaught error. The cost of a missed fact-check or legal review is paid in corrections, legal exposure, and credibility damage that no speed advantage offsets.

For high-stakes stories, treat fact-check and legal review as gating, not optional, stages.

### Coordinate The Roles And Communication

A story touches multiple roles, and coordination among them determines whether the pipeline functions. Establish clear communication about the story's status, open questions, and required next steps, so that no role is working on stale assumptions. Define who has authority at each stage: who can move the story forward, who can hold it, who can kill it, and who gives final sign-off. Ambiguity about authority produces stories that publish without the necessary review, because everyone assumed someone else had signed off. Make the authority and the communication explicit.

For complex stories, a single coordinating editor who tracks the story through the pipeline prevents the gaps that occur when no one owns the whole process.

### Build In A Final Pre-Publication Gate

The last stage before publication is a gate that reviews the story as a complete package, including all the surfaces that will reach the audience: headline, dek, image, caption, social copy, and push alerts. This gate checks that the stages have run, that late changes have been re-verified, that the publication surfaces match the body, and that no claim outruns its evidence. See the publication-readiness-and-gatekeeping skill. The final gate is the last chance to catch the errors that earlier stages missed or that late changes introduced, and it must have real authority to hold the story if it is not ready.

A final gate that rubber-stamps adds no value; the gate must be a genuine review with the power to stop publication.

## Common Traps

### Compressing Or Skipping Stages Under Deadline Pressure

The stages that catch serious errors are the first to be cut when time is short. This is a trap because the saved time is paid for in published errors. Protect time for fact-check and legal review.

### Conflating Roles And Losing The Independent Check

When one role performs another's function, the independent verification is lost. This is a trap because the work appears done while the check has not occurred. Preserve distinct functions for each stage.

### Running Stages In The Wrong Order

Fact-checking or legally reviewing unstable text wastes the review. This is a trap because the reviewed material changes. Sequence stages so each operates on stable input.

### Losing Context And Version Control At Handoffs

Competing versions and lost context produce published errors. This is a trap because the verification status becomes unclear. Manage handoffs and maintain authoritative version control.

### Letting Late Changes Bypass Verification

Changes after fact-check or legal review introduce unverified material. This is a trap because the change occurs under deadline, after the safeguards. Require re-checking of substantive late changes.

### Treating The Workflow As Bureaucracy To Be Minimized

The stages feel like obstacles but exist to catch specific errors. This is a trap because minimizing them removes the catches. Treat the workflow as the defense of accuracy.

### A Final Gate That Rubber-Stamps

A gate without genuine review authority adds no protection. This is a trap because it creates the appearance of a check without the substance. Give the final gate real authority to hold.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the workflow matched to the story's stakes, with required stages defined at assignment based on the claims, subjects, legal exposure, and verification depth?
- [ ] Does each stage preserve its distinct function, performed by the role equipped for it, rather than conflating roles and losing the independent check?
- [ ] Are stages sequenced so that each operates on stable input, with fact-check and legal review running on confirmed rather than changing text?
- [ ] Are handoffs managed explicitly and version control maintained, with a single authoritative current version and a record of changes?
- [ ] Are substantive late changes, after fact-check or legal review, required to be re-checked and re-reviewed before publication?
- [ ] Is time protected for the stages that catch serious errors, with fact-check and legal review treated as gating for high-stakes stories?
- [ ] Are roles coordinated with clear communication and defined authority at each stage, including who may move, hold, or kill the story?
- [ ] Does a final pre-publication gate review the complete package, including all publication surfaces, with genuine authority to hold?
- [ ] Before publication, is there any stage the story was supposed to pass through that was compressed, skipped, or performed by the wrong role?
- [ ] For high-stakes or legally sensitive stories, has the full required pipeline been completed and documented, with editorial leadership's final sign-off?
