# Source Protection Operational Security

A promise of confidentiality is only as strong as the operational practices behind it. Source identity can be exposed not only by a legal demand but by metadata, call records, device tracking, surveillance, leaked logs, insecure storage, careless forwarding, and even the specific phrasing of a story. The judgment problem is that a reporter may keep the source's name out of the article yet leave a trail, in communications, devices, or published details, that allows the source to be identified. Operational security for sources is the discipline of closing those trails before they exist.

Use this skill before contacting a sensitive source, when handling leaked material, when choosing communication tools, and when designing source-protection practices across a story. The main risk is that an agent may rely on a single layer of protection, such as an encrypted app, while ignoring the many other channels through which identity leaks. This skill is editorial guidance only; high-risk source protection may require specialized security review and legal counsel, especially for whistleblowers, dissidents, and sources in repressive environments.

## Core Rules

### Map The Identifying Surface Before Contact

Before engaging a sensitive source, map every channel through which their identity could be exposed: phone records, email metadata, messaging-app logs, location data, financial trails, workplace access logs, surveillance cameras, travel records, social-media traces, and the story's own descriptive details. Each channel is a potential breach. Protection means reducing exposure across all of them, not only the one the reporter happens to think of first.

Prioritize the channels most likely to be monitored by the actors who would harm the source. A government source faces different surveillance than a corporate whistleblower, and the threat model should drive the tools and practices chosen.

### Choose Communication Tools To Match The Threat Model

No single tool is universally safe. Encrypted messaging protects message contents but may expose metadata, contact lists, and account identifiers. Email leaves durable records. Phone calls generate call-detail records. In-person meetings avoid digital trails but create physical exposure. Choose tools based on the specific threat: who could monitor the channel, what they could learn, and what the source can realistically use.

For high-risk sources, prefer end-to-end encrypted messaging with disappearing messages, avoid linking the source's real identity to the account, and consider secure drop systems or in-person contact. Confirm the source understands the tool and its limits; a tool the source misuses provides no protection.

### Separate Source Identity From Reporting Material

Do not store source-identifying information alongside the reporting material that could be subpoenaed or breached. Keep identities, contact details, and meeting notes in separate, secured locations with restricted access. A single compromised notebook, device, or cloud account should not reveal both the story and the source.

Limit who in the newsroom knows the source's identity. Need-to-know access reduces the number of people who could be compelled, coerced, or socially engineered into disclosure.

### Minimize Metadata And Digital Footprints

Metadata often betrays identity even when content is encrypted. Phone numbers, account handles, timestamps, location pings, and connection patterns can identify a source. Where possible, use tools that minimize metadata, avoid contacting sources from devices or accounts tied to the reporter's identity in ways that create durable association logs, and be aware that workplace systems may log access.

Consider whether the act of contact itself is detectable. A sudden encrypted-message contact from a reporter to an employee can be as revealing as the message contents.

### Scrub Published Details That Could Identify The Source

A story can identify a source through descriptive details even without naming them. The number of people with access to the information, the source's role, department, seniority, recent interactions, or the timing of disclosure can narrow identity to one person. Before publishing, review every descriptive detail and ask whether, in context, it points to the source. Generalize or remove details that do.

Have a second editor perform this review independently, because the reporter may be too close to see the identifying pattern.

### Handle Leaked Documents To Prevent Forensic Tracing

Leaked documents often contain hidden identifiers: print tracking dots, metadata, revision history, watermarks, embedded identifiers, or distinctive formatting that traces back to the source. Before publishing or even storing leaked material, understand these risks. Avoid publishing documents in forms that preserve forensic traces, and do not return or forward documents through channels that expose the source.

For high-stakes leaks, consult specialists in document sanitization and security before publication.

### Defend Against Legal And Compelled Disclosure

Operational security complements, but does not replace, legal protection. Even well-protected sources can be exposed by subpoenas, search warrants, or compelled decryption. Maintain practices that limit what exists to be demanded: minimize retention of identifying material, use secure channels that do not retain logs, and have a legal response plan. Coordinate with counsel so that operational and legal protections reinforce each other.

### Prepare The Source For Their Own Risk

Sources often underestimate their exposure. Help the source understand the realistic risks, the limits of the protection offered, and the steps they must take on their end, such as using secure devices, avoiding workplace systems, and not discussing the contact. A source who is careless on their side defeats the reporter's operational security.

### Plan For The Long Term

Source protection does not end at publication. Identifying material may sit in archives, backups, or notes for years, available to future legal demands or breaches. Apply retention limits to identifying material, secure archives, and revisit protection if circumstances change, such as a new investigation, a change of government, or the source's changing situation.

## Common Traps

### Relying On A Single Layer Of Encryption

Encrypting messages while ignoring metadata, call records, device association, and published details leaves the source exposed through other channels.

### Storing Identity And Story Together

Keeping the source's name in the same notebook, document, or cloud account as the reporting material means a single breach or demand exposes both.

### Contact Trails That Reveal The Relationship

A reporter's call log, messaging contact, or sudden meeting with an employee can itself identify the source, regardless of message content.

### Descriptive Details That Narrow Identity

Publishing the source's role, access level, or the timing of disclosure can pinpoint a single person within a small group.

### Forensic Traces In Leaked Documents

Print dots, metadata, revision history, and watermarks in published documents can trace back to the source and are often invisible to the reporter.

### Over-Sharing Identity Inside The Newsroom

Telling more colleagues than necessary increases the number of people who could be compelled or tricked into disclosure.

### Assuming The Source Understands The Tools

A tool the source misuses, such as contacting from a work device or failing to enable disappearing messages, provides no protection.

### Neglecting Long-Term Archives

Identifying material retained indefinitely in archives or backups remains available to future demands and breaches.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I mapped the identifying surface, including communications, metadata, devices, location, and published details, and prioritized the channels most relevant to the threat model?
- [ ] Are communication tools chosen to match the specific threat, with the source's realistic ability to use them correctly confirmed?
- [ ] Is source-identifying information stored separately from reporting material, with need-to-know access inside the newsroom?
- [ ] Have I minimized metadata and digital footprints, and considered whether the act of contact itself is detectable?
- [ ] Have I, and an independent editor, scrubbed published descriptive details that could narrow identity to the source?
- [ ] For leaked documents, have I considered forensic traces and consulted specialists where stakes require it?
- [ ] Are operational protections reinforced by a legal response plan, with retention of identifying material minimized?
- [ ] Has the source been prepared for their own risk and the limits of the protection offered?
- [ ] For high-risk sources such as whistleblowers or dissidents, have I obtained specialized security review and legal counsel, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and not a substitute for expert security or legal review?
