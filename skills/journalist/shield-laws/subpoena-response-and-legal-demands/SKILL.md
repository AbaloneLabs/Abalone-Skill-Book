# Subpoena Response And Legal Demands

Legal demands for journalistic material are designed to extract what reporters have gathered, and they arrive in many forms: grand jury subpoenas, trial subpoenas, administrative orders, national-security letters, search warrants, and pre-litigation demand letters. The judgment problem is that the wrong response, whether reflexive compliance, defiant refusal, or delayed inaction, can expose sources, waive privileges, create evidence, or trigger sanctions. A journalist who treats a legal demand as routine paperwork, or as a matter to handle alone, can cause irreversible harm.

Use this skill when any legal demand for notes, outtakes, drafts, source identity, devices, or testimony arrives, and when designing newsroom readiness for such demands. The main risk is that an agent may not recognize the urgency, may not preserve material correctly, may respond without counsel, or may inadvertently disclose protected information. This skill is editorial guidance only; every legal demand requires immediate qualified legal counsel.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Demand As Urgent And Route It Immediately

Legal demands carry deadlines that can be short, sometimes days or hours. Treat receipt of any subpoena, order, or demand letter as an emergency. Immediately notify legal counsel, the editor responsible for the story, and the outlet's designated response lead. Do not wait, do not attempt to evaluate the merits alone, and do not assume a deadline can be informally extended. Document the date and time of receipt, the method of delivery, and the exact scope of the demand.

### Preserve Material And Avoid Destruction

Once a demand is received or reasonably anticipated, material relevant to it must be preserved. Destroying notes, recordings, drafts, or messages after a demand arrives can constitute obstruction, spoliation, or contempt, and can be used against the outlet and the sources. At the same time, routine, pre-existing retention practices that minimize unnecessary retention are defensible; ad hoc deletion triggered by a demand is not.

Issue a litigation-hold-style instruction to everyone who may hold relevant material, and suspend any routine deletion that would affect it.

### Do Not Respond Or Negotiate Without Counsel

The reporter who receives the demand should not respond, negotiate, or even acknowledge the scope without legal counsel. Casual responses can waive privilege, create admissions, or reveal information that harms sources. Route all communication through counsel, and direct anyone who contacts the reporter about the demand to counsel.

### Identify The Type Of Demand And The Applicable Protection

Different demands carry different weight and different defenses. A grand jury subpoena, a trial subpoena, an administrative order, a search warrant, and a national-security letter each raise distinct legal issues, and shield laws and privilege apply differently to each. Counsel should identify the type, the issuing authority, the jurisdiction, and the realistic defenses, including shield-law coverage, qualified privilege, overbreadth, lack of relevance, and unavailability from other sources.

### Protect Source Identity As The Highest Priority

Where a demand seeks source identity or material that could identify a confidential source, source protection is the central concern. Evaluate whether compliance can be narrowed to avoid identifying sources, whether the shield law or privilege blocks the demand, and whether the outlet is prepared to litigate or accept contempt rather than disclose. Confirm with editors that the outlet will defend the promise of confidentiality.

### Distinguish Published From Unpublished Material

Demands for published material are often weaker, because the information is already public, while demands for unpublished notes, outtakes, and drafts are more dangerous and often more contested. Identify which category each requested item falls into, and resist unpublished material more strongly, citing the chilling effect on newsgathering and the availability of the published record.

### Consider Quashing, Narrowing, Or Challenging The Demand

Many demands can be challenged: motions to quash, motions for protective orders, challenges to overbreadth or relevance, and negotiations for narrowing scope. Counsel should evaluate every available challenge rather than assuming compliance is the only option. Even where compliance is ultimately required, narrowing can protect sources and limit disclosure.

### Prepare The Reporter For Possible Testimony

If testimony cannot be avoided, prepare the reporter with counsel to give as little as legally required, to assert available privileges, and to avoid volunteering information beyond the scope of the order. Rehearse the boundaries of what must be answered and what can be refused.

### Document The Public-Interest And Chilling-Effect Case

Courts balancing demands often weigh the public interest in the journalism and the chilling effect of compelled disclosure. Document the newsworthiness, the harm disclosure would cause to sources and to future reporting, the alternatives the demanding party has, and the importance of source confidentiality to the story. This record supports motions to quash and public-interest objections.

## Common Traps

### Missing Or Ignoring A Deadline

Legal demands have deadlines that courts enforce strictly. Ignoring or delaying can result in default judgments, contempt, or waiver of defenses.

### Destroying Material After Receipt

Deleting notes, recordings, or messages once a demand arrives can be treated as obstruction or spoliation and can create separate liability and credibility damage.

### Responding Without Counsel

A reporter's informal response can waive privilege, create admissions, or reveal source-identifying information. All communication should go through counsel.

### Conflating Demand Types

Treating a search warrant like a subpoena, or an administrative order like a court order, leads to wrong responses. Each demand type has distinct rules and remedies.

### Assuming Compliance Is Inevitable

Many demands can be quashed, narrowed, or challenged. Defaulting to compliance forfeits protections and exposes sources unnecessarily.

### Volunteering More Than Ordered

Even when some testimony or material must be produced, reporters can inadvertently disclose more than required by answering broadly or producing unrequested material.

### Failing To Preserve The Public-Interest Record

Without documented newsworthiness and chilling-effect arguments, motions to quash and public-interest objections are weaker.

### Letting Routine Deletion Continue

Failing to suspend automated deletion after a demand can destroy material and create spoliation issues.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Was the demand routed immediately to legal counsel and senior editors, with the date, time, and scope documented?
- [ ] Has a preservation hold been issued and routine deletion suspended for all relevant material?
- [ ] Has all communication about the demand been handled through counsel, with no unilateral reporter response?
- [ ] Has counsel identified the demand type, issuing authority, jurisdiction, and applicable shield or privilege defenses?
- [ ] Where source identity is at stake, has source protection been treated as the highest priority and the outlet confirmed it will defend confidentiality?
- [ ] Have published and unpublished material been distinguished, with unpublished material resisted more strongly?
- [ ] Have motions to quash, protective orders, and narrowing challenges been evaluated rather than defaulting to compliance?
- [ ] If testimony is unavoidable, is the reporter prepared with counsel to answer only what is legally required?
- [ ] Is the public-interest and chilling-effect case documented to support challenges, and has qualified legal counsel been engaged for every stage, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and not a substitute for legal representation?
