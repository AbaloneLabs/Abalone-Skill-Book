# Reporter Privilege And Confidential Sources

Confidential sourcing is one of the most powerful and most exposed tools in journalism. Reporters promise sources that their identity will be protected, and then rely on reporter's privilege and shield laws to resist legal demands to reveal them. The judgment problem is that the strength of these protections varies enormously by jurisdiction, by court, and by the type of proceeding, and a promise of confidentiality that cannot be defended can expose the source to firing, prosecution, or physical harm. A journalist who grants confidentiality without understanding whether it can be kept is making a promise they may be forced to break.

Use this skill before granting confidentiality, before relying on a shield law to protect unpublished material or source identity, and when responding to subpoenas, court orders, or law-enforcement demands for notes, outtakes, or testimony. The main risk is that an agent may treat "reporter's privilege" as a uniform, reliable protection when it is in fact partial, contested, and jurisdiction-specific. This skill is editorial guidance only; privilege and shield-law questions are legal questions that require qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Know The Jurisdiction's Shield Framework Before Promising Confidentiality

Shield protection varies dramatically. Some jurisdictions have strong statutory shield laws covering confidential and non-confidential material; some have qualified or absolute privilege recognized by courts; some have no shield at all or only narrow common-law protection. Federal courts in some countries recognize only limited or no privilege. The protection also differs by type of proceeding, criminal versus civil, grand jury, administrative, and by whether the material is confidential, unpublished, or already published.

Before granting confidentiality, determine which jurisdiction's law would govern any demand for the source's identity, what protection realistically exists there, and whether the outlet can defend the promise. If protection is weak, weigh whether the source's risk is acceptable and whether the story can be told without the confidential source.

### Make The Promise Explicit And Limited

Ambiguity about confidentiality breeds broken promises and legal exposure. Before a source shares sensitive information, agree explicitly on the ground rules: whether identity is fully confidential, whether the source can be described by role or affiliation, whether the information can be shared with editors, and whether the promise survives the source's death or a legal demand. Do not let confidentiality be implied by context.

Limit the promise to what is necessary. Partial confidentiality, such as describing a source as "a senior official in the agency" without naming them, may serve the story while reducing risk. Avoid blanket promises broader than the reporting requires.

### Confirm The Outlet Can Defend The Promise

A promise of confidentiality is only as strong as the outlet's willingness and ability to defend it. Confirm that editors and legal counsel are prepared to resist subpoenas, to litigate if necessary, and to support the reporter if ordered to comply. If the outlet cannot or will not defend confidentiality to the point of contempt, do not make the promise. A confidential source deserves to know the realistic level of protection before relying on it.

### Treat Unpublished Material As Distinct From Published

Shield laws and privilege often draw a line between published and unpublished material. Published material is generally harder to compel because it is already public, while unpublished notes, outtakes, drafts, and outtakes of interviews may be more vulnerable, especially in criminal proceedings. Some shields protect only confidential sources; others extend to all unpublished material; some protect neither in certain proceedings.

Understand which category each piece of material falls into, and avoid retaining unpublished material that identifies sources unless it is needed. Minimizing retention reduces exposure.

### Document The Confidentiality Basis Without Exposing The Source

Maintain records sufficient to defend the journalism and the confidentiality decision, but do not store identifying information in ways that defeat the protection. Keep notes that explain why anonymity was granted, what the source knows, and how the information was verified, without creating a roadmap to identity that a subpoena could capture. Store sensitive source identifiers securely and separately, and limit access to those who need it.

### Plan For Subpoenas And Court Orders In Advance

Subpoenas and court orders for source identity or unpublished material often arrive with little warning and tight deadlines. Have a response plan: who is notified, who engages counsel, how material is preserved, and how the reporter is supported. Do not destroy material in response to a demand, which can constitute obstruction or spoliation, but do ensure routine retention practices limit what exists to be demanded.

When a demand arrives, do not respond unilaterally. Route it immediately to legal counsel and senior editors.

### Weigh The Public Interest Against Compelled Disclosure

When courts apply a balancing test, the strength of the public interest in the story and in source protection matters. Document the newsworthiness, the harm the source would face, the alternatives exhausted, and the chilling effect disclosure would have on future reporting. Strong, well-documented public-interest justifications strengthen resistance to compelled disclosure.

### Recognize Limits And Plan For The Worst Case

Even strong shields have limits. Some proceedings, such as grand jury investigations or criminal trials where the information is critical and unavailable elsewhere, can override privilege. A reporter may face contempt sanctions, including jail time or fines, for refusing to comply. Understand the realistic worst case before granting confidentiality, and ensure the source and the outlet accept that risk.

## Common Traps

### Granting Confidentiality Without Checking The Shield

Promising anonymity before knowing whether the jurisdiction protects it can create an undefendable promise. Determine the protection first.

### Vague Or Implied Confidentiality

Allowing a source to believe they are confidential without an explicit agreement leads to disputes about what was promised and can break trust.

### Overbroad Promises

Promising more than the story requires, such as never publishing any detail that could lead to the source, creates unnecessary legal and ethical exposure.

### Assuming Federal And State Protection Are The Same

State shields often do not apply in federal court, and federal privilege is limited. A source protected in state court may be exposed in federal proceedings.

### Retaining Identifying Unpublished Material

Keeping notes, recordings, or drafts that identify confidential sources creates material that a subpoena can demand. Minimize and secure such material.

### Responding To A Subpoena Alone

A reporter who tries to negotiate or respond to a legal demand without counsel can inadvertently waive privilege or create evidence against the source.

### Destroying Material After A Demand

Destroying notes or recordings once a subpoena arrives can constitute obstruction or spoliation and can damage the outlet's credibility and legal position.

### Believing Contempt Cannot Happen

Reporters have been jailed for refusing to identify sources. Treat the worst case as real when deciding whether to promise confidentiality.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Before granting confidentiality, did I determine which jurisdiction's shield or privilege framework applies and how strong the protection realistically is?
- [ ] Is the confidentiality promise explicit, limited to what the story requires, and agreed before sensitive information was shared?
- [ ] Have I confirmed that the outlet and legal counsel are prepared to defend the promise, including through litigation if necessary?
- [ ] Do I understand which material is published versus unpublished, and how that distinction affects protection in the relevant jurisdiction?
- [ ] Are records of the confidentiality basis maintained without creating a roadmap to source identity, and is identifying material stored securely with limited access?
- [ ] Is there a plan for responding to subpoenas or court orders, including immediate notification of counsel and senior editors?
- [ ] Have I documented the public-interest justification to support resistance to compelled disclosure?
- [ ] Have I honestly assessed the worst case, including contempt sanctions, and confirmed the source and outlet accept that risk?
- [ ] For any actual or threatened legal demand, have I engaged qualified legal counsel immediately, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and not a substitute for legal representation?
