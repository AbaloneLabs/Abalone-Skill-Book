# Ending And Resolution

Endings are what readers remember. A strong ending crystallizes significance, resonates, and sends the reader away understanding why the story mattered; a weak one trails off, overreaches, or imposes false closure. The harm this skill prevents is the failure of conclusion: the story that simply stops, the ending that manufactures a resolution the facts do not support, or the kicker that undercuts or contradicts the reporting. Crafting an ending is a discipline of recognizing what kind of resolution the story honestly supports, and delivering closure, or honest openness, in a way that leaves readers both satisfied and accurately informed.

## Core Rules

### Match the ending to the story's true state of resolution
Stories end differently. A completed investigation may support a definitive conclusion; an ongoing saga may require acknowledgment of what remains unresolved; a profile may close on an image that captures the subject. Choose an ending form that reflects the actual state of the story. Forcing a neat resolution onto an unresolved situation, or leaving a resolvable story dangling, both mislead readers about where things stand.

### Provide honest closure rather than false finality
Readers want a sense of arrival, but real news often lacks neat endings. Offer the closure the facts support: a clear conclusion where the evidence warrants one, an honest statement of what remains unknown where it does not. Acknowledging that a story is ongoing or its outcome uncertain is a valid and often more credible ending than a manufactured sense of completion.

### Crystallize significance without overstating
A good ending returns the reader to why the story matters, distilling its significance in a way that resonates. This crystallization should reflect the actual weight of the reporting, not inflate it. An ending that overstates the story's importance sets up a credibility gap readers will sense.

### Use the kicker deliberately
The final line carries disproportionate weight; readers remember it. Choose a kicker that earns its prominence, a resonant quote, a telling detail, a forward-looking note, a return to an opening image, rather than settling for the last thing you happened to write. But ensure the kicker is honest and representative, not merely dramatic.

### Avoid moralizing and editorializing beyond the evidence
Endings are tempting places for the writer to step in and deliver a verdict or lesson. Resist drawing conclusions the reporting does not establish. Let the significance emerge from the facts and the structure; where you offer analysis, keep it grounded in evidence and clearly distinguished from assertion.

### Connect to the opening where it serves the story
Returning to an image, character, or question from the opening can create a satisfying sense of structure and completeness. Use this technique where it illuminates, but avoid mechanical or forced returns that feel artificial. The connection should deepen meaning, not merely demonstrate craft.

### Look forward honestly
Many news stories point toward consequences, next steps, or unresolved questions. A forward-looking ending can be powerful, but it must be grounded in what is genuinely known or reasonably anticipated, not speculation presented as prediction. Flag what to watch for without pretending to know what will happen.

### Revise the ending as deliberately as the opening
Like the opening, the ending deserves focused revision. First-draft endings often trail off or overreach. Return to the ending with the full story in mind and shape it to deliver the resonance and accuracy the piece deserves.

## Common Traps

### The story that just stops
An ending that merely runs out of material, with no sense of conclusion or significance, leaves readers unsatisfied and under-informed about why the story mattered. Deliver a deliberate ending.

### False closure
Imposing a neat resolution on a story that is genuinely unresolved misleads readers about the state of affairs. Honest openness about what remains unknown is more credible than manufactured finality.

### Overstating significance
An ending that inflates the story's importance beyond what the reporting supports creates a credibility gap. Crystallize the real significance, not an inflated version.

### The unearned editorial verdict
Stepping in at the end to deliver a moral or judgment the reporting did not establish turns reporting into editorializing. Keep conclusions grounded in evidence.

### The mismatched kicker
A dramatic or clever final line that does not genuinely represent the story feels forced and can undercut the reporting. Choose a kicker that is resonant and honest.

### Speculation presented as foresight
Forward-looking endings that predict specific outcomes go beyond what journalism can responsibly claim. Frame futures as questions to watch, not as predictions.

### Neglecting the ending in revision
Treating the ending as fixed after the first draft, while revising the rest, leaves a weak conclusion on an otherwise polished piece. Revise the ending with the same care as the opening.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the ending reflect the story's actual state of resolution, rather than imposing false closure or leaving a resolvable story dangling?
- [ ] Have I offered honest closure, acknowledging what remains unresolved where that is the truth?
- [ ] Does the ending crystallize the story's genuine significance without overstating it?
- [ ] Is the kicker chosen deliberately, and is it honest and representative rather than merely dramatic?
- [ ] Have I avoided moralizing or editorializing beyond what the reporting establishes?
- [ ] If the ending returns to the opening, does the connection deepen meaning rather than feel mechanical?
- [ ] If the ending looks forward, is it grounded in what is known rather than presented as prediction?
- [ ] Did I revise the ending as deliberately as the opening, with the full story in mind?
- [ ] Does the ending leave readers both satisfied and accurately informed about where things stand?
- [ ] Would a reader finishing the story feel the ending was earned by the reporting that preceded it?
