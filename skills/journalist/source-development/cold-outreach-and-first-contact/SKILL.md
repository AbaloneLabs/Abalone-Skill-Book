# Cold Outreach And First Contact

First contact is where most sourcing relationships are won or lost before any real reporting begins. A cold approach is not a request for a quote; it is a request for trust from someone who has no reason to give it and every reason to be cautious. The judgment problem is that reporters under deadline pressure default to blunt, extractive outreach that reads as suspicious to the very people whose cooperation matters most, while over-promising warmth or downplaying the scrutiny ahead corrupts the relationship from the first message. Good first contact is honest, specific, respectful of the source's risk, and designed so that the source can make an informed decision about whether and how to engage.

Use this skill when composing a cold message, planning an approach to a wary insider or affected person, deciding what to disclose about the story, or handling the first conversation with someone who may become a long-term source. The main risk is that an agent optimizes for a fast reply rather than for a relationship that can sustain truthful reporting.

## Core Rules

### Be Honest About Who You Are And What You Are Reporting

Identify yourself and your outlet clearly in the first contact. State the subject of your reporting specifically enough that the source understands what they would be engaging with, without overloading them. Do not disguise your identity, omit your employer, or vaguely hint at a topic to lure someone into a conversation they would avoid if they knew the full scope. Deception in first contact is reserved for rare, editor-approved investigations where the public interest overrides the default of honesty; it is never a convenience tactic.

If the story is sensitive or critical of the source's institution, do not hide that, but do not lead with an accusation either. A clear, neutral statement of what you are examining, what you hope to understand from them, and why their perspective matters gives the source the information needed to decide whether to talk.

### Make The Ask Specific And The Burden Low

Vague requests ("can we talk about the agency?") force the source to do the work of figuring out what you want, which usually results in no reply. Specific requests ("I'm reporting on the 2024 procurement contract with Vendor X and would value your perspective on how the bidding was structured") let the source assess quickly whether they have something to offer and whether it is safe to offer it.

Offer flexibility in how and when to engage: a short call, a written response, an in-person meeting, encrypted messaging. Let the source choose the channel that fits their risk. For sources in vulnerable positions, the safest channel may be one the reporter does not normally use; accommodate it.

### Disclose The Stakes So The Source Can Assess Risk

A source approached cold often cannot judge the risk of talking. They may not know whether their institution monitors communications, whether speaking to press violates their employment, whether they could face legal exposure, or whether being named would endanger them. Where you are aware of such risks, surface them honestly rather than letting the source discover them after the fact.

For routine sources this may be a brief acknowledgment. For insiders, whistleblowers, people in authoritarian contexts, or employees under nondisclosure agreements, it is an ethical obligation to help them understand the stakes before they share anything sensitive. Do not promise protections you cannot guarantee; instead, explain the options, including speaking on background, off the record, or anonymously, and what each means in your newsroom.

### Respect Silence And Refusal

Not every source will, or should, talk to you, and a refusal is information, not an obstacle to overcome. If someone does not respond, follow up once or twice with genuine new context rather than pressure, and then stop. If someone declines, accept it unless there is an overriding public-interest reason to pursue them, and even then pursue the story through other means rather than harassing the person.

Aggressive or repeated contact toward a reluctant source can amount to harassment, can expose the source to suspicion, and damages the reputation that makes future outreach succeed. The same applies to contacting people in vulnerable moments, grieving families, victims, or people in custody: default to restraint and let humanity outrank the story.

### Establish Ground Rules Before Sensitive Information Is Shared

The first conversation is when attribution expectations should be set. State whether the conversation is on the record by default in your newsroom, and offer the source the chance to request background, deep background, or off-the-record treatment before they say anything they would not want published. Do not let a source believe they were speaking off the record when you believed otherwise.

If a source begins to share sensitive material, pause and confirm the ground rules explicitly: "Before you go further, I want to make sure we're clear, is this on the record?" This protects both parties and prevents the bitterness that follows a misunderstood arrangement. See the off-the-record and confidential-sources skills for the operational definitions.

### Lead With Curiosity, Not A Thesis

A cold contact that telegraphs a conclusion, "I know your agency covered up X and I want your comment", invites a defensive or scripted response. A contact that leads with genuine questions, "I'm trying to understand how decisions about X were made and where you fit in that process", invites a more useful conversation. You can still hold institutions accountable; doing so does not require front-loading accusation in the first message.

Curiosity is not naivety. You should still have done your homework, read the available documents, and identified the gaps only this source can fill. The source will respect that you have prepared, and preparation lets you ask precise questions rather than generic ones.

### Keep Records Of Outreach

Document your outreach attempts: when, through what channel, what you said, and whether there was a response. This matters for fairness, so you can accurately report that a subject was contacted and given a chance to respond, and for your own protection if a source later disputes what was offered or agreed. Keep these records securely, especially where they reveal source identities.

## Common Traps

### Bait-And-Switch Outreach

Promising a friendly or neutral conversation to secure a meeting, then ambushing the source with criticism, destroys trust not only with that source but across the network, because people talk. If the story is critical, be honest about its direction while still inviting the source's perspective. A subject who feels deceived will not engage substantively and may warn others off.

### Overpromising Anonymity Or Protection To Get A Meeting

Telling a wary source "don't worry, you'll be totally anonymous" to lower their guard can endanger them if the reality of your newsroom's standards, or the legal environment, does not match the promise. Grant anonymity and protection deliberately, with editor approval where required, and only after understanding what you can actually deliver. See the confidential-sources skill.

### Treating Non-Response As Confirmation Of Guilt

Silence is not an admission. A source may not respond because they are busy, constrained, afraid, or simply unwilling, none of which establishes anything about the facts. If a subject does not respond, report that they did not respond by deadline; do not imply guilt from silence.

### Contacting Vulnerable People Without Care

Cold-contacting victims, minors, grieving families, or people in unsafe situations without considering the harm of the contact itself is a recurring ethical failure. Slow down, consider whether the contact is justified by public interest, offer the person control over whether and how to engage, and accept that some stories should not be pursued through certain people at all.

### Using Pressure Tactics That Mimic Harassment

Repeated calls, showing up unannounced at homes or workplaces, contacting family members, or threatening exposure can cross from journalism into harassment. These tactics may be defensible in narrow investigative contexts with senior editorial approval, but as default outreach habits they are unethical, often counterproductive, and legally risky.

## Self-Check

Before treating a cold outreach or first contact as sound, verify:

- The first message identifies the reporter, outlet, and reporting subject honestly, without disguise or misleading vagueness.
- The ask is specific enough that the source can quickly judge relevance and safety, and the burden of channel and timing is placed on the reporter, not the source.
- The stakes of engaging, including any known risks to the source, were surfaced before sensitive information was shared.
- Silence or refusal was respected, with at most one or two genuine follow-ups and no harassment.
- Attribution ground rules were stated and confirmed before the source disclosed anything they would not want published.
- The outreach led with prepared, specific curiosity rather than a front-loaded accusation or thesis.
- Outreach attempts were documented securely, including channel, content, and response, for fairness and protection.
- No anonymity or protection promise exceeded what the reporter and newsroom can actually guarantee; high-risk assurances were cleared with an editor.
- Vulnerable people were approached with restraint proportionate to the harm of contact, and some were not contacted at all where the public interest did not justify it.
- First-contact decisions support editorial judgment; sensitive approaches, especially to vulnerable or high-risk sources, should involve a responsible editor, and legal counsel where exposure is significant.
