# Beat Source Roster Management

A beat is only as strong as the roster behind it. A roster is not a contact list dumped into a phone; it is a living, judged, secured record of who knows what, how reliable they have proven, how they should be contacted, and what story each voice is genuinely suited to. The judgment problem is that rosters decay silently: people change jobs, become compromised, lose access, or drift into advocacy, and a reporter who does not actively manage the roster will keep reaching for voices that no longer fit, producing coverage that is stale, distorted, or dependent on the same handful of convenient names.

Use this skill when standing up a new beat, auditing an inherited roster, deciding which sources to approach for a specific story, or maintaining a database of contacts over time. The main risk is that an agent treats the roster as a static address book rather than as a judged, secured, evolving tool whose composition shapes every story the beat produces.

## Core Rules

### Build The Roster Around Knowledge, Not Convenience

Convenient sources, the ones who answer fast and give clean quotes, tend to dominate rosters by default. But convenience is not knowledge. Organize the roster by what each source actually knows from direct experience: which agency decisions they witnessed, which documents they can interpret, which communities they belong to, which technical domains they have worked in. A source who is slow to respond but has peerless knowledge of procurement records is more valuable than a spokesperson who replies in ten minutes with talking points.

For each entry, record the domains where the source is strong, the domains where they are weak or biased, and the kinds of claims they should not be relied on for. This lets you reach for the right voice per question rather than defaulting to whoever picks up.

### Track Reliability As A Track Record, Not A Feeling

Reliability is earned over many interactions and should be recorded, not remembered vaguely. After each meaningful interaction, note whether the source's information held up when checked against documents, subsequent events, or other sources. Track patterns: a source who is reliably accurate about budgets but repeatedly wrong about personnel decisions is a differentiated tool, not a uniformly reliable one.

Avoid the trap of rating sources on a single gut "credibility" score. Break reliability down by subject, by time horizon (immediate facts versus predictions), and by motive. A source whose predictions are often wrong may still be excellent on documented facts. Record enough that a colleague inheriting the beat could use the source intelligently without relearning everything.

### Rotate Voices To Avoid Roster Capture

Every beat has a tendency to ossify around a few familiar voices. If the same three names appear in story after story, readers stop hearing the beat and start hearing those three people, whose biases become the beat's frame. Actively rotate. For each story, ask whether a less-used source could speak to the question as well or better, and whether the familiar voices are there because they are the best fit or because they are easy.

Rotation also protects the sources. A source quoted in every story becomes a public target, may face retaliation, and may lose the inside position that made them useful. Spreading attribution across a wider roster distributes risk and keeps any single voice from becoming indispensable.

### Maintain Diversity Of Perspective And Position

Audit the roster for diversity across hierarchy, geography, community, political view, and institutional role. A roster drawn only from senior officials, only from one political party, only from one neighborhood, or only from people who return calls will produce coverage that reflects those limits even when each individual source is honest. Include junior staff, administrative workers, contractors, retirees, community members, regulators, academics, and affected residents.

Diversity is not decorative. It is the correction for the structural blind spot that any single network carries. When a roster is diverse, contradictions surface that a homogeneous roster would never reveal.

### Secure The Roster And Its Notes

A beat roster, especially one containing confidential or sensitive sources, is a target. Store it securely, separate notes about source identity from notes about story content where possible, and apply your newsroom's security standards. Consider what a subpoena, a leak, a lost device, or a hacked account would expose. For sources whose exposure would endanger them, hold the minimum identifying detail necessary and keep it off networked systems where feasible.

This is not paranoia. Reporters have had notes and contact lists seized or exposed, with consequences for sources. Treat roster security as part of the obligation you owe the people who talk to you. Consult the digital-security and source-protection skills for the operational details.

### Keep The Roster Current Through Active Pruning And Refreshing

Rosters rot. People change jobs, retire, get promoted into roles where they can no longer speak freely, take positions that create conflicts, or simply become less reliable. Schedule periodic reviews. For each entry, ask: is this person still in a position to know what I have them down for? Have their circumstances changed in a way that affects their independence or their safety? Should they be reclassified, retired from the active roster, or replaced?

Refresh continuously. Add new voices encountered through reporting, conferences, community meetings, document bylines, and referrals from trusted sources. A roster that is not growing is shrinking in practice, because the beat is always changing.

### Record Contact Preferences And Constraints

Record how each source prefers to be contacted, when they can be reached, whether they will speak on the record or only on background, and any constraints such as needing approval from a communications office. Respecting these preferences is both ethical and practical: a source who is contacted the wrong way at the wrong time may stop talking altogether.

Note also any sensitivities: a source who is worried about being seen talking to press, a source in a country where contact itself is risky, a source who has asked for specific protections. Building these into the roster prevents careless mistakes during deadline pressure.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To The Fastest Responders

The sources who reply fastest are often those with the least to lose and the most agenda. A roster driven by response speed drifts toward professional commentators, spokespeople, and self-promoters, while the knowledgeable insiders who need time to think, check, and weigh risk get crowded out. Build in patience and reach the slower voices before deadline closes the window.

### Confusing Volume With Value

A source who is always available and always quotable can feel essential but may be adding confirmation rather than knowledge. Three stories that all rest on the same gregarious source are weaker, not stronger, than three stories that draw on different expert voices. Measure value by what only this source can provide.

### Letting The Roster Become Public Through Repetition

If the same names appear constantly, competitors, subjects, and hostile actors can map your sourcing. This can expose confidential patterns, invite pressure on your sources, and let institutions pre-empt your reporting. Rotation and breadth are protective, not just editorially sound.

### Failing To Reassess After A Source Changes Role

A source who was an independent insider last year may this year be a paid consultant to the industry they once critiqued. If you do not reassess, you will keep using them as if nothing changed, and your coverage will inherit a conflict you never disclosed. Tie reassessment to role changes, new jobs, and public shifts in stance.

### Storing Sensitive Rosters Insecurely Out Of Convenience

The convenience of a synced contact app or a shared cloud spreadsheet can override security judgment, especially under deadline. A single breach can compromise an entire network of sources. Default to the most secure storage your newsroom supports for anything identifying sensitive sources, and consult security staff or editors for high-risk beats.

## Self-Check

Before treating a beat roster as well managed, verify:

- Each entry records the source's domains of strength, domains of weakness or bias, and the kinds of claims they should and should not be relied on for.
- Reliability is tracked as a subject-specific, time-horizon-specific record, not a single gut score, and is updated after meaningful interactions.
- Voices are rotated across stories so no small set of convenient names dominates the beat, and less-used sources are actively considered for each story.
- The roster is diverse across hierarchy, community, geography, political view, and institutional role, and the diversity is treated as a structural correction, not decoration.
- The roster and its notes are stored securely, with sensitive identifying detail minimized and held off vulnerable systems where feasible.
- The roster is pruned and refreshed on a schedule, with role changes, conflicts, and declining reliability triggering reclassification.
- Contact preferences, availability, attribution constraints, and sensitivities are recorded and respected, including for sources in risky contexts.
- No source is being overused to the point of becoming a public target or exposing the beat's sourcing patterns.
- Security and protection choices for the roster were reviewed against the risk a subpoena, leak, lost device, or breach would pose to sources.
- Roster management supports editorial judgment; sensitive handling, secure storage decisions, and high-risk source inclusion should involve a responsible editor, and security or legal specialists where exposure is significant.
