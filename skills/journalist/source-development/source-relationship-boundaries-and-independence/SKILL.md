# Source Relationship Boundaries and Independence

Sources are not interchangeable inputs. The most valuable ones are cultivated over years, and closeness is often what produces the information. But the same closeness that generates access also generates obligation, bias, and vulnerability to manipulation. Reporters who treat source relationships as purely transactional miss stories; reporters who let relationships become unbounded lose the independence that makes their reporting credible.

The judgment problem is that the line between legitimate cultivation and compromised dependence is rarely announced. It is crossed gradually, through accumulated small favors, shared confidences, and reciprocal expectations, until the reporter can no longer report critically on the source without feeling, or appearing, disloyal. Managing this requires deliberate boundaries, not just good intentions.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Access and Independence Are in Tension

Every gift, meal, favor, exclusive, friendship gesture, or shared confidence creates a reciprocal pull. This is not a moral failing; it is how human relationships work. The discipline is to recognize the tension explicitly and manage it, rather than pretending access is free. Ask of every relationship:

- What does this source gain by talking to me, beyond the public interest?
- What am I receiving, materially or emotionally, that creates expectation?
- Could I publish a hard, critical story about this source tomorrow without hesitation?

If the answer to the last question is no, the relationship has already affected the reporting, whether or not a critical story is pending.

### Set and Document Your Own Boundaries in Advance

Boundaries decided in the moment, under social pressure, tend to erode. Decide in advance, ideally in writing or in conversation with an editor, what you will and will not accept:

- Meals, drinks, travel, lodging, gifts, discounts, and event access.
- Off-the-record socializing and the circumstances under which you will attend.
- Personal favors, loans, introductions, or reciprocal disclosures.
- Romantic or financial relationships with anyone in your coverage area.

Documenting your own standards in advance gives you a reference point when a source presses, and protects you from drift.

### Apply the Conflict-of-Interest Test Seriously

A conflict of interest exists whenever your judgment could reasonably appear to be affected, regardless of whether you believe it is. The test is not whether you feel compromised; it is whether a reasonable reader, knowing the facts, would trust your independence. Apply it to:

- Financial interests, including investments, employment, and family interests in subjects you cover.
- Personal relationships, including family, friendship, romance, and enmity.
- Prior employment, consulting, or advocacy involving the subject.
- Political activity, donations, and public statements.

When a conflict exists, disclose it, recuse yourself, or reassign the story. Do not rely on your own sense of incorruptibility.

### Separate Friendship, Romance, and Coverage

Romantic or close personal relationships with sources or subjects are among the most damaging conflicts because they are hardest to disclose and hardest to end. The safest standard is to avoid them entirely within your coverage area. If one exists or develops:

- Disclose it to your editor immediately.
- Recuse yourself from coverage of that person and their interests.
- Do not assume secrecy is possible or sustainable.

Friendships that predate coverage, or that develop through a beat, require the same scrutiny. A friend is not an independent source on themselves.

### Handle Exclusives and Embargoes as Access Currency

Exclusives, embargoes, leaks, and special access are forms of currency that sources use to shape coverage. They are not inherently corrupting, but they create obligation and can be used to reward favorable coverage and punish critical reporting. When offered:

- Ask why you are being offered this and not others.
- Assess whether acceptance creates expectation of favorable treatment.
- Maintain the right to report critically regardless of the access granted.
- Be transparent with readers about access arrangements where relevant.

Access that comes with implicit conditions is not access; it is a transaction dressed as a favor.

### Resist Becoming a Conduit for a Source's Agenda

Skilled sources use reporters to plant narratives, damage rivals, test messages, or shape public perception. A leak that fits your priors is especially dangerous because it feels like confirmation. Before publishing sourced material:

- Ask what interest the source has in this information becoming public.
- Seek independent corroboration beyond the motivated source.
- Consider whether you are being used to launder an unverified or self-serving claim.
- Represent the source's possible motivation to readers where relevant.

Being used is not always avoidable, but being used unknowingly is a failure.

### Maintain the Right and Ability to Report Critically

The ultimate test of a source relationship is whether you can still report critically on that source. Preserve this by:

- Not making promises of favorable coverage, explicit or implicit.
- Not sharing unpublished reporting, competitor information, or editorial plans.
- Keeping relationships professional enough that distance is possible.
- Periodically stress-testing the relationship by imagining a critical story.

If you cannot imagine publishing a true, well-sourced critical story about a source, the relationship has captured the reporting.

### Disclose Relationships to Editors and Readers

Internal disclosure to editors is the minimum; it ensures someone else can assess the conflict. Reader disclosure is required when a relationship could reasonably affect perception of the story's independence. Err toward more disclosure when the relationship is close, ongoing, financial, or personal. Concealment that is later discovered destroys credibility far more than the disclosure would have.

### Manage Departure and Estrangement Honestly

Relationships end, sour, or become conflicts. When a source becomes a subject of critical coverage, or when a relationship turns adversarial:

- Do not let personal loyalty soften or harden the reporting unjustly.
- Disclose the prior relationship if it is relevant to the story.
- Hand off coverage if your judgment is compromised.
- Protect sources from retaliation unrelated to the journalism.

Estrangement does not license score-settling any more than friendship licenses protection.

## Common Traps

### Drift Through Accumulated Small Favors

No single meal or gift seems to matter, but accumulated reciprocity creates real obligation. Track the pattern, not just each instance, and notice when a source's favors have become routine.

### Believing You Are Incorruptible

The most dangerous reporters are those certain they cannot be influenced. Confidence in your own objectivity is itself a risk factor, because it lowers vigilance. Assume you are influenceable and build safeguards accordingly.

### Letting Access Become Dependency

When one source provides most of your scoops, you become dependent on them and unable to offend them. Diversify your source base so that no single relationship is load-bearing.

### Confusing Social Warmth With Professional Closeness

Sources cultivate social warmth strategically. A friendly dinner is not a friendship, and treating it as one blurs the boundary. Keep social occasions professional and bounded.

### Accepting Access With Implicit Conditions

Access offered with an expectation of favorable coverage is a transaction. If you cannot report critically, you do not have access; you have a role in their communications operation.

### Concealing Relationships That Later Surface

Undisclosed relationships that are discovered later destroy credibility more thoroughly than the relationship itself. Disclose proactively, especially when the relationship is close or financial.

### Being Used to Launder a Source's Claim

A motivated leak that fits your assumptions still needs independent corroboration. Publishing a self-serving claim because it came from a trusted source is being used, not being well-sourced.

### Letting Personal Loyalty Distort Critical Coverage

When a source becomes the subject of a true critical story, friendship must not soften it, and enmity must not harden it. Recuse or hand off if you cannot be fair.

## Self-Check

- Could I publish a true, well-sourced, critical story about this source tomorrow without hesitation? If not, what has changed the answer?
- Have I set and documented my own boundaries in advance regarding gifts, meals, travel, favors, and personal relationships, rather than deciding under pressure?
- Applying the reasonable-reader test, do I have any financial, personal, prior-employment, or political conflicts that require disclosure or recusal?
- Am I maintaining romantic and close personal relationships outside my coverage area, and disclosing and recusing where they exist within it?
- When offered exclusives, embargoes, or special access, have I assessed the implicit conditions and preserved the right to report critically?
- Have I sought independent corroboration for material from motivated sources, rather than laundering their claims because they fit my priors?
- Am I diversifying my source base so that no single relationship is load-bearing or dependency-creating?
- Have I disclosed relevant relationships to my editor, and to readers where a reasonable reader would want to know?
- Am I stress-testing the relationship periodically by imagining a critical story and checking my own reluctance?
- If a relationship has soured or the source is now a subject, am I avoiding both unjust softening and score-settling?
