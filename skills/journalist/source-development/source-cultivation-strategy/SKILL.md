# Source Cultivation Strategy

Source cultivation is the deliberate, ethical work of building relationships with people who can provide reliable information over time. It is not transactional collecting of phone numbers, and it is not flattery aimed at extracting a single quote. A cultivated source is someone who has come to trust a reporter's fairness, accuracy, and discretion enough to share what they know, including when it is uncomfortable. The judgment problem is that cultivation is slow, easy to corrupt, and easy to neglect: a reporter can spend months building access that turns out to be captured by an institution, or can burn a decade of trust with one careless detail in a story.

Use this skill when planning beat coverage, deciding how to open a relationship with a new potential source, maintaining a roster of contacts, or handing a beat to a colleague. The main risk is that an agent treats sources as interchangeable quote-providers rather than as human relationships with reciprocal obligations, power dynamics, and limits.

## Core Rules

### Treat Cultivation As A Long Game, Not A Single Pitch

Sources are built across many interactions, most of which produce no immediate story. The strongest sourcing networks come from sustained presence: showing up to routine meetings, reading what an institution publishes, remembering details from one conversation to the next, and being reachable when a source needs to talk. A reporter who only calls when they need something trains sources to be guarded.

Plan cultivation with a horizon of months and years. For each beat or institution, identify who the knowledgeable insiders are, who the credible outside observers are, and who the affected people are. Then maintain light, regular contact that is not extractive. A brief check-in, a shared relevant article, or a question that does not require a story-worthy answer keeps the relationship warm without demanding payment in information.

### Open New Relationships With Honesty About Your Role

When approaching a potential source for the first time, be clear about who you are, what outlet you work for, and what you are reporting on. Do not misrepresent your identity, your employer, or your intent in order to gain access. Undercover methods are reserved for rare investigations where the deception itself is justified by an overriding public interest and approved at a senior editorial level; they are not a default cultivation tactic.

Explain the ground rules of the conversation early. If the conversation is on the record, say so. If you are willing to consider background or off-the-record treatment, explain what those terms mean in your newsroom and let the source choose. Do not let ambiguity about attribution fester, because a source who believes they were speaking off the record when you believed otherwise is a source who will feel betrayed when published.

### Map Sources By Knowledge, Access, And Independence

Not every contact is useful for every story. Maintain a mental or secure roster that classifies sources along three axes: what they have direct knowledge of, what institutional access they have, and how independent they are from the subject of your reporting. A source inside an agency has access but may be constrained by loyalty, fear, or talking points. A former employee has independence but may be operating from grievance. A regulator or auditor has knowledge but a institutional perspective of their own.

For any given claim, ask which of your sources is best positioned to confirm, deny, or contextualize it, and which sources would be compromised by being asked. Do not go to the same two or three convenient voices for every story; that produces a distorted beat where the same framing recurs.

### Reciprocate Without Compromising Independence

Relationships involve reciprocity, but the reciprocity must never become a quid pro quo that compromises coverage. Acceptable reciprocity includes treating sources with respect, listening to their concerns even when not newsworthy, correcting errors promptly, and protecting genuinely vulnerable sources. Unacceptable reciprocity includes killing stories to preserve access, softening criticism to keep a source friendly, trading favorable coverage for tips, or accepting gifts, meals, travel, or favors that create a sense of obligation.

When in doubt about whether a courtesy has crossed into compromise, ask whether you would describe the arrangement to your editor and your audience. If the arrangement would be embarrassing to disclose, it is already a problem. Newsrooms vary on rules about meals and gifts; follow the stricter of your outlet's policy and your own conscience.

### Maintain Diversity Across The Network

A sourcing network that draws from the same demographic, professional, or ideological pool produces a narrow beat. Actively cultivate sources across hierarchy levels, communities, political views, and institutional roles. Junior employees, administrative staff, contractors, community organizers, retirees, and people outside elite networks often know things that senior officials and official spokespeople do not, or will not, share.

Audit your own roster periodically. If every source on a beat looks the same, attended the same schools, or lives in the same neighborhoods, the coverage will reflect those blind spots even if no individual source is wrong. Diversity is not a box to tick; it is a correction for the structural limits of any single network.

### Document And Hand Off Relationships Ethically

When you leave a beat, the relationships you built do not belong to you personally; they belong to the newsroom's capacity to cover the subject. Prepare a handoff that introduces your successor to key sources where appropriate, with the source's consent. Do not hand over the identities of confidential sources without their explicit permission.

Maintain notes on each source's preferences: how they like to be contacted, what their institutional constraints are, what subjects they are strong on, and what subjects they should not be relied on for. This protects the source, the reporter, and the accuracy of future stories.

### Recognize When A Source Has Been Captured

Some sources, over time, become extensions of the institutions they once provided independent insight on. A former insider may take a consulting role, sign an NDA, join a trade group, or simply become invested in defending their old decisions. Reassess sources periodically. If a source's reliability has declined or their framing has shifted toward advocacy, adjust how you use them and, where relevant, tell readers about the change in their position.

## Common Traps

### Mistaking Access For Truth

An official who returns your calls promptly and gives you good quotes is not necessarily telling you the truth; they may be managing you. Access is valuable, but a source who is always available and always on-message is often a channel for an institution's preferred narrative. Corroborate access-rich sources against documents, data, and independent voices, and notice when a source's usefulness to you aligns suspiciously with their usefulness to their employer.

### Becoming Identified With One Faction

If a beat is contentious, sources may try to recruit you as an ally. A reporter who is seen as sympathetic to one faction will receive leaks and tips from that faction and silence from the other. Over time this narrows the reporting and damages credibility. Maintain relationships across factions and be willing to report things that displease your most generous sources.

### Burning A Source Through Careless Detail

Even when a source is named, specific phrasings, unique documents, or precise timing in a story can reveal which insider provided other information. Before publishing, read every detail from the perspective of someone inside the institution trying to identify the leaker. This is a recurring trap because the detail that makes a story vivid is often the detail that identifies the source.

### Neglecting Maintenance Between Stories

Sources who only hear from you during a crisis learn to be guarded. A network that is not maintained between major stories collapses precisely when you need it most. Build in low-stakes contact that does not demand information.

### Promising Protection You Cannot Guarantee

Telling a source "I will protect you" without understanding the legal and operational limits of that promise can endanger them. You may face a subpoena, a search warrant, or surveillance that you cannot prevent. Be honest about the limits of your protection and consult editors and, where appropriate, counsel before making assurances. See the source-protection and confidential-sources skills for the operational details.

## Self-Check

Before treating source cultivation as sound, verify:

- The relationship was opened with honest disclosure of identity, outlet, and reporting purpose, with attribution ground rules stated up front.
- The network includes sources across hierarchy, community, political view, and institutional role, not just the most accessible insiders.
- Reciprocity has not crossed into compromise: no story was softened, killed, or shaped in exchange for access, and any gifts or courtesies would withstand disclosure to editor and audience.
- Each source is classified by direct knowledge, institutional access, and independence, and is used for stories where those attributes fit rather than as a default voice.
- Maintenance contact occurs between stories so the network does not collapse during gaps.
- Sources have been reassessed for capture, declining reliability, or changed institutional position, and usage adjusted accordingly.
- Handoff notes exist and do not expose confidential source identities without explicit consent.
- Every published detail has been reviewed from the perspective of an insider trying to identify the source.
- No protection promise exceeds what the reporter and newsroom can actually guarantee; high-risk assurances were cleared with an editor and, where stakes require, legal counsel before being made.
- This guidance supports editorial judgment; final decisions on sensitive cultivation, access, and protection should involve a responsible editor, and legal counsel where exposure is significant.
