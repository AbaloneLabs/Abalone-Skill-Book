# Diverse and Underrepresented Source Recruitment

Source diversity is not a cosmetic addition to a finished story. It determines whose knowledge counts as evidence, whose experience frames the problem, and which communities see themselves represented accurately. Reporters who default to official sources, English-speaking elites, and people who return calls quickly produce stories that are systematically skewed toward the powerful, even when the reporting is conscientious within that narrow pool.

The judgment problem is that the easiest sources to reach are rarely the most representative, and the communities most affected by a story are often the hardest to reach safely and credibly. Building a diverse source roster is a structural discipline, not a gesture, and it must be done without tokenizing, endangering, or extracting from the people involved.

## Core Rules

### Audit Your Source Roster Against the Community Affected

Before and during a story, audit who is speaking. Compare the source roster against the population actually affected by the issue, not against the population that is easiest to contact. Ask:

- Whose voices appear, and whose are absent?
- Are the people most affected by the policy, harm, or event represented, or only officials and experts commenting on them?
- Do sources span race, ethnicity, language, gender, class, disability, age, geography, and immigration status as relevant to the community?
- Is the same small set of "go-to" voices being reused across unrelated stories?

When the roster does not reflect the affected community, the absence is itself a finding about how the story is being framed.

### Treat Diversity as a Structural Practice, Not a Quota

Tokenism, adding a single representative voice to satisfy a diversity expectation, produces shallow and often harmful coverage. A single source cannot speak for a community, and being asked to do so is itself a burden. Aim for structural diversity: multiple voices from within a community, reflecting internal disagreement, variation, and specificity, rather than one person treated as the community's spokesperson.

Distinguish between sources who speak from lived experience, sources who speak from expertise, and sources who speak from organizational authority. A story needs all three, and conflating them flattens both the community and the analysis.

### Invest in Relationship Building Before You Need Sources

Communities that have been misrepresented, surveilled, or exploited by media have reason to distrust reporters. Trust cannot be manufactured on deadline. Build relationships over time:

- Attend community events and spaces without an immediate ask.
- Listen to how community members frame their own concerns before importing your own.
- Recruit and respect community guides, fixers, and translators, and credit or protect them appropriately.
- Be honest about what you can and cannot offer in return.
- Follow up after stories, including when coverage gets things wrong.

Relationships built only at the moment of need reproduce extraction and are recognized as such.

### Address Language and Access Barriers Deliberately

Language is one of the largest invisible filters in sourcing. Reporters who only interview fluent speakers of their own language systematically exclude communities. Address this structurally:

- Work with qualified translators and interpreters, and understand the difference between literal translation and cultural mediation.
- Do not rely on family members, employers, or officials to translate for a vulnerable source, as this compromises candor and safety.
- Provide materials and consent explanations in the source's language.
- Recognize that dialect, literacy, disability, and communication style all affect access and accuracy.

Budget for translation and access as a core reporting cost, not an optional extra.

### Protect Vulnerable Sources From Harm

Recruiting underrepresented sources often means recruiting people who face elevated risk: undocumented immigrants, dissidents, whistleblowers, survivors of violence, people in authoritarian contexts, and members of criminalized communities. Protection is a primary obligation:

- Assess what could happen to the source if they are identified, quoted, or even seen speaking to you.
- Offer and explain anonymity, on the record, on background, and other protections, and honor the chosen level precisely.
- Strip identifying details and metadata from published material.
- Consider legal, immigration, employment, and physical safety consequences.
- Never promise protection you cannot deliver, and never reveal a source's identity without explicit informed consent.

A source's willingness to speak is not proof of safety. The reporter must assess risk on the source's behalf.

### Avoid Extractive and Parachute Reporting

Parachute reporting, arriving in a community for a story and leaving, tends to extract testimony and depart with the most dramatic material, leaving the community with misrepresentation and no benefit. Resist it by:

- Investing time in understanding local context, history, and power dynamics.
- Partnering with local journalists and outlets where appropriate.
- Returning results to the community, including sharing the published story and being accountable for errors.
- Avoiding framing that exoticizes, victimizes, or flattens the community for an outside audience.

Communities are not raw material. Coverage that treats them as such damages future access for all journalists.

### Examine Your Own Position and Assumptions

Reporters carry assumptions about credibility, articulateness, and newsworthiness that shape who they approach and who they trust. Examine:

- Whom you instinctively find credible, and why.
- Whether you require higher proof from marginalized sources than from official ones.
- Whether you are framing the community through deficit, danger, or exceptionality rather than ordinary complexity.
- Whether you are equipped to cover this community, or whether a reporter from within it should lead.

Humility about position is not a limitation; it is a quality control on the accuracy of what you ultimately report.

### Diversify Not Only Sources but Framing

A diverse source roster used to confirm a pre-existing frame is still skewed reporting. Let diverse sources shape the questions, the framing, and the definition of the problem, not only the quotes. When affected communities define the issue differently than officials do, that difference is part of the story.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to Official and Elite Sources

Police, government spokespeople, academics, and CEOs are easy to reach and quotable, but they are not the community. Overreliance on them produces coverage that explains communities to outsiders rather than representing communities to themselves and to power.

### Tokenizing a Single Representative Voice

Asking one person to represent an entire community burdens that person and flattens the community's internal diversity. Recruit multiple voices and represent disagreement within the group.

### Reusing the Same "Diverse" Voices Across Stories

A small set of frequently quoted minority voices becomes a new elite, overexposed and unrepresentative. Continuously expand the roster rather than returning to the same names.

### Relying on Family, Employers, or Officials to Translate

Using a spouse, boss, caseworker, or officer to interpret for a vulnerable source compromises candor, introduces bias, and can endanger the source. Use independent, qualified interpreters.

### Endangering Sources Through Careless Identification

Even when a source is not named, identifying details, location, timing, or metadata can expose them. Scrub details that could allow identification against the source's wishes, and confirm the level of protection with the source.

### Extracting Testimony Without Reciprocity

Taking a community's stories and departing without follow-up, credit, or accountability reproduces exploitation. Return results, correct errors publicly, and maintain the relationship beyond the single story.

### Framing Communities Through Deficit or Danger

Default framings of marginalized communities as victims, criminals, or exceptions distort reality and reinforce stereotypes. Seek ordinary complexity, agency, and structural context.

### Requiring Higher Proof From Marginalized Sources

Reporters often unconsciously demand more corroboration from marginalized sources than from officials. Apply consistent evidentiary standards, and recognize that official records themselves may be biased or incomplete.

## Self-Check

- Does my source roster reflect the community actually affected by the story, not just the community easiest to reach?
- Have I recruited multiple voices from within each underrepresented group, rather than tokenizing a single representative?
- Did I build relationships and understanding before I needed sources, rather than extracting testimony on deadline?
- Have I addressed language, disability, and access barriers with qualified interpreters and appropriate materials, budgeted as a core cost?
- Have I assessed and protected vulnerable sources from identification, retaliation, legal, immigration, and physical harm?
- Am I avoiding parachute and extractive reporting by investing in context, partnering locally, and returning results to the community?
- Have I examined my own assumptions about credibility, articulateness, and framing, and applied consistent evidentiary standards across all sources?
- Are diverse sources shaping the questions and framing, not just supplying quotes to confirm a pre-existing angle?
- Have I avoided reusing the same small set of "diverse" voices across unrelated stories?
- Will the community recognize itself in the coverage, including its internal complexity and disagreement?
