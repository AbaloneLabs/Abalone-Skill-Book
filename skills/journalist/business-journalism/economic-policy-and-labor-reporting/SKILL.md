# Economic Policy And Labor Reporting

Economic policy and labor reporting connects the abstract world of macroeconomics, employment, inflation, trade, and regulation, to the concrete reality of people's livelihoods. The judgment problem is that economic data is technical and contested, policy debates are dominated by interested experts, and the lived experience of workers and households is often treated as anecdote rather than evidence. A reporter who covers only the data and the policymakers produces coverage that is technically accurate but disconnected from consequence; one who covers only the personal stories produces coverage that is vivid but ungrounded. Strong economic and labor reporting integrates both, translating data honestly, scrutinizing policy claims, and centering the workers and households whose lives the economy shapes.

Use this skill when covering economic policy, employment, wages, inflation, trade, and labor, and when connecting macroeconomic trends to household consequences. The main risk is that an agent may over-rely on official data or interested experts, present contested economic claims as settled, or treat worker experience as color rather than evidence. This skill is editorial guidance only; economic analysis involves judgment calls where expert consultation strengthens the work.

## Core Rules

### Translate Economic Data Honestly

Economic data, employment figures, inflation, GDP, trade balances, and wage growth, is the backbone of economic reporting, but it is technical, revised, and often misunderstood. Report data honestly: explain what it measures and what it does not, note the margin of error and the revisions, and contextualize the number within its trend rather than treating a single release as definitive. Distinguish between nominal and real values, between levels and rates of change, and between headline and underlying measures. Avoid the false precision of declaring a trend from one data point. Honest translation serves readers who depend on the coverage to understand their economic world.

When experts disagree about what data shows, report the disagreement and the reasons.

### Scrutinize Policy Claims Against Evidence

Economic policy claims, that a tax cut will pay for itself, that a regulation will kill jobs, that a trade policy will revive an industry, are often self-serving and rarely as simple as presented. Scrutinize them against independent evidence, including historical experience, academic research, and the assessments of nonpartisan analysts like budget offices and research institutions. Report what the evidence shows, including when it contradicts the claim, and avoid false equivalence that treats a discredited claim as merely contested. Be honest about uncertainty, because economics rarely offers certainty, but do not use uncertainty as cover for refusing to assess claims. The reporter's job is to test claims, not to relay them.

Follow the policy to its actual consequences, not just its stated goals.

### Center Workers And Households

The economy is not an abstraction; it is the sum of people's livelihoods. Center workers and households in economic reporting, not as anecdote but as primary evidence of how the economy actually functions. Cover wages, working conditions, job security, benefits, and the cost of living from the perspective of the people who experience them. Include workers, union representatives, and household voices as sources, alongside the economists and officials who usually dominate. A story that reports a strong economy without examining whether workers feel it is incomplete, because the aggregate can conceal widespread struggle.

Seek out workers in the industries and regions the data describes, not just in convenient locations.

### Cover Labor, Unions, And Workplace Power

Labor reporting is a core accountability beat: it covers the power relationships between employers and workers, the state of unions and collective bargaining, workplace safety, wage theft, misclassification, and the enforcement of labor law. Cover these stories with the same rigor as any institutional beat, cultivating sources among workers, organizers, regulators, and labor lawyers, and using documents including OSHA records, NLRB filings, and court cases. Do not treat labor as a niche interest; the workplace is where most adults spend most of their waking lives, and the power dynamics there are a public story. Report on employer tactics, including union-busting, with the same scrutiny applied to worker tactics.

Cover the full range of work, including gig, informal, and immigrant labor, which are often underreported.

### Connect Macro Policy To Micro Consequence

Macroeconomic policy, interest rates, fiscal policy, trade policy, regulation, affects households through concrete channels: mortgage payments, rent, food prices, job availability, and the cost of healthcare and education. Connect the macro to the micro, showing how a central bank decision or a tariff change reaches a family's budget. This translation is the public service of economic reporting, because it makes abstract policy legible and consequential. Avoid both the wonk's error of staying at the macro level and the feature writer's error of staying at the personal level; integrate them.

The same policy can help one household and hurt another; report the distribution, not just the average.

### Beware Interested Experts And Ideological Framing

Economic debates are saturated with interested experts: think tanks funded by industries, economists paid by interested parties, and commentators with ideological commitments. Treat expert claims with the same scrutiny as any other source: examine the evidence, disclose conflicts of interest, and seek independent perspectives. Beware ideological framing that presents a contested position as consensus or a political preference as economic necessity. Identify the assumptions behind a claim, because economics is full of models whose conclusions depend on assumptions that may not hold. The reporter who relays expert claims without scrutiny becomes a vehicle for advocacy.

Disclose think-tank funding and expert ties when they are relevant to the claim.

### Cover Inflation, Prices, And Cost Of Living Rigorously

Inflation and the cost of living are among the most consequential economic stories for households, and they are also among the most manipulated. Cover them rigorously: explain what the official measures capture and miss, including the difference between headline and core inflation and the limitations of aggregate price indices. Report on the specific prices that matter to households, housing, food, energy, healthcare, and education, which may diverge from the aggregate. Distinguish between temporary and persistent inflation, and between supply-driven and demand-driven price changes, because the causes determine the consequences. Avoid both alarmism and complacency, and ground claims in data and household experience.

Housing costs, in particular, are often underweighted in official measures and overweighted in household budgets.

### Cover Economic Inequality And Its Consequences

Economic inequality, in income, wealth, and opportunity, is a defining feature of many economies and a major public story. Cover it with data and with consequence: how inequality is measured, how it has changed, what drives it, and how it affects health, mobility, political stability, and democratic functioning. Report on the policies and structures that produce inequality, including tax policy, labor market institutions, education, and discrimination. Do not treat inequality as natural or inevitable; it is the product of choices, and reporting on those choices is accountability journalism. Include the voices of those at the bottom of the distribution, whose experience the aggregate conceals.

Inequality is not just an economic statistic; it shapes the society people live in.

## Common Traps

### Data Without Consequence

Reporting numbers without connecting them to household budgets produces technically accurate but disconnected coverage.

### Relaying Policy Claims Uncritically

Treating self-serving economic claims as merely contested abdicates the verification role.

### Workers As Anecdote

Treating worker experience as color rather than evidence misses the primary reality of the economy.

### Interested Experts As Neutral

Relaying think-tank and paid-expert claims without scrutiny makes the reporter a vehicle for advocacy.

### Aggregate Concealing Distribution

A strong aggregate economy can conceal widespread household struggle; report the distribution.

### Labor Treated As Niche

The workplace is a core accountability beat, not a specialty interest.

### False Precision From One Data Point

Declaring a trend from a single release ignores revisions, error, and context.

### Ignoring Inequality's Consequences

Inequality is not just a statistic; it shapes health, mobility, and democratic stability.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is economic data reported honestly, with measurement limits, revisions, context, and the distinction between nominal and real values?
- [ ] Are policy claims scrutinized against independent evidence, with false equivalence avoided and uncertainty handled honestly?
- [ ] Are workers and households centered as primary evidence, including their wages, conditions, and cost of living?
- [ ] Is labor covered as a core accountability beat, including unions, workplace safety, wage theft, and enforcement?
- [ ] Does coverage connect macroeconomic policy to its micro consequences for household budgets?
- [ ] Are interested experts scrutinized, with conflicts disclosed and assumptions examined?
- [ ] Are inflation and cost of living covered rigorously, including the gap between official measures and household experience?
- [ ] Is economic inequality covered with data and consequence, including the policies and structures that produce it?
- [ ] Has complex economic analysis been strengthened by consultation with independent, nonpartisan experts, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance only and that economic claims involve judgment calls?
