# Publishing and Engagement on Social Platforms

Publishing journalism on social platforms is not merely distributing a link; it is producing a distinct piece of journalism that most readers will encounter instead of, not in addition to, the full story. A social post is read on its own, screenshotted, quoted, and shared far from its original context, often by people who never click through. This means the post itself must meet the standards of accuracy, attribution, and fairness that the full story meets, and it must stand alone. The judgment problem is that the platform's incentives, brevity, emotional punch, performance-optimized framing, pull against the nuance and hedging that honest reporting requires. This skill covers how to publish and engage on social platforms in ways that serve the story and the audience without sacrificing accuracy, becoming a vector for misinformation, or compromising the publication's voice and integrity.

## Core Rules

### Make the Social Post a Complete, Accurate Mini-Story

Treat each social post as a standalone piece of journalism, because for most readers it will be. The post should convey the key verified fact, the necessary attribution, and the appropriate hedge on its own, without requiring a click to be accurate. A post that is technically true only when read with the full article is misleading on a platform where most readers never click. Write the post so that a reader who sees only the post understands the claim, its source, and its limits. If a claim cannot be conveyed accurately within the platform's constraints, do not post it; link without making the claim, or wait until it can be stated properly.

### Preserve Attribution and Hedging in Every Post

The platform's brevity pressure tempts writers to drop attribution ("police say," "according to," "allegedly") to save characters or sharpen impact. This converts a sourced, contingent claim into a bare assertion attributed to the publication itself, which is both misleading and legally risky. Carry attribution into social posts whenever the underlying claim is sourced or contested. "Mayor resigns amid fraud probe, prosecutors say" is accurate; "Mayor committed fraud" is a libel claim. Hedging and attribution are not optional polish; they are part of the claim.

### Match Tone to the Story, Not to the Platform's Default

Social platforms reward emotional, punchy, and often sensational tone, but that tone must be earned by the story. A post about a mass tragedy, a wrongful conviction, or a death should not adopt the breezy or provocative tone that performs well for lighter news. Match the tone of the post to the gravity and nature of the story, even at the cost of engagement. A publication that jokes about a serious story to win reach betrays its subjects and its audience. Let the story set the tone, not the algorithm.

### Avoid the Temptation to Sensationalize for Reach

The metrics on social platforms reward exaggeration, conflict, and emotional provocation, which creates constant pressure to frame stories more dramatically than the reporting supports. Resist this. A post that overstates the story to win shares is a form of misinformation, and the gap between the post and the story erodes trust among readers who do click through. Frame social posts as accurately as the full story, and accept that honesty may cost some reach. Reach gained through distortion is not worth the credibility it costs.

### Engage With Audiences Honestly and Respectfully

Social platforms enable direct engagement with readers, which is valuable for accountability and trust. Engage honestly: respond substantively to legitimate questions and critiques, acknowledge errors, and explain decisions. Do not engage in bad faith, mock readers, or get drawn into unproductive arguments from official accounts. Distinguish between official publication voice and personal accounts, and maintain professional standards on official channels. Constructive engagement builds the trust that pays off when errors or controversies arise; dismissive or hostile engagement destroys it.

### Correct Errors on the Platform Where They Occurred

If an erroneous post was published on a social platform, correct it on that platform, visibly and promptly. Do not delete the erroneous post silently (unless deletion is warranted for safety or legal reasons, with explanation); instead, post a clear correction so that followers who saw the original see the fix. If the error is significant, consider whether the original post should be deleted and replaced with a corrected version, with a note explaining the change. Correcting the article on your site while leaving the erroneous social post standing is only a partial correction. Plan to propagate corrections to every platform where the error traveled.

### Protect Sources, Subjects, and Staff on Public Platforms

Social platforms are public, hostile, and permanent, and engagement there carries risks to people connected to the story. Do not expose sources, subjects, or staff to harassment by tagging them, sharing their handles, or drawing attention to them without consideration. Be cautious about publishing identifying information in posts that will spread widely. Consider the likely comment and reaction environment before posting about sensitive topics, and have a plan for moderating or disabling comments where harassment is likely. The platform's openness is not a reason to abandon duty of care.

### Maintain Consistent Standards Across Platforms

Each platform has its own conventions, but the publication's standards do not change. A claim that would not pass editorial review on the site should not appear on social media, even informally. Maintain consistent accuracy, attribution, fairness, and tone standards across every platform, and ensure that staff posting to official accounts understand and follow them. Ad hoc, lower-standard posting on social media is a common source of errors and reputational damage.

## Common Traps

### Posting Claims That Are Only Accurate With the Full Article

Writing posts that technically link to an accurate story but make a misleading standalone claim. This is a trap because most readers see only the post. Make posts accurate on their own.

### Dropping Attribution to Sharpen the Post

Removing "police say" or "allegedly" to make a punchier post converts a sourced claim into the publication's assertion. This is a trap because it increases legal exposure and misleads readers. Carry attribution into posts.

### Adopting Provocative Tone for Engagement

Using sensational, jokey, or provocative tone to win reach on a story that deserves gravity. This is a trap because it betrays the subjects and erodes trust. Match tone to the story.

### Sensationalizing Framing Beyond the Reporting

Framing a post more dramatically than the story supports to drive shares. This is a trap because the post becomes misinformation and the gap erodes trust. Frame posts as accurately as the story.

### Deleting Erroneous Posts Silently

Removing a wrong post without a correction so followers never see the fix. This is a trap because the error continues to spread and silent deletion looks like concealment. Correct visibly on the platform.

### Exposing People to Harassment Through Tagging or Identification

Tagging or identifying sources, subjects, or staff in posts that will spread widely, exposing them to harassment. This is a trap because the public platform is hostile and the duty of care remains. Consider exposure before posting.

### Lowering Standards Because "It's Just Social Media"

Posting looser, less verified, or more opinionated content on social than would pass site editorial review. This is a trap because social posts are real journalism read by large audiences. Maintain consistent standards.

## Self-Check

- Does each social post convey the key verified fact, attribution, and appropriate hedge accurately on its own, without requiring a click?
- Is attribution preserved in every post where the underlying claim is sourced or contested, rather than dropped for brevity or impact?
- Is the tone of the post matched to the gravity and nature of the story, rather than to platform performance incentives?
- Have you resisted sensationalizing the framing beyond what the reporting supports, accepting any reach cost?
- When errors occurred in social posts, were they corrected visibly and promptly on the same platform, not just on the site?
- Have you considered whether the post exposes sources, subjects, or staff to harassment, and avoided unnecessary tagging or identification?
- Are editorial standards for accuracy, attribution, and fairness consistent across all platforms, with no "social media exception"?
- For social posts involving legal risk, accusations, or sensitive identifications, has an editor reviewed the post, and where defamation or safety risk exists, has counsel or appropriate expertise been consulted before publishing?
