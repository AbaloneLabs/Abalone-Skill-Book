# Physical Risk Assessment

Physical risk in journalism is not limited to war zones. Reporters face violence and injury at protests, crime scenes, disaster zones, courtrooms, election coverage, investigations of organized crime, and increasingly in routine domestic reporting where polarization and hostility toward the press have risen. The judgment problem is that risk assessment is often treated as a box to tick before a trip rather than as a continuous discipline, and that the pressure to get the story, the camaraderie of a press pack, and the normalization of danger all push reporters to accept risks they would reject if assessed coldly. A defensible risk assessment identifies the specific threats of an assignment, weighs them against the story's public value, puts mitigations in place, and preserves the reporter's authority to decline or withdraw.

Use this skill when planning field reporting, assessing whether an assignment is safe, deciding on protective measures, or building a risk assessment for domestic or international coverage. The main risk is that an agent under deadline or competitive pressure treats risk as an obstacle to manage around rather than as a factor that should shape whether and how the reporting proceeds.

## Core Rules

### Assess Specific Threats, Not A General Sense Of Danger

A useful risk assessment identifies the specific threats of a specific assignment, not a vague sense that a place is dangerous. For each assignment, enumerate the threats: violence from state actors, non-state actors, crowds, or individuals; arrest or detention; surveillance and device seizure; environmental hazards such as weather, fire, structural collapse, or disease; road and transport risks; kidnapping; sexual violence; harassment; and psychological trauma. Each threat has different likelihood, severity, and mitigation.

For each threat, ask how likely it is given the location, timing, the reporter's profile, the story's subject, and recent events; how severe the consequences would be; and what reduces the likelihood or severity. A protest at night carries different risks than one during the day; a reporter whose face has been circulated online faces different risks than one who has not; an investigation of organized crime carries different risks than a routine agency story. Specificity drives the mitigation.

### Weigh Risk Against Public Value Honestly

Not every story justifies every risk. The decision to accept physical risk should turn on the public value of the reporting weighed against the likelihood and severity of harm, with mitigations factored in. A major accountability story may justify significant risk; a routine color piece rarely does. Be honest about whether the public value is sufficient, and resist the pressure to treat any assignment as automatically worth the risk because it is "the story."

This weighing belongs to the reporter and the editor together, not to the reporter alone under field pressure. An editor who sends a reporter into danger without understanding the risk, or a reporter who accepts danger without editorial sign-off, has failed the safety system. The reporter retains the authority to decline or withdraw, and that authority must be real, not theoretical.

### Build A Mitigation Plan Before Deployment

Before deployment, build a mitigation plan tailored to the specific threats. Mitigations include: hostile environment and first aid training; personal protective equipment appropriate to the threat, body armor, helmets, eye protection, respirators where relevant; communication plans with check-in protocols and escalation triggers; a fixer or local partner with knowledge of the environment; identified safe routes, safe locations, and exit strategies; insurance and accreditation; medical information and emergency contacts carried with the reporter; and awareness of local laws, customs, and flashpoints.

The mitigation plan should be written, shared with the editor and a designated safety contact, and revisable as conditions change. Plans that live in the reporter's head fail under stress. For high-risk assignments, professional risk assessment support from organizations specializing in journalist safety is warranted.

### Preserve The Right To Decline Or Withdraw

A reporter must retain the authority to decline an assignment or to withdraw from the field when the risk exceeds what they are willing to bear, without professional penalty. This authority is the foundation of ethical safety practice. Editors must make clear, before deployment, that withdrawal is supported, and reporters must feel able to exercise the right without being seen as weak or uncommitted.

The corollary is that reporters should not take on risks they have not honestly assessed, and should not let competitive pressure, peer dynamics, or a desire to prove themselves override their own judgment. The story is never more important than the person reporting it, and a reporter injured, killed, or traumatized is a failure of the system, not the cost of the work.

### Plan For Communication And Check-Ins

In risky environments, communication is safety. Establish a check-in protocol with a designated editor or safety contact: when the reporter will check in, by what method, what happens if a check-in is missed, and what triggers escalation to emergency response. Build redundancy: a primary channel, a backup channel, and a plan for when both fail. For high-risk assignments, consider professional monitoring or security support.

The check-in protocol must account for the realities of the environment: connectivity may be poor, devices may be seized or monitored, and routine check-ins may themselves create risk if they reveal location. Plan for these contingencies. A missed check-in should trigger a defined response, not anxiety and improvisation.

### Consider The Safety Of Fixers, Stringers, And Local Partners

Local partners, fixers, stringers, drivers, and translators often face greater long-term risk than the visiting reporter, because they remain in the environment after the reporter leaves, may be identifiable as press collaborators, and may lack the protections of international accreditation or evacuation support. Their safety is the reporter's responsibility, not an afterthought. Assess the risks they face, compensate them fairly, and plan for their protection, including what happens to them if the situation deteriorates or after the story publishes.

Do not treat local partners as expendable infrastructure. A reporter who leaves a fixer exposed to retaliation after extracting the story has behaved unethically, regardless of the story's value. See the conflict-reporting skill for working in hostile environments.

### Plan For Psychological As Well As Physical Harm

Physical risk assessment often overlooks psychological harm: trauma from witnessing violence, death, or suffering; the cumulative effects of repeated exposure; and the specific risks of online harassment and threats that follow field reporting. Plan for psychological safety: recognize the signs of trauma, build in recovery time after high-exposure assignments, provide access to professional support, and destigmatize seeking help.

Psychological harm is real harm. A reporter who returns physically intact but traumatized has still been injured, and the failure to plan for that injury is a failure of safety practice. See the harassment-response skill for online threats.

## Common Traps

### Normalizing Danger Through Repeated Exposure

Reporters who routinely work in risky environments can lose their sensitivity to risk, treating genuinely dangerous situations as normal. Reassess each assignment coldly, and do not let familiarity override the assessment.

### Letting Competitive Pressure Override Safety

The pressure to get the story, to match competitors, or to prove commitment pushes reporters to accept risks they would otherwise decline. The story is never more important than the person; competitive pressure is not a justification for unsafe decisions.

### Skipping Mitigation Because The Assignment Seems Routine

Assignments that seem routine, domestic protests, court coverage, disaster zones, can carry serious risks that are overlooked because they are not "war zones." Assess every field assignment, not just the obviously dangerous ones.

### Failing To Plan For Local Partners' Safety

Fixers and local partners often face greater long-term risk than visiting reporters. Their safety must be planned for, not treated as their own problem.

### Treating The Check-In Protocol As A Formality

A check-in protocol that no one monitors or enforces provides no safety. Define the escalation triggers and ensure the designated contact will act on a missed check-in.

### Overlooking Psychological Harm

Trauma and cumulative stress are injuries. Failing to plan for psychological safety leaves reporters harmed in ways that are invisible until they become disabling.

## Self-Check

Before treating a physical risk assessment as sound, verify:

- Specific threats were enumerated for the assignment, with likelihood, severity, and mitigation assessed for each, rather than a general sense of danger.
- Risk was weighed against public value honestly, with the assignment justified by sufficient public interest rather than accepted automatically as "the story."
- A written mitigation plan exists, tailored to the threats, shared with the editor and a safety contact, and revisable as conditions change.
- The reporter retains real authority to decline or withdraw without penalty, and this was made clear before deployment.
- A communication and check-in protocol is in place, with redundancy, defined escalation triggers, and a designated contact who will act on a missed check-in.
- The safety of fixers, stringers, and local partners was assessed and planned for, including post-publication exposure, with fair compensation.
- Psychological as well as physical harm was planned for, with recovery time and access to professional support.
- No assignment was accepted under competitive or peer pressure that overrode the reporter's own risk judgment.
- Professional risk assessment and safety support were engaged for high-risk assignments.
- Physical risk assessment supports editorial judgment; significant risk decisions should involve a responsible editor and, for high-risk assignments, professional safety advisors, with the reporter's authority to decline or withdraw preserved.
