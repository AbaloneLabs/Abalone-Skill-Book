# Source and Fixer Safety and Protection

The people who make journalism possible in difficult environments, local fixers, interpreters, drivers, guides, stringers, and confidential sources, often face far greater risk than the visiting reporters they assist. Association with foreign or independent media can mark a local as a target for surveillance, detention, violence, or professional destruction. Reporters who treat fixers and sources as instruments of the story, rather than as people whose safety they partly carry, produce harm that is invisible from afar but devastating up close.

The judgment problem is that the reporter's departure does not end the risk. Locals remain in the environment, often identifiable long after the story is published, and the power imbalance means they may accept risk they should not, or be pressured into danger by the reporter's presence and expectations. Protecting them is a primary ethical obligation, woven into every decision from first contact through long after publication.

## Core Rules

### Treat Source and Fixer Safety as a Primary Obligation, Not an Afterthought

The safety of locals who assist journalism is not subordinate to getting the story. Before engaging anyone, accept that their risk is likely greater than yours, persists longer, and is harder for you to see. Make their protection a condition of the work, not a concern that arises only when something goes wrong. If the story cannot be done without endangering them unacceptably, the calculus is whether to do the story at all.

### Assess Risk to Locals Before, During, and After Engagement

Risk assessment for sources and fixers must be specific, ongoing, and conducted with them, not for them. Consider:

- What happens to this person if they are seen with me, or if their involvement becomes known?
- What surveillance, informer networks, or social hostility operate in this environment?
- Is the person in a position of vulnerability, dependency, or prior risk that raises the stakes?
- What is the risk trajectory after I leave, and after the story publishes?
- Does the person fully understand the risk, free from pressure to minimize it?

Conduct this assessment honestly, including the possibility that the correct decision is not to use this person, or not to do the story.

### Vet and Select Fixers and Local Partners Carefully

Fixers and local partners are not interchangeable services. Their competence, integrity, networks, and judgment materially affect both the story and the safety of everyone involved. Vet them through:

- Reputation among trusted local and international journalists.
- Demonstrated understanding of security and source protection.
- Absence of conflicts of interest, political ties, or state affiliations that could compromise sources.
- Clarity about what they will and will not do, and at what risk.

A fixer who is careless with sources, or who has competing loyalties, endangers everyone. Vet before relying, not after.

### Agree on Terms, Credit, and Protection in Advance

Before work begins, agree explicitly on:

- Compensation, including after the assignment, and fairness relative to risk and local norms.
- Whether the fixer or source will be credited, named, or kept confidential, and the consequences of each.
- What material they will and will not be exposed to or hold.
- What happens if the story is delayed, killed, or legally challenged.
- What protection, relocation, or support you can realistically offer if things go wrong.

Do not promise protection you cannot deliver. Honest acknowledgment of limits is better than false assurance that collapses under pressure.

### Protect Identity Rigorously and Persistently

Identity protection extends far beyond not naming someone. Scrub and consider:

- Names, aliases, and identifying details in the published story.
- Metadata in photos, video, audio, and documents.
- Locations, timing, and circumstantial details that could allow identification.
- Digital communications and the channels used to contact sources.
- The visibility of meetings themselves, and who might observe them.
- The source's own digital footprint and whether publication could expose them.

A source can be identified through accumulated detail even when unnamed. Confirm the level of protection with the source, and re-check before publication.

### Recognize the Power Imbalance and Pressure It Creates

Visiting reporters, foreign outlets, and well-resourced newsrooms carry power and resources that can pressure locals into accepting risk they would otherwise refuse. A fixer or source may minimize danger to secure work, access, money, or favor. Counter this by:

- Actively inviting the person to decline or set limits, and honoring refusal without penalty.
- Not treating willingness as proof of safety.
- Paying fairly and not using compensation to extract greater risk.
- Being honest about what the story will and will not do for them.

Willingness under pressure is not informed consent.

### Plan for Post-Publication and Long-Term Risk

The period after publication is often the most dangerous for sources and fixers, as attention and retaliation converge. Plan in advance:

- How the person will be supported if exposed or targeted.
- Whether relocation, legal support, or security assistance is possible and by whom.
- How to maintain contact safely after the story.
- What follow-up obligations the reporter or outlet accepts.

Do not treat publication as the end of the relationship or the risk. The duty of care extends beyond the byline.

### Avoid Endangering Locals Through Careless Reporting Choices

Specific reporting choices can endanger locals even when the reporter is safe. Be cautious about:

- Naming local staff, fixers, or assistants in bylines or credits when naming could endanger them.
- Describing methods, routes, or intermediaries in ways that expose them.
- Publishing images or video in which locals are identifiable.
- Quoting locals in ways that allow attribution back to them.
- Leaving behind records, contacts, or devices that could compromise them.

Every published detail is a potential identifier. Review the final story through the lens of local safety, not only newsworthiness.

### Support Fixer and Stringer Sustainability

Local journalists, fixers, and stringers often work in precarious conditions with limited institutional support. Where you can, contribute to their sustainability: fair pay, credit where safe, recommendation to other outlets, training, and solidarity when they face threats. Extractive relationships that use locals and discard them damage both individuals and the field.

## Common Traps

### Treating Fixers as Disposable Services

Using a fixer for a story and then severing contact leaves them exposed and unrewarded. Fixers are colleagues whose ongoing safety and sustainability matter.

### Promising Protection You Cannot Deliver

Assuring a source or fixer of safety, relocation, or support that you cannot actually provide creates false security and can be lethal. Be honest about limits.

### Confusing Willingness With Safety

A local's willingness to help under pressure, financial need, or hope of access is not evidence that the risk is acceptable. Assess risk independently of their eagerness.

### Identifying Sources Through Accumulated Detail

Even unnamed sources can be identified through location, timing, role, and circumstance. Scrub identifying detail and confirm protection levels with the source.

### Leaving Identifying Material in Published Output

Metadata, background faces, recognizable locations, and method descriptions can expose locals. Review all output for inadvertent identification.

### Endangering Locals Through Bylines and Credits

Naming a fixer or local journalist who would be endangered by association can be a serious harm. Let safety, not credit norms, decide naming.

### Neglecting Post-Publication Risk

The danger often peaks after publication, when retaliation and exposure converge. Planning that ends at publication abandons sources at the most dangerous moment.

### Extracting Testimony and Departing

Taking a community's or fixer's help and leaving without follow-up, fair compensation, or accountability reproduces exploitation and endangers those left behind.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed the risk to every local who assists this story, before, during, and after engagement, and conducted that assessment with them rather than for them?
- Did I vet fixers and local partners for competence, integrity, and absence of conflicting loyalties before relying on them?
- Have I agreed in advance on compensation, credit, confidentiality, and what protection I can and cannot realistically deliver?
- Am I protecting identity rigorously, including names, metadata, locations, timing, circumstantial detail, communications channels, and visibility of meetings?
- Have I recognized and counteracted the power imbalance that may pressure locals into accepting risk they should refuse?
- Have I planned for post-publication and long-term risk, including relocation, legal support, security assistance, and safe ongoing contact?
- Have I reviewed the final story, images, video, and methods through the lens of local safety, scrubbing inadvertent identifiers?
- Have I avoided naming bylines or credits where naming would endanger locals, letting safety rather than convention decide?
- Am I supporting the sustainability of local journalists, fixers, and stringers through fair pay, credit, recommendation, and solidarity?
- Have I been honest with sources and fixers about the limits of my protection, rather than offering false assurance?
