# Hostile Environment Preparation

Reporting from a hostile environment, a conflict zone, a repressive state, a volatile protest, a disaster area, or territory controlled by hostile actors, requires preparation that begins long before deployment and that determines whether the reporter can work, survive, and recover. The judgment problem is that hostile-environment work is governed by preparation that is invisible until it fails: training that seems excessive until it is needed, equipment that seems paranoid until it matters, relationships with fixers and security support that seem like overhead until extraction is required. A reporter who deploys without this preparation is not brave; they are a liability to themselves, their team, their local partners, and the editors who must rescue them.

Use this skill when preparing for deployment to a hostile environment, deciding on training and equipment, arranging local partners and evacuation support, or preparing for contingencies like arrest, injury, or device seizure. The main risk is that an agent treats preparation as optional or as something to shortcut under deadline, when preparation is precisely what makes the deadline-driven work survivable.

## Core Rules

### Complete Appropriate Training Before Deployment

Hostile environment and first aid training is not a luxury for conflict reporters; it is baseline preparation for anyone deploying to environments where they may face violence, injury, arrest, or environmental hazard. The training covers threat recognition, risk mitigation, battlefield and crowd first aid, navigation, communication under stress, behavior under detention, and psychological resilience. The skills are perishable and should be refreshed periodically.

Match the training to the environment. A reporter covering urban protests needs crowd dynamics, chemical irritant response, and interaction with law enforcement; a reporter deploying to a conflict zone needs combat first aid, checkpoint behavior, and mines awareness; a reporter investigating organized crime needs surveillance detection and source protection. Generic preparation is better than none, but specific preparation is what keeps reporters alive in specific environments.

### Assemble A Written Deployment And Safety Plan

Before deployment, assemble a written plan that the editor, a designated safety contact, and the reporter all hold. The plan covers: the assignment's purpose and public-value justification; the specific threats and mitigations; the itinerary, accommodations, and movement plan; the communication and check-in protocol with escalation triggers; emergency contacts including the embassy, local counsel, medical facilities, and security support; the insurance and accreditation status; and the decision criteria for withdrawal. The plan is a living document, updated as conditions change.

A plan that exists only in the reporter's head fails under stress and leaves editors unable to help. The discipline of writing the plan also forces honest assessment: threats that seemed manageable in conversation look different when enumerated on paper, and gaps become visible.

### Arrange Fixers, Local Partners, And Security Support

Local knowledge is often the difference between safe and unsafe reporting in a hostile environment. A trusted fixer, local journalist, driver, or guide who knows the terrain, the actors, the customs, the language, and the flashpoints can steer the reporter away from danger and toward the story. Vet fixers carefully, through recommendations and prior work, and treat them as partners whose safety is your responsibility, not as service providers.

For high-risk environments, arrange professional security support: risk assessment consultancies, evacuation insurance and services, and monitoring. Know the limits of what each provides and what triggers activation. Do not assume that accreditation or press credentials will protect you; in many hostile environments they mark you as a target rather than shielding you. See the conflict-reporting skill.

### Prepare Equipment For The Specific Threats

Equipment should match the specific threats, not a generic packing list. For protest coverage: eye protection, respirator or mask for chemical irritants, layered clothing, identification as press where appropriate and safe, and a basic first aid kit. For conflict zones: personal protective equipment appropriate to the threat level, tourniquets and trauma kits, and communications equipment with redundancy. For surveillance and seizure risk: clean devices, encrypted communications, and a plan for what happens if equipment is taken. For environmental hazards: appropriate protection for weather, fire, disease, or structural risk.

Carry only what you need; excess equipment impedes movement and creates additional seizure risk. Label critical equipment, carry emergency medical and contact information on your person, and ensure that someone off-site knows what you are carrying and why.

### Prepare For Arrest, Detention, And Device Seizure

In many hostile environments, arrest or detention is a realistic contingency, whether by state actors, non-state actors, or authorities at checkpoints. Prepare for it: know your rights and the relevant legal framework, carry contact information for counsel and your embassy where relevant, have a communication plan for what happens if you are detained, and brief your editor on the protocol. Some environments require a "proof of life" protocol and predetermined signals.

Device seizure is a near-certainty in some contexts and a serious risk in others. Prepare clean devices with no sensitive source material, use encrypted communications, assume that anything on a seized device may be accessed, and have a plan for notifying sources and editors if equipment is compromised. See the digital-security skill. Do not carry material that would endanger sources if seized.

### Plan For Evacuation And Medical Emergencies

Before deployment, know how you will get out if the situation deteriorates: the routes, the transport options, the border crossings, and the support available. Arrange evacuation insurance and services where the environment warrants, and know what triggers activation and what the services cover, including medical evacuation. Identify medical facilities along the route and at the destination, carry sufficient medication for existing conditions, and ensure that someone off-site has your medical information.

The evacuation plan must be realistic. A plan that assumes you can simply leave often fails when borders close, transport stops, or authorities prevent departure. Build redundancy: multiple routes, multiple options, and a willingness to withdraw earlier than the story demands if the window is closing.

### Prepare For Psychological Exposure

Hostile environments impose psychological as well as physical strain. Witnessing violence, death, suffering, and fear, often while unable to intervene, produces trauma that can accumulate across assignments. Prepare for it: recognize that psychological harm is likely, build in recovery time after deployment, arrange access to professional support, and destigmatize seeking help within the team. Brief less experienced team members on what to expect.

Psychological preparation also means honest self-assessment before deployment. A reporter who is exhausted, unwell, or already carrying trauma is more vulnerable to further harm. The decision to deploy should account for the reporter's current state, not just the assignment's demands.

## Common Traps

### Deploying Without Training Because The Assignment Seems Short

Short assignments in hostile environments carry the same risks as long ones, and preparation cannot be shortcut under deadline. Training is baseline, not optional, regardless of duration.

### Skipping The Written Plan

A plan in the head fails under stress and leaves editors unable to help. Write the plan, share it, and update it as conditions change.

### Treating Fixers As Service Providers

Local partners face greater long-term risk than visiting reporters and are partners whose safety is your responsibility, not infrastructure to be used and left behind.

### Packing For A Generic Threat Rather Than The Specific One

Generic packing lists miss the specific threats of the specific environment. Match equipment to the actual risks of the assignment.

### Carrying Sensitive Source Material Into Seizure Risk

Devices seized in hostile environments can expose sources to serious harm. Carry clean devices and assume anything seized may be accessed.

### Assuming Accreditation Will Protect You

In many hostile environments, press credentials mark you as a target rather than shielding you. Do not rely on accreditation as a safety measure where it provides no real protection.

### Ignoring Psychological Preparation

Trauma is a likely outcome of hostile-environment work, not a rare exception. Failing to prepare for psychological harm leaves reporters injured in invisible ways.

## Self-Check

Before treating hostile environment preparation as sound, verify:

- Appropriate hostile environment and first aid training was completed and is current, matched to the specific threats of the deployment.
- A written deployment and safety plan exists, shared with the editor and a safety contact, covering purpose, threats, mitigations, itinerary, communication, emergencies, and withdrawal criteria.
- Fixers and local partners are vetted, treated as partners, and their safety, including post-publication exposure, is planned for.
- Professional security and evacuation support is arranged where the environment warrants, with activation triggers and coverage understood.
- Equipment matches the specific threats, with no excess that impedes movement or creates seizure risk, and emergency medical and contact information carried on the person.
- Arrest, detention, and device seizure contingencies are prepared for, with clean devices, legal and embassy contacts, and a protocol for compromised equipment.
- Evacuation routes, transport, medical facilities, and insurance are identified, with redundancy built in and realistic withdrawal criteria.
- Psychological exposure is anticipated, with recovery time and professional support arranged and self-assessment of the reporter's state factored into the deployment decision.
- No sensitive source material is carried into seizure-risk environments.
- Hostile environment preparation supports editorial judgment; deployment decisions should involve a responsible editor and, for high-risk environments, professional safety and security advisors, with the reporter's authority to decline or withdraw preserved.
