# Incident Response And Extraction

No amount of preparation eliminates the possibility that a reporting assignment will go wrong. A protest turns violent, a reporter is detained, a fixer is threatened, an injury occurs, a situation deteriorates faster than anticipated. The judgment problem is that incident response is governed by decisions made under fear, confusion, and time pressure, when the instincts that feel right, push through, stay for the story, handle it alone, may be exactly wrong. A defensible response relies on plans made in advance, communication protocols that hold under stress, and the discipline to extract early rather than late, even at the cost of the story. The reporter and editor who have rehearsed the response recover situations that improvisation loses.

Use this skill when an incident occurs or is developing during field reporting, when executing an emergency plan, when deciding whether to extract, or when managing the aftermath of a safety event. The main risk is that an agent treats the unfolding incident as something to push through for the story rather than as a situation whose safe resolution is the first priority.

## Core Rules

### Treat Safety As The First Priority, Ahead Of The Story

When an incident develops, the first priority is the safety of the reporter and the team, including local partners, not the story. A story can be reported later, from a safer position, by another reporter, or not at all; an injury, arrest, kidnapping, or death cannot be undone. Establish this priority in advance, with editors, so that the reporter in the field does not face a conflict between safety and perceived professional obligation when the incident occurs.

This priority must be real, not rhetorical. Editors who have said "safety first" but who pressure a reporter to stay for one more quote when the situation is deteriorating have undermined the system. The reporter must know that extracting, even at the cost of the story, is supported and expected when safety requires it.

### Execute The Pre-Existing Plan, Do Not Improvise

Incident response should execute the plan built before deployment: the communication protocol, the check-in escalation triggers, the evacuation routes, the emergency contacts, the legal and embassy support, the medical and evacuation insurance. Under stress, the plan is what prevents paralysis and bad decisions. Improvisation in a crisis produces worse outcomes than a mediocre plan followed faithfully.

If the plan no longer fits the situation, adapt deliberately, with the editor and safety contact involved, rather than silently abandoning it. A reporter who goes dark to handle an incident alone deprives the support system of the chance to help and turns a manageable situation into a crisis. Communication, even imperfect, is the lifeline.

### Communicate Early And Often, Even When Uncertain

In a developing incident, communicate with the editor and safety contact early and often, even when the situation is unclear. Uncertainty is not a reason to delay communication; the support system needs to know there is a problem to begin mobilizing help. A brief message, "situation deteriorating at the protest, moving to secondary location, will update in 30 minutes," is more valuable than silence followed by a detailed account once things have resolved.

Build redundancy into communication: a primary channel, a backup, and a plan for when both fail. If the primary channel is compromised or monitored, switch to the backup. If all channels fail, execute the protocol for loss of contact, which should include the safety contact's escalation triggers. See the physical-risk-assessment skill.

### Set And Respect Extraction Triggers

Before deployment, define the triggers that prompt extraction: a missed check-in, a specific escalation in violence, a credible threat, an arrest, a device seizure, a deteriorating security environment, or a fixed time limit. When a trigger is hit, extract, even if the story feels close. The temptation to stay "just a little longer" is the mechanism by which manageable risks become serious incidents.

Extraction triggers must be respected by both reporter and editor. A reporter who ignores a trigger because the story is good, or an editor who waives a trigger because the coverage is important, has broken the safety system. The triggers exist precisely because judgment under stress is unreliable; they substitute a decision made coldly in advance for one made in fear.

### Coordinate With Editors And Safety Support

Incident response is a team function, not a solo one. The editor and safety contact bring perspective the reporter in the field may have lost, access to resources and information the reporter does not have, and the authority to mobilize evacuation, legal, medical, or consular support. Keep them informed and let them help. A reporter who tries to manage an incident alone often makes it worse.

For serious incidents, injury, detention, kidnapping, or threats, engage professional support immediately: the newsroom's safety advisor, evacuation services, the relevant embassy, local counsel, and organizations that respond to journalist emergencies. Do not wait to see if the situation resolves; the early hours often determine the outcome.

### Protect Local Partners As Well As The Reporter

Local partners, fixers, stringers, and drivers often face greater risk than the visiting reporter, especially after the reporter is extracted. An incident response that secures the reporter but leaves local partners exposed is incomplete and unethical. Plan for their safety in the response: how they will be protected during the incident, whether they need to relocate, and what support they receive after extraction or publication.

Local partners may not have the insurance, accreditation, or consular support that the reporter does, and they remain in the environment after the reporter leaves. Their safety is a continuing obligation, not a one-time concern at the moment of extraction. See the conflict-reporting skill.

### Manage The Aftermath, Including Psychological Care

An incident does not end with extraction. The aftermath includes medical treatment for any injuries, legal response to any arrest or detention, security assessment if the reporter or sources are now at ongoing risk, communication with family and the newsroom, and psychological care for trauma. Plan for the aftermath as deliberately as for the incident itself, and recognize that recovery takes time.

Psychological care is not optional. Reporters who have experienced or witnessed violence, detention, or serious threat should have access to professional support, and the newsroom should normalize its use. Trauma that is untreated compounds across assignments and can end careers. See the harassment-response skill for online threats and campaigns.

### Document The Incident For Learning And Accountability

After the incident, document what happened: the timeline, the decisions, what worked, what failed, and what would be done differently. This documentation supports learning within the newsroom, accountability where the safety system failed, and reporting where the incident itself is newsworthy, such as an assault on press freedom. Treat each incident as a case study that should improve future preparation, not as an embarrassment to move past.

## Common Traps

### Pushing Through For The Story

The instinct to stay for one more quote or one more image, when safety requires extraction, is the mechanism by which incidents become tragedies. The story is never more important than the person.

### Going Dark To Handle It Alone

A reporter who stops communicating to manage an incident alone deprives the support system of the chance to help and often makes the situation worse. Communicate early and often.

### Ignoring Extraction Triggers Under Pressure

Triggers exist because judgment under stress is unreliable. Ignoring a trigger because the situation "feels manageable" substitutes fear-driven judgment for cold advance judgment.

### Securing The Reporter But Leaving Local Partners Exposed

An incident response that extracts the visiting reporter but leaves fixers and local partners to face retaliation is incomplete and unethical. Plan for their safety throughout.

### Delaying Professional Support

For serious incidents, the early hours determine the outcome. Waiting to see if the situation resolves before engaging evacuation, legal, medical, or consular support can close windows that do not reopen.

### Neglecting The Aftermath

An incident does not end with extraction. Medical, legal, security, and psychological aftermath require deliberate management, and untreated trauma compounds over time.

## Self-Check

Before treating incident response as sound, verify:

- Safety of the reporter and team, including local partners, was treated as the first priority, ahead of the story, with this priority established in advance with editors.
- The pre-existing plan was executed rather than improvised under stress, with deliberate adaptation involving the editor and safety contact where the plan no longer fit.
- Communication with the editor and safety contact was early and frequent, with redundancy across channels and a protocol for loss of contact.
- Extraction triggers defined before deployment were respected, with extraction executed when triggers were hit, even at the cost of the story.
- Editors and safety support were coordinated with, and professional support, evacuation, legal, medical, or consular, was engaged immediately for serious incidents.
- Local partners' safety was planned for throughout the response and after extraction, recognizing their continuing exposure.
- The aftermath, including medical, legal, security, and psychological care, was managed deliberately, with professional support for trauma normalized.
- The incident was documented for learning, accountability, and reporting where newsworthy.
- No trigger was ignored or waived under field pressure, and no reporter was pressured to stay when safety required extraction.
- Incident response supports editorial judgment; serious incidents should involve a responsible editor and professional safety, legal, and security advisors, with organizational support for the reporter's recovery.
