# Source Identity Protection

Protecting a source's identity is a continuous obligation that runs from the first conversation through every note, draft, conversation, and published story, and it fails most often not through dramatic betrayal but through small, inadvertent disclosures that no one noticed. The judgment problem is that the details that make a story vivid are often the details that identify a source, and the people who can spot those details, insiders trying to locate a leak, hostile actors seeking retaliation, are often more attentive to them than the reporters and editors who handled the copy. A defensible protection practice treats every detail as a potential identifier, reviews copy systematically from the adversary's perspective, and builds protection into the workflow rather than treating it as a final read-through.

Use this skill when handling notes and drafts that contain source identities, deciding how to describe a source in a story, reviewing copy for inadvertent exposure, or building source protection into the reporting and editing process. The main risk is that an agent protects the source's name while leaving a trail of identifying details that an adversary can recombine to expose them.

## Core Rules

### Treat Identity As A Pattern Of Details, Not A Name

A source's identity is not just their name. It is the combination of role, department, timing of access, unique knowledge, location, phrasings, documents they handled, meetings they attended, and the specific facts only they could have provided. Each detail in isolation may seem anonymous; together, they form a fingerprint that an insider can read. Protecting identity means protecting the pattern, not just removing the name.

When reviewing copy, ask not "is the name here?" but "could someone inside this institution, with knowledge of who had access to what, narrow down the source from these details?" A story that says "a senior official in the division" who attended "a meeting on March 12" and revealed "the contents of the draft memo" may identify the source to any colleague who knows who was in that meeting, even though no name appears. The adversary's perspective, not the reader's, is the test.

### Minimize Identifying Detail At Collection, Not Just At Publication

Protection begins at the moment information is collected. Notes that record exactly who said what, in which meeting, on what date, create a detailed record of the source's access that becomes a liability if the notes are subpoenaed, leaked, or seized. Develop note-taking practices that separate the substance of information from the identifying circumstances of its collection, especially for sensitive sources. Record what is needed to verify and report; do not record gratuitous identifying detail that adds risk without value.

For the most sensitive sources, consider whether certain details need to be written down at all. Some information can be held in memory or in a form that separates it from identifying context. This is a trade-off against verifiability and must be handled carefully, but the default of recording everything is not always the protective choice. See the confidential-sources skill.

### Separate Source Identity From Story Substance Where Possible

Maintain a separation between the records that identify sources and the records that contain the story substance. Notes that link a specific claim to a named source are necessary for verification, but they should be stored securely and separately from the working drafts and the published material, so that a compromise of the working files does not immediately expose the source map. This compartmentalization limits the blast radius of any single breach.

Within the newsroom, restrict knowledge of source identities to those who need it. Editors who review the story for publication may need to know the source's role and reliability, but not every person in the editing chain needs the full identity. The principle of least privilege applies: share identifying detail only with those whose role requires it, and make the sharing deliberate.

### Describe Sources By Role And Access, Honestly And Protectively

The published description of a source must give readers enough to judge credibility without giving adversaries enough to identify the source. This is a genuine tension, and it must be navigated deliberately. "A person familiar with the matter" tells readers almost nothing and is often a cover for weak access; "the only senior engineer who attended the March 12 review meeting" tells readers everything and identifies the source to any colleague.

Work with the source to agree on a description that is honest about their role and basis of knowledge without being so specific that it functions as identification. Where a precise description would expose the source, generalize enough to protect while preserving the credibility signal, and consider whether the story can note, transparently, that the description was generalized to protect the source. See the anonymity-decision-criteria skill.

### Review Copy From The Adversary's Perspective

Before publication, review every detail from the perspective of someone inside the institution or context trying to identify the source. This review is distinct from fact-checking and fairness review, and it should be done by someone who understands the threat. Walk through the story asking: who had access to this specific information, who attended this meeting, who handled this document, who would know this detail, and does the combination of details in this story narrow the field to one person or a small group?

This review often catches exposures that ordinary editing misses, because ordinary editing optimizes for clarity and impact, which push toward specificity, while protection review optimizes for anonymity, which pushes toward generalization. Both matter, and the tension between them must be resolved deliberately, with the source's safety carrying significant weight.

### Protect Identity Across All Channels, Not Just The Story

Source identity leaks through channels beyond the published story: casual mentions to colleagues, overheard phone calls, calendar entries showing meetings, metadata in shared documents, emails forwarded without redaction, and conversations with other sources who may infer or reveal the identity. Build protection across all channels. Be disciplined about whom you discuss the source with, where you take calls, what records you create, and what metadata your tools expose.

A common failure is protecting the source in the story while exposing them in the surrounding workflow: a calendar entry labeled with the source's name, an email to a colleague that names them, a draft stored on a shared drive with track-changes attributing comments. Audit the full workflow, not just the published text, for exposures.

### Plan For The Source's Exposure Beyond The Story

Even a perfectly protected story may expose a source through circumstances outside the copy: the timing of publication relative to the source's access, the institution's reaction prompting an internal investigation, the source's changed behavior under stress, or other reporters pursuing the same sources. Anticipate these exposures with the source in advance. Discuss what they will do if identified despite protections, whether they need legal or relocation support, and how the newsroom will respond.

This planning is part of the protection obligation, not a separate concern. A source who is exposed through circumstances the reporter did not anticipate has still been harmed, and the failure to anticipate is a failure of protection. See the confidential-source-onboarding skill.

## Common Traps

### Protecting The Name While Leaving Identifying Details

Removing the name while leaving role, timing, access, and unique knowledge in the copy exposes the source to any insider who can recombine the details. Protect the pattern, not just the name.

### Recording Gratuitous Identifying Detail In Notes

Notes that capture more identifying context than needed for verification become a liability if subpoenaed, leaked, or seized. Record what is needed; do not record everything by default.

### Optimizing For Vivid Specificity Over Protection

The details that make a story vivid are often the details that identify a source. Resolve the tension deliberately, with the source's safety carrying significant weight, and generalize where specificity would expose.

### Reviewing For Clarity And Fairness But Not For Exposure

Ordinary editing optimizes for clarity and impact, which push toward specificity. A distinct protection review, from the adversary's perspective, catches exposures that ordinary editing misses.

### Leaking Identity Through The Workflow

Calendar entries, emails, shared drafts, and casual mentions can expose a source even when the published story is protected. Audit the full workflow, not just the text.

### Failing To Anticipate Circumstantial Exposure

Even a protected story may expose a source through timing, institutional reaction, or the source's own behavior. Anticipate these exposures with the source and plan for them.

## Self-Check

Before treating source identity protection as sound, verify:

- Identity is treated as a pattern of details, role, timing, access, knowledge, phrasings, not just a name, and copy is reviewed for the combination, not just for the name.
- Identifying detail is minimized at collection, with notes separating substance from identifying circumstances and avoiding gratuitous context.
- Source identity records are stored separately from story substance, with compartmentalization limiting the blast radius of any single breach.
- Source descriptions in the story are honest about role and access while generalized enough to protect, with the tension resolved deliberately and the source's safety weighted heavily.
- Copy is reviewed from the adversary's perspective, by someone who understands the threat, asking who could have provided each detail and whether the combination narrows the field.
- Protection extends across all channels, including casual mentions, calendar entries, emails, shared drafts, and metadata, not just the published text.
- The source's circumstantial exposure, through timing, institutional reaction, and behavior, is anticipated and planned for with the source.
- No detail that would identify the source to a determined insider remains in the published story or the surrounding workflow.
- The protection review is distinct from fact-checking and fairness review, and all three are completed before publication.
- Source identity protection supports editorial judgment; significant protection decisions should involve a responsible editor, and legal counsel where the source's exposure carries legal risk, recognizing that protection is probabilistic and that the source's safety may require foregoing some vivid detail.
