# Source Meeting Security

In-person meetings with sensitive sources are often the most secure channel for high-stakes conversations, but they introduce physical exposure that digital channels do not: the meeting itself can be observed, the location can connect the source to the reporter or the story, travel to and from the meeting can be tracked, and devices brought into the meeting can record or betray the conversation. The judgment problem is that a meeting that feels private may be compromised in ways the participants cannot detect, and that the security of the meeting depends on choices made before, during, and after it, not just on the conversation itself. A defensible practice treats the meeting as a security event, plans the location and logistics to avoid surveillance and association, and disciplines the devices and behaviors that could create a record.

Use this skill when planning in-person meetings with sensitive sources, choosing locations, managing travel, handling devices, or reviewing physical meeting practices. The main risk is that an agent focuses on the conversation's security while ignoring the meeting's physical exposure, or that the meeting location or logistics inadvertently connect the source to the story.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Meeting As A Security Event, Not Just A Conversation

A secure meeting is planned as a whole, from the decision to meet through the departure, with attention to every step that could expose the source. This includes the initial arrangement, the choice of location, the travel of both parties, the meeting itself, the devices present, the records created, and the departure. A conversation that is private in the moment still exposes the source if the meeting was observed, the location connects them, or a device recorded it.

Plan the meeting with the source where possible, agreeing on location, timing, and conduct. Walk through the meeting from the adversary's perspective: who could observe the meeting, who could connect the location to either party, what records the meeting creates, and what each party's travel reveals. The meeting is secure only when every step is accounted for.

### Choose Locations That Do Not Reveal The Relationship Or The Story

The meeting location must not connect the source to the reporter, to the news organization, or to the story. Meeting at the source's workplace, home, or regular haunts exposes them to anyone who observes. Meeting at the reporter's office, regular locations, or a location tied to the news organization exposes the relationship. Meeting near the subject of the story, such as outside the institution being investigated, connects the source to the inquiry.

Choose neutral locations that neither party frequents, that do not require either to travel in a way that reveals the purpose, and that allow private conversation without being overheard or observed. Consider whether the location itself, a particular restaurant, park, or neighborhood, carries associations that could reveal the story. Rotate locations for repeated meetings so that a pattern does not emerge. See the source-identity-protection skill.

### Manage Travel To Avoid Surveillance And Tracking

Travel to and from the meeting can expose both parties, through license plate readers, transit card records, ride-share records, location tracking on phones, CCTV, and physical surveillance. For sensitive meetings, plan travel that does not create a traceable record connecting either party to the location. This may mean avoiding ride-shares and transit cards that log trips, leaving devices that track location behind, traveling by routes that do not reveal the destination, and varying the approach so that surveillance cannot establish a pattern.

Consider whether either party is under active surveillance, and if so, whether a meeting is safe at all. If the source is monitored, traveling to meet may expose the meeting before it occurs. In such cases, the meeting may need to be abandoned, deferred, or conducted through a different channel. The travel plan is part of the meeting's security, not a separate logistical concern.

### Discipline Devices Brought To The Meeting

Devices brought into a sensitive meeting, phones, laptops, smartwatches, fitness trackers, can betray the conversation through location tracking, microphone access, unintentional recording, cloud syncing, and malware. A phone in a pocket still logs its location at the meeting, connecting the reporter and source in time and place. A compromised device can record the conversation. A smartwatch or fitness tracker can log the meeting's location and timing.

For the most sensitive meetings, leave devices behind entirely, or use devices dedicated to sensitive work that do not carry the owner's routine location history. If devices must be brought, consider whether they should be powered off, stored in a Faraday container, or kept away from the conversation. Agree with the source on device conduct before the meeting, and recognize that the source's devices are as much a risk as the reporter's. See the digital-security skill.

### Avoid Creating Records Of The Meeting

A secure meeting creates no record that outlives it. Avoid taking notes on devices that sync or back up; if notes are necessary, use paper that is secured and later destroyed or stored under the source-protection practice. Avoid paying by methods that log the location and time, such as personal credit cards, favoring cash or methods that do not connect the parties. Avoid signing in, using loyalty programs, or otherwise creating a transactional record of presence.

Calendars, messaging apps, and email used to arrange the meeting create records of the meeting's existence, timing, and sometimes location. Arrange sensitive meetings through channels that minimize these records, and avoid putting identifying details in arrangement messages. The meeting's records begin with its arrangement, not with the conversation.

### Assess Whether The Meeting Is Safe Given The Source's Circumstances

Before and during the meeting, assess whether meeting is safe for the source given their circumstances. A source under internal investigation, facing surveillance, or in a volatile personal situation may be exposed by the meeting itself, regardless of how well it is planned. Check in with the source about their circumstances, their sense of safety, and any changes since the last contact. If the source's risk has increased, reconsider whether to meet, when, and how.

During the meeting, remain alert to signs of surveillance, interruption, or the source's distress. Have a plan for ending the meeting quickly and safely if circumstances change. The source's safety is the priority, and a meeting that puts them at risk, even one that advances the story, may need to be cut short or not occur.

### Brief The Source On Meeting Security And Agree On Conduct

A secure meeting requires both parties to follow the plan, and the source may not share the reporter's assumptions about what is safe. Brief the source on the meeting's security plan, in terms they can act on: where to meet, how to travel, what to bring and not bring, what to do if they feel they are being followed or observed, and what to do if the meeting is interrupted. Agree on conduct before the meeting, and confirm the source understands and can follow it.

This briefing is part of the source relationship, not a one-time instruction. Sources under stress may forget or disregard security practices, and repeated meetings require renewed attention. A source who cannot follow the security plan may need a different channel, additional coaching, or a reassessment of whether the story can be reported safely.

## Common Traps

### Focusing On The Conversation While Ignoring Physical Exposure

A private conversation still exposes the source if the meeting was observed, the location connects them, or a device recorded it. Plan the meeting as a whole, not just the talk.

### Meeting At Locations That Reveal The Relationship Or Story

Workplaces, homes, regular haunts, and locations tied to the news organization or the story connect the source to the inquiry. Choose neutral, varied locations.

### Letting Travel Create A Traceable Record

Ride-shares, transit cards, location-tracking phones, and predictable routes connect both parties to the meeting location. Plan travel that minimizes traceable records.

### Bringing Devices That Betray The Meeting

Phones, laptops, smartwatches, and fitness trackers log location, may record, and can be compromised. Leave devices behind or discipline them for sensitive meetings.

### Creating Transactional Records Of Presence

Credit cards, loyalty programs, sign-ins, and calendar entries create records of the meeting. Use cash, avoid sign-ins, and arrange meetings through channels that minimize records.

### Assuming The Source Shares The Reporter's Security Assumptions

The source may not understand or follow the security plan without briefing. Agree on conduct before the meeting, and confirm the source can follow it.

## Self-Check

Before treating source meeting security as sound, verify:

- The meeting is planned as a security event from arrangement through departure, with every step that could expose the source accounted for from the adversary's perspective.
- The location does not connect the source to the reporter, the news organization, or the story, and is neutral, varied, and allows private conversation.
- Travel to and from the meeting does not create traceable records connecting either party to the location, considering ride-shares, transit cards, location tracking, CCTV, and surveillance.
- Devices brought to the meeting are disciplined or left behind, recognizing that phones, laptops, smartwatches, and fitness trackers log location, may record, and can be compromised.
- The meeting creates no records that outlive it, with notes on secured paper, payment by cash or non-connecting methods, and arrangement through channels that minimize records.
- The source's circumstances are assessed before and during the meeting, with a willingness to cut short or cancel if their risk has increased.
- The source is briefed on the security plan and agrees on conduct, with confirmation they understand and can follow it, recognizing that sources under stress may need renewed attention.
- Repeated meetings use varied locations and approaches so that a pattern does not emerge.
- The meeting security plan is consistent with the source's threat model and the overall source-protection practice.
- Source meeting security supports editorial judgment; meeting plans for high-risk sources should involve a responsible editor and, where the source faces surveillance or physical threat, a security adviser or legal counsel, recognizing that no meeting is risk-free and that the source's safety may require foregoing the meeting.
