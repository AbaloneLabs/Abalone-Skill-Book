# Character Portrayal

When journalism portrays real people, it shapes how readers see them, sometimes for a moment, sometimes durably, sometimes with consequences for the subject's life and reputation. The harm this skill prevents is portrayal that distorts: the flattening of a complex person into a type or caricature, the selective emphasis that serves the story's narrative at the subject's expense, the stereotype that substitutes for understanding, or the invasion of private life unrelated to public significance. Fair, full character portrayal is a craft of representing subjects as complex human beings, with the context and dignity that honest journalism requires, while still telling a vivid and true story.

## Core Rules

### Portray subjects as complex, not flattened into types
Real people are contradictory, context-shaped, and multifaceted. Resist the narrative temptation to reduce a subject to a single trait, hero, villain, eccentric, victim. Include the detail and context that convey complexity, even where it complicates a cleaner story. Fair portrayal represents the whole person, not the convenient slice.

### Let subjects reveal themselves through their own words and actions
The most honest portrayal comes from the subject's own speech, behavior, and choices, presented accurately and in context, rather than from the reporter's characterization. Show the subject acting and speaking; let readers form judgments from evidence rather than from the reporter's verdict. This requires rich observation and fair selection.

### Balance virtues and flaws proportionally to the truth
Every person has strengths and weaknesses; honest portrayal represents both, in proportion to their actual prominence in the person's character and their relevance to the story. Inflating flaws to manufacture conflict, or suppressing them to flatter, both distort. Calibrate the balance to the reality and to the story's legitimate scope.

### Avoid stereotypes and assumptions
Do not let the subject's demographic, profession, or background determine the portrayal through assumption. Stereotypes substitute lazy familiarity for genuine understanding and harm both the subject and the reader. Portray each person as an individual, observed and reported, not as a representative of a category.

### Distinguish public significance from private life
Include personal detail only where it bears on the story's public significance or is genuinely necessary to understand the subject in context. Private matters unrelated to significance should not be exposed merely for color or interest. The fact that information is obtainable does not mean publishing it serves the public or treats the subject fairly.

### Provide context for actions and statements
A quote or action presented without context can mislead. Provide the circumstances, history, and motivation that let readers understand the subject's behavior fairly. Context is not excuse-making; it is the difference between understanding and misreading.

### Give subjects fair opportunity to shape their portrayal
Where portrayal may be unflattering or significant, give the subject the chance to respond, provide context, and correct factual errors. Incorporate their input fairly. Subjects cannot control their portrayal, and should not, but they are entitled to fairness and accuracy in how they are depicted.

### Recognize the power asymmetry between journalist and subject
The journalist has the platform and the last word; the subject lives with the consequences. This asymmetry demands care, especially with subjects who are not public figures, who did not seek the attention, or who are vulnerable. Wield the power of portrayal with the responsibility its one-sidedness requires.

## Common Traps

### The narrative caricature
Reducing a complex person to a single vivid trait, the colorful eccentric, the sinister villain, the saintly victim, makes a cleaner story but misrepresents the subject. Complexity is honesty.

### Selective emphasis to serve the story
Choosing only the details that fit the desired portrayal, and omitting those that complicate it, manufactures a distortion presented as observation. Portray the whole, not the convenient part.

### Stereotype as shortcut
Letting category assumptions, demographic, regional, professional, stand in for observed individuality produces portrayals that are both lazy and harmful. See the individual.

### Invasion of irrelevant private life
Exposing personal details that do not bear on public significance, for color or reader interest, violates the subject's privacy without journalistic justification. Significance, not obtainability, should govern.

### Decontextualized quotes and actions
Present a subject's words or behavior stripped of the context that gives them meaning, and readers misread. Context is essential to fair portrayal.

### The unchallenged assumption
Accepting a subject's self-portrayal, or a source's characterization, without independent reporting produces a portrait that may flatter or distort. Corroborate and complicate.

### Forgetting the consequences
The story moves on; the subject lives with the portrayal. Failing to consider the real-world consequences of how a person is depicted, especially a private or vulnerable one, is an ethical failure.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I portrayed the subject as a complex individual rather than flattening them into a type or caricature?
- [ ] Does the portrayal emerge from the subject's own words and actions, fairly presented, rather than from my characterization?
- [ ] Are virtues and flaws represented in proportion to their actual prominence and relevance?
- [ ] Have I avoided stereotypes and category assumptions, portraying the person as an observed individual?
- [ ] Is personal detail included only where it bears on public significance or necessary context?
- [ ] Have I provided the context that lets readers fairly understand the subject's actions and statements?
- [ ] Did the subject have a fair opportunity to respond, provide context, and correct errors?
- [ ] Have I recognized and acted on the power asymmetry between journalist and subject, especially for vulnerable subjects?
- [ ] Have I avoided selective emphasis that serves the story at the subject's expense?
- [ ] Would the subject, even if uncomfortable with aspects, recognize the portrayal as fair and not a distortion?
