# Campaign And Election Coverage

Election coverage is where journalism's obligations to accuracy, fairness, and public service are most visible and most contested. The judgment problem is that campaigns are sophisticated media operations designed to shape coverage, and reporters must distinguish genuine news from stage-managed theater, weigh horse-race narrative against substantive policy, resist manipulation by both access and hostility, and serve voters who depend on the coverage to make a consequential decision. A reporter who chases the horse race produces coverage that entertains but does not inform; one who avoids conflict produces access journalism that flatters campaigns at voters' expense. The discipline is to cover the election as a public-service obligation, prioritizing what voters need to know over what campaigns want covered.

Use this skill when covering campaigns and elections, handling polling and platforms, and managing campaign access and fairness decisions. The main risk is that an agent may default to horse-race framing, amplify unverified claims, or grant favorable coverage in exchange for access. This skill is editorial guidance only; election law, including rules on equal time and contributions, requires specialist legal advice, and editorial standards should be set by senior leadership.

## Core Rules

### Prioritize Voter Information Over Campaign Narratives

The purpose of election coverage is to help voters make an informed decision, not to entertain them with spectacle or to advance a campaign's preferred storyline. Evaluate every story against this standard: does it give voters information they need about candidates' records, platforms, fitness, and the stakes, or does it recycle campaign spin, gaffes, and trivia? Campaigns will push the latter relentlessly, because spectacle favors them and crowds out substance. Resist the gravitational pull of the horse race and consciously allocate reporting effort to policy, records, and consequences.

When a campaign event is theater, report it as theater, not as news.

### Balance Horse-Race And Policy Coverage

Horse-race coverage, who is winning, who is surging, who is faltering, is not illegitimate, but it crowds out policy when it dominates. Voters deserve to know the state of the race, but they need far more to know how to vote. Allocate substantial coverage to candidates' platforms, their records, the feasibility and cost of their proposals, and the interests behind them. Analyze policy claims rigorously, checking them against independent evidence and experts, rather than reporting them as he-said-she-said. The balance should tilt toward substance, because that is what campaigns underprovide.

Track the ratio of horse-race to policy stories over the cycle and correct it when it drifts.

### Scrutinize Polls And Polling Aggregators Rigorously

Polls are estimates, not oracles, and their misuse distorts coverage. Before reporting a poll, assess its quality: the methodology, sample size, margin of error, likely-voter versus registered-voter screen, and the pollster's track record. Report the margin of error and the confidence interval, and avoid declaring a lead that falls within it. Distinguish between national and state-level polling, especially in systems with electoral colleges or district-based races, where national polls can be misleading. Be honest about what polls cannot tell you, including turnout, late deciders, and the effect of events. Do not present a single poll as a trend, or a trend as a certainty.

Polling aggregators are tools, not authorities; understand their weighting and treat their outputs with appropriate caution.

### Resist Manipulation By Access And Exclusives

Campaigns offer access, interviews, leaks, and exclusives to reward favorable coverage and punish critical coverage. This creates a structural pressure toward access journalism, where reporters soften coverage to preserve the relationship. Recognize this dynamic and resist it: the value of access is the information it yields, not the relationship itself, and no access is worth distorting coverage. Be willing to report critically even when it costs access, and build reporting that does not depend on campaign cooperation, including records, former officials, and independent experts. Editors should monitor whether reporters are going soft to protect relationships.

An exclusive that is actually a campaign plant is not a scoop; it is stenography.

### Apply Fairness Across Candidates

Fairness in election coverage means giving each viable candidate scrutiny proportionate to their standing and the stakes, not identical coverage regardless of relevance. A frontrunner's record deserves more scrutiny than a long-shot candidate's, because the consequences are greater; but every candidate deserves a fair hearing on their merits. Do not tilt coverage to favor or disfavor a candidate, and be honest about the difference between scrutiny, which serves voters, and bias, which serves the journalist's preference. Apply the same standards of evidence and verification to all candidates, and avoid false equivalence that treats unequal claims as equally valid.

Document the rationale for play and scrutiny decisions so they can be reviewed.

### Verify Claims And Counter Disinformation

Campaigns make claims, about their records, their opponents, the economy, crime, and more, that may be false, misleading, or stripped of context. Verify claims against independent evidence and report the findings clearly, including when a claim is false. Do not simply relay competing claims as a he-said-she-said; that abdicates the journalist's role and leaves voters to guess. Develop the capacity to identify and counter disinformation, including manipulated media and coordinated inauthentic behavior, and report on it as a story in itself when it affects the election. Speed matters, but accuracy matters more.

When correcting a false claim, avoid amplifying it; state the truth clearly and explain why the claim is wrong.

### Cover The Stakes And The Electorate

Election coverage should tell voters not only who is running and what they propose, but what is at stake: which policies will change, which rights are at risk, which interests will benefit, and how the result will affect different communities. Cover the electorate itself, including who can vote, who is being disenfranchised, how districts are drawn, and how the rules shape the outcome. These structural stories are often undercovered because they are harder, but they are essential to understanding the election. Do not treat the election as a game whose rules are fixed; report on the rules and who sets them.

Voter suppression and election administration are news, not background.

### Protect Against Coordinated Influence Operations

Elections are targets for domestic and foreign influence operations that use social media, hacked material, and planted stories to shape coverage. Be alert to the signs: unsolicited leaked material timed to the cycle, suspicious social media accounts amplifying a narrative, and stories that align with a known influence operation's goals. Verify the provenance of leaks, assess the motivation behind their release, and consider whether publishing serves the public or the manipulator. Coordinate with platforms and security researchers where appropriate, and report on influence operations as a story. Do not become a conduit for a covert operation.

A scoop that serves a foreign power's operation is a failure, not a triumph.

## Common Traps

### Horse-Race Dominance

Coverage that focuses on who is winning entertains but fails to inform voters about what matters.

### Access Journalism

Softening coverage to preserve campaign access trades voter service for reporter convenience.

### Poll Misuse

Reporting a single poll as a trend, or a within-margin lead as a real one, distorts the race.

### False Equivalence

Treating unequal claims as equally valid abdicates the verification role and misleads voters.

### Stenography Of Campaign Spin

Relaying campaign narratives as news, especially as exclusives, makes the reporter a tool of the campaign.

### Amplifying Disinformation

Repeating a false claim to debunk it can still amplify it; correction requires care.

### Ignoring Structural Stories

Voter suppression, gerrymandering, and election administration are essential but often undercovered.

### Conduit For Influence Operations

Publishing hacked or planted material without assessing its provenance serves the manipulator.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does each story prioritize voter information over campaign narratives, distinguishing genuine news from stage-managed theater?
- [ ] Is coverage balanced between horse-race and policy, with substantial scrutiny of platforms, records, and consequences?
- [ ] Are polls reported with methodology, margin of error, and appropriate caution about what they cannot show?
- [ ] Is coverage resistant to manipulation by access and exclusives, with reporting that does not depend on campaign cooperation?
- [ ] Is fairness applied across candidates, with scrutiny proportionate to standing and the stakes, and without false equivalence?
- [ ] Are claims verified against independent evidence, with false claims reported as false rather than relayed as he-said-she-said?
- [ ] Does coverage address the stakes, the electorate, and the structural rules of the election, including suppression and administration?
- [ ] Are influence operations identified and assessed, with the provenance and motivation of leaked material examined before publication?
- [ ] Have fairness and standards decisions been reviewed by senior editors, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance only and that election law and equal-time rules require specialist legal advice?
