# Subpoena And Compelled Disclosure

Subpoenas and other legal demands for a journalist's unpublished material, notes, or source identities are among the most serious legal threats to journalism because they seek to turn the reporter into an arm of law enforcement or a party to litigation, destroying the confidentiality that sources rely on and chilling future reporting. The judgment problem is that compliance betrays sources and the reporting function, while resistance carries legal risk including contempt and jail, and the reporter is caught between duties to sources, the public, and their own safety. A defensible response treats any subpoena as a serious matter requiring immediate legal counsel, asserts available reporter privilege, and resists compelled disclosure of source identities and unpublished material while recognizing that privilege is partial, jurisdiction-dependent, and not absolute.

Use this skill when responding to a subpoena or legal demand for unpublished material or source identities, asserting privilege, or planning source protection against compelled disclosure. The main risk is that an agent responds to a subpoena as a routine legal matter, fails to assert privilege timely, or complies under pressure without understanding the consequences for sources and future reporting.

## Core Rules

### Treat Any Subpoena As A Serious Matter Requiring Immediate Counsel

A subpoena for a journalist's material is not a routine legal request but a serious threat to the reporting function, and it requires immediate involvement of legal counsel experienced in media law. Do not respond, acknowledge, or comply with a subpoena before counsel reviews it. The timeline for responding, asserting privilege, and moving to quash is short, and missed deadlines can result in waiver of rights or default compliance.

Notify the editor and counsel immediately upon receiving any subpoena, demand letter, or court order seeking unpublished material, notes, testimony, or source information. Preserve potentially responsive material but do not produce it, do not discuss the underlying reporting in a way that could waive privilege, and let counsel drive the response. Treating a subpoena as routine, or attempting to handle it without counsel, risks both the source protection and the reporter's legal position.

### Preserve Potentially Responsive Material But Do Not Produce It

Upon receiving a subpoena, the reporter and newsroom have a duty to preserve potentially responsive material, to prevent claims of destruction or spoliation, but preservation is not production. Preserve the material, including notes, drafts, communications, and unpublished material, in a form that maintains privilege claims, and do not produce any of it to the requesting party until the privilege questions are resolved.

Do not destroy material in response to a subpoena, which can constitute obstruction or spoliation regardless of the underlying privilege questions. Do not produce material voluntarily, which can waive privilege and undermine later resistance. The posture is preserve and resist, with counsel managing the legal process, until the court resolves the privilege claims or the newsroom makes a deliberate decision about compliance.

### Assert Available Reporter Privilege Timely And Specifically

Reporter privilege, the legal protection against compelled disclosure of unpublished material and source identities, varies by jurisdiction and is partial rather than absolute. In the United States, some states have shield laws protecting reporters, federal privilege is uncertain after conflicting court decisions, and many other countries have varying protections. Assert available privilege timely, through counsel, in response to the subpoena, because failure to assert can result in waiver.

Assert privilege specifically, identifying the material or testimony sought and the privilege grounds, rather than making a blanket objection. The stronger and more specific the privilege assertion, the better the position in any motion to quash. Understand the limits of privilege in the relevant jurisdiction: it may not protect against criminal subpoenas in some contexts, it may be qualified rather than absolute, and it may be balanced against the requesting party's need. See the media-law skill.

### Move To Quash And Litigate Resistance Where Appropriate

Where privilege is asserted and the subpoena is not withdrawn, the path is to move to quash, asking the court to invalidate the subpoena, and to litigate resistance through the courts. This is a serious undertaking with costs and risks, including the risk of contempt and, in extreme cases, jail for the reporter who refuses to comply with a final court order. The decision to litigate resistance is made by the newsroom, with counsel, weighing the source protection duty against the costs and risks.

Resistance is not always the right choice; there may be cases where the material sought does not implicate source confidentiality, where the public interest favors disclosure, or where the costs of resistance outweigh the benefits. But the default for subpoenas seeking source identities or unpublished material is resistance, because compliance destroys the confidentiality that reporting depends on. The decision to comply, where made, should be deliberate and explained, not a default under pressure.

### Protect Source Identities As The Highest Priority

Within the material a subpoena may seek, source identities are the highest priority for protection, because disclosure betrays the specific people who trusted the reporter and chills all future sources. Resist disclosure of source identities with the full weight of available privilege and litigation, recognizing that the duty to sources is a foundational ethical obligation, not merely a preference.

For unpublished material that does not reveal sources, the calculus may differ, but source-identifying information, contact details, communications that reveal sources, and notes that link information to sources, must be protected. Build source protection into the record-keeping so that even compelled production of some material does not reveal sources. See the source-identity-protection skill.

### Plan For Source Protection Against Compelled Disclosure In Advance

The best protection against compelled disclosure is built into the reporting practice before any subpoena arrives. Minimize the records that link sources to information, separate source-identifying records from story substance, and consider what material would be responsive and damaging if compelled. For the most sensitive investigations, plan the record-keeping with compelled disclosure in mind, so that the reporter could comply with a narrowly tailored order without betraying sources.

This planning is part of the source protection practice, not a separate concern. A reporter whose notes comprehensively link every claim to a named source has no protection against compelled disclosure regardless of privilege, because an order compelling the notes compels the identities. A reporter whose notes separate substance from identity has more room to resist or comply narrowly. See the source-identity-protection skill.

### Support The Reporter Through The Legal Process

A subpoena and the resulting litigation impose significant burdens and risks on the individual reporter, who may face contempt, jail, legal costs, and professional consequences. The newsroom must support the reporter through the process, providing legal representation, covering costs, and backing the resistance publicly and institutionally. A reporter resisting compelled disclosure on behalf of the journalism should not face the consequences alone.

This support is part of the duty of care and part of the institutional commitment to source protection. A newsroom that compels or allows a reporter to face subpoena consequences without backing fails both the reporter and the principle. Plan for this support in advance, as part of the newsroom's subpoena response plan, so that it is available when needed rather than improvised under pressure.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Subpoena As Routine

A subpoena for journalistic material is a serious threat to the reporting function. Involve counsel immediately and do not respond before review.

### Producing Material Voluntarily Or Destroying It

Preserve potentially responsive material but do not produce it, which can waive privilege, or destroy it, which can constitute spoliation.

### Failing To Assert Privilege Timely And Specifically

Missed deadlines and blanket objections can result in waiver or weak positions. Assert privilege timely and specifically through counsel.

### Defaulting To Compliance Under Pressure

Compliance destroys source confidentiality and chills future reporting. The default for source-identifying material is resistance, with compliance deliberate if chosen.

### Failing To Protect Source Identities As The Highest Priority

Source identities are the core of the protection duty. Resist their disclosure with the full weight of privilege and litigation.

### Not Planning Record-Keeping For Compelled Disclosure

Notes that link every claim to a named source provide no protection against compelled production. Plan record-keeping to separate substance from identity.

### Leaving The Reporter To Face The Process Alone

Subpoena resistance imposes burdens and risks on the reporter. The newsroom must provide legal, financial, and institutional support.

## Self-Check

Before treating a subpoena response as sound, verify:

- Any subpoena, demand letter, or court order seeking journalistic material triggers immediate involvement of media law counsel, with no response, acknowledgment, or compliance before review.
- Potentially responsive material is preserved in a form maintaining privilege, with no production to the requesting party and no destruction that could constitute spoliation.
- Available reporter privilege is asserted timely and specifically through counsel, identifying the material and grounds, with awareness of the jurisdiction's protections and limits.
- The decision to move to quash and litigate resistance is made by the newsroom with counsel, weighing source protection against costs and risks, with resistance the default for source-identifying material.
- Source identities are treated as the highest protection priority, with resistance to their disclosure carrying the full weight of privilege and litigation.
- Record-keeping is planned for compelled disclosure in advance, separating substance from identity so that narrowly tailored compliance need not betray sources.
- The reporter receives legal representation, cost coverage, and institutional support through the process, with the newsroom backing the resistance publicly.
- The response is coordinated across counsel, editors, and the reporter, with clear roles and communication.
- The privilege assertions and resistance strategy are documented for the legal record and for accountability.
- Subpoena and compelled disclosure response supports editorial judgment; all responses should involve media law counsel, recognizing that reporter privilege is partial and jurisdiction-dependent, that resistance may carry serious legal risk including contempt, and that decisions about compliance or resistance after court orders require professional legal advice and senior editorial judgment.
