# Journalist Legal Risk Assessment

Legal risk assessment in journalism is the practice of identifying, before publication, the ways a story could expose the journalist or newsroom to legal liability, defamation, privacy invasion, copyright infringement, breach of contract, or subpoena, and deciding how to mitigate those risks while preserving the journalism. The judgment problem is that legal risk and public interest often pull in opposite directions, the most important stories often carry the most risk, and the assessment must avoid two opposite failures: suppressing or softening important journalism out of vague legal fear, and publishing recklessly without identifying avoidable exposure. A defensible assessment identifies specific risks against the specific facts and jurisdiction, distinguishes real exposure from chilling speculation, and involves legal counsel at the right points without surrendering editorial judgment to legal caution.

Use this skill when assessing legal risk before publication, deciding when to involve counsel, or weighing public interest against legal exposure. The main risk is that an agent treats legal risk as a single vague fear to be avoided rather than as specific exposures to be identified and managed, or that legal caution overrides editorial judgment without a genuine conflict.

## Core Rules

### Identify Specific Risks Against Specific Facts And Jurisdiction

Legal risk is not a single quantity but a set of specific exposures that depend on the facts of the story, the jurisdiction, and the legal claims that could be brought. A story may carry defamation risk if it makes damaging false assertions about an identifiable person, privacy risk if it discloses private facts or intrudes on seclusion, copyright risk if it uses protected material without authorization, contract risk if it breaches a source agreement or NDA, and subpoena risk if it puts the reporter in possession of sought-after evidence. Each risk has its own elements, defenses, and mitigation.

Assess the story against these specific risks, in the relevant jurisdiction, rather than treating legal risk as a general caution. Different jurisdictions have different defamation standards, privacy protections, and reporter shield laws, and a story that is low-risk in one may be high-risk in another. Identify the jurisdiction whose law applies, often where the plaintiff is located or the publication reaches, and assess against that law. Vague legal fear that is not tied to a specific claim in a specific jurisdiction chills journalism without protecting it. See the media-law skill.

### Distinguish Real Exposure From Chilling Speculation

Not every legal risk a story could theoretically carry is a risk worth changing the journalism for. Plaintiffs can threaten or file meritless claims, and the fear of litigation, even unsuccessful litigation, can chill journalism as effectively as a valid claim. Distinguish real exposure, claims that have legal merit and a realistic chance of success, from chilling speculation, threats that would fail but that the newsroom fears for cost or distraction.

This distinction requires understanding the legal standards and the story's ability to meet them. A well-reported, well-documented story that makes accurate assertions and can prove them carries less defamation risk than the subject's threats suggest, because truth and substantial truth are defenses. A story that relies on unverified allegations or implies damaging conclusions it cannot support carries real risk regardless of how carefully it is worded. Assess the actual merit of potential claims, with counsel, rather than reacting to the volume of threats.

### Involve Legal Counsel At The Right Points

Legal counsel should be involved at points where their expertise adds value: when a story carries specific, identified risks; when the legal standards are complex or uncertain; when the story makes serious allegations that could trigger litigation; or when the newsroom needs to make a public-interest judgment against legal risk. Involving counsel too late, after the story is locked, limits their ability to help; involving them on every routine story wastes resources and can over-legalize the journalism.

The right involvement is early enough to shape the reporting and the editing, focused on the specific risks, and framed as risk management rather than editorial control. Bring counsel the story, the reporting behind it, and the specific questions, not a request for a generic blessing. And maintain the distinction between legal advice and editorial judgment: counsel advises on risk, editors decide on publication. See the section below.

### Preserve Editorial Judgment Against Legal Caution

Legal counsel's role is to assess and advise on risk, not to decide what to publish, and a newsroom that surrenders editorial judgment to legal caution fails its readers. There are stories where the public interest justifies legal risk, where the journalism is sound and the exposure, while real, is worth bearing to publish important information. These decisions are editorial, made by editors accountable for the journalism, informed by but not dictated by legal advice.

The dynamic to avoid is one where legal caution becomes a veto, where any identified risk leads to softening or killing the story regardless of public interest. This serves the subjects who would face scrutiny, not the public. Conversely, the dynamic to avoid is one where legal advice is ignored entirely and risk is dismissed. The healthy dynamic is one where counsel identifies and quantifies risk, editors weigh it against public interest and make the call, and both roles are respected. See the defamation skill.

### Document The Reporting To Support Defenses

Strong legal defenses in journalism, truth, substantial truth, fair report, opinion, and public interest, depend on the reporting behind the story, and that reporting must be documented in a way that can support the defense if challenged. Document the sources, the evidence, the verification, and the basis for each assertion, so that if a claim is filed, the newsroom can demonstrate the work that supports the story.

This documentation is part of legal risk management, not separate from it. A story that is true but cannot be proven true carries the same defamation risk as a false story, because the defense depends on the ability to prove. Build documentation into the reporting practice, and ensure that the basis for each significant assertion is recorded in a form that survives the news cycle. See the reporting-and-source-verification skill.

### Plan For Post-Publication Legal Response

Legal risk does not end at publication; in many cases it begins, as subjects threaten or file claims in response to the story. Plan for the post-publication legal response: who handles complaints and threats, how requests for correction or retraction are assessed and responded to, when counsel is re-engaged, and how the newsroom supports the story and the reporter if litigation follows.

A planned response prevents panic and inconsistency when a threat arrives. It also ensures that legitimate correction requests are handled promptly and accurately, which both serves accuracy and, in many jurisdictions, supports legal positions by demonstrating good faith. Treat the post-publication legal response as part of the story's lifecycle, not as an unforeseen crisis.

### Account For The Reporter's Personal Legal Exposure

Legal risk in journalism falls not only on the newsroom but on the individual reporter, who may be named in lawsuits, subpoenaed, or, in some jurisdictions and situations, face criminal exposure. The assessment must account for the reporter's personal exposure and the newsroom's obligation to support them. This includes indemnification, legal representation, and protection against compelled disclosure of sources and notes.

Reporters should not face personal legal exposure for newsroom-directed journalism without the newsroom's backing, and the assessment should confirm that backing before publication. For freelancers, who may lack institutional support, the assessment of personal exposure is especially important, and the decision to publish may depend on the available protection. See the source-protection skill for related concerns about compelled disclosure.

## Common Traps

### Treating Legal Risk As A Single Vague Fear

Legal risk is specific exposures in specific jurisdictions. Assess against actual claims and law, not general caution.

### Chilling Journalism Over Meritless Threats

Plaintiffs can threaten or file meritless claims. Distinguish real exposure from chilling speculation, with counsel.

### Involving Counsel Too Late Or Too Generically

Bring counsel early enough to shape the story, focused on specific risks, not for generic blessing.

### Surrendering Editorial Judgment To Legal Caution

Counsel advises on risk; editors decide on publication. Do not let legal caution become a veto over public-interest journalism.

### Failing To Document The Reporting For Defenses

Defenses depend on the documented reporting. Build documentation into the practice so the basis for each assertion survives.

### Ignoring Post-Publication Legal Response

Legal risk often begins at publication. Plan the response to complaints, corrections, and litigation.

### Overlooking The Reporter's Personal Exposure

Legal risk falls on the individual reporter as well as the newsroom. Account for their exposure and the newsroom's backing.

## Self-Check

Before treating a legal risk assessment as sound, verify:

- Legal risks are identified as specific exposures, defamation, privacy, copyright, contract, subpoena, against the specific facts of the story and the applicable jurisdiction, not as a single vague fear.
- Real exposure is distinguished from chilling speculation, with counsel, by assessing the legal standards and the story's ability to meet them, rather than reacting to threat volume.
- Legal counsel is involved at the right points, early enough to shape reporting and editing, focused on specific risks, and framed as risk management rather than editorial control.
- Editorial judgment is preserved against legal caution, with editors weighing risk against public interest and making the publication decision, informed by but not dictated by legal advice.
- The reporting is documented to support defenses, with sources, evidence, verification, and the basis for each assertion recorded in a form that survives the news cycle.
- A post-publication legal response is planned, covering complaints, correction requests, counsel re-engagement, and litigation support.
- The reporter's personal legal exposure is accounted for, with newsroom backing, indemnification, and representation confirmed before publication.
- The assessment distinguishes between risks that warrant changing the journalism and risks that warrant accepting with mitigation.
- The assessment is documented and reviewed by a responsible editor and legal counsel before publication of high-risk stories.
- Journalist legal risk assessment supports editorial judgment; significant risk decisions should involve legal counsel and a senior editor, recognizing that this skill does not provide legal advice, that journalists are not lawyers, and that the public interest may justify bearing identified and mitigated legal risk after professional consultation.
