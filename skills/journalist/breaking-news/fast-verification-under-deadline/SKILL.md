# Fast Verification Under Deadline

Breaking news compresses the verification process into minutes, sometimes seconds. The pressure to publish first is immense, and the cost of error is amplified because early, wrong reports spread fastest and are hardest to fully correct. The harm this skill prevents is the publication of unverified rumors, misidentified victims, inflated casualty figures, or false attributions that mislead the public during moments of maximum attention and anxiety, and that damage the outlet's credibility for far longer than the scoop was worth. Fast verification is not the absence of verification; it is a disciplined, triaged version of it that protects the most consequential facts even when time is short.

## Core Rules

### Triage claims by consequence and verify accordingly
Not every fact carries equal weight. The identity of a named suspect, a death toll, a claim of responsibility, and an accusation against a specific person are high-consequence and demand the strictest verification you can muster in the time available. Background color, atmospheric detail, and low-stakes context can be published with lighter checking. Sort incoming information by the harm that would result from error, and apply your scarce time where the stakes are highest.

### Establish a verification threshold before you need it
Decide in advance, with editors, what standard a claim must meet to go live. A single official source? Two independent sources? On-record only? Having the threshold agreed beforehand prevents frantic, ad-hoc decisions under pressure and ensures consistency across a fast-moving story.

### Distinguish confirmed, reported, and unconfirmed
Maintain and communicate explicit status labels. "Confirmed" means you have met your verification standard. "Reported by others" means another outlet has it but you have not. "Unconfirmed" means it is circulating without support. Never let these categories blur, and tell readers which is which. "Police say" is not the same as "it happened."

### Prioritize official and primary sources for the hardest facts
For casualty figures, identities, and official actions, the primary authority, police, medical examiners, government agencies, is the appropriate source, while remembering officials can be wrong or slow. Go to the primary source rather than relying on aggregations, and note the source and its timestamp explicitly.

### Beware the echo chamber of early reports
In the first minutes of breaking news, a single unconfirmed claim can ripple across outlets and social media until it appears confirmed by repetition. Trace claims to their origin rather than counting mentions. "Everyone is reporting it" often means everyone is reporting the same one unverified source.

### Apply extra scrutiny to the dramatic and the convenient
The most viral early claims, shocking footage, dramatic quotes, simple villains, are the most likely to be wrong or manipulated, and the most tempting to publish. Treat convenience and drama as warning signs, not as signals of truth. The claim that fits the emerging narrative perfectly deserves the most skepticism.

### Update transparently and correct immediately
When new information supersedes earlier reporting, update clearly, noting what changed and why. When you got something wrong, correct it promptly and visibly. Speed in correction partially limits the harm of error; silence or stealth edits compound it. Readers trust outlets that correct themselves openly.

### Know when to wait
The hardest discipline in breaking news is deciding not to publish something you cannot yet verify, even when competitors have it. Being second and right beats being first and wrong. Cultivate the willingness to hold a claim until it clears your threshold, and make that willingness a point of editorial pride, not weakness.

## Common Traps

### Publishing because competitors have it
The fear of being beaten leads to publishing unverified claims simply because rivals did. This imports their errors and makes you responsible for them. Verify to your own standard regardless of what others are doing.

### Treating official statements as confirmed fact
Officials are often working from incomplete early information and have incentives to shape the narrative. Report what they say, attributed and timestamped, but do not convert "authorities say" into unqualified assertion of fact.

### Trusting viral eyewitness content without verification
Dramatic eyewitness video and photos spread instantly and are often miscaptioned, old, or from a different event. Reverse-search, geolocate, and seek corroboration before amplifying, especially for high-stakes imagery.

### Inflating casualty figures from early rumors
Early death and injury counts are notoriously unreliable, often inflated by panic or understated by chaos. Report figures only from authoritative sources, attribute them clearly, and avoid presenting tentative numbers as final.

### Misidentifying suspects or victims
Naming the wrong person, whether a suspect or a victim, causes immediate and severe harm. Confirm identities through official channels or multiple independent sources before publishing any name. The social-media-driven misidentification of suspects is a recurring, avoidable disaster.

### Blurring attribution to sound more authoritative
Writing "the explosion was caused by X" when you actually have "officials suspect X" overclaims the evidence and sets up a correction. Match the verb and attribution precisely to what you actually know.

### Failing to correct early errors visibly
Stealth-editing or quietly dropping a wrong claim without noting the correction leaves the false information alive in readers' memories and in competitors' stories. Visible, prompt correction is the only way to limit the damage.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I triaged incoming claims by consequence, applying the strictest verification to the highest-stakes facts?
- [ ] Was a verification threshold agreed with editors in advance, and am I applying it consistently?
- [ ] Am I explicitly distinguishing confirmed, reported-by-others, and unconfirmed claims, and labeling them for readers?
- [ ] For casualty figures, identities, and official actions, am I relying on primary authorities with clear attribution and timestamps?
- [ ] Have I traced circulating claims to their origin rather than treating repetition as confirmation?
- [ ] Did I apply extra scrutiny to the most dramatic and convenient early reports?
- [ ] Am I updating transparently and correcting errors promptly and visibly when new information supersedes earlier reporting?
- [ ] Did I hold claims that did not meet the verification threshold, even when competitors published them?
- [ ] Have I confirmed any names of suspects or victims through official or multiple independent sources before publication?
- [ ] Before going live, is every high-consequence fact in the story matched to its source and labeled with the appropriate level of certainty?
