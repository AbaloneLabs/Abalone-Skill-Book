# Rolling Coverage Updates

Rolling coverage, live blogs, continuously updated stories, is now the default format for unfolding events. Unlike a single published article, rolling coverage accumulates: each update adds to a running record that readers may enter at any point and that competitors and aggregators will quote out of context. The harm this skill prevents is the gradual erosion of accuracy and coherence as a story develops, where early wrong claims persist in the live blog even after being superseded, where the narrative becomes contradictory across updates, and where readers cannot tell what is current versus stale. Managing rolling coverage is a distinct discipline of continuous verification, clear time-stamping, visible correction, and narrative maintenance.

## Core Rules

### Treat each update as a new verification obligation
Every new development requires the same verification discipline as a standalone story, compressed in time. Do not let the momentum of continuous publishing lower your standard. An unverified detail slipped into an update spreads as fast as a verified one and is harder to retract because it is embedded in a running feed.

### Time-stamp and attribute every significant claim
In rolling coverage, the timing and source of a claim are essential context because information changes. Each material assertion should carry when it was reported and on whose authority. This lets readers, and your future self, track how the story evolved and prevents stale claims from being mistaken for current fact.

### Reconcile new information with what was previously reported
Before publishing an update, check it against prior updates. If new information contradicts or supersedes earlier reporting, address the discrepancy explicitly rather than letting the record contradict itself. A live blog that contains both "five dead" and later "three dead" without explanation destroys trust and confuses every reader who arrives mid-stream.

### Make corrections within the live format, visibly
When an earlier update was wrong, correct it in the stream with a clear, timestamped note explaining what was wrong and what is now known. Do not silently delete the error; the false information has already spread, and visible correction is the only way to limit the harm. Corrections in rolling coverage should be as prominent as the original error.

### Maintain a clear "what we know now" synthesis
As updates accumulate, readers need a current, authoritative summary that supersedes the chronological feed. Maintain and refresh a top-line synthesis of the established facts, distinct from the running updates. This is the version most readers will rely on, so it must be the most carefully verified and the most promptly updated.

### Distinguish developments from restatements
Not every update carries new information. Beware of padding the feed with restatements, reaction quotes, or speculation dressed as progress. Readers trust rolling coverage less when updates are frequent but content-light. Publish updates when there is genuinely something new and verified to add.

### Manage the lifecycle of the live coverage
Rolling coverage should not run forever. Decide when the story has stabilized enough to convert from a live format to a conventional article, and execute that transition cleanly, preserving the verified record and discarding the transient. An open-ended live blog that drifts into irrelevance devalues the earlier, vital reporting.

### Preserve a coherent narrative across the arc
Even within the urgency of rolling coverage, maintain a coherent sense of the story's shape. Readers who follow from the beginning and those who arrive late should both be able to understand what happened, what it means, and where it stands. Periodically step back and ensure the running coverage tells a comprehensible story, not just a sequence of alerts.

## Common Traps

### Letting early errors persist uncorrected in the feed
A wrong claim in update three remains visible to every reader who scrolls, even after update seven corrects it, unless the correction is in-place and visible. Unaddressed early errors make the entire live blog unreliable.

### Publishing updates for the sake of activity
The pressure to show continuous movement leads to low-value updates that dilute the feed. Readers learn to discount a live blog that cries wolf with non-developments. Reserve updates for genuine, verified news.

### Contradictory information across updates
Without reconciliation, a live blog accumulates internal contradictions that confuse readers and provide ammunition to those who wish to discredit the coverage. Each update must be checked against the existing record.

### Losing the reader who arrives mid-stream
A reader landing on a live blog an hour in needs to understand the current state quickly. If the only entry point is a long chronological feed, they will leave. A prominent, current synthesis is essential.

### Treating reaction as news
Quotes from officials reacting to events are often presented as developments when they add no new factual information. Distinguish genuine new facts from commentary and reaction, and avoid inflating the latter into updates.

### Failing to convert to a stable article
When the story stabilizes, a lingering live blog becomes stale and confusing. Without a clean transition to a conventional article, the strong early reporting is buried under hours of diminishing updates.

### Over-relying on a single source across the arc
In fast-moving coverage, it is easy to keep returning to the same responsive official or wire service for each update. This builds a one-source narrative that collapses if that source is wrong. Continuously seek independent corroboration as the story develops.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has every update been verified to the same standard as a standalone story, without letting momentum lower the bar?
- [ ] Does each significant claim carry a timestamp and clear attribution so readers can track how the story evolved?
- [ ] Have I reconciled new updates against prior reporting, addressing contradictions explicitly rather than leaving them to stand?
- [ ] Are corrections made visibly within the live format, with clear notes explaining what was wrong and what is now known?
- [ ] Is there a current, prominently displayed "what we know now" synthesis that supersedes the chronological feed?
- [ ] Am I publishing updates only when there is genuinely new and verified information, not merely to show activity?
- [ ] Have I planned the lifecycle of the coverage, including when and how to convert from live format to a stable article?
- [ ] Does the running coverage maintain a coherent narrative that both loyal and late-arriving readers can follow?
- [ ] Have I distinguished genuine developments from reaction and commentary, avoiding inflating the latter into updates?
- [ ] As the story developed, did I continually seek independent corroboration rather than relying on the same source throughout?
