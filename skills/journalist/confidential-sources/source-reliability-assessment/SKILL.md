# Source Reliability Assessment

Assessing the reliability of a confidential source is harder than assessing a named one, because the usual signals, reputation, institutional position, public track record, are hidden from the audience and often from the reporter too. The judgment problem is that confidential sources tend to be evaluated emotionally: a source who is cooperative, articulate, and tells a compelling story feels reliable, while a source who is hesitant, conflicted, or tells an inconvenient story feels less so. Neither feeling tracks accuracy. Reliability must be assessed structurally, through access, motive, corroboration, track record, and behavior under pressure, and the assessment must be revisable as new evidence arrives.

Use this skill when deciding how much weight to place on a confidential source's claims, when a single source contradicts documents or other sources, when a source's motives are mixed, or when an editor is reviewing whether a story's reliance on unnamed sourcing is defensible. The main risk is that an agent treats a source's fluency or likeability as evidence of truth, or treats a source's inconvenient contradictions as evidence of unreliability.

## Core Rules

### Separate Access From Reliability

A source's access describes what they could know; their reliability describes how accurately they report what they know. These are different and must be assessed separately. A source with excellent access can be unreliable, because they filter what they saw through bias, self-protection, or selective memory. A source with narrow access can be highly reliable within that narrow band. Map each claim to the source's actual access: did they witness it, participate in it, review the documents, or hear about it second-hand? Then ask, separately, whether their account of what they had access to holds up.

Record the access-reliability split for each important claim. A claim resting on first-hand access from a reliable source is strong; a claim resting on second-hand access from a reliable source is weaker than it sounds; a claim resting on first-hand access from an unreliable source is dangerous because it feels authoritative.

### Probe Motive Without Dismissing The Information

Every source has motives, and mixed motives do not invalidate information. A whistleblower motivated by grievance may still possess accurate documents; a former employee motivated by revenge may still describe real misconduct. The error to avoid is twin: dismissing accurate information because the source has an axe to grind, and accepting false information because the source seems principled. Assess motive to understand the lens, not to decide truth.

Ask what the source gains from disclosure, what they stand to lose, whether their account consistently favors one interpretation, whether they omit facts that would complicate their narrative, and whether their story has shifted over time. A source whose account is suspiciously complete, with every detail pointing the same direction, may be curating rather than recalling. A source who volunteers complicating facts is often more trustworthy, not less.

### Corroborate Against Independent Evidence Types

The strongest reliability test is corroboration across independent evidence types: documents, data, other sources with separate access, physical evidence, and public records. Independence matters more than count. Three sources who all learned the same fact from the same leaked memo are one source. A single confidential source whose account is independently confirmed by a procurement record, a court filing, and a separate witness is far stronger than three sources echoing one origin.

For each material claim, record which evidence types corroborate it and which are absent. A claim that rests only on the confidential source's say-so, with no document, no data, and no independent witness, should be framed as such and usually cannot carry the weight of a definitive assertion. See the reporting-and-source-verification skill for the verification discipline.

### Build A Track Record Through Incremental Testing

Reliability is proven over time, not declared in a single meeting. Test the source incrementally: ask about facts you can independently verify, documents you have already seen, events whose outcome you know. A source who is accurate on checkable details earns credibility on the details you cannot check; a source who is wrong, vague, or evasive on checkable details loses credibility on everything else.

Keep a record of these tests. A source whose reliability has been built through dozens of verified interactions can carry more weight than a brand-new source making dramatic claims. Conversely, a previously reliable source whose accuracy has started to slip may be under new pressure, compromised, or drifting, and should be reassessed rather than trusted on past reputation.

### Watch For Behavior Under Pressure And Shifting Accounts

How a source behaves when challenged reveals reliability. A reliable source who is mistaken will often acknowledge uncertainty, correct themselves, and distinguish what they know from what they assume. An unreliable or agenda-driven source will double down, shift the claim, attack the questioner, or add new dramatic details to deflect. Note these patterns. A source whose story changes to fit each new piece of contrary evidence, always landing on the side that preserves their narrative, is not being reliable even if each individual version sounds plausible.

Pressure can also come from outside. A source who is being leaned on by their institution, by law enforcement, or by another party may start to recant, hedge, or feed the reporter misleading material. If a source's account shifts suddenly, investigate why before treating either version as settled.

### Distinguish Sincerity From Accuracy

A sincere source can be wrong. Eyewitnesses sincerely misremember; insiders sincerely misjudge the significance of what they saw; whistleblowers sincerely overstate the scope of misconduct they witnessed directly. Sincerity, emotional conviction, and detail are not evidence of accuracy. This is a recurring trap because sincere, detailed, emotional testimony is exactly the material that feels most credible in an interview.

Test sincere accounts the same way you test cynical ones: against documents, data, independent witnesses, and checkable details. Where a sincere source conflicts with a document, the document usually wins, and the story should reflect that.

### Reassess Reliability As The Story Evolves

Reliability is not a fixed attribute; it changes as the source's circumstances change, as more evidence emerges, and as the story's reliance on the source shifts. A source reliable for minor corroboration may not be reliable enough to carry the central allegation. A source reliable at the start of an investigation may become compromised or pressured months in. Build reassessment into the process, especially before publication and when the story takes a direction that raises the stakes for the source.

## Common Traps

### Equating Fluency And Detail With Truth

A source who tells a vivid, coherent, emotionally compelling story is not necessarily accurate; fluency can reflect rehearsal, selection, or fabrication as easily as genuine recall. The most dangerous unreliable source is the one who sounds most convincing. Anchor credibility to verifiable facts, not to narrative polish.

### Dismissing Inconvenient Sources As Unreliable

When a source contradicts the story the reporter wants to tell, there is a temptation to find them less credible. This is confirmation bias operating on reliability assessment. Apply the same standards to sources who support and sources who undermine the working hypothesis, and actively seek out sources likely to disagree.

### Trusting A Source Because They Trust You

A source who has opened up, taken risks, and treated the reporter as an ally generates reciprocal loyalty. That loyalty can quietly lower the bar for credibility. Remember that a source's warmth toward you is not evidence about the facts; evaluate their claims as if they came from a stranger.

### Overweighting A Single Compelling Source

One source with great access and a dramatic account can dominate an investigation, especially when documents are scarce. But a single source, however compelling, can be wrong, compromised, or pursuing an agenda the reporter has not detected. Insist on independent corroboration for material claims, and narrow the story to what the evidence sustains if corroboration does not arrive.

### Treating Past Reliability As Permanent

A source who was reliable for years can become unreliable under new pressure, after a job change, or as their interests shift. Relying on reputation without reassessment leaves the reporter blind to a source who has changed. Reassess periodically and whenever circumstances shift.

## Self-Check

Before treating a confidential source's reliability as established, verify:

- Access and reliability were assessed separately for each material claim, with the source's basis of knowledge, first-hand or second-hand, explicitly recorded.
- Motive was probed to understand the lens, without dismissing accurate information from a conflicted source or accepting false information from a principled one.
- Material claims are corroborated across genuinely independent evidence types, not merely repeated from a common origin.
- A track record was built through incremental testing against checkable facts, and the results are recorded.
- Behavior under pressure was observed, with shifting accounts, evasiveness, or sudden recantation investigated rather than accepted at face value.
- Sincerity was not conflated with accuracy; sincere testimony was tested against documents and data the same as any other.
- Reliability was reassessed as the story evolved, especially before publication and when the stakes for the source rose.
- No single compelling source carries material claims without independent corroboration, and the story is narrowed where corroboration is absent.
- The assessment applied the same standards to sources who support and sources who contradict the working hypothesis.
- Reliability assessment supports editorial judgment; significant reliance on a confidential source should be reviewed by a responsible editor, and legal counsel consulted where the source's claims carry legal exposure.
