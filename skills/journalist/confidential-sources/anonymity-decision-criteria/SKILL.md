# Anonymity Decision Criteria

Granting anonymity is one of the most consequential and most abused decisions in journalism. Done well, it lets vulnerable people reveal information the public needs and could not otherwise obtain. Done casually, it hands powerful actors a shield to attack rivals without accountability, erodes audience trust, and weakens every story that genuinely needs anonymity. The judgment problem is that anonymity looks like a small editorial courtesy but functions as a contract with legal, safety, and credibility consequences that extend well beyond the single quote.

Use this skill when a source requests anonymity, when an editor is weighing whether to approve unnamed attribution, when drafting the sourcing language that will appear in a story, or when reviewing whether a story's reliance on anonymous sources is justified. The main risk is that an agent grants anonymity by default to smooth an interview, rather than treating it as a last-resort tool that must be earned by specific, articulable reasons.

## Core Rules

### Treat Anonymity As A Last Resort, Not A Default

The starting position is on the record. Named sources let the audience judge credibility, motive, and knowledge, and they force speakers to stand behind their claims. Move to anonymity only when a specific, articulable reason makes on-the-record publication impossible or harmful to the source. "The source prefers not to be named" is not a reason; it is a preference. The reason must be something like fear of losing employment, physical danger, prosecution, retaliation against family, or breach of a binding nondisclosure that protects genuinely sensitive information.

Before granting anonymity, exhaust the alternatives. Can any portion be moved on the record? Can the source be described by role and employer rather than named, in a way that still lets readers judge credibility? Can the information be obtained from a named source, a document, or public records instead? Anonymity should survive only when these alternatives have been genuinely tried and failed.

### Require Direct Knowledge, Not Opinion Or Spin

Anonymous sourcing is defensible when it carries factual information the source directly knows and the public needs. It is not defensible as a vehicle for opinion, speculation, character attacks, or institutional spin. A source who wants to be unnamed while calling a rival "incompetent" or "corrupt" is using anonymity to avoid accountability for an attack; that is exactly the abuse anonymity standards exist to prevent.

For each anonymous claim, ask: does this source have direct, first-hand knowledge of this specific fact? Is the claim factual or interpretive? Could the source make the same statement on the record without genuine harm? If the answer is that the source is offering opinion, second-hand hearsay, or an attack, decline anonymity or decline the quote.

### Make The Justification Specific And Articulable

Before publishing, you should be able to state, in one or two sentences, exactly why this source needed anonymity for this information. "They could face retaliation" is a start but is too generic. "They still work at the agency and could be fired for confirming internal deliberations about the contract" is specific. If you cannot articulate the specific harm, the anonymity is probably not justified.

Record the justification in your notes, not just in your head. Editors reviewing the story, and you yourself months later, need to be able to reconstruct why anonymity was granted. A vague justification that felt right at the time will not survive scrutiny.

### Describe The Source Enough For Readers To Judge Credibility

Anonymity does not mean vagueness. Readers need enough description to assess why this person is worth believing. "A person familiar with the matter" tells the reader almost nothing. "A senior engineer who reviewed the safety tests before launch" tells the reader the source's role, proximity to the facts, and basis of knowledge, without identifying them.

Work with the source to agree on a description that is honest about their access without exposing them. Avoid descriptions so generic they could apply to anyone, and avoid descriptions so specific they function as identification. Where possible, explain in the story why the source was granted anonymity, for example "because they were not authorized to discuss internal deliberations and feared professional retaliation."

### Distinguish Levels Of Attribution Precisely

Newsrooms use several attribution levels, and conflating them causes betrayal and disputes. Be precise: on the record (name and role usable), on background (information usable, source described by agreed characterization but not named), deep background (information usable without any source attribution, woven into the reporter's own narrative), and off the record (information not usable for publication, used only to inform the reporter's understanding). Confirm the level before sensitive material is shared, in the source's own words where possible.

A common failure is a source saying "this is off the record" and the reporter assuming "background." Another is the reporter offering "I'll keep you out of it" without specifying whether that means background, deep background, or full non-use. Spell it out. See the off-the-record skill for the operational handling.

### Require Editor Approval And Source Identification Internally

In most serious newsrooms, anonymity is not the reporter's alone to grant; it requires editor approval, and the editor must know the source's identity so they can judge the justification and reliability. A reporter who hides a source's identity from their own editor is asking the newsroom to vouch for information it cannot verify, which defeats the purpose of the standard.

Explain to sources that their identity will be known to the editor but protected within the newsroom. Most legitimate sources accept this. A source who demands that even the editor not know who they are is asking for an arrangement that bypasses accountability and should rarely be accepted.

### Reassess Anonymity If Circumstances Change

Anonymity granted at one point may need to be revisited if circumstances shift. A source who feared retaliation may leave the job and become free to speak on the record. A source whose identity has already become publicly known through other means may no longer need the shield. A source who turns out to have misled you may forfeit a promise made in good faith. Reassess before publication and on significant updates, and consult editors where the original basis has eroded.

## Common Traps

### Granting Anonymity To Smooth The Interview

A source who says "can we go off the record?" mid-conversation often gets a reflexive yes from a reporter trying to keep the interview flowing. That reflex grants a protection that may not be justified and that constrains later publication. Pause, ask what specifically the source wants protected and why, and decide deliberately.

### Letting Officials Launder Spin Through Anonymity

Government and corporate officials often request anonymity to say things that serve their institution's agenda while avoiding accountability. An unnamed official attacking a political rival, a regulator, or a foreign government is using the press as a deniable weapon. Reserve anonymity for information, not for spin, and refuse to carry attacks that the source will not stand behind publicly.

### Vague Descriptions That Conceal Weak Access

"A source close to the negotiations" could be a participant or someone who heard a rumor in a hallway. Vague descriptions let weakly informed sources sound authoritative. Pin the description to the source's actual basis of knowledge, and if the source will not permit an honest description, treat the information as weaker than it appears.

### Promising Anonymity The Law May Not Honor

In some jurisdictions, courts can compel reporters to identify sources, and shield laws vary widely. Promising absolute anonymity without understanding the legal exposure can endanger the source and trap the reporter in a contempt situation. Be honest with the source about the limits, and consult counsel on high-stakes cases. See the source-protection skill.

### Failing To Reassess When The Story Evolves

A source granted anonymity for a minor corroborating detail may end up as the central voice of a much bigger story. What was justified for a small role may not be justified when the source carries the weight of the investigation. Reassess attribution as the story's reliance on each source shifts.

## Self-Check

Before treating an anonymity decision as sound, verify:

- Anonymity was treated as a last resort, with on-the-record, document, and named-source alternatives genuinely tried first.
- The source has direct first-hand knowledge of the specific facts being attributed, and is offering information rather than opinion, spin, or attack.
- A specific, articulable justification for the anonymity exists and is recorded in notes, not just a vague preference.
- The published description tells readers enough about the source's role, access, and basis of knowledge to judge credibility, and explains why anonymity was granted.
- The attribution level, on record, background, deep background, or off the record, was confirmed with the source in clear terms before sensitive material was shared.
- An editor approved the anonymity and knows the source's identity, unless an extraordinary, documented exception applies.
- The decision accounts for the legal limits of anonymity in the relevant jurisdiction, with counsel consulted on high-stakes cases.
- Circumstances were reassessed before publication and on updates, with anonymity revised where the original basis no longer holds.
- No official or institutional source is using anonymity to launder spin or launch attacks they would not make on the record.
- Anonymity decisions support editorial judgment; final approval should rest with a responsible editor, and legal counsel should be consulted where source exposure carries significant legal risk.
