# Confidential Source Onboarding

Onboarding a confidential source is the structured process of turning a wary person with sensitive information into a source who understands the rules, the risks, and the limits of what the reporter can promise. It is not a single conversation; it is a sequence of decisions about trust, security, attribution, and protection that, if mishandled, can endanger the source, corrupt the reporting, or trap the newsroom in obligations it cannot meet. The judgment problem is that the most valuable confidential sources, whistleblowers, insiders under nondisclosure agreements, people in authoritarian contexts, are often the least able to assess the risks of talking, and they rely on the reporter to set honest expectations.

Use this skill when a source has indicated willingness to share sensitive information off the record or anonymously, when structuring the first substantive conversations, when establishing how the relationship will operate, or when bringing a sensitive source into an investigation. The main risk is that an agent rushes toward the information without building the protective scaffolding that keeps the source safe and the reporting defensible.

## Core Rules

### Establish Ground Rules Before Any Sensitive Disclosure

The single most important onboarding step is to set attribution ground rules before the source shares anything they would not want published. Confusion about whether a conversation was on the record, on background, or off the record is the most common cause of source betrayal disputes, and once sensitive material is disclosed, it cannot be taken back. Open the relationship by stating your newsroom's defaults and asking the source to choose the level that fits their risk.

Be explicit about what each level means in your practice, because terms are used differently across outlets and countries. Confirm the choice in the source's own words. If the source is unsure, default to the most protective level for the first conversation and reassess once trust and understanding are established. See the off-the-record skill for definitions and the anonymity-decision-criteria skill for when anonymity is justified at all.

### Explain What You Can And Cannot Guarantee

Sources often arrive with an inflated sense of what a reporter can protect. They may believe anonymity is absolute, that their identity will never be discoverable, that the reporter can refuse any court order, or that the newsroom will shield them from all consequences. Honest onboarding requires correcting these assumptions gently but clearly.

Explain, in terms the source can act on, the realistic limits: courts in many jurisdictions can compel testimony or records; metadata, billing records, and device forensics can expose contact even when content is encrypted; colleagues may infer the source from the story's details; and the newsroom's resources and legal position have limits. Do not promise more than you can deliver. Where the stakes are high, consult editors and counsel before making assurances, and tell the source that you will come back with a clear picture of what is and is not possible. See the source-protection skill for the operational detail.

### Establish Secure Communication From The First Contact

If the source is sensitive, the channel of first contact may already be compromised. A workplace email, a personal phone, a messaging app tied to a real identity, or a meeting in a surveilled location can expose the relationship before it begins. Discuss secure options early: encrypted messaging with disappearing messages, secure email, secure drop platforms, in-person meetings away from the source's usual locations, and, in high-risk cases, devices or accounts dedicated to the source.

Meet the source where they are technically. A source who cannot safely install signal may need a different approach than one who is technically fluent. Do not impose a tool that creates a record on a monitored device. The goal is to reduce exposure, not to demonstrate sophistication. Consult the digital-security skill for tool selection and the source-protection skill for the broader operational plan.

### Verify The Source's Knowledge And Motives Without Exposing Them

Onboarding includes assessing whether the source is who they claim, what they actually know first-hand, and why they are coming forward. This is delicate: probing too hard can feel like an interrogation and push the source away, while probing too little leaves the reporter relying on someone whose motives or access are unclear. Ask open questions about how they came to know what they know, what their role was, and what they hope will come of disclosure.

Pay attention to motive without judging it harshly. Whistleblowers often act from a mix of principle and grievance; a disgruntled motive does not make documents false, and a noble motive does not make allegations true. Record what you learn about access and motive, because it will matter for the anonymity justification, the sourcing language, and the editor's assessment. See the source-evaluation skill.

### Bring The Source Into The Reporting Plan Realistically

Confidential sources often want to know what will happen with what they share: when it will publish, how they will be described, whether they will be contacted again, whether they will be named if the story breaks wide. Give honest, calibrated answers. Do not promise a publication date you cannot control, do not promise a framing that may change as reporting develops, and do not promise that they will never be contacted again if follow-up is needed.

Explain the reporting process: that their information will be corroborated against documents and other sources, that the subject of the story will be given a chance to respond, that details will be reviewed for the risk of identifying them, and that the story may be delayed or reshaped by what verification finds. A source who understands the process is less likely to feel betrayed when it does not unfold as they imagined.

### Involve Editors And Specialists Early

Confidential source relationships should not be carried alone by a single reporter. Bring in an editor at the point the source becomes serious, so the editor can assess the justification, the reliability, and the risk. For high-stakes sources, involve newsroom security staff, legal counsel, and, where relevant, organizations that support at-risk journalists and sources. The earlier these people are looped in, the more they can help; the later, the more they can only react to decisions already made.

Explain to the source that editors and, where necessary, counsel will know their identity within the newsroom, and that this internal knowledge is what allows the newsroom to stand behind the reporting. Most legitimate sources accept this once they understand it protects them as much as the story.

### Plan For The Source's Life After Publication

Onboarding does not end at the first conversation; it extends through publication and beyond. Discuss with the source what they will do if the story exposes them despite protections: whether they have a lawyer, whether their employment is at risk, whether they may face legal action, whether they need to relocate. You cannot solve these problems for the source, but you can help them think about them before crisis hits, and you can connect them to resources where appropriate.

Do not encourage the source to take risks for the story that they have not thought through. A source who loses their livelihood, faces prosecution, or endangers their family because the reporter did not help them anticipate the consequences is a failure of the relationship, not just bad luck.

## Common Traps

### Rushing To The Information Before Setting Rules

The pressure to get the story can push a reporter to start collecting disclosures before ground rules are set, leaving both parties with incompatible assumptions about what can be published. The few minutes spent confirming attribution rules up front prevent disputes that can sink a story or endanger a source.

### Promising Absolute Protection To Reassure A Nervous Source

Telling a frightened source "I'll protect you no matter what" feels kind but is often dishonest. If a court compels disclosure, or if forensic detail exposes them, the promise collapses and the source is harmed with no preparation. Honest limits, explained early, are kinder than false assurance.

### Using A Tool That Creates A Record On A Monitored Device

Recommending an encrypted app installed on a workplace phone can create an identifiable record that defeats the encryption. Onboarding security must fit the source's actual environment, not the reporter's preferred toolkit. When in doubt, choose the channel that leaves the least trace on the source's exposed devices.

### Letting The Source Drive The Framing

A confidential source who provides valuable documents may expect to shape how they are reported, what is emphasized, and what is left out. Listening to a source's perspective is good; handing them editorial control is not. The reporter, with editors, decides the framing based on evidence and public interest, and the source's role is to provide information, not to direct coverage.

### Failing To Loop In Editors Until The Story Is Mature

A reporter who keeps a confidential source secret from editors until the story is nearly done denies the newsroom the chance to assess reliability and risk, and forces last-minute decisions under deadline pressure. Involve editors early enough that their judgment shapes the relationship, not just the headline.

## Self-Check

Before treating confidential source onboarding as sound, verify:

- Attribution ground rules were established and confirmed in the source's own words before any sensitive disclosure.
- The source understands, in realistic terms, what anonymity and protection can and cannot guarantee in the relevant legal and technical environment.
- A secure communication channel was chosen that fits the source's actual environment and does not create exposing records on monitored devices.
- The source's knowledge, access, and motives were assessed and recorded without exposing them or turning the conversation into an interrogation.
- The source was given an honest picture of the reporting process, including corroboration, subject response, possible delays, and reframing as evidence develops.
- An editor was brought in early enough to assess justification, reliability, and risk, and counsel or security specialists were involved where stakes require.
- The source's post-publication risks, including employment, legal, and safety exposure, were discussed and anticipated rather than discovered after the fact.
- No promise of protection, framing, or publication timing exceeds what the reporter and newsroom can actually deliver.
- The source is not being allowed to direct editorial framing in exchange for their information.
- Onboarding supports editorial judgment; sensitive source relationships should involve a responsible editor throughout, and legal counsel plus security specialists where exposure is significant.
