# Evidence And Impact Evaluation In Solutions Reporting

The credibility of solutions journalism rests on the quality of the evidence it presents for whether a response works, and this is where the reporting is most demanding and most often fails. The judgment problem is that program effectiveness is a methodological question, not a narrative one, and the evidence is usually partial, contested, and hard to interpret. A reporter who accepts a program's self-reported outcomes as proof misleads the public; one who demands randomized trials for every claim produces no coverage at all. Strong solutions reporting understands what constitutes credible evidence, interprets outcome data and evaluations honestly, distinguishes what is proven from what is promising, and conveys the strength of the evidence to readers. The discipline is to bring methodological rigor to the reporting without letting methodological perfectionism paralyze it.

Use this skill when evaluating evidence and impact in solutions reporting, assessing program effectiveness, and interpreting outcome data and evaluations. The main risk is that an agent may overstate weak evidence, confuse activity with outcomes, or present a program's claims as established results. This skill is editorial guidance only; impact evaluation involves methodological and statistical judgment, and specialist consultation with researchers and evaluators is essential.

## Core Rules

### Understand What Constitutes Credible Evidence

Credible evidence of effectiveness comes in a hierarchy, from rigorous experimental designs to weaker observational and descriptive evidence, and understanding that hierarchy is essential to reporting it honestly. Randomized controlled trials, when well-conducted, provide the strongest evidence of causation; quasi-experimental designs and natural experiments can support causal claims with caveats; observational studies show association but are vulnerable to confounding; and descriptive data, output metrics, and testimonials establish activity but not effectiveness. Assess each piece of evidence for where it sits in the hierarchy, and calibrate your claims accordingly. A response supported only by testimonials is not proven effective, however compelling the stories.

Report the type of evidence, not just the conclusion, so readers can assess its strength.

### Distinguish Outputs, Outcomes, And Impact

One of the most common errors in solutions reporting is conflating what a program does, outputs, with what it achieves, outcomes, and with the difference it makes, impact. Outputs are activities and counts: people served, sessions held, materials distributed. Outcomes are the changes that result: knowledge gained, behavior changed, conditions improved. Impact is the causal contribution to those changes: how much of the outcome is attributable to the program rather than to other factors. A program can generate impressive outputs without changing outcomes, and outcomes can change for reasons unrelated to the program. Report each level distinctly, and reserve claims of impact for evidence that supports causation.

Activity is not achievement, and achievement is not necessarily attributable.

### Interpret Outcome Data With Methodological Care

Outcome data must be interpreted with methodological care, because the obvious reading is often wrong. Beware before-and-after comparisons that ignore broader trends: if a problem was declining before the program, the decline may not be due to the program. Beware selection effects: if participants are chosen or self-selected, their outcomes may reflect who they are rather than what the program did. Beware small or non-representative samples, multiple comparisons that inflate apparent significance, and outcome measures that are easy to game. Look for comparison groups, independent evaluation, and pre-registered analysis that guards against cherry-picking. Honest interpretation requires understanding these pitfalls, not just reporting the numbers.

Ask: compared to what, and what else could explain this?

### Assess Causation, Not Just Correlation

The central question of impact evaluation is whether the response caused the observed outcomes, and causation is harder to establish than correlation. Correlation, that participants improved, does not prove the program caused the improvement, because participants may have improved anyway, or because of differences between participants and non-participants. Look for evidence that supports causation: comparison groups that are similar except for the program, designs that rule out alternative explanations, and replication in different settings. Report associational evidence as associational, and reserve causal claims for designs that support them. The gap between correlation and causation is where solutions reporting most often overclaims.

Be especially careful with programs that serve motivated or selected populations.

### Report The Strength Of The Evidence Honestly

Honest solutions reporting conveys not just whether a response works but how strong the evidence is. A response supported by multiple rigorous trials warrants stronger claims than one supported by a single small study or by testimonials. Report the quantity, quality, and consistency of the evidence, and be explicit about what is well-established, what is promising, and what is unknown. Do not present weak evidence as strong, and do not bury strong evidence under excessive hedging. Calibrate the language to the evidence: proven, effective, promising, unproven, and unknown are different claims, and readers deserve to know which applies.

The strength of a claim should match the strength of the evidence, no more and no less.

### Examine Independent Evaluation And Replication

The most credible evidence comes from independent evaluation, by researchers without a stake in the program's success, and from replication, the response working in different settings. Seek out independent evaluations and report their findings, including when they contradict the program's own claims. Look for evidence of replication, because a response that works in one setting may fail in another, and replication is the test of transferability. Report on failed replications and negative findings, which are as important as positive ones. A response that has only been evaluated by its promoters, or only in one setting, carries less evidential weight than one with independent and replicated support.

Report who conducted the evaluation and whether they have a stake in the result.

### Report Costs, Scale, And Sustainability

Effectiveness is not the only question; a response must also be affordable, scalable, and sustainable to matter. Report the costs of the response, including who pays and what the opportunity costs are, and compare the cost-effectiveness to alternatives. Examine whether the response can work at scale, because many programs that succeed as pilots fail when expanded, due to implementation challenges, staffing, and context. Assess sustainability, including whether the response depends on temporary funding, charismatic leadership, or conditions that may not persist. A response that works only at small scale, only with extraordinary resources, or only temporarily is a different story than one that is scalable and sustainable.

Cost-effectiveness and scalability are part of effectiveness, not separate from it.

### Consult Evaluators And Methodologists

Impact evaluation is a specialized methodological field, and reporters should consult evaluators and methodologists to assess evidence they cannot fully evaluate. Independent researchers can identify flaws in a program's evidence, situate findings within the broader literature, and explain what the evidence does and does not show. Seek experts without conflicts of interest, and disclose any conflicts they have. Methodological consultation is not optional garnish; it is how a reporter avoids overclaiming on weak evidence or underclaiming on strong evidence. A solutions story that reports only what the program says about its own effectiveness is a press release.

Consult multiple experts when the evidence is contested or the stakes are high.

## Common Traps

### Outputs Mistaken For Outcomes

Reporting activities and counts as if they prove effectiveness conflates what a program does with what it achieves.

### Before-And-After As Causation

Attributing a change to a program without ruling out trends and alternatives overstates impact.

### Selection Effects Ignored

Participants who self-select or are chosen may have different outcomes regardless of the program.

### Weak Evidence As Strong Claim

Presenting testimonials or a single small study as proof misleads readers about what is known.

### Promoter-Only Evaluation

Accepting a program's self-reported results without independent assessment invites overclaiming.

### Ignoring Cost And Scale

A response that works only with extraordinary resources or at small scale is a limited solution.

### Correlation As Causation

Reporting an association as a proven effect is the central error of weak impact reporting.

### Excessive Hedging On Strong Evidence

Burying well-supported findings under caveats understates what is known and fails to inform.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the type and strength of evidence reported, so readers can assess whether claims rest on rigorous trials, observational studies, or testimonials?
- [ ] Are outputs, outcomes, and impact distinguished, with claims of impact reserved for evidence supporting causation?
- [ ] Is outcome data interpreted with methodological care, addressing trends, selection effects, sample limits, and comparison groups?
- [ ] Is causation distinguished from correlation, with causal claims reserved for designs that support them?
- [ ] Is the strength of the evidence reported honestly, with language calibrated to proven, effective, promising, unproven, or unknown?
- [ ] Are independent evaluations and replications sought and reported, including failures and contradictions?
- [ ] Are costs, scalability, and sustainability examined as part of assessing whether the response matters?
- [ ] Have independent evaluators and methodologists been consulted to assess the evidence, with conflicts disclosed?
- [ ] Has the impact assessment been reviewed by editors and, where the stakes are high, by specialist researchers, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance only and that impact evaluation involves methodological judgment requiring expert consultation?
