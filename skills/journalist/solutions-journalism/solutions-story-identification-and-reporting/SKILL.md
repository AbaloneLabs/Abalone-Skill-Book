# Solutions Story Identification And Reporting

Solutions journalism is rigorous reporting on responses to social problems, not feel-good coverage or advocacy. The judgment problem is that coverage of solutions is pulled toward two failures: uncritical boosterism that promotes programs without evidence of effectiveness, and cynical dismissal that treats any solution story as fluff unworthy of serious reporting. Strong solutions reporting applies the same standards of evidence, independence, and scrutiny as any accountability journalism, while focusing on what is being done about a problem and whether it works. The reporter who cheers a program without evidence misleads the public; the one who refuses to cover responses leaves audiences informed only about what is broken. The discipline is to report on solutions with the rigor usually reserved for problems.

Use this skill when identifying and reporting solutions-journalism stories, investigating responses to social problems, and assessing evidence of effectiveness. The main risk is that an agent may drift into advocacy or boosterism, accept program claims without verification, or abandon scrutiny because the subject is well-intentioned. This skill is editorial guidance only; assessing program effectiveness involves methodological judgment, and specialist consultation strengthens the work.

## Core Rules

### Report On Responses With The Rigor Of Problems

Solutions journalism is not a softer form of reporting; it applies the same standards of evidence, verification, and independence to responses that traditional journalism applies to problems. Investigate the response as you would investigate a scandal: examine the evidence, follow the money, cultivate independent sources, and verify claims. The subject being a solution rather than a problem does not lower the standard; it changes the question from what went wrong to what is being tried and whether it works. A solutions story that is not rigorous is promotion, not journalism.

Apply the same skepticism to a program's claims that you would to a corporation's.

### Identify A Genuine Social Problem First

A solutions story begins with a social problem that matters: homelessness, educational inequity, addiction, violence, environmental degradation. Identify the problem clearly, establish its scale and consequences, and ground it in evidence, because the response is only as significant as the problem it addresses. Avoid solutions stories that float free of a problem, celebrating a program without establishing what it is responding to, because that is promotion rather than reporting. The problem framing also sets up the standard by which the response will be assessed.

Cover the problem honestly, including its difficulty, so the response is assessed realistically.

### Investigate The Response And Its Evidence

The heart of a solutions story is the response: what is being done, how it works, and what evidence there is that it is effective. Investigate the response in detail, including its design, its implementation, its costs, and its outcomes. Examine the evidence of effectiveness rigorously: is there outcome data, independent evaluation, comparison to alternatives, and evidence of causation rather than correlation? Report what the evidence shows, including what it does not show, and be honest about the limits of the evidence. A response without evidence of effectiveness is a hypothesis, not a solution.

Distinguish between the response's promise and its proven results; they are often different.

### Avoid Advocacy And Boosterism

The central failure mode of weak solutions coverage is advocacy: promoting a response because the reporter wants it to work, rather than reporting whether it does. Maintain independence from the program, its advocates, and its funders, and resist the pull to cheerlead. Report the drawbacks, the limitations, the unintended consequences, and the failures alongside the successes, because honest reporting serves the reader better than promotion. A solutions story that reads like a fundraising pitch has failed; one that reads like an investigation has succeeded. The reporter's role is to inform, not to persuade.

Be especially wary of programs that court coverage, because they are often seeking validation rather than scrutiny.

### Assess Effectiveness With Methodological Honesty

Assessing whether a response works is a methodological question, and reporting it honestly requires understanding evidence. Look for outcome data, controlled or comparison-group studies, independent evaluations, and replication, and report the strength of the evidence accordingly. Be honest about the common pitfalls: before-and-after comparisons that ignore trends, selective outcome reporting, small or non-representative samples, and the gap between a program's internal results and its effectiveness at scale. Distinguish between correlation and causation, and between efficacy under ideal conditions and effectiveness in the real world. Methodological honesty protects readers from overclaiming.

Consult independent researchers and methodologists to assess evidence you cannot fully evaluate.

### Report Limitations And Caveats Prominently

Every response has limitations, and reporting them prominently is essential to honest solutions journalism. Report what the response does not address, who it does not help, what it costs and who pays, what tradeoffs it involves, and what is unknown about its effectiveness. Report failures and setbacks, including programs that looked promising but did not work, because those stories are as valuable as success stories. A solutions story that presents only the upside is incomplete and potentially misleading; one that presents the full picture, including the limits, serves the reader. Limitations are not disqualifications; they are context.

The most useful solutions stories often explain why a response is harder than it looks.

### Follow The Money And The Interests

Solutions, like problems, involve money and interests, and following them is part of the reporting. Who funds the response, who profits, who advocates for it, and whose interests does it serve? Report on the funders, including foundations, governments, and private interests, and on the financial and political stakes. Examine whether the response serves the interests of its promoters as much as the people it claims to help. Follow the money in solutions as in any other beat, because the interests explain the advocacy and the resistance. A response backed by powerful interests deserves the same scrutiny as one opposed by them.

Beware philanthropic and corporate funding that shapes which solutions get attention.

### Provide Transferable Insight, Not A Blueprint

A solutions story is most valuable when it provides insight that readers, policymakers, and communities can use, but it should not present a single response as a blueprint to be copied. Report what makes the response work, including the principles, the conditions, and the adaptations, and be honest about what is transferable to other contexts and what is specific. Avoid the implication that a solution that worked in one place can simply be transplanted, because context matters and replication often fails. The insight is in understanding the mechanism and the conditions, not in copying the program. Transferable insight serves readers better than a prescription.

Report on replication and adaptation where evidence exists, including where it has failed.

## Common Traps

### Boosterism Over Evidence

Promoting a program because it seems worthy, without evidence of effectiveness, is advocacy, not journalism.

### Solution Without A Problem

Celebrating a response without establishing the problem it addresses is promotion, not reporting.

### Accepting Program Claims Uncritically

Treating a program's self-reported outcomes as proven results misleads readers.

### Ignoring Limitations And Failures

Presenting only the upside hides the costs, tradeoffs, and unknowns readers need.

### Methodological Naivete

Before-and-after comparisons and selective outcomes overstate effectiveness.

### Advocacy Disguised As Reporting

Writing to persuade rather than to inform betrays the journalistic role.

### Unexamined Funding And Interests

Failing to follow the money in solutions leaves the interests and incentives unscrutinized.

### Blueprint Prescription

Presenting a single solution as a copyable template ignores context and the difficulty of replication.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the story apply the same standards of evidence, verification, and independence to the response that traditional journalism applies to problems?
- [ ] Is a genuine social problem identified and established with evidence before the response is assessed?
- [ ] Is the response investigated in detail, with rigorous examination of the evidence of effectiveness?
- [ ] Is independence maintained from the program, its advocates, and its funders, with drawbacks and failures reported alongside successes?
- [ ] Is effectiveness assessed with methodological honesty, distinguishing causation from correlation and efficacy from real-world effectiveness?
- [ ] Are limitations, costs, tradeoffs, and unknowns reported prominently rather than buried?
- [ ] Has the money been followed, including funders, profits, advocates, and the interests the response serves?
- [ ] Does the story provide transferable insight about mechanisms and conditions rather than a blueprint prescription?
- [ ] Has the assessment of effectiveness been strengthened by consultation with independent researchers and methodologists, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance only and that evaluating program effectiveness involves methodological judgment?
