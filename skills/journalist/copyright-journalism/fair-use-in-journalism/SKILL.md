# Fair Use In Journalism

Fair use, the doctrine that allows limited use of copyrighted material without permission for purposes such as criticism, commentary, news reporting, and scholarship, is essential to journalism because reporting often requires quoting documents, excerpting content, and referencing copyrighted works to inform the public. The judgment problem is that fair use is a flexible, fact-specific standard rather than a bright-line rule, that news reporting is a favored fair use purpose but not an automatic defense, and that over-reliance on fair use without analysis can expose the newsroom to copyright liability, while over-caution can suppress important journalism. A defensible use of fair use understands the four factors, applies them to the specific material and use, and documents the reasoning so the decision can be defended.

Use this skill when using copyrighted material in reporting, assessing fair use, deciding whether to quote or excerpt, or determining when to seek permission. The main risk is that an agent assumes news reporting is always fair use, applies the factors superficially, or uses more copyrighted material than the use justifies.

## Core Rules

### Understand That Fair Use Is Fact-Specific, Not Automatic

Fair use is an affirmative defense that depends on the specific facts of the use, evaluated through four factors, and there is no automatic fair use for news reporting despite its favored status. The same use of the same material may be fair use in one context and infringement in another, depending on the purpose, the amount, the effect on the market, and the nature of the work. News reporting, criticism, and commentary are favored purposes, but favor is not certainty, and the other factors must still be weighed.

Approach each use of copyrighted material as a specific fair use analysis, not as a category that is automatically covered. The fact that the use is for news reporting weighs in favor of fair use, but it does not excuse uses that fail the other factors, such as using an entire copyrighted work where a portion would suffice, or using material in a way that substitutes for the original in the market. ### Apply The Four Factors To The Specific Use

The four fair use factors are: the purpose and character of the use, including whether it is commercial or nonprofit and whether it is transformative; the nature of the copyrighted work, with factual works receiving thinner protection than creative ones; the amount and substantiality of the portion used relative to the whole; and the effect of the use on the potential market for the original. Apply each factor to the specific material and use, weighing them together rather than treating any single factor as decisive.

The purpose factor favors transformative uses that add new meaning, commentary, or context, and news reporting that uses copyrighted material to report on or critique it is transformative. The nature factor favors use of factual works over creative ones. The amount factor favors using no more than necessary, and using the heart of the work weighs against fair use. The market effect factor disfavors uses that substitute for the original or harm its market. Weigh all four for the specific use, and recognize that the weight of each varies with the circumstances.

### Use No More Than Necessary Of The Copyrighted Work

The amount factor requires using no more of the copyrighted work than is necessary for the journalistic purpose, and this is where fair use analyses often fail. Using an entire copyrighted photograph when a cropped portion would convey the point, quoting an entire article when a paragraph would suffice, or embedding a full video when a clip would serve, all weigh against fair use because they use more than necessary.

Use the minimum necessary to serve the journalistic purpose, whether that is reporting on the work, critiquing it, or providing context. For some purposes, such as reporting on the existence or general character of a work, a small portion suffices. For others, such as critiquing the work's specific content, more may be necessary, but the amount should be justified by the purpose, not by convenience or impact. Document the reasoning for the amount used, because it may be challenged.

### Favor Transformative Uses That Add Commentary Or Context

Transformative uses, those that add new meaning, message, or purpose to the original, are favored under the purpose factor, and journalism that uses copyrighted material to report on, critique, or contextualize it is transformative. Using a politician's photograph to report on the politician is transformative; using the same photograph to illustrate a generic concept is less so. Using a document's text to report on its contents is transformative; reproducing the document to provide access to it is less so.

Favor uses that add journalistic commentary, context, or reporting to the copyrighted material, rather than uses that merely reproduce it. The transformative character strengthens the fair use analysis and serves the journalistic purpose. Where the use is more reproductive than transformative, such as republishing a photograph for its aesthetic value rather than to report on it, the fair use analysis weakens and permission may be required.

### Assess The Market Effect Honestly

The market effect factor asks whether the use, if widespread, would harm the potential market for the original or its derivatives, and this factor can weigh heavily against fair use even for news reporting. A use that substitutes for the original, allowing readers to avoid purchasing or licensing it, harms the market and weighs against fair use. A use that reports on or critiques the original, without substituting for it, does not harm the market in the same way and weighs in favor.

Assess honestly whether the use substitutes for the original or harms its market. Quoting from a book to report on its contents does not substitute for the book; reproducing an entire article to provide the news it contains may. Using a photograph to report on the photograph does not substitute for licensing the photograph; using it to illustrate a story might, if the photograph's value is in its illustration. The market effect assessment requires understanding the original's market and the use's impact on it.

### Distinguish Fair Use From Permissions And Licensing

Fair use is a defense to unauthorized use, distinct from permissions and licensing, which involve obtaining authorization from the copyright holder. Where fair use is uncertain or the use exceeds what fair use would justify, seek permission or license the material. Do not use fair use as a default to avoid the effort of seeking permission; use it where the analysis supports it, and seek permission where it does not.

For uses that are clearly fair use, such as brief quotations for reporting or criticism, the analysis may be straightforward and permission unnecessary. For uses that are uncertain, such as extended excerpts, full works, or uses near the market substitute line, the analysis requires care and may warrant permission. For uses that are clearly not fair use, such as reproducing full copyrighted works for their own sake, permission is required. Match the approach to the analysis.

### Document The Fair Use Reasoning

Document the fair use reasoning for each significant use of copyrighted material, so that the decision can be defended if challenged. The documentation should identify the material used, the source, the purpose of the use, the amount used, the transformative character, and the market effect assessment. This documentation supports the fair use defense and demonstrates a serious practice.

Documentation is part of fair use risk management, not a secondary task. A use whose fair use reasoning is not documented is harder to defend, regardless of its actual merit, because the defense depends on the specific analysis. Build documentation into the editorial workflow for copyrighted material, and involve counsel for uses where the analysis is uncertain or the stakes are high. ## Common Traps

### Assuming News Reporting Is Always Fair Use

News reporting is a favored purpose but not an automatic defense. Apply the four factors to the specific use.

### Using More Than Necessary Of The Copyrighted Work

The amount factor requires no more than necessary. Using entire works where portions suffice weighs against fair use.

### Reproducing Rather Than Transforming

Transformative uses that add commentary or context are favored. Mere reproduction weakens the analysis.

### Ignoring The Market Effect

Uses that substitute for the original harm its market and weigh against fair use. Assess honestly.

### Using Fair Use To Avoid Seeking Permission

Fair use is a defense, not a convenience default. Seek permission where the analysis does not support fair use.

### Applying The Factors Superficially

Fair use is fact-specific. Apply all four factors to the specific material and use, and document the reasoning.

### Failing To Document The Reasoning

Undocumented fair use decisions are hard to defend. Document the material, purpose, amount, transformative character, and market assessment.

## Self-Check

Before treating a fair use assessment as sound, verify:

- The use is analyzed as a specific fair use, not assumed to be covered by the news reporting category, with the four factors applied to the specific material and use.
- The purpose and character of the use, including its transformative nature, is assessed, with uses that add commentary, context, or reporting favored over mere reproduction.
- The nature of the copyrighted work is considered, with factual works receiving thinner protection and creative works stronger protection.
- The amount used is no more than necessary for the journalistic purpose, with the heart of the work avoided unless essential.
- The market effect is assessed honestly, with uses that substitute for the original or harm its market weighing against fair use.
- Transformative uses that add journalistic meaning are favored, and reproductive uses are scrutinized for whether fair use applies or permission is required.
- The fair use reasoning is documented, identifying the material, source, purpose, amount, transformative character, and market assessment.
- Permission or licensing is sought where fair use is uncertain or the use exceeds what it justifies.
- The assessment is reviewed by a responsible editor and, for significant or uncertain uses, legal counsel.
- Fair use assessment supports editorial judgment; significant fair use questions should involve legal counsel, recognizing that this skill does not provide legal advice, that fair use is fact-specific and jurisdiction-specific, and that reliance on fair use for high-stakes material requires professional legal advice and editorial judgment about the risk.
