# Licensing And Permissions

Licensing and permissions, the process of obtaining authorization from copyright holders to use their material, is the path journalism takes when fair use does not justify the use and the material is not in the public domain, and it is a domain where the mechanics of seeking, negotiating, and documenting rights determine whether the newsroom can use the material lawfully and at a sustainable cost. The judgment problem is that licensing requires time, money, and negotiation that can conflict with deadlines, that the terms of licenses vary widely and can impose restrictions that affect the journalism, and that the failure to obtain or document licenses exposes the newsroom to copyright liability and takedown demands that can disrupt publication. A defensible practice assesses whether material requires licensing, seeks it with enough lead time, negotiates terms that preserve editorial control, and documents the licenses for defense.

Use this skill when seeking licenses or permissions, negotiating terms, drafting or reviewing license agreements, managing rights across media types, or determining public domain status. The main risk is that an agent assumes licensing is a formality, accepts terms that compromise the journalism, or fails to document licenses, any of which can result in liability or disruption.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether Material Requires Licensing At All

Before seeking a license, determine whether the material requires one, because not all copyrighted material requires licensing for journalistic use. Material in the public domain, because its copyright has expired or it was never eligible, can be used freely without license. Material for which fair use justifies the use can be used without license, though the analysis must support it. Material produced by the federal government, in the United States, is generally not subject to copyright and can be used freely. Material licensed under Creative Commons or similar open licenses can be used under the license terms without separate permission.

Determine the material's status first, because licensing material that is free to use wastes resources and may concede rights the newsroom has. Assess public domain status, fair use, government work status, and open license terms before pursuing a license. Where the material requires licensing, proceed; where it does not, document the basis for use without license. See the fair-use-in-journalism skill.

### Seek Licenses With Adequate Lead Time

Licensing takes time: identifying and contacting the rights holder, negotiating terms, obtaining the license, and sometimes paying fees, and this process often conflicts with journalistic deadlines. Build licensing into the editorial workflow with adequate lead time, identifying material that requires licensing early in the story's development and initiating the process well before publication. Last-minute licensing is often impossible, forcing the newsroom to either delay the story, forego the material, or risk using it without license.

For routine material, such as stock photographs or wire content, establish standing licenses or relationships that streamline the process. For unique material that requires case-by-case licensing, such as archival footage or specialized content, start early and have fallback options if the license cannot be obtained. Treat licensing lead time as part of the story planning, not as an afterthought near publication.

### Negotiate Terms That Preserve Editorial Control

License terms vary widely, and the terms the rights holder offers may include restrictions that affect the journalism, such as limits on how the material can be used, requirements to present it in a certain context, prohibitions on alteration or cropping, or termination rights that allow the rights holder to withdraw permission. Negotiate terms that preserve the newsroom's editorial control and that support the journalistic purpose, rather than accepting terms that compromise the story or create ongoing vulnerability.

Pay attention to the scope of the license: what uses it permits, for what duration, in what territories, and in what media. Pay attention to alteration and context restrictions, which may prevent cropping, editing, or contextualizing that the journalism requires. Pay attention to termination and revocation rights, which may allow the rights holder to withdraw permission and force takedown. Negotiate these terms deliberately, with counsel for significant licenses, and walk away from terms that compromise the journalism.

### Document The License For Defense And Accountability

A license that is not documented is difficult to defend, because the authorization to use the material must be demonstrable if challenged. Document each license clearly, identifying the material, the rights holder, the scope of the license, the duration, any fees, and any restrictions. Retain the documentation in a form that survives the news cycle and that can be produced in response to a challenge or takedown demand.

Documentation is part of the licensing practice, not a secondary task. A use whose license is not documented may be treated as unauthorized, regardless of actual permission, because the newsroom cannot prove the authorization. Build documentation into the licensing workflow, with a system for tracking licensed material, its terms, and its documentation, so that the rights position is clear for each piece of material the newsroom uses.

### Manage Rights Across Text, Images, Audio, And Video

Different media types have different licensing considerations, and the practice must account for them. Text licensing, such as for excerpts or quotations, often falls under fair use for shorter excerpts and requires permission for longer ones. Image licensing, for photographs and illustrations, often requires permission from the photographer or their agency, with attention to the depicted subjects' rights, such as model releases for recognizable people in some contexts. Audio and video licensing, for music, footage, and recordings, involves multiple layers of rights, including the composition, the recording, and the performance, each of which may have a different rights holder.

Manage rights according to the media type, recognizing the layered rights in audio and video and the subject rights in images. For complex material, such as video with music, assess each layer of rights and obtain the necessary licenses for each. Do not assume that a license for one layer, such as the footage, covers another, such as the music, because the rights holders may be different and the licenses independent. See the media-law skill.

### Assess Fees And Budget For Licensing

Licensing often involves fees, and the costs can be significant, particularly for unique or high-demand material. Assess the fees for licensed material as part of the story budget, weighing the value of the material to the story against the cost. For material that is essential to the story, the cost may be justified; for material that is illustrative or convenient, lower-cost or free alternatives may serve. Do not allow licensing costs to suppress important journalism, but also do not license material indiscriminately without budget awareness.

Establish budgets and approval processes for licensing fees, so that decisions are made deliberately rather than ad hoc. For standing needs, such as stock imagery, negotiate institutional rates or subscriptions that reduce per-use costs. For unique material, assess the value to the specific story and authorize the cost where justified. Treat licensing fees as a normal part of the production budget, planned and managed rather than improvised.

### Handle Takedown And Dispute Scenarios

Even with licenses, disputes arise: rights holders may claim that use exceeds the license, may dispute the license's validity, or may issue takedown demands under the Digital Millennium Copyright Act or similar procedures. Handle these scenarios with a documented process: review the license and the use, assess the claim's validity, respond appropriately, and involve counsel where the dispute is significant. A takedown demand that is valid should be complied with promptly; one that is invalid should be contested through the appropriate process.

Do not ignore takedown demands, which can escalate to legal action, but also do not capitulate to invalid demands, which concedes rights the newsroom has. Assess each demand against the documented license and the use, and respond based on the assessment. Maintain the documentation that supports the response, because the license and its terms are the foundation of the defense. See the journalist-legal-risk-assessment skill.

## Common Traps

### Licensing Material That Is Free To Use

Public domain, fair use, government work, and open-licensed material may not require licensing. Determine status first.

### Leaving Licensing To The Last Minute

Licensing takes time and conflicts with deadlines. Build it into the workflow with adequate lead time.

### Accepting Terms That Compromise Editorial Control

License terms may restrict use, context, alteration, or carry termination rights. Negotiate to preserve editorial control.

### Failing To Document The License

Undocumented licenses are hard to defend. Document each license and retain it in a survivable form.

### Treating All Media Types The Same

Text, images, audio, and video have different licensing considerations, with layered rights in audio and video. Manage by media type.

### Licensing Indiscriminately Without Budget Awareness

Licensing costs can be significant. Assess fees against value and budget for them deliberately.

### Ignoring Or Capitulating To Takedown Demands

Takedown demands require assessment against the license and use. Comply with valid demands and contest invalid ones.

## Self-Check

Before treating a licensing and permissions practice as sound, verify:

- Each piece of material is assessed for whether licensing is required, considering public domain, fair use, government work, and open license status, before pursuing a license.
- Licenses are sought with adequate lead time, built into the editorial workflow, with standing relationships for routine material and early initiation for unique material.
- License terms are negotiated to preserve editorial control, with attention to scope, duration, alteration and context restrictions, and termination rights.
- Each license is documented clearly, identifying the material, rights holder, scope, duration, fees, and restrictions, retained in a survivable form.
- Rights are managed according to media type, with layered rights in audio and video and subject rights in images assessed and licensed as needed.
- Licensing fees are assessed against value and budgeted deliberately, with approval processes and institutional arrangements for standing needs.
- Takedown and dispute scenarios are handled through a documented process, with valid demands complied with and invalid ones contested.
- The licensing practice is consistent across the newsroom, with clear roles and documentation systems.
- Significant or complex licenses are reviewed by legal counsel before execution.
- Licensing and permissions practice supports editorial judgment; significant rights questions should involve legal counsel, recognizing that this skill does not provide legal advice, that licensing terms and copyright law are jurisdiction-specific, and that reliance on licenses for high-stakes material requires professional legal advice and editorial judgment about the terms and the public interest.
