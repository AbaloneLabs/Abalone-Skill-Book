# User-Generated Content Rights

User-generated content, the photos, videos, posts, and updates that members of the public publish to social media and other platforms, is a vital source for journalism, providing real-time documentation of events, on-the-ground perspectives, and material that professional reporters cannot capture. The judgment problem is that the rights to use UGC are governed by a tangle of copyright, platform terms of service, and privacy law, that the person who posted the content may not own the rights to it, and that the apparent availability of content on the public internet does not automatically grant news organizations the right to use it. A defensible practice assesses the rights to each piece of UGC, seeks permission where required, verifies the content's authenticity and provenance, and navigates platform terms and embedding with care.

Use this skill when using user-generated content, assessing rights, seeking permission, navigating platform terms, or attributing and verifying UGC. The main risk is that an agent assumes public social media content is free to use, ignores the distinction between embedding and reproducing, or uses UGC without verifying its authenticity or provenance.

## Core Rules

### Assess The Rights To Each Piece Of UGC Before Use

The rights to use UGC depend on copyright, platform terms of service, and the intended use, and each piece requires assessment before publication. The person who posted the content is generally the copyright holder if they created it, but they may have posted someone else's work, in which case the rights lie with the original creator. Platform terms of service may grant the platform broad licenses to the content but generally do not grant third parties, including news organizations, the right to use it outside the platform's embedding or sharing features.

Assess the rights to each piece of UGC: who created it, who posted it, whether they are the same, what the platform terms allow, and what the intended use requires. For routine uses of publicly posted content with clear fair use justification, such as reporting on the content itself, the assessment may be straightforward. For uses that reproduce the content in the news organization's own publication, beyond fair use, permission from the copyright holder is generally required. See the fair-use-in-journalism skill.

### Distinguish Embedding From Reproducing

Embedding, using a platform's functionality to display content in a way that pulls it from the platform's servers, and reproducing, copying the content onto the news organization's own servers or pages, have different legal implications. Embedding, where the content remains hosted by the platform and is displayed through its provided tools, may be permitted by the platform's terms and may not constitute reproduction of the copyrighted work, though the law on embedding is evolving and varies by jurisdiction. Reproducing, such as downloading a photo and republishing it, generally requires the copyright holder's permission unless fair use applies.

Distinguish embedding from reproducing, and prefer embedding where the platform's terms permit it and where embedding serves the journalistic purpose. Embedding also preserves the connection to the original post, which supports attribution and allows readers to verify the content's context. Where reproducing is necessary, such as for archival or because the original may be deleted, assess the rights and seek permission or confirm fair use. Do not treat downloading and republishing as equivalent to embedding.

### Seek Permission For Uses Beyond Fair Use

For uses of UGC that exceed fair use, such as reproducing photographs or videos as illustrations or for their own sake rather than to report on them, seek permission from the copyright holder, generally the person who created and posted the content. Permission can often be obtained quickly through direct contact, and many creators are willing to have their work used by news organizations with attribution. The effort of seeking permission is part of the rights practice, not a burden to be avoided through over-reliance on fair use.

Seek permission clearly, identifying the news organization, the intended use, the duration, and the attribution. Document the permission, so that it can be demonstrated if challenged. Where permission is denied or cannot be obtained, reassess whether fair use applies or whether the content should not be used. Do not use content without permission where fair use does not clearly justify it, as this exposes the newsroom to copyright liability.

### Verify The Content's Authenticity And Provenance

Rights assessment is separate from verification, and UGC must be verified for authenticity and provenance before use, regardless of the rights. UGC can be miscaptioned, manipulated, staged, or taken from a different event or time, and the apparent authenticity of a social media post is not sufficient verification for publication. Verify who created the content, when and where it was created, whether it depicts what it claims to depict, and whether it has been altered.

Use verification tools and techniques, including reverse image search, metadata analysis, geolocation, cross-referencing with other sources, and contact with the creator. Document the verification, so that the basis for treating the content as authentic is recorded. A news organization that publishes unverified UGC risks both accuracy and credibility, regardless of the rights, and the verification practice is as important as the rights practice. See the managing-user-generated-submissions skill.

### Attribute The Content To The Creator Accurately

Attribution of UGC serves accuracy, credibility, and fairness to the creator, and it should identify the creator, the platform, and the date, in a form that allows readers to find the original. Accurate attribution also supports the rights practice, because it demonstrates that the use is grounded in the creator's publication and it respects the creator's interest in being credited. Attribute consistently and prominently, not buried or generic.

Attribution is not a substitute for permission where permission is required, but it is part of any defensible use, whether under fair use or with permission. Attribute the creator by name or handle, the platform, and the date, and link to the original where possible. Where the creator wishes to remain anonymous or where attribution could expose them, handle that with care, but do not omit attribution as a convenience. See the source-identity-protection skill for cases where the creator's identity requires protection.

### Navigate Platform Terms Of Service Carefully

Platform terms of service govern what the platform allows users and third parties to do with content, and they interact with copyright law in complex ways. Some platforms' terms grant broad licenses to the platform but do not grant third parties rights to use content outside the platform's features. Some platforms provide embedding tools that permit display on third-party sites; others restrict such use. Violating platform terms can result in the content being removed or the news organization's access being restricted, separate from copyright liability.

Read and understand the relevant platform's terms before relying on them for UGC use, and recognize that terms change. Do not assume that because content is publicly viewable it can be freely used, or that because a platform offers an embed feature any use of it is protected. For high-stakes uses, assess both the copyright position and the platform terms, and seek counsel where the analysis is complex. See the media-law skill.

### Account For Privacy And Sensitivity In UGC Use

UGC often depicts people in private or sensitive situations, and the rights to use the content do not eliminate privacy and sensitivity obligations. A photograph or video that is lawfully obtained and used may still raise privacy concerns if it depicts people in private moments, minors, victims, or vulnerable situations, and the news organization must weigh the public interest against the privacy impact. See the privacy-law-and-newsgathering skill.

Account for privacy and sensitivity in the decision to use UGC, beyond the rights assessment. Consider whether the people depicted consented to the recording, whether publication would expose them to harm, and whether the public interest justifies the use. For content depicting minors, victims of violence, or people in private moments, apply heightened sensitivity, and consider blurring, partial use, or foregoing the content where the privacy impact outweighs the public interest.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Public Social Media Content Is Free To Use

Public availability does not grant use rights. Assess copyright, platform terms, and the intended use before publication.

### Confusing Embedding With Reproducing

Embedding and reproducing have different legal implications. Prefer embedding where permitted and assess rights for reproducing.

### Using UGC Without Seeking Permission Beyond Fair Use

Uses beyond fair use require permission. Seek it clearly, document it, and do not default to fair use to avoid effort.

### Publishing UGC Without Verification

Rights assessment is separate from verification. Verify authenticity and provenance before use, regardless of rights.

### Omitting Or Burying Attribution

Attribution serves accuracy, credibility, and the creator's interest. Attribute consistently and prominently.

### Ignoring Platform Terms Of Service

Platform terms govern use and interact with copyright law. Read and understand them before relying on them.

### Overlooking Privacy And Sensitivity

Lawful use does not eliminate privacy obligations. Weigh public interest against privacy impact, especially for minors, victims, and private moments.

## Self-Check

Before treating a UGC rights assessment as sound, verify:

- The rights to each piece of UGC are assessed before use, including who created and posted it, the platform terms, and the intended use's requirements.
- Embedding is distinguished from reproducing, with embedding preferred where permitted and rights assessed for reproducing.
- Permission is sought for uses beyond fair use, clearly and documented, with fair use not used as a default to avoid effort.
- The content's authenticity and provenance are verified before use, using verification tools and techniques, with the verification documented.
- Attribution identifies the creator, platform, and date, in a form that allows readers to find the original, applied consistently and prominently.
- Platform terms of service are read and understood before reliance, recognizing that public viewability does not grant use rights.
- Privacy and sensitivity are accounted for, with public interest weighed against privacy impact, especially for minors, victims, and private moments.
- The assessment distinguishes content the news organization can use under fair use from content requiring permission.
- The assessment is documented and reviewed by a responsible editor and, for significant or uncertain uses, legal counsel.
- UGC rights assessment supports editorial judgment; significant rights questions should involve legal counsel, recognizing that this skill does not provide legal advice, that UGC rights involve copyright, terms of service, and privacy law, and that use of high-stakes UGC requires professional legal advice and editorial judgment about the rights and the public interest.
