# Records Interpretation And Analysis

Obtaining records is only the first half of records work; the second, harder half is interpreting them honestly. A document does not speak for itself. It is written in institutional language, structured by bureaucratic conventions, full of codes and cross-references, and shaped by the incentives of whoever created it. The judgment problem is that a reporter can hold authentic records and still draw conclusions they do not support, because the temptation to read significance into unfamiliar entries, to treat allegations as findings, or to force a pattern onto scattered data is constant. Defensible records analysis separates what the documents actually establish from what they suggest, and is honest about the gap.

Use this skill when interpreting a document set, reading legal or financial filings, cross-referencing records across custodians, finding patterns, or deciding what conclusions records will support in a story. The main risk is that an agent converts authentic but ambiguous records into confident claims that the documents do not actually sustain.

## Core Rules

### Separate What Records Establish From What They Suggest

For every conclusion you want to draw from records, ask whether the documents establish it or merely suggest it. A contract establishes that parties agreed to terms; it does not establish that the terms were followed, that the work was done, or that the pricing was fair. A court filing establishes that an allegation was made; it does not establish that the allegation is true. A budget establishes planned allocation; it does not establish actual spending. An inspection record establishes what was inspected and recorded; it does not establish the full state of compliance.

Write your analysis in two columns: what the records establish directly, and what they suggest or are consistent with. The story can include both, but they must be labeled differently. Established facts can be stated firmly; suggestions must be attributed, hedged, and corroborated through other evidence before they carry weight.

### Read Each Record In Its Procedural And Institutional Context

Records are generated by processes with rules, stages, and conventions. A docket entry reflects a procedural step; a regulatory letter reflects a specific enforcement stage with defined consequences; a financial filing reflects accounting standards and reporting periods; a procurement record reflects a bidding framework with rules about what must be disclosed. Reading a record without understanding the process that produced it leads to systematic misinterpretation.

Before drawing conclusions, learn the process: what triggers the record, what each field means, what the standard sequence is, what deviations signify, and what the institution's incentives were at the time. Consult clerks, agency staff, auditors, and experienced beat reporters to build this literacy. An entry that looks damning may be routine; an entry that looks routine may be the one that matters.

### Cross-Reference Records Across Custodians And Time

A single custodian's records rarely tell the whole story, because institutions record different aspects of the same reality. A procurement record shows the contract; a budget shows the appropriation; payment records show the disbursement; property records show what was bought; court records show any disputes; regulatory records show oversight. Cross-referencing across custodians reveals inconsistencies, gaps, and patterns that no single record set contains.

Cross-referencing across time is equally important. A pattern that looks suspicious in one year may be routine over a decade; a relationship that looks new may be the continuation of an older one obscured by entity changes. Build timelines that place records from multiple custodians in chronological order, because contradictions and gaps often appear only in sequence. See the investigative-reporting-techniques skill for timeline discipline.

### Test Patterns Against A Baseline

A pattern of records that looks incriminating may reflect ordinary practice in a domain the reporter does not understand. Five no-bid contracts in a year might indicate corruption, or might reflect an emergency procurement framework that all agencies use. A cluster of inspection failures might indicate a uniquely bad operator, or might reflect a regulatory crackdown that affected everyone. Without a baseline, pattern is innuendo.

Establish what normal looks like: how comparable institutions, time periods, or jurisdictions behave; what the typical rates, sequences, and practices are; and whether the pattern you found deviates meaningfully from the baseline. Where you cannot establish a baseline, present the pattern as a question, not a conclusion, and say what further reporting would resolve it.

### Distinguish The Record From The Underlying Reality

Records are representations, not reality itself. A permit records that approval was granted; it does not guarantee that the permitted activity was carried out safely. A personnel file records an official employment history; it may omit transfers, settlements, or departures handled quietly. A financial filing records what was disclosed under the rules; it may not capture off-book activity, related-party transactions structured to avoid disclosure, or subsequent amendments.

Treat records as evidence of what the institution recorded, then ask what the underlying reality might be and how else it could be evidenced. Where records and reality are likely to diverge, pursue the divergence through testimony, physical evidence, data, or other records rather than assuming the record is the whole truth.

### Quantify Carefully And Show Your Method

When records analysis involves numbers, budgets, counts, rates, or comparisons, quantify carefully and show the method. State the source of each figure, the period it covers, what is included and excluded, what denominator makes it meaningful, and whether the figure is preliminary, revised, or final. A percentage without a base, a total without a period, or a comparison without a baseline can mislead even when the underlying number is correct.

For data extracted from records, document the extraction: which records, which fields, what cleaning or transformation was applied, and what was excluded. This lets the analysis be checked and defended, and lets errors be caught before publication. See the data-journalism skill for the broader discipline.

### Attribute Records Precisely In The Story

When writing from records, attribute each material fact to the specific document, custodian, and date, in a way the audience can follow. "According to the contract dated X and filed with Y" is stronger and more honest than "records show." Precise attribution lets readers judge the basis of the claim and lets subjects respond to the actual evidence rather than to a vague assertion.

Where records are incomplete, contested, or interpreted, say so. A story that presents documentary evidence as seamless and unambiguous, when the records actually contain gaps, contradictions, or interpretive choices, overstates what the documents prove and invites rebuttal.

## Common Traps

### Reading Significance Into Routine Entries

Unfamiliarity with a record type's conventions makes ordinary entries look suspicious. A standard disclaimer, a routine amendment, a common code can be misread as evidence of wrongdoing. Build literacy before interpreting, and verify surprising readings with someone who knows the system.

### Treating Allegations As Findings

Filings contain allegations and institutional positions, not established facts. A complaint's claims are evidence that the claims were made, not that they are true. Report them as allegations, attribute them, and verify independently.

### Pattern Without Baseline

A cluster of suspicious-looking records may be ordinary practice. Without comparing to a baseline of comparable institutions or periods, a pattern proves nothing and may be innuendo.

### Confusing The Record With Reality

A record reflects what was recorded, which may diverge from what happened. Pursue the gap between record and reality through other evidence rather than treating the record as definitive.

### Overstating Quantitative Conclusions

Numbers pulled from records can be miscounted, misperiodized, or miscompared. Document the method, show the denominator, and avoid presenting preliminary or partial figures as definitive.

### Vague Attribution That Hides The Evidence Base

"Records show" or "documents indicate" tells the audience nothing about the basis of the claim. Attribute to specific documents and custodians so the evidence can be assessed and contested.

## Self-Check

Before treating records interpretation as sound, verify:

- Each conclusion is separated into what the records establish directly versus what they suggest, and the two are labeled differently in the story.
- Each record was read in its procedural and institutional context, with conventions, codes, and standard sequences understood before interpretation.
- Records were cross-referenced across custodians and across time, with a timeline exposing inconsistencies and gaps.
- Patterns were tested against a baseline of comparable practice, and pattern claims without a baseline are presented as questions, not conclusions.
- The distinction between the record and the underlying reality was recognized, and likely divergences were pursued through other evidence.
- Quantitative analysis documents its source, period, inclusions, exclusions, denominator, and method, and avoids presenting preliminary figures as final.
- Material facts are attributed to specific documents, custodians, and dates, in a way the audience can follow and the subject can contest.
- Gaps, contradictions, and interpretive choices in the records are acknowledged rather than smoothed over.
- No authentic but ambiguous record is converted into a confident claim it does not sustain.
- Records interpretation supports editorial judgment; significant documentary conclusions should be reviewed by a responsible editor, and legal counsel or specialist review consulted where interpretation carries legal or technical risk.
