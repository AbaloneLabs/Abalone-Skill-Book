# Records Research Strategy

Public records are the backbone of accountability reporting. They fix facts that testimony can deny, reveal patterns that insiders will not describe, and expose relationships that institutions prefer hidden. But records are not a search box; they are a landscape scattered across agencies, jurisdictions, formats, and access regimes, each with its own logic, gaps, and delays. The judgment problem is that a reporter who dives into records without a strategy either drowns in irrelevant documents or seizes on the first convenient record without understanding what it does and does not show. Strong records work begins with a map: what exists, where it lives, what it covers, what it omits, and in what order to pursue it.

Use this skill when planning a document-based investigation, deciding which records to seek for a person, company, or institution, mapping records across jurisdictions, or building a documentary foundation before writing. The main risk is that an agent treats records as self-explanatory evidence rather than as artifacts created by institutions with incentives, gaps, and conventions that shape what they contain.

## Core Rules

### Map The Records Landscape Before Requesting Anything

Before requesting or pulling a single document, build a map of what records exist for your subject across the relevant agencies and jurisdictions. A person generates records through birth, education, employment, property ownership, licensing, voting, court involvement, business filings, vehicle registration, professional discipline, and death. A company generates records through incorporation, filings, taxes, permits, litigation, regulatory correspondence, procurement, property, and employment. A government body generates records through budgets, contracts, meeting minutes, correspondence, inspections, audits, personnel, and decisions.

Each record type lives in a specific custodian: a registry, court clerk, agency records office, assessor, secretary of state, regulator, or archive. Knowing where each record lives, and in which jurisdiction, lets you plan an efficient sequence rather than chasing documents at random. Maintain a records map for your beat that you update as you learn the landscape.

### Understand What Each Record Does And Does Not Show

Every record is an artifact created for an institutional purpose, not for the reporter's convenience, and it reflects that purpose's biases and limits. A police report reflects what officers chose to record and may omit what they did not. A corporate filing reflects what the company was required to disclose and may obscure structure through layers of entities. A budget reflects planned spending, not actual spending. A permit reflects approval, not compliance. A court filing contains allegations, not established facts.

For each record you rely on, ask: who created it, for what purpose, under what rules, what does it include, what does it exclude, how current is it, and what incentives shaped its contents. A record that shows what an institution wanted to record is evidence of that, not necessarily of the underlying reality. Cross-reference records against each other and against testimony to find the gaps and contradictions that often reveal the real story.

### Sequence Records From Easiest To Hardest, Cheapest To Costliest

Not all records are equally accessible. Some are online and free; some are online behind paywalls; some require a formal request with fees and delays; some require in-person visits to specific clerks; some are restricted by law or practice. Sequence your pursuit to build momentum and test hypotheses cheaply before investing in expensive, slow requests. Start with what is publicly available online, then move to low-friction requests, then to formal access requests, then to litigation or specialized archives.

This sequencing also lets you decide early whether a story is viable. If the publicly available records already contradict the official narrative, you know to invest in the harder requests. If they reveal nothing, you may need to reconsider the hypothesis before spending months on requests that may not pay off. See the access-and-foia skill for the request process itself.

### Build A Document Foundation Before Writing

Records-based reporting should rest on a documentary foundation, not on a few convenient documents plucked to support a narrative. Assemble the relevant record set, organize it so each central claim maps to specific documents, and identify the gaps where records should exist but do not. A missing audit, a gap in meeting minutes, a contract that cannot be found, a period with no filings, may indicate concealment, poor record-keeping, or a genuine institutional limit, and the distinction matters.

Maintain a master file that tracks every document, its source, its date, its verification status, and the claim it supports. This lets another reporter or editor follow the evidentiary basis of the story and lets you defend each assertion. See the investigative-reporting-techniques skill for the master file discipline.

### Verify Provenance And Authenticity

A record is only as good as its provenance. Before relying on a document, establish where it came from, who created it, whether it is an original or a copy, whether it has been altered, and whether you obtained it from a reliable custodian. Documents that arrive through leaks, intermediaries, or unattributed channels need particular scrutiny, because they may be partial, altered, or fabricated to mislead.

For official records, obtain them from the custodian where possible, or verify a leaked copy against the custodian's version. For digital records, check metadata where available without over-relying on it, and compare against known formats and conventions of the issuing institution. Authentication is a distinct step from interpretation: a document can be authentic and still misleading, or inauthentic and convincing. See the document-verification skill.

### Read Records In Their Institutional Context

A record makes sense only in the context of the institution and process that produced it. A line item in a budget reflects a coding system; a court docket entry reflects procedural rules; a regulatory letter reflects a specific enforcement stage; a procurement record reflects a bidding framework. Without understanding that context, a reporter can misread a routine entry as significant or a significant entry as routine.

Learn the conventions of each record type on your beat: what normal looks like, what deviations mean, what the standard timelines are, what the jargon signifies. Consult experts, clerks, and experienced reporters to build this literacy. Misreading a record because of unfamiliarity with its context is a common and embarrassing error.

### Treat Absence As Information, Carefully

When a record that should exist cannot be found, the absence is itself information, but interpret it carefully. Absence may indicate destruction, concealment, poor record-keeping, jurisdictional confusion, a records request that was framed wrongly, or simply that the record was never created. Do not assert destruction or concealment without evidence; do say what you sought, what you could and could not find, and what explanations are consistent with the gap.

Document your search effort: which custodians you contacted, what you requested, what responses you received, and what alternatives you tried. A documented gap strengthens the story; an undocumented assertion of missing records weakens it.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Convenient Record As The Whole Story

A single striking document can feel like proof, but it may be partial, atypical, or misleading outside its context. Build the record set before drawing conclusions, and test each document against others in the same domain.

### Misreading Records Through Unfamiliarity

Records have conventions, codes, and procedural meanings that outsiders misread. A routine entry can look damning and a damning entry can look routine to someone who does not know the system. Build literacy in each record type before relying on it heavily.

### Overlooking Jurisdictional Fragmentation

Records are scattered across local, regional, national, and sometimes international jurisdictions, each with its own custodians and rules. Assuming a record lives where it is convenient, rather than where it actually does, leads to dead ends. Map the jurisdictions before pursuing.

### Confusing Allegations In Filings With Established Facts

Court filings, regulatory complaints, and enforcement letters contain allegations and institutional positions, not established truths. Report what they allege, attribute them clearly, and verify independently before treating their contents as fact.

### Trusting Leaked Documents Without Authentication

A leaked document that supports the story is tempting to use immediately, but leaks can be partial, altered, or fabricated. Authenticate before relying, and establish what the document shows, what it omits, and who benefits from its release.

### Asserting Concealment Without Evidence Of Concealment

A missing record may have many explanations. Asserting destruction or cover-up without proof exposes the story to rebuttal and the reporter to credibility loss. Document the search and the gap, and let the evidence support only what it supports.

## Self-Check

Before treating records research as sound, verify:

- A records map was built before requests, identifying what exists, where it lives, and in which jurisdictions, for the subject and beat.
- Each relied-upon record is understood for what it does and does not show, including its creator, purpose, rules, inclusions, exclusions, currency, and shaping incentives.
- Records were sequenced from easiest and cheapest to hardest and costliest, with hypotheses tested against accessible records before major investment.
- A documentary foundation supports the story, with each central claim mapped to specific documents and gaps explicitly identified.
- Provenance and authenticity were verified, with leaked or intermediary-sourced documents authenticated against custodians where possible.
- Records were read in their institutional context, with conventions, codes, and procedural meanings understood before heavy reliance.
- Absence of records is reported as a documented search gap with possible explanations, not as an unsupported assertion of concealment.
- No single convenient document is treated as the whole story; the record set was assembled and tested.
- Allegations in filings are attributed as allegations, not presented as established facts.
- Records research supports editorial judgment; significant documentary claims should be reviewed by a responsible editor, and legal counsel consulted where records carry legal exposure or where access is contested.
