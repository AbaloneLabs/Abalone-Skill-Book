# Scientific Studies And Research Reporting

Science reporting translates the slow, uncertain, self-correcting process of research into stories the public can use, but it is also where distortion is most common and most consequential. The judgment problem is that a single study is rarely definitive, statistical significance is not practical importance, and correlation is not causation, yet the news format rewards bold, simple claims that overstate what the evidence shows. A reporter who chases each new study as a breakthrough produces coverage that whiplashes and misleads; one who buries findings in caveats produces coverage no one reads. Strong science reporting understands how research actually works, reads studies critically, distinguishes what is established from what is preliminary, and conveys both the finding and its uncertainty honestly.

Use this skill when reporting on scientific studies, research findings, and peer-reviewed literature, and when interpreting study design, statistics, and consensus for general audiences. The main risk is that an agent may overstate a single study's significance, confuse correlation with causation, or present a contested claim as settled. This skill is editorial guidance only; scientific claims involve expert judgment, and specialist consultation strengthens the work.

## Core Rules

### Read The Actual Study, Not Just The Abstract

Before reporting a study, read the full paper, not just the abstract or the press release, because the abstract is a summary that can obscure limitations and the press release is marketing. Examine the study design, the sample, the methods, the statistical analysis, and the results, and identify the limitations the authors acknowledge and those they do not. Pay attention to effect sizes, confidence intervals, and the distinction between statistical significance and practical importance. A reporter who reports from the abstract reports the authors' self-presentation, not the study. Reading the full paper is the baseline of competent science reporting.

If you cannot understand the paper, consult an independent expert who can.

### Assess Study Design And Quality

Not all studies are equal, and the design determines how much weight the findings deserve. Randomized controlled trials, when well-conducted, provide stronger evidence of causation than observational studies, which can show correlation but are vulnerable to confounding. Meta-analyses and systematic reviews synthesize many studies and often provide the most reliable picture, but their quality depends on the studies they include. Animal and in-vitro studies are preliminary and rarely justify claims about humans. Assess each study's design honestly, and calibrate the strength of your reporting to the strength of the evidence. A mouse study is not a human breakthrough.

Understand the hierarchy of evidence and where each study sits within it.

### Distinguish Causation From Correlation

One of the most common and damaging errors in science reporting is presenting an observed association as a proven cause. Observational studies can show that two factors are linked, but they cannot, by themselves, prove that one causes the other, because confounding variables, reverse causation, and chance can all produce the link. Report associations as associations, and reserve causal language for study designs, like randomized trials, that can support it. When a study claims causation, examine whether the design and analysis justify the claim. The gap between correlation and causation is where most science reporting goes wrong.

Be especially careful with diet, lifestyle, and health stories, where confounding is pervasive.

### Convey Uncertainty And Consensus Honestly

Science is a process of accumulating evidence, and any single study is a contribution, not a conclusion. Convey the state of the evidence honestly: is this finding novel and preliminary, part of an emerging picture, or part of a robust consensus? Report uncertainty without either hiding it or exaggerating it, and distinguish between the uncertainty within a study and the uncertainty across the field. Do not present a contested claim as settled, or a settled consensus as contested. Where there is scientific consensus, report it as consensus; where there is genuine debate, report the debate and the reasons. Honesty about uncertainty builds the trust that science reporting depends on.

A finding that contradicts a consensus is news; one that confirms it is less so, and should be framed accordingly.

### Avoid Overstating Significance And Importance

Two distinct concepts are often conflated in science reporting: statistical significance, whether an effect is likely real, and practical importance, whether it matters. A study can find a statistically significant effect that is too small to care about, and a practically important effect can fail to reach statistical significance in an underpowered study. Report effect sizes and confidence intervals, not just p-values, and explain whether the effect is large enough to matter to readers. Beware relative risk framings that exaggerate small effects, "a 50 percent increase" that represents a change from 1 in 1000 to 1.5 in 1000. Precision about significance and importance protects readers from misleading claims.

Ask: how big is the effect, and does it matter in real life?

### Consult Independent Experts

Before publishing, consult independent experts, scientists who were not involved in the study and who can assess its quality, context, and significance. Independent experts can identify flaws the authors overlooked, situate the finding within the broader literature, and explain what it does and does not show. Seek experts without conflicts of interest, and disclose any conflicts they do have. Expert consultation is not optional garnish; it is how a reporter avoids being a conduit for a single study's claims. A story that reports only what the authors say is a press release, not journalism.

Consult multiple experts when the finding is contested or consequential.

### Beware Conflicts Of Interest And Public Relations

Studies can be shaped by the interests that fund them, and industry-funded research consistently produces results more favorable to the funder than independent research. Check the funding sources and author conflicts of interest, which are disclosed in the paper, and report them when relevant. Beware the public-relations machinery that surrounds science, including university press releases that exaggerate findings, conferences that promote preliminary results, and embargoes that pressure reporters to cover studies uncritically. Treat science PR with the same skepticism as any other PR. The fact that a finding comes from a prestigious institution does not exempt it from scrutiny.

Follow the money, in science as in business.

### Avoid False Balance On Settled Science

Fairness in science reporting means representing the state of the evidence, not giving equal time to every view. On questions where there is robust scientific consensus, such as the safety of vaccines or the reality of human-caused climate change, presenting a fringe view as a competing perspective is false balance that misleads readers. Report the consensus as consensus, and include dissent only when it represents a genuine scientific debate backed by evidence. False balance is not fairness; it is distortion that serves interests opposed to the evidence. Distinguish between scientific debate, which is healthy, and manufactured doubt, which is not.

The existence of a dissenting scientist does not make a question contested.

## Common Traps

### Abstract-Only Reporting

Reporting from the abstract or press release reports the authors' self-presentation, not the study.

### Correlation As Causation

Presenting an observational association as a proven cause is the most common science-reporting error.

### Single Study As Breakthrough

Treating one preliminary study as a definitive finding misleads readers and produces whiplash.

### Statistical Significance As Importance

A statistically significant effect can be too small to matter; report effect sizes.

### Industry-Funded Spin

Funding sources shape findings; undisclosed or unexamined conflicts distort the story.

### False Balance On Consensus

Giving equal time to fringe views on settled science misleads readers.

### Relative Risk Exaggeration

Framing small effects with large relative risks distorts their real-world importance.

### Press Release As Story

University and journal PR exaggerates findings; treat it as marketing, not news.
