# Whistleblower Handling

Whistleblowers are often the only route to wrongdoing that is concealed inside institutions. They take extraordinary personal risk to disclose what insiders know and outsiders cannot see. Handling a whistleblower is therefore one of the highest-stakes relationships in journalism: the source's career, liberty, and sometimes safety rest on the reporter's competence and integrity. The harm this skill prevents is catastrophic and asymmetric. A mishandled source can be identified, fired, prosecuted, or harmed, while the journalist walks away to the next story. Whistleblower handling demands technical source protection, careful assessment of the source's knowledge and motives, rigorous verification of their disclosures, and an unflinching commitment to the pledges made. It is not a routine interview; it is a duty of care.

## Core Rules

### Assess the source's direct knowledge before relying on them
Determine precisely what the whistleblower saw, did, or accessed firsthand, versus what they heard, inferred, or absorbed from the institution's culture. Firsthand knowledge is strong; secondhand claims need independent corroboration. Map the boundary of their direct knowledge and do not let them, or your story, overstate it.

### Understand and weigh the source's motivations
Whistleblowers act from many motives: conscience, grievance, ideology, personal conflict, or some combination. Motivation does not determine truthfulness, but it shapes how you weigh and test the claims. A disgruntled former employee can be telling the truth; a saintly-seeming insider can be selectively disclosing. Investigate the motive and the underlying grievance as part of assessing the material.

### Protect the source's identity with operational discipline
Assume powerful institutions will attempt to identify the source. Use encrypted communications, meet in person when possible, avoid creating metadata trails that link you, and never include identifying details in notes that could be subpoenaed. Understand your outlet's legal exposure and the limits of reporter privilege in your jurisdiction. Plan protection from first contact, not after a leak investigation begins.

### Verify the disclosures independently
A whistleblower's account is a lead, not a conclusion. Corroborate every significant claim with documents, data, or other sources that do not trace back to the whistleblower. The story must stand on its evidence if the source is discredited, removes their support, or is revealed to have an agenda you did not foresee.

### Make and honor explicit agreements about attribution and protection
Clarify, early and in specific terms, how the source will be characterized, what may and may not be published, and what protections you are pledging. Do not promise more than you can deliver, and do not deliver less than you promised. Ambiguity in these agreements is the root of later betrayal and legal peril.

### Give the institution a genuine chance to respond
Before publishing, present the specific findings to the institution and request comment. This is both an ethical obligation and a practical one: the response often reveals defenses, context, or additional wrongdoing, and it strengthens the story's fairness and defensibility. Do so in a way that does not burn the source.

### Plan for legal and institutional retaliation
Anticipate how the institution may respond: leak investigations, legal threats, attacks on the source's credibility, or attempts to subpoena your records. Consult editors and media lawyers early. Have a plan for how to report through retaliation without exposing the source or abandoning the story.

### Treat the source as a person, not merely a conduit
Whistleblowers often face severe psychological and professional consequences. Be honest with them about the risks and the uncertain outcome, do not encourage them to take actions that increase their exposure unnecessarily, and connect them with legal counsel and support resources where appropriate. Your duty extends beyond extracting information.

## Common Traps

### Falling in love with the source
A compelling, sympathetic whistleblower can seduce a reporter into lowering verification standards. Charm and conscience are not evidence. Maintain independent verification regardless of how trustworthy or admirable the source appears.

### Overpromising protection you cannot guarantee
Pledging absolute anonymity or legal protection that reporter privilege may not uphold sets up betrayal. Be honest about what you can and cannot shield. A source who understands the real risks makes better decisions than one lulled by false assurance.

### Leaving a digital trail that identifies the source
Routine tools, email metadata, phone records, building access logs, and even document metadata in the leaked material itself can unmask a whistleblower. Technical sloppiness is a leading cause of source identification. Audit your communications and the documents themselves for identifying traces before publication.

### Publishing details that inadvertently identify the source
Even with anonymity granted, specific details in the story, the timing of publication, or the nature of the disclosures can point to a small set of insiders. Review the published text from the perspective of an institution trying to identify the leaker and remove or generalize narrowing details.

### Letting the source's framing dictate the story
Whistleblowers have a perspective and often an angle. If you adopt their framing wholesale, you become a vehicle for their agenda rather than an independent reporter. Test their interpretation against other evidence and against the institution's account.

### Failing to corroborate before the source is pressured
Institutions move to identify and silence whistleblowers quickly once they suspect a leak. If you have not independently corroborated the core claims before the source is compromised, the story may die with their access. Corroborate early and continuously.

### Abandoning the source after publication
Once the story runs, the journalist's attention moves on, but the source lives with the consequences. Maintain the relationship, honor ongoing protection pledges, and do not treat the whistleblower as expendable once their information is used.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I established precisely what the whistleblower knows firsthand versus what they inferred or heard?
- [ ] Did I investigate and weigh the source's motivations as part of assessing their disclosures?
- [ ] Am I protecting the source's identity with operational discipline from first contact through publication and beyond?
- [ ] Have I corroborated every significant claim independently, so the story stands without relying solely on the whistleblower?
- [ ] Are our agreements about attribution, publication, and protection explicit, realistic, and honored?
- [ ] Did I present the specific findings to the institution for response, in a manner that does not expose the source?
- [ ] Have I planned for legal and institutional retaliation, consulting editors and media lawyers early?
- [ ] Did I review the story for details that could inadvertently identify the source to the institution?
- [ ] Have I been honest with the source about the real risks rather than overpromising protection?
- [ ] Am I maintaining a duty of care to the source as a person, including legal and support resources, beyond the publication of the story?
