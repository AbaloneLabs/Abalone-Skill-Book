# Financial Investigation and Following the Money

Financial investigation is not merely finding a number and publishing it. It is reconstructing how money moves, who benefits, what is concealed, and what the documentary record can and cannot prove. Agents who treat financial reporting as arithmetic or as a hunt for a single damning figure tend to produce stories that are wrong, legally exposed, or far weaker than the documents actually support.

The central judgment problem is that money is designed to be traceable only by those who know the system, while the most consequential flows are deliberately obscured through structures that look legitimate. A good financial investigation matches the strength of every claim to the strength of the evidence, names what is proven versus inferred, and resists the temptation to convert suspicion into accusation.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish What Documents Prove From What They Suggest

The foundational discipline is separating the documentary record from interpretation. A filing shows that a company exists, who its registered officers are, what it declared, and when. It does not, by itself, prove intent, control, beneficial ownership, or wrongdoing.

Before publishing any financial claim, classify it explicitly:

- **Directly documented**: the primary record states it (a filing lists a director, a deed shows a transfer price, a tax return declares income).
- **Inferred from multiple records**: two or more independent documents together support the conclusion (matching addresses, overlapping signatories, timing patterns).
- **Alleged by a source**: a person claims it, but the documents do not confirm it.
- **Speculative**: a plausible reading not yet supported.

Each tier demands a different evidentiary bar and different language in the published story. Conflating tiers is the most common cause of libel risk and correction.

### Learn the Filing Systems Before You Need Them

Financial investigation depends on knowing where records live and what each record can and cannot reveal. Understand, for the jurisdictions you cover:

- Corporate registries (company formation, directors, registered agents, annual returns, beneficial ownership declarations where available).
- Securities filings (registration statements, periodic reports, insider trading disclosures, prospectuses).
- Property and land registries (ownership, mortgages, transfers, liens).
- Court records (judgments, bankruptcy, civil litigation, asset seizures).
- Tax-exempt and nonprofit disclosures (annual information returns, donor and grant reporting).
- Procurement and government contract databases.
- Sanctions and politically exposed person lists.

Each system has gaps, costs, delays, and varying reliability. Some beneficial ownership registers are incomplete or self-reported. Some property records are held by nominee entities. Know the limits of each source before relying on it as proof.

### Follow the Chain, Not Just the Endpoint

Money investigations fail when the reporter stops at the first company name. A single entity is rarely the story; the network is. Trace the chain: who funded whom, through what intermediary, with what declared purpose, and where the money or asset ended up.

Map relationships explicitly:

- **Ownership and control**: parent, subsidiary, joint venture, holding company, trust.
- **People**: directors, officers, signatories, authorized representatives, beneficial owners.
- **Addresses**: registered office, operational address, shared virtual office services.
- **Transactions**: transfers, loans, consulting fees, royalties, intra-group payments.
- **Timing**: formation dates, transfers shortly before or after key events, backdated documents.

A shared registered agent or address is a signal to investigate further, not a finding of control. State signals as signals.

### Interpret Numbers in Context, Not in Isolation

A financial figure means little without context. Revenue, profit, compensation, donations, and contract values must be read against comparable benchmarks: prior years, peer entities, industry norms, declared versus actual, and the entity's own representations elsewhere.

Ask before publishing any number:

- What period does it cover, and is it comparable to the comparison figure?
- Is it gross or net, realized or accrued, audited or self-reported?
- Does it reconcile with other disclosures by the same entity?
- What is the margin of error or estimation method?
- Could a reasonable alternative explanation account for it?

A number that looks shocking in isolation may be routine in context, and a number that looks routine may be the anomaly when compared to peers. Do both checks.

### Protect Sources of Leaked Financial Records

When working with leaked or hacked financial documents, the provenance, handling, and verification of the records is itself a journalistic and legal question. Establish:

- How the source obtained the records and their motivation.
- Whether the records are authentic and complete, and how you verified that.
- Whether possession or publication creates legal exposure (stolen data, trade secrets, data protection law, court injunctions).
- How you will protect the source's identity, including metadata in the files themselves.

Partnering with established investigative programs or legal counsel is often warranted for large leaked datasets. Do not treat a cache of documents as verified simply because it is large.

### Match Accusation Language to Evidence

The words used in a financial investigation carry legal weight. "Company X transferred funds to Company Y" is a factual claim requiring documentary proof. "Company X appears to have transferred funds" is weaker and still requires support. "Records show a transfer" ties the claim to the evidence. "Company X funneled money to evade taxes" is an accusation of intent and wrongdoing that demands far stronger proof.

Maintain a discipline of writing exactly what the documents show, attributing inference to the records or to named experts, and separating the reporter's analysis from documented fact.

### Consult Specialists for Technical Domains

Financial investigation frequently crosses into accounting, tax, securities, valuation, and forensic specialties. Engage qualified independent experts to interpret complex filings, validate methodology, and stress-test conclusions. Attribute expert analysis clearly, and disclose conflicts of interest of any expert you quote.

Do not let an expert's confidence substitute for your own verification of the underlying records. Experts interpret; reporters confirm.

## Common Traps

### Treating Beneficial Ownership Declarations as Ground Truth

Beneficial ownership registers and corporate filings are often self-reported, outdated, or structured through nominee arrangements. A declared owner may be a front; the absence of a name may reflect concealment, not innocence. Treat registry data as a lead, corroborate with independent records, and disclose the limits of self-reported data to readers.

### Overreading a Single Document

One filing, one transfer, or one contract is rarely conclusive. Reporters under deadline pressure publish a single dramatic document as the story, only to find it has a mundane explanation or is contradicted by other records. Require converging evidence before claiming a pattern, and seek the subject's explanation before publication.

### Confusing Correlation With Causation in Timing

The fact that a transfer, formation, or asset sale occurred near a significant event is suggestive, not proof of a connection. Coincidence, routine business cycles, and independent causes are all possible. State timing as a question worth examining, not as a finding, unless you have additional evidence linking the events.

### Misreading Accounting and Tax Concepts

Revenue is not profit. Cash flow is not income. Carrying value is not market value. A loan is not income. A write-down is not a loss of cash. Reporters without accounting literacy routinely misstate financial meaning. When in doubt, consult a qualified accountant and quote the precise figure with its correct label and period.

### Publishing Names Without Verifying Identity

Corporate registries can contain name collisions, common names, and entities deliberately named to resemble others. Confirm that the person named in a filing is the person you think it is, using additional identifiers such as date of birth, address, signature, or corroborating records. A mistaken identity in a financial story is a serious and avoidable harm.

### Accepting Sanctions or Watchlist Inclusion as Proof of Wrongdoing

Inclusion on a sanctions list, PEP register, or watchlist is a status that must be reported accurately, but it is not a conviction and may reflect political designation, unproven allegation, or administrative error. Report the listing factually, note the basis if known, and seek the subject's response.

### Ignoring the Subject's Lawful Explanation

Many financial structures that look suspicious have lawful purposes: asset protection, estate planning, legitimate tax efficiency, regulatory compliance, or privacy. Failing to put allegations to the subject and report their explanation fairly is both an ethical failure and a libel risk. The explanation may not defeat the story, but it must be sought and represented.

## Self-Check

- Have I classified every financial claim by evidence tier (documented, inferred, alleged, speculative) and matched the published language to the strongest tier supported?
- Did I trace the full chain of entities, people, addresses, and transactions rather than stopping at the first company name?
- Do I understand the specific filing systems I relied on, including their gaps, update delays, and reliability limits, and have I disclosed those limits to readers?
- Have I interpreted every number in context against comparable benchmarks, and confirmed gross versus net, audited versus self-reported, and the correct period?
- If working with leaked records, have I established provenance, verified authenticity, assessed legal exposure, and protected the source including file metadata?
- Does my accusation language match the evidence, avoiding imputations of intent or wrongdoing that the documents alone do not prove?
- Have I consulted qualified independent experts for technical accounting, tax, or valuation questions, and disclosed any conflicts of interest?
- Did I verify the identity of every named individual against additional identifiers to avoid mistaken identity?
- Did I seek and fairly represent the subject's lawful explanation before publication?
- Have I avoided presenting sanctions listings, watchlist entries, or self-reported registry data as proof of wrongdoing?
