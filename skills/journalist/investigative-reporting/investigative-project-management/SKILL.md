# Investigative Project Management

An investigation is not just harder reporting; it is a project with scope, timeline, resources, legal exposure, collaborators, and a publication strategy that must be managed deliberately. Reporters who treat an investigation as ordinary reporting stretched over more time tend to run out of evidence, lose institutional support, burn sources, or publish late and incomplete because no one managed the process.

The judgment problem is that investigative work is inherently uncertain: you cannot know at the outset whether the story will materialize, how long it will take, or what it will cost. Good project management accepts that uncertainty while building structure that keeps the work honest, protected, and publishable.

## Core Rules

### Define Scope and the Threshold Question Before Committing Resources

Before committing substantial time, commit a written scope document that forces honesty about what you are and are not investigating. Address:

- The central question the investigation must answer.
- The minimum evidentiary standard required to publish (what would count as proof).
- The likely subjects, entities, and jurisdictions involved.
- The realistic time and resource envelope, and who approves overruns.
- The kill criteria: what evidence, if absent after a defined period, would end the investigation.

The kill criteria matter most. Investigations that lack a defined stopping rule consume resources indefinitely and produce pressure to publish weak findings. Decide in advance what failure looks like.

### Build and Maintain a Master Timeline

Every investigation needs a living master timeline that records every verified fact, its date, its source, and its evidentiary strength. The timeline is the spine of the final story and the primary defense against legal challenge.

Maintain it from day one, not at the end. Each entry should record:

- The event or fact.
- The date or date range, and confidence in the date.
- The source (document, interview, observation) and a citation or file reference.
- The evidence tier (directly documented, inferred, alleged).
- Open questions or contradictions attached to that entry.

A timeline built incrementally surfaces contradictions early, reveals gaps, and prevents the team from relying on memory of what was proven when.

### Track Evidence Centrally and Securely

Investigations generate large volumes of documents, data, interview notes, and correspondence that must be organized, version-controlled, and protected. Establish:

- A secure central repository with controlled access and an audit trail.
- A consistent naming and filing convention understood by all team members.
- Backup and redundancy, including offline copies of critical records.
- Chain-of-custody notes for original documents and leaked materials.
- Clear separation between verified evidence, working hypotheses, and raw speculation.

Disorganization is not merely inefficient; it becomes a legal and editorial liability when you must demonstrate what you knew and when, or defend the integrity of records.

### Manage People, Roles, and Collaboration Deliberately

Investigative teams fail when roles are ambiguous, when one person becomes a single point of failure, or when collaboration creates security risks. Define:

- Who leads editorially and who leads on reporting, data, legal, and safety.
- Who has access to which sources, documents, and systems.
- How internal communication occurs, and what must not be put in writing or on vulnerable channels.
- How decisions to expand scope, approach subjects, or publish are made and by whom.

For cross-organization collaborations, add a written agreement covering credit, embargo, editorial control, legal responsibility, data handling, and what happens if a partner wants to delay or kill the story.

### Sequence Reporting to Protect Sources and the Story

The order of reporting steps affects both safety and outcome. A common discipline:

1. Gather documentary and open-source evidence first, before approaching human sources who might be endangered or tipped off.
2. Develop and protect whistleblowers and insiders before confronting subjects.
3. Build the corroborated record before seeking comment from the subject.
4. Seek the subject's response only once the evidence is solid, to avoid premature denial or legal maneuvering.
5. Conduct legal review on the substantiated draft, not on shifting working text.

Deviating from this sequence can endanger sources, trigger destruction of evidence, or invite preemptive legal action. Document the rationale when the sequence must change.

### Budget for Legal Review and Pre-Publication Defense

Investigations carry elevated legal risk. Engage legal counsel early, not at the end, and budget for:

- Pre-publication review of the substantiated draft.
- Libel and privacy risk assessment, especially in jurisdictions with claimant-friendly law.
- Defense fund planning for anticipated legal threats or injunctions.
- Documentation of verification steps to support a defense of responsible journalism.

Counsel should review the story the team intends to publish, with the evidence behind each claim mapped. Surprise legal problems at publication usually reflect late engagement.

### Plan Publication and Impact Strategy Deliberately

How an investigation is published affects whether it lands or is buried. Consider:

- Format and length appropriate to the findings, and whether a series is warranted.
- Visual, data, and document presentation that lets readers verify key claims.
- Timing relative to news cycles, legal windows, and partner embargoes.
- Distribution across platforms and languages where relevant.
- Follow-up commitment: who continues to develop the story after publication.
- Reader and community engagement, including how affected communities are informed respectfully.

Publication is not the end of the project. Plan for follow-up, corrections handling, and the long tail of consequences for sources and subjects.

### Sustain the Team and Protect Wellbeing

Long investigations impose psychological and physical costs: exposure to trauma material, threats, isolation, and sustained uncertainty. Manage wellbeing as a project responsibility:

- Rotate exposure to distressing material across team members.
- Provide access to trauma support and security advisors.
- Normalize breaks and peer check-ins.
- Watch for signs of secondary trauma, burnout, or compromised judgment.

A burned or traumatized reporter makes worse decisions and is more vulnerable to error and manipulation. Wellbeing is a quality control measure, not a perk.

## Common Traps

### Falling in Love With the Hypothesis

Investigations often begin with a strong theory. Reporters then selectively notice confirming evidence and explain away contradictions. Build in explicit checkpoints where the team asks: what evidence would disprove our current theory, and have we looked for it? Assign someone the role of stress-testing the dominant hypothesis.

### Scope Creep Without Re-Approval

Investigations expand: new leads, new jurisdictions, new subjects. Uncontrolled scope creep exhausts resources and delays publication without improving the core story. Require a written re-scoping decision, with approver sign-off, when the investigation materially expands.

### Single-Source Dependency

When one source, one document, or one dataset carries the investigation, the project is fragile and vulnerable to discreditation. Seek independent corroboration of every load-bearing claim. If corroboration is impossible, say so explicitly in the story and to editors.

### Premature Subject Approach

Approaching the subject too early invites denial, legal threats, evidence destruction, and source endangerment. Approaching too late denies them a fair chance to respond and risks ethical and legal challenge. Time the approach to the moment the evidence is solid but the story is not yet locked.

### Treating Collaboration as Automatic Quality

Partnering with other outlets or programs adds reach and resources, but it also adds coordination risk, editorial disagreement, and security exposure. A weak partner can leak, delay, or compromise the story. Vet partners, write down the terms, and maintain your own editorial independence.

### Losing the Documentation Thread

When evidence is scattered across notebooks, drives, and inboxes, the team cannot reconstruct what was proven, and legal defense suffers. Invest in centralized, disciplined evidence management from the start, even when it feels slower.

### Publishing Without a Follow-Up Plan

An investigation published and abandoned wastes its impact and abandons sources who took risks. Plan follow-up reporting, community engagement, and ongoing protection before publication day.

## Self-Check

- Have I written a scope document with a central question, evidentiary standard, resource envelope, and explicit kill criteria?
- Is there a living master timeline recording every verified fact with date, source, and evidence tier?
- Is evidence stored centrally and securely, with naming conventions, backups, chain-of-custody notes, and clear separation of verified fact from speculation?
- Are team roles, access, communication norms, and decision rights defined, and is there a written agreement for any cross-organization collaboration?
- Does the reporting sequence protect sources first, build corroboration, and approach the subject only when evidence is solid?
- Has legal counsel been engaged early, with pre-publication review planned on the substantiated draft and defense resources budgeted?
- Is there a publication and impact strategy covering format, timing, distribution, follow-up, and community engagement?
- Have I built in checkpoints to stress-test the hypothesis and look for disconfirming evidence?
- Is there independent corroboration for every load-bearing claim, and have I avoided single-source dependency?
- Is team wellbeing managed as a project responsibility, with rotation, trauma support, and monitoring for burnout?
