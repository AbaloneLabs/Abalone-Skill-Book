# Document-Based Investigation

Documents are the backbone of serious investigation because they are durable, often created for purposes unrelated to your story, and far harder to disavow than human testimony. A document-based investigation builds conclusions from records, contracts, filings, correspondence, and archives rather than from assertions alone. The harm this skill prevents is the common failure of relying on sources' characterizations of what documents say, or on a few cherry-picked records, while missing the fuller paper trail that could confirm, complicate, or overturn the thesis. Done well, document-based investigation produces findings that withstand legal challenge; done poorly, it produces selective readings that collapse when the full record emerges.

## Core Rules

### Let documents drive the thesis, not the other way around
Begin with a hypothesis, then seek the documents that would prove or disprove it. But stay willing to abandon or revise the thesis when the records point elsewhere. The cardinal sin is reverse-engineering a narrative from a few convenient documents while ignoring the rest of the file. Follow the paper wherever it leads, including away from your premise.

### Map the full document universe before drawing conclusions
Before asserting what the records show, identify the complete set of relevant documents: which agencies, companies, courts, or individuals hold them, what format they take, and what is missing. A conclusion drawn from a partial record is provisional. Knowing what you do not have is as important as knowing what you do.

### Obtain originals and preserve them unaltered
Work from original or high-fidelity copies, not transcriptions or summaries. Preserve files exactly as received, with provenance recorded. Summaries and paraphrases introduce error and strip context; an unaltered original can be re-examined and authenticated, a paraphrase cannot.

### Read documents in their full context
A clause quoted in isolation can mean the opposite of what it means in context. Read the entire document, the related documents it references, and the surrounding circumstances of its creation. Understand the document's purpose, audience, and legal force before extracting claims from it.

### Triangulate documents against each other and against testimony
No single document type is sufficient. Cross-check contracts against filings, filings against internal correspondence, correspondence against financial records. Where documents and human sources conflict, investigate the discrepancy rather than choosing the more convenient account. The strongest findings emerge where independent records converge.

### Understand what each document can and cannot prove
A contract proves an agreement existed; it does not prove it was performed. A filing proves something was reported; it does not prove it was true. A memo proves someone wrote something; it does not prove the underlying facts. Be precise about the inferential distance between what a record states and what you conclude from it.

### Organize for retrieval and pattern detection
Investigations generate hundreds or thousands of records. Use a consistent naming scheme, indexing, and ideally a document database that allows search and cross-reference. Disorganization causes you to miss the document that matters most, the one that ties the narrative together or undermines it.

### Treat gaps and absences as evidence
Sometimes the most revealing fact is what is missing: a deleted email, an unfiled disclosure, a gap in a sequence, a record that should exist but does not. Document the absence and investigate why. Deliberate destruction or omission can itself be a finding, but distinguish genuine absence from the routine incompleteness of any records system.

## Common Traps

### Cherry-picking the convenient record
Selecting the documents that support the thesis and quietly setting aside the rest is the most damaging failure mode. An adversarial reviewer will find the omitted records and use them to discredit the entire investigation. Surface and grapple with the contradictory material.

### Reading modern assumptions into old or foreign documents
Legal terminology, corporate structures, and bureaucratic conventions differ across jurisdictions and eras. Misreading a document because you imposed your own context on it produces confident-sounding errors. When in doubt, consult someone fluent in that document's domain.

### Treating a leaked document as self-proving
A leaked file is a lead, not a verdict. It may be partial, altered, or selected by a source with an agenda. Authenticate it, contextualize it, and seek corroboration before building conclusions on it. The drama of a leak does not substitute for verification.

### Overstating what circumstantial documents show
Records often imply rather than state wrongdoing. Resist converting strong implication into declared fact. It is more defensible, and often more powerful, to lay out the documents and let the pattern speak, clearly noting what is established versus inferred.

### Failing to seek the subject's explanation of the documents
Before publishing damaging interpretations of records, present them to the subject and ask for explanation. Documents can have innocent explanations invisible without context: a standard clause, a routine transaction, a known error since corrected. Denying the subject this chance is both unfair and a recipe for error.

### Losing the thread in volume
Large document sets overwhelm. Without rigorous organization and a clear working theory, you drown in detail and miss the connective tissue. Periodically step back, restate the thesis, and confirm which documents actually bear on it.

### Ignoring legal constraints on obtaining and using documents
Some records are obtained unlawfully, some are under seal, some are protected by privilege or privacy law. Understand the legal status of every document before relying on it. Lawfully obtained, properly handled evidence protects the story and the outlet.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I identify the full universe of relevant documents, including what is missing or inaccessible, before drawing conclusions?
- [ ] Am I working from originals or faithful copies, preserved unaltered with provenance recorded?
- [ ] Did I read each key document in full context, including related records and circumstances of creation?
- [ ] Have I been precise about what each document actually proves versus what I infer from it?
- [ ] Did I triangulate documents against each other and against human testimony, investigating discrepancies rather than choosing the convenient account?
- [ ] Have I surfaced and grappled with contradictory or complicating documents rather than setting them aside?
- [ ] Did I present the key documents to the subject and seek their explanation before publication?
- [ ] Is the document set organized so that any specific claim can be traced to its supporting record?
- [ ] Have I distinguished genuine, meaningful absences from routine incompleteness in the records?
- [ ] Before publication, can I defend the authenticity, lawful acquisition, and accurate interpretation of every document on which the story relies?
