# Source Protection And Safety

When a source trusts a journalist with information that could cost them their job, their freedom, or their life, the journalist assumes a duty of protection that is operational, not merely ethical. Source protection is the discipline of ensuring that no adversary, employer, government, hacker, or court can identify the source through the journalist's actions, notes, communications, metadata, or published detail. The harm this skill prevents is catastrophic and irreversible: sources imprisoned, fired, harassed, killed, or disappeared because a newsroom was careless with a chat log, left identifying detail in a story, or surrendered notes under legal pressure without a plan. Once a source is exposed, no correction undoes the harm.

Use this skill from the first contact with any sensitive source through long after publication. This is a high-risk domain involving legal jeopardy and physical safety; consult editors, security specialists, and legal counsel early, and never make protection promises you cannot operationally fulfill.

## Core Rules

### Assess the threat model before contact
Before engaging a sensitive source, identify who would want to identify them and what capabilities they have. Is the adversary an employer with HR records, a government with surveillance powers, a litigant with subpoena power, a criminal network willing to use violence, or a platform that can be compelled to hand over logs? Different threats require different protections. A source hiding a tip from a local employer faces a different model than a whistleblower inside a surveillance state. Match the security posture to the actual adversary, and escalate the posture when in doubt.

### Use secure communications matched to the threat
Plain email, standard phone calls, consumer messaging apps, and newsroom Slack are not appropriate for sensitive source contact against capable adversaries. Use end-to-end encrypted tools, consider secure drop systems, avoid carrying source identifiers on devices that can be seized or compromised, and prefer in-person contact for the most sensitive conversations when feasible. Never assume a commercial platform will refuse a legal demand for your logs. Document the secure-contact procedure so the source knows how to reach you safely and you do not default to convenient but exposed channels.

### Minimize what you record and where you store it
Notes that identify a source are a liability. Where possible, separate the source's identity from the substance of what they shared. Avoid writing names, direct contact details, or identifying specifics into shared systems, cloud notes, or email. Consider whether certain notes should exist at all. If you must keep identifying information, store it encrypted, access-controlled, and segregated from the story material. Plan for what happens to your devices and notes under subpoena, seizure, theft, or a breach.

### Scrub published detail for deductive identification
The most common way sources are burned is not a hack but a story that contains enough detail for the adversary to deduce the identity. If you describe a source as "a mid-level engineer in the payments team who joined the company in 2019," the employer can run that query. Genericize details, alter non-essential specifics, and avoid combinations of attributes that triangulate to one person. Have an editor specifically review the published text for deductive identification risk before publication.

### Plan for legal compulsion before it arrives
Subpoenas, court orders, search warrants, and national-security letters can demand notes, communications, devices, and testimony. Plan in advance: what is the newsroom's legal posture, who is counsel, will the newsroom move to quash, what will happen to materials if devices are seized, and what will you do if ordered to reveal a source. Some protections, like shield laws and reporter's privilege, vary by jurisdiction and are not absolute. Know your jurisdiction's posture before you need it, and never assume you will be able to refuse at the moment of pressure.

### Brief the source on the limits of protection
Be honest with the source about what you can and cannot guarantee. You can protect their identity in your reporting and resist legal compulsion; you cannot guarantee that their own digital footprint, their conversations with others, or their access patterns will not reveal them. Help the source understand their own operational security, because the most careful journalist cannot protect a source who logs into a corporate VPN to leak, discusses the leak with colleagues, or leaves a trail on their own devices.

### Maintain protection after publication
Risk does not end when the story runs. Investigations, lawsuits, follow-up reporting, and time can all surface a source long after publication. Do not discuss the source's identity in interviews about the story, in public events, or with colleagues outside the need-to-know circle. If the source faces escalating risk, maintain the relationship and be prepared to help them connect to legal, security, or support resources. Treat the protection as ongoing, not as a one-time promise.

### Distinguish protection from concealment of fabrication
Source protection must not become a shield for unverifiable claims or, in the worst case, fabrication. Internal accountability, an editor who knows the source or who has verified the basis through other means, and corroboration through documents or additional reporting are how a newsroom maintains honesty while protecting a source. If protection makes a claim effectively uncheckable, treat the claim with extreme caution and consider not publishing.

## Common Traps

### Assuming convenience tools are secure enough
Consumer messaging apps, newsroom chat, personal email, and standard phone calls feel sufficient because they are how normal work happens. Against a capable adversary they are transparent. The trap is that the channel works fine right up until it does not, and by then the source is exposed.

### Burning a source through published detail
A vivid, specific source description reads as strong journalism but can identify the source to the adversary through triangulation. The trap is that the very specificity that builds reader trust is what endangers the source. Always weigh descriptive texture against deductive risk.

### Overpromising absolute protection
Promising a source they will never be identified, when subpoenas, surveillance, or the source's own mistakes could expose them, sets up a betrayal. The trap is making the pledge to secure the cooperation without the operational capacity to back it.

### Treating protection as the reporter's solo problem
A single reporter carrying source identity alone is fragile: illness, departure, legal pressure, or a single mistake exposes the source. The trap is treating protection as personal loyalty rather than an institutional system with editors, counsel, and security practice.

### Forgetting metadata and access logs
Even when message content is encrypted, metadata, login times, building access records, print logs, and document access trails can identify a leaker. The trap is focusing on the content of communications while ignoring the surrounding digital exhaust.

### Letting protection block all verification
In the name of protecting a source, a newsroom may end up with a claim no one can check, which is dangerous. The trap is that protection and accountability pull in opposite directions, and resolving it requires real corroboration work, not just a pledge.

## Self-Check

- Have you assessed the threat model, identified the likely adversary and their capabilities, and matched your security posture accordingly?
- Are communications with the source conducted through channels appropriate to the threat, with secure alternatives for the most sensitive contact?
- Have you minimized identifying records, segregated identity from substance, and planned storage that survives subpoena, seizure, or breach?
- Has an editor specifically reviewed the published text for details that could allow deductive identification of the source?
- Do you have a plan for legal compulsion, including counsel, jurisdictional posture, and what happens to materials under pressure?
- Have you been honest with the source about the limits of protection and helped them understand their own operational security?
- Is protection treated as ongoing after publication, with the source's identity guarded in follow-up, interviews, and internal discussion?
- For high-risk sources, have editors, security specialists, and legal counsel been involved, recognizing that source protection failures can cause irreversible harm including imprisonment or physical danger?
