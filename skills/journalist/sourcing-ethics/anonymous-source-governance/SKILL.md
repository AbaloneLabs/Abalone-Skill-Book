# Anonymous Source Governance

Anonymity is a tool, not a default. Used well, it lets journalists reveal information that powerful actors are hiding, protect whistleblowers and vulnerable people from retaliation, and serve the public when no on-the-record path exists. Used casually, it degrades trust, lets officials smear opponents without accountability, launder spin as news, and leave readers unable to judge credibility. The harm this skill prevents is the slow erosion of accountability journalism into a conduit for unattributed influence, plus the very real danger to sources who were promised protection that the newsroom later cannot or will not uphold. Anonymous source governance is the discipline of granting anonymity rarely, justifying it explicitly, and protecting it absolutely once given.

Use this skill before any conversation where anonymity might come up, before publishing any anonymously sourced claim, and whenever a source requests a change in attribution ground rules. Journalism carries legal and safety risks for sources; consult editors on every anonymity grant and legal counsel where exposure, subpoenas, or source safety are plausible.

## Core Rules

### Set ground rules before any sensitive information is shared
Attribution agreements must be established before a source discloses anything they would not want published with their name attached. Once a source has spoken, you cannot retroactively impose rules, and they cannot retroactively demand them. At the start of any sensitive conversation, confirm explicitly whether the source is speaking on the record, on background, on deep background, or on the condition of anonymity, and confirm that you both understand each term identically. Ambiguity at this stage creates disputes and danger later.

### Apply a strict test before granting anonymity
Anonymity should be the exception, granted only when all of the following hold: the information is strongly newsworthy, the information is factual rather than opinion or speculation, the source has direct knowledge, the information cannot be obtained on the record through reasonable effort, and the source faces a real risk of harm, retaliation, job loss, legal jeopardy, or physical danger if named. If any of these fail, push for on-the-record attribution or find another path. Anonymity granted to make a source comfortable, rather than safe, is not justified.

### Reject anonymity used to attack without accountability
A common abuse is the official who demands anonymity to level accusations, smear rivals, float trial balloons, or spin reporters while avoiding consequences. Anonymity that shields a powerful source from accountability for an attack is almost never justified. The test: would granting anonymity here serve the public, or serve the source's interest in deniability? When in doubt, decline and explain that attacks require a name.

### Tell readers why anonymity was granted
Readers cannot evaluate an unnamed source without knowing the basis. When publishing anonymously sourced information, explain in the story, to the extent safety allows, why anonymity was necessary and what basis the source has for knowing the information. "A person familiar with the matter" is weak; "a senior official directly involved in the negotiations, who was not authorized to speak publicly" tells readers more. Vague sourcing invites distrust and should be improved, not defended.

### Confirm someone in the newsroom knows the source's identity
For accountability and safety, at least one editor beyond the reporter should know the identity of any anonymous source whose information is central to a story. This protects against fabrication, allows independent judgment of credibility, and ensures continuity if the reporter leaves. Exceptions, such as sources whose identity must be known to no one, require explicit senior editorial approval and a documented safety plan.

### Describe the source enough for readers to judge credibility
Even without a name, you can often describe the source's role, access, motivation for speaking, and relationship to the story. "A career civil servant who reviewed the documents" carries different weight than "a political appointee who left the agency last year." Provide enough texture for readers to assess whether the source is in a position to know and whether they have an ax to grind.

### Protect the source absolutely once anonymity is promised
A pledge of anonymity is a pledge of protection. Do not reveal a source's identity in casual conversation, in notes that could be subpoenaed or stolen, in metadata, in background detail that could identify them by deduction, or under legal pressure without exhausting every legal alternative. Plan for subpoena risk, source separation, secure communications, and what happens if the source is contacted by law enforcement. If you cannot guarantee protection, do not make the promise.

### Move as much as possible onto the record
Even when some information requires anonymity, parts often can be attributed. Push the source at every stage: can this fact go on the record? Can this quote? Can we name them for the institutional role while protecting identity? Can they go on the record after publication if the reaction is manageable? Incremental movement toward attribution strengthens the story and reduces dependence on anonymity.

## Common Traps

### Granting anonymity by default to official sources
Officials who have communications staffs, press secretaries, and the ability to speak publicly do not need anonymity the way whistleblowers do. Granting it to them as a convenience lets them avoid accountability and trains them to expect it. The trap feels professional but erodes the on-the-record record over time.

### The "not authorized to speak" laundering
Sources "not authorized to speak publicly" are often speaking with the quiet blessing of their institution, floating a message the institution wants disseminated without owning. Treating these as genuine leaks, rather than coordinated messaging, lets institutions use journalism as an unattributed channel. The trap is that the information is real and useful, which masks the manipulation.

### Vague source descriptions that hide weakness
"A source said" can hide that the source is a single low-level aide, a rival with an interest, or someone passing on hearsay. Vague sourcing reads as authoritative but cannot be evaluated. The trap is that vagueness protects the source at the cost of the reader's ability to judge.

### Overpromising protection you cannot deliver
Promising absolute anonymity without the operational security to back it up endangers the source when subpoenas, leaks, or surveillance arrive. The trap is making the pledge in the moment of the interview without planning for legal and digital exposure.

### Letting anonymity shield fabrication risk
Anonymous claims that cannot be checked are dangerous. If a story rests on a single unnamed source making a sensational allegation, with no documents, no corroboration, and no on-record evidence, the anonymity is shielding the story from any accountability. The trap is that the claim is too good to check carefully.

### Drift in ground rules over a long relationship
A source who began on the record may start requesting anonymity, or vice versa, and assumptions creep in. Without reconfirming ground rules each conversation, you can publish something the source believed was off the record, or attribute something they expected to be protected. The trap is familiarity breeding dangerous sloppiness.

## Self-Check

- Were attribution ground rules confirmed explicitly before any sensitive information was shared, with shared understanding of each term?
- Does the anonymity pass the strict test: newsworthy, factual, direct knowledge, unavailable on the record, and real risk of harm if named?
- Is the anonymity protecting a vulnerable whistleblower, or shielding a powerful source attacking others without accountability?
- Does the published story explain to readers why anonymity was granted and what basis the source has for the information?
- Does at least one editor know the source's identity, or is there a documented exception with a safety plan?
- Is the source described specifically enough for readers to judge credibility and possible motive?
- Have you planned for source protection across legal, digital, and operational dimensions, and avoided overpromising what you can guarantee?
- Have you pushed to move as much information as possible onto the record, and consulted editors and, where legal or safety exposure exists, legal counsel before publishing centrally anonymous claims?
