# Source Credibility Assessment

Every story rests on judgments about how much to trust the people providing information. Source credibility assessment is the disciplined evaluation of whether a given source is likely to be truthful, knowledgeable, and free enough from distortion to be relied upon. The harm this skill prevents is the two great failures of sourcing: trusting a source who is wrong, self-interested, or compromised, and dismissing a source who is actually reliable. Credibility is not a fixed trait; it is contextual, and a source reliable on one topic may be unreliable on another. The skill is in building, maintaining, and applying a nuanced, evidence-based view of each source rather than relying on reputation, warmth, or convenience.

## Core Rules

### Assess credibility on multiple dimensions
Reliability is not a single score. Evaluate a source along distinct axes: direct knowledge, do they have firsthand access to the information; track record, have they been accurate before; expertise, do they understand the domain; motivation, what do they gain; independence, are they free from coercion or from a shared origin with other sources; and stability, is their account consistent over time and under questioning. Weigh these together rather than collapsing them into a gut judgment.

### Favor direct knowledge over proximity and rank
A senior official repeating what they heard is a weaker source than a mid-level staffer who was in the room. Rank confers authority but not necessarily firsthand knowledge. Ask not only who the source is but how they know what they claim, and prefer the source closest to the actual event or document.

### Build and consult a track record over time
Maintain an internal record of what each source has told you and how it held up. A source who has been repeatedly accurate earns trust; one who has fed you wrong or self-serving information earns skepticism. New sources have no track record and should be treated as unproven until their information is independently corroborated.

### Probe the basis of knowledge explicitly
Ask sources how they know. Did they see it, hear it, read it, or infer it? From whom did they learn it? When? A source's confidence is not evidence; their basis of knowledge is. Confident sources with secondhand information are a classic cause of error.

### Evaluate motivation without letting it determine truthfulness
A source's motive, grievance, ambition, ideology, self-protection, shapes what they choose to share and how they frame it, but a motivated source can still be telling the truth. Understand the motive so you can weigh and test the account, not so you can dismiss it. Conversely, a source with no apparent motive can be wrong.

### Test for consistency and specificity
Reliable accounts tend to be consistent across tellings and contain verifiable specific detail. Accounts that shift, that resist specifics, or that conveniently match whatever you suggest, are weaker. Ask the same question in different ways and over time; inconsistencies are diagnostic.

### Recognize the context-dependence of credibility
A source highly reliable on their own agency's operations may be unreliable when speculating about a rival's. An expert in one subfield may be out of their depth in an adjacent one. Tag credibility to topic, and do not generalize a source's reliability from one domain to another without basis.

### Treat anonymous sources with heightened scrutiny
Anonymity removes a check that public attribution provides, the risk to the source's reputation if they are wrong or misleading. Compensate with stricter internal verification: require a stronger basis of knowledge, clearer documentation of who they are and how they know, and independent corroboration before relying on anonymous claims.

## Common Traps

### Trusting authority over access
Impressive titles and confident delivery substitute for firsthand knowledge in the reporter's mind. The official who sounds authoritative but is relaying secondhand information is a frequent source of error. Probe access, not just authority.

### The halo effect
A source who is charming, helpful, or shares your worldview earns unearned trust. Warmth and agreement are not evidence of accuracy. Audit whether you are trusting the information or liking the person.

### Confirmation bias in source selection
When a source tells you what you already believe, you weigh them more heavily. Seek out and fairly weigh sources who challenge your working thesis, and be suspicious of your own readiness to accept a convenient account.

### Failing to update on disconfirmation
Once a source is labeled reliable, reporters stop checking them. But reliability can decay: sources lose access, develop grievances, or are co-opted. Update your assessment as new evidence about the source arrives, not just once.

### Confusing volume and confidence with accuracy
Sources who speak often, in detail, and with certainty can dominate a reporter's attention while being wrong. Certainty is a performance, not a credential. Weigh the basis of the claims, not the force of the delivery.

### Over-relying on a single reliable source
Even a genuinely reliable source can be wrong, misinformed, or selectively disclosing on a particular point. Reliability lowers the burden of corroboration; it does not eliminate the need for it. High-stakes claims still require independent support.

### Ignoring the source's own sources
A source relaying what they heard from someone else is only as reliable as that upstream source. Trace provenance. "I heard it from an official" makes the official, not your source, the real source, and you may know nothing about them.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I assessed this source across multiple dimensions, knowledge, track record, expertise, motivation, independence, and consistency, rather than a single gut judgment?
- [ ] Am I relying on the source closest to the actual event or document, based on direct access rather than rank or proximity?
- [ ] Do I have a track record for this source, and if they are new, am I treating them as unproven until corroborated?
- [ ] Did I explicitly probe how the source knows what they claim, distinguishing firsthand from secondhand?
- [ ] Have I understood the source's motivation and weighed it, without letting motive either validate or discredit the account?
- [ ] Did I test the account for consistency and verifiable specificity?
- [ ] Am I assessing credibility for this specific topic, rather than generalizing from the source's reliability in another domain?
- [ ] If the source is anonymous, have I applied heightened scrutiny and required stronger internal verification?
- [ ] Have I updated my assessment based on how this source's past information held up, rather than treating reliability as fixed?
- [ ] Before relying on this source, have I traced where they got their information, rather than stopping at their say-so?
