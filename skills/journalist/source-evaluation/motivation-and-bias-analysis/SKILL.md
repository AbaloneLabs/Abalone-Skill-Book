# Motivation And Bias Analysis

Sources do not hand over information in a vacuum. Every disclosure is shaped by what the source hopes to achieve, what they want to protect, and how they want the story framed. Understanding motivation and bias is not cynicism; it is the minimum competence required to avoid becoming a conduit for someone else's agenda. The harm this skill prevents is the publication of stories that are technically sourced but functionally manipulated, where a reporter, failing to ask why this source is telling me this and why now, amplifies a selective disclosure designed to damage a rival, advance a policy, or settle a score. Motivation analysis is the discipline of treating every source's account as an action taken for reasons, and investigating those reasons as part of the reporting.

## Core Rules

### Ask "why is this person telling me this" for every disclosure
Every piece of information from a source is also an act with a purpose. Make the question explicit: what does this source gain by my publishing this, in this framing, at this time? The answer does not determine whether the information is true, but it determines how you should test and contextualize it.

### Map the source's stake in the outcome
Identify what the source stands to gain or lose. Are they a rival of the subject, a former employee with a grievance, an official advancing a factional fight, a litigant building a public record, an advocate seeking a policy change? Stake does not invalidate information, but it tells you where to look for selectivity, exaggeration, and omission.

### Detect bias through omission and emphasis
Bias is often clearer in what a source does not say than in what they do. Does the account omit exculpatory context, favorable facts, or the subject's side? Does it emphasize the damaging and minimize the mitigating? Compare the source's framing against the full record; the gaps reveal the agenda.

### Consider whether you are the target, not the reporter
Sophisticated actors leak to journalists as a tactic, choosing the outlet, the timing, and the framing to achieve a goal unrelated to journalism's purposes. Ask whether the disclosure is arriving because the source wants the public informed, or because they want a specific outcome that publication will produce. Being used is not always avoidable, but being used unknowingly is.

### Triangulate against sources with opposing stakes
The strongest defense against a single source's bias is to seek sources with opposing motivations. If a critic and a defender, a plaintiff and a defendant, a rival and an ally, converge on the same fact, that convergence is powerful. If only one side is talking, you have a partial account regardless of how detailed it seems.

### Separate the information from the framing
A source may provide accurate facts wrapped in a tendentious interpretation. Extract the checkable facts and verify them independently; subject the interpretation to scrutiny rather than adopting it. The facts may be true while the conclusion the source wants you to draw is not.

### Watch for coordinated and laundered disclosure
Information may reach you through cutouts, intermediaries, or apparently independent sources that trace back to a single origin. A advocacy group, a law firm, or a political operation may feed the same narrative to multiple outlets through different-looking channels. Trace provenance to detect laundering and avoid presenting a coordinated campaign as organic multiplicity.

### Reassess motivation when the story turns
A source's motivation can shift as a story develops, especially if publication begins to harm them or benefit a rival they did not anticipate. Revisit your motivation analysis at each stage. A source helpful at the outset may become a liability or a manipulator as their stake in the outcome clarifies.

## Common Traps

### Accepting the helpful source's framing wholesale
A source who gives you everything you need, documents, access, a clear narrative, is the most dangerous, because gratitude and momentum make you suspend scrutiny. The most complete, convenient package is often the most engineered. Interrogate the gift.

### Assuming a sympathetic source has no agenda
Sources who share your values or appear to act from conscience still have objectives that shape their account. Sympathy is not neutrality. Apply motivation analysis regardless of whether you like or agree with the source.

### Failing to ask why now
Timing is a tell. A disclosure arriving just before a vote, a deal, a trial, or an election often serves a tactical purpose. The timing of a leak is part of its meaning and should be examined, not treated as coincidence.

### Treating omission as innocence
When a source's account conveniently lacks the facts that would complicate it, do not assume the omissions are accidental. Probe for what is missing and seek it elsewhere. Selective disclosure is a form of bias as real as outright falsehood.

### Letting a single motivated source define the story
When one source drives the narrative and others merely react, the story reflects that source's interests. Even if the facts check out, the selection and emphasis may be entirely the source's. Diversify the voices shaping the story.

### Mistaking coordinated multiplicity for independence
Three sources all echoing a narrative that originated with one operation is still one source. Count provenance, not mentions. Verify that corroborating sources are genuinely independent of the originating interest.

### Forgetting your own bias in evaluating theirs
Reporters have motivations too: a thesis to confirm, a competitor to beat, a source to protect. Your own stake colors how you weigh a source's. Audit your own incentives as part of assessing the source's.

## Self-Check

- [ ] For each significant disclosure, have I explicitly asked why this source is telling me this, and why now?
- [ ] Have I mapped what the source stands to gain or lose from publication, in this framing, at this time?
- [ ] Did I examine what the source omitted or de-emphasized, and seek the missing context elsewhere?
- [ ] Have I considered whether I am the target of a tactical disclosure rather than a neutral recipient?
- [ ] Did I seek sources with opposing stakes to triangulate, rather than relying on voices that all share an interest?
- [ ] Have I separated the checkable facts from the source's interpretation, and verified each independently?
- [ ] Did I trace provenance to detect whether apparently independent sources trace back to a single coordinated origin?
- [ ] Have I reassessed the source's motivation as the story developed and their stake clarified?
- [ ] Am I applying motivation analysis to sympathetic and convenient sources, not only to hostile ones?
- [ ] Have I audited my own incentives and how they might be coloring my evaluation of the source's?
