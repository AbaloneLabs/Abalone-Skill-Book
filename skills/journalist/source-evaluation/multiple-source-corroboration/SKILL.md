# Multiple Source Corroboration

Corroboration, the confirmation of a claim by additional evidence, is the central mechanism by which journalism moves from assertion to established fact. But corroboration is widely misunderstood: two sources that share an origin are not two sources, and three sources repeating a wire report prove nothing about the underlying claim. The harm this skill prevents is the false confidence of apparent multiplicity, where a reporter believes a claim is well-sourced because several voices say it, when in fact they all derive from one unverified origin. Genuine corroboration requires independence, and building it is a disciplined practice of tracing provenance, seeking diverse source types, and calibrating the number and strength of sources to the stakes of the claim.

## Core Rules

### Calibrate corroboration to the stakes of the claim
Not every claim requires multiple sources. Routine, low-stakes, and easily verifiable facts may rest on a single reliable source or a primary document. But claims that accuse, expose, assign blame, or carry high potential harm demand multiple, genuinely independent sources. Set the corroboration standard by the consequence of error, not by a uniform rule.

### Verify independence, not just headcount
The defining requirement is that corroborating sources did not obtain their information from each other or from a common origin. Ask each source how they know, and trace the chain backward. Two officials who both read the same internal memo, or three outlets all citing one wire service, are one source, not three. Independence is what makes corroboration meaningful.

### Seek diverse source types, not just more sources of the same type
The strongest corroboration combines different kinds of evidence: a human source, a document, a dataset, an official record, a physical artifact. Multiple human sources of the same type, all insiders, are weaker than one insider plus one document plus one dataset. Diversify the evidentiary types so that the weakness of one kind is covered by another.

### Distinguish confirmation of the fact from confirmation of the framing
Sources may agree on what happened while disagreeing on why it matters or what it means. Corroborate the underlying factual claim specifically, and do not let agreement on the fact smuggle in agreement on an interpretation that the sources did not actually confirm. "Multiple sources confirm the meeting occurred" does not mean multiple sources confirm the characterization of the meeting as a conspiracy.

### Corroborate the load-bearing claims most rigorously
In any story, a few claims carry the weight of the conclusion. Identify them and apply the strictest corroboration standard there. Peripheral detail can rest on lighter sourcing; the claims without which the story collapses cannot.

### Treat single-source claims as provisional and label them honestly
Sometimes only one source is available, and the claim may still be newsworthy. If you publish on a single source, be honest about it with readers and with yourself, apply maximum scrutiny to that source's credibility and basis of knowledge, and seek corroboration actively even after publication. Single-sourcing is sometimes necessary; presenting single-sourced material as if it were corroborated is not.

### Beware circular corroboration across outlets
In a fast-moving story, Outlet A reports a claim, Outlet B cites Outlet A, and Outlet C cites both; the claim now appears to have triple sourcing but rests on one original report. When you cite other outlets as corroboration, trace to the original sourcing. Do not let the echo chamber of the press create an illusion of confirmation.

### Document the corroboration structure
For each significant claim, record the sources, their independence from each other, their basis of knowledge, and the source types involved. This map lets editors assess whether the corroboration is genuine and lets you defend the story if challenged. A claim defended by "we had multiple sources" is indefensible if you cannot show the sources were independent.

## Common Traps

### Counting heads instead of tracing provenance
"Three sources told us" is meaningless if all three got the information from the same place. The discipline is to trace each source back to origin, not to tally mentions. Provenance, not headcount, is the measure of corroboration.

### Assuming officials of the same agency are independent
Two officials from the same department, especially if briefed from the same materials, often share a common origin for their information. Institutional co-location is not independence. Probe whether they learned the fact separately.

### Corroborating with sources who share a motive
Sources with the same grievance or interest may converge on an account because they share an agenda, not because they independently observed the same facts. Seek corroboration from sources whose interests differ; agreement across opposing interests is far stronger.

### Letting a document corroborate what it does not actually address
A document that confirms one element of a claim is often treated as corroborating the whole. A contract confirming a relationship does not corroborate an allegation about how that relationship was used. Match the corroboration precisely to the claim being made.

### Over-weighting quantity of weak sources
Five weak sources do not add up to one strong source. A pile of secondhand, motivated, or vague accounts is weaker than a single primary document. Weight sources by quality and directness, not just by number.

### Failing to corroborate because the first source was trusted
Once a trusted source makes a claim, reporters often stop seeking corroboration. But trusted sources err too, and high-stakes claims need independent support regardless of the first source's reliability. Reliability reduces suspicion; it does not replace corroboration.

### Presenting collaborative sourcing as independent
When sources have spoken to each other before speaking to you, their accounts may be coordinated rather than independent. Ask whether your sources have discussed the matter among themselves, and discount apparent corroboration that may have been manufactured by coordination.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the corroboration standard calibrated to the stakes, with the strictest multi-source requirement applied to high-harm claims?
- [ ] For each corroborating source, have I verified genuine independence by tracing how each knows, back to origin?
- [ ] Have I diversified source types, combining human sources with documents, data, and records, rather than relying on many sources of one kind?
- [ ] Am I corroborating the specific factual claim, and not letting agreement on the fact smuggle in an uncorroborated interpretation?
- [ ] Have I identified the load-bearing claims and corroborated them to the highest standard?
- [ ] If publishing a single-source claim, am I honest about it and applying maximum scrutiny and ongoing corroboration efforts?
- [ ] When citing other outlets as support, have I traced back to the original sourcing rather than counting secondary reports?
- [ ] Have I checked whether my sources spoke to each other, which would undermine their independence?
- [ ] Is the corroboration structure documented so that editors and future reviewers can assess its genuineness?
- [ ] Before publication, can I defend each significant claim with sources whose independence and basis of knowledge I can specifically articulate?
