# Characterization And Profile Craft

A profile is an act of power: the journalist chooses which version of a real person to present to the public, and that version may follow the subject for years. The craft of characterization is the discipline of rendering a subject with dimension, complexity, and fairness, neither flattening them into a hero nor reducing them to a villain, and never letting access, sympathy, or animus distort the portrait. The harm this skill prevents is the profile that misrepresents a life: the hagiography that flatters a powerful subject in exchange for access, the hatchet job that confirms a pre-chosen narrative by cherry-picking, the reductive type, the genius, the villain, the eccentric, that strips a real person of their complexity, and the invasion of private life that serves no public purpose. Strong profile craft treats the subject as a human being whose full reality deserves honest, contextualized depiction.

Use this skill when writing any profile, character-driven feature, or depiction of an identifiable person. Consult editors on profile framing, especially where the depiction could affect the subject's reputation, safety, or legal standing.

## Core Rules

### Render the subject with dimension, not type
Real people are contradictory, evolving, and contextual. A profile that reduces a subject to a single trait, the visionary, the fraud, the rebel, the saint, ignores the complexity that makes them human and misleads readers about who they actually are. Seek the contradictions, the context, the history, and the range of perspectives, including the subject's own self-understanding and the views of critics, allies, family, and adversaries. Dimension is not the absence of judgment; it is judgment grounded in the full person rather than a convenient slice.

### Resist both hagiography and hatchet job
Two opposite failures distort profiles. The hagiography flatters the subject, often in exchange for access, omitting criticism, failure, and contradiction to produce a flattering portrait. The hatchet job confirms a pre-chosen negative narrative by cherry-picking the worst material and omitting context that would complicate it. Both fail the reader and the subject. Approach the subject with genuine curiosity rather than a thesis to confirm, and let the evidence shape the portrait rather than the portrait selecting the evidence.

### Balance access and independence
Access, the subject's willingness to be interviewed, observed, and introduced to their world, is valuable, but it creates pressure toward favorable coverage. The subject grants access expecting something in return, and the journalist's ongoing relationship can soften scrutiny. Maintain independence: the access is a means to reporting, not a quid pro quo for a favorable portrait. Be willing to include material the subject would prefer omitted, and do not let the promise of future access shape the current story. Disclose the conditions of access where they are relevant.

### Distinguish public role from private life
A profile of a public figure, whose conduct affects the public, justifies more scrutiny of relevant behavior than a profile of a private person. But even public figures have private lives, and not every private detail is fair game. Include private material only when it bears on the public role, reveals character relevant to the subject's public impact, or serves a specific public interest. Gratuitous exposure of family, health, sexuality, or past personal matters, unrelated to the public role, fails the threshold and causes harm without justification.

### Use the subject's own words, in context
The subject's voice, through direct quotation, is essential to a fair profile. Let readers hear how the subject speaks, thinks, and frames themselves. But quotes must be in context: do not select fragments that distort the whole statement, omit qualifying context, or stitch together non-adjacent words. A profile that makes a subject look foolish or extreme through selective quotation has misrepresented them, even if each quote is technically accurate.

### Include perspectives beyond the subject
A profile built only on the subject's self-presentation is incomplete and vulnerable to manipulation. Include the perspectives of others: allies, critics, employees, family members, adversaries, and independent observers. These external perspectives test the subject's self-narrative, reveal patterns the subject may not acknowledge, and give readers a fuller picture. Attribute external perspectives clearly and weigh them against the subject's account, recognizing that critics and allies each have their own interests.

### Show character through action and detail
Character is revealed through what people do, not only what they say about themselves. Show the subject in action: how they treat staff, how they handle pressure, how they make decisions, how they interact with family and strangers. Specific, observed detail, verified and purposeful, reveals character more powerfully than the writer's assertion. "He was demanding" tells; a scene of him reworking a subordinate's draft at midnight shows. Prefer showing to telling, grounded in real observation.

### Acknowledge the limits of the portrait
No profile captures a whole person. Be honest about what the portrait can and cannot show: the time spent, the access granted, the perspectives included and omitted, the dimensions left unexplored. A note acknowledging limits, or a framing that signals the portrait is partial, respects the reader and the subject. Pretending to omniscience overstates what reporting can establish about a human life.

## Common Traps

### The access-for-flattery exchange
Granting favorable coverage in exchange for, or in hope of, continued access produces hagiography. The trap is that the access feels like a relationship, but it is a transaction that compromises the portrait.

### The pre-chosen narrative
Deciding the subject is a hero or villain before reporting, then selecting material to confirm it, produces distortion. The trap is that the narrative is satisfying, but it is not what the full evidence shows.

### Reduction to type
Casting the subject as a familiar archetype, the eccentric genius, the ruthless operator, flattens a real person into a cliché. The trap is that types are easy and memorable, but they misrepresent.

### Selective quotation
Choosing fragments that make the subject look better or worse than the full statement warrants misrepresents their meaning. The trap is that the sharper fragment reads better, but it breaches context.

### Gratuitous private exposure
Including personal, family, health, or past details unrelated to the public role exposes the subject without public justification. The trap is that private material feels revealing, but it often serves curiosity not public value.

### Single-source self-presentation
Building the profile only on the subject's account leaves it untested and vulnerable to spin. The trap is that the subject's narrative is coherent and accessible, but external perspectives would complicate it.

### Overclaiming omniscience
Presenting the portrait as definitive, without acknowledging the limits of time, access, and perspective, overstates what reporting can establish. The trap is that confidence reads as authority, but it overclaims.

## Self-Check

- Does the portrait render the subject with dimension and complexity, rather than reducing them to a type or single trait?
- Have you resisted both hagiography and hatchet job, approaching the subject with curiosity rather than a thesis to confirm?
- Is independence maintained despite access, with material the subject would prefer omitted included where relevant?
- Is private material included only when it bears on the public role or serves a specific public interest, not merely for revelation?
- Are the subject's own words presented in context, free of selective quotation or stitching that distorts meaning?
- Does the profile include external perspectives, allies critics and observers, that test the subject's self-narrative?
- Is character shown through observed action and detail rather than only told through assertion?
- Are the limits of the portrait acknowledged, and have editors reviewed the framing where the depiction could affect the subject's reputation, safety, or legal standing?
