# Document Authentication

A document can be the most powerful evidence in a story, or its most dangerous liability. An authentic record can prove what testimony never could; a forgery, published as genuine, can destroy an outlet's credibility and inflict grave harm on the falsely accused. The harm this skill prevents is the publication of fabricated, altered, or misattributed documents as authentic, a failure that is simultaneously an ethical violation, a legal exposure, and a propaganda victory for whoever created the forgery. Document authentication is the disciplined practice of establishing, before relying on a record, that it is what it claims to be, that it has not been altered, and that it actually supports the use to which the story puts it. Journalists are not forensic examiners, but they must know how to triage authenticity and when to escalate.

## Core Rules

### Establish provenance before assessing content
Before evaluating what a document says, establish where it came from. Who created it, when, for what purpose, and how did it reach you? A document with a clear, verifiable chain of custody is far more trustworthy than one that appeared anonymously with no history. Gaps and ambiguities in provenance are warnings, not necessarily disqualifying, but demanding deeper authentication.

### Match authentication rigor to the document's role and the stakes
A routine public record used for background needs light verification. A document that is the centerpiece of an accusation, or that comes from an anonymous source, demands the fullest authentication you can muster. Calibrate effort to how much the story depends on the document and to the harm that would follow if it were forged.

### Verify the obvious markers, but do not stop there
Letterheads, signatures, seals, logos, formatting, and official reference numbers can be checked against known genuine examples. But these are also the easiest elements to copy. Their presence is consistent with authenticity, not proof of it. Treat surface markers as a first screen, then go deeper.

### Check internal consistency and external corroboration
Does the document contradict itself, use anachronistic language, reference events out of chronological order, or employ formatting inconsistent with its claimed date? Does its content align with independent records, known facts, and other documents? Internal anomalies and external mismatches are strong indicators of fabrication or alteration.

### Authenticate digital documents at the file level
For digital records, examine metadata, file format, creation and modification timestamps, software signatures, and embedded revisions. Compare hashes against any known original. Be aware that metadata can be stripped or spoofed and that platform processing alters files. Inconsistency between declared and actual file properties is a red flag.

### Seek confirmation from the originating institution
Where possible, ask the institution that allegedly created the document to confirm or deny its authenticity. They may have the original, can verify signatures, or can identify discrepancies a journalist would miss. Present the document and request verification, while recognizing the institution may have reasons to deny authentic records that embarrass them.

### Escalate to qualified forensic experts for high-stakes documents
When a story turns on a document whose authenticity is contested or cannot be established through routine checks, engage a qualified document examiner or forensic lab. Disclose their role and methods. Journalists performing first-pass triage is valuable; journalists claiming forensic conclusions beyond their competence is dangerous.

### Preserve the document unaltered and document the authentication process
Keep the document exactly as received, work on copies, and record every step of your authentication: what you checked, what you found, who you consulted. This record supports the story's defensibility and allows later expert review. An authentication process that was not documented cannot be reconstructed or defended.

## Common Traps

### Trusting surface appearance
A document that looks official, correct letterhead, professional formatting, convincing signature, can still be fabricated. Modern tools make surface-level forgery trivial. Appearance is a starting point, not a conclusion.

### Authenticating the format and assuming the content
Confirming that a letterhead or template is genuine does not confirm that the specific text on this instance is authentic. Alterations can insert false content into a genuine shell. Authenticate the specific document and its specific claims, not just its format.

### Failing to consider partial alteration
A document can be genuine in part and altered in part, a real letter with an inserted paragraph, a true record with a changed figure. Partial alteration is harder to detect than wholesale forgery and is a favored technique. Examine the whole document for inconsistencies, not just its overall appearance.

### Over-relying on a willing source's assurance
The source who provided the document has an interest in you accepting it as genuine. Their assurance of authenticity is not authentication. Verify independently of the provider.

### Ignoring the institution's denial without investigating it
When an institution denies a document's authenticity, investigate the denial rather than dismissing it as self-defense. Sometimes the denial is itself misleading; sometimes it is accurate and the document is forged. Weigh the denial against the evidence and investigate the specific reasons given.

### Publishing before authentication is complete
Deadline pressure leads to publishing documents whose authentication is incomplete, intending to verify later. If the document is later shown to be forged, the harm is done and a correction cannot fully undo it. Complete authentication before publication, or publish with explicit, prominent acknowledgment of the authentication's limits.

### Confusing "we could not prove it is fake" with "we proved it is genuine"
Inability to detect a forgery is not proof of authenticity. If your authentication is inconclusive, say so internally and to readers. Treat an unauthenticated document as a lead, not as established evidence.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I established the document's provenance, including origin, chain of custody, and how it reached me?
- [ ] Is the authentication effort calibrated to the document's role in the story and the stakes of error?
- [ ] Did I check surface markers against known genuine examples, while recognizing their limits?
- [ ] Have I tested internal consistency and sought external corroboration of the document's content?
- [ ] For digital documents, did I examine metadata, file properties, and hashes, accounting for the possibility of spoofing?
- [ ] Did I seek confirmation or denial from the originating institution and investigate the response?
- [ ] For high-stakes or contested documents, did I engage a qualified forensic expert and disclose their role?
- [ ] Is the document preserved unaltered, with the full authentication process documented for later review?
- [ ] Did I examine the document for partial alteration, not only wholesale fabrication?
- [ ] Before publication, can I affirmatively defend the document's authenticity, or have I honestly disclosed where authentication remains inconclusive?
