# Public Records Request

Public records laws, freedom of information statutes at various levels of government, are among the journalist's most powerful tools for extracting what institutions would rather keep hidden. But the laws are full of friction: vague requests produce vague or empty responses, agencies exploit exemptions and delays, and the journalist who does not know how to frame, follow up, and appeal requests will get little of value. The harm this skill prevents is the quiet surrender in which reporters file a perfunctory request, receive a heavily redacted or delayed response, and move on, never obtaining the records that would have supported the story. Effective public records work is a craft of precise drafting, persistent follow-up, strategic appeal, and realistic expectation management.

## Core Rules

### Draft requests with precision and appropriate scope
Vague or overbroad requests invite delay, denial, or massive irrelevant productions. Identify the specific records you want, the custodian likely to hold them, the time period, and the format. Narrow enough to be manageable, broad enough to capture what you need. Cite the specific statutory provisions where useful, and request fee waivers or reductions on journalistic grounds where the law allows.

### Know the specific law and its procedures
Records laws differ by jurisdiction, agency, and record type. Know which statute applies, what it covers and excludes, the statutory deadlines, the fee structure, and the appeal process. Misapplying the law hands the agency grounds for delay or denial. When in doubt, consult the statute, prior successful requests, or a media lawyer.

### Anticipate exemptions and counter them in advance
Agencies commonly invoke exemptions for privacy, law enforcement, deliberative process, trade secrets, or national security. Anticipate which exemptions your request may trigger and argue, in the request or follow-up, why the public interest favors disclosure or why the exemption does not apply. Foreseeing the objection lets you meet it before it becomes a denial.

### Track deadlines and follow up relentlessly
Agencies routinely miss statutory deadlines. Track every deadline, follow up promptly and in writing when they pass, and escalate within the agency. Persistent, documented follow-up is often the difference between getting records and getting stonewalled. Silence is interpreted as the request going away.

### Appeal denials and redactions rather than accepting them
Initial denials are often negotiable or reversible. Use the administrative appeal process, make the public-interest case, and challenge overbroad redactions specifically. Many agencies reverse on appeal; others will not unless pushed. Accepting the first denial forfeits records you may be entitled to.

### Build relationships with records officers and custodians
The people who process requests are not always adversaries. Cultivate professional relationships with records officers, who can clarify what exists, suggest search terms, and expedite within the rules. Hostility and suspicion slow your requests; professionalism and clarity speed them.

### Preserve and organize productions for analysis
Large productions arrive disorganized, sometimes deliberately. Preserve the original production, maintain an index, and use search and document-management tools to find what matters. A records request succeeds only when the relevant material is actually located and used; a dump you never analyze is a failure disguised as success.

### Combine records requests with other reporting
Records are most powerful when triangulated with interviews, documents, and data. Use the production to generate leads, confirm or challenge source accounts, and identify new questions. A records request in isolation yields a document; a records request embedded in reporting yields a story.

## Common Traps

### Filing overly broad requests
"Send me everything about X" produces either nothing, on grounds of burden, or an unmanageable dump designed to bury the relevant material. Scope requests to what you actually need, refining through follow-ups as you learn what exists.

### Accepting the first denial or delay
Initial responses often test whether the requester will persist. Many denials are reversed on appeal, and many delays resolve with pressure. Treating the first response as final surrenders records the law entitles you to.

### Failing to specify format and fee expectations
Without specifying electronic format and addressing fees, you may receive paper productions at high cost, or face unexpected fee bills. State your format preference and request fee waivers or cost limits up front.

### Ignoring the distinction between what exists and what was produced
An agency may produce some responsive records while withholding others under exemption or claiming none exist. Cross-check productions against what sources, other records, or common sense suggest should exist, and investigate gaps and suspicious absences.

### Letting deliberate delay defeat the story
Agencies sometimes stall until the news moment passes or the reporter moves on. Build timelines that account for likely delay, file early, and keep stories alive while requests pend. Do not let delay tactics succeed by default.

### Misreading exemptions as absolute
Many exemptions are discretionary or subject to a public-interest balancing test, not absolute bars. Challenge exemptions that appear overbroad or misapplied, and argue the public interest. Agencies count on requesters not knowing the limits of exemptions.

### Treating the production as the end of the work
Receiving records is the beginning, not the conclusion. The value is in analyzing, contextualizing, and reporting the contents. Productions that sit unexamined represent wasted effort and missed stories.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is my request drafted with precise scope, identifying specific records, custodians, time periods, and format?
- [ ] Do I know the applicable statute, its deadlines, exemptions, fee rules, and appeal process?
- [ ] Have I anticipated likely exemptions and pre-emptively addressed the public-interest case for disclosure?
- [ ] Am I tracking every deadline and following up promptly and in writing when the agency misses them?
- [ ] Will I appeal denials and challenge overbroad redactions rather than accepting the first response?
- [ ] Have I cultivated a professional relationship with the records officers handling my requests?
- [ ] Do I have a system to preserve, index, and analyze large productions so relevant material is actually found and used?
- [ ] Am I combining the records work with interviews, data, and other reporting to build a story, not just collect documents?
- [ ] Have I cross-checked the production against what should exist, investigating suspicious gaps or absences?
- [ ] Have I built realistic timelines that account for likely delay, filing early and keeping the story alive while requests pend?
