# Leaked Document Handling

Leaks are a vital source of accountability journalism, often the only window into what institutions hide. But leaked material arrives with a distinctive cluster of risks: legal exposure for the outlet and the source, authenticity questions, source-protection obligations, and the possibility that the leak itself is a manipulation. The harm this skill prevents spans publishing unlawfully obtained material without assessing legal consequences, exposing a leaker to retaliation through carelessness, amplifying a selective or weaponized disclosure, or failing to verify and contextualize material whose drama overwhelms scrutiny. Handling a leak well is a discipline of verification, protection, legal care, and editorial judgment about what serves the public versus what merely serves the leaker.

## Core Rules

### Verify authenticity before relying on the leak
A leak is a lead, not a verdict. Authenticate the documents using the methods of document authentication, seek corroboration from independent sources, and confirm that the records are genuine and unaltered before building a story on them. The drama and volume of a leak do not substitute for verification; large, well-organized document sets can still contain forgeries or alterations.

### Understand and assess the legal landscape
Leaked material can raise questions of stolen property, trade secrets, classified information, privacy, and confidentiality agreements, and the law varies by jurisdiction. Consult media lawyers early, before publication, to understand the outlet's exposure and any obligations. Do not assume that because the source broke a rule, the journalist is protected; in some contexts, publishing certain leaked material carries real legal risk.

### Protect the source's identity with operational discipline
Assume the institution will investigate the leak. Use secure communication, avoid creating linkable metadata, meet in person where possible, and keep identifying details out of notes that could be subpoenaed. Scrub the documents themselves for metadata or content that could identify the source before any publication or sharing. Plan protection from the first contact.

### Determine the public interest and weigh it against potential harm
Not every leaked document serves the public. Assess whether the disclosure reveals genuine wrongdoing, informs a significant public debate, or exposes matters of legitimate concern, versus serving private interest, voyeurism, or a factional fight. Weigh that public interest against the harm publication may cause, to individuals' privacy, to ongoing operations, to safety. Publish where the public interest clearly outweighs the harm; withhold or redact where it does not.

### Contextualize rather than cherry-pick
Leaks are often selective by design; the leaker chose what to disclose and what to withhold. Read the documents in full context, seek the missing material, and avoid presenting a curated subset as the complete picture. Report what the documents show and what they may omit, and give the institution a genuine chance to respond and to provide context.

### Decide what to publish and what to withhold deliberately
Even authentic, newsworthy leaks may contain material that should not be published: personal information unrelated to the public interest, details that could endanger individuals, ongoing-law-enforcement or military operations, or genuinely sensitive sources and methods. Make redaction and withholding decisions deliberately, documenting the reasoning, rather than publishing everything by default or withholding everything out of caution.

### Guard against being weaponized
Leaks are sometimes released to manipulate the press: to damage a rival, divert attention, plant a narrative, or influence an outcome. Ask who benefits from the disclosure and the timing, whether the material has been selectively curated, and whether you are advancing an agenda disguised as a scoop. Being used knowingly, with the manipulation disclosed, is sometimes acceptable; being used unknowingly is a failure.

### Maintain the verification and decision record
Document the authentication, the legal consultation, the public-interest weighing, the redaction decisions, and the source-protection measures. This record supports the story's defensibility, enables editorial review, and protects the outlet if the leak is later challenged or if the source is identified and disputes the handling.

## Common Traps

### Publishing leaked material without authentication
The volume and apparent detail of a leak can create a false sense of reliability. Large sets can contain forgeries, and even genuine documents can be misread. Authenticate and verify before relying on the material, regardless of its scale.

### Exposing the source through document metadata or details
Leaked files often contain metadata, tracked changes, or content that identifies the source or the narrow circle with access. Publishing or even sharing such files unscrubbed can unmask the leaker. Audit documents for identifying traces before any dissemination.

### Letting the leaker's framing dictate the story
The source who leaked the material often provides an interpretation that serves their interest. Adopting that framing wholesale makes the journalist a vehicle for the leaker's agenda. Independently verify and contextualize, and report the leaker's perspective as one perspective, not as established fact.

### Publishing everything without redaction review
Treating a leak as an all-or-nothing dump leads to publishing material that invades privacy, endangers individuals, or serves no public interest, creating harm and legal exposure without journalistic justification. Review and redact deliberately.

### Withholding everything out of legal fear
The opposite failure is over-caution, declining to publish newsworthy, authentic material out of generalized legal anxiety, and thereby abandoning accountability reporting. Assess the actual legal risk with qualified counsel, weigh it against the public interest, and publish where justified.

### Failing to seek the institution's response
Publishing damaging leaked material without giving the institution a genuine chance to respond and provide context is both unfair and a recipe for error. The response often reveals defenses, corrections, or additional wrongdoing, and presenting it strengthens the story's fairness.

### Ignoring the timing and the beneficiary
A leak arriving at a politically or commercially convenient moment may be a tactical disclosure. Examine the timing and who benefits as part of the reporting, not as a reason to suppress, but as context the audience deserves.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I authenticated the leaked documents and sought independent corroboration before relying on them?
- [ ] Did I consult media lawyers early to understand the legal landscape before publication?
- [ ] Am I protecting the source's identity with operational discipline, including scrubbing documents for identifying metadata?
- [ ] Have I assessed the public interest and weighed it against the potential harm of publication?
- [ ] Did I read the documents in full context and seek the material the leak may omit, rather than presenting a curated subset as complete?
- [ ] Have I made deliberate, documented decisions about what to publish, what to redact, and what to withhold?
- [ ] Did I consider who benefits from the disclosure and the timing, and guard against being weaponized unknowingly?
- [ ] Did I give the institution a genuine chance to respond and provide context before publication?
- [ ] Have I contextualized the material independently rather than adopting the leaker's framing?
- [ ] Is the full handling record, authentication, legal review, public-interest weighing, and redaction decisions, documented for defensibility?
