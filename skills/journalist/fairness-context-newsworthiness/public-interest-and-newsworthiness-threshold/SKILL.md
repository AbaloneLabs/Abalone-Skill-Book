# Public Interest And Newsworthiness Threshold

Every story idea passes through a gate: should this be published, and if so, why? The newsworthiness threshold is the judgment call that separates journalism that serves the public from exposure that merely satisfies curiosity. The harm this skill prevents is twofold. On one side, publishing stories that lack public value exposes private people to scrutiny, reputational damage, and harassment for no defensible reason. On the other side, suppressing stories that do have public value, out of timidity, deference to power, or fear of backlash, allows harm, corruption, and failure to continue unchecked. The threshold is not a feeling; it is a reasoned justification that can survive scrutiny by editors, subjects, and readers.

Use this skill before committing to publish, before assigning scarce reporting time, and especially when the subject involves private individuals, leaked personal material, past mistakes, or emotionally charged material. Journalism is a high-stakes domain: get the threshold wrong and you can cause real harm or enable it. Consult editors and, where legal exposure is plausible, legal counsel before publishing borderline material.

## Core Rules

### State the public-interest reason explicitly
Before publishing, write down the public-interest justification in one sentence. "The public needs to know this because..." If the sentence only completes with "because people are interested" or "because it will get attention," the threshold has not been met. Public interest means the information helps people understand power, hold institutions accountable, protect themselves from risk, make decisions, or participate in civic life. Novelty, emotional intensity, and virality are not public interest, even though they often coincide with it.

### Distinguish public figures from private individuals
The threshold for publishing damaging information about a powerful public official is lower than for a private citizen, because the official's conduct affects the public and they chose a public role. A private individual who is dragged into the news through misfortune, accident, crime, or viral attention deserves a far stronger justification for exposure. The fact that someone is already being talked about online does not lower the ethical threshold for a newsroom to amplify them. When in doubt, default to protecting private people.

### Match the disclosure to the public value
Ask whether the specific detail being published is necessary to serve the public interest, or whether the same story could be told with less exposure. A person's full name, address, employer, photograph, medical condition, immigration status, sexual history, or family details should each be justified individually. Often the public interest is served by the pattern or the system, not by naming every individual caught in it. Redaction is not weakness; it is judgment.

### Weigh harm against value, and document the reasoning
For any story with potential harm, conduct an explicit harm-value assessment. List who could be harmed and how: reputational, physical, psychological, legal, financial, or safety harm. List the public value. Decide whether the value justifies the harm, and record why. This record protects the newsroom later and forces an honest reckoning rather than a post-hoc rationalization. Harm to vulnerable people, minors, victims, and sources in repressive contexts carries special weight.

### Check whether the story is timely and durable
Newsworthiness includes a time dimension. Information that mattered years ago may no longer serve the public, especially if it involves a private person's past mistake with no continuing relevance. Conversely, old information may become newly newsworthy if it contradicts a current public claim, reveals a pattern, or bears on a present decision. Ask: why now, and would this still matter in a month?

### Resist manufactured and ginned-up stories
Some stories are newsworthy only because a faction, a campaign, a platform algorithm, or a coordinated effort made them loud. Cover the phenomenon of the amplification if it matters, but do not treat the loudness itself as proof that the underlying claim deserves exposure. A story that exists only because someone wanted it to be a story may still be a story about the manipulation, but it is rarely a story about the underlying private matter.

### Consider the alternative of holding or narrowing
The choice is not only publish-or-kill. You can hold for more reporting, publish a narrower version, remove identifying details, delay until relevance is clearer, or publish with additional context that reduces harm. The threshold decision includes choosing the form and scope of publication, not just whether to publish at all.

## Common Traps

### Confusing public curiosity with public interest
Audience fascination with a private person's misfortune, leaked images, or personal drama generates traffic but not public value. Treating clicks as a proxy for newsworthiness erodes the distinction between journalism and gossip, and it harms people who never sought public attention. The trap is seductive because the audience genuinely wants the content; wanting is not the same as needing.

### Letting the viral cycle set the agenda
When a story is already everywhere, newsrooms feel pressure to weigh in. But "everyone is talking about it" is a description of attention, not a justification for amplification. Publishing to avoid being left out often means publishing without an independent public-interest reason, and it rewards the actors who manufactured the attention.

### Applying the threshold only to damaging stories
Newsworthiness is also about resource allocation. A flattering, soft, or promotional story still needs a public-interest reason; otherwise the newsroom is providing free public relations. Sponsored-feeling coverage of a powerful institution, published without scrutiny, fails the threshold in a different direction.

### Punishing past behavior without continuing relevance
Digging up a private person's old arrest, old post, or old relationship, with no connection to their current public role or any ongoing harm, is exposure dressed as accountability. The trap is that the information is "true" and "documented," which feels journalistic, but truth without present public value is not enough.

### Treating the subject's willingness as sufficient
A source who consents to be named does not by itself make publication ethical or newsworthy. Vulnerable people may consent out of desperation, misunderstanding, pressure, or an inability to foresee consequences. The newsroom still owes an independent threshold judgment, and sometimes must protect people from their own consent.

## Self-Check

- Can you complete the sentence "The public needs to know this because..." with a reason that goes beyond interest, novelty, or traffic?
- Have you distinguished public figures from private individuals, and applied a stricter exposure threshold to private people?
- Is every identifying or sensitive detail justified individually, with less intrusive alternatives considered?
- Have you conducted and documented a harm-value assessment, including special weight to vulnerable people, minors, victims, and at-risk sources?
- Is the timing defensible: why now, and will it still matter later, or is this merely riding a moment?
- Have you resisted publishing solely because the story is already viral, and distinguished covering the amplification from amplifying the underlying private matter?
- Have you considered holding, narrowing, delaying, or adding context rather than a binary publish-or-kill decision?
- For high-risk or borderline cases, has an editor reviewed the threshold decision, and where legal exposure exists, has legal counsel been consulted before publication?
