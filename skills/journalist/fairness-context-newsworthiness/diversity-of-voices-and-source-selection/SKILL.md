# Diversity Of Voices And Source Selection

Whom a journalist chooses to quote, interview, depict, and elevate is not a neutral act. Source selection determines whose knowledge frames a story, who is granted authority to interpret events, and who remains voiceless while others speak about them. The harm this skill prevents is the systematic reproduction of narrow perspectives: stories about communities told entirely through officials and experts, affected people reduced to emotional color, elite voices treated as default authorities, and entire populations absent from coverage that decides their fates. Diversity of voices is not tokenism or quota-filling; it is the journalistic discipline of seeking the people who actually know the story because they live it, hold power over it, or bear its consequences.

Use this skill when building a source list, deciding whom to interview, choosing expert versus lived-experience voices, and reviewing a draft to see who is present and who is missing. Where coverage concerns marginalized or vulnerable communities, consult editors and, where appropriate, community liaisons, and recognize that misrepresentation can cause lasting harm.

## Core Rules

### Map the full set of stakeholders before sourcing
Before reaching out, list everyone with a stake in the story: those with power, those affected by the exercise of power, experts, officials, critics, and people inside the affected community who hold different views. Then ask who is missing from that initial list. The first names that come to mind are usually the most accessible and powerful, not necessarily the most relevant. Deliberately expand the map to include people who are harder to reach because of language, work hours, distrust of media, disability, immigration status, incarceration, poverty, or fear.

### Treat affected people as knowledgeable, not just as victims
People experiencing an issue are sources of expertise about how that issue actually works, not merely providers of emotional testimony. A tenant knows how a landlord evades repairs; a patient knows how a clinic actually functions; a worker knows the real conditions on the line. Quote affected people for their analysis and observation, not only for their suffering. When you only ask them how they feel and ask officials for the facts, you reproduce a hierarchy that strips the affected of authority over their own lives.

### Diversify the types of authority you cite
Authority comes in many forms: official position, academic expertise, professional experience, lived experience, community leadership, and direct observation. A story that cites only credentialed experts and officials presents a narrow and often skewed picture, because those groups have their own blind spots and incentives. Balance credentialed authority with community knowledge, and question whose credentials are being treated as default. An "expert" quoted in every story may reflect convenience more than genuine relevance.

### Avoid the single-representative problem
No one person can speak for a community. Quoting one member of a group and treating their view as the community's view flattens internal diversity and can impose a false consensus or a false conflict. Where a community is central to the story, include multiple voices reflecting genuine disagreement, variation, and complexity within the group. State explicitly when a source speaks only for themselves.

### Audit the draft for presence and framing
Before finishing, review the draft and tally who appears, in what role, and with what kind of contribution. Are officials and experts given analytical authority while affected people provide only emotion or anecdote? Are certain groups present only in stories about problems, never in stories about achievement or ordinary life? Are identities mentioned gratuitously for some groups but not others? Are women, people of color, disabled people, LGBTQ people, immigrants, low-income people, and rural communities appearing across all coverage or only in narrow contexts? Patterns across coverage matter as much as any single story.

### Build relationships before you need them
Diverse sourcing fails when newsrooms only approach communities during crisis, crime, or controversy. Sources built on transactional, extractive contact breed distrust and produce shallow access. Invest in ongoing relationships, beat presence in under-covered communities, and reciprocal engagement so that when a story breaks, you already know whom to call and they are willing to talk. Distrust of media in some communities is often earned; acknowledge it and earn trust back through consistent, fair coverage.

### Match the source to the question being asked
Different questions require different sources. For what happened, you need witnesses and records. For why it happened, you need analysts, insiders, and context. For what it feels like and what it costs, you need those affected. For what should change, you need a range of views including those most affected. Mismatching source to question, asking an official how a community feels, or asking a community member to explain institutional mechanics they cannot see, produces weak sourcing through no fault of the source.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to the rolodex of familiar voices
The sources who answer quickly, speak fluently, and fit deadlines are not necessarily the best sources. Convenience sourcing produces repetitive, elite-heavy coverage. The trap is that it feels efficient and professional while quietly excluding the people closest to the story.

### Tokenism as diversity
Including one member of an underrepresented group to appear inclusive, while centering the story on others' interpretations, is not diversity. Token sources sense when they are being used as decoration, and the resulting quote is often guarded or unrepresentative. The trap produces the appearance of inclusion without the substance.

### Confusing official access with knowledge
Officials often have access to information and the power to act, but they also have incentives to frame events favorably to their institution. Treating official statements as the factual core and community accounts as opinion inverts the actual knowledge distribution in many stories. The trap is that officials are easy to quote on deadline.

### Extractive parachute journalism
Dropping into a community only for a crisis story, taking quotes, and leaving breeds resentment and yields shallow understanding. The trap is most acute for national outlets covering local or marginalized communities without sustained presence or local collaborators.

### Assuming homogeneity within communities
Treating a community as a monolith with one interest and one view erases internal debate, class differences, generational divides, and dissent. The trap flattens real complexity and can misrepresent where a community actually stands.

### Overcorrecting into false representation
In the name of diversity, do not invent consensus, suppress legitimate internal disagreement, or present a source as representing a community when they do not. Forced neatness is its own distortion; honest diversity includes conflict and contradiction within groups.

## Self-Check

- Have you mapped all stakeholders, including hard-to-reach and less powerful ones, before finalizing the source list?
- Are affected people quoted for their knowledge and analysis, not only for emotion or anecdote?
- Does the story balance credentialed authority with lived experience, and question whose credentials are treated as default?
- Have you avoided treating any single source as speaking for an entire community, and included internal diversity where relevant?
- Have you audited the draft for who appears, in what role, and checked for gratuitous identity mentions or narrow-context-only representation?
- Are your sourcing relationships built over time rather than only during crisis, and have you acknowledged and addressed earned distrust?
- Is each source matched to the specific question being asked, rather than mismatched to produce convenient but weak answers?
- For coverage of marginalized or vulnerable communities, has an editor reviewed representation choices, and where appropriate have community liaisons been consulted to reduce the risk of lasting harm from misrepresentation?
