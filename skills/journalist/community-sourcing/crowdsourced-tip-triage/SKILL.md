# Crowdsourced Tip Triage

A tip line, secure drop, or public callout can surface stories no individual reporter would ever find alone: a worker who saw the dump site, a tenant with the leaked memo, a patient who recognized the pattern. But the same channel that delivers genuine leads also delivers fabrications, agenda-driven dumps, mistaken observations, harassment, and noise. The judgment problem is that triage under volume rewards the loud, the confident, and the sensational, while the most valuable tips are often hesitant, partial, and easy to overlook. Without disciplined triage, a newsroom either chases false leads into exhaustion or misses the real story buried in the pile.

Use this skill when operating a tip channel, sorting incoming submissions, deciding which tips to pursue, designing a public callout, or verifying material that arrived unsolicited from the public. The main risk is that an agent treats crowdsourced input as either automatically credible, because it came from a real person, or automatically dismissable, because it is unverified, when the right posture is structured skepticism applied uniformly.

## Core Rules

### Triage On Signal, Not Volume Or Confidence

A tip's value is not proportional to its length, urgency, or emotional intensity. The most useful tips are often brief, specific, and verifiable: a date, a location, a document reference, a named official. The least useful are often long, dramatic, and unfalsifiable: sweeping accusations, conspiratorial framing, demands for immediate action. Build triage criteria that surface specificity and verifiability rather than confidence and volume.

Useful triage signals include: does the tip identify specific, checkable facts; does it point to documents, records, or witnesses; does it describe first-hand observation or hearsay; does it match a known pattern or open question in your reporting; does it come with contact information for follow-up; does the submitter understand the risks of disclosure. Weight these signals consistently, regardless of how compelling the tip sounds.

### Acknowledge And Track Every Serious Tip

People who take the risk to submit a tip deserve acknowledgment, even if the tip is not pursued. Build a system that records each serious submission, its triage outcome, and the response given. This protects against tips being lost when staff turn over, lets you revisit tips as reporting evolves, and lets you report back to submitters honestly. Silence is corrosive: a submitter who hears nothing assumes their risk was wasted and will not come forward again.

For high-volume channels, an automated acknowledgment with a realistic timeline is better than silence. For sensitive channels, ensure the acknowledgment itself does not expose the submitter, for example by replying on an unencrypted channel to a tip submitted securely.

### Verify Before Pursuing, And Verify Differently By Type

Different tip types require different verification. A tip alleging a specific document should lead to seeking that document. A tip about an event should lead to records, witnesses, and physical or digital evidence. A tip naming individuals should lead to public records, prior reporting, and corroboration. A tip making a broad institutional claim should lead to data and pattern testing, not to a single anecdote.

Apply the same verification discipline to crowdsourced material as to any other source: trace claims to origin, corroborate across independent evidence types, distinguish first-hand from second-hand, and check the submitter's basis of knowledge and motive. See the reporting-and-source-verification skill. A tip is a lead, not a finding.

### Design Callouts That Invite The Right Material

A public callout, "we want to hear from workers who have seen wage theft", shapes what you receive. Vague callouts produce vague tips; specific callouts produce specific ones. Tell people exactly what you are investigating, what kind of information would help, what you can and cannot promise about anonymity and protection, and how to submit securely. A well-designed callout filters out much of the noise at the source and signals seriousness to genuine sources.

Avoid callouts that invite speculation, rumor, or personal attacks, which generate volume without value. Avoid callouts that overstate what you can do for submitters, which sets up betrayal when you cannot deliver protection or outcomes. Test the callout's wording against the risk it poses to potential submitters, especially in workplaces or contexts where being seen responding could invite retaliation.

### Protect Submitters Throughout The Process

A submitter's first contact may be the most exposed moment. They may have used a work device, a real-name account, or a channel that logs identifying detail. From the first response, operate on the assumption that the submitter is at risk until proven otherwise. Use secure channels for follow-up, minimize identifying detail in your records, and never forward a tip or submitter detail to a third party without explicit consent.

For tips that could expose the submitter to legal, employment, or physical risk, involve editors and, where appropriate, counsel or security specialists before pursuing. The decision to publish identifying detail, even inadvertently through recognizable circumstances, should be a deliberate one. See the source-protection skill.

### Watch For Coordinated And Bad-Faith Submissions

Not every tip is organic. Interests threatened by reporting, political operatives, and agenda-driven actors sometimes flood tip lines with material designed to mislead, distract, or smear. A sudden surge of tips making the same dramatic claim, tips that arrive with unusually polished framing, or tips that align suspiciously with an institution's defense can be signs of coordination.

Treat coordinated patterns as a signal to slow down, not speed up. Corroborate independently, check whether multiple tips trace to a common origin, and be wary of material that arrives at a suspiciously convenient moment. This does not mean dismissing organized input, but it means applying extra scrutiny to its provenance and motive.

### Close The Loop Without Exposing Submitters

When a tip leads to a story, credit the contribution in a way that is honest and protective. "After a tip from a reader" may be enough; naming or identifying a submitter who expected anonymity is a betrayal. When a tip does not lead anywhere, tell the submitter honestly where things stand, without implying the tip was worthless or making promises about future coverage.

Closing the loop sustains the channel. Submitters who feel respected, even when their tip was not used, are more likely to come forward again and to recommend the channel to others.

## Common Traps

### Chasing The Dramatic Tip And Missing The Boring One

A vivid, alarming tip demands attention; a dry, specific tip about procurement irregularities looks dull. The dull tip is often the real story. Build triage that elevates verifiable specificity over dramatic framing, and assign someone to read past the headline of each submission.

### Treating Volume As Validation

A claim repeated across many tips can feel confirmed, but if the tips all trace to one viral post, one press release, or one coordinated campaign, the volume is an illusion of corroboration. Trace tips to their origin before treating repetition as evidence.

### Burning A Submitter Through The Follow-Up Channel

Replying to a securely submitted tip via standard email, or calling a phone number that links to a real identity, can expose a submitter who took care to contact you securely. Match the security of your follow-up to the security of the original submission, and confirm the submitter's preferred channel before using it.

### Overpromising Anonymity In The Callout

A callout that promises "total anonymity" may attract tips but cannot be guaranteed; legal process, metadata, and forensic detail can expose submitters. Promise what you can deliver: secure handling, minimized records, editor oversight, and best-effort protection, with honest limits.

### Letting Tips Expire In An Unmanaged Inbox

Tips that sit unread lose value as records are destroyed, witnesses move, and memories fade. A tip channel without active triage and a response timeline betrays the people who took risks to submit. Staff the channel, even modestly, and set a realistic response standard.

### Publishing A Tip Without Independent Verification

The temptation to publish a dramatic, well-documented-looking tip quickly is strong, especially under competitive pressure. A tip is a lead, not a verified fact. Pursue it through reporting before it appears in a story as anything more than an unverified allegation clearly labeled as such.

## Self-Check

Before treating crowdsourced tip triage as sound, verify:

- Triage criteria elevate specific, verifiable facts over dramatic framing, and are applied consistently regardless of a tip's volume or confidence.
- Every serious tip is acknowledged, recorded with its triage outcome, and revisitable as reporting evolves.
- Verification is applied by tip type, with each material claim traced to origin and corroborated across independent evidence types before publication.
- Public callouts are specific about what is needed, honest about what can be promised, and tested for the risk they pose to potential submitters.
- Submitters are protected throughout, with secure follow-up channels, minimized identifying records, and no third-party sharing without consent.
- Coordinated or bad-faith submission patterns are recognized and trigger extra scrutiny rather than accelerated publication.
- The loop is closed with submitters honestly, without exposing those who expected anonymity.
- No tip is published as fact without independent verification, and unverified material is clearly labeled as such or held.
- The tip channel is actively staffed with a realistic response timeline, so tips do not expire unread.
- Tip triage supports editorial judgment; significant pursuit decisions and protection choices should involve a responsible editor, and legal counsel where submissions carry legal or safety exposure.
