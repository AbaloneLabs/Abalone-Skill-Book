# Managing User-Generated Submissions

User-generated submissions, photos, videos, documents, datasets, and first-person accounts sent in by the public, can carry reporting no newsroom could gather alone. But they arrive without the controls a newsroom applies to its own reporting: no known provenance, no verified authorship, no assured consent, no checked safety. The judgment problem is that compelling user material feels ready to publish the moment it arrives, when in fact it requires a distinct chain of verification, permission, and safety checks that, if skipped, can propagate falsehood, violate rights, or endanger the person who captured it. Treating a submission as publishable because it is vivid is the central failure this skill exists to prevent.

Use this skill when a member of the public sends visual material, documents, or first-hand accounts; when integrating citizen journalism into a professional story; when seeking permission to republish; or when assessing whether publishing user content could expose its creator. The main risk is that an agent optimizes for speed and impact, publishing striking material before establishing what it actually shows, who owns it, and what happens to the person who made it.

## Core Rules

### Verify What The Material Actually Shows Before Describing It

A photo or video depicts something, but the caption, context, location, time, and meaning are separate claims that each need verification. A clip from one event can be re-captioned as another; a real image can be framed with a false claim; authentic footage can be edited to alter meaning; a screenshot can misrepresent a source. Establish, separately, the authenticity of the material and the accuracy of the description.

Verification steps include checking the earliest known appearance, examining metadata where available without over-relying on it, comparing landmarks, weather, signage, uniforms, and language to claimed location and time, reverse-searching images, reviewing the contributor's account history for patterns, and seeking independent witnesses or records that place the event where and when claimed. See the document-verification and verification-tools skills. If verification is incomplete but the material is newsworthy, publish only what is established and label the rest as unverified.

### Establish Provenance And Authorship

Before using user material, establish who created it and how it reached you. Material that circulated widely before arriving may have lost its provenance; a viral clip's original creator may not be the person who sent it to you, and republishing without establishing authorship can violate the creator's rights and mislead the audience about source. Ask the submitter whether they captured the material, where, when, and under what circumstances, and trace any chain of forwarding.

Provenance also bears on credibility. Material that arrived through a single anonymous forward with no recoverable origin is weaker than material whose creator you can reach and interview. Where the creator is reachable, their account of what they saw adds reporting value beyond the material itself.

### Obtain Clear Permission To Publish, With Scope

Republishing user-generated content without permission can infringe copyright, violate platform terms, and breach the trust of contributors. Obtain explicit permission to publish, and be clear about scope: where it will appear, in what form, whether it will be edited or cropped, whether it will be syndicated, and whether the creator will be credited or kept anonymous. Confirm the creator actually holds the rights, not everyone who forwards a clip does.

Permission should be informed. A contributor who says yes to publication may not have considered that their footage could expose them to retaliation, legal action, or unwanted attention. Where publication carries risk for the creator, surface that before seeking permission, and offer options such as credit, anonymized use, or non-publication. See the source-protection skill.

### Assess The Contributor's Safety Before And After Publication

The person who captured footage of an atrocity, a crime, a protest, or workplace misconduct may face serious consequences if identified as the source: arrest, violence, dismissal, harassment, or prosecution. Assess this risk before publishing, not after. Consider whether the footage's vantage point, timing, or unique detail could identify the creator to someone inside the institution or context depicted.

Where risk exists, options include withholding the material, publishing with the creator anonymized, blurring identifying detail, delaying publication, or publishing in a form that does not reveal how the material was obtained. Do not treat a contributor's willingness to be credited as proof that being credited is safe; they may not have judged the risk. For high-stakes material, consult editors and, where relevant, organizations that support at-risk contributors.

### Distinguish Authentic Material From Authentic Claims

Authentic footage, a real, undoctored recording of a real event, does not guarantee that the claims attached to it are accurate. A genuine video of a confrontation may be described as police violence when it shows something more ambiguous; a real document may be authentic as a document while the conclusions drawn from it are wrong. Verify the material and the claims independently. A story that says "video shows X" must establish both that the video is genuine and that X is what it shows.

This is especially important with material that arrives with ready-made framing from an interested party. A company, a campaign, or a state actor may distribute genuine material packaged with a misleading interpretation. Treat the framing as a claim to be verified, not as a description to be repeated.

### Integrate Citizen Material Without Surrendering Editorial Judgment

Citizen journalism enriches professional reporting, but integrating it does not transfer editorial responsibility to the contributor. The newsroom remains responsible for accuracy, framing, fairness, context, and the decision to publish. A contributor's preferred caption, emphasis, or interpretation is input, not instruction. Apply the same standards of verification, attribution, and fairness to user-generated material as to staff reporting, and be transparent with the audience about what is known about the material's origin and limits.

Where citizen material contradicts official accounts, present the contradiction honestly rather than resolving it by deferring to whichever source feels more authoritative. State what each shows, what remains in dispute, and what further reporting would resolve.

### Handle Graphic And Sensitive Material With Care

User submissions often include graphic violence, injury, death, abuse, or other distressing content. Even when authentic and newsworthy, such material requires decisions about what to publish, how to warn audiences, whether to crop or blur, and how to frame the surrounding story. Consider the dignity of those depicted, the risk of re-traumatizing victims and audiences, and whether the graphic detail is necessary to the public-interest purpose or serving spectacle. See the fairness-context-and-newsworthiness skill.

Some material should not be published at all, even when authentic and newsworthy, because the harm to those depicted or to the contributor outweighs the public value. That is a legitimate editorial decision, not a failure of nerve.

## Common Traps

### Publishing Striking Material Before Verification

Vivid footage or a dramatic document feels publishable on arrival, but speed without verification propagates falsehood and exposes the newsroom to correction, legal risk, and credibility damage. The more striking the material, the more disciplined the verification must be.

### Assuming Circulation Equals Permission

A clip that has gone viral is not automatically free to republish. Copyright, platform terms, and the creator's wishes still apply, and the original creator may not have consented to wider use. Establish rights before republishing, even for widely shared material.

### Exposing The Creator Through The Material Itself

The content of a submission can identify its creator: a reflection, a distinctive vantage point, a timestamp that matches a known location, a detail only an insider would capture. Review material from the perspective of someone trying to identify the source before publishing.

### Treating The Contributor's Framing As Fact

A contributor's description of their own material is a claim, not a verified account. People genuinely misperceive, misremember, and mischaracterize what they witnessed, and interested parties deliberately attach misleading framing to genuine material. Verify claims independently of the contributor's interpretation.

### Ignoring The Contributor's Post-Publication Exposure

A contributor who consented to publication may face consequences only after the story runs: arrest, retaliation, online harassment, legal threats. Anticipate post-publication risk, stay reachable, and be prepared to support or adjust coverage if the contributor comes under threat.

### Overusing Graphic Material For Engagement

Graphic user content drives attention, but using it for engagement rather than public-interest purpose exploits those depicted and degrades the audience's trust. Apply a clear public-interest test to graphic material, and prefer less graphic alternatives where they convey the necessary information.

## Self-Check

Before treating the handling of user-generated submissions as sound, verify:

- The material's authenticity and the accuracy of its description were verified separately, with origin, location, time, and context established before publication.
- Provenance and authorship were traced, and the creator was identified and reached where possible, rather than relying on a forwarded copy.
- Clear, scoped permission to publish was obtained from the rights holder, with the creator informed of where, how, and in what form the material would appear.
- The contributor's safety was assessed before and after publication, with options such as anonymization, blurring, delay, or non-publication used where risk exists.
- Authentic material was not conflated with authentic claims; the description of what the material shows was verified independently of the material's genuineness.
- Editorial judgment over framing, accuracy, and publication remained with the newsroom, with the contributor's interpretation treated as input rather than instruction.
- Graphic and sensitive material was handled with a public-interest test, audience warnings, and willingness to withhold where harm outweighs value.
- No widely circulated material was republished on the assumption that virality grants permission; rights were established first.
- The material was reviewed for details that could identify the creator to hostile actors before publication.
- Handling of user-generated submissions supports editorial judgment; significant publication and protection decisions should involve a responsible editor, and legal counsel where the material carries rights, safety, or legal exposure.
