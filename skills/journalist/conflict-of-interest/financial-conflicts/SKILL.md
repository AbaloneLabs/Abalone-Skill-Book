# Financial Conflicts

Financial conflicts of interest arise when a journalist's personal financial interests, or those of close family, could be perceived to influence their coverage. The harm this skill prevents is the corruption of independence that financial entanglement creates: coverage tilted to protect or promote holdings, stories killed or softened to avoid financial loss, beats covered by those with a stake in the outcome, and the loss of reader trust when financial conflicts are exposed. Even the appearance of conflict damages credibility, because readers cannot verify that coverage was uninfluenced. Managing financial conflicts is therefore not only about actual bias but about preserving the conditions under which independence can be believed.

## Core Rules

### Disclose holdings related to the beat
Any direct ownership of securities, debt, or material financial interest in companies or institutions on the journalist's beat must be disclosed to editors, and where relevant to readers. The standard is not whether the holding actually influenced coverage but whether a reasonable reader would perceive it could. Disclosure lets readers judge; concealment denies them that ability.

### Recuse from coverage where conflict is material
Where a financial interest is material enough that coverage cannot credibly be seen as independent, recuse: reassign the story, hand off the beat segment, or decline the assignment. Recusal is the stronger remedy when disclosure alone cannot neutralize the conflict. Editors should enforce recusal where reporters underweight the conflict.

### Do not trade around coverage
Buying or selling securities in anticipation of, or in reaction to, coverage the journalist or outlet is about to publish is a severe conflict, and may constitute illegal trading on material nonpublic information. Even the appearance of trading on advance knowledge of a story destroys credibility. Avoid transactions in covered entities around the time of relevant reporting.

### Treat family finances as your own
The financial interests of a spouse, domestic partner, or dependent family members are treated as the journalist's own for conflict purposes. A holding in a spouse's name does not cure a conflict. Disclose and manage family financial entanglements as if they were personal.

### Scrutinize freelance and secondary income
Freelance work, consulting, paid speaking, books, and other secondary income can create conflicts when the payer is a subject of, or has an interest in, the journalist's coverage. Evaluate each arrangement for whether the payer stands to gain or lose from the coverage, and disclose or decline work that creates a conflict. The appearance of bought coverage is as damaging as the reality.

### Manage gifts, travel, and hospitality
Gifts, paid travel, meals, and hospitality from sources or subjects of coverage create conflicts, because they embed the journalist in a relationship of reciprocity with those they cover. Decline things of material value; pay your own way where feasible; disclose and return what cannot be declined. The threshold is not bribery but the subtle erosion of independence that embedded reciprocity produces.

### Apply the appearance standard, not just actual bias
The governing standard is whether a reasonable reader, knowing the facts, would question the coverage's independence. Actual innocence of bias does not cure an appearance problem, because readers cannot see inside the journalist's head. Manage to the appearance standard.

### Maintain written conflict records
Keep current, written records of holdings, outside income, and relevant family interests, and review them against assignments. Memory and informal management fail; written records make conflicts visible before they become problems.

## Common Traps

### Rationalizing small holdings as immaterial
Journalists often dismiss small holdings as unable to influence them, but readers do not know the journalist's psychology and judge by the appearance. Apply the appearance standard regardless of perceived materiality.

### Trading on advance knowledge of coverage
Buying or selling ahead of a story the outlet will publish is a severe, often legal, breach. The timing alone creates an irrebuttable appearance of impropriety.

### Ignoring family-member finances
Treating a spouse's or family member's holdings as separate is a common rationalization that does not withstand scrutiny. Family finances are the journalist's conflict.

### Undisclosed freelance or consulting income
Secondary income from entities the journalist covers, or from those with an interest in the coverage, creates a hidden conflict. Disclose and evaluate each arrangement.

### Embedded reciprocity through gifts and travel
Accepting travel, meals, and hospitality from covered entities builds relationships of obligation that subtly shape coverage, even when no explicit deal exists. Pay your own way.

### Assuming disclosure cures all conflicts
Disclosure is necessary but not always sufficient. Where the conflict is material, recusal, not mere disclosure, is required.

### Failing to update conflict records
Holdings and income change over time. Stale conflict records miss new conflicts. Review and update regularly, especially against new assignments.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I disclosed all holdings and material financial interests related to my beat to editors, and to readers where relevant?
- [ ] Have I recused from coverage where a financial conflict is too material for disclosure alone to cure?
- [ ] Have I avoided trading in securities of covered entities around relevant reporting and publication?
- [ ] Have I treated the financial interests of spouse, partner, and dependent family as my own for conflict purposes?
- [ ] Have I scrutinized freelance, consulting, speaking, and other secondary income for conflicts with my coverage?
- [ ] Have I declined or paid for gifts, travel, and hospitality from sources and subjects of coverage?
- [ ] Am I applying the appearance standard, evaluating how a reasonable reader would perceive the conflict, not just whether I feel biased?
- [ ] Are my conflict records current, written, and reviewed against my assignments?
- [ ] Would a reader who learned my full financial picture find my coverage credible as independent?
- [ ] Have I escalated uncertain cases to editors rather than resolving them alone?
