# Personal Relationships Disclosure

Personal relationships, friendships, family ties, romantic partnerships, prior employment, and institutional affiliations, create conflicts of interest when they could be perceived to influence a journalist's coverage of people or institutions they are connected to. The harm this skill prevents is the corruption of fairness and independence that undisclosed personal entanglement produces: favorable coverage of friends, softened treatment of family, hostility or favor toward former employers, and the collapse of reader trust when these relationships surface. Personal conflicts are often harder to see than financial ones, because relationships are woven through a journalist's life and identity, but they are no less damaging to credibility. The discipline is to recognize where a relationship compromises the ability to be seen as fair, and to disclose or recuse accordingly.

## Core Rules

### Disclose relationships with subjects and sources
Any close personal relationship with a subject or source of coverage, a friendship, family tie, romantic relationship, or significant prior professional tie, must be disclosed to editors before publication, and where relevant to readers. The question is whether a reasonable reader would want to know the relationship in assessing the coverage's fairness.

### Recuse from coverage of close relations
Where the relationship is close enough that fair coverage cannot credibly be produced, a family member, spouse, close friend, or current romantic partner, recuse from the story. Disclosure does not cure the conflict of covering someone you love or are bound to; recusal does.

### Treat prior employment as a potential conflict
Prior work for an institution being covered creates a conflict through lingering loyalty, relationships, or financial interest such as unvested benefits. Disclose the prior employment, consider a cooling-off period for direct coverage, and recuse where the tie remains material. The passage of time reduces but does not always eliminate the conflict.

### Scrutinize coverage of current or recent romantic partners
Romantic or sexual relationships with sources, subjects, or colleagues on the beat are severe conflicts that generally require recusal, not merely disclosure. Such relationships compromise independence directly and produce conflicts that disclosure cannot neutralize.

### Evaluate friendships on the beat honestly
Friendships that develop through beat reporting are common and not inherently corrupting, but they become conflicts when the friend is the subject of scrutiny. Evaluate honestly whether the friendship would make critical coverage difficult, and disclose or recuse where it would. The test is whether you can still report critically, not whether you feel you are a friend.

### Apply the appearance standard to personal ties
As with financial conflicts, the standard is whether a reasonable reader would question the coverage's independence given the relationship. Actual ability to be fair does not cure an appearance problem. Manage to what readers would perceive.

### Disclose institutional affiliations
Membership in organizations, boards, advisory roles, alumni ties, and political affiliations can create conflicts when those institutions or their interests are covered. Disclose affiliations relevant to the coverage and recuse where the affiliation is material.

### Document and revisit as relationships evolve
Relationships change over time: a source becomes a friend, a former colleague becomes a subject. Revisit conflict assessments as relationships evolve, and disclose new conflicts as they arise. Static assessments miss emerging conflicts.

## Common Traps

### Rationalizing that friendship does not affect coverage
Journalists often believe they can separate friendship from reporting, but readers judge by appearance and the subtle pull of loyalty is hard to self-assess. Apply the appearance standard.

### Covering family or partners with disclosure alone
Disclosure does not cure the conflict of covering those bound to you by love or family. Recusal is generally required.

### Ignoring the lingering loyalty of prior employment
Former employers retain claims on loyalty, relationships, and benefits that can shape coverage. Disclose and consider recusal, especially soon after departure.

### Failing to disclose relationships that developed on the beat
Friendships with sources that grew over time are often not disclosed because they felt natural rather than compromising. Disclose them when the friend becomes a subject.

### Treating romantic relationships with sources as manageable
Romantic or sexual relationships with sources or subjects on the beat are severe conflicts. Recusal, not management, is typically required.

### Concealing affiliations out of privacy concern
Journalists sometimes withhold organizational or political affiliations as private, but affiliations relevant to coverage are disclosable. Privacy yields to the reader's interest in assessing independence.

### Static conflict assessments
Assessing a relationship once and never revisiting it misses the way relationships evolve into conflicts. Revisit as circumstances change.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I disclosed all close personal relationships with subjects and sources to editors, and to readers where relevant?
- [ ] Have I recused from coverage of family, spouses, close friends, or romantic partners where fair coverage cannot be credibly produced?
- [ ] Have I disclosed prior employment with covered institutions and considered recusal where the tie remains material?
- [ ] Have I avoided or recused from coverage involving current or recent romantic relationships with sources or subjects?
- [ ] Have I honestly evaluated whether friendships on the beat would compromise critical coverage, and disclosed or recused where they would?
- [ ] Am I applying the appearance standard, considering how a reasonable reader would perceive the relationship?
- [ ] Have I disclosed institutional affiliations, board roles, and political ties relevant to the coverage?
- [ ] Am I revisiting conflict assessments as relationships evolve over time?
- [ ] Would a reader who learned my full set of relationships find my coverage credible as fair and independent?
- [ ] Have I escalated uncertain cases to editors rather than self-resolving them?
