# FOIA Request Drafting

A freedom of information request is a legal instrument, not a casual ask. The wording of the request determines whether the agency can process it, what it will search for, what it will release, how long it will take, and whether a later denial can be appealed. The judgment problem is that access laws look simple, "any person may request records," but the practical success of a request turns on precision that inexperienced requesters skip: the scope of the search, the specificity of the record description, the date range, the format, and the fee posture. A vaguely drafted request invites delay, over-redaction, or denial that a precisely drafted one would have survived.

Use this skill when drafting a records request, deciding how to word it, choosing between formal and informal channels, or structuring a request to withstand agency review and appeal. The main risk is that an agent treats request drafting as a formality rather than as the decisive factor in whether records are obtained.

## Core Rules

### Describe Records Specifically Enough To Be Found

An agency can only produce records it can locate, and the request must describe them specifically enough for a reasonable records officer to know what to search for. "All records relating to the contract" is too vague; it forces the agency to guess the contract, the parties, the period, and the custodian, and invites a response that nothing could be found or that the search was unduly burdensome. "The contract between Agency X and Vendor Y dated 2023, including all amendments, change orders, and correspondence regarding performance, held by the procurement office" is specific enough to guide a search.

Where you do not know the exact record, describe it by function and context: "inspection reports generated by the Division of Z for Facility W between January 2022 and December 2023." The more you can pin the record type, custodian office, date range, and subject, the harder it is for the agency to claim it cannot find anything, and the easier it is to challenge a no-records response.

### Set A Precise Date Range

Date ranges are among the most important and most neglected elements of a request. Without one, the agency may search an unbounded period, driving up fees and delays, or may default to a narrow recent window that misses the relevant records. Set a range that captures what you need without being gratuitously broad, and explain the basis for the range if it is not obvious.

If you are unsure of the exact dates, set a generous but bounded range and note that you can refine it. A bounded range also lets you detect evasion: if the agency produces nothing for a period you have reason to believe was active, that gap is itself information.

### Choose The Right Scope And Avoid Burdensome Framing

Agencies can refuse requests that are unduly burdensome, requiring an unreasonable search and review effort. While this standard varies by jurisdiction, framing matters. A request that asks for "all emails mentioning X" across an entire agency over years is vulnerable to a burdensomeness rejection. A request that targets specific custodians, specific record types, and a defined period is harder to refuse and cheaper to fulfill.

Where a broad request is genuinely necessary, build it in stages: request an index or log first, then target the specific records the index reveals. This staged approach often obtains more than a single sweeping request, because it lets you refine based on what the agency actually has.

### Specify Format And Delivery

Specify how you want the records: electronic format where available, native file formats for data, redactions applied in a way that preserves the rest of the document, and delivery by email or portal rather than paper where possible. Electronic records are cheaper, faster, and more useful than paper, and requesting them reduces duplication fees. For datasets, request the native format and any documentation, not just a PDF summary.

If the agency produces records in a less useful format than requested, push back; many laws require production in the format requested where feasible. Format disputes are common and often resolvable through clarification.

### State Fee Posture Up Front

Address fees in the request itself. State that you are requesting a fee waiver or reduction on public-interest grounds where the law allows, and set a limit above which you want to be notified before work proceeds. Many laws require the agency to notify the requester if fees will exceed a threshold; setting that threshold protects you from surprise bills and gives you a chance to narrow the request before costs accrue.

If you qualify for news media or public-interest fee treatment under the relevant law, say so explicitly and provide the basis. Agencies do not always apply favorable fee categories automatically. See the records-access-and-cost-management skill for fee negotiation.

### Request Expedited Processing Where Justified

Many access laws provide expedited processing for requests involving urgent public interest, such as matters affecting public safety, government integrity, or time-sensitive policy decisions. If your request qualifies, request expedited processing explicitly and state the grounds. Expedited denials are themselves sometimes worth reporting and can be appealed.

Even without formal expedited treatment, flag time sensitivity in the request and ask whether faster processing is possible. A direct conversation with the records officer about urgency can sometimes move a request forward where formal mechanisms cannot.

### Preserve Appeal Rights Through Clear Wording

Draft every request with appeal in mind. A clear, specific, well-scoped request produces a denial or redaction that is easier to challenge, because you can point to the exact record and the exact legal basis for release. A vague request produces a vague denial that is hard to appeal. Keep a copy of the request, the date submitted, the agency's acknowledgment, and all correspondence, so the appeal record is complete.

When an agency denies or redacts, it must typically cite the specific exemption invoked. If the citation is vague or overbroad, that is grounds for appeal. Frame your appeal around the specific records withheld, the specific exemption cited, and the specific reasons release is required under the law.

## Common Traps

### Vague Record Descriptions That Invite No-Records Responses

A request that does not describe records specifically lets the agency claim nothing was found or that the search was burdensome. Describe records by type, custodian, date range, and subject.

### Omitting Or Over-Broadening The Date Range

Without a date range, searches become expensive and slow or default to narrow windows. Set a precise, justified range and refine it if needed.

### Sweeping Requests That Trigger Burdensomeness Rejections

Requests for all communications across an agency over years are vulnerable to refusal. Target specific custodians and record types, or stage the request through an index first.

### Failing To Address Fees Up Front

Silence on fees invites surprise bills or default high-rate processing. State your fee posture, request waivers where eligible, and set a notification threshold.

### Accepting The Agency's Chosen Format

Agencies may produce paper or PDF when electronic or native formats are available and more useful. Specify the format and push back where the agency defaults to a less useful one.

### Forfeiting Appeal Rights Through Sloppy Drafting

A vague request produces a denial that is hard to appeal. Draft for specificity and keep complete records so the appeal path is preserved.

## Self-Check

Before treating a FOIA request as well drafted, verify:

- Records are described specifically enough by type, custodian, date range, and subject that a reasonable records officer could locate them.
- A precise, justified date range is set, broad enough to capture relevant records without being gratuitously unbounded.
- The scope targets specific custodians and record types, or is staged through an index, to avoid burdensomeness rejection.
- The requested format is specified, with electronic and native formats requested where available and more useful.
- Fee posture is stated up front, including any waiver or reduction request with grounds, and a notification threshold is set.
- Expedited processing is requested where the request qualifies on urgent public-interest grounds, with the basis stated.
- The request is drafted with appeal in mind: specific, well-scoped, and supported by a complete record of submission and correspondence.
- No exemption the agency is likely to invoke is left unaddressed where the law provides a basis for release.
- The request distinguishes formal legal requests from informal asks, and uses the formal channel where legal rights and appeal matter.
- FOIA drafting supports editorial judgment; significant requests, especially those likely to be denied or appealed, should involve a responsible editor, and legal counsel or an open-government attorney where the request carries legal stakes.
