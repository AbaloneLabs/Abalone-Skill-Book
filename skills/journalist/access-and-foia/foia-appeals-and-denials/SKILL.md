# FOIA Appeals And Denials

A denial is not the end of a records request; in many cases it is the beginning of the productive phase. Agencies routinely over-withhold, invoke exemptions broadly, miss deadlines, or quote fees designed to discourage, and the access law's appeal mechanism exists precisely to correct these failures. The judgment problem is that requesters too often accept the first denial as final, when disciplined appeal, escalation, and where necessary litigation would have produced the records. Conversely, not every denial is worth fighting; scarce newsroom resources must be directed where the public-interest value justifies the cost. The skill is knowing which denials to challenge, how to frame the challenge, and when to escalate.

Use this skill when a request has been denied or heavily redacted, when an agency has missed deadlines or stopped responding, when deciding whether to appeal administratively or litigate, or when reporting on an agency's pattern of obstruction. The main risk is that an agent either surrenders at the first denial or pursues every denial indiscriminately without weighing cost against public value.

## Core Rules

### Read The Denial Closely And Identify The Specific Grounds

A valid denial must cite the specific exemption or grounds invoked for each category of withheld material. Read the denial closely: which exemption, which subsection, which records, and what reasoning. Vague invocations, "exemptions apply," or blanket references without specifics are themselves defects that strengthen an appeal. Categorize the withholding into types: full denial, partial denial, redaction, no-records response, or constructive denial through non-response.

Each type calls for a different response. A full denial on weak grounds is highly appealable. A no-records response requires probing the search itself: what terms were used, which custodians were searched, what systems were queried. Constructive denial, where the agency simply never responds past the deadline, may permit immediate appeal or even litigation under some laws. Match the response to the type of denial.

### Demand Specifics On The Search And The Exemptions

For a no-records or incomplete response, ask the agency to describe the search: which offices and custodians were queried, what search terms were used, what systems were searched, and what date range was applied. A deficient search, one that missed obvious custodians or used terms unlikely to locate the records, is challengeable. Agencies sometimes conduct perfunctory searches that fail by design; documenting the deficiency lets you demand a better one.

For redactions and partial denials, demand a specific exemption citation and a Vaughn index or equivalent log where the law provides one, listing each withheld record and the specific basis for withholding. Broad brush redactions without per-record justification are vulnerable to challenge. The more granular the agency's justification must be, the harder it is to over-withhold.

### Use The Administrative Appeal Effectively

Most access laws provide an administrative appeal to a higher authority within or overseeing the agency. Use it. The appeal is where many over-broad denials are reversed, because the appellate reviewer applies the law more carefully than the initial processor. Frame the appeal around the specific records withheld, the specific exemption cited, and the specific reasons release is required: the exemption does not apply, the agency over-read its scope, the public interest favors release, or the search was deficient.

Meet the appeal deadline, which is often short, and cite the law, prior decisions, and any guidance that supports release. A well-framed administrative appeal is cheap relative to litigation and succeeds more often than requesters assume. Document the appeal and the response, since both matter if the case proceeds.

### Distinguish Genuine Legal Exemptions From Evasion

Some withholdings are legally sound: genuine personal privacy interests, narrowly applicable law enforcement sensitivities, or trade secrets properly protected. Others are evasion: deliberative process invoked to hide embarrassment, privacy invoked to shield officials acting in their public capacity, or law enforcement sensitivity invoked for non-sensitive material. Learn to tell the difference, because the appeal strategy differs.

For genuine exemptions, narrow the request: ask for segregable portions that can be released even if part is exempt, or reframe to avoid the exempt material. For evasion, challenge directly: argue the exemption's limits, cite contrary guidance or precedent, and where the pattern is clear, consider reporting the over-withholding itself. An agency that systematically invokes an exemption to avoid embarrassment is behaving in a way the public has an interest in knowing.

### Decide Whether To Litigate Based On Public Value And Cost

Litigation is the ultimate backstop but is expensive, slow, and uncertain. Not every denial justifies a lawsuit. Weigh the public-interest value of the records, the strength of the legal claim, the cost and timeline, the newsroom's resources, and the precedent a win or loss would set. Some cases are worth litigating because the records are central to a major story or because the legal question affects many future requests; others are not worth the cost for marginal material.

Where litigation is warranted, work with media law counsel or open-government organizations, many of which support access cases on a pro bono or reduced-fee basis. Frame the case around both the specific records and the broader principle, since favorable rulings benefit all requesters. See the legal-protection skill for working with counsel.

### Treat Access Obstruction As Reporting

An agency's pattern of denial, delay, over-redaction, excessive fees, or non-response can itself be a story, particularly when it affects the public's ability to understand government conduct. Document the obstruction with specifics: requests submitted, deadlines missed, exemptions over-invoked, fees quoted, appeals denied. Compare the agency's behavior to other agencies handling similar requests. Where the pattern is egregious or systematic, report it, because opacity is a public-interest matter and exposure can prompt reform.

This reporting also creates pressure. Agencies that know their access behavior may be publicized sometimes become more responsive. Combine the substantive story with the access story where the two reinforce each other.

### Maintain The Paper Trail Throughout

Every step of the access fight depends on documentation: the original request, the acknowledgment, the denial, the search description, the exemption citations, the appeal, and the responses. Maintain a complete file for each significant request, so that appeals and litigation rest on a clear record and so that the access story, if reported, is airtight. Loss of correspondence or unclear timelines weaken both the legal case and the reporting.

## Common Traps

### Accepting The First Denial As Final

Initial denials are frequently reversed on appeal. Treating a denial as the end of the road surrenders records that disciplined appeal would obtain.

### Failing To Probe The Adequacy Of The Search

A no-records response often reflects a deficient search, not an absence of records. Without asking what was searched and how, the reporter cannot tell evasion from genuine absence.

### Letting Appeal Deadlines Pass

Administrative appeal deadlines are often short and strictly enforced. Missing them forfeits the cheapest and most effective challenge mechanism.

### Pursuing Every Denial Indiscriminately

Not every denial justifies the cost of appeal or litigation. Direct scarce resources where the public-interest value and legal strength are greatest.

### Conflating Genuine Exemptions With Evasion

Some withholdings are legally sound; others are evasion. The response differs: narrow and segregate for genuine exemptions, challenge directly for evasion. Misreading the type wastes effort.

### Losing The Paper Trail

Without complete documentation, appeals and litigation rest on weak ground and the access story cannot be reported convincingly. Maintain the file from the first request.

## Self-Check

Before treating the response to a denial as sound, verify:

- The denial was read closely and categorized by type, full, partial, redaction, no-records, or constructive, with the specific exemption or grounds identified for each withholding.
- The adequacy of the agency's search was probed, including custodians, terms, systems, and date range, and deficiencies were documented.
- Specific exemption citations and a per-record justification or index were demanded where the law provides them, to expose over-broad redaction.
- The administrative appeal was used where available, framed around specific records, exemptions, and legal grounds, and filed within the deadline.
- Genuine legal exemptions were distinguished from evasion, with the response tailored: narrowing and segregating for the former, direct challenge for the latter.
- The decision to litigate weighed public value, legal strength, cost, timeline, and precedent, and involved media law counsel or open-government support where warranted.
- Access obstruction was documented and, where systematic or egregious, reported as part of the public-interest story.
- A complete paper trail was maintained from the original request through all appeals and responses.
- No denial was accepted as final where appeal or escalation had a reasonable chance of success and the public value justified the effort.
- Appeals and denials support editorial judgment; significant appeals and any litigation should involve a responsible editor and qualified legal counsel, with open-government specialists engaged where the case carries broader stakes.
