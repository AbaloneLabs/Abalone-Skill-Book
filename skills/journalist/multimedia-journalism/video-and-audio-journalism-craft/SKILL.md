# Video And Audio Journalism Craft

Video and audio journalism carry unique power: the human voice, the visible face, the ambient sound, and the moving image convey reality and emotion in ways text cannot, and they create intimacy and credibility with audiences. They also carry unique risks: production choices, editing, framing, music, and sequencing can manipulate perception more subtly than text, and the technical demands can pressure accuracy and ethics under deadline. The harm this skill prevents is the multimedia piece that misleads through production choices: selective editing that distorts meaning, leading music that manufactures emotion, framing that stacks perception, reenactment presented as documentary, or technical pressure excusing verification shortcuts. Strong multimedia journalism holds itself to the same accuracy and ethical standards as text, while using the medium's tools honestly to convey reality.

Use this skill when producing any video, audio, podcast, or broadcast journalism, from planning through post-production. Consult editors on production and editing choices, especially where the medium's persuasive power could mislead or where subjects are vulnerable.

## Core Rules

### Hold multimedia to the same accuracy standard as text
The fact that a piece is video or audio does not lower the accuracy, verification, or ethical standards that apply to text. Claims made on camera or in audio must be checked, sources must be evaluated, and contested material must be attributed and hedged. The technical demands of production, the pressure of deadlines, and the medium's fluency can tempt shortcuts, but a misleading video reaches audiences with more persuasive force than a misleading text. Apply text-level rigor to multimedia, and treat the medium's power as a reason for more care, not less.

### Edit honestly, preserving meaning and context
Editing is where multimedia most easily misleads. Trimming an interview to change meaning, removing qualifying context, sequencing answers to imply positions the subject did not take, or stitching together non-adjacent statements all distort. Edit to tighten and clarify while preserving the speaker's meaning and the context that makes the meaning intelligible. Where material editing changes substance, disclose it. Treat the cut as an ethical act: every edit should be defensible as faithful to what was said and meant.

### Do not use music, sound design, or framing to manipulate
Music and sound design carry enormous emotional weight and can manufacture feeling the content does not earn: ominous music to make a subject seem sinister, swelling strings to manufacture inspiration, comedic cues to mock. Use music and sound design sparingly and honestly, or not at all in serious journalism, and never to lead the viewer to a conclusion the evidence does not support. Similarly, camera framing, angle, lens choice, and lighting stack perception; choose them to represent fairly rather than to flatter or demean by technique.

### Distinguish documentary from reenactment and illustration
Audiences must know whether they are seeing actual events, dramatic reenactment, illustrative B-roll, or stock footage. Blurring these boundaries misleads viewers who treat images as evidence. Label reenactments clearly, avoid presenting stock or file footage as current without disclosure, and do not stage events for the camera while presenting them as spontaneous. The credibility of video journalism depends on viewers trusting that what they see depicts what actually happened.

### Conduct interviews with rigor and fairness on camera
On-camera and on-mic interviews carry the same obligations as any interview, plus additional considerations. The subject's performance under technical pressure, lights, microphones, crew, is not the same as their considered views, and editing can exaggerate or soften it. Ask fair, clear questions, give the subject genuine opportunity to respond, do not ambush for dramatic effect without justification, and represent the exchange honestly in the final cut. The drama of a confrontational interview does not excuse misrepresentation.

### Verify multimedia material, especially user-generated content
Video, audio, and images from social media, witnesses, and unofficial sources require the same verification as any other evidence, often more, because manipulation is easier and more common. Check origin, time, location, and authenticity through reverse search, geolocation, metadata, and cross-referencing with independent reporting. Be alert to deepfakes, AI-generated audio and video, edited material, and content from other events or times. Publishing unverified multimedia spreads misinformation with the medium's persuasive force.

### Plan production to serve the story, not the spectacle
Production choices, locations, crew, equipment, and scheduling should serve the story's journalistic purpose, not its visual impressiveness. Spectacle that does not serve understanding wastes resources and can distort: dramatic locations or cinematic treatment can inflate a minor story's significance. Match production ambition to the story's actual weight. A modest story told clearly serves readers better than a thin story dressed in impressive production.

### Respect subjects throughout the production process
Video and audio production are intrusive: cameras, microphones, lights, and crews impose on subjects, and the resulting material follows them permanently. Secure genuine consent, explain how the material will be used, allow subjects to understand the process, and treat vulnerable subjects with extra care. Do not exploit the technical asymmetry between a crewed production and a private individual. The subject's dignity persists after the shoot ends.

## Common Traps

### Selective editing that distorts meaning
Trimming to change what the subject said or meant misrepresents them. The trap is that the edited version is tighter and more dramatic, but it is no longer faithful.

### Music and sound design manufacturing emotion
Ominous or swelling audio leads viewers to conclusions the content does not earn. The trap is that music is effective at manipulating feeling, which is exactly why it must be used carefully.

### Reenactment or stock presented as documentary
Blurring the line between actual events and dramatization misleads viewers who treat images as evidence. The trap is that dramatization is more vivid, but it breaches the documentary compact.

### Lower standards under technical pressure
The demands of production excuse verification shortcuts or weak sourcing. The trap is that multimedia is hard to produce, but difficulty does not lower accuracy obligations.

### Unverified user-generated multimedia
Publishing social video or audio without verification spreads misinformation with added persuasive force. The trap is that the material is compelling and timely, but manipulation is common.

### Spectacle displacing substance
Cinematic production inflating a thin story's significance. The trap is that impressive visuals feel like important journalism, but they cannot substitute for reporting.

### Exploiting technical asymmetry
Crewed productions imposing on private individuals who cannot meaningfully push back. The trap is that the technical gap feels normal to the crew, but it is intimidating to the subject.

## Self-Check

- Does the multimedia piece meet the same accuracy, verification, and ethical standards as text reporting?
- Is editing honest, preserving the speaker's meaning and context, with material changes disclosed?
- Are music, sound design, and framing used to represent fairly rather than to manipulate emotion or stack perception?
- Are documentary, reenactment, illustration, and stock footage clearly distinguished, with no staged events presented as spontaneous?
- Are on-camera and on-mic interviews conducted with rigor and fairness, representing the exchange honestly in the final cut?
- Is multimedia material, especially user-generated content, verified for origin, time, location, and authenticity before publication?
- Does production serve the story's journalistic purpose rather than spectacle, matched to the story's actual weight?
- Are subjects treated with respect throughout production, with genuine consent and extra care for vulnerable subjects, and have editors reviewed production and editing choices where the medium could mislead?
