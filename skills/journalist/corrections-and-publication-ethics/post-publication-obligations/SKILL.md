# Post-Publication Obligations

A story's publication is not the end of a journalist's obligations to it, to its subjects, or to its audience. The harm this skill prevents is the damage that follows when journalists treat publication as closure: errors that go uncorrected because no one is watching, subjects left exposed to consequences the reporting caused, sources whose protection was promised but not maintained, follow-up developments ignored, and misuse or misrepresentation of the work left unchallenged. Post-publication obligations are the ongoing duties that sustain the integrity and accountability of journalism over time.

## Core Rules

### Monitor the published story for accuracy
After publication, watch for new information, challenges, or corrections the story needs. If a fact is wrong, correct it promptly and visibly. If a claim is contested, weigh the challenge seriously. Publication does not freeze the story's accuracy; it begins the period in which the story must be kept accurate.

### Fulfill obligations to subjects
Subjects who cooperated with reporting may face consequences from the coverage: attention, retaliation, legal exposure, or harm. Consider whether the reporting created obligations, to warn of likely consequences, to check in, to follow up, or to stand by commitments made. Do not treat subjects as expendable once the story is filed.

### Maintain source protection over time
Promises of confidentiality or protection extend indefinitely. If a source's identity is at risk of exposure through legal process, hacking, or careless handling, take ongoing steps to protect them. Source protection is not a one-time pledge but a continuing duty.

### Respond substantively to complaints and challenges
When readers, subjects, or institutions challenge the story, respond substantively rather than dismissively. Evaluate the challenge on its merits, investigate where warranted, and correct or clarify where the challenge is valid. Dismissing legitimate complaints erodes trust; engaging them strengthens it.

### Pursue follow-up coverage where the story continues
Many stories develop after initial publication: investigations proceed, policies change, subjects respond, consequences unfold. Where there is continued public interest, follow up. Abandoning a story at its most consequential moment leaves the reporting incomplete and the public underserved.

### Challenge misuse and misrepresentation
If the work is misrepresented, taken out of context, or weaponized in ways that distort its meaning, the journalist has an interest in correcting the record. This includes challenging bad-faith citations, clarifying what the story did and did not find, and pushing back against distortions in public discourse. Silence in the face of misuse can read as endorsement.

### Honor commitments made during reporting
Promises made to sources or subjects during reporting, to show them quotes, to protect identities, to follow up, to publish at a certain time, must be honored after publication as well. Circumstances do not void commitments; if a commitment cannot be honored, the source must be told why.

### Document and archive for accountability
Keep records of the reporting, sources, documents, and decisions, consistent with source protection, so that the story can be defended, corrected, or built upon later. Accountability requires that the basis of the work survives publication.

## Common Traps

### Treating publication as closure
The most common trap is assuming the work ends at publication. Accuracy, subjects, sources, and follow-up all require continued attention.

### Ignoring or dismissing complaints
Dismissing complaints as defensive or bad-faith, without evaluating their merits, lets real errors stand and alienates readers. Evaluate each challenge on its substance.

### Abandoning subjects after publication
Subjects who face consequences from coverage, especially vulnerable ones, can be left exposed if the reporter moves on. Consider and where possible address the consequences the reporting caused.

### Letting source protection lapse over time
Promises of confidentiality can erode through inattention: records retained too long, metadata that identifies sources, legal exposure that develops later. Protection is ongoing.

### Failing to follow up on consequential stories
A story that triggers investigation, reform, or backlash often has its most important chapters after the initial report. Abandoning it leaves the public interest underserved.

### Silence in the face of misuse
Allowing the work to be misrepresented without correction lets distortions stand as the public understanding of the story. Challenge misuse where it distorts meaning.

### Breaking post-publication commitments
Commitments made during reporting, to follow up, to protect, to publish on schedule, must be honored. Circumstances do not automatically void them.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Am I monitoring the published story for accuracy and correcting errors promptly and visibly?
- [ ] Have I considered and addressed the consequences the reporting may cause for subjects, especially vulnerable ones?
- [ ] Am I maintaining source protection over time, including against emerging risks?
- [ ] Do I respond substantively to complaints and challenges, evaluating them on their merits?
- [ ] Am I pursuing follow-up coverage where the story continues to develop and public interest remains?
- [ ] Do I challenge misuse and misrepresentation of the work that distorts its meaning?
- [ ] Have I honored all commitments made to sources and subjects during reporting?
- [ ] Are records of the reporting archived, consistent with source protection, for future accountability?
- [ ] Would subjects and sources, looking back, feel that I met my obligations to them after publication?
- [ ] Is the story being kept accurate and accountable over time, not just at the moment of publication?
