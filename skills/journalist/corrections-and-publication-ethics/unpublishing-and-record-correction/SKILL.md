# Unpublishing And Record Correction

The decision to remove, alter, or substantially revise a published story is among the most consequential editorial choices in journalism, because it touches a tension at the heart of the craft: the integrity of the published record versus the real harm that record can cause to the individuals it names. The harm this skill prevents is twofold. First, capitulating to takedown demands erodes the historical record, enables censorship through pressure, and undermines the credibility of independent journalism. Second, rigidly refusing ever to correct or remove the record leaves genuine errors, outdated information, and disproportionate harm unaddressed, harming individuals and ultimately the public's trust. The journalist's task is to distinguish legitimate correction and proportionate mitigation from illegitimate erasure, and to act on principle rather than convenience or pressure.

## Core Rules

### Preserve the integrity of the published record as the default
Once published, a story is part of the historical record. The default presumption is that it remains. Unpublishing is an exceptional action that requires a stronger justification than mere preference, embarrassment, or pressure. The burden of argument falls on those seeking removal, not on those defending the record.

### Correct errors rather than erasing them
When a published story contains an error, the standard response is a visible correction that preserves the record while fixing the inaccuracy, not silent removal. Correction maintains accountability: readers see what was wrong and how it was fixed. Erasure destroys the evidence of both the mistake and the accountability.

### Distinguish factual error from regret
A subject's regret that a story was published, or that they were named in it, is not grounds for unpublishing. Legitimate grounds include factual error, legal defect (such as defamation established through due process), new information that materially changes the story's accuracy, or circumstances where continued publication serves no public interest and causes disproportionate harm. Regret, embarrassment, or reputational concern without these elements do not justify removal.

### Evaluate proportionality and public interest
When considering removal or substantial alteration, weigh the public interest in the record's preservation against the harm to the named individual. A decades-old minor-crime story that continues to dominate a person's search results raises different proportionality questions than recent coverage of a public official's misconduct. Proportionality considers recency, the subject's role, the severity and relevance of the conduct, and the availability of the information elsewhere.

### Resist pressure that seeks to manipulate the record
Takedown demands driven by litigation threats, political pressure, financial leverage, or coordinated campaigns must be evaluated on their merits, not conceded to avoid friction. Conceding to pressure that lacks merit establishes a precedent that the record is negotiable and invites further manipulation. Where a demand has merit, act on the merit; where it does not, defend the record.

### Prefer mitigation over removal where possible
Before unpublishing, consider lesser measures that address harm while preserving the record: adding an editor's note with new context or developments, updating with the subject's response or subsequent events, de-indexing from search without removing the article, or archiving rather than featuring. Mitigation respects both the record and the individual.

### Apply consistent standards, not ad hoc judgment
Decisions about unpublishing and correction should follow stated standards applied consistently across cases, not the persuasive skill of the requester or the mood of the editor. Inconsistent standards invite bias and undermine credibility. Document the policy and apply it evenly.

### Be transparent about changes and removals
When a story is corrected, updated, or removed, be transparent with readers about what changed and why, to the extent consistent with privacy and legal constraints. Stealth removal and silent edits erode trust. A note explaining the action, even briefly, preserves accountability.

## Common Traps

### Conceding to the loudest or best-resourced demand
Decisions that track the pressure applied rather than the merits of the case produce a record shaped by power rather than principle. Apply consistent standards regardless of who is asking.

### Silent edits that rewrite history
Altering a published story without any visible note destroys the record of what was originally reported and why it changed. Significant changes require visible notation.

### Treating unpublishing as a favor or customer service
Removing stories to appease subjects, advertisers, or powerful interests, framed as accommodation, corrupts the independence of the record. Removal is an editorial judgment about accuracy and proportionality, not a service.

### Refusing all correction in the name of the record
A rigid stance that the published record is immutable, even in the face of clear error or new information, sacrifices accuracy and fairness to a false notion of integrity. The record must be correctable.

### Ignoring the cumulative harm of stale coverage
Failing to consider how old coverage compounds over time, especially for minor matters, ignores real harm. Proportionality requires attention to how the record functions years later.

### Removing without considering availability elsewhere
If accurate information is widely available from other sources, removing one outlet's version does not undo the public record but may create inconsistency. Consider whether removal meaningfully addresses harm or merely creates gaps.

### Allowing legal threat to substitute for merit
The presence of a legal threat does not establish that a claim has merit. Evaluate the underlying substance; do not let the cost of defense become the deciding factor without analysis.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Am I treating preservation of the published record as the default, with removal requiring strong justification?
- [ ] For errors, am I preferring visible correction over silent removal?
- [ ] Have I distinguished legitimate grounds (error, legal defect, new information, disproportionate harm) from mere regret or embarrassment?
- [ ] Have I weighed the public interest in preservation against the proportionate harm to the named individual, including recency and role?
- [ ] Am I resisting pressure, litigation threat, or campaigns that lack merit, while acting on demands that have merit?
- [ ] Have I considered mitigation measures, editor's notes, updates, or de-indexing, before full removal?
- [ ] Am I applying consistent, documented standards rather than ad hoc judgment influenced by the requester?
- [ ] Have I been transparent with readers about what was corrected, updated, or removed, and why?
- [ ] Would defending this decision to peers and readers withstand scrutiny on principle?
- [ ] Does my action preserve both the integrity of the record and the fairness owed to those it names?
