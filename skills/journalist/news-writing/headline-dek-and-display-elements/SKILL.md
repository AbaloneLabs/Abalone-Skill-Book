# Headline Dek And Display Elements

Most readers never read the body. They see the headline, the dek, the image, the caption, the social card, and the push notification, and from those elements they form their understanding of the story. The harm this skill prevents is the gap between a careful body and a misleading display: a headline that overstates certainty, a thumbnail that distorts tone, a caption that misidentifies, a pull quote stripped of context, a push notification that sensationalizes. Each display element is a miniature story in its own right, and each carries the same accuracy and fairness obligations as the full piece. A body that is precise cannot rescue a headline that is not; the misleading element is the one most readers encounter and remember.

Use this skill whenever writing or selecting any display element: headline, dek, image, caption, pull quote, social copy, push notification, or search snippet. Consult editors on display elements for high-stakes stories, since these elements carry disproportionate legal and reputational weight.

## Core Rules

### Treat every display element as a standalone claim
Each display element, headline, dek, caption, image, social card, push, must be accurate and fair on its own, because most readers will encounter it without the body. Do not write a headline that "the body explains" or use an image "the caption clarifies." If the element misleads in isolation, it has failed, regardless of what the body says. Test each element by imagining it is the only thing the reader will ever see.

### Match headline certainty to the evidence
Headlines carry the strongest claim and therefore the strictest accuracy obligation. A headline that says "Official Resigns Amid Scandal" implies confirmed scandal; if the scandal is alleged, the headline must say so. A headline that says "Study Proves" overstates what studies do; "Study Suggests" or "Study Finds Association" is more accurate. Resist the pressure toward certainty and drama that drives clicks; the headline sets the public record for most readers, and an overstated headline is a widespread falsehood even if the body is careful.

### Keep the dek informative and proportional
The dek, the subheadline or summary line, should add information and context, not merely repeat or inflate the headline. A good dek tells readers what the story is about and why it matters in a sentence or two, calibrated to the same accuracy standard as the headline. Avoid deks that sensationalize, bury the lede, or promise more than the story delivers. The dek is the reader's second chance to understand the story; use it to inform, not to tease.

### Choose images that are accurate, fair, and not misleading
Images carry enormous persuasive weight and are often remembered when text is forgotten. An image must depict what the caption and story say it depicts: the right event, the right people, the right time, the right context. Avoid stock images presented as documentary, file photos presented as current without labeling, images from a different event, and images cropped or framed to distort meaning. Consider whether the image stereotypes, stigmatizes, or exposes a subject to disproportionate harm, especially for vulnerable groups. The image is part of the journalism, not decoration.

### Write captions that identify and contextualize accurately
Captions must correctly identify who, what, when, and where, and should add context rather than merely labeling the obvious. A misidentified person, a wrong date, a wrong location, or an omitted material fact in a caption is an error as serious as one in the body. Captions are often read by people who skim, so their accuracy matters disproportionately. Verify caption details against the image source and the reporting, not against memory or assumption.

### Pull quotes that preserve context and meaning
A pull quote is a magnified excerpt, and its prominence gives it weight beyond its length. Select pull quotes that represent the speaker's meaning in context, not striking fragments that distort when isolated. A pull quote that reads dramatically but meant something different in context misleads readers who treat it as the story's essence. Where a quote needs surrounding context to be fair, either include the context or choose a different quote.

### Calibrate social copy and push notifications to accuracy, not just engagement
Social posts and push notifications are written to drive clicks, which creates pressure toward sensation, but they carry the same accuracy obligation as the headline. A push notification that overstates, a social card that distorts, or a tweet that strips context are widespread misleading elements in their own right. Calibrate engagement-oriented copy to the evidence, and resist the temptation to optimize purely for clicks at the cost of accuracy.

### Audit every display surface for the same standard
A story has many surfaces: headline, dek, image, caption, pull quote, social posts, push, newsletter blurb, search snippet, syndicated summary. Each must meet the same accuracy and fairness standard as the body. Audit them as a set, because inconsistency between a careful body and a sensational surface is itself a form of misleading the reader. The display elements are where most misleading occurs, because they are written last, under pressure, and optimized for engagement.

## Common Traps

### The headline the body explains
Writing an overstated headline on the assumption that readers who click will get the full picture ignores that most readers never click through. The trap is that the dramatic headline drives traffic, but it spreads a falsehood to everyone who sees only the headline.

### Certainty inflation for clicks
"Proves," "confirms," "destroys," "slams," and "reveals" generate engagement but overstate what the evidence supports. The trap is that stronger words perform better in metrics, pulling headlines toward overstatement.

### Misleading or mismatched images
Stock photos, file photos, images from other events, and decontextualized pictures mislead readers who treat the image as evidence. The trap is that a striking image fills the slot, but if it does not depict the story it misrepresents.

### Caption errors on skimmed elements
Misidentified people, wrong dates, and wrong locations in captions are serious errors that readers catch and that damage credibility. The trap is that captions are written quickly and verified less rigorously than the body.

### Pull quotes stripped of context
A dramatic fragment magnified as a pull quote can distort the speaker's meaning when isolated. The trap is that the fragment is more striking than the full quote, but it is no longer fair.

### Engagement-optimized social and push copy
Social posts and pushes written for clicks often overstate or sensationalize. The trap is that these elements reach the widest audience and carry the least editorial scrutiny.

### Inconsistent standards across surfaces
A careful body paired with a sensational headline, image, or push creates a misleading overall package. The trap is treating each surface as a separate task rather than auditing them as a set against a single standard.

## Self-Check

- Is every display element, headline, dek, image, caption, pull quote, social copy, and push, accurate and fair on its own, as if it were the only thing the reader sees?
- Does the headline match the evidence, avoiding certainty, scale, causation, or blame overstatement?
- Does the dek add information and context proportionally, without sensationalizing or burying the lede?
- Is the lead image accurate to the event, time, people, and context, free of misleading stock or file use, and considerate of harm to subjects?
- Does the caption correctly identify who, what, when, and where, verified against the source and reporting?
- Does any pull quote preserve the speaker's meaning in context rather than distorting through isolation?
- Are social copy and push notifications calibrated to accuracy, not just engagement, with the same standard as the headline?
- Have all display surfaces been audited as a set against a single standard, and have editors reviewed display elements for high-stakes stories where they carry disproportionate legal and reputational weight?
