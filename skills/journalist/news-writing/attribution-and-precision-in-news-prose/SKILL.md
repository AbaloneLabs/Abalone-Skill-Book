# Attribution And Precision In News Prose

The words a journalist chooses carry the weight of fact, and imprecise language smuggles claims the evidence does not support. Attribution and precision in news prose are the disciplines of matching language to evidence, attributing claims to their actual source, choosing verbs that describe rather than interpret, and distinguishing what is known from what is alleged, estimated, or predicted. The harm this skill prevents is the steady drift from careful reporting to careless prose: "refused" when the subject merely did not respond, "slammed" for ordinary criticism, "admitted" for a routine statement, "chaos" for a complex situation, "crisis" for an unmeasured problem. Each imprecision is small; cumulatively they distort the public's understanding and expose the newsroom to credibility and legal risk.

Use this skill whenever drafting or editing news prose, choosing verbs, handling quotes, and calibrating attribution. Consult editors on language choices in legally sensitive stories and where wording could imply guilt, intent, or misconduct.

## Core Rules

### Attribute at the level the evidence supports
Match attribution strength to evidentiary strength. A documented fact can be stated directly; an official statement is attributed to the official; an allegation is labeled as an allegation; an estimate is labeled as an estimate; a prediction is labeled as a prediction. Do not let strong attribution carry weak evidence or weak attribution carry strong claims. "Records show" is appropriate for documented facts; "officials say" for official statements; "according to a person familiar with the matter" for attributed but unnamed sourcing. Misalignment between attribution and evidence is the most common prose-level error.

### Choose precise, neutral verbs over interpretive ones
Verbs carry interpretation. "Refused" implies willful defiance; "did not respond by deadline" states what happened. "Admitted" implies guilt or concession; "said" is neutral. "Slammed," "blasted," "destroyed," and "eviscerated" dramatize ordinary disagreement. "Admitted," "confessed," "conceded," and "acknowledged" each imply different things about the speaker's posture. Choose the verb that describes what occurred without adding interpretation the evidence does not support. When in doubt, prefer the plain "said," which is accurate and does not editorialize.

### Avoid loaded language that smuggles judgment
Words like "controversial," "chaotic," "corrupt," "controversial," "scandal," "crisis," "wave," "flood," "surge," "explosive," "shocking," "stunning," and "bombshell" carry evaluative judgments often unsupported by the evidence presented. They make stories feel more dramatic but substitute the writer's assessment for the reader's. Where the situation genuinely is dramatic, the facts will convey it; where the language outruns the facts, it misleads. Strip evaluative adjectives and let the documented reality carry the weight.

### Handle quotes with fidelity to meaning and context
Quotes must reproduce the speaker's words accurately and in a context that preserves their meaning. Do not truncate quotes to change meaning, select fragments that distort the whole statement, clean up quotes without disclosure in ways that change substance, or stitch together non-adjacent words. Partial quotes within paraphrase must be faithful. Where a quote is unclear, awkward, or in need of light editing for readability, follow the outlet's policy on quote cleaning and disclose material alterations. The reader's trust depends on quotes being what the person actually said.

### Distinguish paraphrase from direct quotation clearly
Paraphrase summarizes in the journalist's words; direct quotation reproduces the source's words. Keep them distinct: do not present paraphrase inside quotation marks, do not present direct quotation as paraphrase, and do not blur the boundary with partial quotes that imply more verbatim fidelity than they have. Readers use quotation marks to judge what the source actually said; blurring the line misleads them about the source's exact words.

### Calibrate hedging to uncertainty, not to safety
Hedging words, "apparently," "seemingly," "reportedly," "allegedly," "purportedly," serve a purpose when uncertainty is real, signaling to readers that a claim is not confirmed. But hedging applied to dodge attribution, avoid commitment, or imply doubt where none exists is evasive. Calibrate hedging to the actual uncertainty: use it where the evidence is genuinely incomplete, and remove it where the facts are established. Vague hedging that sounds careful but conveys nothing is a form of imprecision.

### Make uncertainty specific rather than performative
When something is uncertain, say specifically what is uncertain and why: "the cause remains under investigation," "the death toll has not been independently verified," "officials gave conflicting accounts of the timeline." Specific uncertainty helps readers understand the state of the story. Performative uncertainty, "the situation remains fluid," "details are still emerging," tells readers nothing and often fills space where reporting should be.

### Avoid passive constructions that hide responsibility
Passive voice, "mistakes were made," "it was alleged," "concerns were raised," "the policy was implemented," conceals who did what, who said what, and how the reporter knows. Active voice assigns responsibility and lets readers evaluate agency. Use passive only when the actor is genuinely unknown or when active voice would be misleading; default to active, specific, attributed prose.

## Common Traps

### Strong verbs that imply more than the evidence
"Refused," "admitted," "conceded," "slammed," and "confessed" feel vivid but carry interpretive weight the facts may not support. The trap is that strong verbs make prose lively, but they smuggle judgment.

### Loaded adjectives that editorialize
"Controversial," "explosive," "stunning," and similar words add drama the facts may not warrant. The trap is that they generate engagement while substituting the writer's assessment for evidence.

### Truncated or context-stripped quotes
Selecting a fragment that changes the speaker's meaning misrepresents them. The trap is that the sharper fragment makes a better quote, but it is no longer what they said.

### Vague hedging as false precision
"Reportedly" and "allegedly" applied without basis let the writer imply doubt or distance without taking responsibility. The trap is that hedging reads as careful while conveying nothing concrete.

### Passive voice to avoid attribution
"Mistakes were made" and "it was alleged" obscure agency and sourcing. The trap is that passive constructions feel neutral while hiding who is responsible.

### Cleaning quotes without disclosure
Lightly editing quotes for readability is sometimes acceptable, but substantive alteration without disclosure misleads readers about the speaker's words. The trap is that the cleaned version reads better, but it is no longer verbatim.

### Paraphrase presented as quotation
Presenting the journalist's summary inside quotation marks implies the source said those exact words. The trap is that the formulation is cleaner than what was actually said, but the quotation marks promise fidelity they do not deliver.

## Self-Check

- Is attribution strength matched to evidentiary strength for each claim, with strong claims backed by strong evidence?
- Are verbs chosen to describe rather than interpret, with neutral "said" preferred over loaded alternatives unless the evidence supports the stronger word?
- Have loaded adjectives and evaluative language been stripped in favor of documented facts that carry their own weight?
- Are quotes accurate, in context, and free of truncation or stitching that changes meaning, with material cleaning disclosed?
- Is paraphrase clearly distinguished from direct quotation, with no blurring of the boundary?
- Is hedging calibrated to genuine uncertainty rather than used to dodge attribution or imply false doubt?
- Is uncertainty stated specifically, naming what is unknown and why, rather than performed with vague phrases?
- Has passive voice been replaced with active, attributed constructions, and have editors reviewed language in legally sensitive stories where wording could imply guilt, intent, or misconduct?
