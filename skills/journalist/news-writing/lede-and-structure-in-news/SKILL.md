# Lede And Structure In News

A news story is not just a container for facts; it is a sequence of information experienced by a reader under time pressure. The lede and structure determine whether readers understand what happened, why it matters, and what to pay attention to, often within the first few seconds. The harm this skill prevents is the two opposite failures of news structure: the flat recitation that delivers facts competently but never conveys significance, leaving readers uninformed about why they should care, and the over-engineered or sensationalized structure that misleads about importance, buries essential information for dramatic effect, or distorts the relative weight of facts through ordering. Strong news structure is the discipline of putting the right information first, in the right proportion, so that a reader who stops at any point has received the most important things first.

Use this skill when drafting any news story, choosing a lede, deciding structure, and ordering paragraphs. Consult editors on structural choices for high-stakes or complex stories where misordering could distort understanding.

## Core Rules

### Put the news first, where "the news" is what is new and most significant
The lede should convey the most newsworthy and significant element of the story: what happened, what changed, what the reader most needs to know. This sounds obvious but is frequently violated when writers lead with context, atmosphere, a minor detail, or a dramatic scene that is not actually the news. Identify the single most important new development and make it the spine of the lede. If the most newsworthy element is buried in paragraph six, the structure has failed, no matter how well-written the opening.

### Write a lede that is accurate, specific, and free of overclaim
The lede carries the most weight and therefore bears the strictest accuracy obligation. Avoid overstatement, vague language, unattributed generalization, and dramatic framing the body does not support. "Officials say" in the lede must be backed by named officials in the body. A lede that implies causation, scale, or certainty beyond the evidence misleads every reader who never reads past it, which is most of them. Specificity, real names, real numbers, real places, makes a lede more informative and more credible than abstraction.

### Choose the structure that serves the story, not a default template
The inverted pyramid, most important first in declining order of importance, serves breaking news and time-pressed readers well, but it is not the only structure. A thematic structure may serve an analytical piece; a chronological structure may serve a narrative of events where sequence carries meaning; a question-and-answer or explainer structure may serve a complex policy story. Choose the structure that best conveys this story's significance to this audience. Defaulting to a single template produces competent but lifeless coverage of stories that deserved a different shape.

### Establish significance early, even in longer pieces
Readers decide quickly whether to continue. Even in a piece that develops slowly, signal the stakes, the conflict, or the consequence within the opening, rather than withholding significance until late. This is not sensationalism; it is respect for the reader's time. If the significance is genuinely complex, signal the complexity early rather than hiding it. A reader who never reaches paragraph twelve should still understand why this story matters.

### Order context to support, not bury, the news
Context, history, background, and explanation are essential, but they should be ordered to help readers understand the news, not to delay it. Provide the minimum context needed to make the lede intelligible, then develop deeper context as the story unfolds. A common failure is the long contextual windup that delays the actual news, losing readers who came for what happened. Another failure is the absence of context that leaves the news unintelligible. Calibrate context to comprehension.

### Attribute and hedge in proportion to the claim
Strong, documented facts can be stated firmly; allegations, estimates, interpretations, and disputed claims need visible attribution and appropriate hedging. Match the language to the evidence throughout the structure, not only in the lede. "The company will lay off 500 workers" requires a different evidentiary basis than "The company is expected to consider layoffs, according to a person familiar with the discussions." Structural choices about where to place attribution and hedging shape how solid the story reads.

### Reconcile narrative flow with the duty to inform
Storytelling techniques must never displace the core obligation to convey essential facts clearly. A beautifully structured piece that leaves readers confused about the basic who, what, when, and where has failed. Test the draft against whether a reader comes away both informed and engaged, and revise toward both. Narrative flow and informational completeness are not enemies; they are partners, and the structure must serve both.

### Revise structure with the facts firmly in hand
Structure is a function of editing, applied after the reporting is solid. Restructuring can clarify, but it cannot create significance the reporting did not establish. If the story lacks a satisfying structure, the answer may be more reporting, not more artful arrangement of thin material. Do not impose a dramatic arc the facts do not support.

## Common Traps

### Burying the lede under context or atmosphere
A contextual or atmospheric opening delays the news and loses readers who came for what happened. The trap is that the windup feels like good writing, but it fails the reader's primary need.

### The sensationalized lede the body does not support
A dramatic lede generates clicks but misleads readers about the story's actual weight. The trap is that the dramatic version is more engaging, but the gap between lede and body destroys credibility.

### Defaulting to inverted pyramid for every story
Applying the same structure regardless of story type produces competent but undifferentiated coverage. The trap is that the template is safe and fast, but it under-serves stories that needed a different shape.

### Over-attributing or under-attributing
Excessive hedging makes straightforward facts sound uncertain; missing attribution makes contested claims sound settled. The trap is that calibration requires judgment about each claim, and defaults in either direction mislead.

### Imposing a false narrative arc
Forcing a dramatic structure onto events that do not have that shape distorts the truth. The trap is that the arc is more satisfying than the messy reality, but readers sense the manipulation.

### Structure disconnected from reporting
Arranging thin reporting into an impressive-looking structure produces a hollow piece. The trap is that good structure can disguise thin substance, but only until a reader or a competitor notices.

### Over-narrativizing routine news
Not every story needs dramatic structure. The trap is that forcing narrative ambition onto routine coverage inflates significance and wastes reader attention.

## Self-Check

- Does the lede convey the most newsworthy and significant element, rather than context, atmosphere, or a minor detail?
- Is the lede accurate, specific, and free of overclaim, with every assertion supported by the body?
- Is the structure chosen to serve this story's significance, rather than defaulting to a single template?
- Is the significance established early, even in a piece that develops slowly, so readers who stop early still understand why it matters?
- Is context ordered to support comprehension rather than to bury the news, with the minimum needed to make the lede intelligible?
- Are attribution and hedging calibrated to the evidentiary strength of each claim throughout the structure?
- Does the piece leave readers both clearly informed of essential facts and engaged by the telling, with narrative flow reconciled to the duty to inform?
- Is the structure grounded in solid reporting rather than imposed to compensate for thin substance, and have editors reviewed structural choices for high-stakes or complex stories where misordering could distort understanding?
