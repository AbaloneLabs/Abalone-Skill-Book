# Quoting And Paraphrasing In News

Quotes and paraphrases are how a news story carries the voices of its sources, and they are among the most scrutinized elements of journalism. A direct quote asserts that these exact words were spoken; a paraphrase asserts that this is a fair representation of what was meant. Both are factual claims, and both can mislead. The judgment problem is that the choice between quoting and paraphrasing, the cleaning and condensing of spoken language, and the framing of attribution all shape meaning in ways that are easy to get wrong and hard to detect. A quote slightly trimmed can reverse a speaker's intent; a paraphrase that drifts can attribute a view the source never held; a cleaned-up quote can make a halting speaker sound decisive or a careful speaker sound careless. These errors erode trust, trigger corrections, and can cause real harm to subjects and to the publication's credibility.

Use this skill when incorporating quotes or paraphrases into a news story, deciding what to quote directly, cleaning or condensing quotes, attributing paraphrases, or ensuring that spoken material is represented accurately and in context. The main risk is that an agent treats quotation as transcription and paraphrase as summary, without recognizing that both are interpretive acts with accuracy obligations.

## Core Rules

### Quote Against The Original Recording Or Faithful Transcript

A direct quote must reproduce what was actually said, verified against the original recording or a faithful transcript, not against memory or notes. Human memory conflates, reorders, and smooths spoken language, and notes taken in real time are inevitably incomplete. Before publishing a direct quote, confirm it against the recording word for word, including the small words and the punctuation that governs meaning. If no recording exists and the quote rests on notes, treat it with appropriate caution, confirm with the source where possible, and consider paraphrasing instead of presenting reconstructed words as a direct quote.

The obligation is to the words actually spoken, not to a polished version of them.

### Preserve Context That Governs Meaning

A quote is not only its words; it is its words in context. The same sentence can mean different things depending on what preceded it, the tone, the question that prompted it, and what the speaker assumed. Before quoting, confirm that the surrounding context supports the meaning the quote will carry in the story. A quote stripped of qualifying context can attribute to a speaker a position they did not hold, and this is among the most common and damaging quotation errors. Where context is necessary for the quote to be honest, provide it; where the quote cannot be honestly presented without misleading context, paraphrase or do not use it.

Sarcasm, conditionals, and hypothetical framing are especially vulnerable to context-stripping, because the words alone convey the opposite of the intent.

### Choose Direct Quote Versus Paraphrase By What Serves Accuracy

The choice between quoting directly and paraphrasing should serve accuracy and clarity, not convenience or effect. Quote directly when the exact words matter: when they are distinctive, revealing, contested, or legally significant, or when the speaker's specific phrasing carries meaning that a paraphrase would lose. Paraphrase when the substance matters more than the wording, when the original was rambling or unclear and a faithful paraphrase serves the reader better, or when the exact words are not sufficiently verified to quote directly. Do not quote directly for drama what should be paraphrased for accuracy, and do not paraphrase away the specificity that makes a quote valuable.

The decision should be explicit: is the value in the words or in the meaning?

### Clean And Condense Honestly, With Disclosure

Spoken language is messy: it contains false starts, repetitions, filler, and digressions. Some cleaning is accepted practice, but it must be honest. Removing filler words, condensing repetition, and combining sentences across a pause are generally acceptable when they preserve meaning; changing words, reordering clauses to change emphasis, merging separate statements to imply continuity, or inserting words the speaker did not say are not. Where material cleaning has occurred, disclose it with an ellipsis for omissions and, where conventions allow, a note that the quote has been condensed. When in doubt, paraphrase rather than present a heavily altered construction as a direct quote.

The test is whether the speaker would recognize the quote as a faithful representation of what they said and meant.

### Attribute Paraphrases Honestly And Specifically

A paraphrase asserts that this is what the source meant, and it must be attributed clearly so the reader knows whose view it is. Vague attribution, "sources said," "officials indicated," blurs who is making the claim and weakens accountability. Attribute as specifically as the sourcing allows: name the source where possible, describe their position and basis for knowledge where anonymity is warranted, and distinguish what the source stated from what the reporter infers. A paraphrase that drifts into the reporter's interpretation, unattributed, presents the reporter's view as the source's, which is both inaccurate and a form of editorializing disguised as reporting.

Match the hedge to the evidence: a paraphrase of a careful statement should not become a confident assertion.

### Do Not Manufacture Quotes Or Composite Speakers

Every quote must come from a real, identifiable exchange with a real source. Manufacturing quotes, inventing dialogue, or compositing statements from multiple sources into a single attributed quote are among the most serious violations of journalistic integrity, because they present fiction as fact. Even where the substance is accurate, attributing composite or reconstructed material to a specific source as a direct quote is fabrication. If a scene or exchange cannot be verified, do not present it as quotation; use paraphrase, attribute to the record, or omit.

This rule admits no exception for color, narrative effect, or the desire to make a story read better.

### Handle Partial, Unclear, And Off-Record Material Carefully

Spoken material often arrives partial, unclear, or under an off-record or background agreement. A partial quote must be presented so the partial nature is clear, not smoothed into a complete thought. An unclear statement should be clarified with the source rather than interpreted into confidence. Material shared off the record or on background must be used only according to the agreement, and never presented as a direct quote without the source's permission and clear attribution. The boundaries of the agreement govern what may be quoted and how; see the off-the-record skills.

## Common Traps

### Quoting From Memory Or Notes Rather Than The Recording

Memory and notes distort spoken language. This is a trap because the reconstructed quote feels right and is wrong. Verify direct quotes against the recording or faithful transcript.

### Stripping Context That Changes Meaning

A quote without its qualifying context can reverse intent. This is a trap because the words are accurate but the meaning is false. Preserve the context that governs meaning.

### Cleaning Quotes Until They Misrepresent

Excessive cleaning can make a speaker say something cleaner, clearer, or different from what they said. This is a trap because the result looks like a quote and misleads. Clean honestly, with disclosure, or paraphrase.

### Paraphrasing Away Specificity

Reducing a precise, revealing statement to a vague summary loses the value of the original. This is a trap because the paraphrase is technically fair but uninformative. Quote directly when the words carry the meaning.

### Vague Attribution Disguising The Source Of A Claim

"It is believed" or "sources say" without specificity weakens accountability and can mask the reporter's own view. This is a trap because the reader cannot judge the claim's basis. Attribute paraphrases specifically.

### Manufacturing Or Compositing Quotes

Inventing or merging dialogue into a single attributed quote is fabrication. This is a trap because it presents fiction as documented fact. Every quote must come from a real, verifiable exchange.

### Presenting Conditionals As Declarations

A hypothetical or conditional statement quoted as a declaration misrepresents the speaker. This is a trap because the words match but the framing does not. Preserve the conditional nature in context.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is every direct quote verified against the original recording or a faithful transcript, word for word, rather than reconstructed from memory or notes?
- [ ] Does each quote retain the context necessary for it to carry its intended meaning, with sarcasm, conditionals, and hypotheticals preserved rather than stripped?
- [ ] Was the choice between direct quote and paraphrase made to serve accuracy and clarity, quoting where words matter and paraphrasing where meaning does?
- [ ] Where quotes were cleaned or condensed, was the cleaning honest, meaning-preserving, and disclosed, rather than altering emphasis or inserting words?
- [ ] Are paraphrases attributed specifically and honestly, distinguishing what the source said from what the reporter infers, with hedges matched to evidence?
- [ ] Is every quote in the story traceable to a real, identifiable exchange, with no manufactured, composite, or reconstructed dialogue presented as direct quotation?
- [ ] Is partial, unclear, or off-record material handled according to its nature and the applicable agreement, never presented as direct quote without permission?
- [ ] Before publication, is there any quote whose accuracy rests on reconstruction rather than verification, or whose meaning shifts when read in full context?
