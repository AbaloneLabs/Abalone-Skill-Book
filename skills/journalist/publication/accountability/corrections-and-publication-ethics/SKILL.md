# Corrections And Publication Ethics

Publication is not the end of journalism. It is the moment when reporting enters public life and can inform, harm, expose, distort, pressure, or correct institutions. A journalist must decide not only whether the facts are supported, but whether the story is ready, fairly framed, transparently sourced, independent, and accountable after release.

Use this skill before publishing or finalizing any journalistic output, especially work involving allegations, private individuals, minors, sensitive images, anonymous sources, public records, health or safety claims, conflicts of interest, sponsored material, or fast-moving updates. The main risk is that an agent may optimize for a complete answer while skipping publication consequences, correction duty, labeling, independence, and post-publication responsibility.

## Core Rules

### Treat Publication As A Public Commitment

Publishing means the newsroom or journalist is standing behind the work. Do not publish merely because the draft is coherent, the topic is trending, or competitors have already published. Ask whether the piece is accurate enough, fair enough, necessary enough, and transparent enough for the audience to rely on.

Before publication, identify:

- the central public-interest value;
- the strongest evidence;
- the weakest important claim;
- unresolved disputes;
- potential harm;
- who was contacted for response;
- what remains unknown;
- what would require correction or update;
- who is responsible for monitoring new information.

If the story cannot survive this review, narrow it, hold it, or label it more carefully.

### Make Corrections Prompt, Visible, And Specific

Errors happen. Accountability depends on correcting them clearly. A correction should tell the audience what was wrong and what has changed. It should not bury the mistake, use vague language, or quietly rewrite material that materially changed.

Use a correction when a published fact was wrong. Use a clarification when the original wording was incomplete or potentially misleading but not strictly false. Use an update when new information has emerged after publication. Do not use "update" to avoid acknowledging an error.

Correction notes should usually include:

- the date or time of correction;
- the incorrect information;
- the accurate information;
- whether headline, caption, graphic, social copy, or article text changed;
- whether the error affected the story's conclusion.

For serious errors, consider an editor's note explaining process failure and review steps.

### Preserve Transparency Without Exposing Sources

Audiences need enough sourcing transparency to judge credibility. That does not require exposing confidential sources, private victims, minors, or sensitive records. The publication should describe the evidence base as much as possible without creating unnecessary harm.

Transparency can include:

- links to public documents;
- data methodology;
- interview count and source categories;
- reason anonymity was granted;
- limitations of records;
- attempts to obtain comment;
- whether images or audio were verified;
- whether AI tools, data analysis, translation, or reconstruction were used;
- conflicts or affiliations relevant to the work.

Do not reveal protected source details for performative transparency. Explain the basis without breaking promises or creating danger.

### Check Independence And Conflicts

Journalism serves the public, not sources, advertisers, donors, political actors, owners, friends, employers, clients, or the journalist's personal interest. Conflicts can be financial, relational, ideological, professional, familial, political, or reputational.

Before publication, ask:

- Does the reporter, editor, outlet, funder, or partner have a stake in the outcome?
- Was access, travel, data, product, hospitality, or payment provided by a subject?
- Does sponsored or partner content need clearer labeling?
- Are advertisers, donors, owners, or internal leaders pressuring the story?
- Has the journalist expressed public advocacy that affects credibility?
- Is disclosure enough, or should someone else handle the story?

Disclosure does not cure every conflict. Some conflicts require reassignment, recusal, additional review, or non-publication.

### Label Content Clearly

Audiences should not have to guess whether they are reading news, analysis, opinion, review, satire, sponsored content, advocacy, user-generated material, archival material, reconstruction, or AI-assisted output. Blurred labels damage trust.

Label:

- opinion and commentary;
- sponsored or branded content;
- advertising-like partnerships;
- analysis that goes beyond straight reporting;
- archival photos or file video;
- illustrations, re-enactments, composites, or generated images;
- translated material when relevant;
- corrections, updates, and editor's notes;
- unverified or partially verified material if publication is justified.

The label must be visible where the audience encounters the content, including social posts, newsletters, video captions, graphics, and embeds.

### Weigh Harm Before Publishing Sensitive Details

Legal access does not settle ethical publication. Public records, court filings, police reports, leaked documents, social media posts, and images may contain details that are true but unnecessary or dangerous.

Review whether to include:

- home addresses;
- phone numbers or emails;
- medical details;
- immigration status;
- names of minors;
- names of sexual assault victims;
- graphic images;
- suicide methods;
- identifying details of vulnerable sources;
- personal records unrelated to the public-interest issue;
- old allegations that were dismissed or unproven;
- criminal history not relevant to the current story.

Ask whether each sensitive detail changes public understanding. If not, remove it, generalize it, or explain why it is withheld.

### Review Legal And Safety Risk Without Letting Fear Replace Judgment

Publication may raise defamation, privacy, contempt, copyright, source-protection, election, court-order, national security, employment, or physical safety issues. The journalist should identify these risks early and seek qualified review where needed.

For allegations of wrongdoing, check:

- factual basis;
- attribution;
- response opportunity;
- wording of certainty;
- public/private status of the subject;
- actual malice risk in relevant jurisdictions;
- document reliability;
- whether opinion implies undisclosed defamatory facts.

Legal review should improve precision. It should not be used to bury newsworthy reporting solely because it is uncomfortable.

### Plan Post-Publication Monitoring

Some stories require follow-up. Breaking news changes. Investigations provoke responses. Data stories may reveal errors. Sources may face retaliation. Readers may provide corrections. Officials may release new records. The journalist should know who watches the story after publication.

Plan:

- correction intake path;
- reader contact method;
- monitoring for official updates;
- source safety follow-up when appropriate;
- social copy correction if the error traveled there;
- archive or SEO update if old versions remain visible;
- follow-up story criteria;
- takedown or unpublishing policy for rare cases.

Do not publish and disappear when the story can materially affect people's lives.

## Common Traps

### Silent Fixes

Quietly changing a material fact avoids embarrassment but damages trust. Correct visibly when the audience may have been misled. Minor typos that do not affect meaning can be fixed silently, but factual changes need accountability.

### Treating "Already Public" As Enough

Information can be public and still ethically unnecessary. A court database, mugshot site, social profile, livestream, or leaked spreadsheet may expose people beyond the original context. Publication amplifies.

### Letting Competitive Pressure Lower Standards

If competitors publish an unverified name, death count, motive, suspect identity, or accusation, that does not make it safe. Attribute what is known, hold what is not, and update when verification improves.

### Burying The Method

Complex stories need methodology. Without it, audiences cannot judge whether a data conclusion, investigation, or fact-check is credible. Explain enough of the process to make the work inspectable.

### Overusing Editor's Notes As Shields

An editor's note cannot repair weak reporting. If the story needs a long disclaimer to survive, consider whether the evidence is strong enough or the frame is too broad.

### Publishing AI Or Synthetic Material Without Disclosure

Generated images, reconstructed scenes, AI-assisted summaries, translated quotes, automated transcripts, and synthetic audio can mislead if not labeled. Use AI as a tool only when the output is checked and the audience is not deceived about what is real.

### Forgetting Non-Article Surfaces

Errors often spread through headlines, push alerts, captions, charts, newsletters, social posts, and video thumbnails. Correction duty applies to those surfaces too, not just the article body.

## Self-Check

Before treating publication as ready, verify:

- The public-interest reason is clear enough to justify publication and any foreseeable harm.
- Every central factual claim has evidence, attribution, and appropriate certainty language.
- The people or institutions facing serious criticism received a meaningful response opportunity.
- Anonymous sources are described as fully as safety permits and are not used for avoidable opinion or speculation.
- Sensitive private details have been removed unless they materially serve the public-interest purpose.
- Visuals, audio, graphics, social copy, headlines, captions, and thumbnails do not distort the story.
- Opinion, analysis, sponsored content, archival material, generated material, reconstructions, and updates are clearly labeled.
- Conflicts of interest were checked for the reporter, outlet, funder, partner, and sources.
- Legal and safety risks were identified, and qualified review was sought when stakes require it.
- The story explains its method when conclusions depend on data, documents, investigation, fact-checking, translation, or reconstruction.
- A correction and update plan exists, including who monitors responses and new facts.
- Any post-publication changes that affect meaning will be noted visibly.
- Social posts and distributed versions can be corrected if the article changes.
- The publication does not expose vulnerable sources, private people, or minors through unnecessary direct or indirect identifiers.
- The final version is narrower where evidence is narrow and stronger only where evidence is strong.
