# Surveillance Awareness And Countermeasures

Surveillance is the silent adversary of sensitive reporting. It does not announce itself; it manifests as a strange feeling that someone knows too much, a source who is suddenly nervous, a device that behaves oddly, a meeting whose subject matter appears to leak. The judgment problem is that surveillance is designed to be invisible, and reporters who have not built awareness will not recognize it until it has already compromised sources, investigations, and safety. At the same time, paranoia without method is its own failure: seeing surveillance where none exists distorts reporting and damages relationships. Effective surveillance awareness is disciplined pattern recognition paired with proportionate countermeasures, calibrated to the realistic capabilities of whoever might be watching.

Use this skill when assessing whether surveillance is occurring, recognizing its signs, deploying countermeasures, understanding adversary capabilities, or deciding how surveillance risk should reshape a reporting plan. The main risk is that an agent either ignores surveillance indicators until compromise is obvious or overreacts to ordinary events as surveillance, both of which produce bad decisions.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Realistic Capabilities Of Likely Adversaries

Surveillance capabilities vary enormously by adversary, and an effective response depends on understanding who might be watching and what they can do. A local police force, a private investigator, an organized crime group, a corporate security firm, a national intelligence service, and a state actor in a repressive regime have vastly different tools, from physical following and trash pulls to IMSI catchers, spyware, network intrusion, and bulk metadata collection. Calibrate awareness and countermeasures to the realistic adversary, not to a generic worst case.

Build knowledge of the capabilities relevant to your beat and reporting context. For domestic accountability reporting, the realistic threats may include employer monitoring of source devices, subpoenas for communications records, and occasional physical surveillance. For reporting that touches state interests, organized crime, or powerful corporations, the capabilities escalate to targeted spyware, network exploitation, and sophisticated social engineering. Match the response to the actual adversary, because over-securing against a weak threat wastes effort and under-securing against a strong one invites compromise.

### Recognize Surveillance Indicators Through Pattern, Not Single Events

No single event proves surveillance; patterns do. A source who reports being followed, a colleague who mentions odd questions from strangers, a device that drains battery unusually fast, a meeting whose substance appears to leak, a series of too-convenient encounters, or a source who is suddenly detained or questioned can each have innocent explanations. Together, and especially when they cluster around a specific investigation, they form a pattern worth taking seriously.

Build the habit of noting anomalies and reviewing them periodically rather than dismissing each in isolation. Surveillance is designed to be explained away, and the instinct to rationalize odd events is exactly what it relies on. A pattern log, even a simple one, turns isolated oddities into recognizable signal. Share concerns with editors and, where warranted, with security specialists who can assess whether the pattern reflects genuine surveillance.

### Treat Device Compromise As A Leading Threat

For most reporters, the most likely and most damaging surveillance vector is device compromise: spyware that reads messages and tracks location, malware that exfiltrates files, or network tools that intercept communications. The indicators are often subtle: unusual battery drain, unexpected data usage, devices running hot, apps behaving oddly, or the receipt of a suspicious message that may have carried a exploit. Some advanced spyware leaves no detectable trace on the device at all.

Countermeasures include keeping devices updated, avoiding clicking unknown links, using hardware security keys, and for high-risk contexts, using clean or disposable devices, avoiding carrying sensitive material on everyday devices, and seeking forensic analysis if compromise is suspected. Recognize that no device used for routine communication is fully secure against the most capable adversaries, and plan accordingly. See the device-and-account-security skill.

### Deploy Counter-Surveillance Proportionate To The Threat

Counter-surveillance measures range from routine hygiene to intensive operational security, and they should be proportionate to the threat. Routine hygiene, strong authentication, encryption, phishing awareness, and compartmentalization, is baseline for any sensitive reporting. More intensive measures, clean devices, in-person meetings without devices, varied routes and timing, counter-surveillance routes to detect following, and professional security support, are warranted only where the threat justifies the overhead.

Proportionality matters because excessive security measures can themselves become a signal, can alienate sources who find them onerous, and can consume effort that would be better spent on the reporting. A reporter who treats every story as a state-level intelligence threat will produce less journalism; one who treats no story that way will eventually be compromised. Calibrate deliberately to the realistic adversary.

### Change The Reporting Plan When Surveillance Is Suspected

If surveillance is suspected or confirmed, the reporting plan may need to change, not just the security measures. A channel that is compromised cannot be repaired by adding encryption; it must be abandoned. A source who is being watched may need to go quiet, relocate, or be protected by being removed from the story. An investigation whose existence has been detected may need to accelerate, decelerate, or restructure to protect sources and the outcome. Security is not a layer added on top of a fixed plan; surveillance risk should reshape the plan itself.

Involve editors in these decisions, because they carry consequences for the story, the sources, and the newsroom. Do not treat surveillance as a personal security problem to handle alone; it is a reporting problem that affects the entire project. See the source-protection skill for protecting compromised sources.

### Document And Share Surveillance Concerns

Document suspected surveillance, with dates, specifics, and the pattern as it develops. This documentation supports assessment by security specialists, supports any later reporting on press freedom violations, and helps other reporters recognize similar patterns. Share concerns within the newsroom and with peer networks, because adversaries often target multiple reporters and outlets, and shared awareness strengthens everyone's defense.

Documentation also disciplines the assessment. Writing down the specifics forces honest evaluation of whether the pattern is real or imagined, and it creates a record that distinguishes genuine surveillance from ordinary coincidence. A pattern that looks compelling in the moment may look different on review, and a pattern that looks dismissible in the moment may look serious in aggregate.

### Engage Specialist Support For Serious Suspicions

For serious surveillance suspicions, engage specialist support: the newsroom's security advisor, organizations that investigate journalist surveillance, forensic analysts who can assess device compromise, and legal counsel where surveillance may be illegal or where communications records may be subpoenaed. Do not attempt to assess or counter sophisticated surveillance alone; the capabilities involved exceed what an individual reporter can match, and specialist support is often available through press freedom organizations.

Specialist engagement is not an admission of failure; it is the proportionate response to a serious threat. A reporter who suspects state-level surveillance and tries to handle it personally is making the same error as a reporter who tries to set a broken bone without a doctor.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Anomalies In Isolation

Surveillance is designed to be explained away event by event. Review anomalies as a pattern over time, not as isolated coincidences.

### Treating Every Odd Event As Surveillance

Paranoia without method distorts reporting and damages relationships. Calibrate to realistic adversary capabilities and look for patterns, not single events.

### Underestimating Device Compromise

The most likely and most damaging surveillance vector is device compromise. Treat device security as central, not peripheral, and seek forensic analysis when compromise is suspected.

### Over-Securing Against A Weak Threat

Excessive measures waste effort, alienate sources, and can themselves become a signal. Calibrate countermeasures to the realistic adversary.

### Trying To Repair A Compromised Channel

A channel that is compromised cannot be fixed by adding encryption; it must be abandoned, and the reporting plan may need to change.

### Handling Serious Suspicions Alone

Sophisticated surveillance exceeds what an individual can counter. Engage specialist support for serious suspicions rather than attempting a solo response.

## Self-Check

Before treating surveillance awareness and countermeasures as sound, verify:

- The realistic capabilities of likely adversaries for the beat and reporting context are understood, and awareness is calibrated to them rather than to a generic worst case.
- Surveillance indicators are recognized through patterns over time, documented in a pattern log, rather than dismissed in isolation or overreacted to as single events.
- Device compromise is treated as a leading threat, with baseline hygiene, clean devices for high-risk contexts, and forensic analysis sought when compromise is suspected.
- Counter-surveillance measures are proportionate to the threat, avoiding both under-securing against serious adversaries and over-securing against weak ones.
- The reporting plan is changed, not just the security layer, when surveillance is suspected or confirmed, with editors involved in the consequences for story, sources, and newsroom.
- Suspected surveillance is documented with specifics and shared within the newsroom and peer networks to support assessment and collective defense.
- Specialist support, security advisors, forensic analysts, press freedom organizations, and counsel, is engaged for serious suspicions rather than attempting a solo response.
- No compromised channel is repaired by adding encryption; compromised channels are abandoned and the plan restructured.
- The response is calibrated to the actual adversary and the actual evidence, not to fear or to convenience.
- Surveillance awareness supports editorial judgment; significant surveillance suspicions and countermeasure decisions should involve a responsible editor and specialist security and legal advisors, recognizing that surveillance capabilities may exceed what individuals can counter and that no response is guaranteed.
