# Geolocation And Timestamping

Knowing where and when something happened is foundational to verifying visual evidence. A real image of the wrong place, or the right place at the wrong time, can mislead as thoroughly as a forgery. Geolocation establishes the location depicted; timestamping establishes when. The harm this skill prevents is the publication of accurately-captioned-as-to-content but misattributed-as-to-place-or-time visuals, which can place events incorrectly, distort chronologies, and provide false corroboration for narratives. Geolocation and timestamping are crafts of patient observation, cross-referencing visible details against maps, weather records, and astronomical data, and they require both rigor and humility about what the evidence can and cannot establish.

## Core Rules

### Extract every locatable and timeable detail from the image or video
Systematically inventory the visual evidence: landmarks, signage, architecture, vegetation, street furniture, vehicles, language on signs, business names, utility poles, road markings, terrain, and shadows. Each detail is a potential clue to location or time. Thorough extraction is the foundation; overlooking a detail means missing a verification path.

### Cross-reference landmarks against maps and street-level imagery
Use satellite maps, street-level imagery, and 3D terrain data to match distinctive features, building shapes, unique structures, road configurations, coastlines. Work from the most unique features outward. Confirm matches through multiple independent visual references rather than a single apparent fit, which can be coincidental.

### Use signage, language, and cultural details to narrow the region
Scripts, languages, units, business names, telephone formats, vehicle license plates, and architectural styles narrow the geographic region and often the specific country or city. These details are especially valuable when landmarks are ambiguous. Combine them with mapping to converge on a location.

### Verify time through shadows, solar position, and weather
Shadows indicate sun direction and angle, which correspond to time of day and season for a given location; weather and sky conditions can be checked against historical weather records. Cross-reference these against the claimed time. Consistency supports the claimed time; inconsistency challenges it. Use solar calculation tools rather than estimating by eye.

### Establish consistency, not just possibility
Geolocation and timestamping must show that the details are consistent with the claimed location and time, and ideally inconsistent with alternatives. A single matching feature may be coincidental; a convergence of multiple independent features is strong. Pursue enough independent points of consistency to rule out reasonable alternative locations or times.

### Triangulate with independent reporting
Visual geolocation and timestamping are strongest when corroborated by independent evidence, witness accounts, other recordings, official records, or news reports from the same event. Triangulation confirms the visual analysis and catches errors that a single method might miss.

### Preserve the analysis and document the reasoning
Record the details identified, the tools and references used, the matches found, and the conclusion, with screenshots and links. This documentation supports the verification claim and allows others to check the work. Geolocation that cannot be reconstructed cannot be defended.

### Recognize the limits and avoid overclaiming
Sometimes the evidence supports only a probable location or a time range, not a precise point. State what the analysis establishes and what remains uncertain. Overclaiming precision that the evidence does not support undermines credibility when the limits are exposed.

## Common Traps

### Matching a single landmark and declaring victory
One matching feature can be coincidental, especially with common architecture or terrain. Confirm through multiple, independent features before claiming a location. The more unique the features that match, the stronger the conclusion.

### Ignoring that authentic footage can be from a different event
Real footage of a real location can be presented as depicting a different event at that location. Geolocation confirms where; it does not confirm what is happening or when. Combine with timestamping and content verification.

### Trusting embedded timestamps and metadata blindly
Metadata can be spoofed, stripped, or set incorrectly, and device clocks are often wrong. Treat embedded timestamps as one data point to be checked against environmental evidence, not as authoritative. Inconsistency between metadata and shadows or weather is a red flag.

### Overlooking seasonal and temporal details
Vegetation state, snow, foliage, shadow length, and weather place an image in a season and often a time of day. Ignoring these details misses verification opportunities and can lead to accepting an image as recent when seasonal evidence shows otherwise.

### Confusing similar-looking locations
Many places look alike, especially in generic urban or natural environments. A plausible match is not a confirmed match. Require enough unique, converging detail to distinguish the specific location from similar ones.

### Estimating shadow and solar analysis by eye
Visual estimation of sun position is unreliable. Use solar calculation tools that compute sun angle and direction for a given location, date, and time, and compare precisely against the shadows in the image.

### Claiming precision the evidence cannot support
When details are ambiguous, claiming a precise location or exact time overstates the analysis. State the supported conclusion with its actual precision, including ranges and probabilities, and disclose the uncertainty.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I systematically inventoried all locatable and timeable details in the image or video?
- [ ] Did I cross-reference distinctive landmarks against maps and street-level imagery, confirming through multiple independent features?
- [ ] Have I used signage, language, plates, and cultural details to narrow and confirm the region?
- [ ] Did I verify time through shadow and solar analysis, checked with calculation tools, and against historical weather records?
- [ ] Have I established enough converging consistency to rule out reasonable alternative locations and times?
- [ ] Did I triangulate the visual analysis with independent reporting, witness accounts, or other recordings?
- [ ] Is the full analysis documented with details, tools, references, and conclusions, so it can be reconstructed?
- [ ] Have I accounted for the possibility that authentic footage is misattributed to a different event or time?
- [ ] Did I treat embedded metadata as a checkable data point rather than authoritative?
- [ ] Before publication, am I claiming only the location and time precision the evidence actually supports, with uncertainty disclosed?
