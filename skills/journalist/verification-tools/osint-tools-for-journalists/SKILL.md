# OSINT Tools For Journalists

Open source intelligence, the systematic use of publicly available information to investigate and verify, has become a core journalistic method. Satellite imagery, social media, corporate registries, flight trackers, and public databases can reveal what reporting on the ground cannot. The harm this skill prevents is twofold: neglecting these powerful tools and missing stories that were hiding in public view, and misusing them by treating search results as verified fact, violating privacy or law, or producing conclusions the public data cannot actually support. OSINT is a discipline of methodical search, careful verification, documented process, and ethical restraint, not a set of magic queries.

## Core Rules

### Treat OSINT as a method requiring documentation, not ad-hoc searching
Effective OSINT is systematic: define what you are looking for, identify the relevant sources, search methodically, and record what you found, when, and how. Screenshots with timestamps, archived pages, and saved queries are the evidence chain. Undocumented searching produces findings you cannot defend or reconstruct, especially as online content changes or disappears.

### Verify before relying, and corroborate across independent sources
Public information is often partial, outdated, misattributed, or fabricated. A social media profile, a registry entry, or a satellite image is a lead until verified. Cross-check OSINT findings against other independent sources, documents, testimony, or records, before treating them as established. The accessibility of the information does not guarantee its accuracy.

### Preserve and archive what you find
Online content changes and disappears, sometimes deliberately when subjects realize they are being investigated. Archive pages through archival services, save screenshots with timestamps and URLs, and retain the raw material. Preservation protects your findings against later denial or alteration and supports the story's defensibility.

### Understand the limits and biases of each source type
Every OSINT source has blind spots. Social media overrepresents certain populations and can be manipulated; corporate registries lag behind reality; satellite imagery depends on cloud cover, revisit time, and resolution. Know what each source can and cannot show, and do not infer absence of evidence from a source's limitations.

### Protect subjects' privacy and avoid doxxing
OSINT can expose personal information about private individuals who are not public figures and not central to the story. Distinguish what is genuinely in the public interest from voyeuristic or harmful exposure. Apply the same privacy and minimize-harm standards to OSINT-derived information as to any other reporting, and avoid publishing personal details, addresses, or identifying information unrelated to the story.

### Combine OSINT with traditional reporting
OSINT is most powerful when integrated with documents, interviews, and on-the-ground reporting. It surfaces leads, corroborates accounts, and provides evidence, but the why and the human significance usually require traditional journalism. Treat OSINT as one method in a larger toolkit, not a replacement for reporting.

### Be aware of legal and ethical constraints
Accessing publicly available information is generally lawful, but some actions, using stolen credentials, violating terms of service in ways that raise legal questions, or deceiving subjects to gain access, can create legal or ethical exposure. Understand the boundaries in your jurisdiction and consult editors or lawyers for edge cases.

### Disclose your methods appropriately
Tell readers, at a level of detail that does not compromise sources or techniques, how you obtained and verified the information. Transparency about OSINT method strengthens credibility and lets readers assess the evidence. Opaque claims of "open source research showed" invite skepticism; documented method invites trust.

## Common Traps

### Treating a search result as verified fact
Finding information online is not the same as confirming it. Profiles are impersonated, registries contain errors, and content is miscaptioned. Verify each OSINT finding before relying on it.

### Confirmation bias in searching
It is easy to find evidence supporting a hypothesis and overlook contradictory information, especially when using targeted queries. Actively search for disconfirming evidence and test alternative explanations for what the OSINT appears to show.

### Overstating what the data can prove
OSINT can establish that something exists, appears, or is connected; it often cannot establish intent, causation, or the full picture. Claim more than the public information supports and you invite correction and credibility damage. Distinguish what the OSINT shows from what you infer.

### Ignoring manipulation and inauthentic content
Social media and public databases are subject to coordinated inauthentic behavior, fake accounts, astroturfing, and planted records. Consider whether what you found reflects reality or a manipulation campaign, and verify accordingly.

### Privacy violations and doxxing
The ability to find personal information does not justify publishing it. Exposing private individuals' details unrelated to the public interest causes harm and violates ethical standards, even when the information is technically public.

### Failing to preserve evidence
Relying on live web pages or social posts that can be edited or deleted leaves findings vulnerable to disappearance. Archive and timestamp everything you rely on.

### Opacity about method
Claiming findings from "open source research" without explaining the method prevents readers from assessing the evidence and invites skepticism. Document and disclose the process.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is my OSINT process documented, with sources, queries, timestamps, and archived evidence, so findings are reproducible and defensible?
- [ ] Have I verified each OSINT finding and corroborated it across independent sources before relying on it?
- [ ] Did I archive and preserve the online material I depend on, against alteration or disappearance?
- [ ] Do I understand the specific limits and biases of each source type I used, and avoid inferring from absence?
- [ ] Have I protected subjects' privacy, publishing only personal information genuinely in the public interest?
- [ ] Am I integrating OSINT with documents, interviews, and traditional reporting rather than treating it as self-sufficient?
- [ ] Have I stayed within legal and ethical bounds, consulting editors or lawyers for edge cases?
- [ ] Did I actively search for disconfirming evidence and alternative explanations, not only supporting material?
- [ ] Have I claimed only what the public information actually supports, distinguishing evidence from inference?
- [ ] Before publication, have I disclosed my OSINT methods at a level that lets readers assess the evidence without compromising sources?
