# Arts Sourcing And Access

Cultural journalism depends on access to artists, institutions, and productions, and that access is mediated by publicists, gatekeepers, and economic relationships that create distinctive pressures on independence and accuracy. The defining tension is that the people a cultural journalist most needs to reach, established artists, festival programmers, museum directors, label executives, are precisely those whose access is controlled by professionals whose job is to shape coverage favorably. Accept the access on its terms and coverage drifts toward promotion; push too hard and the access closes. The discipline is to build sourcing that does not depend solely on official channels, to handle publicist-mediated access without surrendering editorial control, to report on the labor and money behind cultural production rather than only the polished surface, and to maintain independence even when access, exclusives, and embargoes are dangled as incentives. Weak cultural journalism is recognizable by its dependence on press releases, granted interviews, and festival junkets; strong cultural journalism cultivates its own sources inside scenes, institutions, and industries, and reports what the official channels would prefer left unexamined.

Use this skill when reporting on arts and culture, sourcing interviews, managing publicist and institutional relationships, or navigating the access-and-independence tensions of cultural journalism. The goal is to prevent the agent from becoming a conduit for promoted access, and from neglecting the independent sourcing and labor-and-money reporting that distinguish real cultural journalism from publicity.

## Core Rules

### Build Sourcing Beyond Official Channels

Official channels, publicists, press offices, festival accreditation, are necessary but insufficient. Diversify sourcing:

- Independent sources: cultivate artists, technicians, administrators, critics, scholars, and audience members reachable without a publicist intermediary.
- Scene knowledge: develop enough familiarity with a scene or field to know who matters beyond the heavily promoted names.
- Institutional sources: current and former staff of arts organizations, funders, and industry bodies can speak to conditions the official line obscures.
- Documentary sourcing: tax filings, funding records, contracts where available, and production credits provide facts independent of press releases.

A journalist whose sources are only those provided by publicists is, functionally, an extension of the publicity apparatus. Independent sourcing is what makes the coverage journalism rather than promotion.

### Handle Publicist-Mediated Access Without Surrendering Control

Publicists control much of the access to prominent artists, and working with them requires protecting editorial independence:

- Editorial control: retain control over questions, framing, and what is published; do not agree to conditions (question approval, quote approval, positive coverage guarantees) that compromise independence.
- Embargoes and exclusives: weigh whether accepting an embargo or exclusive serves the reader or merely serves the promoter's timing; exclusives can be valuable but create dependence.
- Junkets and sponsored access: festival junkets, paid press trips, and sponsored access create conflicts that must be disclosed and, often, declined.
- Reciprocity awareness: publicists grant access in expectation of coverage; be honest with yourself about whether access is shaping what you cover and how.

Access is not free. The terms on which it is granted shape the coverage, and a journalist who does not actively protect independence will find it eroded by the access relationship.

### Report The Labor And Money Behind Cultural Production

Cultural production is work, funded by specific sources, performed under specific conditions. Report it:

- Funding and economics: who funds the artist, institution, or production (grants, investors, labels, studios, patrons), and what does the funding depend on?
- Labor conditions: how are the workers, performers, crew, writers, technicians, paid, and what are their working conditions?
- Gatekeeping and exclusion: who decides what gets made, shown, or funded, and who is systematically excluded?
- Institutional accountability: arts organizations are often nonprofits or publicly funded; their governance, finances, and decisions are legitimate subjects of reporting.

Coverage that addresses only the finished work and the artist's biography misses the conditions that determine what culture exists. Labor-and-money reporting is cultural journalism, not a separate business beat.

### Manage Relationships With Artists And Institutions Over Time

Cultural journalism is ongoing, and relationships with sources and subjects persist across stories:

- Independence across stories: a favorable profile this year must not prevent critical coverage next year; do not trade independence for relationship.
- Honesty with subjects: be clear about the nature of the coverage; do not mislead an artist or institution about whether the story will be favorable.
- Conflict awareness: personal relationships, financial ties, and prior coverage all create conflicts that must be disclosed or managed.
- Recusal when necessary: where a conflict cannot be managed, recuse from the coverage rather than compromise it.

The long horizon of cultural journalism means that short-term access decisions have long-term consequences for independence. Manage relationships with the next story in mind.

### Verify Claims From Promotional And Institutional Sources

Artists, publicists, and institutions make claims that serve their interests, and cultural journalism must verify them like any other:

- Biographical and achievement claims: sales figures, awards, attendance, and historical claims should be checked, not taken from press materials.
- Funding and financial claims: statements about budgets, funding sources, and finances can often be checked against filings or independent sources.
- Artistic claims: claims about originality, influence, or significance are interpretations; attribute them rather than presenting them as fact.

Promotional materials are starting points for verification, not sources of record. A cultural journalist who repeats press-release claims without checking is doing the publicist's work.

### Protect Vulnerable Sources In Small Fields

The arts can be small worlds where sources face retaliation for speaking, and protection matters:

- Anonymity for legitimate reasons: sources who risk professional consequences (loss of work, blacklisting, harassment) may warrant anonymity, with clear explanation to the reader.
- On-record preference: prefer on-record sourcing where safety allows, and explain when anonymity is necessary.
- Context for retaliation risk: small fields, tight funding, and reputational economies make retaliation real; weigh it seriously.

Cultural fields are often smaller and more reputationally interconnected than they appear. Protect sources whose honesty could cost them their livelihood or standing.

## Common Traps

### Sourcing Only Through Publicists

Building coverage on granted interviews and press releases. The trap is that official access is easy, but it produces publicity, not journalism.

### Accepting Access Conditions That Compromise Independence

Question approval, quote approval, or coverage guarantees. The trap is that access seems worth the conditions, but the conditions make the coverage promotional.

### Covering Only The Surface, Not The Labor And Money

Profiles and reviews without the economics, labor, and gatekeeping behind the work. The trap is that the surface is what's promoted, but it misses the story.

### Trading Independence For Relationship

Soft coverage to preserve access to an artist or institution. The trap is that access yields stories, but it erodes the independence that gives the stories value.

### Repeating Promotional Claims Unchecked

Sales, attendance, and achievement claims taken from press materials. The trap is that the claims are convenient, but unverified claims are publicity repeated as fact.

### Exposing Vulnerable Sources In Small Fields

Naming sources who face retaliation in tight, reputationally driven communities. The trap is that naming seems transparent, but it can destroy a source's career.

## Self-Check

- Is sourcing diversified beyond official channels, with independent sources, scene knowledge, institutional sources, and documentary evidence?
- Is publicist-mediated access handled with editorial control retained, embargoes and exclusives weighed, junkets disclosed or declined, and reciprocity honestly assessed?
- Does the coverage report the labor, funding, gatekeeping, and institutional accountability behind cultural production, not only the finished work?
- Are relationships with artists and institutions managed for independence across stories, with honesty about coverage nature and conflicts disclosed or recused?
- Are claims from promotional and institutional sources verified, with biographical, financial, and artistic claims checked or attributed rather than repeated?
- Are vulnerable sources in small, reputationally interconnected fields protected, with anonymity used for legitimate reasons and explained to the reader?
- Is the coverage recognizably journalism, with independent sourcing and verification, rather than a conduit for promoted access?
- Has editorial review confirmed that access relationships have not compromised independence, and that labor-and-money reporting is present where relevant?
