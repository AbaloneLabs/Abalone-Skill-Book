# Story Selection And News Judgment

News judgment is the decision of what becomes news and what does not, and it is the most consequential editorial act in journalism. Every day produces far more potential stories than any newsroom can cover, and the choices of which to pursue, which to lead with, and which to set aside shape what the public knows, what it ignores, and ultimately what it believes about the world. These choices are not neutral; they reflect and reinforce assumptions about what matters, who matters, and what counts as a story. The judgment problem is that news judgment is exercised under pressure, speed, resource limits, and audience metrics, all of which push toward the dramatic, the conflict-driven, the already-attention-grabbing, and the familiar, while the stories that most serve the public are often slower, less visually striking, and concerned with people and systems that lack existing prominence. A newsroom that selects stories by reflex rather than judgment becomes an amplifier of whatever is loud, and quietly abandons the work that only journalism can do.

Use this skill when deciding what to cover, ranking stories, judging newsworthiness, allocating reporting resources, or deciding whether to pursue a tip or lead. The main risk is that an agent lets habit, metrics, or the path of least resistance make the selection, producing coverage that is reactive, skewed, and disconnected from what most serves the audience and the public interest.

## Core Rules

### Judge Newsworthiness Against Explicit Criteria, Not Impression

The feeling that something "is a story" is shaped by habit, recency, and what is already receiving attention, and it is unreliable. Make the criteria for newsworthiness explicit so that selection can be examined rather than felt. Typical criteria include the significance of the impact, the number of people affected, the power and accountability at stake, the consequence of the information for audience decisions, the timeliness, the originality, and the degree to which the story reveals something hidden or corrects a misunderstanding. Apply these criteria deliberately to competing stories, and be willing to articulate why one story ranks above another. A selection that cannot be justified in explicit terms is a selection made by reflex.

Explicit criteria also expose systematic bias in what gets covered and what does not.

### Weigh Public Interest Against Mere Interest

A central tension in story selection is between what the public is interested in and what is in the public interest. The former drives attention and metrics; the latter drives civic value. Both matter, because journalism that no one reads serves no one, but a newsroom that selects only what drives attention abandons its public function. The discipline is to weigh both honestly: to seek stories that serve the public interest and to make them engaging enough to be read, rather than choosing between civic importance and audience appeal. Reserve meaningful space for stories whose value is civic rather than viral, and resist the drift toward coverage shaped entirely by what the metrics reward.

Be honest when a story is being pursued for attention rather than consequence, and do not dress the one up as the other.

### Allocate Resources Against Significance, Not Convenience

Reporting resources are finite, and where they go determines what gets covered deeply and what gets covered shallowly or not at all. The easy path is to allocate resources to stories that are cheap, fast, and already prominent, while the stories that require sustained effort, documents, travel, and source development, often the most consequential, go uncovered. Allocate resources against the significance of the story and the depth it requires, not against the convenience of covering it. An investigation that takes months may matter more than a dozen quick takes, and a newsroom that never invests in depth produces coverage that any aggregator could replicate.

Consider opportunity cost explicitly: choosing one story means not choosing another, and the unseen cost of selection is the coverage that does not happen.

### Resist The Pull Of Familiarity And Prominence

News judgment drifts toward the familiar: the institutions, people, and topics already known to drive coverage. This produces systematic skew, over-covering powerful and visible actors while under-covering the communities, systems, and issues that lack prominence but affect people's lives. Actively counter the pull of familiarity by asking whose stories are missing, which communities are covered only as subjects of crisis, and which systems operate without scrutiny. Diversify sourcing and story origins, not as a token gesture but as a structural correction to the bias toward the prominent.

A beat or topic that is covered only when it erupts into crisis is not actually covered; the routine work of accountability happens in the quiet periods.

### Evaluate Tips And Leads For Provenance And Viability

Not every tip deserves pursuit. Evaluate leads against their provenance, specificity, plausibility, and the feasibility of verifying them. A tip from a reliable source with specific, checkable detail warrants attention; a vague, sensational, or anonymously circulated claim warrants skepticism. Consider the resources the story would require, the likelihood of verification, and the potential significance if confirmed. Pursue the tips that offer the best ratio of public value to reporting cost, and be willing to set aside leads that are thin, unverifiable, or driven by a source's agenda rather than evidence.

A newsroom that chases every sensational tip wastes resources and becomes vulnerable to manipulation by actors who feed it stories.

### Consider What The Audience Needs To Know, Not Only What It Wants

Audience metrics reveal what people click, share, and dwell on, and they are valuable signals. But they describe past behavior, not present need, and optimizing to them produces a feedback loop that narrows coverage toward what already performs. The audience needs information it may not seek: the policy change that will affect them, the accountability failure that is hidden, the context that corrects a widespread misunderstanding. Reserve judgment for stories that serve audience need even where metrics do not reward them, and use metrics to inform presentation and reach rather than to dictate selection.

The most valuable journalism often tells people what they did not know they needed to know.

### Sequence And Play Stories To Reflect Significance

Selection continues into how stories are played: placement, prominence, headline weight, and the resources devoted to follow-up. A story's play should reflect its significance, not the accident of when it arrived or how it performed in early metrics. Inflating a minor story because it is dramatic, or burying a significant story because it is complex, distorts the audience's understanding of what matters. Maintain proportion between the weight of the news and the weight of the coverage, and revisit play as the story develops.

## Common Traps

### Letting Metrics Dictate Selection

Optimizing coverage to clicks and shares narrows the news to what already performs and abandons civic value. This is a trap because the metrics reward behavior that erodes the public function over time. Use metrics to inform, not to dictate.

### Defaulting To The Dramatic And Conflict-Driven

Drama and conflict are easy to cover and easy to consume, but they distort the picture of the world if they dominate. This is a trap because the dramatic is often less significant than the slow, structural story. Reserve judgment for significance over spectacle.

### Covering Only The Prominent

Familiar institutions and visible actors absorb coverage while less prominent communities and systems go unexamined. This is a trap because it produces a skewed record of whose lives and interests matter. Actively counter the bias toward prominence.

### Chasing Every Sensational Tip

Sensational tips are often thin, unverifiable, or agenda-driven. This is a trap because pursuing them wastes resources and exposes the newsroom to manipulation. Evaluate leads for provenance and viability before committing.

### Confusing Public Interest With Audience Interest

What people want to read is not always what they need to know. This is a trap because conflating the two lets metrics masquerade as public service. Weigh both honestly and reserve space for civic value.

### Ignoring Opportunity Cost

Choosing one story means not choosing another. This is a trap because the unseen cost of selection is the uncovered story. Consider what is not being covered when resources are committed.

### Inflating Or Burying Play Against Significance

Play that reflects timing or early metrics rather than significance distorts understanding. This is a trap because the audience reads prominence as importance. Maintain proportion between news weight and coverage weight.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Were the criteria for newsworthiness made explicit and applied to competing stories, rather than selection left to impression?
- [ ] Was public interest weighed honestly against audience interest, with space reserved for stories of civic value that metrics may not reward?
- [ ] Were reporting resources allocated against significance and required depth, with opportunity cost considered, rather than against convenience?
- [ ] Did selection resist the pull of familiarity and prominence, actively seeking the communities, systems, and stories that lack existing attention?
- [ ] Were tips and leads evaluated for provenance, specificity, plausibility, and viability before resources were committed?
- [ ] Did selection consider what the audience needs to know, not only what metrics indicate it wants, using metrics to inform rather than dictate?
- [ ] Does the play and prominence of selected stories reflect their significance, maintained in proportion rather than inflated or buried by timing or metrics?
- [ ] Can the selection for the day or week be justified in explicit terms against the criteria, including what was deliberately not covered and why?
- [ ] Is there systematic review of what the coverage over time includes and excludes, to detect skew in whose stories and which issues are covered?
- [ ] Before committing, has the selection been examined for whether it serves the public function or merely follows the path of least resistance?
