# Accuracy In Headlines

The headline is the part of the story most people read, and often the only part. It travels further than the body, survives in search results and social feeds long after publication, and shapes what the audience believes the story said. The judgment problem is that the incentives of headlines, brevity, impact, clickability, pull directly against accuracy, and a headline that is technically defensible in isolation can still mislead when read without the body. A headline that outruns the story does not just disappoint readers; it publishes a distortion that the correction can rarely catch up to.

Use this skill when writing or reviewing headlines, subheads, deks, push alerts, social posts, and any surface that summarizes a story for an audience that may read nothing else. The main risk is that an agent may optimize a headline for engagement while treating accuracy as a constraint to edge up against rather than a standard to meet. This skill is editorial guidance; headline decisions for high-stakes stories should be reviewed by editors and, where claims are legally sensitive, by counsel.

## Core Rules

### The Headline Must Be True If It Is All You Read

The core test is whether the headline, read alone, conveys a true and fair account of what the story establishes. Most readers will not read the body, the nuance, the attribution, or the caveats. If the headline asserts something the body only alleges, implies certainty the body only suggests, or omits a qualification that changes the meaning, it is inaccurate regardless of whether the body is correct. Write the headline as if it will be the only sentence the audience encounters.

This test is stricter than "is the headline literally defensible." A headline can be literally true and still misleading if its emphasis, omission, or certainty creates a false impression.

### Match Certainty To The Body

Headlines tend to harden allegations into facts. A body that says "officials allege" becomes a headline that says "official did." A body that says "may cause" becomes a headline that says "causes." A body that reports a single study becomes a headline that declares a settled finding. Audit every headline for certainty creep: verbs that imply proof, attribution that disappears, conditionals that vanish, and singular sources that generalize.

Preserve the body's hedging where it matters. "Probe alleges," "study suggests," and "sources say" are not weak headlines; they are accurate ones when the body warrants them.

### Preserve Attribution

If the body attributes a claim to a source, an official, or a document, the headline should not strip the attribution in a way that makes the claim the outlet's own assertion. "Company lied," as a headline, asserts the outlet has proven lying; "Whistleblower alleges company misled regulators" preserves the attribution and the status of the claim. Disappearing attribution converts an allegation into a finding and exposes the outlet to the liability of a finding it has not made.

This is especially dangerous with anonymous sourcing, where the headline can present as fact what the body carefully attributes to unnamed sources.

### Do Not Overstate Scope Or Generalize

A story about one incident, one company, one city, or one study should not be headlined as a trend, a crisis, or a universal finding unless the body establishes the broader scope. "Resident complains" is not "residents revolt." "Study finds association in a sample" is not "X causes Y." Scope creep in headlines manufactures significance the reporting did not establish and misleads readers about the state of the evidence.

If the body's evidence is narrow, the headline's claim must be narrow too.

### Avoid Loaded And Distorting Language

Headlines compress, and compression tempts loaded verbs and labels: "slams," "blasts," "destroys," "meltdown," "chaos," "bombshell," "destroyed." These words convey drama and judgment that the body may not support, and they frame the story before the reader encounters the facts. Choose precise verbs that describe what happened rather than words that perform an opinion. A headline that calls a statement "controversial" has already taken a side the body may not have earned.

Loaded language is not just a style problem; it is an accuracy problem because it asserts a characterization the reporting may not justify.

### Check The Headline Against The Strongest Counter-Evidence

Before approving a headline, ask what the strongest contrary evidence or denial in the story is, and whether the headline fairly accommodates it. If the body contains a serious denial, a competing explanation, or a limitation that changes the meaning, the headline should not present the claim as settled. A headline that ignores the body's most important qualification misleads even if its core assertion appears in the story.

This is the headline equivalent of seeking disconfirming evidence in reporting.

### Review All Summary Surfaces Together

The headline is one of several surfaces that summarize the story: subhead, dek, image, caption, pull quote, push alert, social post, and newsletter blurb. Each must be accurate on its own and consistent with the others. A correct headline paired with a misleading push alert still misleads. Review every surface the audience will encounter, because each travels independently and each can be the only thing a reader sees.

A correction to the body does not correct a misleading social post unless the post is also corrected.

### Treat Updated Stories' Headlines As Part Of The Record

When a story updates, the headline may need to update too, but changes to headlines must be handled with care. Quietly changing a headline that implied more than the story supported is appropriate; quietly changing a headline to soften criticism after pressure is not. For significant changes, note the update. Remember that old headlines persist in screenshots, search caches, and archives, so the inaccurate version continues to mislead even after correction.

## Common Traps

### Certainty Creep

Allegations become facts, suggestions become causes, and single sources become settled findings as the headline hardens the body's careful language.

### Disappearing Attribution

Stripping the source from a claim turns an attributed allegation into the outlet's own assertion, with the liability that implies.

### Scope Exaggeration

A single incident or study is headlined as a trend or crisis, manufacturing significance the reporting did not establish.

### Literal-But-Misleading

A headline can be technically defensible word by word yet create a false impression through emphasis, omission, or framing.

### Loaded Language As Drama

"Slams," "blasts," and "chaos" inject judgment and drama the body has not earned, framing the story before the facts.

### Ignoring The Body's Qualifications

A headline that presents as settled what the body carefully hedges or denies misleads even when the qualification exists lower in the story.

### Surface Inconsistency

An accurate headline paired with a misleading push alert, caption, or social post still reaches readers with the distortion.

### Headline Changes Without Transparency

Quietly rewriting headlines, whether to fix or to soften, without noting significant changes obscures the record.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the headline true and fair if it is the only sentence the reader encounters?
- [ ] Does the headline's certainty match the body, with allegations, suggestions, and single-source claims preserved rather than hardened?
- [ ] Is attribution preserved so that attributed claims do not become the outlet's own assertions?
- [ ] Does the headline's scope match the body's evidence, without generalizing one incident or study into a trend or crisis?
- [ ] Is the language precise rather than loaded, avoiding verbs and labels that assert judgment the body has not earned?
- [ ] Does the headline fairly accommodate the body's strongest contrary evidence, denial, or qualification?
- [ ] Have all summary surfaces, subhead, dek, image, caption, push alert, social post, newsletter blurb, been reviewed for accuracy and consistency?
- [ ] For updated stories, are headline changes handled transparently, with awareness that old versions persist?
- [ ] For high-stakes or legally sensitive stories, has the headline been reviewed by an editor and, where warranted, legal counsel, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and not a substitute for editorial and legal sign-off?
