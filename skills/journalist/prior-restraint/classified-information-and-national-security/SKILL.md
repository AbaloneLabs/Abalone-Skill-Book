# Classified Information And National-Security Reporting

Reporting on classified and national-security material is among the most consequential and most legally exposed journalism. It can expose wrongdoing, inform democratic oversight, and reveal hidden policy, but it can also implicate espionage and official-secrets laws, endanger sources to prosecution, and, in some jurisdictions, expose the journalists themselves. The judgment problem is that the public-interest value of disclosure must be weighed against legal risk, source safety, and genuine harm to legitimate security interests, and that the analysis differs sharply by country and by the type of material.

Use this skill when handling classified documents, intelligence material, military information, leaked diplomatic cables, or any content whose publication could trigger secrecy law, and when protecting the sources of such material. The main risk is that an agent may publish without assessing the legal regime, the harm to sources, or the genuine security harm, or may self-censor newsworthy disclosure out of undifferentiated fear. This skill is editorial guidance only; national-security publication decisions require senior editorial review and qualified legal counsel.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Legal Regime Before Handling Or Publishing

National-security law varies dramatically. Some jurisdictions criminalize receipt or publication of certain classified material; some prosecute only leakers; some recognize strong press protections for lawfully received material; some use official-secrets acts broadly; and some have asserted extraterritorial reach over foreign publishers. Before handling or publishing classified material, identify which jurisdiction's law applies to the outlet, the source, and the servers, and what conduct each regime criminalizes.

Assume that the source faces the most direct legal risk, and that the journalist's risk, while often lower, is not zero in every jurisdiction. Counsel must map the legal exposure before publication decisions are made.

### Distinguish Genuine Security Harm From Embarrassment

National-security officials routinely invoke harm to oppose disclosure, but much classified material is classified for reasons of embarrassment, policy preference, bureaucratic habit, or political protection rather than genuine security harm. The journalist's task is to distinguish the two. Genuine harm includes exposing specific intelligence methods that would be degraded, identifying cooperating individuals who would be endangered, revealing troop movements or operational plans that could cost lives, or disclosing information that would help adversaries target people or systems.

Engage substantively with the government's harm claims. Request specific harm explanations, redact genuinely harmful details, and publish what serves the public interest without the harmful specifics. Do not accept blanket "it's classified" assertions as dispositive, but do not dismiss genuine harm claims either.

### Weigh The Public Interest Specifically

The public-interest justification for publishing classified material must be specific, not generic. "The public has a right to know" is a starting point, not an argument. Identify what the disclosure reveals about government conduct, policy, legality, waste, deception, rights violations, or democratic accountability, and why that revelation justifies the risk and any residual harm. The stronger and more specific the public interest, the more defensible the publication.

Document the public-interest rationale so the decision can be reviewed and defended.

### Protect The Source Above All

The source of classified material usually faces the most severe legal risk, including prosecution under espionage or official-secrets law. Source protection must be a primary concern from first contact through long after publication. Apply rigorous operational security, minimize identifying material, scrub descriptive details that could trace back, and coordinate with counsel on the source's legal exposure. A source endangered by careless reporting is both a human harm and a blow to future whistleblowing.

Confirm the source understands the risk and the limits of the protection offered.

### Engage With The Government Before Publication

For serious national-security stories, consider contacting the government before publication to seek comment, to understand harm claims, and to negotiate redactions. This engagement can improve accuracy, can produce specific harm information that supports responsible redaction, and can strengthen the public-interest defense. It also carries risks, including pressure to kill the story, surveillance of the engagement, or efforts to identify the source. Conduct engagement through counsel and senior editors, and protect the engagement itself from surveillance.

Document the government's response, including any specific harm claims and redaction requests.

### Redact To Minimize Harm While Preserving Newsworthiness

Responsible national-security reporting often publishes less than the full leaked material. Redact details that would cause genuine harm without adding public value, such as names of cooperating individuals, specific technical methods, operational timings, or identifying information. Preserve the material that documents the conduct, policy, or wrongdoing at the heart of the public interest. Redaction is not self-censorship when it removes genuine harm while keeping the accountability function.

Have editors and, where appropriate, specialists review proposed redactions to ensure they remove harm without gutting the story.

### Avoid Becoming A Conduit For Harmful Disclosure

Publishing selected, verified material in the public interest differs from dumping entire archives of classified material without review. Bulk publication of unredacted classified material can endanger individuals, damage legitimate operations, and undermine the public-interest justification. Curate, verify, redact, and publish responsibly, even when the full archive is available.

### Plan For Legal And Reputational Response

National-security publication can trigger investigations, demands for source identity, equipment seizures, and political attacks framing the journalism as treasonous. Plan the legal response, the source-protection response, and the public defense of the journalism in advance. Coordinate with counsel, security, and communications so the outlet is prepared rather than reactive.

## Common Traps

### Publishing Without Mapping The Legal Regime

Assuming press protections apply universally leads to publishing into legal exposure the outlet did not anticipate.

### Treating All Classification As Genuine Harm

Accepting classification as proof of harm leads to over-redaction and self-censorship of newsworthy disclosure.

### Dismissing All Harm Claims As Spin

The opposite error, treating every harm claim as propaganda, risks publishing material that genuinely endangers people or operations.

### Bulk-Dumping Unredacted Archives

Publishing entire classified archives without curation, verification, or redaction can cause serious harm and undermine the story's legitimacy.

### Careless Source Identification

Descriptive details, contact trails, or forensic traces in published documents can identify the source and expose them to prosecution.

### Engaging Government Without Protecting The Engagement

Contact that is itself surveilled can expose the source and the story. Engagement must be protected.

### Vague Public-Interest Justification

Without a specific public-interest argument, the publication is harder to defend against legal and political attack.

### Handling The Decision Without Senior Review

National-security publication is too consequential for a single reporter or editor. It requires senior editorial, legal, and security review.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has counsel mapped the legal regime, including which jurisdictions' secrecy, espionage, or official-secrets law applies to the outlet, source, and servers?
- [ ] Have I distinguished genuine security harm from embarrassment or political protection, engaging substantively with harm claims rather than accepting or dismissing them wholesale?
- [ ] Is the public-interest justification specific and documented, identifying what the disclosure reveals about government conduct or accountability?
- [ ] Is source protection a primary concern from first contact onward, with operational security, scrubbed details, and coordination with counsel on the source's legal exposure?
- [ ] Has the government been engaged before publication, where appropriate, to seek comment, understand harm, and negotiate redactions, with the engagement itself protected?
- [ ] Are redactions removing genuine harm while preserving the newsworthy, accountability-relevant material, reviewed by editors and specialists?
- [ ] Am I publishing curated, verified material rather than bulk-dumping unredacted archives?
- [ ] Is there a plan for legal, source-protection, and public-response contingencies?
- [ ] Has the publication decision gone through senior editorial, legal, and security review, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and not a substitute for qualified legal counsel on national-security law?
