# Defamation Risk Assessment

Defamation law creates liability for false statements of fact that damage reputation, and journalism that accuses, exposes, or criticizes is where that risk concentrates. The judgment problem is that a story can be accurate in spirit yet defamatory in wording, that opinion can imply undisclosed defamatory facts, that a single overreaching word can convert a defensible story into a vulnerable one, and that the legal standards differ by jurisdiction and by the subject's status. A journalist who treats defamation as a worry only for the lawyers, after the draft is done, will eventually publish avoidable risk.

Use this skill before publishing allegations, criticism, exposés, opinion pieces, or any content naming individuals or companies in a damaging light, and when responding to defamation threats after publication. The main risk is that an agent may use language that outruns the evidence, may fail to distinguish fact from opinion, or may not understand how the subject's public or private status changes the standard. This skill is editorial guidance only; defamation is a serious legal risk and high-stakes publication requires qualified legal counsel.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Statements Of Fact From Opinion

Defamation law targets false statements of fact, not opinion, but the line is subtle. Pure opinion, clearly labeled and based on disclosed facts, is generally protected. Opinion that implies undisclosed defamatory facts, however, can be actionable: a column that calls someone "corrupt" while implying knowledge of specific bribes functions as a factual accusation. Make the factual basis explicit, label opinion clearly, and ensure the opinion does not rest on or imply facts the outlet cannot prove.

When an opinion piece criticizes a person, the supporting facts should be stated or linked so the reader can see what is fact and what is commentary.

### Match The Language To The Evidence

The most common defamation trap is language that outruns proof. "Refused to answer" implies a deliberate refusal that may not be proven; "did not respond by deadline" is safer. "Lied" requires proof of knowing falsity; "made a statement contradicted by the records" attributes the contradiction to evidence. "Stole," "fraud," "cover-up," "corrupt," and "admitted" each carry specific legal meanings that require corresponding proof.

Audit every damaging verb and characterization against the evidence. Where proof falls short, use precise, attributable, evidence-tied language and avoid conclusory labels.

### Identify The Subject's Status And The Applicable Standard

Defamation standards depend on the subject's status. Public officials and public figures generally must prove actual malice, knowing falsity or reckless disregard for the truth, which is a demanding standard. Private individuals need only prove negligence or fault in many jurisdictions, and some jurisdictions impose stricter standards or different frameworks entirely. The subject's status affects how much verification and caution is required.

Determine whether the subject is a public official, a public figure, a limited-purpose public figure, or a private individual, and apply the corresponding standard. When in doubt, treat the subject as more protected rather than less.

### Verify The Defamatory Facts To The Required Standard

For each defamatory implication, verify the underlying facts to a standard that matches the subject's status and the severity of the accusation. Serious accusations against private individuals require the strongest verification. Document the evidence for each damaging fact so the basis can be defended. A story that is "probably true" is not enough where the accusation is severe; aim for verification that would withstand challenge.

Corroborate with independent evidence, not just a single source, for serious accusations. Anonymous accusations of serious wrongdoing carry heightened risk and require exceptional justification.

### Give A Meaningful Right Of Response

Offering the subject a fair opportunity to respond before publication is both an ethical obligation and a practical defense. The response may correct errors, supply context, or reveal a defense that changes the story. Document the request, the deadline given, and the response, and reflect any substantive response in the piece. A subject who was not given a chance to respond is harder to defend against in litigation and weaker ethically.

Make the request specific enough that the subject can address the actual allegations, not so vague that the response is meaningless.

### Watch For Implication By juxtaposition And Headline

Defamation can arise not only from explicit statements but from implication, juxtaposition, headlines, captions, and images that convey a false meaning. A headline that accuses while the body hedges, a photo placed beside unrelated text, or a sequence that implies causation can each create a defamatory implication distinct from the literal words. Review headlines, subheads, captions, images, and structure for the meaning a reasonable reader would take away.

### Handle Rumor, Allegation, And Charged Language Carefully

Reporting that "X has been accused of Y" is itself a statement that the accusation occurred, and repeating an accusation can be defamatory if the accusation is false, even if attributed. Attribute precisely, report the status of the accusation, and avoid treating an unproven allegation as established. Charged terms like "scandal," "crisis," or "cover-up" should be earned by the evidence, not used for emphasis.

### Document The Verification Path

Maintain notes, documents, and source records that establish the basis for each defamatory fact. If challenged, the outlet must be able to show what it knew, when, and why it believed the statement was true. Actual malice turns on the reporter's and editor's state of mind, so contemporaneous documentation of good-faith verification is a critical defense. Preserve the verification record without exposing confidential sources.

### Respond To Threats Through Counsel

Defamation threats, demand letters, and retraction requests should be routed to legal counsel, not handled by the reporter. Some demands are baseless bluffs; others reveal genuine errors that warrant correction. Counsel can assess the merits, manage the response, and preserve defenses. Do not apologize, retract, or settle without review, because missteps can create liability or concede ground unnecessarily.

## Common Traps

### Language That Outruns The Evidence

Conclusory verbs and labels, such as "lied," "stole," or "covered up," that exceed what the evidence proves are the most common defamation trap.

### Opinion Implying Undisclosed Facts

Commentary that implies knowledge of specific damaging facts functions as factual accusation and loses opinion protection.

### Headline Accusation, Body Hedging

A defamatory headline paired with a careful body creates the false impression the headline conveys, and the headline travels further.

### Treating All Subjects As Public Figures

Misclassifying a private individual as a public figure applies the wrong, more lenient standard and under-protects the verification required.

### Repeating Unproven Allegations As Fact

"Attributed" repetition of a false accusation is still defamation if the accusation is false and damaging.

### Juxtaposition And Implication

Placing unrelated facts, images, or quotes together can imply a false, defamatory meaning.

### Failing To Document Verification

Without contemporaneous records of good-faith checking, defending an actual-malice claim or even a negligence claim becomes far harder.

### Handling Threats Without Counsel

Responding to demand letters or retracting without legal review can concede liability or waive defenses.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are statements of fact clearly distinguished from opinion, with the factual basis for opinion disclosed?
- [ ] Does every damaging verb and characterization match the evidence, with conclusory labels replaced by precise, attributable language where proof falls short?
- [ ] Has the subject's status, public official, public figure, limited-purpose, or private individual, been identified and the corresponding standard applied?
- [ ] Is each defamatory fact verified to a standard matching the subject's status and the severity of the accusation, with independent corroboration for serious claims?
- [ ] Was the subject given a specific, fair opportunity to respond before publication, and is the response reflected in the piece?
- [ ] Have headline, subhead, caption, image, and structure been reviewed for the defamatory meaning a reasonable reader would take away?
- [ ] Are rumor, allegation, and charged terms handled so that unproven accusations are not presented as established fact?
- [ ] Is the verification path documented and preserved without exposing confidential sources?
- [ ] Have all defamation threats and retraction demands been routed to qualified legal counsel, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and not a substitute for legal review before high-risk publication?
