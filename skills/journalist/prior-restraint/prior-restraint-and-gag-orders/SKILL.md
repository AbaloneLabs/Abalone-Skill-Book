# Prior Restraint And Gag Orders

Prior restraint, government action forbidding speech before it occurs, is among the most disfavored forms of censorship in press-freedom traditions. Courts have historically treated injunctions against publication as presumptively invalid, yet they still issue in forms ranging from national-security injunctions to sealing orders, super injunctions, and gag orders on participants in litigation. The judgment problem is that a journalist facing a restraint must distinguish a genuine, challengeable prior restraint from a lawful limit, must decide whether to publish before, during, or after a restraint takes effect, and must avoid both reckless defiance and timid surrender.

Use this skill when an injunction, gag order, sealing order, or threat seeks to stop or delay publication, when covering sealed or secret proceedings, and when deciding whether to challenge a restraint. The main risk is that an agent may either publish into a valid order and incur liability, or withhold newsworthy reporting under an invalid threat that could have been challenged. This skill is editorial guidance only; prior-restraint questions are legal questions that require immediate qualified counsel.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Full Range Of Prior Restraints

Prior restraints are not only explicit publication bans. They include injunctions against disclosing particular information, sealing orders that hide court files, gag orders on parties and lawyers, national-security letters with nondisclosure requirements, super injunctions that forbid even mentioning the injunction exists, and licensing or permit schemes that gate speech. Each form raises distinct legal issues and distinct challenges. Identify precisely what the restraint forbids, who it binds, and what exceptions exist.

A restraint that binds only a party to litigation, such as a gag order on litigants, may not bind the press, while one directed at the press itself is the core prior-restraint problem.

### Determine Whom The Order Binds

A critical first question is whether the order binds the journalist or outlet at all. Many gag orders bind only the parties, their lawyers, or government employees, and do not prevent the press from publishing information lawfully obtained from other sources. Publishing information the press lawfully obtained, even if a participant is gagged, is often protected, though the source may face consequences. Confirm with counsel whether the outlet is actually restrained before treating an order as binding.

### Assess Whether The Restraint Is Challengeable

Strong press-freedom traditions hold that prior restraints on lawfully obtained, newsworthy information are presumptively invalid, and the government bears a heavy burden to justify them. The classic test asks whether the information is lawfully obtained, whether the restraint is necessary to serve a compelling interest, and whether narrower alternatives exist. Many restraints fail this test and can be challenged, vacated, or narrowed.

Do not assume an injunction is final or valid. Many are issued ex parte, without the press's participation, and can be overturned on challenge. Engage counsel immediately to evaluate standing to challenge, the applicable test, and the realistic prospects.

### Distinguish Prior Restraint From Lawful Limits

Not every limit on publication is an invalid prior restraint. Restrictions on obscenity, incitement, classified information unlawfully obtained, intellectual property, false commercial speech, election-period limits in some jurisdictions, and court secrecy designed to protect fair-trial rights or juveniles may be lawful. The line between a challengeable prior restraint and a lawful regulation is fact-specific. Counsel must classify the restraint correctly before deciding to defy or challenge it.

### Weigh The Decision To Publish Before A Restrait Takes Effect

When a restraint is threatened but not yet in force, the journalist faces a strategic choice: publish before the order issues, which may moot the restraint but may carry other legal risks, or wait and challenge. Publishing first can preserve the public's right to know and can render an injunction moot, but it can also trigger separate liability, such as defamation, trade-secret, or national-security exposure, that exists independently of the restraint. The decision requires balancing the public interest, the legal risks of publication, and the prospects for challenge.

This is a decision for senior editors and counsel, not for a reporter alone.

### Treat Sealing And Secret Proceedings As Reportable

Courts and governments sometimes seal files, close proceedings, or issue super injunctions that hide the very existence of a case. Such secrecy is itself often newsworthy, and reporting on the existence and effect of the secrecy, where lawful, serves the public interest. Distinguish reporting about the secrecy from publishing the sealed material itself, which may carry different risks. Seek to unseal through legal motion where possible.

### Plan The Challenge, Not Just The Response

Challenging a prior restraint is an active strategy, not a passive defense. File motions to intervene, to unseal, to vacate, or to narrow. Seek emergency stays. Bring in press-coalition counsel where available. Document the public-interest justification and the chilling effect of the restraint. A well-prepared challenge can turn a gag into a public vindication of press freedom.

### Protect Sources When A Restraint Targets The Investigation

Sometimes a restraint is aimed not at the article but at the investigation, through demands for source identity, search warrants for devices, or orders to participants. In those cases, source protection and prior-restraint strategy intersect. Coordinate with counsel and security to protect sources while challenging the investigative restraint.

## Common Traps

### Assuming An Order Binds The Press

Many gag orders bind only parties or lawyers and do not restrain the press. Treating a non-binding order as binding leads to unnecessary self-censorship.

### Surrendering To An Invalid Threat

Some restraints are bluffs or overbroad orders that would not survive challenge. Withholding newsworthy reporting without evaluating the challenge abandons a defensible story.

### Publishing Recklessly Into A Valid Order

Not all restraints are invalid. Publishing into a lawful order on classified, trade-secret, or restricted material can create serious liability and endanger sources.

### Conflating The Source's Risk With The Press's

A participant gagged by an order may face sanctions for leaking, while the press publishing lawfully obtained material may be protected. The two risk profiles differ and must be analyzed separately.

### Ignoring Sealing As A Story

Court secrecy is often itself newsworthy. Failing to report on the existence and effect of a seal lets the secrecy pass without public scrutiny.

### Missing The Window To Publish Or Challenge

Timing matters. Once an injunction issues, publishing may carry contempt risk; before it issues, the window may be open. Delay can foreclose both options.

### Handling The Challenge Without Counsel

Prior-restraint challenges are legally complex. A reporter or editor navigating them without counsel can waive rights or make irreversible errors.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I identified precisely what the restraint forbids, whom it binds, and what exceptions exist?
- [ ] Has counsel confirmed whether the order actually binds the outlet, or whether it binds only parties, lawyers, or employees?
- [ ] Has the restraint been assessed against the prior-restraint test, including whether it is lawfully obtained, necessary, and narrowly tailored, with narrower alternatives considered?
- [ ] Have I distinguished a challengeable prior restraint from a lawful regulation of speech?
- [ ] Where a restraint is threatened but not yet in force, have senior editors and counsel weighed publishing first against waiting and challenging?
- [ ] Is court or government secrecy being treated as reportable, with efforts to unseal where possible?
- [ ] Is there an active challenge strategy, including motions to intervene, unseal, vacate, or narrow, supported by documented public-interest justification?
- [ ] Where the restraint targets the investigation, are source protection and prior-restraint strategy coordinated with counsel and security?
- [ ] Has qualified legal counsel been engaged immediately for every prior-restraint question, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and not a substitute for legal representation?
