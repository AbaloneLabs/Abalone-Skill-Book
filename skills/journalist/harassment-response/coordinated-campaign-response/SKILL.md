# Coordinated Campaign Response

Coordinated campaigns against journalists and newsrooms, whether harassment campaigns, organized disinformation efforts, or pressure campaigns seeking to chill or discredit coverage, differ from organic criticism in their organization, intent, and required response, and misreading them leads to responses that either overreact to legitimate criticism or underreact to organized attacks. The judgment problem is that a campaign's effectiveness depends on the target's confusion about what it is, and the right response to organic criticism, engage and address the substance, is the wrong response to a coordinated attack, which seeks to drain attention, manufacture controversy, and force the target into a defensive posture. A defensible response identifies the campaign's organization and intent, refuses to engage on the campaign's terms, protects the targeted journalists and the coverage, and decides deliberately whether and how to respond publicly.

Use this skill when responding to coordinated harassment or disinformation campaigns, distinguishing them from organic criticism, deciding on response, or maintaining coverage under pressure. The main risk is that an agent treats a coordinated attack as organic criticism, engages on the campaign's terms, or abandons coverage under pressure without recognizing the campaign's intent to chill.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Coordinated Campaigns From Organic Criticism

The first task is to determine whether the pressure is organic criticism, which deserves engagement on its substance, or a coordinated campaign, which requires a different response. Organic criticism arises from genuine readers or sources reacting to the coverage, varies in message and origin, and engages with the story's substance. A coordinated campaign has signatures: a sudden spike in volume, similar or identical messaging across many accounts, accounts that appear dedicated to the campaign or that coordinate timing, escalation across platforms, and targeting that follows a shared script or list.

Assess the pressure against these signatures before deciding how to respond. A coordinated campaign often reveals itself through its uniformity and timing, which organic criticism lacks. Misreading a campaign as organic criticism leads to engaging on its terms, treating bad-faith messaging as good-faith feedback, and a defensive posture that serves the campaign's aim. Misreading organic criticism as a campaign leads to dismissing legitimate feedback and alienating genuine readers. The distinction matters and must be made deliberately.

### Understand The Campaign's Intent And Sponsor

Coordinated campaigns are organized for a purpose, and understanding the intent shapes the response. A campaign may seek to silence a journalist covering a sensitive subject, to discredit a story ahead of or after publication, to drive a journalist out of a beat or the profession, to manufacture controversy that distracts from the story, or to punish the newsroom for past coverage. The sponsor may be a government, a political movement, an industry facing scrutiny, an organized online community, or a combination.

Assess the likely intent and sponsor using the campaign's messaging, targets, timing relative to coverage, and connections to known actors. This assessment informs whether the campaign is an attempt to chill coverage, which warrants a different response than a campaign seeking to discredit a published story. A campaign intended to chill coverage is an attack on press freedom, and the response should account for that, including by continuing the coverage the campaign sought to stop. See the online-harassment-assessment skill.

### Refuse To Engage On The Campaign's Terms

A coordinated campaign seeks to draw the target into a defensive posture, forcing the journalist or newsroom to respond to bad-faith framing, to litigate the campaign's manufactured controversies, and to spend attention and credibility on the attackers' terms. Engaging on these terms serves the campaign, regardless of how well the engagement is done, because it grants the campaign's framing legitimacy and keeps the target on defense.

Refuse to engage on the campaign's terms. Do not litigate bad-faith framing in public, do not treat manufactured controversies as genuine issues to address, and do not allow the campaign to set the agenda. This does not mean ignoring the campaign, but responding on the newsroom's terms: addressing the substance of the coverage, correcting factual errors where they exist, and naming the campaign as a campaign where appropriate. The discipline is to respond to what serves the journalism and the journalists, not to what the campaign demands.

### Protect The Targeted Journalists And The Coverage

A coordinated campaign targets individual journalists as well as the newsroom, and the response must protect both. For the journalists, this means the full harassment response: assessment of threats, doxxing mitigation, psychological support, and relief from the platforms where the campaign is concentrated. The journalists should not face the campaign alone, and the newsroom must provide coordinated support. See the doxxing-response-and-mitigation skill.

For the coverage, the response must protect the journalism the campaign seeks to chill. A campaign that succeeds in stopping coverage achieves its aim, and the newsroom's continued reporting is itself a response. Assess whether to continue, expand, or follow up on the targeted coverage, with editorial backing, as a deliberate decision that resists the pressure. Abandoning coverage under campaign pressure rewards the campaign and invites future campaigns, while continuing coverage with adequate support for the journalists resists the chill.

### Document The Campaign For Accountability And Response

Document the coordinated campaign thoroughly: the accounts and actors involved, the messaging and its evolution, the timing relative to coverage, the platforms used, the threats and harassment directed at journalists, and any connections to known sponsors or movements. This documentation supports platform intervention, potential legal action, law enforcement involvement where threats cross criminal thresholds, and public response if the newsroom chooses to name the campaign.

Documentation also enables pattern recognition across campaigns, because coordinated actors often reuse tactics and targeting across journalists and stories. A newsroom that documents campaigns builds intelligence that improves future response and that may reveal connections between seemingly separate attacks. Treat documentation as part of the response, not as a secondary task, and preserve evidence before platforms remove it.

### Decide On Public Response Deliberately

Whether to respond publicly to a coordinated campaign is a deliberate decision with trade-offs. Public response can expose the campaign, mobilize solidarity, signal that the newsroom will not be chilled, and inform the public about pressure on the press. It can also amplify the campaign, draw more attackers, and put the targeted journalists further in the spotlight. The decision should weigh these trade-offs and should be the targeted journalists' informed choice, with editorial support, about whether public response serves their safety and the journalism.

Where the campaign is an attempt to chill coverage, public disclosure of the campaign can be part of the journalism itself, informing the public about the forces seeking to shape what is reported. Where the campaign seeks attention and amplification, public response may feed it. There is no universal right answer, and the decision must be made based on the specific campaign, the journalists' wishes, and the editorial judgment of what serves the public and the press.

### Coordinate Across The Newsroom And With External Partners

A coordinated campaign response requires coordination across the newsroom and often with external partners. Within the newsroom, the response involves editors, security leads, legal counsel, platform liaisons, communications staff, and the targeted journalists, and it must be organized so that the response is coherent and the journalists are supported. Designate a response coordinator who organizes the different tracks.

With external partners, consider coordinating with other news organizations facing similar campaigns, with press freedom organizations that track and support responses to harassment, and with platform safety teams. Coordinated actors often target multiple journalists and newsrooms, and a coordinated response that shares intelligence and support is more effective than isolated responses. The isolation of the targeted journalist is the campaign's advantage, and coordination counters it.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Coordinated Attack As Organic Criticism

Engaging a campaign on its terms grants its framing legitimacy and keeps the target on defense. Distinguish campaigns from criticism before responding.

### Engaging On The Campaign's Terms

Litigating bad-faith framing and manufactured controversies serves the campaign. Respond on the newsroom's terms, not the campaign's.

### Abandoning Coverage Under Pressure

A campaign that stops coverage achieves its aim. Continued reporting, with support, resists the chill.

### Leaving Targeted Journalists To Respond Alone

The campaign targets individuals as well as the newsroom. Provide coordinated support and relief.

### Failing To Document The Campaign

Documentation supports platform intervention, legal action, and pattern recognition. Preserve evidence before platforms remove it.

### Defaulting To Public Response Or Silence

Public response has trade-offs. Make the decision deliberately, with the targeted journalists' informed choice.

### Responding In Isolation

Coordinated actors target multiple journalists and newsrooms. Coordinate with external partners to share intelligence and support.

## Self-Check

Before treating a coordinated campaign response as sound, verify:

- The pressure is assessed against campaign signatures, sudden spikes, uniform messaging, coordinated timing, cross-platform escalation, to distinguish coordinated attacks from organic criticism before responding.
- The campaign's intent and likely sponsor are assessed from messaging, targets, timing relative to coverage, and connections to known actors, informing whether the aim is to chill, discredit, or punish.
- The response refuses to engage on the campaign's terms, not litigating bad-faith framing or manufactured controversies, and responding on the newsroom's terms.
- Targeted journalists receive coordinated support, including threat assessment, doxxing mitigation, psychological support, and relief from concentrated platforms.
- The coverage the campaign seeks to chill is assessed for continuation, expansion, or follow-up, with editorial backing, resisting the pressure to abandon.
- The campaign is documented thoroughly, including accounts, messaging, timing, threats, and sponsor connections, with evidence preserved for platform, legal, and public response.
- The decision on public response is deliberate, weighing amplification against exposure, and reflecting the targeted journalists' informed choice with editorial support.
- The response is coordinated across the newsroom, with a designated coordinator organizing editors, security, legal, platform, and communications tracks.
- External coordination with other newsrooms, press freedom organizations, and platform safety teams is considered to share intelligence and counter isolation.
- Coordinated campaign response supports editorial judgment; campaigns involving credible threats, criminal harassment, or state-sponsored disinformation should involve legal counsel, security advisers, and, where appropriate, law enforcement and platform escalation, recognizing that organized pressure on the press may require sustained newsroom and professional support and that the response may need to continue well beyond the initial campaign.
