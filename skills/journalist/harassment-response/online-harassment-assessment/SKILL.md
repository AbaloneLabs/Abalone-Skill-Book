# Online Harassment Assessment

Journalists, particularly women, journalists of color, and those covering polarized subjects, face online harassment that ranges from routine abuse to coordinated campaigns that threaten safety, chill coverage, and cause serious psychological harm. The judgment problem is that the volume of abuse makes it tempting to dismiss all of it as noise, while the rare credible threat hidden in the noise can escalate to physical danger, and the cumulative effect of sustained harassment causes harm regardless of whether any single message is credible. A defensible assessment distinguishes the levels of severity, takes the cumulative psychological impact seriously even when no single message is a threat, and triggers escalation when the harassment crosses into credible danger or coordinated attack.

Use this skill when assessing online harassment against a journalist, distinguishing threats from noise, deciding on escalation, or coordinating a newsroom response. The main risk is that an agent dismisses harassment as routine, fails to identify a credible threat, or treats the psychological impact as a personal problem rather than a newsroom responsibility.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish The Levels Of Harassment Severity

Online harassment exists on a spectrum, and the appropriate response depends on the severity. Routine abuse, insults, profanity, and dismissiveness, is unpleasant and cumulative but generally not a direct threat. Targeted harassment, sustained campaigns against an individual, often coordinated, that may include doxxing, impersonation, and smears, is more serious and may warrant organized response. Credible threats, specific threats of physical or sexual violence, threats that identify the journalist's location or family, or threats from actors with the means and history to act, require immediate escalation.

Assess each instance and pattern against this spectrum. Do not treat all abuse as equivalent, but also do not dismiss routine abuse as harmless, because its cumulative effect is real. The assessment should identify what level the harassment has reached, whether it is escalating, and what response the level warrants. See the digital-security skill.

### Take The Cumulative Psychological Impact Seriously

The psychological harm of online harassment is not proportional to the severity of any single message but to the volume, duration, and coordination of the campaign. A journalist receiving hundreds of abusive messages over weeks, even if no single message is a credible threat, suffers real harm: stress, fear, distraction, sleep disruption, and changes in behavior that affect both work and life. This harm is compounded for journalists from groups disproportionately targeted, who may face harassment that intersects with racism, sexism, homophobia, or other bigotry.

Take the cumulative impact seriously as a newsroom responsibility, not a personal weakness. Provide support, including counseling, peer support, and temporary relief from the platforms or beats where the harassment is concentrated. Do not require the journalist to tough it out or treat their distress as unprofessional. The macho culture that dismisses harassment as the cost of visibility is a failure of duty of care and a contributor to the harm, and it drives journalists, especially women and minorities, out of the profession.

### Identify Credible Threats And Escalate Immediately

Within the noise of harassment, credible threats require immediate escalation. A credible threat is specific about the target, the harm, the means, or the timing; it identifies the journalist's location, family, or routine; it comes from an actor with the means and history to act; or it is accompanied by offline behavior, such as appearances at the journalist's home or workplace. These threats are rare relative to the volume of abuse, but they are the ones that can escalate to physical danger.

Establish a clear escalation path for credible threats: the journalist reports to a designated editor or security contact, who assesses with security advisers and law enforcement where appropriate, and who coordinates protective measures. The path must be known in advance, so that a journalist under threat does not have to figure out whom to call. Treat the absence of a prior credible threat as no guarantee, since first offenses occur, and err on the side of escalation when a threat has specific elements.

### Recognize Coordinated Campaigns And Organized Attacks

Some harassment is not spontaneous but coordinated, organized campaigns by groups seeking to silence, discredit, or drive out a journalist, often using amplified accounts, bot networks, and shared targeting lists. Coordinated campaigns have signatures: a sudden spike in volume, similar messaging across many accounts, targeting that follows a shared script, and escalation across platforms. Recognizing coordination changes the response, because it indicates organized intent rather than individual malice and may require platform-level intervention, public response, or law enforcement.

Document the campaign, preserve evidence, and report coordinated activity to the platforms, which may have policies against coordinated harassment. Assess whether the campaign is connected to the journalist's coverage, which may indicate an attempt to chill reporting and may warrant public disclosure of the campaign as part of the story. Coordinated campaigns are a form of pressure on press freedom, and the response should account for that, not only for the individual journalist's safety.

### Protect The Journalist's Personal Information And Family

Harassment campaigns often escalate through doxxing, the publication of the journalist's personal information, home address, family details, phone numbers, and through threats targeting the journalist's family. Protect personal information proactively: remove or limit public exposure of home address, family details, and personal contact information; use privacy services to scrub data brokers; and secure accounts against takeover, which can expose private information and contacts.

If doxxing occurs, respond quickly to limit the spread: request removal from platforms, contact law enforcement if threats accompany the doxxing, and assess whether the journalist or family need temporary relocation or physical protection. The family's safety is part of the assessment, not an afterthought, and threats against family are credible threats even when the journalist is accustomed to abuse. See the journalist-safety skill.

### Coordinate A Newsroom Response, Not An Isolated One

The response to harassment is a newsroom responsibility, not the targeted journalist's alone. The journalist should not have to assess severity, decide on escalation, manage platform reports, and protect themselves while also doing their work. The newsroom should have a designated response lead, a protocol for assessment and escalation, and resources including security advisers, legal counsel, and platform liaison contacts.

Coordinate the response across the newsroom: inform the editor and security lead, assess severity, implement protective measures, document evidence, and decide on public response. Support the journalist's continued work, including by providing coverage that does not abandon the story the harassment sought to chill. A newsroom that leaves a targeted journalist to fend for themselves fails both the journalist and the journalism.

### Decide On Public Response Deliberately

Whether to respond publicly to harassment is a deliberate decision, not a default. Public response can mobilize solidarity, expose coordinated campaigns, deter harassers by naming them, and signal that the newsroom will not be chilled. It can also amplify the harassment, draw more attackers, and put the journalist at the center of a story they did not choose. The decision should be the journalist's, with editorial support, based on what serves their safety and the journalism.

Do not force a public response on a journalist who prefers to handle it privately, and do not force silence on one who wants to speak. The newsroom's role is to support the journalist's informed choice and to provide the resources, platform, and backing to act on it. Where the harassment is an attempt to chill coverage, the newsroom's continued reporting is itself a response, regardless of whether the harassment is named publicly.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing All Harassment As Routine Noise

The volume of abuse makes it tempting to dismiss all of it, but credible threats hide in the noise and the cumulative impact is real. Assess severity and pattern.

### Treating Psychological Harm As A Personal Problem

The cumulative impact of sustained harassment causes real harm regardless of any single threat. Treat it as a newsroom responsibility, not personal weakness.

### Missing A Credible Threat In The Noise

Credible threats are rare but can escalate. Establish a clear escalation path and err on the side of escalation when threats have specific elements.

### Failing To Recognize A Coordinated Campaign

Sudden spikes, similar messaging, and cross-platform escalation signal organized attacks. Recognizing coordination changes the response.

### Leaving Personal Information And Family Exposed

Doxxing and family threats escalate harassment. Protect personal information proactively and respond quickly if doxxing occurs.

### Leaving The Targeted Journalist To Respond Alone

The response is a newsroom responsibility. Provide a designated lead, protocol, resources, and support for continued work.

### Defaulting To Public Response Or Forced Silence

Public response is a deliberate choice, the journalist's with editorial support. Do not force response or silence.

## Self-Check

Before treating an online harassment assessment as sound, verify:

- The harassment is assessed against a severity spectrum, distinguishing routine abuse, targeted campaigns, and credible threats, with the level and escalation trend identified.
- The cumulative psychological impact is taken seriously as a newsroom responsibility, with support provided and the macho dismissal of harm rejected.
- A clear escalation path exists for credible threats, known in advance, with the journalist reporting to a designated editor or security contact who coordinates assessment and protective measures.
- Coordinated campaigns are recognized by their signatures, documented, reported to platforms, and assessed for connection to the coverage and intent to chill.
- Personal information and family are protected proactively, with doxxing response plans and recognition that family threats are credible.
- The response is coordinated across the newsroom, with a designated lead, protocol, and resources, not left to the targeted journalist alone.
- The decision on public response is deliberate, the journalist's with editorial support, based on what serves safety and the journalism.
- Evidence is preserved for potential legal action or platform intervention.
- The assessment and response are documented and reviewed by a responsible editor and, where threats are credible, a security adviser.
- Online harassment assessment supports editorial judgment; credible threat assessment should involve a security adviser and, where threats constitute crimes, law enforcement and legal counsel, recognizing that harassment chills coverage and that the journalist's safety and continued work may require newsroom-wide support and changed coverage decisions.
