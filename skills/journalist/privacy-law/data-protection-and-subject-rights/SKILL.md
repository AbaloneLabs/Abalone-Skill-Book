# Data Protection And Subject Rights

Journalists collect, store, and publish personal data as a core part of their work, and that data is increasingly governed by privacy and data-protection law. Frameworks such as the EU General Data Protection Regulation, the UK GDPR, California privacy law, and analogous regimes worldwide give individuals rights to access, correct, erase, and restrict the processing of their personal information. The judgment problem is that these rights collide with press freedom and the public interest: a person may demand erasure of accurate reporting, and the journalist must decide when data-protection obligations yield to journalistic purpose and when they genuinely apply.

Use this skill when handling personal data of sources or subjects, when fielding requests to remove or correct published material, when publishing across borders, and when designing how long to retain identifying information. The main risk is that an agent either overcomplies, deleting accurate newsworthy material under legal pressure it need not yield to, or undercomplies, ignoring genuine obligations and exposing sources to harm or the outlet to liability. This skill is editorial guidance only; data-protection law is jurisdiction-specific and fact-dependent, and high-stakes decisions require qualified legal counsel.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Journalistic Exemption And Its Limits

Many data-protection regimes include a journalistic exemption or balancing provision that allows processing of personal data for journalism, literature, or art when it is in the public interest. The exemption is not blanket immunity: it typically applies only to processing undertaken solely for journalistic purposes, only where the public interest is served, and only to the extent necessary to reconcile data-protection rights with freedom of expression. The exemption rarely covers non-editorial processing such as marketing, analytics, or unrelated data sales.

Before relying on an exemption, confirm it applies to the specific processing, the jurisdiction, and the purpose. Do not assume a single global rule; the scope varies by country and by whether the outlet or the data subject is in the regulated jurisdiction.

### Recognize That Publication Is Cross-Border

A story published online is published everywhere simultaneously. Material lawful in the journalist's home jurisdiction may be subject to data-protection rights, erasure requests, or regulatory action in jurisdictions where readers or subjects are located. Courts and regulators have increasingly asserted that local residents can invoke local law against foreign publishers, especially where the publisher directs activity toward that jurisdiction.

Assess the jurisdictions of the subjects, the sources, and the likely audience. For stories with international reach, consider the most restrictive plausible jurisdiction, not just the most convenient one.

### Take Subject-Access And Erasure Requests Seriously

Individuals may request access to the personal data a journalist holds about them, correction of inaccurate data, or erasure. Even where the journalistic exemption ultimately applies, requests should be reviewed promptly, in good faith, and with legal guidance. Ignoring or dismissing requests reflexively risks both liability and reputational harm.

For each request, identify the data involved, determine whether the journalistic exemption or freedom-of-expression balancing applies, document the reasoning, and respond within the timeframe the applicable law requires. Erasure of accurate, public-interest reporting is rarely required, but correction of genuine errors, removal of unnecessary retained data, or restriction of further processing may be appropriate.

### Distinguish Active Reporting From Retained Data

Data-protection obligations often turn on purpose and necessity. Personal data needed for an active story, for verification, or for defending the journalism is generally within the journalistic purpose. Personal data retained indefinitely after the story is published, with no ongoing purpose, is harder to justify and more exposed to erasure claims.

Establish retention practices: keep what is needed for verification, legal defense, and follow-up; secure or minimize what is no longer needed; and avoid retaining sensitive identifiers longer than necessary. Document the retention basis.

### Minimize And Protect The Personal Data You Collect

Collect only the personal data necessary for the story. Avoid harvesting bulk personal data on the assumption it might be useful later. Secure personal data, especially sensitive categories, against breach, leak, and unauthorized access, because insecure storage can itself be a data-protection violation and can endanger sources.

Sensitive data, such as health, biometric, political, religious, sexual, or criminal-justice information, often triggers stricter obligations. Apply heightened protection and stronger justification.

### Handle Children's Data With Extra Care

Data relating to children receives special protection under many regimes, including heightened erasure rights and restrictions on processing. Publishing identifying information about children, or retaining it, requires a strong public-interest justification and usually editorial and legal review. Default to non-identification of minors.

### Balance Accuracy, Public Interest, And The Right To Be Forgotten

The "right to be forgotten" allows individuals, in some circumstances, to request delisting of search results linking to accurate material about them. Search delisting is distinct from removing the underlying article: a delisting decision may not require unpublishing, and unpublishing accurate public-interest reporting is rarely warranted. Treat delisting requests, takedown demands, and legal threats as triggers for a careful review, not automatic compliance.

The relevant factors typically include the public interest, the role of the subject, the accuracy of the material, the time elapsed, and the consequences of continued availability. Document the analysis.

### Coordinate With Legal And Editorial Review

Data-protection decisions often have legal consequences and editorial implications simultaneously. Route requests, complaints, and cross-border publication questions to legal counsel and senior editors who can balance the two. Do not let a single reporter resolve a complex request alone.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Journalistic Exemption As A Blanket Immunity

Assuming all journalistic processing is exempt leads to ignoring genuine obligations, such as security, retention limits, and responses to access requests. The exemption is narrow and conditional.

### Automatic Compliance With Every Takedown Demand

The opposite error is reflexively deleting accurate public-interest reporting whenever a complaint arrives. This chills journalism and can itself mislead the public record.

### Ignoring Cross-Border Exposure

Publishing from one country does not insulate the outlet from the law of jurisdictions where subjects or readers are located. Cross-border reach requires cross-border legal awareness.

### Over-Retention Of Source Data

Holding identifying source data indefinitely, "just in case," increases risk to sources and to the outlet. Retain only what is necessary and secure it.

### Conflating Search Delisting With Unpublishing

Granting a delisting request does not require removing the article, and removing the article is rarely required by a delisting. Keep the two decisions distinct.

### Insecure Storage Of Sensitive Source Data

Storing identifying notes, documents, or recordings on unsecured systems can breach data-protection law and endanger sources. Security is part of compliance.

### Publishing Children's Data Without Heightened Review

Children's data triggers stricter rules and stronger ethical obligations. Publishing it without review risks both liability and harm.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I confirmed whether a journalistic exemption or freedom-of-expression balancing applies to the specific processing, jurisdiction, and purpose?
- [ ] Have I assessed the cross-border reach of the story and considered the most restrictive plausible jurisdiction?
- [ ] Are subject-access, correction, and erasure requests reviewed promptly, in good faith, with documented reasoning and legal guidance?
- [ ] Is personal data retained only as long as necessary for verification, legal defense, and follow-up, with a documented retention basis?
- [ ] Have I minimized collection and secured storage of personal data, especially sensitive categories and children's data?
- [ ] Are takedown and delisting requests treated as triggers for careful review, not automatic compliance or automatic refusal?
- [ ] Have I distinguished search delisting from unpublishing, and avoided removing accurate public-interest reporting unless a strong, documented reason exists?
- [ ] Have I routed complex requests, complaints, and cross-border questions to legal counsel and senior editors?
- [ ] For high-stakes or uncertain data-protection questions, have I obtained qualified legal advice, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and not a substitute for legal review?
