# Private Facts And Intrusion

Newsgathering is not limited to what a journalist can legally obtain; it is also constrained by how the information is gathered. Intrusion and private-facts doctrines, professional ethics, and basic human dignity all limit methods that intrude upon a person's reasonable expectation of privacy, even when no law is broken. The judgment problem is that aggressive reporting can shade into harassment, stalking, deception, or the public exposure of intimate truths that serve no public purpose, and a reporter under deadline pressure may not notice the line until it is crossed.

Use this skill before approaching vulnerable subjects, before using surveillance or monitoring tools, before publishing intimate personal facts, and before contacting the family, employer, or associates of a subject. The main risk is that an agent may treat effective newsgathering as inherently justified, or assume that truthful information can always be published, without weighing the intrusiveness of the method, the sensitivity of the fact, and the public-interest value of the story. This skill is editorial guidance, not legal advice; intrusion and privacy law vary by jurisdiction and require qualified counsel for high-risk methods.

## Core Rules

### Apply A Public-Interest Test To Intrusive Methods

Intrusive newsgathering methods, such as persistent doorstep approaches, surveillance, monitoring of private communications, hidden recording, pretexting, or contacting a subject's family and employer, require a public-interest justification proportional to the intrusion. The stronger the intrusion, the stronger the justification must be. Routine reporting on a minor story does not license tactics that would be defensible only for a serious investigation of wrongdoing.

Before using an intrusive method, articulate the specific public-interest purpose, why less intrusive alternatives are inadequate, and what the method is expected to reveal. If the justification is weak or speculative, use a less intrusive approach.

### Respect Reasonable Expectations Of Privacy

People are entitled to privacy in spaces and contexts where a reasonable person would expect it: the home, medical and therapeutic settings, places of worship, grief and mourning, private communications, intimate relationships, and spaces explicitly marked private. Reporting that intrudes into these zones, even truthfully, requires an unusually strong public-interest justification and often fails without one.

Public-facing social media is not equivalent to a private space, but it is also not a free-fire zone. Consider whether the audience for the material was limited, whether the subject had a reasonable expectation of limited circulation, and whether publication would expose the subject to harm out of proportion to the story.

### Distinguish Public-Figure Conduct From Private Life

Public figures surrender some privacy in matters relevant to their public roles, but they do not surrender all privacy in every aspect of their lives. A public official's performance of duties, conflicts of interest, and conduct bearing on fitness for office carry a strong public-interest claim. Their family, health, intimate relationships, and private grief generally do not, unless those matters directly affect their public duties or they have themselves made the private matter public.

For private individuals, the presumption favors privacy. Intrusion into a private individual's life is justified only by a serious and specific public-interest purpose, not by the fact that they became newsworthy through misfortune.

### Limit Contact With Vulnerable Associates

Contacting a subject's family, friends, employer, school, or neighbors can expose people who did not choose public attention and can cause collateral harm. Do so only when the contact is essential to the story, when less intrusive routes are inadequate, and when the potential harm to those third parties has been weighed. Identify yourself honestly, explain the subject of inquiry, and respect refusals.

Approaching grieving families, children, trauma survivors, or people in crisis requires particular care. Default to restraint, offer the option to decline, and never exploit vulnerability for emotional material.

### Be Cautious With Surveillance And Monitoring

Surveillance tools, location tracking, social-media monitoring, and analysis of digital footprints can produce powerful reporting but also intrude deeply. Before using them, assess whether the method is proportionate to the story, whether it targets conduct of genuine public concern, and whether it could chill lawful activity or expose innocent people. Persistent physical following, photographing in private spaces, or monitoring private communications almost always requires senior editorial and legal review.

Document the method, its justification, and the alternatives considered so the decision can be reviewed.

### Handle Intimate And Sensitive Facts With Restraint

Truthful publication of intimate private facts, such as sexual conduct, health conditions, mental-health history, addiction, trauma, or family conflicts, can cause serious harm with little public benefit unless the facts bear directly on a matter of public concern. Default to non-publication of such facts unless they are central to accountability reporting, the subject has made them public, or consent has been given.

When sensitive facts are necessary, publish only what is essential and frame them without sensationalism.

### Give Notice And An Opportunity To Respond

When reporting will intrude upon or expose a person, give them notice and a fair opportunity to respond before publication where practicable. Notice respects dignity, surfaces corrections and context, and often improves the story. Surprise intrusion is rarely justified outside the most serious investigations where notice would defeat the purpose.

### Protect Sources And Subjects From Retaliation

Intrusive reporting can expose sources, witnesses, and bystanders to retaliation. Consider whether publishing identifying details, even truthful ones, would endanger people, and apply anonymity or partial identification where the public-interest purpose can be served without exposure.

## Common Traps

### Doorstep Ambush As Default

Confronting a subject at their home or workplace can feel like effective journalism but is often disproportionate, intimidating, and unnecessary when other methods are available. Reserve doorstep approaches for cases of serious wrongdoing where the subject has refused other opportunities to respond.

### Mining Social Media Without Context

Treating all social media as public and publishable ignores audience expectations, context, and the harm of decontextualized exposure. A post meant for a limited audience can be weaponized when republished to a mass audience.

### Exposing Family And Children

Reporting on a subject often drags in family members and children who did not choose attention. Publishing their identities, locations, or personal details usually adds no public value and causes collateral harm.

### Treating Grief As Content

Approaching people in acute grief, at funerals, at hospitals, or at scenes of tragedy for emotional quotes exploits vulnerability. Approach with consent, offer an exit, and prioritize the subject's welfare over material.

### Persistent Contact That Becomes Harassment

Repeated calls, messages, visits, or pressure after a refusal crosses from journalism into harassment. Respect a clear refusal and document the refusal rather than escalating pressure.

### Publishing Intimate Facts For Traffic

Intimate or salacious truths generate engagement but rarely serve public interest. Publishing them trades dignity for clicks and damages credibility.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the intrusive method serve a specific, proportional public-interest purpose, and have I documented why less intrusive alternatives are inadequate?
- [ ] Am I respecting spaces and contexts where a reasonable person would expect privacy?
- [ ] Have I distinguished public-figure conduct relevant to their role from private life that lacks public-interest justification?
- [ ] Have I limited contact with family, employers, neighbors, and vulnerable associates to what is essential, and respected refusals?
- [ ] Is any surveillance or monitoring proportionate, targeted to genuine public concern, and reviewed by senior editors and counsel?
- [ ] Are intimate and sensitive facts published only when central to accountability, consented to, or already made public by the subject?
- [ ] Did I give the subject notice and a meaningful response opportunity where practicable?
- [ ] Have I avoided exposing sources, witnesses, children, and bystanders to retaliation through unnecessary identification?
- [ ] For methods that could constitute intrusion under applicable law, have I consulted legal counsel and a senior editor, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and not a substitute for qualified legal review?
