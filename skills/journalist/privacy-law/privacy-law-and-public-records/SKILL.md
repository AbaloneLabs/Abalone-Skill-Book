# Privacy Law And Public Records

Journalists constantly work with material that is legally public but ethically charged. A court filing, a property record, a police blotter, a licensing database, or a mugshot repository may be open to anyone, yet publishing its contents can still expose private individuals to harassment, discrimination, violence, or lasting reputational damage. The judgment problem is that legal access and ethical publication are different standards, and a reporter who treats the first as a license for the second will eventually cause avoidable harm and erode trust.

Use this skill before publishing any identifying details drawn from public records, before naming private individuals who appear in official documents, and before republishing government data that aggregates sensitive personal information. The main risk is that an agent may assume "it is public, so it is fine" without weighing the public-interest justification, the vulnerability of the subject, the recency and relevance of the record, and the foreseeable consequences of amplification. This skill does not replace legal counsel; it prepares the journalist to make a defensible editorial decision and to know when to escalate.

## Core Rules

### Treat Legal Access As Necessary But Not Sufficient

Public-records law determines what a journalist may obtain and read. It does not determine what a journalist should publish. A record can be open by statute and still contain details that are cruel, gratuitous, or dangerous to republish. Before publishing, ask whether the information serves a concrete public-interest purpose that outweighs the privacy intrusion, not merely whether the document was lawfully obtained.

The strongest justification is accountability: exposing official misconduct, documenting systemic failure, verifying claims made by public figures, or correcting a record that affects public decisions. Curiosity, embarrassment value, search-engine optimization, or competitive pressure are not sufficient justifications on their own.

### Distinguish Public Figures From Private Individuals

The privacy calculus differs sharply between public officials, public figures, and private individuals. Public officials and limited-purpose public figures have voluntarily entered public life or a public controversy, and their conduct in that role carries a stronger public-interest claim. Private individuals, including ordinary people swept into a news event through misfortune, accident, crime, or bureaucratic error, deserve far greater privacy protection.

A person does not become a public figure merely because they appear in a record. A defendant in a minor case, a witness, a victim, a license applicant, or a property owner named in a filing is still a private individual for most purposes. Escalate scrutiny whenever the subject is not themselves the story.

### Assess The Relevance And Recency Of The Record

Old records carry different weight than recent ones. An arrest that did not lead to conviction, a bankruptcy from a decade ago, a dismissed complaint, or a sealed-then-unsealed filing may be technically public yet misleading or vindictive to republish. Ask whether the record reflects the current, accurate situation or whether it trades on stale, superseded, or one-sided information.

Consider whether the record has been corrected, expunged, sealed, vacated, or overturned. Publishing a record that the legal system has since repudiated can compound an injustice and may expose the outlet to legal risk. When in doubt, obtain the most current version and note its status.

### Weigh Foreseeable Harm From Amplification

Publication amplifies. A name buried in a courthouse database becomes the first search result after a news story. A mugshot that was locally available becomes globally indexable. Before publishing identifying details, anticipate downstream effects: online harassment, employment discrimination, immigration consequences, retaliation by abusers, doxxing, or physical danger. The fact that harm is hard to measure does not make it irrelevant.

For especially vulnerable subjects, consider partial identification, pseudonymization with disclosure, generalization, or withholding. If full identification is not essential to the public-interest purpose, narrow the disclosure.

### Apply Heightened Protection To Sensitive Categories

Certain categories warrant special caution regardless of legal access: the identity of sexual-assault victims, minors, juveniles in the justice system, immigration status, health and disability information, mental-health records, HIV status, sexual orientation where it could endanger the subject, home addresses, financial account details, and information that could facilitate stalking or violence. Many jurisdictions also impose statutory restrictions on some of these even when other parts of a record are public.

Default to non-publication for these categories unless the subject has consented, the information is central to a serious accountability story, or publication is essential to understand a matter of genuine public concern. Document the reasoning when an exception is made.

### Verify The Record Before Relying On It

Public records can contain errors, mismatched identities, stale data, or incomplete context. A name collision can implicate the wrong person. A database entry may reflect an arrest that was later dropped, a charge that was reduced, or a judgment that was reversed. Before naming someone based on a record, confirm identity through additional evidence and check for subsequent developments that change the meaning.

Cross-check spelling, dates of birth, addresses, and identifying numbers. A single-record match is often insufficient to responsibly name a private individual.

### Give Subjects Notice And A Response Opportunity

When a record will be used to criticize, expose, or identify a private individual, give the subject notice and a meaningful chance to respond before publication, where practicable. They may supply context, corrections, expungement records, or safety information that changes the decision. Notice is both an fairness obligation and a practical safeguard against error.

### Document The Public-Interest Rationale

Record the reasoning behind publication decisions involving private individuals and sensitive records. Note the public-interest purpose, the alternatives considered, the harm assessment, and any consultations with editors or counsel. This documentation supports accountability and helps later reviewers understand the basis for the decision.

## Common Traps

### Equating "Public Record" With "Publishable"

The most common trap is treating legal accessibility as ethical permission. A record can be lawfully obtained and still be gratuitous, cruel, or misleading to republish. The publication decision requires its own justification, independent of access.

### Republishing Mugshots And Booking Photos

Mugshot galleries and booking-photo slidesheds generate traffic but rarely serve a public-interest purpose proportional to the harm, especially when charges are minor, unadjudicated, or dropped. They can follow people indefinitely and fuel extortionate mugshot-removal sites. Treat mugshot republishing as a high-harm, often low-public-interest action.

### Stale Or Superseded Records

Publishing an old arrest, dismissed case, or reversed judgment as if current misleads the audience and harms the subject. Always check the disposition and the most recent status before relying on a record.

### Identity Mismatch

Relying on a name match alone, especially with common names, can implicate an innocent person. Verify identity with corroborating evidence before naming a private individual from a record.

### Aggregated Data Re-identification

A "de-identified" government dataset can often be re-identified when combined with other public data. Publishing derived findings that effectively identify individuals, even without naming them, can recreate the privacy harm the de-identification was meant to prevent.

### Treating Juvenile Records Like Adult Records

Juvenile justice records are often protected by statute and by a rehabilitative rationale that adult records lack. Publishing them can derail a young person's life for conduct the legal system treated as redeemable. Apply the strongest presumption against publication.

### Forgetting That Digital Is Permanent

A detail that was tolerable in a print edition of limited circulation becomes permanent and globally searchable online. The durability and reach of digital publication raise the harm threshold, especially for minor or old matters.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is there a concrete public-interest purpose for publishing this record that outweighs the privacy intrusion, beyond mere curiosity or traffic?
- [ ] Have I distinguished between public officials or public figures and private individuals, and applied stronger protection to the latter?
- [ ] Is the record current, accurate, and not superseded, expunged, sealed, vacated, or overturned?
- [ ] Have I verified the identity of the named subject through corroborating evidence, not a name match alone?
- [ ] Have I assessed foreseeable harm from amplification, including harassment, discrimination, retaliation, or physical danger?
- [ ] Have I applied heightened protection to sensitive categories such as sexual-assault victim identity, minors, juvenile records, immigration status, health information, and home addresses?
- [ ] Did I give the subject notice and a meaningful response opportunity where practicable?
- [ ] Have I documented the public-interest rationale and the alternatives considered, and consulted an editor?
- [ ] For high-risk or uncertain cases, have I consulted legal counsel and a senior editor before publishing, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and not a substitute for qualified legal review?
