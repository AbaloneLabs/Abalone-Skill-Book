# Claim Verification Methodology

A single unverified claim can collapse an entire story, trigger a defamation suit, or mislead millions of readers before a correction can catch up. Claim verification is the discipline of treating every assertion of fact as unproven until it is independently supported. The failure mode this skill prevents is not laziness; it is the quiet drift where a journalist accepts a claim because it came from a credible-seeming source, because it confirms a working theory, or because time pressure makes deeper checking feel optional. Verification methodology exists to make that drift visible and to produce a record that can withstand later challenge.

## Core Rules

### Treat every factual claim as a hypothesis, not a fact
A claim is a proposition that something is true. Until you have evidence, it is an unsupported assertion. Separate what a source said from what is actually known. "The mayor said the budget is balanced" is verifiable (did she say it?). "The budget is balanced" requires independent financial evidence. Conflating the two is the most common verification failure.

### Match verification rigor to potential harm
A claim that a restaurant has great pasta needs light checking. A claim that a named individual committed a crime, defrauded investors, or lied under oath demands the highest rigor: primary documents, multiple independent sources, and direct response from the subject. Calibrate effort to the reputational, legal, and social stakes, but never let a low-stakes claim slide entirely unexamined if it is load-bearing for the narrative.

### Require the right kind of source, not just any source
For each claim, identify the ideal evidence: an official record, a contemporaneous document, a named on-record source, a recording, data. Then determine what you actually have. A claim sourced to "officials familiar with the matter" is weaker than one sourced to a named official, which is weaker than a primary document. Know exactly which tier you are relying on and say so internally.

### Demand independence between sources
Two sources are not "two independent sources" if they both read the same press release, share the same supervisor, or are repeating a wire service report. Independence means neither source obtained the information from the other or from a common origin. Trace provenance: ask sources how they know, not just what they know.

### Go to the primary source whenever one exists
Secondary reporting, summaries, and press releases introduce paraphrase, omission, and spin. The primary document, dataset, recording, or statute is almost always more reliable and often contains details the summary omitted. Cite the primary when possible and note when you are relying on a secondary because the primary is unavailable.

### Give the subject of an accusation a genuine chance to respond
Before publishing a damaging claim, contact the person or institution it concerns, present the specific allegations in enough detail that they can meaningfully respond, and give a reasonable deadline. Document the attempt and the response (or non-response). This is both an ethical obligation and a practical protection against error and legal challenge.

### Document the verification chain for every load-bearing claim
For each significant claim, record: the claim itself, the source(s), how each source knows, the corroborating evidence, what remains unverified, and who was contacted for response. This record lets editors challenge weak links and lets you reconstruct the basis for publication if it is later disputed.

### Distinguish "verified," "consistent with," and "unverified"
Maintain explicit internal labels. "Verified" means the claim is supported to the standard appropriate to its stakes. "Consistent with" means it fits available evidence but is not independently confirmed. "Unverified" means you could not confirm it. Publish accordingly, and when a claim is only consistent-with or unverified, say so to readers rather than implying certainty.

## Common Traps

### Treating repetition as corroboration
A claim repeated by ten outlets that all cite the same single original report is still one source. Count provenance chains, not mentions. A viral fact is not a verified fact.

### Accepting a claim because the source is usually reliable
Even trustworthy sources are wrong, misremember, or have agendas on specific topics. Reliability is a prior, not proof. A reliable source lowers the burden of suspicion; it does not eliminate the need for evidence.

### Verifying the convenient claim and skipping the inconvenient one
Journalists under deadline verify the claims that are easy to check or that support the story, and quietly skip the ones that are hard or that might undermine it. Audit your own verification list for which claims got checked and which did not.

### Confusing "could not disprove" with "proved"
Absence of contradictory evidence is not evidence of truth. If you searched and found nothing contradicting the claim, you have shown the claim is unchallenged, not that it is true. State this accurately.

### Over-relying on a single dramatic document
One compelling record can feel like proof, but documents can be partial, forged, taken out of context, or describe only one slice of reality. Authenticate the document and contextualize it; do not let a single artifact carry a conclusion it cannot support alone.

### Failing to verify the negation
For high-stakes claims, ask what evidence would prove the claim false, and check whether that evidence exists. If you only look for confirmation, confirmation is all you will find.

### Letting the subject's denial count as the final word
A denial is a data point, often self-interested. Record it, weigh it, and investigate the substance rather than treating "they denied it" as closure.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is every load-bearing factual claim in the story matched to at least one identified source, with the source tier recorded?
- [ ] Have I confirmed that corroborating sources are genuinely independent in origin, not sharing a common upstream source?
- [ ] Did I locate and consult the primary source (document, record, dataset) wherever one exists, rather than relying on a summary or secondary report?
- [ ] For each damaging claim, did I contact the subject with specifics and a reasonable deadline, and document the attempt and any response?
- [ ] Have I labeled each significant claim internally as verified, consistent-with, or unverified, and reflected that status accurately to readers?
- [ ] Did I check for evidence that would disprove the claim, not only evidence that supports it?
- [ ] Have I traced the provenance of any claim sourced to anonymous officials or other outlets, rather than counting repetition as corroboration?
- [ ] Is the verification chain documented well enough that an editor or a future fact-checker could reconstruct and challenge each claim?
- [ ] Did I calibrate verification rigor to the potential harm, applying the strictest standard to accusations against named individuals?
- [ ] Before publication, is there any claim I am asserting as fact that I would be unable to defend with specific evidence if challenged?
