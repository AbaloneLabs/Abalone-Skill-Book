# Verification Chain Documentation

A story's accuracy is only as defensible as the record behind it. Verification chain documentation is the discipline of capturing, while reporting, exactly how each claim was established, so that editors, fact-checkers, lawyers, and the journalist's future self can reconstruct the basis for every assertion. The harm this skill prevents is the all-too-common situation where a story is challenged, an internal review is demanded, or a correction is contemplated, and no one can say precisely what evidence supported the contested claim or whether it was ever independently confirmed. Good documentation is not bureaucratic overhead; it is the spine of accountable journalism and the difference between a story that withstands scrutiny and one that collapses under it.

## Core Rules

### Document continuously, not retrospectively
Capture source details, evidence, and reasoning at the moment of reporting, when memory is fresh and context is intact. Reconstructing a verification chain days or weeks later, under deadline or legal pressure, produces gaps, confusions, and confident-sounding errors. Make documentation part of the reporting rhythm, not a post-publication chore.

### Tie every load-bearing claim to its evidence
For each significant factual assertion in the draft, maintain a record that names the claim, the source(s), how each source knows, the corroborating material, the verification status, and any remaining uncertainty. A claim floating free of its evidence in your notes is a claim that cannot be defended later.

### Record provenance, not just content
It matters not only what a source said but how the information reached you. For documents, note where they came from and through what hands. For data, record the source agency, release date, and any cleaning applied. For tips, trace the chain back as far as possible. Provenance is what distinguishes a primary record from a fourth-hand rumor dressed up as fact.

### Preserve the originals and the links
Keep original documents, recordings, emails, datasets, and screenshots in a secure, organized store, and link each to the claim it supports. Originals can be re-examined; paraphrases and memories cannot. When a source provides material, preserve it unaltered and note any redactions or transformations you apply for publication.

### Make independence explicit
When you rely on multiple sources for a claim, record whether they are genuinely independent or share a common origin. "Two sources" that both derived their information from the same report is not two-source confirmation. Documenting independence forces honesty about how strong the corroboration really is.

### Flag what is unverified or uncertain
A verification record is most useful when it is honest about its gaps. Explicitly mark claims that are consistent-with but not confirmed, claims resting on a single source, and claims you could not check. These flags guide editors to ask the right questions and prevent uncertain material from being published as established fact.

### Maintain a response and right-of-reply log
For every subject of an accusation or significant claim, record that they were contacted, what specific allegations were put to them, the deadline given, and the substance of any response or non-response. This log is essential for fairness, for legal protection, and for demonstrating that the subject had a genuine opportunity to comment.

### Design documentation to survive handoff and challenge
Write notes that an editor, fact-checker, or lawyer unfamiliar with the story can follow. Use clear claim-by-claim structure, avoid shorthand that only you understand, and keep the record organized so a specific assertion can be located quickly under pressure. Documentation that only the original reporter can interpret fails the moment it is most needed.

## Common Traps

### Trusting memory over records
Human memory is unreliable, especially for dates, numbers, and exact wording. A confident recollection of "I'm sure I verified that" is not a substitute for a note showing how. If it is not written down with its evidence, treat it as unverified.

### Documenting the supporting evidence and omitting the contradictory
A verification chain that records only what confirms the story is advocacy, not accountability. Note the evidence you found that cut against your conclusions and explain how you weighed it. Honest documentation includes the counter-evidence.

### Letting anonymity erode the record
Anonymous sourcing is sometimes necessary, but a verification chain that says only "a source" with no internal record of who it was, their basis of knowledge, and their reliability is indefensible. Maintain a confidential internal record of source identity and access even when the public attribution is anonymous, subject to your outlet's source-protection policies.

### Confusing "we have a document" with "we verified the claim"
A document supports a claim only if it is authentic, relevant, and correctly interpreted. Recording "verified per document X" without assessing what the document actually establishes invites overclaiming. Document the interpretive step, not just the possession of the artifact.

### Over-structuring until documentation becomes theater
Elaborate templates that no one fills in honestly are worse than simple notes. Documentation must be rigorous about evidence and provenance, but it should fit the story's scale. A brief, accurate chain beats a comprehensive-looking form full of guesswork.

### Failing to update the chain as the story changes
Stories evolve through editing; claims are added, softened, or sharpened. If the verification chain is not updated alongside the draft, it drifts out of sync and may no longer support what is actually published. Reconcile the record with the final text before publication.

### Discarding the chain after publication
The verification record is needed most when a story is later challenged, when a correction is considered, or when a source disputes their quotation. Preserve documentation according to your outlet's records policy, securely and for the long term, so the basis for publication remains answerable.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does every load-bearing claim in the final draft have a corresponding entry tying it to its source(s), evidence, and verification status?
- [ ] Is the provenance of each piece of evidence, documents and data included, recorded back toward its origin?
- [ ] Have I recorded whether corroborating sources are genuinely independent or share a common upstream origin?
- [ ] Are original documents, recordings, and datasets preserved unaltered and linked to the claims they support?
- [ ] Are uncertain, single-sourced, or unverified claims explicitly flagged rather than presented as established?
- [ ] Does the documentation include a response log showing which subjects were contacted, with what specifics, and their replies?
- [ ] Have I recorded counter-evidence and explained how I weighed it, not only the evidence that supports the story?
- [ ] Is the verification chain written clearly enough that an editor or lawyer new to the story could follow and challenge it?
- [ ] Has the chain been reconciled with the final published text, so it supports what actually appears in the story?
- [ ] Is the documentation preserved securely for future challenge, rather than discarded once the story is filed?
