# Synthetic And Manipulated Media Detection

The boundary between authentic and fabricated media has become technically blurred. Generative AI can produce photorealistic images of events that never happened, clone voices from seconds of sample audio, synthesize video of people saying things they never said, and manipulate real footage in ways that are increasingly difficult to detect. At the same time, simple manipulation, selective editing, cropping, and miscaptioning, remains effective and far more common. The judgment problem is dual: journalists must avoid being fooled by fabricated media, publishing synthetic content as authentic documentation, and they must avoid the opposite failure, dismissing authentic material as fake because it looks unusual or because bad actors promote false claims of fabrication to discredit genuine evidence. Both errors cause real harm: the first spreads disinformation under a credible masthead, and the second suppresses truthful reporting. This skill exists to bring discipline to an environment where seeing is no longer believing, but where seeing still matters.

Use this skill when assessing the authenticity of any photograph, video, audio, or screenshot of uncertain provenance, detecting manipulation or synthetic generation, or setting verification standards for visual and audio material. The main risk is that an agent either over-trusts compelling media or, reacting to the threat of fakes, over-dismisses authentic material, without applying a structured assessment.

## Core Rules

### Treat Authenticity As A Verified Conclusion, Not A Default

In an earlier era, a compelling photograph could be presumed authentic until questioned. That default is no longer safe. Treat the authenticity of any media of uncertain provenance as a conclusion that must be established through verification, not a starting assumption. This does not mean assuming everything is fake; it means that the bar for treating media as authentic documentation is active verification of provenance, context, and integrity, proportional to the stakes. Routine, low-stakes material may require lighter checking; material that accuses, exposes, or carries significant consequence demands rigorous authentication.

Calibrate the authentication effort to the harm that would follow from publishing a fake or from suppressing a real item.

### Establish Provenance As The First Defense

The strongest protection against synthetic and manipulated media is provenance. Media whose origin can be traced to a reliable creator, captured at a known time and place, with a clean chain of custody, is far less vulnerable to fabrication concerns than media that appeared anonymously on a platform. Prioritize establishing where the media came from, who made it, and how it reached the newsroom. Media with no establishable provenance carries inherently higher risk and should be treated accordingly, qualified, held, or subjected to more intensive technical authentication.

Contact the original creator wherever possible to confirm the circumstances of capture and to detect signs that the supposed creator is not the actual source.

### Apply Technical Authentication Proportional To The Stakes

Technical authentication examines the media itself for signs of manipulation or synthesis. For images, inspect metadata, look for inconsistencies in lighting, shadows, reflections, textures, and anatomy, check for artifacts characteristic of generative models, and use reverse image search to detect prior circulation or origin in a known dataset. For video, examine frames for temporal inconsistencies, boundary artifacts around inserted elements, audio desynchronization, and signs of face-swap or lip-sync manipulation. For audio, listen for unnatural prosody, artifacts, inconsistent background noise, and signs of voice cloning. Use specialized forensic tools and consult specialists for high-stakes or technically complex material.

Recognize the limits of technical detection: generative quality is improving, simple manipulations can be subtle, and the absence of detectable artifacts is not proof of authenticity. Technical signals inform but rarely settle the question alone.

### Cross-Check Against Independent Reality

Synthetic media often fails consistency with the real world. Cross-check the depicted content against independent evidence: does the scene match the known location, weather, time of day, and surrounding events? Do shadows, reflections, and environmental details align with the claimed time and place? Does the content conflict with other verified documentation of the same event? Does the depicted person's appearance, voice, and behavior match their known characteristics? Convergence across independent evidence strengthens confidence; conflict flags the material for deeper scrutiny.

A synthetic image may be internally flawless but inconsistent with the physical reality of the claimed event, which is why contextual cross-checking complements technical analysis.

### Resist Both Naive Trust And Cynical Dismissal

Two opposite failures plague verification in the synthetic media era. The first is naive trust: accepting compelling media because it looks real. The second, and increasingly common, is cynical dismissal: rejecting authentic media because it is unusual, because a bad actor claims it is fake, or because the verifier has internalized that nothing can be trusted. Both cause harm. The discipline is to hold media to a verification standard proportional to its stakes, neither accepting nor rejecting on impression. When authentic material is dismissed as fake, truthful reporting is suppressed and the disinformation goal of undermining credible evidence is achieved.

When bad actors promote claims that genuine media is synthetic, treat those claims as themselves requiring verification, not as reasons to abandon the material.

### Detect Simple Manipulation, Not Only Sophisticated Synthesis

While deepfakes and generative media receive attention, the majority of harmful manipulation remains simple: selective editing that reverses meaning, cropping that removes context, splicing audio from one source onto video from another, re-speeding or reordering footage, and miscaptioning authentic material. These techniques are technically unsophisticated but highly effective, and they may escape detection because the verifier focuses on detecting advanced synthesis. Examine all media for editing, sequencing, and contextual manipulation, not only for signs of generative fabrication.

The edit is often the manipulation: what was cut, reordered, or juxtaposed constructs a false meaning from authentic components.

### Document The Assessment And Label Honestly

For media of any significance, document the authentication performed, the tools and consultations used, the independent evidence checked, the residual uncertainty, and the basis for the authenticity conclusion. When publishing, label media honestly: distinguish verified authentic material from material with residual uncertainty, and never present synthetic or manipulated media as authentic documentation. If synthetic media is published to illustrate the existence of a disinformation campaign, label it clearly as fabricated and explain the context.

Honest labeling protects the audience and the masthead, and documentation defends the decision if it is later challenged.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Compelling Media Is Authentic

High-quality visuals suspend skepticism, but quality is no longer evidence of authenticity. This is a trap because generative media can be visually flawless. Verify provenance and integrity; do not infer authenticity from realism.

### Dismissing Authentic Media As Fake

Reacting to the threat of fakes by rejecting genuine evidence suppresses truthful reporting and advances disinformation goals. This is a trap because cynical dismissal is as damaging as naive trust. Hold media to a verification standard, not to an assumption of falsity.

### Trusting Metadata And Watermarks As Proof

Metadata can be stripped or spoofed; watermarks and platform labels can be absent, forged, or misleading. This is a trap because technical-looking signals feel authoritative. Use them as one signal among many.

### Focusing Only On Deepfakes And Missing Simple Edits

Attention to sophisticated synthesis can cause verifiers to miss unsophisticated but effective manipulation. This is a trap because selective editing and miscaptioning remain the most common harmful techniques. Examine all media for editing and context, not only for generative artifacts.

### Accepting Bad-Actor Claims That Real Media Is Fake

Disinformation campaigns routinely assert that authentic evidence is synthetic to discredit it. This is a trap because the claim itself feels prudent in the deepfake era. Treat claims of fabrication as requiring their own verification.

### Publishing Without Provenance Because The Content Looks Verified Internally

Internal consistency of an image or video is not sufficient without external provenance. This is a trap because a flawless synthetic can be internally consistent. Establish provenance and cross-check against independent reality.

### Overstating Detection Confidence

Forensic tools produce probabilistic signals, not certainty. This is a trap because overconfident pronouncements of "authentic" or "fake" collapse when wrong. Convey residual uncertainty honestly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the authenticity of the media treated as a conclusion to be verified rather than a default assumption, with effort calibrated to the stakes?
- [ ] Has provenance been established, tracing the media to a reliable creator and a clean chain of custody, with uncertain provenance reflected in how the media is treated?
- [ ] Has technical authentication been applied proportional to the stakes, using appropriate tools and specialist consultation, with its limits recognized?
- [ ] Has the content been cross-checked against independent reality, including location, weather, timing, environmental details, and consistency with other verified evidence?
- [ ] Has the assessment resisted both naive trust of compelling media and cynical dismissal of authentic material, holding each item to a verification standard rather than an impression?
- [ ] Has the media been examined for simple manipulation, editing, splicing, and contextual distortion, not only for sophisticated generative synthesis?
- [ ] Are claims that authentic media is fake, when promoted by bad actors, treated as requiring their own verification rather than accepted as reasons to abandon the material?
- [ ] Is the authentication documented, including tools, consultations, independent evidence, and residual uncertainty?
- [ ] Is the media labeled honestly to the audience, distinguishing verified authentic material from material with residual uncertainty, and never presenting synthetic or manipulated media as authentic documentation?
- [ ] For high-stakes or technically complex material, has specialist or forensic review been sought, recognizing that synthetic media detection is a specialized technical discipline?
