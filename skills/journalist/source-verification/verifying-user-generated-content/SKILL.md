# Verifying User-Generated Content

User-generated content, photographs, video, audio, and testimony captured by ordinary people and shared on platforms, has become a primary raw material of modern newsgathering. It appears first at the scene, often before any reporter arrives, and it can document events that no journalist was present to see. But UGC arrives without the safeguards of professional newsgathering: it has no verified provenance, no confirmed context, no editorial accountability, and often no identifiable or trustworthy author. The judgment problem is that the same properties that make UGC valuable, its immediacy and its presence at the scene, also make it dangerous, because fabricated, miscaptioned, recycled, and manipulated material circulates alongside genuine documentation, and the volume and speed of social platforms make careful verification feel optional. A newsroom that publishes unverified UGC under its own masthead lends its credibility to material that may be false, and once a false image or video circulates with a credible attribution, no correction travels as fast as the original.

Use this skill when verifying any material originating from the public or from social platforms: photographs, video clips, audio, posts, livestreams, and citizen testimony. The main risk is that an agent, under deadline and competitive pressure, treats striking UGC as verified because it looks authentic and is widely shared, when its provenance, context, and authorship remain unestablished.

## Core Rules

### Establish Provenance Before Treating Content As Evidence

The first task with any UGC is to determine where it came from, who created it, when, and under what circumstances. Material that circulates widely is often far removed from its origin, and each reshare strips context and invites misattribution. Trace the content to its earliest discoverable instance: the original post, the original uploader, the device or account that first published it. Use reverse image and video search, platform search, and timestamp analysis to locate the origin. Content whose provenance cannot be established should not be presented as established documentation, however compelling it appears, and should be qualified or held.

Provenance is not only who posted it last, but who captured it and whether the poster is the original creator. A repost by a large account is not provenance.

### Verify The Depicted Event Against Independent Evidence

Authentic content can depict the wrong event, the wrong time, or the wrong place. Confirm that the content shows what it purports to show by checking it against independent evidence: other verified material from the same event, official records, weather and shadow analysis, geolocation of visible landmarks, corroboration from witnesses on the scene, and consistency with reported facts. Geolocate the content by matching visible features to known locations, and verify the timing through metadata, environmental cues, and platform timestamps. A video that is genuine but from a different event misleads as fully as a fabrication.

Cross-check multiple independent pieces of UGC from the same event; convergence across independent captures strengthens confidence, while a single dramatic piece standing alone warrants extra scrutiny.

### Assess The Reliability And Motivation Of The Source

The person who created or shared the content is a source, and sources have motivations, biases, and reliability profiles. Identify the account or individual: their history, their location, their apparent vantage on the event, their prior content, and any signs of coordination, inauthenticity, or agenda. A first-hand witness at the scene is a different source than an account that aggregates and reposts, and a source with a record of reliable documentation is different from one with a pattern of sensationalism or fabrication. Where possible, contact the original creator directly to confirm the circumstances of capture and obtain additional context and permission.

Be alert to coordinated inauthentic behavior, accounts operated to inject false or misleading material into news cycles, and to sources with a stake in how the event is portrayed.

### Authenticate Against Manipulation And Fabrication

UGC may be manipulated or fabricated, and the technical sophistication of manipulation is increasing. Examine images and video for signs of editing: inconsistent lighting and shadows, mismatched metadata, cloned or repeated elements, unnatural boundaries, compression artifacts suggesting re-saving, and resolution inconsistencies. Check audio for signs of editing, desynchronization, and generation loss. Use technical tools appropriate to the medium: reverse image search, metadata inspection, frame analysis, forensic tools, and specialist consultation for high-stakes or technically complex material. Treat the absence of manipulation evidence as one signal, not as proof of authenticity.

For material where fabrication is plausible and the stakes are high, do not publish without specialist or forensic review.

### Preserve Context And Avoid Misleading Use

Verified UGC can still mislead if presented without its context. Establish and convey what the content shows and, critically, what it does not show: the moments before and after, the wider scene outside the frame, the duration and completeness of the recording, and any ambiguity in what is depicted. A few seconds of a longer encounter, presented as the whole, can distort meaning entirely. Provide the reader with the context needed to interpret the content honestly, and avoid cropping, editing, or framing UGC in ways that change its meaning.

State clearly what has been verified and what remains uncertain, rather than implying complete confirmation.

### Secure Rights, Consent, And Ethical Use

UGC is created by people who hold rights to their material and who may be depicted in it. Obtain permission from the creator before publishing, which is both an ethical and often a legal obligation, and assess whether publishing the content exposes identifiable individuals, especially minors, victims, or vulnerable subjects, to harm. A verified, well-contextualized piece of UGC may still be unethical to publish because of who it depicts or what it reveals. Rights, consent, and harm are part of the verification, not a separate concern.

### Document The Verification Chain

For each piece of UGC, record the provenance, the authentication performed, the independent evidence consulted, the source assessment, the context established, the rights and consent status, and any residual uncertainty. This documentation supports editorial review, defends the use of the material against later challenge, and creates the trail needed if a correction or takedown becomes necessary. UGC published without a documented verification chain is indefensible if challenged.

## Common Traps

### Treating Viral Spread As Verification

Content shared thousands of times feels verified by consensus, but virality measures spread, not truth. This is a trap because widespread circulation often originates from a single unverified source. Count provenance, not mentions.

### Confirming The Content Exists And Calling It Verified

Locating a piece of UGC is not verifying it. This is a trap because the check confirms presence, not authenticity or context. Establish provenance, depicted event, and source reliability before treating it as evidence.

### Using Authentic Content From The Wrong Event

A genuine image or video from a different time or place is the most common UGC error. This is a trap because the material is real, lowering suspicion, while the attribution is false. Geolocate and time-verify against independent evidence.

### Trusting Metadata And Timestamps Without Question

Metadata can be stripped, spoofed, or inherited; platform timestamps reflect posting, not capture. This is a trap because technical-looking data feels authoritative. Use metadata as one signal among several.

### Presenting A Fragment As The Whole Event

A few seconds or a cropped frame cannot represent a full encounter. This is a trap because the fragment is authentic but the implied completeness is false. Convey what is and is not shown.

### Ignoring The Source's Motivation And Reliability

Content from an account with an agenda or a history of fabrication carries different weight than content from a reliable first-hand witness. This is a trap because the content is evaluated in isolation from its source. Assess who made it and why.

### Publishing Without Rights, Consent, Or Harm Review

Verified UGC may still be unlawful or unethical to publish. This is a trap because verification of content crowds out verification of use. Check rights, consent, and harm as part of the process.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the provenance of the content been traced to its origin, identifying the original creator and the circumstances of capture, rather than the most recent poster?
- [ ] Has the depicted event been verified against independent evidence, including geolocation, timing, and corroboration from other sources and reporting?
- [ ] Has the source, account or individual, been assessed for reliability, motivation, history, and signs of coordination or inauthenticity?
- [ ] Has the content been examined for manipulation or fabrication using appropriate technical tools and specialist review where stakes warrant?
- [ ] Is the context established and conveyed, including what the content does not show, so that a fragment is not presented as a complete account?
- [ ] Are rights, permission from the creator, and consent and harm considerations for depicted individuals resolved before publication?
- [ ] Is the verification chain documented, including provenance, authentication, independent evidence, source assessment, context, rights, and residual uncertainty?
- [ ] Before publication, is there any UGC in the story whose authenticity or context rests on assumption rather than established evidence?
- [ ] For high-stakes, contested, or technically suspicious material, has specialist or forensic review been sought rather than relying on lay judgment?
- [ ] Is it clear to the reader what has been verified and what remains uncertain, rather than implying complete confirmation where gaps remain?
