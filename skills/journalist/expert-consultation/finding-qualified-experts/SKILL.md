# Finding Qualified Experts

Expertise lends journalism authority and accuracy, but only if the experts are genuinely qualified for the specific question and free from undisclosed interests. The harm this skill prevents is the chronic failure of relying on experts who are prominent rather than relevant, who speak outside their actual competence, who are paid advocates presented as independent scholars, or who represent a narrow slice of opinion presented as the consensus of a field. Finding qualified experts is a craft of matching expertise to the precise question, verifying credentials independently, seeking diverse and independent voices, and recognizing the difference between genuine expertise and confident commentary.

## Core Rules

### Match the expert's specific expertise to the specific question
Expertise is narrow. A distinguished cardiologist is not qualified to opine on virology; a legal scholar of contracts is not an authority on constitutional interpretation. Identify the precise subfield the question belongs to and seek experts whose work sits squarely within it. Prominence in an adjacent field is not a substitute for relevant expertise.

### Verify credentials independently, not from the expert's self-presentation
Check academic appointments, peer-reviewed publications, professional standing, and disciplinary recognition through independent sources, not from the expert's biography or a press release. Apparent authority, titles, media presence, and confident delivery are easy to manufacture and are routinely mistaken for genuine qualification.

### Disclose and weigh conflicts of interest
Experts often have financial ties, consulting relationships, ideological commitments, or institutional positions that shape their views. Ask about conflicts directly, verify through disclosure databases and funding records where possible, and disclose them to readers. An expert with a conflict may still be informative, but the audience is entitled to know the interest.

### Seek independent and diverse expert voices
Do not rely on a single expert, or on experts who all share an institutional, ideological, or disciplinary perspective. Seek voices from different institutions, different methodological traditions, and, where relevant, different demographic and geographic backgrounds. A narrow expert pool produces a narrow account of what a field actually thinks.

### Distinguish consensus from minority and contested views
Understand where the expert's view sits within their field. Is it the prevailing consensus, a mainstream-but-contested position, or a minority or fringe view? Represent each appropriately: consensus as the established position, contested views as debated, and minority views as such. Presenting a fringe view as mainstream, or vice versa, misleads readers about the state of knowledge.

### Prefer experts who engage with evidence over advocates who assert
Strong experts explain the basis of their views, acknowledge uncertainty and counter-evidence, and distinguish what is established from what is speculative. Advocates assert conclusions, dismiss contrary evidence, and speak with false certainty. Prefer the former; their intellectual honesty makes them more useful and more credible to readers.

### Cultivate expert relationships over time, with independence
Build a network of trusted experts across beats whom you can consult for context and referral. Maintain the relationship through genuine intellectual engagement, not transactional favor-seeking, and preserve your independence: do not become so close to any expert that you cannot report critically on their field or institution.

### Use experts to understand, then verify independently
Experts help you grasp the significance of facts, frame questions, and identify what to look for, but their characterizations are not a substitute for your own verification of the underlying claims. Treat expert input as guidance and context, corroborate the factual assertions, and retain editorial judgment over the conclusions.

## Common Traps

### Relying on prominence over relevance
The expert who is most visible in media is not necessarily the most qualified for your specific question. Media prominence is often a function of availability, willingness to speak boldly, and public-relations machinery, not of relevant expertise. Seek relevance first.

### Using a qualified expert outside their field
A Nobel laureate in physics opining on public policy, or a famous surgeon on infectious disease, trades on generalized authority to cover a lack of specific expertise. Confine each expert's contribution to the domain they actually work in.

### Quoting paid spokespeople as independent experts
Industry-funded think tanks, PR firms, and consulting arrangements produce "experts" whose role is advocacy dressed as scholarship. Check funding sources and institutional affiliations, and present paid advocates as such, never as disinterested authorities.

### Presenting a single expert as the voice of the field
One expert's view, however distinguished, is one view. Without context about where it sits in the field, readers cannot judge its weight. Triangulate across experts and convey the range of informed opinion.

### Failing to disclose conflicts
An expert with undisclosed financial or ideological ties misleads readers about the independence of the view. Always ask about conflicts, verify where possible, and disclose in the story.

### Confusing confidence with correctness
Experts who speak with great certainty are more quotable and more reassuring, but certainty is not accuracy, especially in fields where knowledge is uncertain. Prefer experts who convey the actual state of confidence in their field, including its limits.

### Over-relying on a small circle of go-to experts
Newsrooms develop Rolodexes of familiar experts and return to them repeatedly, producing coverage that reflects that small circle's perspectives and blind spots. Continuously expand and diversify the expert network.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the expert's specific, demonstrated expertise match the precise question the story is asking?
- [ ] Have I verified credentials independently through publications, appointments, and disciplinary recognition?
- [ ] Have I asked about and disclosed conflicts of interest, including financial ties and ideological commitments?
- [ ] Am I consulting a diverse, independent set of experts rather than a single voice or a narrow circle?
- [ ] Do I understand where each expert's view sits within the field, and am I representing consensus, contested, and minority views appropriately?
- [ ] Am I preferring experts who engage with evidence and uncertainty over advocates who assert with false certainty?
- [ ] Are my expert relationships cultivated with independence, allowing me to report critically on their field when needed?
- [ ] Am I using expert input to understand and frame, while independently verifying the factual claims?
- [ ] Have I checked that I am not relying on prominence, media visibility, or generalized authority in place of relevant expertise?
- [ ] Before publication, would a knowledgeable reader in the field recognize my expert sourcing as qualified, diverse, and fairly represented?
