# Managing Expert Relationships

Experts are not interchangeable quote-dispensers; they are ongoing relationships that, well managed, provide reliable context, referrals, and reality-checking across years of coverage. The harm this skill prevents is the corrosion of these relationships through misuse: over-relying on a few experts until they burn out, growing so close that independence and critical distance are lost, failing to reciprocate or acknowledge, or allowing a single expert's perspective to dominate a beat's framing. Managing expert relationships is a long-term discipline of mutual respect, independence, diversification, and clear expectations, and it determines whether your expert network deepens or decays over time.

## Core Rules

### Treat expert relationships as long-term and reciprocal
Experts give time because they value informed coverage of their field and respect journalists who engage seriously. Reciprocate with fairness, accuracy, attribution when promised, credit for their contributions, and genuine intellectual engagement. Transactional use, calling only when you need a quote, exhausts experts and ends the relationship. Invest in the relationship across stories, not just at deadlines.

### Preserve independence and critical distance
Closeness with experts can shift your framing toward their perspective and dull your willingness to report critically on their field, institution, or allies. Maintain a diverse expert pool so no single voice dominates, and preserve the editorial distance to challenge or look beyond any expert's view. Independence is maintained actively, not assumed.

### Diversify the expert network continuously
Any stable of experts develops blind spots, shared assumptions, and institutional biases. Continuously seek new experts, especially from underrepresented institutions, demographics, methodological traditions, and viewpoints. A diverse network produces more complete coverage and guards against capture by a single school of thought.

### Be clear about attribution and expectations
Establish how each expert will be used: on-record, for background, for context only, for referral to others. Honor those terms. Experts who are surprised to find themselves quoted, or misattributed, lose trust in the journalist and the outlet. Clarity protects the relationship and the reporting.

### Do not overuse any single expert
The same expert quoted in every story becomes a crutch and a signal of shallow sourcing, to readers and to the field. Rotate voices, and recognize when you are returning to a familiar expert out of habit rather than fit. Overuse also burns out the expert and crowds out other qualified voices.

### Verify expert claims like any other source
Expertise lends credibility but does not guarantee accuracy or freedom from bias. Apply verification to expert assertions, especially when they are surprising, self-serving, or outside the expert's core competence. Trust earned through track record lowers suspicion; it does not eliminate the duty to check.

### Handle disagreement and error professionally
When an expert is wrong, or when you must report against an expert's interest or institution, do so with fairness and transparency. Communicate directly when coverage may be unwelcome to them. Conversely, when you make an error involving an expert's work, correct it promptly and directly. Professionalism in difficulty sustains relationships that easy moments cannot test.

### Give credit and maintain visibility
Acknowledge experts' contributions appropriately, cite their work when relevant, and let them know when their input shaped a story. Visibility and credit are part of the reciprocal economy of expert relationships and sustain engagement over time.

## Common Traps

### Over-reliance on a familiar few
Returning to the same experts is easy and fast, but it narrows coverage and signals lazy sourcing. The familiar expert is not always the best fit. Resist the gravitational pull of the Rolodex.

### Going native with a school of thought
Deep engagement with one expert or one institution can assimilate their framing and blind spots into your coverage. Actively seek experts from competing traditions to maintain perspective.

### Transactional, deadline-only contact
Contacting experts only when you urgently need a quote treats them as instruments and erodes goodwill. Sustained, non-urgent engagement, sharing relevant work, asking for their read on developments, builds the trust that pays off at deadline.

### Failing to disclose or manage conflicts
Experts develop conflicts over time, new funding, institutional roles, advocacy positions. Revisit conflict disclosures periodically, not just at first contact, and disclose changes to readers.

### Misattributing or surprising an expert
Quoting an expert in a way they did not expect, or using background comments on record, breaches trust and ends the relationship. Confirm attribution terms and share relevant context before publication.

### Burning out a helpful expert
The expert who always responds gets asked most often, until they stop responding. Rotate requests and respect experts' time, recognizing that responsiveness is a finite resource.

### Losing independence through friendship
When professional relationships become personal friendships, the ability to report critically on the expert, their institution, or their field can be compromised. Recognize when friendship has created a conflict and manage it through disclosure, recusal, or additional sourcing.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Am I investing in expert relationships across stories, with reciprocity and intellectual engagement, not just at deadlines?
- [ ] Have I preserved enough independence and critical distance to report against an expert's view or institution when warranted?
- [ ] Am I continuously diversifying my expert network across institutions, demographics, traditions, and viewpoints?
- [ ] Are attribution and usage expectations clear and honored for each expert?
- [ ] Am I rotating voices and avoiding over-reliance on any single expert, however convenient?
- [ ] Do I apply verification to expert claims, especially surprising or self-serving ones, rather than taking them on authority?
- [ ] Do I handle disagreement, unwelcome coverage, and my own errors with directness and professionalism?
- [ ] Have I revisited conflict disclosures for long-standing experts, not just at first contact?
- [ ] Am I giving appropriate credit and maintaining the visibility that sustains expert engagement?
- [ ] Have I recognized where professional relationships have become personal friendships, and managed any resulting conflicts?
