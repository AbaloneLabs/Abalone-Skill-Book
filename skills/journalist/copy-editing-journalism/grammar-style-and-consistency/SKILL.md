# Grammar Style And Consistency

Copy-editing is the last layer of defense before publication, and its errors are the most visible to readers. A grammar mistake, an inconsistent name spelling, a wrong title, or a style violation undermines credibility even when the reporting is sound, because readers use surface quality as a proxy for the care behind the whole story. The judgment problem is that copy-editing must fix errors without changing meaning, must enforce consistency without pedantry, and must catch the small mistakes that large-scale editing misses, all under deadline pressure.

Use this skill when copy-editing drafts for grammar, usage, house style, consistency, and mechanics. The main risk is that an agent may "correct" a phrasing in a way that alters meaning or introduces a factual error, or may apply style rules mechanically in ways that harm clarity. This skill is editorial guidance; copy-editing decisions follow the outlet's house style and the responsible editor.

## Core Rules

### Copy-Edit For Accuracy First, Style Second

The copy-editor's first duty is to ensure the words are correct: names spelled right, titles accurate, dates and numbers consistent, quotes faithful, attributions precise, and facts internally consistent with the rest of the piece. A style preference never overrides accuracy. If enforcing a style rule would change a name, a title, a quote, or a factual detail, do not make the change; query it. Accuracy errors introduced at the copy-edit stage are particularly damaging because they pass final review.

Treat every name, number, date, title, and quote as a potential error point and verify each against the source material or fact-check.

### Follow House Style Consistently

House style exists to give the outlet a consistent voice and to settle recurring decisions: capitalization, abbreviations, numerals versus words, titles, datelines, courtesy titles, and punctuation conventions. Apply house style consistently within and across pieces, because inconsistency confuses readers and signals carelessness. When house style is silent or ambiguous on a point, make a defensible choice and apply it consistently, and flag the question for the style keeper.

Do not impose personal preferences over house style. The outlet's consistency matters more than any individual's stylistic taste.

### Preserve Quotes Faithfully

Quotation marks signal that the words are the speaker's, and copy-editing must not alter that contract. Correct obvious transcription errors only when the meaning is unambiguous and the correction is non-substantive, such as a clear "the" versus "a," and even then, query when in doubt. Do not silently smooth a speaker's grammar, remove filler, or restructure a quote for readability, because these changes misrepresent what was said. Use brackets for necessary insertions and ellipses for omissions, and use them sparingly and honestly.

A quote that has been silently rewritten is no longer a quote, and the distortion can be exposed and damaging.

### Maintain Internal Consistency

A piece must be consistent with itself: the same name spelled the same way throughout, the same title, the same date, the same number, the same order of events. Internal inconsistency is a common and credibility-damaging error. Read through specifically for consistency, checking that a person introduced one way is referred to consistently, that figures match when they recur, and that the timeline is coherent.

Cross-references within a piece, such as "as mentioned earlier," must point to what was actually said earlier.

### Edit For Clarity Without Changing Meaning

Copy-editing improves clarity, but clarity edits must preserve meaning. Restructuring a sentence to make it clearer is legitimate; restructuring it in a way that changes what it says is not. When a sentence is ambiguous, prefer the interpretation the surrounding context supports, and if the ambiguity is material, query rather than guess. Beware of edits that shift emphasis, alter scope, or introduce implications, because these are meaning changes disguised as style improvements.

When in doubt about whether an edit changes meaning, leave the original and query the reporter.

### Handle Numbers, Dates, And Units Carefully

Numbers are a frequent source of error and distortion. Check that figures are consistent, that units are correct, that percentages have their base, that ranges make sense, that comparisons are like-for-like, and that large numbers are not confused, millions versus billions, for example. Dates must be unambiguous, with time zones and formats consistent. Currency should be specified and, where relevant, converted with a stated rate and date.

A transposed digit or a misplaced unit can change a story's meaning and is hard to catch by reading alone; verify against the source.

### Query, Do Not Guess

When the copy-editor is unsure whether something is an error, the right response is a query to the reporter or editor, not a guess. Guessing risks introducing an error or "fixing" something that was correct. Queries should be specific, noting the potential problem and the reason for uncertainty, so the reporter can resolve them efficiently. The copy-editor's judgment adds value by flagging issues, not by unilaterally resolving substantive questions.

Build a query process that is efficient under deadline, so that uncertainty does not become a bottleneck.

### Match Copy-Edit Depth To Story Stakes

Not every story needs the same copy-editing depth. A brief needs a quick accuracy and consistency pass; a long investigation, an obituary, or a story with many names and numbers needs careful, methodical copy-editing. Calibrate effort to stakes, concentrating attention where errors are most likely and most damaging. Under-copy-editing high-stakes stories lets errors through; over-copy-editing routine briefs wastes scarce time.

## Common Traps

### Style Over Accuracy

Enforcing a style rule in a way that changes a name, title, or fact introduces an error to satisfy consistency.

### Silent Quote Rewriting

Smoothing a speaker's grammar or restructuring a quote misrepresents what was said and breaks the quotation contract.

### Meaning Changes Disguised As Clarity

Restructuring a sentence for readability can shift emphasis, scope, or implication without the editor noticing.

### Number And Unit Errors

Transposed digits, confused magnitudes, and wrong units change a story's meaning and are hard to catch by reading.

### Internal Inconsistency

A name spelled two ways or a figure that changes within a piece signals carelessness and confuses readers.

### Guessing Instead Of Querying

Resolving uncertainty by guessing risks introducing errors or "fixing" correct text.

### Mechanical Style Application

Applying rules without judgment can harm clarity, such as forcing a construction that obscures meaning.

### Uniform Depth

Treating a brief and an investigation the same either over-invests in trivia or under-invests in error-prone work.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have all names, titles, dates, numbers, quotes, and attributions been verified for accuracy against source material or fact-check?
- [ ] Is house style applied consistently within and across pieces, without personal preference overriding it?
- [ ] Are quotes preserved faithfully, with corrections limited to unambiguous non-substantive transcription fixes and bracketed insertions used sparingly?
- [ ] Is the piece internally consistent in names, titles, figures, dates, and timeline?
- [ ] Do clarity edits preserve meaning, with ambiguous material queried rather than guessed?
- [ ] Are numbers, dates, units, currency, and percentages checked for consistency, correct magnitude, and like-for-like comparison?
- [ ] Are uncertainties flagged as specific queries rather than resolved by guessing?
- [ ] Is copy-edit depth calibrated to the story's stakes and error likelihood?
- [ ] Has the copy-edited version been checked against the fact-checked version so that no edit introduced or restored an error, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that final authority rests with the responsible editor?
