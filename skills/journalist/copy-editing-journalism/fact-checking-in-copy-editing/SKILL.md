# Fact Checking In Copy Editing

Fact-checking is the systematic verification of every checkable assertion in a story against source material, and it is distinct from copy-editing, editing, and reporting, though it overlaps with all three. The judgment problem is that a story can read fluently and survive editing while containing small factual errors, transposed numbers, misspelled names, misattributed quotes, or unsupported assertions, and these errors erode credibility and create liability even when the story's thesis is sound. Fact-checking catches what the human reading process misses, because the reader, editor, and reporter all know what the text is supposed to say.

Use this skill when fact-checking a draft before publication, when integrating fact-check corrections, and when deciding what level of fact-checking a story requires. The main risk is that an agent may treat fact-checking as a formality or may apply it unevenly, verifying easy facts while skipping the central claims that matter most. This skill is editorial guidance; fact-check standards and authority follow the outlet's process and the responsible editor.

## Core Rules

### Fact-Check Proportionate To Stakes

Not every story needs the same fact-checking depth. A brief needs verification of names, dates, and key figures. An investigation, an exposé, an obituary, or a story making damaging claims needs comprehensive checking of every checkable assertion, with the central claims receiving the most rigorous scrutiny. Calibrate the fact-check to the stakes: the more damaging the claims, the more vulnerable the subjects, or the greater the legal exposure, the deeper the checking must be.

Define what "checked" means for this story before beginning, so the process is complete rather than impressionistic.

### Check The Central Claims Most Rigorously

The most important assertions, the thesis, the headline implication, the accusations, and the quantified claims, deserve the strongest verification, because errors there do the most damage. It is common for fact-checks to verify peripheral details thoroughly while assuming the central claims have been vetted by reporting. Reverse the tendency: confirm that the central claims are supported by the cited evidence and the reporting record, not just that the names and dates are right.

If a central claim cannot be verified to the standard its stakes require, escalate to the editor rather than passing the check.

### Verify Against Primary Sources Where Possible

Prefer primary sources over secondary ones: the court filing over the summary, the dataset over the press release, the recording over the transcript, the official record over the news report. Secondary sources, including other outlets, can propagate errors that a primary check would catch. For each checkable assertion, identify the most authoritative source and verify against it, noting where the source is secondary and therefore less reliable.

When the only available source is secondary, note the limitation and consider whether the assertion should be hedged.

### Check Names, Titles, Numbers, Dates, And Quotes Methodically

These are the most common error points and the most credibility-damaging when wrong. Verify the spelling of every name against an authoritative source. Verify titles, affiliations, and roles, which change over time. Verify numbers for correct magnitude, unit, and consistency, including denominators and bases for percentages. Verify dates, including year and time zone where relevant. Verify quotes against the recording or transcript, word for word, including attribution and context.

A single transposed digit or misspelled name can undermine a story's credibility and, in some cases, its legal position.

### Preserve The Distinction Between Checking And Rewriting

The fact-checker verifies; the reporter and editor rewrite. When the fact-check finds an error, the correction should be made by or in consultation with the reporter, because the reporter knows the material and the sources. The fact-checker should not silently rewrite, because uncoordinated changes can introduce new errors or alter meaning. Document each finding, the proposed correction, and the source, and route corrections through the reporter and editor.

This preserves accountability and prevents the fact-check from becoming an uncontrolled edit.

### Re-Check After Late Edits

Edits made after the fact-check, often under deadline, are a frequent source of error because they bypass the checking process. Any substantive change after fact-check, a new sentence, a revised number, a changed quote, an added claim, must be re-checked before publication. Establish a rule that the fact-checked version is the baseline and that deviations require re-verification, however small the window.

Late edits are where carefully checked stories acquire last-minute errors.

### Document The Check And The Sources

Maintain a record of what was checked, against what source, and with what result. This documentation supports the story's defensibility, enables later review if a claim is challenged, and creates an institutional record of the checking process. For anonymous or sensitive sources, document the verification without exposing identities. The record should allow a later reviewer to understand how each checkable assertion was verified.

Documentation is also what distinguishes a real fact-check from an impression of care.

### Integrate Fact-Check With Legal Review Where Needed

For stories with legal exposure, the fact-check and legal review should inform each other. The fact-check confirms what is asserted and supported; legal review assesses the risk of those assertions. A claim that passes fact-check may still raise legal concerns, and a legal concern may require additional checking. Coordinate the two so that neither operates in isolation.

## Common Traps

### Checking Peripherals, Skipping Centrals

Thoroughly verifying names and dates while assuming the central claims are sound misses the errors that matter most.

### Relying On Secondary Sources

Verifying against summaries or other outlets propagates the errors a primary-source check would catch.

### Silent Rewriting By The Fact-Checker

Uncoordinated corrections can introduce new errors or alter meaning, bypassing the reporter's knowledge.

### Late Edits After Fact-Check

Changes made after checking, under deadline, bypass the process and introduce last-minute errors.

### Number Magnitude Confusion

Transposed digits and confused units, millions versus billions, change meaning and are hard to catch by reading.

### Quote Drift

Quotes that drift from the recording, through paraphrase or smoothing, misrepresent the speaker.

### Undocumented Checks

Without a record of what was checked and how, the fact-check cannot be reviewed or defended later.

### Fact-Check Isolated From Legal Review

Checking and legal assessment operating in isolation miss the interaction between what is asserted and what is risky.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the fact-check depth proportionate to the story's stakes, with the standard of checking defined before beginning?
- [ ] Are the central claims, thesis, headline implication, and accusations verified to the most rigorous standard, with escalation when they cannot be supported?
- [ ] Are checkable assertions verified against primary sources where possible, with secondary-source limitations noted?
- [ ] Are names, titles, numbers, dates, units, and quotes verified methodically against authoritative sources?
- [ ] Are corrections made by or in consultation with the reporter, with the fact-checker documenting findings rather than silently rewriting?
- [ ] Are substantive changes after the fact-check re-checked before publication?
- [ ] Is the check documented with sources, allowing later review without exposing sensitive identities?
- [ ] Is the fact-check coordinated with legal review for stories with legal exposure?
- [ ] Has the responsible editor confirmed that the fact-check is complete to the standard the story requires, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that fact-check authority follows the outlet's process?
