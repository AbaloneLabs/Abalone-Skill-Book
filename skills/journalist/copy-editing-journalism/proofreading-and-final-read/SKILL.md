# Proofreading And Final Read

Proofreading is the final read before publication, and it catches a different class of error than editing or fact-checking: typos, broken links, mismatched captions, formatting corruption, layout problems, and discrepancies between the approved final and the version that actually publishes. The judgment problem is that these errors are superficial yet highly visible, they survive every earlier stage because they are introduced late or exist in surfaces earlier stages did not review, and they reach the audience as evidence of carelessness even when the journalism is excellent.

Use this skill when performing a final proofread before publication and when verifying that the published version matches the approved final across all surfaces. The main risk is that an agent may treat proofreading as redundant after editing and fact-checking, missing the errors that only a fresh, surface-level read can catch. This skill is editorial guidance; final publication sign-off rests with the responsible editor.

## Core Rules

### Proofread A Fresh Render, Not The Editing Draft

Proofreading must occur against the version the audience will see, not the draft that was edited. Read the rendered article, the actual published or preview view, because formatting, layout, embedding, and content-management transformations can introduce errors that do not exist in the source text. A clean editing draft can publish with a broken pull quote, a misrendered character, a missing paragraph break, or a corrupted table if no one reads the rendered version.

Always proof the render that will go live, ideally on both desktop and mobile where the layout differs.

### Read Every Surface The Audience Encounters

The article body is one surface among many. Proofread the headline, subhead, dek, byline, dateline, image, caption, alt text, pull quotes, embedded media, charts and their labels, related links, tags, and any modules that appear with the story. Each surface can carry its own error, and each reaches readers independently. A correct body with a wrong caption or a mismatched image still misleads.

Pay particular attention to surfaces that are auto-generated or templated, because they are less likely to have been reviewed manually.

### Check Links, Embeds, And Interactive Elements

Broken links, dead embeds, non-functional interactive elements, and missing media are common and damaging. Click every link to confirm it resolves to the intended destination, not a paywall, a 404, a competitor, or an unintended page. Confirm embeds, videos, audio players, charts, and interactive features render and function. A story that points readers to a broken resource frustrates the audience and undermines credibility.

Verify that links go to the intended and current version of the destination, not a stale or revised page.

### Verify Captions, Images, And Visual Elements

Captions must match their images, images must match the story, and visual elements must be accurate and correctly labeled. Check that a photo depicts what the caption says, that the people identified are correctly named, that archival or file images are labeled as such, that charts use the correct data and axes, and that no visual element distorts the story. Mismatched visuals are a frequent and embarrassing error.

Confirm image rights and credits are present and correct, and that any required disclosure, such as archival or composite labeling, appears.

### Confirm The Published Version Matches The Approved Final

Between final approval and publication, content-management steps, scheduling, and distribution can introduce discrepancies. After publication, verify that the live version matches the approved final: the same headline, the same body, the same images, the same links, and the same metadata. A scheduled or auto-published version can differ from what was approved, sometimes reverting to an earlier draft or omitting a correction.

For high-stakes stories, confirm the live version immediately after publication and again after any update.

### Proofread Across Desktop And Mobile

Layout, truncation, and rendering differ between desktop and mobile, and errors that are invisible on one surface may be glaring on another. A headline that fits on desktop may truncate badly on mobile; an image that renders on desktop may fail on mobile; a layout that works in one view may break in another. Proofread on both, because a large share of the audience reads on mobile.

Where feasible, check additional surfaces such as email renders, RSS feeds, and AMP or accelerated versions, which can strip or alter formatting.

### Catch Typos, Spelling, And Punctuation In The Final Read

Even after copy-editing, typos survive, especially in late-added material, headlines, captions, and metadata that may not have received the same scrutiny as the body. Read for spelling, punctuation, repeated or omitted words, and formatting artifacts. A single typo in a headline or a person's name can dominate reader response to an otherwise strong story.

Read slowly and out of sequence where possible, because the brain corrects familiar text and misses errors in context.

### Verify Metadata, SEO, And Distribution Fields

Metadata that the audience may not see directly still affects how the story is discovered and represented: the SEO title and description, social card text, URL slug, tags, and section assignment. Verify that these fields are accurate and consistent with the story, because they shape search results, social previews, and aggregation. A wrong SEO title or social card can misrepresent the story to readers who never click through.

Confirm the URL slug is clean, permanent, and descriptive, and that it has not been auto-generated into an inaccurate or embarrassing form.

## Common Traps

### Proofing The Draft, Not The Render

Errors introduced by formatting and content-management transformations are invisible if the proofread uses the editing draft rather than the rendered version.

### Ignoring Secondary Surfaces

Captions, alt text, pull quotes, and metadata carry errors that a body-only proofread misses.

### Broken Links And Embeds

Unverified links and embeds frustrate readers and undermine credibility, and they are common.

### Mismatched Visuals

Captions that do not match images, or images that distort the story, are frequent and embarrassing.

### Desktop-Only Proofing

Mobile rendering differs, and mobile-only errors are invisible to a desktop proofread.

### Late Typos In Headlines And Captions

Material added late, especially in headlines and captions, often escapes the copy-edit and carries typos into publication.

### Unverified Published Version

Auto-publication and scheduling can introduce discrepancies between the approved final and the live version.

### Neglected Metadata And Social Fields

SEO titles, social cards, and slugs shape discovery and can misrepresent the story if unverified.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Was the proofread performed against the rendered, published-or-preview version rather than the editing draft?
- [ ] Have all surfaces, headline, subhead, dek, caption, image, alt text, pull quotes, embeds, charts, and modules, been read for errors?
- [ ] Have all links been clicked to confirm they resolve to the intended, current destination?
- [ ] Do captions match images, do images match the story, and are credits, rights, and disclosures present and correct?
- [ ] Has the live published version been verified to match the approved final, immediately after publication and after any update?
- [ ] Has the story been proofread on both desktop and mobile, and on other relevant surfaces such as email and RSS?
- [ ] Have typos, spelling, punctuation, and formatting artifacts been caught, especially in late-added headline and caption material?
- [ ] Are metadata, SEO title and description, social card text, URL slug, tags, and section assignment accurate and consistent with the story?
- [ ] Has the responsible editor confirmed final sign-off, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that publication authority rests with editorial leadership?
