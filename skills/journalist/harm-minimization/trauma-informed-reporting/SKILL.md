# Trauma Informed Reporting

Trauma changes how people remember, speak, trust, and behave. A journalist who approaches a traumatized source the way they would approach a routine official, expecting linear narrative, immediate recall, and emotional composure, will both fail to get the story and harm the person they are interviewing. Trauma-informed reporting is the discipline of understanding the psychological effects of trauma, adjusting interview technique to reduce re-traumatization, and writing about suffering in ways that preserve dignity and accuracy rather than exploiting pain for dramatic effect. The harm this skill prevents is double: sources re-traumatized by insensitive interviewing, and audiences misled by trauma-distorted recall presented as definitive fact, plus the deeper harm of eroding trust between vulnerable communities and the press.

Use this skill whenever the reporting involves victims, survivors, witnesses, refugees, veterans, abuse survivors, disaster affected communities, or any source who has experienced violence or loss. This is a domain where journalistic craft intersects with psychological safety; consult editors on approach and, where subjects are at acute risk, connect them to support resources rather than substituting journalism for care.

## Core Rules

### Understand how trauma affects memory and recall
Traumatic memory is often fragmented, sensory, non-linear, and inconsistent across tellings. A survivor may remember vivid sensory details, a smell or a sound, while being unable to reconstruct a clean timeline, and may give different accounts at different times without dishonesty. Expecting a crisp, consistent, chronological story sets up both the source and the reporter for failure. Ask open questions, allow silence, follow the source's order of recall, and do not treat inconsistency as evidence of lying. Document what is remembered and how, and acknowledge uncertainty rather than forcing coherence.

### Give the source control over the interview
Trauma strips people of control; trauma-informed practice restores it. Let the source choose the time, place, and pace, explain the process clearly in advance, offer breaks, allow them to skip questions, and give them the option to stop at any time without penalty. Tell them what will happen with their words, including whether they will be quoted, named, or photographed, and let them choose. Control reduces re-traumatization and often produces more complete, more accurate accounts than a pressured interview.

### Avoid re-traumatizing through interview technique
Aggressive, rapid-fire questioning, demands for graphic detail, surprise confrontation with painful material, and pressure to perform emotion for the camera all re-traumatize. Ask permission before approaching difficult topics, use the source's own language, let them lead the depth of detail, and watch for signs of distress. The goal is the story the source is able and willing to tell, not the most dramatic extractable quote.

### Distinguish the survivor's truth from forensic fact
A survivor's account is their truth and is essential, but it may not align perfectly with documentary, forensic, or legal records, because trauma affects recall and because different perspectives see different parts of an event. Report the survivor's account faithfully, attribute it clearly, and where it conflicts with other records, investigate and reconcile rather than dismissing either. Do not let the impossibility of perfect recall erase a credible account, and do not let emotional weight substitute for verification of specific claims.

### Write about trauma without sensationalizing
The way trauma is written shapes whether it informs or exploits. Avoid voyeuristic detail, performative emotion, and framing that reduces survivors to their suffering. Center the survivor's agency, context, and the systems involved, not just the horror. Use precise, non-sensational language, and consider whether each graphic detail serves understanding or merely shocks. Graphic depiction of suffering can dehumanize the very people the story should humanize.

### Recognize vicarious trauma in yourself and colleagues
Repeated exposure to traumatic material affects journalists too: intrusive imagery, emotional numbing, cynicism, and burnout. Vicarious trauma is an occupational hazard, not a weakness, and unaddressed it degrades reporting and harms the journalist. Take it seriously, use available support, structure exposure, and watch for it in colleagues. A traumatized journalist is less able to serve sources and audiences well.

### Be honest about what trauma reporting can and cannot do
Journalism cannot provide therapy, justice, or safety, and overpromising any of these betrays sources. Be clear about the limits of what publication can achieve, do not imply that telling the story will fix the harm, and where the source needs support, help connect them to appropriate resources. Recognize that for some sources, speaking is itself part of processing, while for others it deepens the wound; let them judge.

### Build trust with trauma-affected communities over time
Communities that have experienced collective trauma, war, disaster, or systemic abuse often distrust media, sometimes because of prior harmful coverage. Trust is built through sustained, respectful presence, follow-through, and accuracy, not through a single parachuted interview. Where possible, work with community members and local journalists who understand context and language, and acknowledge that distrust may be earned.

## Common Traps

### Expecting linear, consistent recall
Treating fragmentation and inconsistency as dishonesty misjudges how trauma memory works and can both discredit a credible survivor and pressure them into false coherence. The trap is applying routine-source expectations to a traumatized person.

### Extracting the most dramatic quote
Pushing for the most visceral detail or emotional statement, because it will land in the story, prioritizes impact over the source's welfare and accuracy. The trap is that dramatic material is compelling, which masks the extraction.

### Conflating the survivor's account with the full record
A survivor's truth is essential but partial; treating it as the complete forensic record overstates it, and dismissing it for not matching documents understates it. The trap is binary thinking, either total belief or total skepticism, when the right response is faithful reporting plus independent verification.

### Performative empathy that is actually extraction
Tears, visible compassion, and emotional framing can be genuine, but they can also be a technique for eliciting more material. The trap is that performed care feels ethical while functioning as a softer form of extraction.

### Ignoring the journalist's own vicarious trauma
Treating repeated exposure to traumatic material as something to push through leads to numbing, cynicism, and degraded reporting. The trap is a culture that treats vicarious trauma as weakness rather than as a predictable occupational effect.

### Parachuting in without context or relationships
Dropping into a trauma-affected community for a single story, without language skills, cultural context, or relationships, produces shallow and often harmful coverage. The trap is most acute for outside outlets covering disasters, conflict, or abuse in communities they do not normally cover.

### Overpromising outcomes to secure the interview
Implying that the story will bring justice, accountability, or healing, in order to secure a source's cooperation, betrays them when publication does not deliver. The trap is the temptation to overstate impact in the moment of recruitment.

## Self-Check

- Do you understand that trauma affects memory, expecting fragmentation and inconsistency rather than linear recall, and avoiding treating either as dishonesty?
- Does the source have control over the time, place, pace, and depth of the interview, with clear explanation of how their words will be used?
- Have you avoided aggressive or surprise questioning that could re-traumatize, asking permission before difficult topics and following the source's lead on detail?
- Is the survivor's account reported faithfully and attributed clearly, with conflicts against other records investigated and reconciled rather than dismissed?
- Does the writing avoid sensationalism, voyeuristic detail, and reduction of survivors to their suffering, centering agency and context instead?
- Have you recognized and addressed vicarious trauma in yourself and colleagues, treating it as an occupational hazard rather than a weakness?
- Have you been honest about what the reporting can and cannot achieve, avoided overpromising outcomes, and connected sources to support resources where appropriate?
- For trauma-affected communities, have you built or worked through existing trust and local context, and consulted editors on approach where subjects are at acute risk?
