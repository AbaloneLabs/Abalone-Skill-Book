# Pre-Publication Legal Review

Pre-publication legal review is the stage at which a story's legal exposure is examined before it reaches the public, and it is one of the most important safeguards in serious journalism. The judgment problem is that legal review sits at the intersection of two legitimate but distinct concerns: the lawyer's duty to identify and minimize legal risk, and the journalist's duty to publish newsworthy information in the public interest. These concerns align when legal review catches genuine errors and exposures that would harm the publication and the subjects; they conflict when legal caution, sometimes excessive, would suppress or weaken reporting that the public interest requires. A newsroom that treats legal review as a veto surrenders editorial independence and under-serves the public; a newsroom that treats legal review as an obstacle to be circumvented publishes avoidable errors and exposures. The discipline is to use legal review as it should be used: to identify real risk, to improve the precision and defensibility of the reporting, and to publish legally exposed material responsibly when the public interest justifies it.

Use this skill when preparing a story for legal review, deciding what warrants review, organizing material for scrutiny, responding to legal concerns, or balancing legal risk against newsworthiness. The main risk is that an agent either over-defers to legal caution, weakening or killing newsworthy reporting, or dismisses legal concerns, publishing avoidable exposures. This skill is journalistic guidance; specific legal questions require qualified counsel, and legal review is conducted by lawyers, not by reporters or editors applying lay judgment.

## Core Rules

### Identify Which Stories Warrant Legal Review

Not every story needs legal review, but failing to identify those that do is a serious failure. Stories that warrant legal review include those containing allegations of wrongdoing against named individuals or institutions, exposure of private individuals, national security or classified material, content from ongoing legal proceedings, material from leaked or stolen documents, and stories with significant defamation, privacy, or source-protection exposure. Build legal review into the workflow for these categories as a default, not as an afterthought. The decision about what warrants review should be made consciously at assignment or as the story develops, not at the end when publication pressure is highest.

When in doubt about whether a story warrants review, err toward review; the cost of an unnecessary review is far lower than the cost of a missed exposure.

### Organize The Story And Evidence For Effective Review

Legal review is only as effective as the material provided to counsel allows. Organize the story for review by presenting, for each significant claim, the specific text, the evidence supporting it, the sources, the verification status, and the response or non-response of the subject. This lets counsel assess the defensibility of each claim against the actual evidence rather than against the prose alone. A story reviewed in isolation from its supporting evidence receives a shallow review, because the legal question is rarely whether the words are problematic in themselves but whether they are defensible given what the reporting establishes. Provide the underlying documents, data, and source materials that the claims rest on.

Annotate the areas of known legal sensitivity, so that counsel's attention is directed to the highest-risk elements rather than diffused across the whole piece.

### Treat Legal Review As Risk Identification, Not Editorial Veto

Legal review identifies risk; it does not make the publication decision. The decision to publish, with what modifications, rests with editorial leadership, informed by the legal assessment but exercising independent editorial judgment. Treat counsel's concerns as input to be understood and weighed, not as instructions to be followed without question. Understand the basis of each concern: is it a genuine legal exposure, a defensible risk that the public interest may justify accepting, or an excess of caution that would unnecessarily weaken the reporting? Distinguish among these, and make the editorial decision consciously, with the legal risk understood and accepted or mitigated as the stakes require.

This requires editors who understand legal risk well enough to weigh counsel's advice, not merely to defer to or reject it.

### Respond To Defamation And Privacy Concerns With Precision

Most legal concerns in pre-publication review center on defamation and privacy, and the response to these concerns is precision rather than wholesale weakening. For defamation, examine whether each challenged statement is a provable factual claim, opinion, or fair report of official proceedings; whether it is supported by evidence adequate to its weight; whether attribution is precise; and whether the subject's response is reflected. Often the right response is not to remove the claim but to tighten its language, attribute it more precisely, or add the qualifying context that makes it defensible. For privacy, examine whether the published material intrudes on private facts, whether it is newsworthy, and whether the public interest justifies the intrusion. See the defamation and privacy-law skills.

Precision serves both legal defensibility and journalistic quality, because the same tightening that reduces legal risk often improves accuracy.

### Preserve Newsworthiness While Managing Risk

The goal of legal review is to publish the newsworthy story with its risk managed, not to reduce risk to zero by eviscerating the journalism. Each legal concern has a range of responses, from removing the claim, to tightening it, to attributing it, to qualifying it, to publishing it as-is with the risk understood. Choose the response that manages the risk while preserving the story's public value. A story so weakened by legal caution that it no longer informs the public has failed its purpose, even if it is legally safe. Conversely, a story that preserves its punch by ignoring a genuine exposure has traded lasting credibility for momentary impact.

The discipline is to find the form of the story that is both legally defensible and journalistically meaningful, and where these genuinely conflict, to make the tradeoff consciously at a senior level.

### Document The Review And The Decisions

Document the legal review process: what was reviewed, what concerns were raised, how each was addressed, and the basis for the editorial decisions, especially where risk was accepted. This documentation supports the publication if the story is later challenged, demonstrates that the review was conducted responsibly, and creates accountability for the decisions made. A story published without documented legal review, or with undocumented decisions to accept risk, is harder to defend and harder to learn from. Build documentation into the review process as part of the publication record.

Documentation also supports institutional learning, because patterns of legal concern across stories reveal where the newsroom's practices need strengthening.

### Maintain Source Protection Through The Review Process

Legal review can create tension with source protection, because thorough review may require sharing source identities or materials with counsel. Manage this carefully: share what counsel needs to assess defensibility while preserving the protections owed to sources, especially confidential ones. Understand the newsroom's rules about what may be shared within the publication, and ensure that counsel's review does not become a vector for source exposure. For stories relying on confidential sources, the legal review must accommodate the protection obligations, sometimes by describing the source's reliability and basis of knowledge without identifying them.

## Common Traps

### Treating Legal Review As A Veto Over Newsworthy Reporting

Excessive deference to legal caution suppresses or weakens journalism the public needs. This is a trap because it substitutes the lawyer's risk tolerance for the editor's judgment. Treat review as input, with the publication decision resting editorially.

### Dismissing Legal Concerns As Obstacles

Ignoring genuine exposures publishes avoidable errors and liability. This is a trap because the consequences fall on the publication and the subjects. Weigh legal concerns seriously and respond with precision.

### Reviewing The Prose Without The Supporting Evidence

A story reviewed in isolation receives a shallow review. This is a trap because defensibility depends on the evidence, not the words. Organize the story with its evidence for effective review.

### Weakening Claims Wholesale Rather Than Tightening

Removing claims to eliminate risk eviscerates the journalism. This is a trap because precision often achieves the same risk reduction while preserving meaning. Tighten, attribute, and qualify rather than remove.

### Failing To Identify Which Stories Warrant Review

Stories that need legal review but do not receive it publish with avoidable exposure. This is a trap because the omission is invisible until challenged. Identify review-worthy stories as a default.

### Letting Legal Review Become A Vector For Source Exposure

Sharing source material for review can breach protection obligations. This is a trap because the review process itself creates exposure. Manage what is shared to preserve source protection.

### Undocumented Decisions To Accept Risk

Accepting risk without documentation is hard to defend and learn from. This is a trap because the basis for the decision is lost. Document the review and the editorial decisions.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Was the story identified as warranting legal review based on its allegations, subjects, legal exposure, and material, with review built in as a default rather than an afterthought?
- [ ] Is the story organized for effective review, with each significant claim accompanied by its evidence, sources, verification status, and subject response?
- [ ] Is legal review treated as risk identification informing an editorial decision, rather than as a veto over newsworthy reporting?
- [ ] Are defamation and privacy concerns responded to with precision, tightening language, attribution, and context rather than wholesale weakening?
- [ ] Does the published form of the story preserve its newsworthiness while managing legal risk, rather than reducing risk by eviscerating the journalism?
- [ ] Is the review process documented, including concerns raised, responses, and the basis for editorial decisions, especially where risk was accepted?
- [ ] Is source protection maintained through the review process, with confidential sources protected even as counsel assesses defensibility?
- [ ] Before publication, is there any legal concern raised in review that was dismissed without being understood and weighed?
- [ ] For stories with significant legal exposure, has senior editorial leadership made the publication decision consciously, with the legal risk understood and the public interest explicit?
- [ ] For specific legal questions, has qualified counsel been consulted, recognizing that this skill is journalistic guidance and that legal review must be conducted by lawyers familiar with the relevant jurisdiction and subject matter?
