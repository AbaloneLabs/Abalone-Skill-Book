# Privacy Law And Newsgathering

Privacy law in journalism governs the tension between the public's right to information and individuals' right to be free from intrusion into their private lives and from exposure of private facts, and it is a domain where the methods of newsgathering and the content of publication each carry distinct legal risks. The judgment problem is that legitimate journalism often requires intruding into privacy, interviewing grieving families, photographing in public, disclosing conduct that is private but newsworthy, and the line between lawful newsgathering and actionable invasion is fact-specific, jurisdiction-dependent, and often unclear until litigated. A defensible approach understands the distinct privacy claims, intrusion, publication of private facts, false light, and appropriation, assesses newsgathering methods and publication decisions against them, and recognizes that public interest is a defense in some but not all privacy claims.

Use this skill when assessing privacy law risks in newsgathering or publication, deciding on recording, photographing, or disclosing private information, or weighing methods against privacy rights. The main risk is that an agent treats privacy as a single concept, applies a public-interest defense where it does not legally apply, or assumes that newsgathering in public is always lawful.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Distinct Privacy Claims And Their Elements

Privacy law is not a single concept but a set of distinct claims with different elements, defenses, and applications. Intrusion upon seclusion covers offensive or highly objectionable intrusion into a person's private space or affairs, regardless of what is published; the harm is the intrusion itself. Publication of private facts covers public disclosure of highly offensive private facts that are not of legitimate public concern; the harm is the disclosure. False light covers publication that places a person in a false light highly offensive to a reasonable person, related to but distinct from defamation. Appropriation, or right of publicity, covers commercial use of a person's name or likeness, generally not applicable to news but relevant to commercial exploitation.

Understand these distinctions, because the same story may raise some claims and not others, and the defenses differ. A story that lawfully gathers information through public records may still face a publication of private facts claim if it discloses private facts not of public concern. A newsgathering method that intrudes on seclusion may be actionable even if nothing is published. Assess the specific story and methods against the specific claims that could be brought. ### Assess Newsgathering Methods Against Intrusion Law Separately From Publication

Intrusion upon seclusion is a newsgathering claim, focused on how information is obtained rather than what is published, and it must be assessed separately from publication risks. Methods that may constitute intrusion include trespassing on private property, using hidden recording devices in jurisdictions that restrict them, accessing private communications or data without authorization, persistent or harassing pursuit, and photographing or recording in private spaces. Lawful newsgathering in public, photographing in public spaces, attending public events, and using public records, generally does not constitute intrusion, but the line can be fact-specific.

Assess each newsgathering method against intrusion law in the relevant jurisdiction, recognizing that recording laws vary significantly, with some jurisdictions requiring all-party consent for recordings. Do not assume that because the information is true or newsworthy, the method of obtaining it was lawful; intrusion can be actionable regardless of the publication outcome. Where a method carries intrusion risk, consider alternative methods that obtain the information lawfully. See the recording-consent-and-two-party-laws skill.

### Apply The Public Interest Defense Where It Legally Applies

The public interest, or legitimate public concern, is a defense to publication of private facts claims, but it is not a defense to all privacy claims, and its scope varies by jurisdiction. For publication of private facts, information that is of legitimate public concern is not actionable even if it is private and offensive to the subject; the public interest overrides the privacy claim. For intrusion, the newsworthiness of the information is generally not a defense to the intrusive method of obtaining it. For false light, the defense is truth and accurate presentation, not public interest per se.

Apply the public interest defense where it legally applies, and do not assume it covers all privacy risks. The fact that a story is newsworthy does not license intrusive newsgathering, and it does not protect against false light if the presentation is misleading. Assess the specific claim and the specific defense, and recognize that public interest is a legal standard, not a journalistic assertion; what the journalist considers newsworthy must also meet the legal standard of legitimate public concern.

### Recognize That Public Figures Have Different Privacy Expectations

Privacy law often distinguishes between public figures, who have reduced privacy expectations in matters related to their public roles, and private individuals, who retain stronger privacy protections. A politician's conduct in office, a business leader's decisions affecting their company, and a public figure's public activities are subject to greater scrutiny and lower privacy protection than the same conduct by a private individual. But public figures do not forfeit all privacy; their private family lives, homes, and personal information unrelated to their public roles may retain protection.

Assess privacy expectations according to the subject's status and the matter's relation to their public role. Do not treat a public figure as having no privacy rights, which overreaches, and do not treat a private individual drawn into news as a public figure, which under-protects. The distinction matters especially for involuntary public figures, people who become newsworthy through events they did not seek, who retain more privacy protection than voluntary public figures. ### Navigate Recording And Consent Laws By Jurisdiction

Recording and consent laws vary significantly by jurisdiction and are a common source of privacy and criminal risk in newsgathering. Some jurisdictions require one-party consent, allowing recording if one party to the conversation consents, including the reporter. Others require all-party consent, making secret recording of a private conversation illegal regardless of newsworthiness. The laws differ for in-person recording, phone recording, and video recording, and they may have additional restrictions for recording in private spaces.

Know the recording and consent laws in the jurisdiction where the recording occurs, not where the newsroom is located, and comply with them. Secret recording that is lawful in one jurisdiction may be criminal in another, and a story built on unlawfully obtained recordings faces both criminal exposure and civil privacy claims. Where all-party consent is required, consider whether the recording is necessary, whether consent can be obtained, or whether alternative methods can obtain the information. See the recording-consent-and-two-party-laws skill.

### Weigh Disclosure Of Private Facts Against Public Concern

Publication of private facts claims turn on whether the disclosed information is of legitimate public concern, and this weighing is central to privacy-conscious journalism. Highly offensive private facts, sexual conduct, health information, family matters, financial details, are actionable if disclosed without legitimate public concern, but the same facts may be lawfully disclosed if they are of public concern, such as when they relate to a public figure's official duties or to matters of significant public interest.

Weigh each disclosure of private facts against the public concern it serves, honestly and specifically. The fact that a story is interesting to the public is not the same as the facts being of public concern; gossip and prurient interest do not meet the legal standard. Disclose private facts only where the public concern is genuine and substantial, and consider whether the facts can be reported without the most private details, preserving the story while limiting the exposure.

### Consider The Subject's Dignity And The Ethical, Not Just Legal, Dimension

Privacy law sets the floor for conduct, but ethical journalism considers the subject's dignity and the proportionality of the intrusion beyond what the law requires. A disclosure that is lawful may still be unethical if it causes disproportionate harm without proportionate public benefit, and a newsroom that considers only legal risk may publish material that, while lawful, fails the ethical standard the public expects. Balance the legal assessment with editorial judgment about dignity, proportionality, and the genuine public interest.

This ethical dimension is not a substitute for legal assessment but a complement to it. The legal question is whether disclosure is actionable; the editorial question is whether it should be published. Both must be answered, and a story that clears the legal bar may still warrant modification on ethical grounds. See the fairness-context-and-newsworthiness skill.

## Common Traps

### Treating Privacy As A Single Concept

Privacy law comprises distinct claims with different elements and defenses. Assess the specific story against the specific claims.

### Assuming Newsgathering In Public Is Always Lawful

Most public newsgathering is lawful, but recording laws and specific contexts vary. Assess methods against intrusion and recording law in the relevant jurisdiction.

### Applying Public Interest Where It Does Not Legally Apply

Public interest is a defense to publication of private facts, not to intrusion or false light. Apply the defense where it legally applies.

### Treating Public Figures As Having No Privacy

Public figures have reduced privacy in matters related to their public roles but retain privacy in personal and family matters. Assess by status and relevance.

### Ignoring Recording And Consent Laws

Recording laws vary by jurisdiction, with some requiring all-party consent. Comply with the law where the recording occurs.

### Confusing Public Interest With Public Curiosity

Gossip and prurient interest do not meet the legal standard of legitimate public concern. Disclose private facts only where the public concern is genuine and substantial.

### Considering Only Legal Risk And Ignoring Ethics

Lawful disclosure may still be unethical if it causes disproportionate harm. Balance legal assessment with editorial judgment about dignity and proportionality.

## Self-Check

Before treating a privacy law assessment as sound, verify:

- The distinct privacy claims, intrusion, publication of private facts, false light, appropriation, are understood and the specific story is assessed against the specific claims that could be brought.
- Newsgathering methods are assessed against intrusion law separately from publication, recognizing that intrusive methods may be actionable regardless of newsworthiness.
- The public interest defense is applied where it legally applies, not assumed to cover all privacy risks, and the legal standard of legitimate public concern is distinguished from journalistic assertion.
- The subject's status as public figure, private individual, or involuntary public figure is assessed, with privacy expectations adjusted by status and the matter's relation to the public role.
- Recording and consent laws in the jurisdiction where recording occurs are known and complied with, with one-party versus all-party consent distinctions observed.
- Each disclosure of private facts is weighed against the genuine and substantial public concern it serves, distinguishing public concern from public curiosity.
- The ethical dimension of dignity and proportionality is considered alongside the legal assessment, with lawful but disproportionate disclosure subject to editorial judgment.
- The assessment is specific to the jurisdiction whose law applies, recognizing significant variation.
- The assessment is documented and reviewed by a responsible editor and, for high-risk stories, legal counsel before publication.
- Privacy law assessment supports editorial judgment; significant privacy decisions should involve legal counsel, recognizing that this skill does not provide legal advice, that privacy law is jurisdiction-specific, and that the public interest may justify lawful disclosure of private facts after professional consultation.
