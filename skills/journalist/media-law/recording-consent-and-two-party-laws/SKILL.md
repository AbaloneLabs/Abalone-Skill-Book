# Recording Consent And Two-Party Laws

Recording conversations for journalism is governed by a patchwork of laws that vary by jurisdiction and by the type of recording, and the consequences of getting it wrong are severe: criminal liability, civil privacy claims, exclusion of the recording as evidence, and damage to the story and the newsroom's credibility. The judgment problem is that what is lawful in one jurisdiction is criminal in another, that the rules differ for in-person, phone, and video recording, and that a recording obtained lawfully in one place may be unusable if it crosses into a jurisdiction with stricter rules. A defensible practice knows the consent law of the jurisdiction where the recording occurs, obtains consent where required, and treats secret recording as a last resort with its own legal and ethical threshold.

Use this skill when recording interviews or conversations for reporting, deciding on consent, navigating one-party versus all-party consent laws, or assessing the legality of recordings. The main risk is that an agent applies the consent rule of their home jurisdiction to a recording made elsewhere, assumes consent is never required, or uses secret recording without assessing the legal and ethical threshold.

## Core Rules

### Know The Consent Law Where The Recording Occurs

The consent law that governs a recording is generally the law of the jurisdiction where the recording occurs, not where the newsroom is located or where the story is published, and these laws vary significantly. One-party consent jurisdictions allow recording if at least one party to the conversation consents, which can be the reporter doing the recording. All-party, or two-party, consent jurisdictions require the consent of all parties to the conversation, making secret recording of a private conversation illegal regardless of newsworthiness.

Know the law of the specific jurisdiction where the recording will occur, including whether it is one-party or all-party consent, and any additional restrictions on recording in private spaces, recording video, or recording particular types of communications. Do not assume that because recording is lawful in your jurisdiction it is lawful where the recording occurs, and do not rely on the publication jurisdiction's law. For recordings that cross jurisdictions, such as phone interviews across state or national lines, the stricter law may apply, and counsel should be consulted. See the privacy-law-and-newsgathering skill.

### Obtain Consent Where Required, Clearly And Before Recording

In all-party consent jurisdictions, consent must be obtained from all parties before or at the start of the recording, and the consent must be clear and affirmative. A muttered disclosure that the call is being recorded may not satisfy the requirement, and silence or continued participation does not always constitute consent. Obtain consent explicitly, state that the conversation is being recorded and by whom, and confirm the other party's agreement.

Even in one-party consent jurisdictions, obtaining consent is often the better practice, because it avoids legal risk, builds trust, and produces a cleaner record. Consent also supports the use of the recording as evidence and protects against disputes about accuracy. Make consent the default unless there is a specific, justified reason to record secretly, and where secret recording is contemplated, assess it against the threshold below.

### Treat Secret Recording As A Last Resort With A Specific Threshold

Secret recording, recording without the knowledge or consent of other parties, is lawful in one-party consent jurisdictions if the reporter is a party, but it carries legal and ethical weight that requires a specific threshold. Reserve secret recording for situations where there is genuine reason to believe that overt recording would prevent the disclosure of information of significant public interest, where the subject is in a position of power relative to the public, and where the recording is necessary to document wrongdoing that would otherwise be denied.

Do not use secret recording as a convenience, a default, or a fishing expedition. The ethical and reputational costs of secret recording are real: it can appear deceptive, it can damage trust with sources and the public, and it can be portrayed as entrapment even when lawful. Use it deliberately, with editorial approval, and where the public interest clearly justifies the method. Document the justification, because it may be challenged legally and ethically.

### Distinguish Recording Law From Use And Publication Law

The lawfulness of making a recording is distinct from the lawfulness of using or publishing it, and a recording made lawfully may face restrictions on use, particularly if it was made in a jurisdiction with stricter rules than where it is published, or if it contains material protected by other laws, such as privacy, copyright, or trade secret. Assess both the making and the use of the recording, recognizing that clearing one does not clear the other.

A recording made lawfully in a one-party consent jurisdiction may be unusable if it was intended for use in an all-party consent jurisdiction, or if its publication violates privacy or other law. A recording made unlawfully may be excluded as evidence and may expose the recorder to criminal and civil liability regardless of its newsworthiness. Assess the full chain, from making to use, with counsel for high-stakes recordings.

### Assess Recordings Obtained By Sources And Third Parties

Journalists often receive recordings obtained by sources or third parties, leaked calls, secretly recorded meetings, surveillance footage, and the lawfulness of these recordings affects whether and how they can be used. A recording made unlawfully by a third party may still be publishable in some jurisdictions if the journalist did not participate in the unlawful recording and the material is of significant public concern, but the rules vary and the analysis is complex.

Assess the source and circumstances of third-party recordings before use. Consider whether the recording was made lawfully, whether the journalist's use is lawful, and whether the public interest justifies publication of potentially unlawfully obtained material. Do not assume that because the recording exists and is newsworthy it can be published; the manner of its making and the journalist's role matter. See the public-records skill for related concerns about leaked documents.

### Account For Video, Public Space, And Private Space Differences

Recording law differs for audio, video, and photography, and for public versus private spaces. Audio recording of conversations is governed by consent law as described. Video recording and photography in public spaces, where there is no reasonable expectation of privacy, are generally more permissible, though they may be restricted by specific laws regarding harassment, surveillance, or particular locations. Recording in private spaces, homes, hotel rooms, medical facilities, is subject to stronger restrictions regardless of whether audio is captured.

Understand the differences and assess the specific recording against them. A video recording in a public space may be lawful even if an audio recording of the same encounter would require consent. A recording in a private space may be unlawful regardless of the public interest. Match the assessment to the specific medium and location, and do not generalize from one type of recording to another.

### Document Consent And The Recording's Circumstances

Document the consent obtained and the circumstances of the recording, so that the lawfulness can be demonstrated if challenged. For consensual recordings, record the consent at the start of the recording or in a contemporaneous note. For secret recordings made under the public interest threshold, document the justification, the legal basis, and the editorial approval, so that the decision can be reviewed and defended.

This documentation supports the use of the recording in the story, defends against legal challenge, and provides accountability for the decision. A recording whose consent or justification is not documented is harder to defend, regardless of its actual lawfulness. Build documentation into the recording practice, as part of the legal risk management. See the journalist-legal-risk-assessment skill.

## Common Traps

### Applying The Home Jurisdiction's Law Elsewhere

Consent law is generally where the recording occurs, not where the newsroom is. Know the law of the specific jurisdiction.

### Assuming Consent Is Never Required

All-party consent jurisdictions require all parties' consent. Obtain consent where required, clearly and before recording.

### Using Secret Recording As A Convenience

Secret recording carries legal and ethical weight. Reserve it for significant public interest where overt recording would prevent disclosure, with editorial approval.

### Confusing Lawful Making With Lawful Use

A recording made lawfully may face restrictions on use. Assess the full chain from making to publication.

### Publishing Third-Party Recordings Without Assessing Their Lawfulness

A recording made unlawfully by a third party may or may not be publishable. Assess the source and circumstances before use.

### Generalizing Across Audio, Video, And Public Or Private Space

Recording law differs by medium and location. Match the assessment to the specific recording.

### Failing To Document Consent And Justification

Undocumented consent or justification is hard to defend. Document as part of the recording practice.

## Self-Check

Before treating a recording consent assessment as sound, verify:

- The consent law of the jurisdiction where the recording occurs is known, including one-party versus all-party consent and any additional restrictions, and is not assumed from the home jurisdiction.
- Consent is obtained where required, clearly and affirmatively, before or at the start of recording, with the default being to obtain consent even in one-party jurisdictions.
- Secret recording is reserved for situations of significant public interest where overt recording would prevent disclosure, with editorial approval and documented justification.
- The lawfulness of making the recording is assessed separately from the lawfulness of use and publication, with the full chain considered.
- Third-party recordings are assessed for the circumstances of their making and the journalist's role before use, recognizing that unlawfully obtained material has complex rules.
- The assessment accounts for the differences between audio, video, photography, and public versus private spaces, matched to the specific recording.
- Consent and justification are documented contemporaneously, to support use and defend against challenge.
- For recordings crossing jurisdictions, the stricter law is identified and counsel consulted where the analysis is complex.
- The assessment is documented and reviewed by a responsible editor and, for high-risk recordings, legal counsel before use.
- Recording consent assessment supports editorial judgment; significant recording decisions should involve legal counsel, recognizing that this skill does not provide legal advice, that recording law is jurisdiction-specific and carries criminal as well as civil risk, and that secret recording requires professional legal advice and senior editorial judgment about the public interest.
