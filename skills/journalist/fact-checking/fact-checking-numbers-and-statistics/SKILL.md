# Fact-Checking Numbers And Statistics

Numbers carry a false authority. A sentence with a statistic feels more rigorous, more verified, and more objective than a sentence without one, and this is precisely why numbers are the most dangerous category of factual claim in journalism. Readers and editors tend to suspend skepticism in the presence of a figure, and the figure itself may be miscounted, misattributed, misinterpreted, stripped of its context, drawn from a flawed methodology, or technically accurate but deeply misleading. The harm this skill prevents is the publication of numerical claims that look authoritative and are wrong, claims that survive ordinary fact-checking because the checker confirmed the number exists but never confirmed that it means what the story says it means. Fact-checking numbers is not arithmetic; it is a discipline of provenance, methodology, context, and interpretation.

Use this skill when verifying any quantitative claim in a story: statistics, percentages, financial figures, poll results, survey findings, budget numbers, counts, rates, and comparisons. The main risk is that an agent treats a number as checked once its value is confirmed, without examining whether the source is primary, whether the methodology is sound, and whether the number is being used honestly in context.

## Core Rules

### Trace Every Number To Its Primary Source

Most numbers in circulation are repetitions of an original figure published somewhere else, and each repetition introduces the risk of transcription error, unit confusion, and stripped context. The fact-checker's first task is to trace the number back to the primary source: the original dataset, the official report, the peer-reviewed study, the regulatory filing, the census, the budget document. A number sourced to "according to reports" or to a secondary news article is not verified; it is borrowed. Locate the origin and confirm the figure against it, because secondary sources paraphrase, round, and sometimes invert numbers without notice.

When the primary source is unavailable, paywalled, or proprietary, note the reduced certainty and seek corroboration from independent analysis of the same underlying data.

### Verify The Methodology, Not Only The Result

A number can be exactly what the source reports and still be wrong or misleading, because the method that produced it was flawed. A poll with a biased sample, a survey with a leading question, a study with a small or unrepresentative cohort, a count that excludes relevant cases, or a metric defined in a non-standard way can all generate figures that are technically accurate to their source and useless or deceptive as evidence. When a number is load-bearing for the story, examine how it was produced: who was sampled, how, what was asked, what was excluded, what the margin of error is, and whether the definition matches the story's use of the figure.

For polls and surveys, check the sample size, the sampling method, the response rate, the weighting, the question wording, and the margin of error. A poll of self-selected respondents is not a measure of public opinion, however precise its percentage.

### Check The Definition And Unit Against The Story's Use

Numbers are defined by what they count and how, and a story that uses a number without matching its definition misleads even when the figure is correct. "Unemployment" has multiple official definitions that yield different rates. "A case" may mean confirmed, probable, or suspected, with very different totals. "Budget" may mean proposed, enacted, or spent. "Average" may mean mean or median, and the choice can flip a conclusion about income or housing. Before accepting a number, confirm exactly what it measures, in what units, over what period, with what inclusions and exclusions, and verify that the story uses it consistently with that definition.

A classic failure is comparing two numbers that use different definitions, periods, or units and presenting the comparison as meaningful.

### Examine Context That Changes The Number's Meaning

A number in isolation is often meaningless or misleading. A 20 percent increase sounds large until you learn the base was tiny; a rate that seems high may be normal for the population and period; a figure that is a record in nominal terms may be unremarkable adjusted for inflation or population. Fact-checking requires the context that lets a reader judge the number: the base rate, the trend over time, the comparison population, the adjustment for inflation or population, and the relevant benchmark. A story that reports a striking number without the context that would let the reader interpret it is misleading even if the number is correct.

Ask what a reader would need to know to avoid being misled, and provide it or qualify the claim.

### Distinguish Correlation, Causation, And Coincidence

Stories frequently imply that one thing caused another because the two moved together, when the evidence supports only correlation or coincidence. A fact-checker must examine causal claims with special rigor: was the comparison controlled for confounding variables, was the time sequence right, is there an alternative explanation, and does the source itself claim causation or only association? Scientific and statistical sources are usually careful to say "associated with" where journalism often converts this to "causes." Preserve the source's actual claim and do not inflate correlation into causation.

For health, crime, economic, and social statistics, the gap between association and causation is where the most damaging misreporting occurs.

### Check Calculations And Conversions

When a story derives a number through calculation, percentage change, rate per capita, currency conversion, inflation adjustment, or aggregation, redo the calculation independently from the primary figures. Arithmetic errors, misplaced decimal points, inverted numerator and denominator, wrong base for percentage change, and incorrect unit conversions are common and produce confidently wrong numbers. A fact-checker who trusts the reporter's or source's math without redoing it has not checked the number.

For derived figures, record the inputs and the formula, so the calculation can be reviewed and reproduced.

### Assess Whether The Number Is Used Honestly

A number can be accurate, well-sourced, correctly defined, and properly contextualized, and still be used dishonestly, through cherry-picking the favorable period, selecting the favorable comparison, presenting an outlier as typical, or implying a trend from a single data point. Examine whether the story's use of the number represents the underlying data fairly or selects the framing that supports the desired conclusion. If the fuller data tells a different story than the selected figure, the selection is misleading even if the figure is real.

This is the hardest check, because it requires judgment about honesty rather than verification of a value, but it is where numerical claims most often mislead.

## Common Traps

### Confirming The Number Exists And Calling It Checked

Verifying that a figure appears in a source is not the same as verifying it is correct and correctly used. This is a trap because it satisfies a superficial check while leaving methodology, definition, and context unexamined. Trace to the primary source and examine how the number was produced.

### Treating Precision As Accuracy

A number reported to several decimal places feels more reliable, but precision is not accuracy. This is a trap because a precisely stated wrong number is more misleading than a rounded correct one. Examine the underlying figure, not its apparent precision.

### Comparing Numbers With Incompatible Definitions

Comparing an unemployment rate defined one way to one defined differently, or a budget figure in nominal to real terms, produces a false comparison. This is a trap because the comparison looks meaningful and is not. Match definitions, units, and periods before comparing.

### Ignoring The Base Rate And Trend

A striking number without its base rate, trend, or benchmark misleads about significance. This is a trap because the reader cannot judge whether the figure is high, low, normal, or changing. Provide the context that makes the number interpretable.

### Inflating Correlation Into Causation

Reporting that A is linked to B as though A causes B overstates what the evidence shows. This is a trap because causal claims have real-world consequences, from policy to health behavior, that association does not support. Preserve the source's actual claim.

### Trusting Calculations Without Redoing Them

Derived numbers carry arithmetic and conversion errors that survive because no one recomputed them. This is a trap because the error is invisible until the math is redone. Recalculate from primary inputs.

### Cherry-Picking The Favorable Figure

Selecting the period, comparison, or metric that supports the conclusion while the fuller data contradicts it is dishonest even when each selected number is real. This is a trap because it is hard to detect and easy to rationalize. Examine whether the selection represents the data fairly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has every quantitative claim been traced to its primary source, rather than accepted from a secondary report or repetition?
- [ ] For load-bearing numbers, has the methodology been examined, including sample, definition, margin of error, and known limitations?
- [ ] Does the story's use of each number match the source's definition, unit, period, and inclusions or exclusions?
- [ ] Is the context provided that lets a reader judge the number's significance, including base rate, trend, comparison, and adjustment for inflation or population?
- [ ] Are causal claims examined to distinguish causation from correlation or coincidence, and is the source's actual claim preserved rather than inflated?
- [ ] Have all derived calculations and conversions been redone independently from the primary figures?
- [ ] Has the honesty of the number's use been assessed, examining whether the selected figure represents the underlying data or cherry-picks the favorable framing?
- [ ] Are numbers with reduced certainty, because the primary source is unavailable or the methodology is weak, qualified rather than presented as established?
- [ ] Before publication, is there any number in the story that has been confirmed to exist but not confirmed to mean what the story claims it means?
