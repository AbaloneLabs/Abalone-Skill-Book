# Correction And Retraction Handling

Errors in published journalism are inevitable; how they are handled defines an outlet's credibility. Corrections, clarifications, and retractions are not admissions of defeat but demonstrations of the accountability that distinguishes professional journalism from propaganda. The harm this skill prevents is twofold: the quiet, inadequate correction that leaves the false information dominant in readers' minds and in the record, and the overreaction that retracts solid reporting under pressure, rewarding manipulation and eroding the outlet's authority. Handling corrections well is a discipline of promptness, proportion, transparency, and protection of the underlying reporting where it was sound.

## Core Rules

### Correct promptly and visibly
When an error is confirmed, correct it as quickly as possible, with prominence proportionate to the original error's visibility. A correction buried at the bottom of an updated story, or hidden in a corrections box no one reads, fails to reach the audience that absorbed the error. The correction must travel nearly as far as the mistake did.

### Match the remedy to the error
Distinguish the categories. A minor factual error, a wrong date or title, requires a straightforward correction. An error that changes the meaning or fairness of the story requires a clarification or a more substantial correction note. An error that invalidates the central thesis, where the story should not have been published as it was, may require retraction. Choose the remedy that fits, neither minimizing nor overreacting.

### Explain what was wrong and what is now correct
A useful correction states specifically what the original story got wrong, what the accurate information is, and how the error occurred if that is knowable. "An earlier version misstated X; the correct figure is Y" is clearer than a vague note. Readers and the subject are entitled to understand the nature of the error, not just that one occurred.

### Preserve transparency about the change
Do not silently edit errors out of the published story. Note that the story was corrected, what changed, and when. Stealth corrections destroy trust when discovered, and they are always discoverable. A visible correction note, dated and specific, is the standard.

### Investigate the cause to prevent recurrence
Each significant error is a signal about a process failure: a verification gap, a source problem, an editing breakdown. Investigate how the error occurred and adjust the workflow, not to assign blame but to reduce the chance of repetition. A newsroom that corrects without learning repeats the same mistakes.

### Protect sound reporting from pressure to retract
Not every complaint is a valid basis for correction, and demands for retraction often come from subjects who simply dislike accurate, well-sourced reporting. Distinguish genuine errors from discomfort with true reporting. Retracting or softening accurate work under legal, political, or commercial pressure corrupts the outlet and abandons the sources and audience who relied on it.

### Treat the affected subject with fairness in the correction
If the error harmed a named individual or institution, the correction should restore accuracy with the same prominence and seriousness as the original claim. Consider notifying the affected party directly of the correction. Fairness in error is part of fairness overall.

### Document the correction decision and reasoning
Record what was alleged, what investigation found, what remedy was applied, and why. This documentation supports consistency, defends the decision if challenged, and builds an institutional record of how errors are handled. A correction made without a record is hard to defend later.

## Common Traps

### The invisible correction
Silently fixing an error, or burying the correction where few will see it, leaves the false information dominant. The audience that saw the error is entitled to see the correction with comparable visibility.

### Overreacting under pressure
Threats of legal action, advertiser pressure, or vocal complaints can push an outlet to retract or soften reporting that was actually sound. Investigate the complaint on the merits; do not let volume of pressure substitute for evidence of error.

### Under-reacting to genuine error
Pride, institutional inertia, or fear of liability can lead to minimizing real mistakes, issuing a terse correction that does not acknowledge the significance of the error, or refusing to correct at all. This compounds the harm and the credibility damage.

### Correcting without investigating
Rushing to correct in response to a complaint, before confirming whether the original was actually wrong, can produce a "correction" that is itself wrong, or that concedes ground the reporting earned. Verify the alleged error before correcting.

### Vague correction language
"A previous version contained an inaccuracy" tells readers nothing. Useful corrections name the specific error and the correct information. Vagueness protects the outlet's ego at the expense of the reader and the subject.

### Failing to correct across all channels
If the error appeared in a headline, social posts, newsletters, and syndication, correcting only the body of the online story leaves the error alive elsewhere. Track and correct every channel where the error propagated.

### Retaliatory or punitive framing of corrections
A correction should restore accuracy, not punish the reporter or grandstand for the audience. Corrections framed to humiliate staff, or to signal virtue to critics, distort the process and discourage honest internal reporting of errors.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Was the error confirmed through investigation before any correction was issued?
- [ ] Is the remedy, correction, clarification, or retraction, proportionate to the nature and significance of the error?
- [ ] Does the correction state specifically what was wrong, what is correct, and how the error occurred?
- [ ] Is the correction visible and prominent enough to reach roughly the audience that saw the original error?
- [ ] Has the story been transparently noted as corrected, with a dated change record, rather than silently edited?
- [ ] Has the error been investigated for its root cause, and has the workflow been adjusted to prevent recurrence?
- [ ] If facing pressure to retract, have I confirmed the reporting was actually erroneous rather than merely disliked?
- [ ] If the error harmed a named subject, has the correction restored accuracy with appropriate prominence and direct notice?
- [ ] Has the correction been applied across every channel, headline, social post, and syndication where the error appeared?
- [ ] Is the correction decision and reasoning documented for institutional memory and future defense?
