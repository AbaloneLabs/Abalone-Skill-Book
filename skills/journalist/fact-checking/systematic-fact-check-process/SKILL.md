# Systematic Fact Check Process

Fact-checking is the structured, claim-by-claim review of a story against its sources before publication. It is distinct from reporting and from editing: where reporting gathers and editing sharpens, fact-checking verifies. The harm this skill prevents is the publication of errors that survive because no one systematically tested them, small mistakes in names, dates, titles, and numbers that erode credibility, and larger ones in causation, attribution, and implication that can trigger corrections, legal action, or real-world harm. A systematic process matters because ad-hoc checking inevitably skips the claims that feel obviously true, which are often the ones that are wrong.

## Core Rules

### Decompose the story into discrete, checkable claims
Before checking, break the text into individual assertions: every name, date, number, title, quote, causal claim, and characterization. Each becomes a checkable item. A story that reads as a flowing narrative is, for fact-checking purposes, a list of propositions. Capturing them all is the precondition for verifying them all.

### Match each claim to the standard its stakes require
A middle initial or a building's address needs a quick authoritative check. A statistic, a causal claim, or an accusation needs primary sources and corroboration. Sort claims by consequence and apply proportionate rigor. Do not apply a single uniform standard that either over-checks trivia or under-checks substance.

### Go to primary sources wherever they exist
Check the original document, dataset, statute, or recording rather than a secondary report of it. Secondary sources paraphrase, omit, and sometimes err. When you must rely on a secondary, note that the primary was unavailable and flag the reduced certainty.

### Verify quotes against the original recording or transcript
Quotes must match what was said, in context. Check against the recording or transcript, not against notes. If a quote was condensed or cleaned up for readability, confirm it remains faithful and note any editing. Out-of-context or altered quotes are a leading source of corrections and of source complaints.

### Check the implications, not only the explicit claims
A story can be technically accurate in every sentence yet misleading in aggregate, by omitting context, juxtaposing unrelated facts, or implying causation that the evidence does not support. Test what a reasonable reader would infer, and verify that the inference is supported. Implication is as much a fact-check target as assertion.

### Require documentation for every check
For each claim, record the source consulted, what it said, and whether it confirmed, refuted, or was silent. This documentation lets editors review the basis for publication, lets the reporter respond to later challenges, and creates the trail needed if a correction is contemplated. An undocumented check is an uncheckable check.

### Build in a cooling-off and re-read before publication
After intensive checking, re-read the story cold, looking for what slipped through. Familiarity breeds blindness; a fresh read catches inconsistencies, unsupported leaps, and claims that were assumed rather than verified. Ideally, a second checker or editor performs this read.

### Subject the most damaging claims to the highest scrutiny
Claims that accuse, expose, or harm named individuals or institutions deserve the most rigorous checking, multiple independent sources, primary documents, and direct response from the subject. Errors here cause the greatest harm and are the hardest to correct. Concentrate effort accordingly.

## Common Traps

### Checking only the claims that look suspicious
Errors hide in the claims that feel obviously true because no one bothers to verify them. A famous name misspelled, a long-standing "fact" that has been quietly overturned, a number repeated so often it is never re-checked. Check systematically, not selectively.

### Trusting the reporter's memory or notes
Even careful reporters misremember, conflate sources, or transcribe incorrectly. The fact-checker's job is to verify against the underlying evidence, not to confirm the reporter's recollection. Deferring to the reporter defeats the purpose of an independent check.

### Verifying the source but not the interpretation
Confirming that a document or dataset exists is not the same as confirming that it says what the story claims it says. Read the source in full and confirm the specific interpretation. A real document misread is as wrong as a fabricated one.

### Letting narrative flow override accuracy
Elegant phrasing, a good quote, or a satisfying arc can seduce checkers into accepting a claim that the evidence does not quite support. Accuracy outranks elegance. If a claim cannot be verified, the prose must change, not the standard.

### Ignoring the absent context
A fact-check that confirms each sentence in isolation can miss that the overall picture is misleading because of what was left out. Test the story against the question of what context a reader would need to avoid being misled.

### Under-time pressure, skipping the hardest checks
When publication is imminent, the temptation is to check the easy items and defer the hard ones. The hard ones, complex statistics, contested claims, are precisely where errors and harm concentrate. Protect time for the difficult checks.

### Confusing fact-checking with copyediting
Spelling, grammar, and style matter, but they are not fact-checking. A story can be perfectly copyedited and factually wrong. Keep the two functions distinct so that verification is not displaced by polish.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I decomposed the entire story into discrete, checkable claims, including names, dates, numbers, quotes, and causal assertions?
- [ ] Is each claim matched to a verification standard proportionate to its stakes, with the strictest rigor applied to damaging claims?
- [ ] Did I consult primary sources wherever they exist, rather than relying on secondary reports?
- [ ] Are all quotes verified against the original recording or transcript, in context?
- [ ] Have I tested the implications and overall inference a reasonable reader would draw, not only the explicit sentences?
- [ ] Is every check documented with the source consulted and its result, so the basis for publication is reconstructable?
- [ ] Did I perform, or arrange for, a fresh cold re-read to catch what familiarity obscured?
- [ ] Were the most damaging claims subjected to multiple independent sources and direct subject response?
- [ ] Did I check the obviously-true and long-assumed claims, not only the suspicious ones?
- [ ] Before publication, is there any claim I am treating as verified that actually rests on the reporter's memory or an untested assumption?
