# Claim Decomposition And Sourcing

A single sentence in a news story often contains multiple factual claims, each requiring different evidence and carrying different stakes. "The company laid off 500 workers last month to cover losses from its failed expansion" asserts a layoff, a number, a timing, a cause, and a characterization of the expansion as failed. Treating that sentence as one claim leads to under-verification: the parts that are easy to confirm get checked, and the parts that are hard, often the causal and evaluative parts, slip through. The harm this skill prevents is the publication of compound assertions where the verified elements lend unearned credibility to the unverified ones. Decomposition is the discipline of separating every assertion into its smallest checkable parts and sourcing each independently.

## Core Rules

### Break every compound assertion into atomic claims
Read each sentence and ask how many distinct factual propositions it contains. A claim about an event, its cause, its scale, and its characterization is four claims, not one. Decompose until each remaining claim asserts a single, independently testable proposition. Only then can you source each properly.

### Classify each atomic claim by type
Identify whether a claim is empirical, a number, a date, a recorded event, interpretive, a cause, a motive, a characterization, or evaluative, a judgment like "failed" or "misleading. Empirical claims are verified with records and data; interpretive and evaluative claims require evidence plus reasoned argument and often demand response from the subject. Different claim types need different treatment.

### Determine the ideal source for each claim
For every atomic claim, ask what evidence would best establish it: an official record, a dataset, a named eyewitness, a document, expert analysis. Then assess what you actually have. The gap between the ideal source and the actual source defines the claim's weakness and the work remaining.

### Assign a source tier and verify independence
Tag each claim with the tier of evidence supporting it: primary document, named on-record source, anonymous official, secondary report, single source, multiple independent sources. Where multiple sources are used, confirm they are genuinely independent. The tier tells you, and your editors, how much weight each claim can bear.

### Treat causal and motivational claims as high-burden
Claims about why something happened, the cause, or what someone intended, the motive, are among the hardest to verify and the easiest to get wrong. They require either direct evidence, documents or statements revealing intent, or a pattern of evidence from which the cause or motive is the best-supported inference. Do not assert causation or motive on thin evidence; distinguish what happened from why it happened.

### Source the evaluative words explicitly
Adjectives like "failed," "botched," "sweeping," "unprecedented," and "controversial" carry embedded claims. Each must be defensible: "failed" requires evidence of failure against a defined standard; "unprecedented" requires a check of the historical record. Either source the characterization or replace it with neutral description and let the facts convey the judgment.

### Map the logical structure from evidence to conclusion
Lay out how the sourced claims combine to support the story's larger conclusion. This map exposes hidden assumptions, missing links, and claims that are necessary to the conclusion but were never sourced. If the conclusion depends on an unsourced link, that link is the story's vulnerability and must be addressed before publication.

### Reconcile decomposition with readability
Decomposition is an analytical tool; the published story need not read like a list of footnoted propositions. Once each claim is sourced, the writing can flow. But the discipline of decomposition must have happened first; the elegance of the final prose must not be allowed to obscure that some element was never actually verified.

## Common Traps

### Treating a sentence as a single claim
The most common failure is verifying the easiest element of a compound assertion, the layoff happened, and letting that stand as verification of the whole, including the cause and the characterization. Decompose before you source.

### Letting the empirical claims launder the interpretive ones
When the verifiable facts in a sentence are correct, readers, and sometimes checkers, infer that the causal and evaluative claims are also correct. This is how well-sourced numbers end up supporting unsourced motives. Source each type independently.

### Asserting cause or motive without the evidence for it
Causal and motivational claims feel like explanations and are tempting to assert for narrative satisfaction. But "to cover losses" is a claim about intent that requires its own evidence. If you do not have it, report what happened and note that the cause is unknown or disputed.

### Using evaluative adjectives as if they were neutral description
Words like "botched" or "sweeping" are judgments, not facts, and they smuggle conclusions into the prose. Either defend them with evidence or strip them out. Loaded adjectives are a frequent source of bias complaints and corrections.

### Failing to identify the load-bearing claim
In any story, one or two claims carry the weight of the conclusion. If you do not identify which they are, you may over-check the peripheral and under-check the essential. Find the load-bearing claims and verify them to the highest standard.

### Relying on a source tier insufficient to the claim
An anonymous official may suffice for a low-stakes factual lead but is insufficient for a damaging accusation. Match the source tier to the stakes, not to what was easiest to obtain.

### Decomposing but not re-sourcing after revisions
Stories are revised through editing, and new compound assertions are created in the process. If decomposition happened early and was not refreshed, the final text may contain claims that were never decomposed or sourced. Re-run the decomposition on the final draft.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have I decomposed every compound assertion in the story into atomic, single-proposition claims?
- [ ] Is each atomic claim classified by type, empirical, interpretive, or evaluative, and treated accordingly?
- [ ] For each claim, have I identified the ideal source and assessed the gap between it and the source I actually have?
- [ ] Is each claim tagged with a source tier, and where multiple sources are used, have I confirmed their independence?
- [ ] Are causal and motivational claims supported by direct evidence or a well-supported inference, rather than asserted for narrative convenience?
- [ ] Have I sourced or removed every evaluative adjective, so that no smuggled judgment stands without evidence?
- [ ] Have I mapped the logical structure from evidence to conclusion and identified any unsourced links the conclusion depends on?
- [ ] Did I re-run the decomposition on the final, edited draft to catch claims introduced during revision?
- [ ] Have I identified the load-bearing claims and verified them to the highest standard?
- [ ] Before publication, can I defend every element of every compound assertion independently, not just the easiest parts?
