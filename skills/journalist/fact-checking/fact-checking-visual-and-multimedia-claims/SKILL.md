# Fact-Checking Visual And Multimedia Claims

Visual material carries a persuasive power that text does not. A photograph feels like proof; a video feels like irrefutable evidence; a chart feels like objective data. This is exactly why visual and multimedia claims require the most rigorous fact-checking, because the audience, and often the reporter and editor, suspends skepticism in the presence of an image. The harm this skill prevents is the publication of visual material that is misattributed, miscaptioned, decontextualized, manipulated, or simply not what it claims to be, errors that are especially damaging because they are widely believed, rapidly shared, and hard to correct once they circulate. Fact-checking visuals is not a matter of confirming that an image exists; it is a discipline of provenance, authentication, context, and honest representation.

Use this skill when verifying any visual or multimedia element in a story: photographs, video clips, audio recordings, charts, maps, infographics, screenshots, and user-generated visual content. The main risk is that an agent treats a compelling image as self-verifying, confirms only that it was obtained, and publishes it without establishing what it actually depicts, when and where it was made, and whether its use in the story is honest.

## Core Rules

### Establish Provenance Before Use

Before publishing any visual material, establish where it came from, who made it, when, and under what circumstances. A photograph circulating on social media may have been taken years ago in a different country; a video may have been edited from unrelated footage; a screenshot may have been altered. Trace the material to its origin: the original uploader, the photographer, the device or platform metadata, the earliest discoverable instance. Material whose provenance cannot be established to the standard the story's stakes require should not be presented as established fact, however compelling it appears.

For user-generated content, contact the original creator where possible to confirm the circumstances of capture and obtain permission and context. See the verifying-user-generated-content skill.

### Verify That The Visual Depicts What The Caption Claims

A caption is a factual claim about an image, and it must be checked as rigorously as any sentence in the story. Confirm that the people, place, event, time, and action shown match what the caption asserts. Miscaptioning is among the most common visual errors: a photograph from a different event used to illustrate the current one, a generic stock image presented as documentary, a person misidentified, a scene described with details the image does not show. Compare every element of the caption against the visual evidence and against independent reporting about the depicted event.

When a caption cannot be fully verified, narrow it to what can be confirmed or remove the visual.

### Authenticate Against Manipulation

Visual material can be manipulated in ways ranging from obvious to nearly undetectable. Examine images for signs of editing: inconsistent lighting, mismatched shadows, cloned elements, unnatural boundaries, metadata that contradicts the claimed origin, resolution inconsistencies, and compression artifacts that suggest re-saving or compositing. For video, check for cuts, looping, audio desynchronization, and inserted or removed segments. For audio, listen for edits, inconsistent background noise, and signs of generation loss. Use technical tools, reverse image search, metadata inspection, and forensic analysis where the stakes warrant, and consult specialists for high-stakes or technically complex material.

The rise of synthetic media makes authentication an active, ongoing concern rather than a one-time check; see the synthetic-and-manipulated-media-detection skill.

### Check Context And Timing, Not Only Content

An authentic image shown out of context misleads as effectively as a fabricated one. A real photograph from a previous event used to illustrate a current one, a video whose most dramatic seconds are extracted from a longer, more ambiguous encounter, or an image cropped to remove information that changes its meaning all misrepresent reality while being technically authentic. Establish when the material was captured, what was happening before and after the depicted moment, and what lies outside the frame. Provide the context that lets the reader understand the visual honestly, and do not present an isolated fragment as a complete account.

For video, the edit is the argument: what was cut, in what order, and from what original duration shapes the meaning entirely.

### Verify Charts, Maps, And Infographics Against Their Data

Data visualizations are factual claims rendered visually, and they can mislead through design as much as through data. Check that the chart's data matches its source, that axes are labeled and scaled honestly, that truncated y-axes do not exaggerate differences, that proportional symbols and areas are accurate, that color choices do not imply false patterns, and that the visualization type is appropriate to the data. A chart with correct data but a misleading axis can convey the opposite of what the numbers show. Redraw or replace visualizations that misrepresent their underlying data, and cite the data source clearly.

See the chart-design-and-statistical-honesty skill for visualization design discipline.

### Confirm Consent, Rights, And Ethical Use

Visual fact-checking includes verifying that the material can be lawfully and ethically used. Confirm ownership and licensing, obtain permission where required, and assess whether depicting identifiable individuals, especially minors, victims, or vulnerable subjects, raises privacy or harm concerns. A photograph may be authentic and correctly captioned and still be unethical to publish because of who it exposes or what it depicts. Rights and ethics are part of the check, not an afterthought.

### Document The Verification For Each Visual Element

For each visual element, record what it depicts, its provenance, the authentication performed, the context established, the rights and consent status, and any residual uncertainty. This documentation lets editors review the basis for publication, supports the visual's use against later challenge, and creates the trail needed if a correction or takedown is required. An undocumented visual is an uncheckable visual.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Compelling Image As Self-Verifying

The persuasive power of visuals suspends skepticism in reporters and readers alike. This is a trap because the image feels true regardless of its actual provenance or accuracy. Apply the same evidentiary standard to visuals as to text.

### Misattributing Time, Place, Or Event

A real image from the wrong event is the most common visual error. This is a trap because the image looks authentic and the error is invisible without independent verification of when and where it was made. Confirm the depicted event against external reporting.

### Presenting Authentic But Decontextualized Material

A genuine image or video shown without its context can mislead as fully as a fabrication. This is a trap because the material is real, lulling the checker into assuming the use is honest. Establish what the visual does and does not show in context.

### Trusting Metadata Without Further Verification

Metadata can be stripped, spoofed, or inherited from a template. This is a trap because metadata feels technical and authoritative but is easily manipulated. Use metadata as one signal among several, not as proof.

### Ignoring How The Edit Shapes Meaning

For video and audio, the selection and sequence of segments constitute the claim. This is a trap because checking only the depicted content misses that the editing constructed a meaning the raw footage does not support. Examine what was cut and in what order.

### Publishing Misleading Charts As Objective Data

Data visualizations can mislead through axis manipulation, inappropriate chart types, or dishonest color use, even with correct data. This is a trap because a chart looks authoritative. Verify the visualization against the data and against honest design practice.

### Overlooking Rights, Consent, And Harm

An authentic, well-contextualized image may still be unlawful or unethical to publish. This is a trap because the verification of content crowds out the verification of use. Check rights, consent, and harm as part of the visual fact-check.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the provenance of each visual element been established, tracing it to its origin and maker, with uncertain provenance reflected in how it is presented?
- [ ] Does each caption match what the visual actually depicts, verified against independent reporting about the people, place, event, and time?
- [ ] Has each visual been examined for manipulation, using appropriate technical tools and specialist consultation where stakes warrant?
- [ ] Is the context and timing established, including what happened before and after and what lies outside the frame, so the visual is not presented as a complete account from a fragment?
- [ ] For video and audio, has the effect of editing, what was cut and in what sequence, been examined for how it shapes meaning?
- [ ] Are charts, maps, and infographics verified against their underlying data and checked for honest axis scaling, appropriate chart type, and non-misleading design?
- [ ] Have rights, licensing, consent, and ethical use, including depiction of vulnerable subjects, been confirmed for each visual element?
- [ ] Is the verification documented for each visual, including provenance, authentication, context, rights, and residual uncertainty?
- [ ] Before publication, is there any visual in the story that has been confirmed to exist but not confirmed to depict what the caption or context claims?
- [ ] For high-stakes or contested visuals, has specialist or forensic review been sought, recognizing that visual verification is a technical discipline requiring appropriate tools and expertise?
