# Corrections Transparency And Accountability

Corrections are where credibility is tested, because every newsroom makes errors and the audience judges trustworthiness by how errors are handled, not by whether they occur. The judgment problem is that the incentives around corrections point the wrong way: acknowledging an error is uncomfortable, attracts attention to the mistake, and can invite legal or reputational consequences, while the benefits, trust, accountability, accuracy of the record, are diffuse and long-term. A newsroom that follows the short-term incentives will hide or minimize errors and will, over time, lose the trust that hiding was meant to protect.

Use this skill when handling corrections, clarifications, updates, and retractions, and when designing accountability practices. The main risk is that an agent may minimize, bury, or silently fix errors to avoid discomfort, eroding trust and leaving the public record wrong. This skill is editorial guidance; correction standards and practices rest with editorial leadership and the standards function.

## Core Rules

### Correct Promptly And Visibly

When an error is identified, correct it promptly and visibly. Promptness limits the spread of the error, and visibility ensures that those who saw the wrong information encounter the correction. A correction buried at the bottom, hidden behind a generic label, or delayed until attention has moved serves the outlet's comfort, not the audience's need. Place corrections where readers will see them, with a clear note explaining what was wrong and what is now accurate.

Speed matters because errors propagate; the longer an error stands, the more people incorporate it into their understanding.

### Distinguish Corrections, Clarifications, And Updates

These serve different functions and should be labeled distinctly. A correction fixes a factual error. A clarification addresses wording that was incomplete or potentially misleading but not strictly false. An update adds new information that emerged after publication. An editor's note addresses a significant process issue or a correction to the record that warrants explanation. Using "update" to avoid acknowledging an error, or "clarification" to soften a correction, misleads readers about what happened. Match the label to the substance.

The audience should be able to tell, from the label and the note, what kind of change was made and why.

### Explain What Was Wrong And What Changed

A useful correction tells the audience what the error was and what the accurate information is. "An earlier version of this story contained an error. It has been corrected" tells the reader almost nothing. Specify the incorrect information, the correct information, and, where relevant, which surfaces, headline, caption, or social copy, were affected and whether the error changed the story's conclusion. The reader who relied on the original is entitled to know exactly what to unlearn.

For serious errors, consider an editor's note explaining how the error occurred and what review is underway.

### Correct Across All Surfaces

Errors travel across surfaces: the article body, headline, subhead, caption, image, push alert, social post, newsletter, and syndicated or republished versions. A correction to the body that leaves a wrong headline or a misleading social post standing leaves the error in circulation. Identify every surface where the error appeared or was summarized, and correct each one. For syndicated or republished material, notify downstream partners of the correction.

This is especially important for social posts, which often outlive the article in audience attention.

### Acknowledge The Source Of The Error Honestly

When correcting, be honest about the nature of the error, whether it was a reporting failure, an editing failure, a source error, or a production mistake, without deflecting onto others or minimizing the outlet's responsibility. If a source provided wrong information, the outlet still published it and owns the correction. Honest acknowledgment builds trust; deflection and minimization erode it, even when the correction itself is technically present.

Avoid language that softens the error unduly; "misspoke," "inartful," or "imprecise" can minimize what was, in fact, wrong.

### Handle Retractions For Serious Failures

Some errors are so serious, a fundamentally false central claim, a fabricated element, or a story whose premise collapses, that correction is insufficient and the story must be retracted or substantially retracted. A retraction withdraws the story as a reliable account and should be accompanied by a clear editor's note explaining why. Retractions are serious and should follow standards review, but they are necessary when a story cannot stand. Pretending a fatally flawed story needs only a small correction damages credibility.

Distinguish retraction, the story is withdrawn, from correction, the story stands with fixes, and reserve retraction for cases where the story cannot stand.

### Make The Correction Process Easy To Trigger

Corrections often come from readers, and the process for reporting an error should be easy to find and use. Provide a visible contact path, acknowledge reports, and investigate promptly. A reader who reports an error and is ignored learns the outlet does not care about accuracy; a reader whose report leads to a visible correction becomes a source of trust. Treat error reports as valuable intelligence, not as nuisance.

Track error reports to identify patterns that may reveal process problems.

### Learn From Corrections Systematically

Each correction is a signal about a process weakness. Aggregate and review corrections periodically to identify patterns: a recurring type of error, a particular stage where checks fail, or a subject area prone to mistakes. Use the pattern analysis to strengthen the process, not just to fix individual errors. A newsroom that corrects without learning will repeat the same errors; one that learns from corrections reduces them over time.

Connect correction patterns to training, checklists, and standards updates so the learning changes practice.

## Common Traps

### Silent Fixes

Quietly changing material without a visible note avoids discomfort but misleads readers who relied on the original and damages trust if discovered.

### Minimizing Language

Soft words like "misspoke" or "imprecise" understate the error and signal that the outlet is protecting itself rather than informing the audience.

### Buried Or Delayed Corrections

Placing corrections where readers will not see them, or delaying until attention moves, defeats the purpose of correcting the record.

### Wrong Label For The Change

Using "update" to avoid acknowledging an error, or "clarification" to soften a correction, misleads readers about what happened.

### Correcting Only The Body

Leaving the error in the headline, social post, or other surfaces keeps the wrong information in circulation.

### Deflecting Blame

Attributing the error to a source or to circumstances, without owning the outlet's responsibility, erodes trust.

### Retraction Avoided When Warranted

Treating a fatally flawed story as needing only a small correction understates the failure and leaves a false story standing.

### Corrections Without Learning

Fixing individual errors without analyzing patterns guarantees repetition of the same mistakes.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the correction prompt, visible, and placed where readers who saw the error will encounter it?
- [ ] Is the label, correction, clarification, update, or editor's note, matched to the substance of the change?
- [ ] Does the note specify what was wrong, what is accurate, which surfaces were affected, and whether the conclusion changed?
- [ ] Has the error been corrected across every surface it reached, including headline, social copy, and syndicated versions?
- [ ] Is the source and nature of the error acknowledged honestly, without deflecting blame or minimizing?
- [ ] For serious failures, has retraction or substantial retraction been considered, with an editor's note explaining the withdrawal?
- [ ] Is the error-reporting process easy for readers to find and use, with reports acknowledged and investigated?
- [ ] Are corrections aggregated and reviewed for patterns, with findings feeding process, training, and standards changes?
- [ ] Have significant corrections and retractions been reviewed by editorial leadership and the standards function, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that correction standards are established by editorial leadership?
