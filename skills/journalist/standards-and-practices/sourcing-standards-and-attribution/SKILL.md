# Sourcing Standards And Attribution

Sourcing standards determine what a newsroom will publish and on what basis, and they are the backbone of credibility. The judgment problem is that sourcing decisions are made case by case under pressure, and without consistent standards they drift toward whatever gets the story done, with anonymity granted too easily, attribution vague enough to hide weak evidence, and descriptions that mislead readers about a source's reliability. Strong sourcing standards make the evidence base visible and consistent; weak ones let the same claim be published on radically different foundations.

Use this skill when establishing or applying sourcing standards, deciding what a claim requires, handling anonymity, and standardizing attribution. The main risk is that an agent may apply sourcing loosely under deadline or competitive pressure, or may standardize attribution in ways that obscure rather than reveal the evidence base. This skill is editorial guidance; sourcing standards are set by editorial leadership and the standards function.

## Core Rules

### Match Sourcing Requirements To Claim Stakes

Not every claim requires the same sourcing. Routine facts need solid, identifiable sourcing. Significant allegations need strong, often multiple, independent sources. Claims of criminality or serious wrongdoing need the highest standard, with corroboration and senior editorial sign-off. Establish a tiered system that ties the required sourcing to the stakes: the more damaging or consequential the claim, the stronger and more independent the sourcing must be. A single standard for all claims either over-burdens routine facts or under-protects serious ones.

Document which tier each central claim meets, so the sourcing is reviewable rather than assumed.

### Require Independence For Corroboration

Two sources are not twice as strong if they share an origin. A wire story, a local report, a blog, and a social post may all trace to one press release; two officials may both rely on the same briefing. True corroboration requires independence: sources with separate knowledge, not separate repetitions of the same information. When claiming corroboration, verify that the sources are genuinely independent, and when they are not, say so or find independent confirmation.

Trace claims to their origin; if the chain rests on a single source, treat it as single-sourced regardless of how many outlets repeated it.

### Make Anonymity Earned And Described

Anonymous sourcing has legitimate uses, protecting vulnerable sources and enabling disclosure that would not otherwise occur, but it is also overused and abused, allowing interested parties to attack without accountability and weakening reader trust. Establish a high bar: anonymity requires that the information is newsworthy and factual, unavailable on the record, provided by someone with direct knowledge, and justified by a real risk of harm. Describe anonymous sources as fully as safety permits, so readers can judge reliability: their role, their access, and why anonymity was granted.

Anonymity granted casually at the end of an interview, without prior ground rules, is a common failure; set terms before sensitive information is shared.

### Standardize Attribution Language

Consistent attribution language helps readers compare reliability across stories. Define what "according to," "said," "confirmed," "alleged," and descriptive labels like "senior official" or "person familiar with the matter" mean in the outlet's usage, and apply them consistently. Vague labels, "sources said" without number, role, or basis, hide the evidence base and should be avoided. Readers should be able to tell, from the attribution, how strong and how independent the sourcing is.

Avoid labels that inflate reliability, such as "confirmed" when only one source has been checked, or that mislead about the source's knowledge.

### Describe Sources Accurately And Without Misleading

The way a source is described shapes how readers weigh the information. A "person familiar with the matter" may have direct knowledge or may be relaying rumor; a "official" may be senior or junior, with or without access to the specific information. Describe sources as precisely as safety permits, and never describe them in a way that overstates their knowledge or independence. If a source is interested, connected to a party, or advancing an agenda, that context should be available to readers or at least to editors.

When anonymity prevents revealing a source's interest, weigh whether the sourcing is appropriate at all.

### Require Editors To Know Source Identities

For anonymous sourcing, at least one editor responsible for the story should know the source's identity and the reason for anonymity. This protects against fabrication, allows verification of the source's knowledge and independence, and creates accountability. A reporter's unverified claim of an anonymous source is not sufficient; the editor must be able to assess the source directly. For the most sensitive sources, limit knowledge to the minimum necessary.

Editorial knowledge of source identity is a check that exists precisely because anonymity can be abused.

### Distinguish Reporting From Aggregation

When a story relies on another outlet's reporting, attribute it clearly and do not present it as the outlet's own. Aggregation that disguises its dependence on others' original reporting misleads readers about who did the work and exposes the outlet to the original's errors. Link to the original, name it, and distinguish what the outlet has independently confirmed from what it is passing along. Re-reporting to claim originality, by seeking a second source after reading another's scoop, is a form of disguise.

Establish clear rules for when aggregation is sufficient and when independent confirmation is required.

### Preserve The Sourcing Record

Maintain records sufficient to defend the sourcing after publication: who the sources were, what they said, how they were corroborated, and why anonymity was granted where applicable. This supports corrections, legal defense, and later review. Protect sensitive source details within the record, but preserve enough to reconstruct the basis of the story. A story whose sourcing cannot be reconstructed after publication is hard to defend.

## Common Traps

### Uniform Sourcing For All Claims

A single standard either over-burdens routine facts or under-protects serious allegations; requirements must track stakes.

### False Corroboration

Counting sources that share an origin as independent inflates reliability and misleads readers.

### Casual Anonymity

Granting anonymity without a real justification or prior ground rules enables interested parties to attack without accountability.

### Vague Attribution Labels

"sources said" without number, role, or basis hides the evidence base from readers.

### Misleading Source Descriptions

Describing a source in a way that overstates knowledge or conceals interest distorts how readers weigh the information.

### Editor Ignorance Of Source Identity

Anonymous sourcing without editorial knowledge of the source invites fabrication and abuse.

### Disguised Aggregation

Presenting others' reporting as original misleads readers and exposes the outlet to imported errors.

### Unreconstructable Sourcing

Without records, the story's basis cannot be defended after publication.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does each central claim meet a sourcing tier matched to its stakes, with the tier documented?
- [ ] Where corroboration is claimed, are the sources genuinely independent rather than sharing an origin?
- [ ] Is anonymity earned by newsworthiness, direct knowledge, unavailability on the record, and real risk, with sources described as fully as safety permits?
- [ ] Is attribution language standardized and applied consistently, avoiding labels that inflate or obscure reliability?
- [ ] Are sources described accurately, without overstating knowledge or concealing interest?
- [ ] Does at least one responsible editor know the identity of each anonymous source and the reason for anonymity?
- [ ] Is aggregation clearly attributed, with independent confirmation distinguished from passed-along reporting?
- [ ] Is the sourcing record preserved, sufficient to reconstruct and defend the basis while protecting sensitive details?
- [ ] Have sourcing standards been confirmed against the outlet's code by editorial leadership, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that sourcing standards are set by the standards function?
