# Ethics Code And Standards

An ethics code or standards manual is the institutional expression of what a newsroom stands for, and it exists to settle recurring questions consistently rather than case by case. The judgment problem is that standards are general and situations are specific, and a journalist who treats the code as a rigid rulebook will reach absurd results in edge cases, while one who treats it as optional will erode the consistency that gives the code its force. The discipline is to interpret standards in light of their purpose, apply them consistently, and escalate the cases where the purpose and the literal rule conflict.

Use this skill when applying or interpreting an ethics code, resolving an ethical question, and deciding how a standard governs a specific situation. The main risk is that an agent may apply standards inconsistently, interpret them to fit a preferred outcome, or bypass them under deadline pressure. This skill is editorial guidance; standards interpretation and exceptions rest with editorial leadership and the standards function.

## Core Rules

### Know The Code And Apply It Proactively

Standards cannot guide decisions that are made without reference to them. Know the outlet's ethics code, its standards manual, and its specific guidance on recurring issues: sourcing, anonymity, conflicts, corrections, sensitive content, and independence. Apply the standards proactively, before the decision is made, rather than retroactively to justify a result. When a situation arises, the first question is what the code requires, not what seems convenient.

Familiarity must extend to the code's purpose, not just its wording, because purpose governs interpretation when the wording is ambiguous.

### Interpret Standards In Light Of Their Purpose

Every standard serves a purpose: accuracy protects the audience from falsehood; independence protects coverage from improper influence; fairness protects subjects from unjustified harm; harm minimization protects vulnerable people. When a literal application of a rule produces a result that defeats its purpose, the purpose controls, and the case should be escalated for interpretation. Conversely, when a proposed exception serves convenience but not purpose, the rule stands. Purpose is the interpretive anchor that prevents both rigid literalism and opportunistic flexibility.

Document the purpose-based reasoning when departing from a literal reading, so the interpretation is reviewable.

### Apply Standards Consistently Across Cases

Consistency is what gives a code its legitimacy. Similar situations should receive similar treatment, regardless of the subject, the reporter, the beat, or the pressure. Inconsistent application, treating a friend's conflict differently from a stranger's, or a powerful subject's complaint differently from an ordinary one, undermines the code and signals that standards are negotiable. When two cases seem to call for different treatment, identify the principled distinction that justifies it, or treat them the same.

Audit your own consistency by comparing the current case to how similar past cases were handled.

### Distinguish Hard Rules From Judgment Standards

Some standards are hard rules: do not fabricate, do not plagiarize, do not accept bribes. These admit no exception and their violation is a serious breach. Others are judgment standards: when to grant anonymity, when to publish sensitive detail, how to balance harm and public interest. These require interpretation in context and admit reasoned variation. Do not treat judgment standards as if they were hard rules, producing rigid results, and do not treat hard rules as if they were judgment standards, allowing exceptions where none exist.

Confusing the two is a common source of both over- and under-application.

### Escalate Genuine Ambiguity And Novel Cases

Not every case is clearly governed. Novel situations, conflicts between standards, and cases where the purpose and the literal rule diverge require escalation to the standards function or senior editorial leadership. Escalation is not a failure; it is the mechanism by which the code develops and by which hard cases are resolved consistently. Do not resolve genuinely ambiguous cases alone, because individual resolution produces inconsistency and misses the chance to clarify the standard for the future.

Frame the escalation clearly: the situation, the relevant standards, the ambiguity, and the options.

### Do Not Let Deadline Pressure Override Standards

Deadlines create pressure to skip or shortcut standards, and this is when standards matter most. A story published fast but unethically causes more harm than a story held for proper review. Build standards checks into the workflow so that they are part of the normal process, not an extra burden added under deadline. When a deadline genuinely cannot accommodate a required check, the choice is to hold the story or to narrow it to what can be properly vetted, not to waive the standard.

Document any case where deadline pressure shaped a standards decision, so it can be reviewed.

### Treat The Code As Living, Not Fixed

Ethics codes encounter situations their authors did not foresee, and emerging issues, AI-generated content, new forms of sponsored material, novel surveillance, and cross-border publication, require the code to develop. When a situation is not adequately governed, raise it for standards review and code update, rather than improvising an ad hoc rule. A code that does not evolve becomes irrelevant to the situations that actually arise.

Propose updates with specific cases and reasoning, so revisions are grounded in real needs.

### Document Standards Decisions For Institutional Memory

Significant standards decisions, especially exceptions and novel interpretations, should be documented so they inform future cases. Institutional memory allows consistency across staff turnover and prevents the same hard case from being re-litigated each time. Maintain a record of how the code has been interpreted, with the reasoning, so that the code's application is cumulative rather than starting fresh each time.

Documentation also supports transparency; an outlet that can explain its standards decisions earns trust.

## Common Traps

### Retroactive Justification

Applying standards after the decision to rationalize a preferred outcome defeats the code's guiding function.

### Literalism That Defeats Purpose

Rigid application that produces absurd results in edge cases ignores the purpose that should govern interpretation.

### Inconsistent Application

Treating similar cases differently based on subject, reporter, or pressure undermines the code's legitimacy.

### Confusing Rules And Judgment Standards

Treating judgment standards as hard rules, or hard rules as judgment standards, produces both rigidity and unjustified exceptions.

### Resolving Ambiguity Alone

Individual resolution of genuinely ambiguous cases produces inconsistency and forgoes the chance to clarify the code.

### Deadline Waivers

Skipping standards under time pressure causes the ethical failures the code exists to prevent.

### Static Code In A Changing Field

A code that does not address emerging issues becomes irrelevant to the situations journalists actually face.

### Undocumented Decisions

Without records of interpretation and exception, the code's application starts fresh each time and loses consistency.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the relevant standard known and applied proactively before the decision, in light of its purpose?
- [ ] Where a literal application would defeat the standard's purpose, is the purpose-based reasoning documented and the case escalated?
- [ ] Is the application consistent with how similar past cases were handled, with any principled distinction identified?
- [ ] Are hard rules distinguished from judgment standards, with neither treated as the other?
- [ ] Is genuine ambiguity or novelty escalated to the standards function rather than resolved alone?
- [ ] Has deadline pressure been resisted as a basis for waiving standards, with holding or narrowing chosen instead?
- [ ] Has a gap in the code been raised for update where an emerging issue is not adequately governed?
- [ ] Are significant standards decisions documented for institutional memory and consistency?
- [ ] Has the standards decision been confirmed by editorial leadership or the standards function, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that standards interpretation and exceptions rest with editorial leadership?
