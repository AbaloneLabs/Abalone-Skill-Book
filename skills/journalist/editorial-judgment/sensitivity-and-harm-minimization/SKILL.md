# Sensitivity And Harm Minimization

Some journalism causes harm no matter how carefully it is done: covering death, violence, suicide, trauma, abuse, and disaster intrudes into grief, exposes victims, and can traumatize audiences and re-traumatize survivors. The judgment problem is that these stories are often genuinely newsworthy, and the harm is not a reason to avoid them, but the way they are covered, the details included, the identities revealed, the images shown, and the language used, determines how much avoidable harm accompanies the public interest. Harm minimization is the discipline of serving the story while reducing the damage it does to the people in it and the people reading it.

Use this skill when covering tragedy, violence, death, suicide, trauma, abuse, and disaster, and when deciding what graphic detail, identification, or imagery to include. The main risk is that an agent may pursue emotional impact or completeness without weighing the harm to victims, families, survivors, and audiences, or may avoid difficult stories altogether out of an excess of caution that abandons accountability. This skill is editorial guidance; sensitive coverage decisions rest with editorial leadership and may require specialist consultation.

## Core Rules

### Serve The Public Interest, Then Minimize Harm

The starting point is whether the story serves a public interest: accountability, understanding, prevention, or informed compassion. If it does, the task is to tell it with the least harm consistent with that purpose. If it does not, if the story is curiosity or emotion without public value, harm minimization means not publishing. Do not let harm minimization become an excuse to avoid difficult but necessary accountability stories; equally, do not let newsworthiness become a license for gratuitous harm.

State the public-interest purpose explicitly, then test every sensitive detail against whether it serves that purpose.

### Include Sensitive Detail Only When It Serves The Purpose

Graphic detail, identifying information, method of death or injury, intimate trauma, and personal records should be included only when they materially serve the public-interest purpose. Detail that shocks, moved, or completes a picture without adding understanding is gratuitous. For each sensitive element, ask what the audience understands with it that they would not understand without it. If the answer is nothing meaningful, remove or generalize the detail.

This applies to words and images alike: a graphic photograph should serve the same public-interest test as a graphic description.

### Apply Heightened Care To Identification

Identifying victims, survivors, the bereaved, witnesses, and minors carries significant harm, including harassment, re-traumatization, retaliation, and lasting search exposure. Default to non-identification or partial identification unless identification serves a specific public-interest purpose or the subject has consented. For minors, the presumption against identification is strongest. For victims of sexual violence, identification is rarely justified without informed consent.

When identification is necessary, obtain informed consent, explain the consequences, and respect the subject's right to decline or to control what is revealed.

### Handle Suicide And Self-Harm Responsibly

Coverage of suicide can cause contagion, where detailed or sensationalized reporting is associated with increases in suicide, particularly among vulnerable audiences. Follow evidence-based guidance: avoid describing method in detail, avoid sensationalized or romanticized framing, avoid prominent or repetitive placement, include help resources, and consider whether coverage is warranted at all for non-public figures. Treat suicide as a public-health story where possible, focusing on patterns and prevention rather than individual spectacle.

Responsible suicide coverage is one of the clearest cases where reporting choices cause measurable harm or measurable safety.

### Approach Trauma Survivors With Consent And Dignity

Trauma survivors, including victims of violence, disaster, abuse, and loss, should be approached with consent, dignity, and control over their participation. Do not ambush, pressure, or exploit grief for emotional material. Offer the option to decline, to pause, to withdraw, and to control what is shared. Recognize that recounting trauma can re-traumatize, and that the journalist's need for a quote does not override the subject's welfare.

Where possible, work with trauma-informed practices and, for complex cases, consult specialists in victim and survivor engagement.

### Use Graphic Imagery Deliberately

Graphic images, of death, injury, bodies, destruction, and violence, carry power and harm. Use them when they convey essential understanding that words cannot, and withhold or moderate them when they shock without informing. Consider cropping, partial use, warnings, and placement behind an interstitial for particularly graphic content. Balance the audience's right to understand against the harm to the depicted, the depicted's families, and the audience itself, some of whom may be survivors of similar trauma.

Provide content warnings for graphic material so audiences can make informed choices about exposure.

### Choose Language That Does Not Demean Or Sensationalize

Language frames subjects as victims, villains, statistics, or people. Avoid language that demeans the dead, exploits grief, sensationalizes violence, or reduces people to their trauma. Use precise, respectful language that conveys gravity without spectacle. The difference between "the body" and "the victim," between "massacre" and "incident," between respectful and exploitative framing, often lies in word choice that the audience feels even when they cannot articulate why.

Avoid stereotypes that compound harm, particularly around race, mental health, addiction, poverty, and immigration status in trauma coverage.

### Consider The Audience That Has Experienced Similar Harm

Part of the audience for any trauma story has lived through similar events. Graphic detail, vivid imagery, and sensational framing can re-traumatize readers, viewers, and listeners who are themselves survivors. Harm minimization includes the audience, not only the subjects. Content warnings, restraint, and responsible framing protect readers for whom the story is not abstract.

### Plan Follow-Up And Support For Journalists

Covering trauma harms journalists too, through repeated exposure to graphic material and survivors' suffering. Editors should consider rotation, support, and access to resources for reporters and editors immersed in traumatic coverage. Harm minimization includes the newsroom, and a journalist overwhelmed by exposure produces worse and less careful work.

## Common Traps

### Gratuitous Graphic Detail

Including shocking detail or imagery that does not serve understanding trades harm for impact.

### Identifying Vulnerable Subjects

Naming victims, survivors, minors, or the bereaved without consent or public-interest justification causes lasting harm.

### Sensationalized Suicide Coverage

Detailed method, romanticized framing, and prominent placement are associated with contagion and measurable harm.

### Exploiting Grief

Pressuring or ambushing the bereaved for emotional material exploits vulnerability for content.

### Demeaning Or Stereotyping Language

Word choice that demeans the dead, sensationalizes violence, or stereotypes by identity compounds harm.

### Ignoring Audience Trauma

Failing to consider readers who are themselves survivors exposes them to re-traumatization without warning.

### Avoidance As Over-Caution

Refusing to cover difficult but newsworthy accountability stories abandons the public-interest function under the guise of sensitivity.

### Neglecting Journalist Welfare

Failing to support reporters and editors immersed in traumatic coverage harms them and degrades the work.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the public-interest purpose stated, and is every sensitive detail tested against whether it serves that purpose?
- [ ] Are victims, survivors, the bereaved, witnesses, and minors identified only with consent or specific public-interest justification, with minors most strongly protected?
- [ ] Does suicide and self-harm coverage follow evidence-based guidance on method, framing, prominence, and help resources?
- [ ] Are trauma survivors approached with consent, dignity, control, and the option to decline, with specialist consultation for complex cases?
- [ ] Is graphic imagery used only when it conveys essential understanding, with cropping, warnings, and placement considered?
- [ ] Does the language convey gravity without demeaning, sensationalizing, or stereotyping subjects?
- [ ] Has the audience of survivors been considered, with content warnings provided for graphic material?
- [ ] Are journalists covering trauma supported through rotation and resources?
- [ ] Have sensitive coverage decisions been reviewed by editorial leadership and, where appropriate, trauma or subject specialists, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that harm-minimization judgments in high-stakes cases may require expert consultation?
