# News Value And Play Decisions

Deciding how much attention a story gets, where it is placed, how large the headline, how long the piece, whether it leads or is buried, is an editorial act as consequential as the reporting itself. Play decisions signal to the audience what matters, shape the public agenda, and determine which stories reach attention and which disappear. The judgment problem is that play is driven by a mix of genuine public interest, audience metrics, competitive pressure, and institutional preference, and an editor who cannot separate these forces will misallocate attention, overplaying the sensational and underplaying the significant.

Use this skill when deciding story placement, prominence, length, and coverage priority, and when weighing competing stories for limited space or attention. The main risk is that an agent may let traffic, novelty, or competitive instinct drive play decisions while treating the public-interest judgment as secondary. This skill is editorial guidance; play decisions rest with editorial leadership.

## Core Rules

### Separate Public Interest From Audience Interest

Public interest, what the audience needs to know for informed citizenship and accountability, is not the same as audience interest, what the audience wants to read. Both are legitimate considerations, but they pull in different directions, and an editor who serves only the second abandons the accountability function of journalism. Distinguish the two explicitly when deciding play: a story may be important without being popular, or popular without being important, and play should reflect a deliberate weighing of both rather than the unconscious dominance of one.

Document the rationale for significant play decisions so the weighing is visible and reviewable.

### Judge Prominence By Consequence, Not Drama

The stories that deserve the most prominence are those with the greatest consequence for the audience: decisions that affect their lives, accountability for powerful actors, systemic failures, and information needed to participate as citizens. Drama, novelty, conflict, and emotional impact can justify coverage, but they do not by themselves justify top play. An editor who leads with the dramatic over the consequential teaches the audience to value spectacle over substance.

When a dramatic story and a consequential story compete for lead position, ask which one the audience would, in retrospect, have needed to know.

### Resist Competitive And Herd Pressure

When every outlet leads with a story, the pressure to match is strong, but herd coverage is not a measure of news value. A story that competitors overplay may deserve less prominence, not more, if its consequence does not justify the attention. Equally, a story competitors ignore may deserve strong play if its consequence is high. Judge each story on its own public-interest merit, not on what others are doing with it.

Competitive pressure is especially distorting in breaking news, where unverified material spreads because no one wants to be left out.

### Apply Consistent Standards Across Beats And Subjects

Play decisions reveal priorities, and inconsistent standards undermine credibility. If similar conduct by different political figures, companies, or institutions receives different play, the audience infers bias, whether or not it is intentional. Apply consistent news-value standards across beats, subjects, and ideologies, and be able to explain why two superficially similar stories received different treatment.

Inconsistency is often unconscious, driven by familiarity with some subjects and distance from others; explicit standards make it visible.

### Weigh The Cost Of Attention Against The Value

Attention is finite, and every story that receives prominent play displaces others. The decision to lead with one story is a decision not to lead with others. Consider opportunity cost: what is not being covered because this story is leading? A story that is mildly interesting but displaces coverage of a consequential issue may be the wrong lead. Calibrate play to the full landscape of available stories, not just to the story in front of you.

This is especially important on limited surfaces such as a homepage top slot, a front page, or a newsletter lead.

### Distinguish Coverage From Non-Coverage Deliberately

The decision not to cover a story is as editorial as the decision to cover it, and it should be made deliberately. Sometimes non-coverage is right: the story is unverified, trivial, or driven by manipulation. Sometimes non-coverage is a failure: the story is consequential but unfamiliar, inconvenient, or outside the beat. Make non-coverage a conscious choice with a stated reason, not a default produced by inattention.

For consequential stories the outlet is choosing not to cover, document the reason so the decision can be reviewed.

### Calibrate Play To Verification Status

A story's prominence should reflect how well it is verified. Strongly verified, high-consequence reporting deserves strong play. Weakly verified or single-source material, even if dramatic, deserves more cautious play, narrower framing, or holding until verification improves. Overplaying under-verified material forces corrections and damages credibility; the play should match the proof.

This is the play equivalent of matching certainty to evidence in writing.

### Revisit Play As The Story Develops

Play is not fixed at first publication. As a story develops, verification improves, consequences emerge, or the story fades, play should adjust. A story that led at first publication may deserve demotion if it weakens; a story that was buried may deserve elevation if its consequence grows. Build review points into rolling coverage so play tracks the story's actual significance over time.

Static play decisions in dynamic stories leave the audience with an outdated sense of importance.

## Common Traps

### Traffic-Driven Play

Letting audience metrics determine prominence over public interest leads to overplaying the sensational and underplaying the significant.

### Drama Over Consequence

Leading with the dramatic rather than the consequential teaches the audience to value spectacle over substance.

### Herd Coverage

Matching competitors' play regardless of a story's actual news value amplifies distortion across the whole press.

### Inconsistent Standards

Different play for similar conduct by different subjects signals bias and undermines credibility.

### Ignoring Opportunity Cost

Leading with one story without considering what it displaces misallocates finite attention.

### Default Non-Coverage

Failing to cover a consequential story through inattention, rather than a deliberate choice, is an editorial failure.

### Overplaying Under-Verified Material

Strong play for weakly verified material forces corrections and credibility damage when the story shifts.

### Static Play In Dynamic Stories

Failing to adjust play as a story develops leaves the audience with an outdated sense of importance.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has public interest been distinguished from audience interest, with play reflecting a deliberate weighing of both?
- [ ] Is prominence calibrated to consequence rather than drama, novelty, or emotional impact?
- [ ] Has competitive and herd pressure been resisted, with each story judged on its own merit?
- [ ] Are news-value standards applied consistently across beats, subjects, and ideologies, with differing treatment explainable?
- [ ] Has opportunity cost been considered, including what other stories the chosen play displaces?
- [ ] Is non-coverage a deliberate, documented choice rather than a default of inattention?
- [ ] Does play match verification status, with under-verified material played cautiously or held?
- [ ] Are play decisions revisited as the story develops, with elevation or demotion tracking actual significance?
- [ ] Have significant play decisions been reviewed by editorial leadership, recognizing that this skill is editorial guidance and that final play authority rests with editors?
